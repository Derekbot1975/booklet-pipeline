# Formative Assessment in Drama

## Introduction to Formative Assessment in Drama

Formative assessment in Drama education refers to the ongoing process of gathering evidence about student learning to inform teaching decisions and support student progression. Unlike summative assessment, which measures achievement at a fixed point, formative assessment is iterative, diagnostic, and fundamentally integrated into the learning process itself. In Drama, where much learning is experiential, embodied, and collaborative, formative assessment takes on particular significance and complexity.

The work of Dorothy Heathcote emphasised that assessment in Drama should focus on the quality of thinking and engagement rather than purely theatrical outcomes. She argued that "the assessment is of the quality of the exploration, not the quality of the performance" (Heathcote and Bolton, 1995). This principle remains central to effective formative assessment practice in UK Drama education.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Drama-Specific Assessment Challenges

Jonothan Neelands has written extensively about the particular challenges of assessing Drama, noting that the subject requires assessment of "process and product, individual and group work, cognitive and affective learning" (Neelands, 2004). He identifies three distinct but interconnected domains in Drama learning:

1. **Making** (creating and developing dramatic work)
2. **Performing** (presenting and communicating meaning)
3. **Responding** (reflecting, evaluating, and understanding)

These domains provide a framework for formative assessment that recognises the multifaceted nature of Drama learning.

### The Role of Reflection

Gavin Bolton's work emphasises that reflection is not separate from but integral to dramatic learning. He argues that "to be in a drama is to be aware that one is in a drama" (Bolton, 1992), suggesting that metacognitive awareness should be actively cultivated through formative assessment practices. This "metaxis" – the dual consciousness of being both in and out of role – provides unique opportunities for self-assessment and peer feedback.

## National Curriculum Context

### Key Stage 3

The National Curriculum for England requires that students at Key Stage 3 should "receive constructive feedback on their work" and "develop the confidence and skills to evaluate the work of others respectfully" (DfE, 2013). In Drama, this translates to formative assessment practices that:

- Monitor students' ability to use dramatic forms, techniques, and conventions
- Track development in collaborative skills and ensemble working
- Assess understanding of theatrical context and cultural significance
- Evaluate capacity for creative problem-solving within dramatic frameworks

### Key Stage 4

At GCSE level, all major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) incorporate formative assessment principles within their specifications, though the formal assessments remain summative. Teachers must use formative strategies to prepare students for:

- **Component 1/Devising** (typically 40% of final grade): ongoing feedback during the devising process, assessment of collaborative contributions, evaluation of research and development
- **Component 2/Performance from Text** (typically 20%): rehearsal feedback, character development tracking, interpretation discussions
- **Component 3/Written Examination** (typically 40%): formative assessment of analytical skills, understanding of theatrical production elements, and live theatre evaluation

### Key Stage 5

A-Level Drama specifications (AQA Drama and Theatre, Edexcel Drama, OCR Drama and Theatre, WJEC Eduqas Drama and Theatre) require more sophisticated formative assessment practices, including:

- Assessment of research methodologies and contextual understanding
- Evaluation of conceptual development in practical work
- Monitoring of theoretical understanding and analytical capabilities
- Tracking of independent learning and rehearsal discipline

## Formative Assessment Strategies in Practice

### Teacher-Led Strategies

#### In-Process Observation

Effective formative assessment in Drama often occurs whilst students are actively engaged in practical work. Andy Kempe argues that "the Drama teacher must develop the skill of reading the room" (Kempe and Ashwell, 2000), observing not just what is being created but how students are engaging with the creative process. Key indicators include:

- Quality of listening and responsiveness to others
- Willingness to take creative risks
- Application of previously taught techniques or conventions
- Depth of engagement with character, role, or situation
- Problem-solving strategies when facing creative challenges

Teachers should maintain observation notes or use mobile technology to capture moments of significant learning, which can inform subsequent feedback conversations.

#### Questioning Within and Outside Role

Dorothy Heathcote's "teacher in role" strategy provides powerful formative assessment opportunities. By adopting a role within the drama, teachers can question students' understanding, challenge their thinking, and assess the depth of their engagement without breaking the dramatic frame. Questions might probe:

- Character motivation and internal logic
- Understanding of dramatic situation and context
- Emotional and social awareness within the drama
- Ability to sustain and develop dramatic tension

Outside the drama, focused questioning techniques help assess understanding of form and technique: "Why did you choose that convention?", "How did that staging choice affect meaning?", "What alternatives did you consider?"

#### Live Feedback and Side-Coaching

During practical work, immediate verbal feedback can significantly enhance learning. Cecily O'Neill advocates for what she terms "interventionist teaching" within process drama, where the teacher actively shapes and develops the work whilst it unfolds (O'Neill, 1995). This might include:

- Pausing work to highlight effective practice
- Offering technical adjustments in real-time
- Raising the challenge level mid-activity
- Providing vocabulary to articulate what students are discovering

### Peer and Self-Assessment

#### Structured Peer Feedback

Neelands emphasises that Drama is inherently collaborative and that "students learn as much from watching and responding to others as from their own making" (Neelands and Goode, 2015). Effective peer assessment strategies include:

- **Gallery critique**: Groups present work-in-progress in short showings, with specific feedback criteria provided by the teacher (e.g., "Comment on use of space and levels", "Identify where tension was created or lost")
- **Response partners**: Students work in pairs throughout a project, providing ongoing feedback using structured prompts
- **Forum theatre approaches**: Borrowed from Augusto Boal, audiences can stop action and suggest alternatives, providing immediate formative feedback

The teacher's role is to model the language of constructive criticism and establish a classroom culture where feedback is specific, kind, and focused on improvement.

#### Self-Assessment Through Reflective Practice

Bolton argues that "feeling and distanced reflection should occur concurrently" in Drama (Bolton, 1992). Practical self-assessment strategies include:

- **Exit cards**: Brief written reflections at lesson end (e.g., "What technique did you use today?", "What will you work on next lesson?")
- **Video review**: Students record and review their practical work, using rubrics or structured questions to guide analysis
- **Learning journals**: Ongoing documentation of creative process, including sketches, notes, research, and reflections
- **Development logs**: Particularly valuable for devised work and coursework at Key Stages 4 and 5, tracking evolution from initial ideas to final performance

### Digital Tools for Formative Assessment

Contemporary Drama teaching increasingly incorporates digital technologies for formative assessment:

- **Video recording**: Tablets and smartphones enable immediate playback and analysis of practical work
- **Digital portfolios**: Platforms like Google Classroom or Seesaw allow students to collate reflections, images, videos, and research
- **Collaborative documents**: Shared Google Docs or OneNote enable real-time feedback on written analysis and planning
- **Polling and quiz tools**: Kahoot, Mentimeter, or Google Forms can assess understanding of terminology, conventions, or contextual knowledge

## Assessment Criteria and Success Indicators

### Developing Assessment Literacy

Kempe and Ashwell emphasise that students must understand "the what and the why of assessment" (Kempe and Ashwell, 2000). This requires teachers to:

1. Share and unpack assessment criteria in accessible language
2. Provide exemplar material showing work at different standards
3. Co-construct success criteria with students for specific tasks
4. Use consistent terminology aligned with examination specifications at Key Stages 4 and 5

### Criterion-Referenced Assessment in Drama

Rather than comparing students to each other, Drama assessment should focus on criterion-referenced progression. Key criteria across key stages typically include:

**Making/Creating:**
- Originality and creativity in generating ideas
- Application of dramatic conventions and techniques
- Development and refinement of material
- Collaboration and ensemble skills
- Research and contextual understanding

**Performing/Presenting:**
- Use of voice (pitch, pace, pause, projection, tone)
- Use of body and movement (gesture, posture, gait, spatial awareness)
- Character development and portrayal
- Use of space and staging
- Communication of meaning to an audience
- Technical control and confidence

**Responding/Evaluating:**
- Use of appropriate terminology
- Analysis of own and others' work
- Understanding of theatrical context and purpose
- Evaluation of design and technical elements
- Cultural and historical awareness

## Feedback Strategies

### Written Feedback

Effective written feedback in Drama should be:

- **Timely**: Provided whilst the work is still fresh and ongoing
- **Specific**: References particular moments, choices, or techniques
- **Developmental**: Identifies clear next steps for improvement
- **Balanced**: Acknowledges strengths whilst addressing areas for development
- **Dialogic**: Invites student response or reflection

At Key Stages 4 and 5, written feedback should increasingly reference assessment objectives and grade boundaries, helping students understand their current level and targets.

### Verbal Feedback

Given Drama's practical nature, much formative assessment occurs through conversation. Neelands advocates for "critical friendship" between teacher and students, where feedback is:

- Given immediately after practical work when possible
- Focused on specific, actionable improvements
- Framed as questions that prompt student thinking ("What if you tried...?", "How else might you...?")
- Offered both individually and to groups as appropriate
- Recorded by students in learning journals or logs

### Feed-Forward

Drawing on Black and Wiliam's seminal research on assessment for learning (1998), Drama teachers should emphasise "feed-forward" – specific guidance on how to improve – rather than purely evaluative comments. Strategies include:

- **WWW/EBI structure**: "What Went Well" and "Even Better If"
- **Target-setting**: Identifying one or two specific, achievable targets for next lesson or rehearsal
- **Modelling**: Demonstrating improved practice or showing exemplar work
- **Revision opportunities**: Building in time for students to act on feedback and refine work

## Managing Formative Assessment

### Planning for Assessment

Effective formative assessment requires deliberate planning. Each scheme of work should identify:

- Key assessment opportunities (practical showings, reflective tasks, written responses)
- Assessment focus for each lesson or sequence of lessons
- How evidence will be gathered and recorded
- How feedback will be provided
- Time for students to respond to feedback

### Recording and Tracking Progress

While formative assessment should not be bureaucratic, some record-keeping is essential. Practical approaches include:

- **Class tracking grids**: Recording assessment judgements against key criteria for each student
- **Photographic/video evidence**: Capturing key moments of practical work
- **Student portfolios**: Collating student reflections, plans, and self-assessments
- **Mark book comments**: Brief notes on significant observations or feedback given
- **Learning platforms**: Using school VLE or Drama-specific software to track progress

### Ensuring Inclusivity and Equity

Gavin Bolton emphasised that Drama's strength lies in its capacity to "meet children where they are" (Bolton, 1992). Formative assessment should:

- Recognise diverse forms of achievement (not all students will excel at performance, but may demonstrate sophisticated understanding through design, directing, or analysis)
- Accommodate different learning needs through varied assessment methods
- Avoid cultural bias in texts, examples, and success criteria
- Celebrate progress from individual starting points rather than fixed standards
- Provide additional scaffolding or challenge as appropriate

## Linking Formative and Summative Assessment

### Key Stage 4 and 5 Context

At GCSE and A-Level, formative assessment directly feeds into summative outcomes. For devising components:

- Ongoing formative feedback shapes the final piece
- Development logs document the creative journey
- Teacher assessment is informed by observed process as well as final product

For performance components:

- Rehearsal feedback develops technical and interpretative skills
- Mock performances provide formative information before final assessment
- Video review enables detailed analysis and improvement

For written examinations:

- Practice questions and mark schemes provide clear success criteria
- Formative assessment of analytical writing develops examination technique
- Live theatre reviews create opportunities for formative feedback on evaluation skills

### Moderation and Standardisation

All GCSE and A-Level specifications require teachers to maintain evidence of their assessment judgements. Formative assessment records contribute to this evidence base, demonstrating how students have progressed toward final outcomes. Internal standardisation meetings should review:

- Examples of student work at different levels
- Assessment criteria interpretation
- Feedback quality and consistency
- Integration of formative processes into final grades

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge: Assessing Group Work

Drama is inherently collaborative, but accountability systems focus on individual achievement. Solutions include:

- **Individual reflection within group work**: Each student articulates their specific contribution
- **Peer assessment of contribution**: Students evaluate each other's collaborative skills
- **Role rotation**: Ensuring all students experience different responsibilities
- **Video analysis**: Reviewing recordings to identify individual contributions

### Challenge: Subjectivity in Practical Assessment

While Drama involves aesthetic judgement, assessment should be as objective as possible. Strategies include:

- **Clear criteria**: Specific descriptors of quality at different levels
- **Multiple perspectives**: Incorporating self, peer, and teacher assessment
- **Moderation**: Reviewing assessments with colleagues
- **Evidence base**: Supporting judgements with specific examples and observations

### Challenge: Time Constraints

Drama teachers often feel formative assessment takes time from practical work. However, Kempe argues that "assessment and learning are not separate processes" (Kempe and Ashwell, 2000). Efficient approaches include:

- Integrating assessment into activities (e.g., peer feedback as part of sharing work)
- Using structured observation focuses (assessing specific criteria in rotation)
- Brief, frequent feedback rather than lengthy written comments
- Student-led assessment strategies that develop independence

## Conclusion

Formative assessment in Drama is not an addition to teaching and learning but an integral part of it. As Cecily O'Neill observed, effective Drama teaching involves "a continuous process of shaping and responding to emerging meanings" (O'Neill, 1995) – a fundamentally formative process.

The most effective formative assessment in Drama:

- Honours the subject's practical, collaborative, and exploratory nature
- Develops students' capacity for self-evaluation and artistic judgement
- Provides specific, actionable feedback that improves learning
- Recognises diverse forms of achievement and progress
- Prepares students for summative assessments whilst maintaining educational integrity
- Creates dialogue between teacher and students about quality and progress

By embedding these principles into everyday practice, Drama teachers can ensure that assessment genuinely serves learning, developing not only students' theatrical skills but their capacity for critical reflection, creative problem-solving, and collaborative artistry.
