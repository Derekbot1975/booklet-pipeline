# A Level Drama and Theatre Specifications

## Overview of A Level Drama and Theatre Studies

A Level Drama and Theatre Studies represents the culmination of secondary drama education in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, typically studied by students aged 16-18 in Years 12 and 13. The qualification bridges the gap between GCSE Drama (Key Stage 4) and potential higher education study or professional training in theatre and performance disciplines. Unlike GCSE, A Level specifications require students to engage with sophisticated theoretical frameworks, extensive practitioner research, and sustained practical exploration.

All UK A Level Drama specifications are regulated by Ofqual (in England), Qualifications Wales, and CCEA (in Northern Ireland), ensuring comparability of standards while allowing variation in assessment approaches and content emphases. The qualifications typically carry 96-120 guided learning hours per year across two years of study.

## Core Assessment Structure Across Specifications

### Common Assessment Objectives

All exam boards structure their Drama and Theatre A Levels around similar Assessment Objectives (AOs), though weighting varies:

**AO1**: Create and develop ideas to communicate meaning for theatrical performance. This objective emphasises the creative process advocated by practitioners such as Dorothy Heathcote, whose process drama methodology influences contemporary devising approaches.

**AO2**: Apply theatrical skills to realise artistic intentions in live performance. This draws heavily on Stanislavskian naturalism, Brechtian epic theatre, and contemporary physical theatre techniques, reflecting Gavin Bolton's emphasis on embodied learning through dramatic action.

**AO3**: Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of how drama and theatre is developed and performed. Here, students engage with Jonothan Neelands's conventions of drama and recognise how theatrical meaning emerges through structured conventions.

**AO4**: Analyse and evaluate their own work and the work of others. This critical reflection mirrors the reflective practitioner model promoted by Cecily O'Neill, who argued that artistic development requires continuous metacognitive engagement.

### Typical Component Structure

Most specifications divide assessment across three components:
- **Component 1**: Devising (practical coursework, typically 40% of qualification)
- **Component 2**: Text performance (practical examination, typically 20%)
- **Component 3**: Theatre makers in practice (written examination, typically 40%)

## Specific Examination Board Specifications

### AQA Drama and Theatre (7262)

The AQA specification, widely adopted in English comprehensive schools and academies, structures assessment as follows:

**Component 1: Drama and Theatre (40%)** requires students to create an original piece of theatre based on a prescribed stimulus, working collaboratively in groups of 2-6. Students document their process through a written coursework portfolio (maximum 3,000 words) demonstrating engagement with practitioner methodology. This component explicitly values the devising approaches explored by Andy Kempe and Helen Nicholson in their seminal work *Learning to Teach Drama 11-18* (2001).

**Component 2: Creating Original Drama (20%)** assesses students performing either two monologues or one duologue/group piece extracted from one complete performance text. This examination occurs in controlled conditions with a visiting examiner. Students demonstrate their understanding of characterisation, vocal skills, physicality, and spatial awareness.

**Component 3: Making Theatre (40%)** consists of a written examination (2 hours 30 minutes) assessing:
- Live theatre evaluation (one production seen during the course)
- Page-to-stage realisation of one complete performance text from a prescribed list
- Practical exploration of one extract from a different performance text

AQA's prescribed text list includes classical and contemporary works from diverse cultural traditions, including plays by Lorca, Berkoff, Churchill, and Godber, alongside international theatre texts.

### Edexcel/Pearson Drama and Theatre (9DR0)

The Pearson Edexcel specification emphasises theatrical professionalism and industry awareness:

**Component 1: Devising (40%)** requires students to create, develop, and perform original theatre influenced by the work and methodologies of prescribed practitioners. The assessment includes both the devised performance (36 marks) and a detailed portfolio (24 marks, 3,000-3,500 words) analysing the creative process. Notably, Edexcel mandates engagement with specific theatre practitioners from their published list, which includes Stanislavski, Brecht, Artaud, Berkoff, and Frantic Assembly, among others.

**Component 2: Text in Performance (20%)** requires students to perform from two key extracts from a single text or two contrasting monologues. Edexcel provides an extensive text list updated annually. The assessment focuses on interpretation, characterisation, and vocal/physical skills, reflecting Dorothy Heathcote's emphasis on authentic role adoption.

**Component 3: Theatre Makers in Practice (40%)** assesses through written examination (2 hours 30 minutes):
- Section A: Live theatre evaluation (choice of questions)
- Section B: Page-to-stage realisation, requiring directorial, design, and performance analysis
- Section C: Practical exploration with extended written response

Edexcel's approach particularly values the concept of "theatre makers"—practitioners who integrate multiple theatrical roles, reflecting contemporary industry practice.

### OCR Drama and Theatre (H459)

OCR's specification emphasises scholarly research and theoretical engagement:

**Component 1: Drama and Theatre (16% internally assessed practical work, 20% written portfolio)** combines devised performance with extensive theoretical analysis. The portfolio demands rigorous engagement with theatrical theory, research methodology, and critical reflection. This component explicitly acknowledges Cecily O'Neill's concept of "process drama" where meaning emerges through structured improvisational exploration.

**Component 2: Creating and Presenting Theatre (20%)** assesses performance skills through examination of scripted material, with students working in groups of 2-6. OCR allows both monologue and ensemble approaches, providing flexibility for different centres.

**Component 3: Production, Processes and Approaches (40%)** consists of a written examination (2 hours 30 minutes) assessing:
- Section A: Practical exploration documented through written responses
- Section B: Live theatre evaluation
- Section C: Theoretical and practical understanding of complete performance texts

OCR's text list specifically includes texts suitable for ensemble devising and physical theatre, acknowledging Jonothan Neelands's structuring conventions including "marking the moment," "ritual," and "teacher in role" adapted for A Level contexts.

### WJEC Eduqas Drama and Theatre (A680QS)

The Welsh specification offers particular cultural diversity:

**Component 1: Theatre Workshop (40%)** combines practical devising (20%) with supporting written documentation (20%). Students must explore two prescribed theatrical styles or genres, encouraging breadth of theatrical vocabulary. This component values Gavin Bolton's concept of "drama for learning" adapted for senior study, where theatrical form serves to investigate meaningful social questions.

**Component 2: Text in Action (20%)** requires performance of text in examination conditions, assessed by visiting examiners. WJEC emphasises interpretative authenticity and textual understanding.

**Component 3: Text in Performance (40%)** consists of a written examination (2 hours 30 minutes) covering:
- Live theatre evaluation
- Set text study from a prescribed list
- Design or performance analysis of a named text

WJEC's specification particularly acknowledges Welsh theatrical traditions while maintaining engagement with international theatre practices.

## Prescribed Practitioners and Theoretical Frameworks

### Core Practitioners Across Specifications

All specifications require engagement with key theatre practitioners whose methodologies inform both practical and theoretical study:

**Konstantin Stanislavski**: His system of psychological realism, including emotion memory, the magic if, and given circumstances, remains foundational. Students explore naturalistic characterisation techniques applicable to texts by Miller, Williams, and Ibsen.

**Bertolt Brecht**: Epic theatre conventions including alienation effect (Verfremdungseffekt), gestus, historicisation, and direct address inform approaches to political theatre. Brecht's influence appears particularly in devising work addressing social issues, reflecting Andy Kempe's emphasis on drama as critical pedagogy.

**Antonin Artaud**: Theatre of Cruelty concepts challenge students to explore visceral, non-naturalistic performance, particularly relevant to physical theatre and experimental devising.

**Steven Berkoff**: Physical theatre methodology combining total theatre, stylisation, and ensemble work provides accessible contemporary practitioner study. Berkoff's published plays (*Metamorphosis*, *East*) appear regularly on prescribed text lists.

**Frantic Assembly**: Contemporary physical theatre company whose devising processes and movement vocabulary (push hands, round-by-through, chair duets) directly inform practical coursework, particularly for students exploring contemporary social themes.

### Application of Heathcote, Neelands, and Bolton Methodologies

Though primarily associated with educational drama rather than theatre performance, the pedagogical frameworks of Dorothy Heathcote, Jonothan Neelands, and Gavin Bolton significantly inform A Level teaching methodology:

**Dorothy Heathcote's Mantle of the Expert** provides framework for ensemble devising where students adopt collective expertise to explore complex situations. Her concept of "pressing for precision" informs the rigorous analytical expectations of A Level portfolios and written examinations.

**Jonothan Neelands's Structuring Drama Work** (with Tony Goode, 2015) offers systematic conventions applicable to devising processes. Conventions including "hot seating," "thought tracking," "marking the moment," and "ritual" provide vocabulary for practical exploration documented in Component 1 portfolios across all specifications.

**Gavin Bolton's Acting in Classroom Drama** (1998) distinguishes between "illustrative" and "authentic" dramatic action—a distinction crucial for understanding the difference between superficial demonstration and embodied characterisation valued at A Level.

**Cecily O'Neill's Drama Worlds** (1995) conceptualises drama as negotiated fictional contexts where meaning emerges collaboratively. This theoretical framework informs understanding of ensemble devising and audience engagement, particularly relevant to experimental and immersive theatre forms.

## Key Texts and Performance Repertoire

### Classical and Modern Canonical Texts

All specifications include texts representing theatrical history and diverse cultural traditions:

- **Ancient Greek Drama**: Sophocles (*Antigone*), Euripides (*Medea*), providing foundation for understanding theatrical origins, chorus work, and tragic convention
- **Elizabethan/Jacobean**: Shakespeare (*Othello*, *The Tempest*), Marlowe (*Doctor Faustus*), Webster (*The Duchess of Malfi*)
- **Restoration and 18th Century**: Sheridan (*The Rivals*, *The School for Scandal*), Goldsmith (*She Stoops to Conquer*)
- **19th Century Naturalism**: Ibsen (*A Doll's House*, *Hedda Gabler*), Chekhov (*The Cherry Orchard*, *The Seagull*)
- **Early 20th Century**: Shaw (*Saint Joan*), Wilde (*The Importance of Being Earnest*), Synge (*The Playboy of the Western World*)

### Contemporary Theatre Texts

A Level specifications emphasise post-1956 drama representing diverse voices and theatrical innovations:

- **Social Realism**: John Godber (*Teechers*, *Bouncers*), Jim Cartwright (*Road*, *Two*), Mark Wheeller (*Too Much Punch for Judy*)
- **Political Theatre**: Caryl Churchill (*Top Girls*, *Far Away*), David Hare (*The Permanent Way*), Mark Ravenhill (*Shopping and F***ing*)
- **Physical and Experimental**: Steven Berkoff (*Metamorphosis*), Sarah Kane (*Blasted*, *4.48 Psychosis*), Martin Crimp (*The Treatment*)
- **International Perspectives**: Lorca (*Blood Wedding*, *The House of Bernarda Alba*), Dario Fo (*Accidental Death of an Anarchist*), Wole Soyinka (*Death and the King's Horseman*)
- **Contemporary British**: Jez Butterworth (*Jerusalem*), Lucy Kirkwood (*Chimerica*), Dennis Kelly (*DNA*)

Text selection reflects the National Curriculum's emphasis on cultural capital and exposure to significant dramatic literature, whilst acknowledging contemporary theatrical practice.

## Live Theatre Experience Requirements

### Statutory Requirements

All A Level Drama specifications mandate live theatre attendance as essential curriculum enrichment. Minimum requirements typically include:

- **At least one professionally produced performance** evaluated formally for written examination
- **Additional performances recommended** to broaden theatrical vocabulary and comparative understanding
- **Performance styles should vary**, exposing students to naturalism, physical theatre, musical theatre, immersive theatre, and experimental forms

### Pedagogical Value of Theatre Attendance

Research by Matthew Reason and Dee Reynolds (*Kinesthesia, Empathy, and Related Pleasures*, 2010) demonstrates that live performance attendance develops embodied understanding impossible through text study alone. Students develop:

- **Analytical vocabulary** through exposure to professional production choices
- **Embodied spectatorship** connecting to Gavin Bolton's concept of lived-through experience
- **Cultural literacy** essential for contextualising theatrical traditions
- **Critical discrimination** distinguishing effective from ineffective artistic choices

Andy Kempe's research emphasises that theatre attendance functions as "vicarious practitioner experience," enabling students to observe professional application of theoretical concepts studied in classroom contexts.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

**Budgetary considerations**: Schools typically allocate £100-200 per student annually for theatre visits, though financial constraints remain significant barriers for disadvantaged students. Arts Council England and regional theatre education programmes often provide subsidised tickets and transport.

**Geographical equity**: Students in London and major cities access diverse professional theatre; rural students face limited provision. Digital theatre platforms (National Theatre Live, Digital Theatre+) partially address this inequality, though specifications explicitly value live attendance.

**Performance selection**: Teachers should strategically select productions representing:
- Practitioner methodologies studied (e.g., physical theatre companies like Frantic Assembly)
- Prescribed texts or playwrights
- Diverse theatrical conventions and cultural traditions
- High production values demonstrating professional standards

## Practical Implications for Teaching and Learning

### Curriculum Planning and Delivery

Effective A Level Drama teaching requires integration of practical exploration with theoretical study. The specification structure demands:

**Two-year curriculum mapping**: Teachers should sequence practitioner study, text analysis, and practical projects across 60-80 teaching weeks, allowing sufficient time for devising development (typically 8-12 weeks), text rehearsal (6-8 weeks), and examination preparation.

**Workshop-based pedagogy**: Dorothy Heathcote's insight that "drama is not therapy but an art form" reminds teachers to maintain rigorous aesthetic standards whilst valuing collaborative exploration. Workshop sessions should balance structured skill development with creative freedom.

**Theoretical integration**: Unlike process drama at Key Stage 3, A Level demands explicit theoretical knowledge. Teachers must scaffold understanding of Brechtian Verfremdungseffekt, Stanislavskian objectives and super-objectives, Artaudian assault on the senses, ensuring students articulate methodology accurately in portfolios and examinations.

### Assessment Preparation Strategies

**Portfolio development**: Component 1 portfolios demand sophisticated analytical writing. Andy Kempe's concept of "reflective practitioner" informs effective portfolio pedagogy where students continuously document creative decisions, theoretical influences, and evaluative reflection. Teachers should:

- Model analytical writing conventions specific to drama (describing blocking, spatial relationships, design elements)
- Establish regular formative feedback cycles throughout devising periods
- Provide exemplar materials demonstrating grade boundaries
- Encourage multimedia documentation (rehearsal photographs, annotated scripts, design sketches)

**Performance preparation**: Component 2 text performance requires sustained characterisation work. Teachers should apply Gavin Bolton's distinction between "demonstrating" and "embodying" characters, pushing students toward authentic inhabitation rather than superficial indication.

**Examination technique**: Component 3 written papers demand sophisticated theatrical terminology and comparative analysis. Jonothan Neelands's conventions provide vocabulary for describing theatrical events; teachers should explicitly teach this metalanguage enabling precise articulation of performance observations.

### Differentiation and Inclusion

A Level Drama accommodates diverse learners through multiple assessment modes:

**Practical strengths**: Students excelling in embodied performance access success through Component 1 and 2 practical assessment.

**Analytical strengths**: Students preferring critical writing demonstrate understanding through portfolios and written examinations.

**Access arrangements**: Standard examination board provisions include additional time, scribe support, and modified practical requirements for students with disabilities or special educational needs.

Cecily O'Neill's inclusive pedagogy emphasises that all students bring valuable perspectives to collaborative theatre-making, regardless of conventional academic achievement.
