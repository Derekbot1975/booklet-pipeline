# Cultural Diversity in Drama

## Introduction

Cultural diversity in Drama education represents a fundamental principle of inclusive practice, ensuring that all students see themselves reflected in the curriculum whilst developing understanding of cultures beyond their own experience. The 2014 National Curriculum emphasises that Drama should enable pupils to "appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live theatre drawn from different times and cultures" (DfE, 2014). This knowledge file examines how Drama practitioners can embed culturally diverse practice throughout Key Stages 1-5, drawing on established pedagogical approaches and contemporary research.

## Historical Context and Key Thinkers

### Dorothy Heathcote's Approach to Cultural Understanding

Dorothy Heathcote's work at Newcastle University established that drama's unique power lies in enabling participants to "walk in another's shoes" through process drama. Heathcote advocated for what she termed "universal experiences" approached through specific cultural contexts, arguing that drama allows students to explore cultural difference whilst recognising shared humanity. Her "mantle of the expert" methodology enables students to adopt roles from diverse cultural backgrounds with appropriate contextual knowledge and dignity (Heathcote & Bolton, 1995, *Drama for Learning*).

### Jonothan Neelands and the Politics of Performance

Neelands' work on democratic theatre practice emphasises that Drama classrooms must be "sites of cultural democracy" where multiple voices are valued equally (*Beginning Drama 11-14*, 1990). His conventions-based approach provides structures that enable exploration of cultural narratives without appropriation or stereotyping. Neelands argues that Drama teachers must actively curate diverse texts and practices, as "the canon we choose to teach is a political act" (Neelands & Goode, 2015, *Structuring Drama Work*).

### Cecily O'Neill's Process Drama Framework

O'Neill's process drama methodology, detailed in *Drama Worlds* (1995), provides frameworks for exploring cultural narratives through what she terms "pre-text" – cultural stories, images, or objects that generate dramatic inquiry. Her approach emphasises authentic research and collaborative meaning-making, enabling students to engage with cultural material as active interpreters rather than passive recipients.

## National Curriculum Requirements and Statutory Guidance

### Key Stage 1 and 2 Expectations

The National Curriculum for English at Key Stages 1-2 requires that students "participate in performances" and understand stories from "different times and cultures". The non-statutory guidance for Drama emphasises that teachers should:

- Select stories and scenarios from diverse cultural traditions
- Invite exploration of customs, celebrations, and daily life from various communities
- Use drama to develop empathy and understanding of difference
- Challenge stereotypes through nuanced characterisation

### Key Stage 3 Framework

At KS3, the National Curriculum stipulates that students should "know and understand the history and cultural development of their art forms". For Drama, this explicitly requires:

- Study of theatrical traditions beyond naturalism
- Engagement with historical and cultural contexts of performance
- Analysis of how cultural identity shapes theatrical expression
- Understanding of how theatre both reflects and challenges cultural norms

### Key Stage 4 and 5 Examination Specifications

All major examination boards now mandate cultural diversity in Drama specifications:

**AQA GCSE Drama (8261)** requires students to study live theatre from different periods and cultures, with Component 3 (Theatre Makers in Practice) explicitly assessing understanding of cultural and historical context.

**Edexcel GCSE Drama (1DR0)** Component 3 mandates study of one complete performance text, with specifications noting that texts should represent "different periods, styles, and cultures".

**OCR GCSE Drama (J316)** requires exploration of "theatre from different cultures and traditions" in Component 2, with examiners' reports consistently highlighting that successful candidates demonstrate nuanced cultural understanding.

**WJEC GCSE Drama (C660UB)** (Wales) explicitly includes exploration of "Welsh-language theatre and culturally diverse theatrical traditions".

At A Level, cultural diversity is further embedded. **AQA A Level Drama and Theatre (7262)** Component 1 includes compulsory study of practitioners and companies from diverse traditions, whilst **Edexcel A Level Drama and Theatre (9DR0)** requires analysis of "performance texts from different cultural contexts".

## Pedagogical Approaches to Cultural Diversity

### Creating Culturally Responsive Classroom Practice

Andy Kempe's work on applied drama emphasises the importance of what he terms "cultural scaffolding" – providing students with sufficient contextual knowledge to engage meaningfully with diverse material (*The GCSE Drama Coursebook*, 2016). This includes:

**Research-led exploration**: Before engaging with drama from specific cultures, students undertake guided research using primary and secondary sources, potentially including consultation with community members.

**Language and terminology**: Teaching appropriate cultural terminology and pronunciation, avoiding anglicisation or simplification.

**Multiple perspectives**: Examining how cultural narratives contain internal diversity and contested meanings, avoiding monolithic representations.

**Reflective practice**: Regular reflection on how students' own cultural positioning affects their interpretation.

### Dorothy Heathcote's "Commission Model" for Cultural Exploration

Heathcote's later work developed a "commission" approach particularly valuable for cultural exploration. Students are tasked by an external authority (real or fictional) to investigate cultural practices or historical events, positioning them as researchers rather than performers. This creates what Heathcote termed "responsible distance" – students engage deeply without inappropriate appropriation (Bolton, 1998, *Acting in Classroom Drama*).

### Neelands' Conventions for Cultural Material

Specific Neelands conventions prove particularly effective for culturally diverse content:

**Ceremony and ritual**: Students devise ceremonies reflecting cultural practices, emphasising symbolic meaning over authenticity of reproduction.

**Collective drawing**: Groups create visual representations of cultural stories, generating discussion about symbolism and interpretation.

**Forum theatre**: Following Boal's methodology, students examine cultural conflicts or challenges, exploring multiple possible responses.

**Spokesman**: A group member speaks in role while others create tableaux, useful for presenting cultural narratives with appropriate gravitas.

## Repertoire Selection and Canonical Diversity

### Diversifying Text Choices

Gavin Bolton argued that Drama's educational value lies partly in its textual choices: "The plays we study teach students not just about theatre, but what voices matter" (*Drama as Education*, 1984). Contemporary practice requires active curation of diverse playwrights and theatrical traditions.

**Key Stage 3 recommendations** should include:

- *Blood Wedding* (Lorca) - Spanish rural traditions and honour culture
- *Death and the King's Horseman* (Soyinka) - Yoruba tradition and colonial encounter
- *Yerma* (Lorca, adapted by Simon Stone) - exploring cultural adaptation
- *The Bone Setter's Daughter* (Tan/Drabinsky) - Chinese-American experience
- *Shan-E-Mughal* (Aftab) - Mughal historical drama
- *In the Heights* (Miranda) - Latino diaspora experience
- *Pah-La* (Patel) - British South Asian identity

**Key Stage 4 examined texts** might include:

- *The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time* (Stephens) - neurodiversity representation
- *1001 Nights* (Turf/Coyote) - Middle Eastern storytelling traditions
- *Master Harold...and the Boys* (Fugard) - apartheid South Africa
- *Top Girls* (Churchill) - diverse female historical figures
- *Blood Wedding* (Lorca) - cultural tradition and modernity
- *DNA* (Kelly) - contemporary British multiculturalism

### Devising from Diverse Cultural Sources

When students create original work, cultural diversity should inform stimulus selection. The AQA GCSE Component 1 (Devising) and Edexcel Component 2 specifically assess students' ability to develop theatrical material from varied sources. Teachers might provide:

- Oral histories from diverse community members
- Folklore and mythology from non-Western traditions
- Contemporary news stories affecting minoritised communities
- Cultural celebrations and rituals
- Biographical material from diverse historical figures
- Artworks and music from various cultural traditions

## Practical Strategies by Key Stage

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

**Storytelling from diverse traditions**: Use stories from African, Asian, Caribbean, and Indigenous traditions. The work of Taffy Thomas MBE and Pomme Clayton demonstrates how traditional stories can be adapted for educational drama without cultural appropriation.

**Celebrations and festivals**: Explore how different cultures mark significant occasions through dramatic play, movement, and costume.

**Universal themes, specific contexts**: Heathcote advocated exploring themes like "belonging", "fairness", or "courage" through culturally specific stories.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

**Historical drama with diverse perspectives**: When exploring British history (Romans, Vikings, Tudors), include perspectives of diverse peoples present in Britain. Projects like "Black and Asian Britain" (Historical Association) provide authentic historical material.

**Myth and legend**: Compare creation myths, hero journeys, and trickster tales across cultures, exploring common patterns and unique features.

**Contemporary diversity**: Create drama exploring contemporary multicultural Britain, potentially using Neelands' conventions to explore identity and belonging.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

**Practitioner study**: Explore diverse theatre practitioners including Augusto Boal (Brazilian Theatre of the Oppressed), Wole Soyinka (Nigerian total theatre), Tadashi Suzuki (Japanese physical training), Frantic Assembly (diverse British physical theatre), and Complicité (international collaborative devising).

**Text in context**: Study how plays reflect cultural moments, comparing British social realism (Shelagh Delaney, *A Taste of Honey*) with international works addressing similar themes differently.

**Physical theatre traditions**: Explore Kathakali, Beijing Opera, Commedia dell'Arte, and Noh theatre as codified performance systems, examining how culture shapes theatrical language.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

**Component 1/2 (Practical)**: Ensure devising projects draw on culturally diverse stimuli. Moderator reports consistently note that sophisticated work demonstrates cultural research and authentic engagement rather than superficial inclusion.

**Component 3 (Written)**: Teach analytical frameworks for discussing cultural context. OCR's 2022 Examiners' Report praised candidates who discussed "how cultural context informs directorial choices" with specific examples.

**Live theatre analysis**: Select productions featuring diverse companies. Recent examples include National Theatre's *Life of Pi* (Martel/adapt. Lolita Chakrabarti), Talawa Theatre Company productions, Yellow Earth Theatre, Tara Arts, and Tamasha Theatre Company.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

**Component 1 (Devising/Text performance)**: A Level specifications allow sophisticated engagement with cultural material. Students might:
- Adapt culturally significant texts for contemporary contexts
- Research specific cultural performance traditions for practical exploration
- Collaborate with cultural organisations or community groups

**Component 2 (Portfolio/Written examination)**: Essays should demonstrate understanding of how cultural context shapes theatrical creation. Edexcel's 2023 Principal Examiner Report noted that top-band responses analysed "the interplay between cultural traditions and innovative practice".

**Component 3 (Practical exploration)**: Choose practitioners whose work centres cultural identity, such as:
- Robert Lepage (Québécois identity and technology)
- Peter Brook (intercultural experimentation and critique)
- Timberlake Wertenbaker (translation and cultural adaptation)
- deborah brevoort (Asian-American theatrical forms)
- Roy Williams (Black British experience)

## Avoiding Cultural Appropriation and Stereotyping

### Defining Appropriate Engagement

Cultural appropriation in Drama education occurs when dominant groups extract elements from minoritised cultures without understanding, credit, or consent, particularly for assessment or entertainment purposes. O'Neill's work emphasises that drama should create "authentic encounters" rather than performances of otherness.

**Inappropriate practices include**:
- "Acting" ethnic or racial identities through accent, costume, or physicality for entertainment
- Superficial representation of cultural practices without contextual understanding
- Using sacred or ceremonial practices as drama exercises
- Selecting only trauma narratives from minoritised cultures
- Expecting students from specific backgrounds to be "cultural experts"

**Appropriate practices include**:
- Studying culturally specific texts with proper contextual teaching
- Inviting cultural practitioners as workshop leaders
- Using Heathcote's "mantle of the expert" to position students as researchers
- Focusing on universal themes within culturally specific contexts
- Acknowledging complexity and avoiding monolithic representation

### Andy Kempe's "Critical Distance" Approach

Kempe advocates maintaining "critical distance" when exploring cultural material (*Drama Schemes for 11-14 Year Olds*, 2003). This involves:

**Analytical frames**: Rather than "becoming" characters from other cultures, students might work as directors, dramaturgs, or researchers, analysing how cultural context shapes theatrical choices.

**Documentary approaches**: Using testimony, historical records, and interviews to create verbatim or documentary theatre, crediting sources explicitly.

**Meta-theatrical devices**: Acknowledging the artifice of theatrical representation and students' relationship to material, potentially using Brechtian techniques.

## Working with Cultural Communities

### Partnership and Consultation

Effective culturally diverse practice often involves partnership with cultural organisations and communities. Organisations supporting this work include:

**National organisations**:
- Talawa Theatre Company (Black British theatre, education programmes)
- Yellow Earth Theatre (East and Southeast Asian theatre)
- Tara Arts (South Asian theatre and intercultural work)
- Tamasha Theatre Company (British Asian theatre)
- Graeae Theatre Company (Deaf and disabled artists)
- Theatre Centre (diverse new writing for young people)

**Regional partnerships**: Many regional theatres maintain community engagement programmes connecting schools with diverse artists and companies.

### Guest Practitioners and Workshops

Inviting cultural practitioners into schools provides authentic engagement. When arranging such visits:

- Budget appropriately for professional fees, recognising expertise
- Brief practitioners on curriculum requirements and student needs
- Prepare students with contextual knowledge beforehand
- Follow up with curriculum-connected work
- Evaluate impact on student understanding and confidence

## Assessment and Cultural Diversity

### GCSE and A Level Mark Schemes

Examination board mark schemes increasingly reward culturally informed practice:

**AQA GCSE Component 1 (Devising)**: Top marks require "sophisticated understanding of cultural and social context" informing devising choices.

**Edexcel A Level Component 3**: Band 5 responses demonstrate "perceptive analysis of how cultural context influences theatrical interpretation".

**OCR GCSE Component 2**: Examiners specifically note that successful candidates "avoid stereotypical representation" and show "nuanced understanding of cultural material".

### Formative Assessment Approaches

Teachers should assess students' developing cultural understanding through:

**Research portfolios**: Students document cultural research underpinning practical work, citing sources and reflecting on learning.

**Reflective journals**: Kempe advocates regular reflection on how students' own cultural positioning affects interpretation and creative choices.

**Peer discussion**: Structured dialogue where students articulate cultural context and respond to questions, developing explanatory skills.

## Contemporary Issues and Future Directions

### Decolonising the Drama Curriculum

Recent discourse around curriculum decolonisation, influenced by organisations like the National Education Union's Anti-Racism Charter and the Runnymede Trust's work, calls for Drama educators to:

- Examine which voices have been historically centred and marginalised
- Question the "canon" of studied texts and practitioners
- Explore how theatre has been used both to reinforce and challenge colonialism
- Study contemporary practitioners from formerly colonised nations
- Acknowledge British theatre's complex relationship with empire

### Digital Access and Global Theatre

Technology enables access to diverse theatre globally. Resources include:

- National Theatre Collection (National Theatre at Home) - diverse productions available to schools
- Digital Theatre Plus - extensive catalogue including diverse companies
- Marquee TV - international theatre streaming
- Company websites often feature production archives and educational resources

### Inclusive Casting and Representation

Contemporary professional practice increasingly embraces inclusive casting – casting actors regardless of race, ethnicity, or disability in roles not explicitly requiring specific identities. This practice raises important pedagogical questions:

- How do students understand characters when casting doesn't reflect traditional assumptions?
- What opportunities does inclusive casting create for diverse student performers?
- How might colour-conscious versus colour-blind casting be discussed?

## Conclusion and Recommendations

Cultural diversity in Drama education represents both ethical imperative and pedagogical opportunity. Drawing on the foundational work of Heathcote, Neelands, O'Neill, Bolton, and Kempe, contemporary practice should:

1. **Curate diverse repertoire** across all Key Stages, ensuring students encounter theatrical traditions beyond British naturalism
2. **Provide cultural scaffolding** so students engage meaningfully rather than superficially with diverse material
3. **Avoid appropriation and stereotyping** through carefully designed pedagogical approaches that maintain appropriate distance
4. **Partner with cultural organisations** to provide authentic encounters with diverse practitioners
5. **Embed diversity in assessment** through stimulus selection and analytical frameworks
6. **Reflect critically** on whose voices the curriculum centres and why
