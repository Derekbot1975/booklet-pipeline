# SEND in Drama

## Introduction

Drama is uniquely positioned as a subject to support learners with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND), offering multisensory, experiential learning that can bypass traditional literacy barriers whilst developing communication, social interaction, and emotional regulation. The SEND Code of Practice (2015) defines four broad areas of need: Communication and Interaction; Cognition and Learning; Social, Emotional and Mental Health Difficulties; and Sensory and/or Physical Needs. Drama pedagogy, particularly the process-driven approaches championed by Dorothy Heathcote and Gavin Bolton, aligns naturally with inclusive practice by valuing diverse modes of expression and collaborative meaning-making.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Drama as an Inclusive Art Form

Dorothy Heathcote's concept of "mantle of the expert" proves particularly powerful for SEND learners, as it positions all participants as knowledgeable contributors within a fictional context, thus reducing anxiety and building self-efficacy. Heathcote observed that drama creates what she termed a "no-penalty zone" where learners can experiment with language, behaviour, and social interaction without real-world consequences (Heathcote & Bolton, 1995, *Drama for Learning*).

Gavin Bolton's emphasis on "living through" drama rather than demonstrating skill for external audiences removes performative pressure that may disadvantage SEND learners. Bolton argued that the learning happens in the doing, not in polished presentation, making drama accessible to those who struggle with traditional assessment models (Bolton, 1992, *New Perspectives on Classroom Drama*).

Jonothan Neelands' conventions-based approach provides structured frameworks that support learners with autism spectrum conditions (ASC) or those requiring predictable routines. His categorisation of drama strategies in *Structuring Drama Work* (Neelands & Goode, 2015) offers teachers a toolkit for differentiation, from still image work requiring minimal verbal communication to more complex improvisation.

### Universal Design for Learning in Drama

Drama inherently addresses the three principles of Universal Design for Learning (CAST, 2018): multiple means of representation (visual, auditory, kinaesthetic learning); multiple means of action and expression (speaking, moving, creating); and multiple means of engagement (choice, relevance, collaboration). Cecily O'Neill's process drama methodology exemplifies this through its emergent, participant-driven structure that allows for differentiated entry points and outcomes (*Drama Worlds*, 1995).

## SEND Categories and Drama Pedagogy

### Communication and Interaction Needs

Drama provides authentic contexts for developing communication skills for learners with speech, language and communication needs (SLCN), autism spectrum conditions, and selective mutism.

**Specific Strategies:**

- **Role-based communication**: Adopting a role can free learners with selective mutism or social anxiety to speak, as the pressure is deflected onto the character. Teachers should allow non-verbal participation initially, using roles like "silent witness" or "observer" before gradually introducing dialogue.

- **Vocabulary in context**: Gavin Bolton emphasised that drama creates what he called "pressing need to communicate" – functional language development where words have immediate purpose and meaning. For learners with language delay, this contextualised learning aids retention and transfer.

- **Social scripts through drama conventions**: Neelands' conventions such as "meeting" or "interview" provide structured social interactions that help learners with ASC understand conversation patterns. The predictable format reduces anxiety whilst developing turn-taking and topic maintenance.

- **Non-verbal communication pathways**: Still image (tableau), movement work, and soundscaping allow learners with severe SLCN to participate fully without verbal demands. Andy Kempe's work on physical theatre approaches (*The GCSE Drama Coursebook*, 2018) demonstrates how gesture, proxemics, and levels communicate meaning equivalently to dialogue.

**Key Stage Considerations:**

- **KS1-2**: Focus on drama games, story-making, and teacher-in-role to develop foundational communication in safe contexts
- **KS3**: Introduce improvisation with clear structures; use Forum Theatre to explore communication choices
- **KS4-5**: Exam boards provide reasonable adjustments including scribed responses, extended time, and alternative performance formats. AQA's GCSE Drama specification explicitly allows group devising with differentiated roles.

### Cognition and Learning Needs

Learners with moderate learning difficulties (MLD), severe learning difficulties (SLD), and specific learning difficulties like dyslexia benefit from drama's concrete, experiential approach to knowledge construction.

**Specific Strategies:**

- **Concrete to abstract progression**: Heathcote's "mantle of the expert" begins with concrete, practical activities (sorting, making, investigating) before moving toward abstract concepts. For learners with MLD, this scaffolding is essential.

- **Chunked learning through episodic structure**: Breaking drama work into short episodes or scenes prevents cognitive overload. Neelands' convention of "narration" allows teacher-controlled pacing.

- **Visual supports and symbols**: Use of props, images, and visual timetables supports learners with memory difficulties. The National Autistic Society recommends visual structure for ASC learners, equally applicable to broader SEND populations.

- **Repetition and ritual**: Gavin Bolton noted that drama's cyclical nature – returning to similar situations with deepening understanding – supports memory consolidation. Establishing ritual openings and closings provides security for learners with cognitive needs.

- **Script accessibility**: For examination drama, OCR and Edexcel permit use of coloured overlays, enlarged texts, and digital scripts for dyslexic learners. Consider alternatives to traditional script memorisation such as devised work from stimuli.

**Differentiation in Assessment:**

The reformed GCSE specifications (2016) from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas all include written components that may challenge learners with cognition and learning needs. Reasonable adjustments include:

- Use of word processors and assistive technology
- Scribe or speech-to-text software
- Modified papers with simplified language
- Additional time (typically 25% extra)
- Rest breaks

For learners with SLD, schools may consider Entry Level qualifications or the AQA Award in Drama (Entry 3), which assess practical skills without written examination.

### Social, Emotional and Mental Health Difficulties

Drama's capacity to explore emotional terrain through the safety of role makes it particularly valuable for learners with SEMH needs, though requires careful facilitation.

**Specific Strategies:**

- **Distancing techniques**: Cecily O'Neill emphasised that drama works at "the edge of meaning" – close enough to be engaging but distant enough to be safe. Using historical settings, fantasy, or metaphor provides emotional distance from personal trauma.

- **Teacher-in-role for modelling**: Dorothy Heathcote's teacher-in-role technique allows adults to model emotional regulation, conflict resolution, and appropriate social responses within the fiction. This indirect teaching proves more acceptable to learners who resist direct instruction.

- **Choice and agency**: Offering genuine choices about character, situation, or direction of the drama respects learner autonomy, crucial for those with attachment difficulties or oppositional behaviours. Andy Kempe advocates for negotiated curriculum in drama.

- **Therapeutic boundaries**: Distinguish between drama for learning and drama therapy. Teachers are not therapists but can use Jonothan Neelands' "protected space" of drama to explore challenging themes. The Drama Therapy literature (Jones, 2007) provides useful boundary-setting principles.

- **Exit strategies**: Establish clear ways for learners to leave the drama if emotionally overwhelmed: "pause" signals, designated safe spaces, or pre-agreed support systems.

**Whole-School Approaches:**

Drama supports broader SEMH interventions:

- Structured drama interventions (e.g., "Promoting Alternative Thinking Strategies" – PATHS) develop emotional literacy
- Restorative practice using Forum Theatre techniques (Boal, 1992, *Games for Actors and Non-Actors*)
- Social skills groups using process drama methodology

### Sensory and/or Physical Needs

Drama is adaptable for learners with visual impairment (VI), hearing impairment (HI), and physical disabilities (PD), though requires proactive planning.

**Specific Strategies for Visual Impairment:**

- Verbal description of space and action
- Tactile exploration of props and set before drama begins
- Audio description conventions integrated into drama
- Sound-focused conventions: soundscaping, radio play, audio theatre
- Consistent spatial organisation; avoiding unexpected furniture moves

**Specific Strategies for Hearing Impairment:**

- British Sign Language (BSL) integration or Communication Support Workers
- Visual drama conventions: still image, mime, physical theatre
- Amplification systems and acoustic management
- Written or visual cues alongside verbal instructions
- Ensure speaker visibility for lip-reading

**Specific Strategies for Physical Disabilities:**

- Redefine "movement" inclusively: wheelchair users can create levels, patterns, and dynamic stage pictures
- Focus on what bodies *can* do; Andy Kempe's work with inclusive theatre companies demonstrates how limitation generates creativity
- Accessible staging and rehearsal spaces
- Role assignment based on contribution, not mobility
- Consider digital or puppetry alternatives that extend physical capability

**Examination Access:**

Exam boards provide extensive physical access arrangements:

- AQA GCSE Drama allows video submission of performances for mobility-impaired candidates
- Extra time for written papers
- Practical examinations can be adapted (e.g., seated performance, modified staging)
- EDI (Equality, Diversity and Inclusion) statements in coursework explain access challenges

## Curriculum and Framework Integration

### National Curriculum References

The National Curriculum for England (2014) emphasises inclusion across all subjects. In Drama, this manifests through:

**Key Stage 3 (Years 7-9):**
The Drama curriculum should ensure all pupils "improvise, devise and script drama for one another and an audience" whilst developing "physical, vocal and dramatic techniques" (NC, 2014). For SEND learners, this requires differentiated access to improvisation, scripting, and performance.

**Key Stage 4 (Years 10-11):**
GCSE Drama specifications (AQA, 2024; Edexcel, 2024; OCR, 2024; WJEC Eduqas, 2024) all assess three components:
1. Understanding drama and theatre (written exam)
2. Devising drama (practical)
3. Texts in practice (practical)

SEND learners access these through reasonable adjustments whilst maintaining assessment validity. The Equality Act (2010) requires exam boards to make adjustments that do not undermine the assessment construct.

**Key Stage 5 (Years 12-13):**
A-Level Drama and Theatre specifications increase textual analysis and theoretical understanding. Support strategies include:
- Digital annotation of texts
- Model essays and writing frames
- Oral presentation alternatives where permitted
- Extended Project Qualifications (EPQ) allowing individual research into inclusive theatre practice

### The SEND Code of Practice (2015) and Drama

The statutory guidance requires schools to:

1. **Assess**: Identify SEND through quality-first teaching observation. In Drama, notice learners who struggle with group work, emotional regulation during improvisations, or processing multi-step instructions.

2. **Plan**: Create SEND Support Plans specifying drama-specific strategies (e.g., pre-teaching vocabulary, providing visual scripts, allowing movement breaks).

3. **Do**: Implement interventions using Drama pedagogy (process drama for language development, Forum Theatre for social skills).

4. **Review**: Monitor progress using drama-specific success criteria (participation, collaboration, in-role communication).

### Ofsted and Inclusive Drama Practice

Ofsted's Education Inspection Framework (2019) evaluates how well schools support SEND learners within quality first teaching. Inspectors look for:

- Adaptive teaching that meets individual needs without lowering expectations
- SEND learners making strong progress from starting points
- Development of wider skills (communication, confidence, cultural capital)

Drama provides observable evidence of inclusion. The subject's collaborative, dialogic nature demonstrates "inclusive classrooms" valued in the framework.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning for Inclusion

**Differentiation by:**

- **Task**: Offer varied roles within the same drama (narrator, protagonist, silent witness)
- **Outcome**: Accept diverse responses (verbal, visual, written, physical)
- **Support**: Provide scaffolding (writing frames, visual cues, peer buddies)
- **Resource**: Use multisensory stimuli (images, objects, sounds, texts)

**Schemes of Work:**

Include SEND considerations at planning stage:

- Identify potential barriers (language, social interaction, sensory)
- Plan pre-teaching of key vocabulary or concepts
- Prepare visual supports and adapted resources
- Consider grouping strategies (mixed-ability pairs, interest-based groups)
- Build in reflection opportunities for processing learning

### Classroom Management

**Physical Environment:**

- Clear, wide pathways for wheelchair users or visually impaired learners
- Defined drama space with consistent boundaries
- Acoustic consideration (carpets, curtains to reduce echo for HI learners)
- Fidget tools or movement breaks for ADHD learners
- Calm areas for emotional regulation

**Behaviour Management:**

Drama can trigger heightened emotion. Strategies include:

- Clear, consistent routines for transitions into and out of drama
- Explicit teaching of drama's social contract (respect, risk-taking, collaboration)
- Positive behaviour framing: "In drama we listen to everyone's ideas"
- Logical consequences related to dramatic context rather than punitive sanctions
- Restorative conversations using drama techniques (hot-seating, freeze-frame reconstruction)

### Working with Teaching Assistants

Teaching Assistants (TAs) are crucial for SEND inclusion but require clear guidance:

- Avoid creating dependency; position TA as facilitator not rescuer
- Brief TA on drama content and their role (participant, observer, scribe)
- Use TA expertise on individual learner needs and communication methods
- Model inclusive practice so TA can support in future sessions
- Debriefing after lessons to refine support strategies

### Assessment for Learning

Drama's formative assessment opportunities suit SEND learners:

- **Observation**: Note participation, collaboration, problem-solving
- **Questioning**: Use drama conventions like "thought-tracking" to make thinking visible
- **Self-assessment**: Hot-seating or forum theatre allow learners to evaluate choices
- **Peer feedback**: "Audience" role creates purposeful peer assessment
- **Documentation**: Photos, videos, audio recordings capture learning for non-writers

For summative assessment, apply access arrangements systematically whilst maintaining rigour.

## Research Evidence and Impact

### Evidence Base for Drama and SEND

Empirical research demonstrates drama's effectiveness for SEND populations:

**Communication and Language:**

- Helen Nicholson's research (*Theatre, Education and Performance*, 2011) documents language development in drama-based interventions with SLCN learners, showing vocabulary gains and increased spontaneous communication.

- The Communication Trust (2021) identifies drama as an evidence-based approach for speech and language development, particularly functional communication in social contexts.

**Social and Emotional Learning:**

- Studies by Hughes and Wilson (2004) on drama with SEMH learners found improved emotional regulation, empathy, and conflict-resolution skills compared to control groups.

- The Education Endowment Foundation's Teaching and Learning Toolkit (2021) rates arts participation as having moderate impact (+3 months) on academic progress, with particular benefits for disadvantaged and SEND learners.

**Autism Spectrum Conditions:**

- Research by Peter (2003) on drama interventions with autistic children demonstrated improvements in social interaction, theory of mind, and imaginative play – core deficit areas in autism.

- The National Autistic Society advocates structured drama as beneficial for developing social understanding when anxiety-reducing strategies are in place.

### Professional Organisations and Resources

**Key Organisations:**

- **Drama UK**: Professional body offering CPD on inclusive practice
- **National Drama**: Subject association with SEND-specific resources
- **National Association for Special Educational Needs (nasen)**: Publishes guidance on drama for SEND
- **Council for Disabled Children**: Advocates inclusive arts education

**Essential Reading:**

## Conclusion

Drama education's alignment with SEND pedagogy is profound rather than incidental. The subject's emphasis on collaborative meaning-making, multisensory learning, and process over product creates naturally inclusive environments. However, inclusion requires intentional planning, knowledge of individual needs, and skilled facilitation. 

Dorothy Heathcote's assertion that "drama is for everyone" carries an obligation: teachers must actively remove barriers through adaptive teaching, appropriate resources, and genuine belief in all learners' dramatic and creative capability. When
