# Devising Drama

## Introduction and Definition

Devising drama is a collaborative creative process whereby performers generate original theatrical material without working from a pre-existing script. In UK Drama education, devising has become a cornerstone pedagogical approach, recognised in the National Curriculum and examined at GCSE and A-Level across all major examination boards. The process typically involves participants in collaborative exploration, experimentation, and refinement of dramatic ideas, often beginning with a stimulus such as a theme, image, object, or piece of text.

The prominence of devising in UK Drama education reflects broader developments in contemporary theatre practice, where companies such as Frantic Assembly, Complicité, and Forced Entertainment have established devising as a legitimate theatrical form. In educational contexts, devising offers students opportunities to become 'makers' of theatre rather than simply interpreters, developing creative autonomy alongside collaborative skills.

## Historical Context and Key Thinkers

### Dorothy Heathcote and Drama as Learning Medium

Dorothy Heathcote's pioneering work in Drama in Education during the 1960s-1980s established foundational principles for devising approaches. Heathcote advocated for 'teacher in role' and 'mantle of the expert' strategies, where students collaboratively build dramatic contexts through improvisation. Her work, documented extensively by Gavin Bolton in *Drama as Education: An Argument for Placing Drama at the Centre of the Curriculum* (1984), emphasised the epistemological value of making drama rather than merely performing existing texts.

Heathcote's concept of 'productive tension' and 'frame distance' provides crucial understanding for devising practitioners, as students must balance emotional engagement with critical reflection. Her influence persists in contemporary devising pedagogy, particularly in approaches that value process over product.

### Jonothan Neelands and Structuring Devices

Jonothan Neelands' work, particularly *Structuring Drama Work* (co-authored with Tony Goode, 1990, revised 2015), has been instrumental in providing practical frameworks for devising. Neelands identified and categorised approximately 70 drama conventions that function as building blocks for devised work. These conventions—including still image, thought tracking, forum theatre, hot-seating, and collective character—provide students with a vocabulary of dramatic forms to deploy during the devising process.

Neelands' concept of 'dramatic playing' emphasises spontaneity and exploration, whilst 'dramatic performance' involves shaping and refining material for presentation. This distinction helps teachers scaffold devising processes appropriately at different Key Stages. His later work has emphasised the democratic potential of devising, particularly in developing students' cultural voices and critical engagement with social issues.

### Cecily O'Neill and Process Drama

Cecily O'Neill's development of 'process drama' in *Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama* (1995) provides sophisticated theoretical underpinning for devising methodologies. O'Neill's work demonstrates how extended dramatic exploration can occur without predetermined outcomes, with meaning emerging through the collaborative journey. Her concept of the 'dramatic playing field' suggests that devising creates liminal spaces where participants negotiate between reality and fiction.

O'Neill's emphasis on 'pre-text'—the stimulus that initiates dramatic exploration—has particular relevance for GCSE and A-Level devising components, where students must demonstrate how initial stimuli transform into theatrical outcomes. Her frameworks help students understand devising as intellectually rigorous artistic practice rather than unstructured improvisation.

### Gavin Bolton's Educational Framework

Gavin Bolton's extensive writing, including *Acting in Classroom Drama* (1998) and *A History of Drama Education: A Search for Substance* (1998), provides crucial pedagogical context for devising. Bolton distinguished between 'drama for understanding' and 'theatre for communication', suggesting that effective devising bridges both purposes. His work emphasises the internal cognitive and affective dimensions of devising, arguing that the process develops symbolic thinking and metacognitive awareness.

Bolton's concept of 'metaxis'—the simultaneous awareness of both the fictional and real worlds—helps students develop critical distance during devising, enabling reflection whilst remaining creatively engaged.

### Andy Kempe's Practical Pedagogy

Andy Kempe's publications, particularly *The GCSE Drama Coursebook* (various editions with Lionel Warner) and *Drama Education and Special Needs* (2012), translate theoretical frameworks into classroom practice. Kempe emphasises structured approaches to devising that develop students' technical vocabulary alongside creative skills. His work on differentiation within devising processes has particular relevance for inclusive practice, demonstrating how different students can contribute meaningfully through varied roles within collaborative creation.

## National Curriculum References

### Key Stage 3

The National Curriculum for England (2014) states that Drama education should ensure students "improvise, devise and script drama for one another and a range of audiences" and "use a range of techniques and performance skills to engage audiences." These statements explicitly position devising as central to secondary Drama education.

At KS3, devising typically progresses from:
- Spontaneous improvisation exploring characters and situations (Year 7)
- Structured devising using specific conventions and stimuli (Year 8)
- Sophisticated collaborative creation with conscious application of theatrical elements (Year 9)

### Key Stage 4

All GCSE Drama specifications include devising as a substantial assessed component:

**AQA GCSE Drama (8261)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) constitutes 40% of qualification. Students create and develop a devised piece from a choice of stimuli, maintaining a devising log (60 marks) and performing the devised piece (20 marks).

**Edexcel GCSE Drama (1DR0)**: Component 1 (Devising Drama) represents 40% of qualification. Students devise drama in response to a stimulus, documenting process in a portfolio and performing to an examiner.

**OCR GCSE Drama (J316)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) accounts for 30% of qualification, with students devising from a choice of stimuli and completing a written evaluation.

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Drama (C690)**: Component 2 (Devising Theatre) constitutes 40% of qualification, requiring devising from stimulus and documentary portfolio.

### Key Stage 5

A-Level specifications similarly prioritise devising:

**AQA A-Level Drama and Theatre (7262)**: Component 2 (Creating Original Drama) represents 30% of A-Level, requiring students to create and perform original work with a creative log.

**Edexcel A-Level Drama and Theatre (9DR0)**: Component 2 (Theatre Makers in Practice) includes devising as part of the practical examination.

**OCR A-Level Drama and Theatre (H459)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) constitutes 30% of qualification.

**WJEC Eduqas A-Level Drama and Theatre (A680)**: Component 2 (Text into Performance/Devising) offers devising as an option for 30% of qualification.

## The Devising Process: Pedagogical Frameworks

### Initial Stimulus and Exploration

Effective devising begins with carefully selected stimuli that offer sufficient richness for extended exploration. Stimuli categories include:

**Thematic stimuli**: Abstract concepts (justice, identity, displacement) that require students to find concrete dramatic expression

**Visual stimuli**: Photographs, paintings, objects that generate narrative and character possibilities

**Textual stimuli**: Poems, newspaper articles, historical documents that provide content and context

**Physical stimuli**: Movement sequences, soundscapes, locations that privilege embodied response

Teachers must consider stimulus accessibility whilst ensuring sufficient complexity. Neelands emphasises that effective stimuli contain inherent dramatic tension and multiple interpretative possibilities.

### Exploration and Experimentation Phase

This phase employs Neelands' drama conventions systematically:

**Still image/tableau work**: Students create frozen pictures representing themes, enabling composition and symbolic thinking

**Improvisation**: Spontaneous role-play develops character and situation organically

**Hot-seating**: Interrogating characters clarifies motivation and backstory

**Thought tracking**: Articulating internal monologue deepens psychological understanding

**Forum theatre**: Boal-inspired intervention strategies enable collective problem-solving

Kempe advocates for 'scaffolded spontaneity'—providing clear structures within which creative freedom operates. Teachers might set parameters (time limits, spatial restrictions, required elements) that focus rather than constrain exploration.

### Development and Structuring

Bolton's distinction between 'living through' drama and 'performing' drama becomes crucial here. Students must transition from exploratory improvisation to repeatable theatrical material. This involves:

**Selection**: Identifying strongest moments from exploration
**Sequencing**: Arranging material into coherent narrative or thematic structure
**Refinement**: Developing technical precision in execution
**Integration**: Synthesising performance elements (lighting, sound, design)

O'Neill's concept of 'dramatic tension' guides structuring decisions—material should create and resolve tensions dynamically. Teachers might introduce Freytag's pyramid, episodic structures, or non-linear approaches depending on sophistication required.

### Rehearsal and Performance

The final phase emphasises theatrical communication. Students develop:

**Technical precision**: Consistent characterisation, clear vocal/physical choices
**Ensemble awareness**: Responsive collaboration during performance
**Audience consciousness**: Adjusting performance dynamics to engage spectators
**Artistic cohesion**: Ensuring design and technical elements support dramatic intentions

## Assessment Frameworks and Success Criteria

### GCSE Assessment Objectives

Examination boards assess devising through specific criteria:

**AO2** (Apply theatrical skills): Students demonstrate performance skills in realising devised material
**AO3** (Demonstrate knowledge and understanding): Students show understanding of how theatrical elements create meaning
**AO4** (Analyse and evaluate): Students critically reflect on devising process and outcomes

Written documentation typically requires:
- Description of stimulus and initial response
- Explanation of development process with reference to theatrical conventions
- Analysis of performance skills employed
- Evaluation of effectiveness with specific examples
- Consideration of audience reception

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Effective devising pedagogy incorporates continuous assessment:

**Video documentation**: Recording exploratory phases enables metacognitive reflection
**Peer assessment**: Structured feedback protocols develop critical vocabulary
**Teacher conferencing**: Individual/group discussions guide development
**Reflective journals**: Ongoing documentation captures decision-making processes

Kempe emphasises that assessment should recognise different contributions within collaborative work—students might excel as performers, directors, designers, or dramaturgs.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Classroom Management and Group Dynamics

Successful devising requires careful attention to:

**Group formation**: Balancing self-selection with teacher intervention ensures productive combinations
**Role allocation**: Ensuring equitable participation through defined responsibilities (director, scribe, timekeeper)
**Studio protocols**: Establishing respectful watching conventions and constructive feedback cultures
**Time management**: Structured timelines with clear milestones prevent drift

Neelands advocates for rotating leadership within groups, developing distributed creative authority rather than hierarchical dynamics.

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Devising offers natural differentiation opportunities:

**Multiple intelligences**: Visual, kinaesthetic, linguistic, and interpersonal strengths find expression
**Varied roles**: Performance-anxious students can contribute through design, direction, or dramaturgy
**Scaffolding levels**: Teachers can provide more/less structure according to student confidence
**Cultural responsiveness**: Students bring own cultural references and experiences to material

Kempe's work on special educational needs demonstrates how devising can be more inclusive than script-based work, as material adapts to student capabilities rather than imposing external demands.

### Resource Implications

Effective devising requires:

**Flexible space**: Moveable furniture enabling spatial experimentation
**Documentation tools**: Cameras/tablets for recording development
**Stimulus resources**: Image banks, text collections, objects for inspiration
**Technical equipment**: Basic lighting/sound to explore design elements

Many successful devising projects operate with minimal resources—the creative process itself remains paramount.

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge: Lack of Progress Beyond Initial Ideas

**Solution**: Implement Neelands' convention sequences that deepen exploration. Use 'yes, and...' improvisation principles to build ideas. Set specific development tasks between sessions.

### Challenge: Dominance of Confident Students

**Solution**: Structure equal participation through timed contributions. Assign rotating leadership roles. Use paired/individual work before group synthesis. Value written/design contributions equally with performance.

### Challenge: Superficial Engagement with Stimulus

**Solution**: Employ O'Neill's pre-text strategies—extended exploration before making performance decisions. Research tasks that develop contextual understanding. Multiple convention approaches to same stimulus revealing different dimensions.

### Challenge: Inability to Shape Material for Performance

**Solution**: Introduce clear structural frameworks (three-part structure, montage, episodic). Model editing processes. Set length restrictions forcing selection. External eye (other groups, teacher) providing dramaturgical feedback.

### Challenge: Uneven Quality in Written Documentation

**Solution**: Provide clear exemplars at different levels. Break documentation into staged submissions. Teach specific analytical vocabulary. Use video evidence to support written reflection.

## Research Evidence and Contemporary Practice

Research by Nicholson (2005) in *Applied Drama: The Gift of Theatre* demonstrates that devising develops critical thinking and democratic participation beyond purely theatrical skills. Gallagher's *The Theatre of Urban: Youth and Schooling in Dangerous Times* (2007) evidences how devising enables marginalised students to voice experiences often absent from mainstream curricula.

Contemporary professional practice increasingly validates educational devising approaches. Companies like Frantic Assembly have developed specific methodologies (physical storytelling, 'building blocks' approach) that translate effectively into educational contexts. Scott Graham and Steven Hoggett's *The Frantic Assembly Book of Devising Theatre* (2014) provides accessible frameworks for teachers.

The rise of verbatim theatre, documentary theatre, and site-specific work in professional contexts provides authentic models for student devising. Practitioners like Alecky Blythe, Gillian Slovo, and Chris Thorpe demonstrate how devising responds to contemporary social realities.

## Connections to Wider Curriculum

Devising naturally supports cross-curricular learning:

**English**: Textual analysis when working with literary stimuli; narrative construction; creative writing
**History**: Historical devising projects developing period understanding and source evaluation
**PSHE/Citizenship**: Exploring social issues through dramatic form develops critical engagement
**Art & Design**: Visual stimulus work; design element integration
**Music**: Soundscape creation; rhythm and timing in physical theatre

Bolton's advocacy for drama's epistemological value suggests devising develops transferable thinking skills: symbolic representation, perspective-taking, collaborative problem-solving, and critical reflection.

## Conclusion

Devising drama represents both a pedagogical methodology and an artistic practice central to contemporary UK Drama education. The theoretical frameworks provided by Heathcote, Neelands, O'Neill, Bolton, and Kempe offer robust intellectual foundations, whilst examination board requirements ensure devising remains prominent across Key Stages 4 and 5.

Effective devising pedagogy balances creative freedom with structured exploration, individual expression with collaborative responsibility, and process with product. Teachers facilitating devising must develop sophisticated skills in stimulus selection, convention deployment, group management, and formative assessment.

As professional theatre practice continues evolving, devising in education prepares students not only for potential creative careers but also for active cultural citizenship—developing capacities to imagine alternatives, collaborate across differences, and communicate complex ideas through embodied artistic forms.
