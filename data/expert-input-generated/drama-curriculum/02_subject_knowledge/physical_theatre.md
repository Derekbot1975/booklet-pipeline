# Physical Theatre and Movement

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Physical theatre represents a fundamental strand of drama education in the UK, emphasising the primacy of the body as an expressive instrument. The National Curriculum for England (2013) explicitly requires students to develop physical performance skills, stating that pupils should "use a range of techniques and performance skills to engage audiences." Physical theatre and movement work underpins this requirement across all key stages, providing students with kinaesthetic approaches to creating and communicating meaning.

The approach aligns with Dorothy Heathcote's concept of "signing" – the physical manifestation of internal states and dramatic relationships through gesture, posture, and spatial positioning. Heathcote consistently emphasised that meaning in drama is made visible through the body, arguing that physical commitment precedes and enables emotional engagement.

## Historical and Theoretical Foundations

### European Influences

Physical theatre in UK education draws heavily from European practitioners including Jacques Lecoq, whose École Internationale de Théâtre in Paris has influenced generations of British practitioners. Lecoq's emphasis on mime, mask work, and movement analysis provides pedagogical frameworks widely adopted in UK drama teaching. Similarly, Jerzy Grotowski's "poor theatre" – stripping performance back to the actor's body and voice – has profoundly influenced how UK drama teachers approach physical training.

Frantic Assembly, founded in 1994 by Scott Graham and Steven Hoggett, has become particularly significant for UK secondary education, with their techniques now embedded in GCSE and A-Level specifications. Their accessible approach to creating physical sequences through "building blocks" and "choreographic devices" provides practical methodology that bridges professional practice and educational contexts.

### UK Educational Perspectives

Gavin Bolton, in "Acting in Classroom Drama" (1998), distinguished between "showing" and "living through" drama, recognising that physical theatre emphasises the former whilst maintaining dramatic authenticity. He argued that physical externalisation can paradoxically deepen internal understanding, as students discover meaning through bodily exploration rather than purely cognitive analysis.

Jonothan Neelands' conventions, documented extensively in "Structuring Drama Work" (2000, with Tony Goode), include numerous physically-focused techniques: still image, tableau, statue, and sculpting. These conventions provide scaffolded entry points into physical theatre, enabling teachers to introduce complex physical vocabulary progressively.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At KS3, physical theatre work establishes foundational vocabulary and confidence. The National Curriculum emphasises that students should "improvise and devise in different styles and genres," with physical theatre offering distinct stylistic approaches. Teaching should focus on:

**Body awareness and control**: Students develop proprioception, understanding how their bodies occupy and move through space. Exercises derived from Laban's Movement Analysis (Effort, Shape, Space, Body) provide systematic frameworks. Rudolf Laban's work, though originally developed for dance, has been extensively adapted for drama education, particularly his concepts of Weight, Time, Space, and Flow.

**Spatial relationships**: Students explore proxemics (Edward T. Hall's concept of personal space zones) through practical work, understanding how physical distance communicates relationships, status, and emotion. Neelands' "marking the moment" convention often employs physical stillness and positioning to crystallise dramatic significance.

**Physical vocabulary building**: Introduction to gesture, posture, levels, and body shapes as communicative tools. This includes distinguishing between naturalistic and stylised movement, enabling students to make informed aesthetic choices.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16) – GCSE

GCSE Drama specifications across all major exam boards explicitly assess physical performance skills:

**AQA Drama (8261)**: Component 1 (devising) and Component 2 (performance from text) both require "physical skills including movement, spatial awareness, and physical characterisation." The specification references practitioners including Frantic Assembly and DV8 Physical Theatre.

**Edexcel Drama (1DR0)**: Component 1 requires students to demonstrate "physical skills including control of the body, spatial awareness, and physical characterisation." The set texts often include physically demanding roles, and students must analyse professional productions, frequently including physical theatre companies.

**OCR Drama and Theatre (J316)**: The specification explicitly lists "physical skills" including "posture, gesture, spatial awareness, and proxemics" as assessment criteria. Section B of Component 01 may include questions on physical theatre practitioners.

**WJEC Eduqas Drama (C690U)**: Component 1 requires "clear demonstration of physical performance skills" with assessment criteria specifically addressing "control and fluency of movement."

Teaching at this level introduces students to professional practitioners systematically. Frantic Assembly's techniques – including "push hands," "round/by/through," and "chair duets" – feature prominently. Teachers should reference Scott Graham and Steven Hoggett's "The Frantic Assembly Book of Devising Theatre" (2014), which provides detailed breakdowns of their methodology.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18) – A-Level

A-Level specifications require sophisticated understanding of physical theatre as both practice and critical framework:

**AQA Drama and Theatre (7262)**: Component 1 requires creation of "an original performance piece" where physical theatre may be a chosen style. Component 3 (written examination) includes questions on practitioners, with Frantic Assembly and Complicité frequently examined. Students must analyse how "physical, vocal, and interpretative skills" communicate meaning.

**Edexcel Drama and Theatre (9DR0)**: Component 1 demands "application of theatrical skills" with physical theatre as a recognised approach. Component 3 includes analysis of theatrical style, where students may examine physical theatre companies including DV8, Kneehigh, or Gecko.

**OCR Drama and Theatre (H459)**: The specification requires students to "demonstrate complex physical skills" and analyse practitioners including Berkoff, whose use of physical ensemble work and stylisation provides rich analytical material.

**WJEC Eduqas Drama and Theatre (A690U)**: Component 2 requires performance demonstrating "sophisticated physical skills," whilst Component 3's written examination may include essay questions on physical theatre practitioners and their methodologies.

## Core Techniques and Methodologies

### Ensemble Physical Work

Cecily O'Neill, in "Drama Worlds" (1995), emphasised the collaborative nature of effective drama, where physical ensemble work builds trust and collective creativity. Ensemble techniques include:

**Mirroring and shadowing**: Building awareness of others' movement qualities, developing synchronicity and ensemble cohesion. These exercises derive from Lecoq's methodology but have been adapted extensively for educational contexts.

**Group tableaux**: Neelands' conventions of still image and tableau remain central pedagogical tools, enabling students to compose meaning physically. The development from still image to tableau vivant to animated sequence provides clear progression.

**Physical lifts and support**: Drawing from Frantic Assembly's work, students learn safe techniques for physical contact, weight-sharing, and dynamic partner work. Safety remains paramount, requiring clear teaching of spotting and controlled execution.

### Characterisation Through Physicality

Andy Kempe, in "The GCSE Drama Coursebook" (2008, with Marigold Ashwell), emphasises that physical choices precede and inform psychological characterisation. This aligns with Lecoq's concept of "mask work" – allowing the physical form to generate character rather than imposing predetermined psychology.

Techniques include:

**Animal studies**: Students research animal movements, adapting qualities to human characterisation. This approach, derived from Michael Chekhov's acting methodology, enables students to access heightened physical states whilst maintaining human authenticity.

**Status work**: Building on Keith Johnstone's status exercises from "Impro" (1979), students explore how status manifests physically through posture, gesture, eye contact, and spatial relationships.

**Laban's Efforts**: Applying Laban's eight basic efforts (Float, Punch, Glide, Slash, Dab, Wring, Flick, Press) to characterisation, providing students with specific movement vocabularies that communicate distinct psychological states.

### Stylisation and Non-Naturalism

Physical theatre enables exploration beyond naturalism, addressing the National Curriculum's requirement that students experience diverse theatrical styles. Steven Berkoff's work, particularly his adaptations of Kafka's "Metamorphosis" and "The Trial," demonstrates extreme physical stylisation that remains widely taught in UK schools.

Teaching approaches include:

**Symbolic gesture**: Reducing complex emotions or concepts to precise, repeatable gestures. This technique appears in Brechtian practice and Japanese Noh theatre, both frequently referenced in UK specifications.

**Chorus work**: Drawing from Greek theatre traditions, students explore unison movement, shared narration, and collective physical commentary. This connects to Brecht's Verfremdungseffekt (alienation effect), requiring analytical distance whilst maintaining physical precision.

**Physical soundscaping**: Creating environment and atmosphere through body percussion, vocal sounds, and rhythmic movement. This technique features prominently in Complicité's work, particularly in "The Street of Crocodiles" (1992).

## Assessment and Progression

### Practical Assessment Criteria

UK exam specifications consistently assess physical theatre skills through overlapping criteria:

**Control and precision**: Students demonstrate mastery over their physical instrument, executing movements cleanly and deliberately. This includes beginning and ending positions, transitions between states, and spatial accuracy.

**Communication of meaning**: Physical choices must serve dramatic intention, not existing as empty virtuosity. Examiners assess whether movement communicates character, narrative, or thematic content effectively.

**Spatial awareness**: Students demonstrate understanding of stage space, audience sightlines, and relationships with other performers. This includes use of levels, depth, and proxemics to create visual interest and meaning.

**Versatility and range**: Particularly at A-Level, students must demonstrate capacity to employ varied physical registers, from naturalism through to heightened stylisation.

### Written Analysis

GCSE and A-Level written components require students to analyse physical performance critically. Students must develop vocabulary for describing and evaluating movement, including:

- Spatial positioning and proxemics
- Quality of movement (Laban's framework)
- Use of gesture, both deictic (pointing/indicating) and symbolic
- Physical relationships between performers
- Use of levels, rhythm, and tempo
- Integration of movement with voice and other theatrical elements

Bolton's concept of "internal coherence" (1992) applies here – students must articulate how physical choices create unified dramatic meaning rather than existing as isolated effects.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Safe Practice

Physical theatre work requires rigorous attention to safety. The Association for Physical Education's "Safe Practice in Physical Education" (latest edition) provides frameworks applicable to drama teaching, including:

- Appropriate warm-up and cool-down routines
- Clear instruction for contact work, with explicit consent protocols
- Suitable clothing and footwear (bare feet where appropriate, secured hair)
- Risk assessment for ambitious physical sequences
- Progressive skill development before attempting complex techniques

### Inclusive Practice

Kempe and Nicholson, in "Learning to Teach Drama 11-18" (2007), emphasise that physical theatre should be accessible to all students regardless of physical ability. Adaptations include:

**Differentiated movement**: Offering varied complexity levels within the same task, enabling all students to engage meaningfully
**Alternative modes**: Allowing students to direct or design physical sequences rather than performing them
**Assistive technology**: Using video recording for students who cannot sustain extended physical work but can demonstrate understanding through analysis and planning

### Resource Recommendations

Essential resources for teaching physical theatre include:

- **"The Frantic Assembly Book of Devising Theatre"** (Graham & Hoggett, 2014): Practical exercises with clear pedagogical progression
- **"The GCSE Drama Coursebook"** (Kempe & Ashwell, 2008): Connects physical theatre to assessment requirements
- **"Drama Schemes for Secondary Schools"** (Kempe, 2012): Structured units incorporating physical work
- **Digital Theatre+**: Subscription service providing filmed performances of physical theatre companies, essential for Component 3 analysis at GCSE and A-Level

### Cross-Curricular Connections

Physical theatre enables valuable cross-curricular work:

**Physical Education**: Shared focus on body awareness, spatial understanding, and physical literacy
**Dance**: Overlapping movement vocabularies and compositional techniques
**English**: Physical interpretation of literary texts, embodying language
**Science**: Understanding of anatomy, biomechanics, and physiology applied to performance

## Contemporary Context and Future Directions

The prevalence of digital technology in students' lives makes physical theatre increasingly vital. Heathcote's emphasis on "bodily knowing" addresses the need for kinaesthetic learning experiences in screen-dominated contexts. Research by the National Literacy Trust (2019) indicates declining physical activity among young people, making drama's embodied approaches educationally significant beyond aesthetic considerations.

Contemporary companies including Gecko, Headlong, and Kneehigh continue innovating physical theatre forms, providing teachers with fresh stimulus material. The increased prominence of physical theatre in professional practice – with productions regularly transferring to West End and National Theatre stages – validates its curricular significance.

Neelands' recent work on "cultural democracy" in drama education (2009) argues that physical theatre's often abstract, symbolic language provides access points for students from diverse linguistic backgrounds, making meaning through universal bodily expression rather than text-dependent interpretation.

## Conclusion

Physical theatre and movement work represent essential components of comprehensive UK drama education, addressing curriculum requirements whilst developing students' expressive capacities. From Heathcote's foundational insights about physical "signing" through to contemporary practitioners' techniques, physical theatre provides pedagogical approaches that are simultaneously rigorous, accessible, and artistically valid. Teachers who develop students' physical vocabularies enable sophisticated dramatic communication that serves students across educational phases and into potential professional practice.
