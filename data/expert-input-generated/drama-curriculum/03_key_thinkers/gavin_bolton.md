# Gavin Bolton

**Full Name:** Gavin Bolton (1927–2016)

**Known For:** Pioneer of drama in education; theoretical frameworks linking drama to learning; influential educator at Durham University; key figure in developing process drama methodologies

**Focus Areas:** Drama in education, process drama, drama and understanding, educational theory, practical classroom drama

---

## Biography and Career History

Gavin Bolton was born in 1927 and became one of the most influential figures in British drama education during the second half of the 20th century. His career spanned more than five decades, during which he fundamentally shaped how drama came to be understood as a pedagogical tool rather than merely a performance art form within schools.

Bolton began his teaching career in the 1950s, working in secondary schools where he developed his early practical approaches to classroom drama. His formative years as a teacher coincided with the pioneering work of Peter Slade and Brian Way, though Bolton would go on to develop more rigorous theoretical frameworks than his predecessors.

In 1963, Bolton joined the Department of Education at Durham University, where he would spend the majority of his academic career until his retirement. At Durham, he worked alongside Dorothy Heathcote, though the two maintained quite different approaches to drama teaching. Whilst Heathcote focused on experiential, intuitive methods, Bolton concentrated on articulating the theoretical underpinnings of educational drama and developing frameworks that teachers could apply systematically.

Bolton's position at Durham placed him at the epicentre of British drama in education development. Durham became internationally recognised as a centre of excellence for drama education research and practice, attracting students and researchers from across the globe. Bolton supervised numerous postgraduate students who went on to become influential practitioners and academics themselves.

Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Bolton lectured extensively throughout the UK and internationally, particularly in North America and Australia. His visiting professorships and workshops helped disseminate British drama education approaches globally, establishing him as an international authority on the subject.

Bolton retired from Durham University in the early 1990s but remained active in the field through writing, consulting, and speaking engagements. He continued to refine his theoretical positions and engage critically with developments in drama education until shortly before his death in 2016.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Drama as a Learning Medium

Bolton's central contribution was his articulation of drama as a distinct learning medium rather than merely preparation for theatre performance. In his seminal work *Towards a Theory of Drama in Education* (1979), Bolton argued that drama's educational value lay not in producing theatrical performances but in its capacity to generate understanding through the dramatic process itself.

Bolton distinguished between 'making theatre' and 'making meaning'. He proposed that when pupils engage in dramatic activity with educational intent, they are not primarily concerned with creating something to show an audience, but rather with exploring ideas, situations, and human behaviour from the inside. This fundamental distinction helped legitimise drama as a curriculum subject with its own pedagogical rationale.

He developed the concept of the 'dramatic playing/theatre' continuum, suggesting that all dramatic activity exists somewhere along a spectrum between private exploratory drama (dramatic playing) and public performance (theatre). Educational drama, he argued, typically occupies the 'dramatic playing' end of this continuum, where the focus is on the participants' experience and learning rather than on audience reception.

### 2. Types of Drama

Bolton identified and categorised different types of dramatic activity used in education, providing teachers with a taxonomy that helped clarify purposes and approaches. His most influential categorisation appeared in *Drama as Education* (1984) and distinguished between:

**Exercise:** Activities focusing on developing specific skills (voice, movement, concentration) without necessarily creating meaning. Bolton was somewhat ambivalent about pure exercises, questioning whether they constituted 'drama' in the fullest sense.

**Dramatic Playing:** Spontaneous, exploratory drama where participants work in role to investigate situations and ideas. This type emphasises process over product and immediate experience over reflection.

**Theatre:** Activity intended for an audience, where aesthetic considerations and communicative effectiveness become paramount.

**Making Meanings Drama:** Bolton's preferred term for educational drama that combines action with reflection, allowing participants to generate and test understandings about situations, behaviour, and concepts. This type integrates the immediacy of dramatic playing with opportunities for critical examination of what is being explored.

### 3. The Concept of 'Element of Surprise'

Bolton developed important insights into how dramatic tension operates in educational contexts. He argued that effective educational drama requires what he termed the 'element of surprise' – moments where participants encounter something unexpected that challenges their assumptions and provokes genuine inquiry.

This concept differentiated his approach from more scripted or predetermined drama activities. Bolton advocated for structures that allowed for emergent meanings, where neither teacher nor pupils could entirely predict what understandings would develop through the dramatic exploration. The teacher's role became one of creating frameworks flexible enough to accommodate surprise whilst maintaining sufficient structure to ensure educational focus.

### 4. Understanding and Acting

A cornerstone of Bolton's theoretical framework was his detailed analysis of the relationship between understanding and acting. In numerous publications, particularly *Acting in Classroom Drama* (1998), Bolton explored how the act of adopting a role and engaging in dramatic action differs from everyday behaviour and from theatrical acting.

Bolton identified that in educational drama, participants simultaneously experience two levels of engagement: they are 'in' the drama (living through the situation as their character) and 'out' of the drama (aware of themselves as pupils in a classroom exploring ideas). This dual consciousness, which Bolton termed 'metaxis' (drawing on Augusto Boal's terminology), creates the conditions for learning. Pupils can experience situations from the inside whilst maintaining sufficient critical distance to reflect upon those experiences.

He distinguished between 'personification' (showing what a character might do) and 'identification' (experiencing as that character), arguing that effective educational drama often requires movement between these modes. Bolton was critical of approaches that demanded full psychological realism from pupils, instead advocating for a more symbolic or representational quality of acting appropriate to educational contexts.

### 5. The Teacher's Role: 'Teacher-in-Role'

Bolton made significant contributions to theorising the teacher's role in educational drama, particularly the technique of 'teacher-in-role'. Whilst Dorothy Heathcote pioneered the practical use of this technique, Bolton provided much of the theoretical analysis that helped teachers understand how and why it works.

Bolton identified several functions of teacher-in-role:

- **Controlling from within:** The teacher can guide the drama's direction without breaking the dramatic frame
- **Modelling dramatic behaviour:** Demonstrating appropriate levels of commitment and theatrical convention
- **Challenging pupils' thinking:** Introducing information, questions, or obstacles that deepen inquiry
- **Protecting pupils:** The teacher can modify the drama's emotional intensity or redirect focus if needed
- **Elevating the register:** Raising the quality of language, thought, or engagement through example

Bolton emphasised that teacher-in-role is not about theatrical performance by the teacher but about strategic intervention to enhance learning opportunities. The teacher adopts role functionally, using it as a teaching tool rather than an end in itself.

### 6. The Three Dimensions of Dramatic Activity

Bolton proposed that dramatic activity operates simultaneously across three dimensions, and effective educational drama requires attention to all three:

**Existential Dimension:** The immediate, lived experience of being in the drama – the 'now' of the dramatic moment. This is where emotional engagement and imaginative commitment occur.

**Aesthetic Dimension:** The awareness of form, symbol, and meaning-making. Participants recognise they are creating something with shape and significance.

**Social Dimension:** The collaborative, communal nature of drama-making. Learning occurs through negotiation, shared decision-making, and collective meaning construction.

Bolton argued that teachers must consciously attend to all three dimensions when planning and facilitating drama. Neglecting any dimension diminishes the educational potential of the work.

### 7. Drama and Conceptual Understanding

Throughout his later career, particularly in *New Perspectives on Classroom Drama* (1992), Bolton focused increasingly on drama's capacity to develop conceptual understanding across the curriculum. He argued that drama could help pupils grasp abstract concepts by embodying them in concrete dramatic situations.

Bolton distinguished between 'information' and 'understanding', arguing that traditional teaching often focuses on the former whilst drama uniquely addresses the latter. When pupils enact situations involving, for example, justice, loyalty, or responsibility, they don't merely learn definitions but develop felt understandings of these concepts through lived experience.

He developed frameworks for 'concept-focused drama', where teachers deliberately structure dramatic explorations around key concepts drawn from other curriculum areas – history, literature, citizenship, or science. This work significantly influenced cross-curricular approaches to drama teaching.

### 8. Reflection and 'Living Through'

Bolton made crucial distinctions between different forms of engagement in drama, particularly between 'living through' drama and reflecting upon it. He argued that both modes were essential to educational drama but served different functions.

'Living through' refers to the immediate experience of being in the drama, engaging with situations as they unfold. This mode generates experiential knowledge – what it feels like to face particular dilemmas or inhabit particular perspectives.

Reflection, whether during or after the drama, allows participants to examine what they have experienced, make connections, and construct more explicit understandings. Bolton advocated for various reflective strategies including 'freeze-frame' analysis, role-distanced discussion, and writing in role.

His later work emphasised that the balance between living through and reflection should be carefully managed. Too much immediate experience without reflection might be emotionally engaging but educationally shallow; too much reflection without sufficient lived experience might be intellectually sterile.

## Key Publications

### *Towards a Theory of Drama in Education* (1979)

Bolton's first major theoretical work, published by Longman, represented a landmark in drama education literature. Prior to this publication, much writing about educational drama remained descriptive or practitioner-focused. Bolton attempted to construct a comprehensive theoretical framework explaining why and how drama functions as an educational medium.

The book examined the nature of dramatic activity, the relationship between drama and play, and the cognitive processes involved in dramatic engagement. Bolton drew on educational theory, psychology, and theatre studies to build his arguments. The work introduced many concepts that became foundational to subsequent drama education discourse, including his categorisation of drama types and his analysis of the dramatic/theatre continuum.

This publication established Bolton's reputation as the foremost theorist of drama in education and became essential reading for drama teachers and trainers. It remained in print for decades and influenced curriculum development throughout the UK and internationally.

### *Drama as Education* (1984)

Published by Longman, this book represented Bolton's most comprehensive statement of his philosophy and methodology. It synthesised his practical experience with his theoretical insights, offering both conceptual frameworks and practical guidance for teachers.

The book examined drama's relationship to the wider curriculum, its assessment, and its place within different educational philosophies. Bolton engaged critically with progressive education movements, questioning some assumptions whilst defending drama's educational legitimacy. Chapters covered planning drama lessons, working in role, developing dramatic tension, and integrating drama with other subjects.

*Drama as Education* became perhaps Bolton's most widely read and influential work, used extensively in teacher training programmes across the UK. Its clear structure and accessible style made complex ideas comprehensible to practising teachers whilst maintaining theoretical rigour.

### *New Perspectives on Classroom Drama* (1992)

Published by Simon & Schuster Education, this work represented Bolton's mature thinking, incorporating developments in educational theory and responding to changes in the educational landscape of the 1980s and early 1990s.

The book placed greater emphasis on drama's potential for conceptual learning and cross-curricular application. Bolton engaged with constructivist learning theory, showing how drama exemplifies constructivist principles. He also addressed assessment and accountability concerns that had become increasingly prominent in British education following the 1988 Education Reform Act.

Significantly, this publication showed Bolton's willingness to revise his earlier positions. He acknowledged areas where his thinking had evolved and engaged with criticisms of his previous work. This intellectual honesty enhanced his credibility amongst academics whilst demonstrating to practitioners that theory should remain responsive to practice.

### *Acting in Classroom Drama: A Critical Analysis* (1998)

Published by Trentham Books, this represented Bolton's most sustained examination of a single aspect of educational drama. The book provided a detailed phenomenological analysis of what happens when pupils 'act' in classroom drama, distinguishing this from theatrical acting and from everyday behaviour.

Bolton examined different modes of dramatic engagement – demonstration, personification, identification – and their respective educational affordances. He explored how pupils' self-consciousness affects their dramatic work and how teachers can create conditions that allow pupils to engage authentically without demanding unrealistic levels of theatrical skill.

The book drew on observation of classroom practice, including analysis of video recordings, to ground theoretical claims in empirical evidence. It represented Bolton's most philosophically sophisticated work, engaging with phenomenology and semiotics to illuminate the nature of dramatic experience.

### *Drama as Learning Medium* (1980, in *Drama in Education* Vol 1 with Dorothy Heathcote)

Published by Longman, this work paired Bolton's theoretical writing with Heathcote's more practice-focused accounts. Bolton contributed chapters that analysed drama's educational functions and provided conceptual frameworks for understanding its effects.

The pairing was significant, showing how Bolton's analytical approach complemented Heathcote's intuitive, experiential methodology. Bolton's contributions helped make Heathcote's innovative but sometimes opaque practice more accessible to teachers by explaining the principles underlying her techniques.

### Articles and Chapters

Beyond his books, Bolton contributed numerous influential articles to journals including *Drama Contact*, *2D* (later *Drama Research*), and *Research in Drama Education*. Significant articles included:

- 'Drama in Education: Learning Medium or Arts Process?' (1982) – exploring tensions between drama as pedagogy and drama as arts education
- 'Drama as Art' (1989) – revisiting his earlier positions on aesthetics in educational drama
- 'Changes in Thinking about Drama in Education' (1993) – reflecting on developments in the field

His chapter contributions to edited collections helped shape drama education discourse, particularly his pieces in *Hornbrook's collection *Drama/Theatre/Performance* (1980) and *The Cambridge Guide to Theatre* (1988).

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Bolton's theoretical work significantly influenced how drama was conceptualised during the development of England's National Curriculum following the 1988 Education Reform Act. Although drama did not secure status as a separate foundation subject – a disappointment to many in the field – Bolton's frameworks for understanding drama's educational purposes informed its inclusion within English.

His articulation of drama's contribution to speaking and listening, literary understanding, and language development provided arguments that curriculum developers drew upon. Bolton served as consultant to various curriculum development groups and his publications were cited in official guidance documents.

The National Curriculum's Programme of Study for English incorporated drama activities, and the language used – particularly around 'improvisation', 'working in role', and 'exploring situations' – reflected concepts Bolton had developed and popularised.

### GCSE and A-Level Drama

Bolton's distinction between process and product, and his frameworks for understanding different types of dramatic activity, influenced the design of GCSE Drama specifications from their introduction in the late 1980s. His work helped establish the rationale for assessing both performance and written examination components, reflecting understanding of drama as both practical activity and intellectual study.

Examination boards drew on Bolton's categorisations when designing assessment criteria. His emphasis on drama as meaning-making, not merely performance skills, helped shape mark schemes that rewarded conceptual understanding alongside theatrical competence.

At A-Level, Bolton's writings on dramatic theory provided content for specification requirements that students study drama as an academic discipline. His books became set texts on several specifications, ensuring his ideas directly reached advanced-level students.

### Teacher Training Standards

Bolton's frameworks became embedded in teacher training programmes across UK universities. The competencies required of newly qualified teachers included many elements reflecting Bolton's pedagogical principles: ability to use drama across the curriculum, understanding of teacher-in-role, capacity to structure dramatic exploration of concepts.

His conceptual vocabulary – 'living through drama', 'teacher-in-role', 'dramatic playing' – became standard terminology in teacher training, creating shared professional language amongst drama educators. This linguistic legacy continues to shape how drama teachers think about and discuss their practice.

### Policy Documents and Reports

Bolton contributed to various policy documents and reports on arts education. His evidence to governmental and advisory bodies helped maintain drama's profile during periods when arts subjects faced marginalisation. His articulate defence of drama's educational value, grounded in theoretical rigour rather than mere assertion, gave weight to advocacy efforts by drama education organisations.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Planning Principles

Bolton advocated structured improvisation as the core methodology of educational drama. Unlike free improvisation, structured improvisation involves the teacher creating a framework – including roles, situation, constraints, and focus – within which pupils improvise responses.

His planning approach emphasised:

**Clear learning intentions:** Teachers should identify what understanding or concepts the drama will explore, not merely what activities will occur.

**Pre-text:** Following Cecily O'Neill's development of this concept, Bolton endorsed beginning drama from a rich stimulus – an object, image, or piece of text – that implies a dramatic situation without fully determining it.

**Building belief:** Gradually establishing the fictional world through accumulation of detail, commitment, and consistency rather than expecting immediate 'suspension of disbelief'.

**Tension structure:** Deliberately incorporating dramatic tension through obstacles, dilemmas, or mysteries that require resolution, driving the drama forward and maintaining engagement.

**Opportunities for reflection:** Building in moments where participants step back from immediate action to consider implications, make decisions, or examine what they're discovering.
