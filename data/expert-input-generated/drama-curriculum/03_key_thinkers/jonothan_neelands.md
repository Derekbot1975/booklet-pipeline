# Jonothan Neelands

**Full Name:** Professor Jonothan Neelands

**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Creative Education, University of Warwick (retired from full-time position in 2017)

**Known For:** Developing the 'conventions approach' to drama teaching; structuring approaches to drama pedagogy; ensemble practice; research into creative learning; long-standing influence on drama curriculum development in the UK and internationally

---

## Biography and Career History

### Early Career and Academic Development

Jonothan Neelands began his career as a secondary school drama teacher in the 1970s, teaching in comprehensive schools where he developed his practical understanding of drama as a pedagogical tool. This classroom experience fundamentally shaped his later theoretical work, ensuring his academic contributions remained rooted in the realities of teaching practice.

In 1984, Neelands joined the University of Warwick, initially as a lecturer in the Institute of Education. He would remain at Warwick for over three decades, establishing the institution as a leading centre for drama education research in the UK. He was promoted to Professor and served as Head of the Institute of Education and later as Chair of Creative Education.

### Institutional Leadership

At Warwick, Neelands established and directed several significant research initiatives. He founded the National Drama research and development centre, which became instrumental in supporting drama teachers and advancing pedagogical understanding. He also led the Arts, Education and Cultural Policy research group, broadening his influence beyond drama into wider arts education policy.

Neelands served as President of the International Drama in Education Research Institute (IDEA) and worked extensively with professional theatre organisations, including the Royal Shakespeare Company (RSC), developing their education programmes and research partnerships.

### International Influence

Throughout his career, Neelands has worked internationally, lecturing and consulting in over 30 countries. His conventions approach has been adopted in drama curricula across Europe, Asia, Australia, and North America. He has held visiting professorships at universities in Australia, Taiwan, and Hong Kong.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The Conventions Approach

Neelands' most significant contribution to drama education is his systematic categorisation and documentation of drama conventions. First published in his seminal work **"Structuring Drama Work" (1990)**, co-authored with Tony Goode, this framework identifies specific techniques that teachers can employ to structure drama learning.

The conventions approach provides teachers with a 'toolkit' of approximately 70 distinct drama techniques, organised by their pedagogical function. These include conventions such as:

- **Teacher-in-role:** The teacher adopts a role within the drama to guide, challenge, or provide information
- **Hot-seating:** A character is questioned by others in the group
- **Forum theatre:** Based on Boal's work, allowing audiences to intervene and change outcomes
- **Conscience alley:** Creating a physical corridor through which a character walks, hearing conflicting advice
- **Still image/tableau:** Creating frozen pictures to represent moments, themes, or ideas
- **Mantle of the expert:** Dorothy Heathcote's approach where participants adopt expert roles

Neelands argued that understanding these conventions enables teachers to move beyond improvisational chaos toward purposeful, structured learning experiences. The conventions are not presented as a rigid methodology but as flexible tools that can be combined and adapted according to learning objectives.

### 2. Drama Structures and Planning

Building on the conventions framework, Neelands developed approaches to **structuring whole drama lessons and schemes of work**. In **"Structuring Drama Work" (2000, second edition)** and **"Making Sense of Drama" (1984)**, he articulated how conventions could be sequenced to create coherent learning journeys.

Neelands identified several structural approaches:

- **Linear structures:** Drama that follows a chronological narrative
- **Episodic structures:** Using multiple scenes or moments to explore a theme
- **Narrative structures:** Building dramatic tension through story
- **Thematic structures:** Organising drama around ideas rather than plot

He emphasised the importance of **pre-text** (the starting point or initial stimulus) and **context** (the fictional situation established for the drama). This structural thinking helped teachers move beyond simply 'doing activities' toward creating meaningful dramatic experiences with clear learning outcomes.

### 3. Ensemble Practice and Collaborative Learning

Neelands has consistently championed **ensemble** as both a pedagogical method and an educational outcome. Drawing from professional theatre practice, particularly his work with the RSC, he articulated how drama education should develop collaborative, democratic learning communities.

His conception of ensemble includes:

- **Collective creation:** Students working together to devise and shape dramatic material
- **Shared responsibility:** All participants contributing to the success of the work
- **Democratic processes:** Valuing all voices and ideas in the creative process
- **Risk-taking in a supportive environment:** Creating safe spaces for experimentation

In **"Learning Through Imagined Experience" (2004)**, co-authored with Tony Goode, Neelands explored how ensemble practice develops social capital, empathy, and democratic capabilities. He argued that drama's collaborative nature makes it uniquely positioned to develop 21st-century competencies.

### 4. Drama as a Learning Medium

Neelands has consistently advocated for drama not merely as an art form to be studied but as a **medium for learning across the curriculum**. This positions drama as having both intrinsic value (learning in drama) and instrumental value (learning through drama).

His research demonstrated how drama methodologies could:

- Develop literacy and oracy
- Explore historical events and ethical dilemmas
- Understand social and political issues
- Develop emotional intelligence and empathy
- Engage reluctant learners

This dual conception influenced curriculum development, particularly in the 2000s when drama was positioned within the National Curriculum as both an art form and a teaching methodology.

### 5. Critical Pedagogy and Social Justice

Influenced by Brazilian theatre practitioner Augusto Boal and British drama educator Dorothy Heathcote, Neelands' work incorporates **critical pedagogical perspectives**. He views drama education as potentially transformative and political, enabling young people to question, challenge, and imagine alternative social arrangements.

In **"Beginning Drama 11-14" (2000)** and later works, he articulated how drama can:

- Give voice to marginalised perspectives
- Explore power relationships
- Develop critical consciousness
- Enable active citizenship

This critical dimension distinguishes his work from more purely aesthetic or skills-based approaches to drama education.

### 6. Dramatic Playing and the 'Make-Believe' Frame

Neelands developed theoretical understanding of how students engage with fictional contexts in educational drama. Drawing on play theory and phenomenology, he explored how participants simultaneously exist **in and out of the drama** – what he terms the 'dual affect' of dramatic playing.

This theoretical work explained why drama can be such a powerful learning medium: participants can explore challenging situations, emotions, and ideas whilst protected by the 'as if' frame of fiction. The metaxis (Boal's term) or 'both and' quality of drama allows for experimentation without real-world consequences.

### 7. Assessment in Drama

Neelands contributed significantly to understanding how drama learning might be assessed, particularly during the development of GCSE and A-level drama specifications. He argued against overly reductive assessment frameworks that might undermine drama's holistic and collaborative nature.

His approach to assessment emphasised:

- **Process as well as product:** Valuing the learning journey, not just final performances
- **Collaborative achievement:** Assessing group work appropriately
- **Multiple forms of evidence:** Including journals, discussions, and self-assessment
- **Formative assessment:** Assessment for learning rather than solely of learning

### 8. The 'Warwick Approach'

Though not formally codified as such, Neelands' work at Warwick established what might be considered a distinctive philosophical and pedagogical orientation, characterised by:

- Integration of theory and practice
- Research-informed teaching
- Partnership with professional theatre
- International perspective
- Commitment to social justice
- Evidence-based advocacy for drama education

This approach influenced generations of drama teachers trained at Warwick and shaped drama education discourse more broadly.

## Key Publications

### Books

**"Making Sense of Drama: A Guide to Classroom Practice" (1984)** – Heinemann  
One of Neelands' earliest books, establishing his reputation as a practical theorist. Provided accessible guidance for classroom teachers on structuring drama work with clear learning intentions.

**"Structuring Drama Work: A Handbook of Available Forms in Theatre and Drama" (1990, revised 2000)** – Co-authored with Tony Goode, Cambridge University Press  
His most influential publication. This handbook catalogues and explains drama conventions with practical examples, theoretical rationale, and suggestions for use. The 2000 revised edition updated examples and added new conventions. This book has been translated into multiple languages and remains essential reading for drama teachers globally.

**"Beginning Drama 11-14" (2000)** – Co-authored with Tony Goode, David Fulton Publishers  
A practical guide specifically for Key Stage 3 drama teaching, providing schemes of work, lesson structures, and pedagogical guidance grounded in the conventions approach.

**"Learning Through Imagined Experience" (2004)** – Co-authored with Tony Goode, David Fulton Publishers  
A more theoretical exploration of how imaginative engagement in drama creates learning opportunities, drawing on cognitive psychology, play theory, and educational philosophy.

**"Drama and Theatre Studies at AS and A Level" (2004)** – Editor and contributor, Routledge  
A comprehensive textbook addressing the theoretical and practical demands of post-16 drama study in England and Wales.

**"The Cambridge History of British Theatre, Volume 3: Since 1895" (2004)** – Contributor  
Neelands contributed chapters on educational drama and TIE (Theatre in Education), positioning drama education within broader theatre history.

**"Enriching the Learning: Drama for Literacy and Numeracy" (2002)** – Co-authored with Penny Bunyan and William Fletcher  
Demonstrated practical applications of drama across the primary curriculum, particularly supporting the National Literacy and Numeracy Strategies.

### Academic Articles and Chapters

**"Miracles are happening: Beyond the rhetoric of transformation in the Western traditions of drama education" (2004)** – Research in Drama Education  
A critical examination of claims made for drama's transformative potential, arguing for more nuanced understanding of drama's effects.

**"The Transformative Capacities of Drama Education: A Cultural Ecology" (2010)** – Co-authored with Jenny Klein, Warwick Institute of Education  
Research exploring how participation in drama develops creative capacities and cultural understanding.

**"Acting together: ensemble as a democratic process in art and life" (2009)** – Research in Drama Education  
Theoretical and empirical exploration of ensemble practice as democratic education.

**"Taming the Political: The Struggle Over Recognition in the Politics of Applied Theatre" (2007)** – Research in Drama Education  
Critical analysis of how applied theatre and drama education negotiate political dimensions.

### Reports and Policy Documents

**"Creative Partnerships: Changing Young Lives" (2006)** – Arts Council England  
Neelands led research evaluating Creative Partnerships, the government's flagship creative learning programme, providing evidence for arts education's impact.

**"The Art of the Matter: Why Drama Education Is Essential" (2009)** – Report for Warwick Commission  
Advocacy document articulating the case for drama education's place in the curriculum.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Impact

Neelands' work significantly influenced drama's position within the **English National Curriculum** established in 1988 and its subsequent revisions. His structured approach to drama teaching provided practical frameworks that supported drama's inclusion within English (Speaking and Listening) and as a distinct subject within the Arts.

During the 1990s and 2000s, when drama's curricular status was frequently threatened, Neelands was a prominent advocate, providing evidence-based arguments for drama's educational value. His research was cited in government reviews and consultations.

### GCSE and A-level Development

Neelands contributed to the development of **GCSE Drama** (introduced 1986) and **A-level Theatre Studies** specifications, particularly through his work with examination boards. His emphasis on both practical drama-making and critical reflection influenced assessment structures that valued process alongside performance.

His publications, particularly **"Drama and Theatre Studies at AS and A Level"**, became essential resources for teachers navigating qualification requirements.

### Teacher Training and Professional Development

The conventions approach became standard content in **Initial Teacher Training (ITT)** programmes across the UK. Universities offering PGCE Drama courses invariably include Neelands' frameworks, and his books appear on virtually every drama education reading list.

Neelands also contributed to continuing professional development (CPD) through:

- National Drama conferences and workshops
- Regional training programmes
- Online resources and videos demonstrating conventions
- Partnership programmes with schools

### Arts Council England and Cultural Policy

Neelands' research informed **Arts Council England** policy, particularly regarding arts education and cultural learning. His evaluation work on Creative Partnerships (2006) provided evidence that influenced continued investment in arts education partnerships between schools and cultural organisations.

His advocacy contributed to the establishment of Cultural Education Challenge (2013) and Artsmark programmes, which recognise schools' commitment to arts education.

### Royal Shakespeare Company Partnership

Neelands' long-standing collaboration with the **RSC** influenced their education strategy. The RSC's adoption of ensemble methodology and active Shakespeare approaches reflects his pedagogical principles. The RSC's "Stand Up for Shakespeare" manifesto (2008), which Neelands contributed to, argued for practical, ensemble-based approaches to teaching Shakespeare in schools.

### International Curriculum Influence

Neelands' frameworks have been adopted internationally:

- **Australia:** The conventions approach is embedded in state drama curricula
- **New Zealand:** Drama syllabus explicitly references Neelands' structures
- **Hong Kong:** Drama education initiatives draw on his pedagogical frameworks
- **Nordic countries:** His work influenced process drama approaches in Scandinavia

## Pedagogical Approach

### Theoretical Foundations

Neelands' pedagogy synthesises several theoretical traditions:

1. **Social constructivism:** Learning as socially constructed through collaborative activity
2. **Experiential learning:** Kolb's learning cycle adapted for dramatic exploration
3. **Critical pedagogy:** Freire and Boal's emphasis on consciousness-raising and transformation
4. **Play theory:** Understanding drama as structured, purposeful play

### Teaching Principles

Neelands advocates for drama teaching characterised by:

**1. Structured exploration rather than free expression**  
Drama lessons should have clear structures using conventions purposefully selected for learning objectives, not merely opportunities for improvised play.

**2. Protection into understanding**  
The fictional frame of drama protects participants whilst enabling exploration of challenging themes, emotions, and situations.

**3. Teacher as artist and facilitator**  
The drama teacher orchestrates learning through careful selection of conventions, intervention through teacher-in-role, and artful shaping of dramatic material.

**4. Commitment to process**  
Whilst product (performance) may be an outcome, the learning primarily occurs in the process of making drama together.

**5. Collective meaning-making**  
Drama creates opportunities for groups to negotiate understanding together, developing shared interpretations and insights.

**6. Aesthetic engagement**  
Drama education should develop aesthetic awareness and appreciation of theatrical form, not merely use drama instrumentally.

### Typical Lesson Structure

Whilst Neelands emphasises flexibility, his approach typically includes:

1. **Pre-text introduction:** Establishing the stimulus and initial context
2. **Establishing the dramatic world:** Using conventions to build the fictional situation
3. **Deepening engagement:** Employing conventions that increase tension or complexity
4. **Reflection in the drama:** Opportunities to step back and consider (e.g., thought-tracking)
5. **Reflection on the drama:** Discussion, writing, or other activities processing learning

### Role of the Teacher

Neelands articulates a sophisticated conception of the teacher's role:

- **Playwright:** Structuring the drama experience
- **Director:** Shaping and refining dramatic work
- **Fellow artist:** Sometimes participating within the drama
- **Facilitator:** Enabling student creativity and voice
- **Critical friend:** Questioning and challenging to deepen thinking

This multi-faceted role requires considerable expertise, contradicting assumptions that drama teaching is simply about 'letting children play'.

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

### Enduring Influence

More than three decades after his major publications, Neelands' frameworks remain foundational to drama education in the UK. The conventions approach continues to be taught in teacher training, and his books remain in print and widely used.

### Critiques and Developments

Some contemporary practitioners have questioned whether the conventions approach:

- Might become formulaic if applied without artistry
- Adequately addresses diversity and decolonisation in drama curricula
- Sufficiently engages with digital and multimedia forms

However, Neelands himself has acknowledged these concerns, arguing that conventions should be starting points for teacher creativity, not prescriptive formulae.

### Recent Work and Retirement

Since retiring from full-time academic work in 2017, Neelands has continued to:

- Present at international conferences
- Contribute to policy discussions
- Support research projects
- Mentor early-career researchers

His legacy is maintained by former students and colleagues who continue developing his ideas in contemporary contexts.
