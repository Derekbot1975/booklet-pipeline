# Andy Kempe

**Full Name:** Andy Kempe

**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Drama Education, University of Reading

**Known For:** Pioneering work in applied drama, drama pedagogy, process drama methodology, and the integration of drama across the curriculum; significant contributions to drama teacher education and curriculum development in the UK

---

## Biography and Career History

Andy Kempe has been one of the most influential figures in UK drama education for over four decades. His career has encompassed teaching in secondary schools, teacher education, and academic research, making him uniquely positioned to bridge theory and practice in drama pedagogy.

Kempe began his career as a secondary school drama teacher before moving into higher education. He spent a significant portion of his academic career at the University of Reading, where he served as Professor of Drama Education and led the development of drama teacher education programmes. At Reading, he established the university as a major centre for drama education research and practice, supervising numerous doctoral students who have themselves become significant voices in the field.

Throughout his career, Kempe has maintained strong connections with schools and professional theatre, consistently advocating for drama as both an art form in its own right and as a powerful pedagogical tool across the curriculum. His work has been characterised by a commitment to making drama education accessible, rigorous, and relevant to contemporary educational contexts.

He has served on numerous national bodies and advisory committees related to drama education, including involvement with the National Association for the Teaching of Drama (NATD) and consultation work for examination boards. His influence extends beyond the UK through international publications, conference presentations, and collaborative research projects.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Process Drama as Pedagogical Practice

Kempe has been instrumental in developing and disseminating understanding of process drama methodology in the UK context. Building on the work of Dorothy Heathcote and Cecily O'Neill, Kempe has articulated how process drama operates as an extended, improvised dramatic exploration of a particular topic or theme, undertaken by participants working in role.

In his work, Kempe emphasises that process drama is distinct from conventional drama activities because it prioritises the collective creation of meaning over the production of a polished performance. He identifies key features: the absence of a predetermined script, the teacher working in role alongside students, the development of a dramatic world over extended time periods, and the use of pre-text (a stimulus that initiates the drama).

Kempe's contribution has been to make process drama methodology more accessible to mainstream classroom teachers, particularly those who may lack specialist drama training. He provides clear frameworks for structuring process drama lessons whilst maintaining the necessary flexibility and responsiveness that characterise effective practice.

### 2. Drama Across the Curriculum

One of Kempe's most significant contributions has been his sustained argument for drama as a cross-curricular learning medium. Rather than viewing drama solely as a discrete subject or art form, Kempe has consistently demonstrated how dramatic approaches can enhance learning in subjects ranging from history and English to science and mathematics.

In his various publications, Kempe provides detailed examples of how teachers can use drama conventions and strategies to explore complex concepts, develop empathy and perspective-taking, and enable students to engage with abstract ideas through embodied experience. He argues that drama's power lies in its ability to create hypothetical contexts in which students can explore 'what if' questions safely and creatively.

Kempe distinguishes between 'drama for learning' and 'learning about drama', arguing that both are valuable but serve different purposes. His work has helped to legitimise the use of drama as a learning medium across the curriculum whilst maintaining respect for drama as an artistic discipline requiring specialist knowledge and skills.

### 3. The Teacher in Role

Kempe has written extensively about the technique of 'teacher in role' (TIR), which he considers one of the most powerful but challenging strategies available to drama educators. In TIR, the teacher adopts a role within the dramatic fiction, working alongside students to build belief and deepen engagement.

Kempe identifies different types of teacher roles, ranging from high-status positions (such as authority figures who create challenge and tension) to low-status roles (which invite students to take leadership and make decisions). He emphasises that the teacher must maintain a 'dual consciousness', simultaneously existing within the dramatic fiction whilst monitoring and guiding the educational process.

His work provides practical guidance on managing the transitions into and out of role, maintaining appropriate dramatic tension, and using TIR to scaffold learning without dominating the creative space. Kempe argues that effective TIR requires careful planning, theatrical skill, and sensitivity to group dynamics.

### 4. Assessment in Drama

Kempe has made significant contributions to debates about assessment in drama education. He argues against crude application of assessment frameworks that fail to recognise the distinctive nature of dramatic learning and creative processes. His work emphasises the importance of assessment that is both rigorous and appropriate to the subject.

In his publications on assessment, Kempe advocates for approaches that value process alongside product, recognise collaborative achievement as well as individual contribution, and acknowledge the affective and social dimensions of drama work. He has developed frameworks for formative assessment that support learning rather than merely measuring outcomes.

Kempe distinguishes between assessment of drama (evaluating dramatic skill and understanding) and assessment through drama (using drama to assess learning in other curriculum areas). He provides practical strategies for both, including the use of reflection, teacher observation, peer assessment, and documentation of process.

### 5. Drama and Literacy

Throughout his career, Kempe has explored the relationships between drama and literacy development. He argues that drama provides unique opportunities for developing oracy, reading comprehension, and writing skills, whilst also fostering the critical literacy necessary for navigating contemporary media-saturated environments.

Kempe's work demonstrates how drama activities can motivate reluctant readers and writers by providing meaningful contexts and authentic purposes for language use. He shows how role-play and improvisation develop spoken language skills, how hot-seating and forum theatre deepen comprehension of character and motivation, and how drama can generate rich stimulus for creative writing.

He is particularly interested in how drama enables students to 'read' texts (both literary and dramatic) as potential blueprints for performance, encouraging active interpretation rather than passive consumption. His publications provide detailed lesson sequences that integrate drama and literacy objectives seamlessly.

### 6. Applied Drama and Theatre

Kempe has been an important voice in the development of applied drama and applied theatre practice in the UK. Applied drama refers to the use of drama and theatre processes in non-traditional contexts and with non-traditional participants, often with social, educational, or therapeutic aims.

In his writing on applied drama, Kempe explores the ethical dimensions of practice, the relationship between artistic quality and social purpose, and the distinctive skills required by applied drama practitioners. He has championed the professionalisation of applied drama work whilst maintaining its participatory, democratic ethos.

Kempe's work in this area includes examination of theatre in education (TIE), drama in health education, drama with marginalised communities, and drama for conflict resolution. He emphasises the importance of clear contracting, appropriate training, and critical reflection in applied drama contexts.

### 7. Mantle of the Expert

Kempe has been one of the foremost interpreters and developers of Dorothy Heathcote's 'Mantle of the Expert' approach in the UK. Mantle of the Expert is an extended drama-based learning methodology in which students take on the roles of experts (such as historians, scientists, or designers) working on a realistic commission or project.

Kempe's contribution has been to articulate the underlying principles of Mantle of the Expert and to demonstrate its application across different curriculum areas and age groups. He explains how the approach generates authentic learning purposes, develops disciplinary thinking, and builds students' confidence and sense of agency.

In his work, Kempe emphasises that Mantle of the Expert is more than simply role-play; it involves careful curriculum design, the creation of a productive enterprise within the drama, and sustained inquiry within the expert role. He provides guidance on establishing the 'client relationship', managing the tension between dramatic fiction and curriculum learning, and using reflection to consolidate understanding.

### 8. Drama Pedagogy and Teacher Development

A consistent theme throughout Kempe's career has been his commitment to drama teacher education and the development of pedagogical expertise. He has written extensively about what constitutes effective drama teaching and how teachers develop the necessary knowledge, skills, and dispositions.

Kempe argues that drama teachers require a distinctive combination of theatrical knowledge, pedagogical skill, and interpersonal sensitivity. He emphasises the importance of practical dramatic experience, deep understanding of child development, and the ability to manage the particular dynamics of drama classrooms where physical space, noise levels, and emotional intensity differ from conventional lessons.

His work on teacher development includes examination of reflective practice, the role of mentoring and peer observation, and the importance of ongoing professional learning. Kempe has supervised numerous postgraduate students and contributed to teacher education programmes that balance practical skill development with critical engagement with theory and research.

## Key Publications

### Books

**"Drama Education and Special Needs" (1996)** - Co-authored with Marigold Ashwell. This groundbreaking book was one of the first substantial UK publications to address the use of drama with students with special educational needs and disabilities. It provides practical strategies whilst examining the inclusive potential of drama pedagogy. The book challenges assumptions about who can participate in drama and demonstrates how drama can be adapted to support diverse learners.

**"The GCSE Drama Coursebook" (2000)** - Co-authored with Lionel Warner. This textbook for GCSE Drama students has been widely used in UK secondary schools. It covers the key components of GCSE Drama specifications, including practical exploration, performance, and written work. The book exemplifies Kempe's ability to make complex dramatic concepts accessible to young people whilst maintaining rigour and depth.

**"Drama Education and the Body: 'I am, Therefore I Think'" (2017)** - This scholarly monograph examines the embodied nature of dramatic learning. Kempe argues that drama education's distinctive contribution lies in its recognition that learning is not purely cognitive but involves the whole person—body, mind, and emotions. Drawing on phenomenology, neuroscience, and theatre studies, the book provides a philosophical foundation for drama pedagogy whilst remaining grounded in classroom practice.

**"Progression in Secondary Drama" (2000)** - Co-authored with Marigold Ashwell. This influential book addresses a perennial challenge in drama education: how to ensure that students' learning in drama develops systematically over time. Kempe and Ashwell propose a framework for understanding progression that moves beyond simplistic notions of skill acquisition to encompass deepening understanding, increasing sophistication, and growing independence.

**"Speaking the Speech: Working with Shakespeare's Language" (2004)** - In this book, Kempe provides practical approaches to teaching Shakespeare in schools, emphasising active, embodied engagement with the language rather than passive study. He demonstrates how drama strategies can unlock Shakespeare's texts for contemporary students, making the language accessible and exciting.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

Kempe has published extensively in academic journals including *Research in Drama Education*, *Drama Research*, and *Theatre Topics*. His articles cover topics ranging from theoretical examinations of dramatic learning to case studies of specific pedagogical approaches.

**"The Role of Drama in the Teaching of Speaking and Listening as the Foundation of Literacy"** (2012) - Published in *The Curriculum Journal*, this article examines how drama contributes to language development, particularly for students from disadvantaged backgrounds. Kempe argues that drama provides structured opportunities for exploratory talk and perspective-taking that are essential for literacy development.

**"Putting Drama in its Place: Understanding Drama in the Secondary School Curriculum"** - In this influential chapter, Kempe examines drama's contested position within the UK curriculum, arguing for recognition of drama both as an art form and as a cross-curricular methodology. He provides historical context for current curriculum debates and suggests ways forward for drama's inclusion.

**"Finding the Hidden Voice: Language Development Through Drama"** - This article explores how drama can support language learners and students who are reluctant to participate in conventional classroom discourse. Kempe demonstrates how the protective frame of fiction and the embodied nature of dramatic activity can enable students to find their voice.

### Edited Collections

Kempe has contributed chapters to numerous edited collections on drama education, including volumes examining international perspectives, creative pedagogies, and arts education policy. His contributions consistently bridge theoretical insight and practical application.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Kempe's work has had significant influence on drama education policy and practice in the UK, though drama's position in the curriculum has remained contested throughout his career. His influence can be traced through several key areas:

### National Curriculum

During periods when drama was explicitly included in the National Curriculum for England (particularly within the English programmes of study), Kempe's work informed the articulation of drama objectives and expectations. His writing on progression and assessment provided frameworks that teachers and curriculum developers drew upon.

When drama's statutory status has been under threat, Kempe has been among those making the case for its educational value. His research demonstrating drama's contribution to literacy, social development, and cross-curricular learning has provided evidence for advocacy efforts.

### GCSE and A-Level Drama

Kempe has influenced the development of examination specifications for GCSE and A-Level Drama through his writing, consultation work, and publications designed to support teachers and students. His emphasis on practical exploration, devised performance, and critical reflection is evident in contemporary drama specifications.

His textbooks and teaching resources have shaped how thousands of teachers approach examination drama, helping to maintain standards and ensure that assessment supports rather than constrains creative work.

### Teacher Education

Perhaps Kempe's most significant influence has been through teacher education. Generations of drama teachers trained at Reading and other institutions have been influenced by his pedagogical approach and theoretical frameworks. These teachers have disseminated his ideas through their own practice, creating ripple effects across the profession.

Kempe's work has been incorporated into initial teacher education programmes, continuing professional development courses, and postgraduate qualifications in drama education. His publications feature on reading lists for drama teacher education programmes throughout the UK and internationally.

### Professional Associations

Through his involvement with professional associations such as the National Association for the Teaching of Drama and engagement with the National Drama network, Kempe has contributed to the development of professional standards, best practice guidance, and policy positions on behalf of the drama education community.

## Pedagogical Approach

Kempe's pedagogical approach is characterised by several distinctive features:

### Practical and Experiential

Kempe consistently advocates for learning in drama that is active, embodied, and experiential. He argues that dramatic understanding cannot be acquired purely through reading or discussion but requires direct engagement with dramatic processes. His teaching and writing model this approach, frequently using practical examples and case studies drawn from real classroom situations.

### Structured Improvisation

Central to Kempe's methodology is the concept of structured improvisation—providing sufficient framework and constraint to focus creative work whilst maintaining space for spontaneity and genuine discovery. He demonstrates how careful planning and clear dramatic structures paradoxically enable greater freedom and creativity.

### Reflection and Metacognition

Kempe emphasises the importance of reflection as an integral component of dramatic learning. He advocates for regular opportunities for students to step back from the drama and consider what they are learning and how the dramatic process is operating. This metacognitive dimension ensures that experiential learning is consolidated and transferred to new contexts.

### Democratic Practice

Kempe's approach embodies democratic principles, valuing all participants' contributions and recognising drama as a collaborative art form. He advocates for pedagogical strategies that distribute authority, enable student agency, and create classroom communities characterised by mutual respect and shared responsibility.

### Artistic and Aesthetic Awareness

Whilst advocating for drama as a learning medium across the curriculum, Kempe never loses sight of drama's artistic dimensions. He consistently emphasises the importance of aesthetic awareness, theatrical craft, and artistic quality. His work challenges false dichotomies between educational and artistic aims, demonstrating how rigorous dramatic work serves both purposes.

### Responsive Teaching

Kempe's writing emphasises the importance of responsive, adaptive teaching in drama. He argues that effective drama teachers must be able to read the group, recognise emerging opportunities, and adjust plans in response to students' needs and interests. This requires deep pedagogical knowledge combined with confidence and flexibility.

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

Andy Kempe's influence on UK drama education extends beyond his formal academic career. His former students occupy leadership positions in schools, universities, and arts organisations. His publications remain widely cited and continue to shape practice.

Kempe's legacy includes:

- **Theoretical frameworks** that help teachers understand and articulate the distinctive nature of dramatic learning
- **Practical strategies** that have been adopted by countless teachers seeking to develop their drama pedagogy
- **Research methodologies** for investigating drama education that balance rigorous inquiry with respect for creative processes
- **Advocacy arguments** that continue to inform debates about drama's place in the curriculum
- **Teacher education approaches** that have influenced how drama teachers are prepared for the profession

His work exemplifies the possibility of being simultaneously scholarly and accessible, theoretically informed and practically useful. This combination has made his writing valuable to classroom teachers seeking to develop their practice, curriculum leaders making decisions about provision, and researchers investigating drama education.

---

## Critical Perspectives

Whilst Kempe's work has been widely respected, it exists within ongoing debates about drama education. Some practitioners argue for approaches more strongly grounded in theatre practice and performance, whilst others advocate for even stronger emphasis on drama as social practice and political intervention.

Kempe's position—maintaining both artistic and educational dimensions of drama—represents a pragmatic middle ground that has enabled productive dialogue across different perspectives within the field. His willingness to engage with curriculum realities and policy contexts, whilst maintaining advocacy for drama's distinctive value, has made his work influential with policymakers as well as practitioners.
