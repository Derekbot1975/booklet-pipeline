# Cecily O'Neill

**Full Name:** Cecily O'Neill

**Known For:** Process Drama methodology, concept of 'pre-text', pioneering work in structuring dramatic encounters, development of teacher-in-role pedagogy

**Career Focus:** Professor Emerita at Ohio State University; international authority on process drama; influential theorist in Drama education curriculum development

**Primary Contribution Areas:** Process drama theory and practice, pre-text as a structuring device, Drama as aesthetic education, teacher training in Drama pedagogy

---

## Biography and Career History

Cecily O'Neill began her career as a Drama teacher in the United Kingdom during the 1960s and 1970s, working within the progressive Drama education movement that emerged from the work of pioneers such as Peter Slade, Brian Way, and particularly Dorothy Heathcote. Her early teaching experience in British secondary schools provided the foundation for her later theoretical developments.

In the 1980s, O'Neill relocated to the United States, taking up a position at Ohio State University where she became Professor of Drama and Theatre. This appointment marked a significant shift in her career from practitioner to academic researcher and theorist. At Ohio State, she established herself as a leading international voice in Drama education, supervising doctoral students and conducting research that would fundamentally reshape understanding of educational drama practice.

O'Neill's unique position—bridging British Drama-in-Education traditions with North American educational contexts—allowed her to synthesise and articulate practices that had often remained tacit or under-theorised. Her work has been particularly significant in codifying and extending the methodologies developed by Dorothy Heathcote, with whom she collaborated closely throughout the 1970s and 1980s.

Following her retirement from Ohio State University, O'Neill was appointed Professor Emerita and has continued to influence Drama education internationally through publications, conference presentations, and consultancy work. Her influence extends across the UK, North America, Australia, and numerous other countries where process drama has been adopted as a pedagogical approach.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Process Drama as Distinct Methodology

O'Neill's most significant contribution has been the theorisation and naming of 'process drama' as a distinct pedagogical form. Prior to her work, the term 'drama' in educational contexts encompassed various activities from improvisation exercises to theatre performance. O'Neill defined process drama as an episodic, non-performance-oriented dramatic exploration conducted by participants and facilitator working together in role.

In her seminal work *Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama* (1995), O'Neill articulated process drama as having several distinctive features: it is unscripted; participants and teachers work in role together; there is no external audience; the work develops through a series of episodes or encounters; and meaning-making occurs through the dramatic exploration itself rather than through polished presentation.

Process drama differs from conventional theatre in that it privileges the experience of participants over the creation of a product for audience consumption. The 'artistry' in process drama lies in the structuring of dramatic encounters that generate aesthetic experience and critical thinking, rather than in performance skills.

### 2. The Concept of Pre-text

O'Neill introduced and developed the concept of 'pre-text' as a fundamental structuring device in process drama. The pre-text is the impulse, starting point, or stimulus that launches the dramatic inquiry. In *Drama Worlds*, she explains that a pre-text is more than simply a starting stimulus; it must contain sufficient ambiguity, tension, and potential to sustain extended dramatic exploration.

A pre-text might be a letter, an object, an image, a fragment of text, a news report, or any artifact that invites speculation and investigation. O'Neill identified that effective pre-texts share certain characteristics: they contain implicit tension or contradiction; they raise questions rather than providing answers; they suggest human relationships and contexts; and they invite multiple interpretations.

The pre-text operates differently from a 'stimulus' in conventional drama teaching. Whilst a stimulus might prompt improvisation or discussion, a pre-text specifically frames the fictional world and establishes the 'dramatic context'—the social, historical, and relational circumstances within which the drama will unfold. O'Neill's theorisation of pre-text has become central to planning and structuring process drama in UK Drama curriculum documents and teacher education programmes.

### 3. Dramatic Context and the 'As If' World

Building on the concept of pre-text, O'Neill developed sophisticated understanding of how 'dramatic context' operates in educational drama. The dramatic context is the fictional frame—the 'as if' world—that participants and teacher jointly create and inhabit. This context must be sufficiently detailed and compelling to sustain belief and engagement, yet remain flexible enough to accommodate emerging ideas and directions.

O'Neill distinguished between the 'surface' features of dramatic context (time, place, roles) and deeper contextual elements including power relationships, social dynamics, values, and tensions. Effective process drama, she argued, attends carefully to establishing credible contexts that matter to participants and connect to significant human experiences and ethical questions.

The teacher's role in establishing and maintaining dramatic context is crucial. O'Neill's work clarified how teachers must work simultaneously inside and outside the fiction—managing the practical and pedagogical aspects whilst also contributing to the dramatic world through role-taking and dramatic intervention.

### 4. Structuring Drama: Episodes and Encounters

O'Neill revolutionised understanding of how drama sessions should be structured in educational settings. Moving beyond the traditional arc of 'beginning, middle, end', she proposed that process drama develops through a sequence of distinct 'episodes' or 'encounters', each offering different perspectives, tensions, or dimensions of the dramatic world.

In *Drama Worlds* and subsequent publications, O'Neill outlined how effective structuring involves varying dramatic forms, tensions, and participant roles across episodes. A process drama might move from small group improvisation, to whole-group ritual, to teacher-narrated storytelling, to frozen image work, each episode deepening engagement with the central questions or themes.

This episodic structure allows for complexity and depth impossible in single-activity drama sessions. Episodes can shift time frame (flashback, flash-forward), change perspective (exploring events from different characters' viewpoints), or alter distance and engagement (moving between direct participation and reflective observation). O'Neill's framework for structuring has become standard practice in UK Drama teaching and is reflected in GCSE and A-level Drama curricula.

### 5. Teacher-in-Role as Pedagogical Strategy

Whilst Dorothy Heathcote pioneered teacher-in-role as a practice, O'Neill provided crucial theoretical analysis of how and why this strategy functions pedagogically. She identified that teacher-in-role serves multiple purposes: it enables the teacher to model commitment and belief within the fiction; it allows subtle control and direction of the drama's development; and it creates opportunities for challenging participants' thinking through the questioning and perspectives the role embodies.

O'Neill categorised different types of teacher roles according to their status and function within the dramatic world. Low-status roles (servant, messenger, junior employee) can invite participants to adopt positions of authority and responsibility. High-status roles (judge, employer, expert) can create productive tension and challenge participants' ideas. Peer-status roles enable collaborative exploration.

Crucially, O'Neill emphasised that teacher-in-role is not 'acting' in a theatrical sense but rather 'strategic role-taking'—the teacher adopts sufficient signs and behaviours of the role to establish its fictional reality whilst remaining responsive to pedagogical opportunities. This clarification has been essential for teacher education in the UK, where uncertainty about 'performing' has sometimes inhibited use of this powerful strategy.

### 6. Drama as Aesthetic Education

O'Neill positioned process drama firmly within the tradition of aesthetic education, arguing that drama's educational value lies not merely in its capacity to teach 'about' topics but in the distinctive form of knowing and experiencing it enables. Drawing on philosophical aesthetics, she argued that process drama engages participants in creating and inhabiting symbolic worlds that generate meaning through form, metaphor, and felt experience.

This aesthetic dimension distinguishes educational drama from simulation, role-play, or creative problem-solving, though it may incorporate elements of all these. In process drama, participants attend not only to the 'content' being explored but to the dramatic form itself—how tension is built, how roles and relationships are established, how time and space are manipulated for dramatic effect.

O'Neill's insistence on drama's aesthetic nature has been particularly significant in defending Drama's place within arts curriculum rather than seeing it merely as a pedagogical tool for other subjects. Her work influenced the development of Drama as a GCSE and A-level subject in the UK, emphasising drama-making and dramatic form alongside performance skills.

### 7. Reflection and Meta-Axis

O'Neill developed the concept of the 'meta-axis' to describe how participants in process drama operate simultaneously within the fictional world and at a reflective distance from it. This dual consciousness—fully engaged in the dramatic moment whilst also aware of its fictional nature—is central to drama's educational power.

The meta-axis enables participants to experience situations and dilemmas from inside whilst simultaneously thinking critically about them. O'Neill distinguished this from 'breaking the drama to discuss', arguing that effective process drama maintains the meta-axis throughout, with reflection embedded within dramatic form rather than separated from it.

Techniques such as forum theatre, hot-seating, thought-tracking, and still-image creation all operate on the meta-axis, enabling participants to step back from direct enactment whilst remaining within the fictional frame. O'Neill's theorisation of this concept has informed current UK Drama curriculum guidance on reflection and evaluation.

### 8. Language and Dramatic Discourse

O'Neill conducted important analysis of the distinctive forms of language use in process drama. She identified that drama creates contexts for language use that differ significantly from typical classroom discourse, enabling participants to use language for purposes including negotiation, persuasion, challenge, speculation, and emotional expression within meaningful contexts.

Drawing on sociolinguistic theory, O'Neill demonstrated how drama enables students to adopt registers, vocabularies, and discourse patterns they might not encounter in conventional classroom activities. The assumption of role legitimises experimentation with language forms, whilst the dramatic context provides authentic communicative purposes.

This analysis has been particularly influential in positioning Drama as a vehicle for literacy and oracy development in UK schools, supporting Drama's inclusion in English curriculum frameworks and contributing to the rationale for Drama as a core entitlement in the National Curriculum during periods when it has been included.

## Key Publications

### *Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama* (1995)

Published by Heinemann in the USA and subsequently distributed internationally, this remains O'Neill's most significant and widely-cited work. The book provides comprehensive theorisation of process drama methodology, offering both conceptual frameworks and practical examples. It is structured around the key concepts of pre-text, context, role, frame, and focus, with detailed analysis of how these elements operate in practice.

The book includes extended descriptions of process drama sessions with various age groups, demonstrating how the methodology works in practice. O'Neill's analysis examines both the surface structure (sequence of activities) and underlying dramatic architecture of each example. *Drama Worlds* has been core reading for Drama education programmes at universities across the UK, Australia, and North America since publication.

The book's theoretical framework draws on diverse sources including aesthetics, semiotics, performance theory, and educational philosophy, establishing process drama as a serious pedagogical methodology worthy of scholarly attention. It remains in print and continues to be essential reading for Drama teachers and researchers.

### *Dorothy Heathcote on Education and Drama* (co-edited with Liz Johnson, 1984)

This edited collection, published by Hutchinson, brought together Heathcote's writings and provided crucial access to her ideas for an international audience. O'Neill's editorial work included substantial contextualising commentary that helped readers understand Heathcote's often elliptical and practical-focused writing within broader educational and dramatic frameworks.

The book includes transcripts of Heathcote's teaching, analyses of her methodology, and theoretical essays exploring her approach to 'mantle of the expert' and other strategies. O'Neill's contribution to this volume demonstrated her capacity to translate practice into accessible theory, a skill that would characterise all her subsequent work.

This publication was particularly influential in UK teacher education institutions during the 1980s and 1990s, establishing Heathcote's work within academic discourse and supporting the incorporation of drama methodology into initial teacher education programmes.

### Articles and Chapters

O'Neill published numerous influential articles in academic journals including *Research in Drama Education* (RiDE), *Drama Research*, and *NJ: Drama Australia Journal*. Key articles include:

- 'Drama and the Web of Form' (1991) in *NADIE Journal*, exploring the aesthetic dimensions of educational drama
- 'Imagined Worlds in Theatre and Drama' (1988) examining the relationship between educational drama and theatrical traditions
- 'Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama' (1991) article in *Theory into Practice*, which prefigured her book

She contributed chapters to major edited collections including *Learning Through Theatre* (edited by Tony Jackson) and *International Handbook of Research in Arts Education* (edited by Liora Bresler), ensuring her ideas reached diverse academic and practitioner audiences.

### *Fields of Drama* (1977, with Dorothy Heathcote and John Hodgson)

Though published earlier in her career, this collection edited with Heathcote includes O'Neill's writing on classroom drama practice and demonstrates her developing analytical approach. The book examines various applications of drama in education and includes practical examples alongside theoretical discussion.

## Influence on UK Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

O'Neill's work significantly influenced Drama's position within the UK National Curriculum, particularly during the 1990s when Drama was included within English curriculum frameworks. Her theorisation of drama's contribution to speaking and listening, literacy development, and critical thinking provided compelling rationale for curriculum inclusion.

The concept of 'making, performing, and responding' that appears in various iterations of arts curriculum documents reflects O'Neill's framework for understanding drama as both creative process and aesthetic form. Her emphasis on structuring dramatic work informed curriculum guidance on planning and assessment in Drama.

### GCSE and A-Level Specifications

O'Neill's influence is evident in Drama examination specifications from all UK awarding bodies. The emphasis on 'devising' as distinct from scripted performance reflects process drama principles. Assessment criteria valuing exploration, development of ideas through episodic structure, and use of dramatic conventions all derive from frameworks O'Neill articulated.

Specifications requiring students to maintain working notebooks, document their devising process, and reflect on development of dramatic material embody O'Neill's emphasis on the meta-axis and reflective practice. The requirement to engage with stimuli and develop substantial dramatic work from initial pre-texts directly applies her theoretical concepts.

### Teacher Education

O'Neill's work has been central to Drama teacher education in the UK for over three decades. PGCE Drama programmes at institutions including University of Warwick, Royal Central School of Speech and Drama, and Manchester Metropolitan University have included *Drama Worlds* as core reading, whilst initial teacher education courses more broadly have drawn on her concepts to develop trainee teachers' understanding of drama pedagogy.

Professional development programmes offered by organisations including the National Association for the Teaching of Drama (NATD) and Drama UK have disseminated O'Neill's methodologies to serving teachers, supporting continued professional learning and curriculum development.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Planning and Preparation

O'Neill's approach to planning process drama emphasises careful preparation of the pre-text and consideration of potential dramatic contexts, whilst maintaining flexibility for participant contributions. Planning involves identifying:

- An effective pre-text with sufficient ambiguity and tension
- Possible roles and relationships within the dramatic world
- Key questions or themes for exploration
- A sequence of potential episodes employing varied dramatic forms
- Opportunities for reflection within the dramatic frame

This planning framework contrasts with rigid lesson plans, instead providing a 'route map' that can be adjusted responsively. O'Neill emphasised that the teacher must be prepared to follow productive directions that emerge during the drama, even if they diverge from anticipated paths.

### Facilitation and Intervention

O'Neill's pedagogical approach positions the teacher as 'facilitator-participant' rather than director or instructor. The teacher's role involves:

- Establishing and protecting the dramatic frame
- Introducing complications, tensions, and new information that deepen the drama
- Using teacher-in-role to model commitment and challenge thinking
- Managing transitions between episodes
- Identifying and amplifying productive tensions and questions that emerge
- Knowing when to step back and when to intervene

This facilitation requires sophisticated reading of group dynamics, dramatic potential, and learning opportunities—skills O'Neill argued could be developed through practice and reflection on practice.

### Assessment and Documentation

O'Neill advocated for assessment approaches that value process alongside product. She suggested documentation methods including:

- Photographs of key dramatic moments
- Participants' reflective writing from within role
- Teacher's observational notes on engagement and development
- Audio or video recording of selected episodes
- Participants' evaluation of their learning

Assessment should attend to participants' capacity to engage with dramatic form, contribute ideas, work collaboratively in role, and reflect critically on the dramatic experience. O'Neill resisted reductive assessment focused solely on performance skills or subject knowledge acquisition.

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

Cecily O'Neill's contribution to Drama education in the UK and internationally cannot be overstated. She provided the theoretical architecture that transformed educational drama from a collection of practical activities into a coherent pedagogical methodology with clear philosophical foundations and articulated learning processes.

Her concept of pre-text has become standard terminology in UK Drama teaching, whilst her framework for structuring drama episodes informs planning practices from primary through to A-level. The emphasis on process over product that characterises contemporary Drama curriculum derives substantially from her theorisation.
