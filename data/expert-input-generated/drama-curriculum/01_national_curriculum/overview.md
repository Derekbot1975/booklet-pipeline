# Drama in the National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction to Drama's Place in the National Curriculum

Drama occupies a unique and sometimes contested position within the English National Curriculum. Unlike subjects such as English or mathematics, drama does not exist as a discrete statutory subject at Key Stages 1-3. Instead, it is embedded within the English programmes of study under 'Spoken Language' and is recognised as an essential pedagogical tool across the curriculum. This positioning has significant implications for curriculum planning, resourcing, and the status of drama within schools.

The National Curriculum in England: Framework Document (DfE, 2013) specifies that pupils should be taught to "participate in role play and improvisation" within the English curriculum. However, the extent and quality of drama provision varies considerably between schools, depending on leadership priorities, staff expertise, and timetabling decisions.

## Statutory Requirements Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

Within the English programme of study, pupils should be taught to:

- Participate in discussions, presentations, performances, role play, improvisations and debates
- Use spoken language to develop understanding through speculating, hypothesising, imagining and exploring ideas

At this stage, Dorothy Heathcote's concept of 'teacher in role' is particularly relevant, allowing practitioners to guide young learners through imaginative scenarios that develop language, social understanding, and creative thinking. The emphasis is on process drama, where the dramatic activity itself is the learning medium rather than preparation for performance.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The statutory requirements continue within English:

- Pupils should participate in performances, role play and improvisations
- They should use discussion to learn, elaborating and explaining clearly their understanding and ideas

Gavin Bolton's work on 'drama for understanding' (Bolton, 1979, 1984) is particularly applicable here. Bolton argued that drama at this stage should focus on deepening children's understanding of human behaviour, moral dilemmas, and social situations through active exploration. His concept of the 'dramatic playing mode' distinguishes educational drama from theatrical performance.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At KS3, drama appears in the English curriculum under Spoken Language:

- Pupils should improvise, rehearse and perform play scripts and poetry
- They should use Standard English confidently in a range of formal and informal contexts

Many schools choose to offer drama as a discrete subject at KS3, though this is not statutory. The National Curriculum (2013 onwards) removed previous references to drama schemes of work, creating both challenges and opportunities for curriculum design.

Jonothan Neelands' structuring devices, documented in "Structuring Drama Work" (Neelands and Goode, 1990, 2015), provide essential frameworks for KS3 drama teaching. His taxonomy of conventions (including conscience alley, thought tracking, forum theatre, and mantle of the expert) has become foundational to UK drama pedagogy.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

Drama becomes optional at KS4 but is available as a GCSE qualification through all major examination boards:

**AQA GCSE Drama (8261)** focuses on:
- Component 1: Understanding Drama (40%)
- Component 2: Devising Drama (40%)
- Component 3: Texts in Practice (20%)

**Edexcel GCSE Drama (1DR0)** offers:
- Component 1: Devising (40%)
- Component 2: Performance from Text (20%)
- Component 3: Theatre Makers in Practice (40%)

**OCR GCSE Drama (J316)** provides:
- Component 1: Devising Drama (30%)
- Component 2: Presenting and Performing Texts (30%)
- Component 3: Drama: Performance and Response (40%)

**WJEC GCSE Drama (C690QS)** structures assessment as:
- Component 1: Devising Theatre (40%)
- Component 2: Performing from a Text (20%)
- Component 3: Interpreting Theatre (40%)

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-Level Drama and Theatre Studies is offered by AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas, with specifications requiring theoretical understanding of practitioners such as Konstantin Stanislavski, Bertolt Brecht, and Antonin Artaud, alongside practical performance and devising skills.

## The Influence of Key Thinkers on National Curriculum Practice

### Dorothy Heathcote

Dorothy Heathcote's pioneering work at Newcastle University established drama as a learning medium rather than merely a performance art. Her 'mantle of the expert' approach, where children adopt roles as experts to solve problems, exemplifies how drama develops cross-curricular learning. Heathcote's influence is evident in the National Curriculum's emphasis on role play and improvisation as tools for understanding.

Heathcote argued that drama creates a "now" time in which children can explore past, present, and future scenarios with emotional and intellectual engagement. Her work emphasises that teachers need not be drama specialists to use drama effectively—they must simply understand how to create productive dramatic contexts.

### Gavin Bolton

Bolton's concept of 'drama for learning' (as opposed to 'learning drama') underpins much of the National Curriculum's integration of drama within English and across subjects. His research demonstrated that drama's educational value lies in its capacity to create what he termed "metaxis"—the simultaneous awareness of being in both the real world and the dramatic world.

Bolton's work with Heathcote, documented in "Drama for Learning: Dorothy Heathcote's Mantle of the Expert Approach to Education" (2010), provides theoretical and practical frameworks still used in primary and secondary schools across the UK.

### Jonothan Neelands

Neelands' taxonomy of dramatic conventions has become standard practice in UK schools. His work categorising drama strategies into context-building, narrative, poetic, and reflective actions provides teachers with accessible structures for lesson planning. The influence of "Structuring Drama Work" (now in its third edition, 2015) is visible in schemes of work, teacher training programmes, and examination specifications.

Neelands has also contributed significantly to debates about drama's place in the curriculum, arguing for drama as both an art form in its own right and a powerful pedagogical tool for other subjects.

### Cecily O'Neill

O'Neill's concept of "process drama" has shaped how drama is understood and taught, particularly at KS1-3. Her book "Drama Worlds" (1995) articulates how drama creates fictional contexts within which real learning occurs. O'Neill's work emphasises that dramatic activity need not lead to polished performance; instead, the exploratory process itself is educationally valuable.

Her distinction between 'presentational' and 'representational' drama helps teachers understand when drama serves as a learning medium versus when it becomes theatrical art-making—both legitimate but different educational purposes.

### Andy Kempe

Kempe's work bridges drama education and literacy, making his research particularly relevant to drama's positioning within English. His publications, including "Drama Education and Special Needs" (with Nicola Abraham, 2000) and "The GCSE Drama Coursebook" (with Ashwell, 2000), have provided practical guidance for teachers implementing National Curriculum requirements.

Kempe's research on progression in drama learning has influenced assessment practices and curriculum sequencing, particularly at KS3 and KS4.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning for Progression

The non-statutory status of drama at KS1-3 creates challenges for ensuring progression. Schools should develop schemes of work that build skills systematically:

**Skills progression might include:**
- Creating and sustaining roles (KS1-2)
- Using drama conventions with increasing sophistication (KS2-3)
- Analysing and evaluating dramatic work (KS3-4)
- Understanding theatrical practitioners and contexts (KS4-5)

### Cross-Curricular Integration

Drama supports learning across the curriculum. Examples include:

- **History**: Using hot-seating and forum theatre to explore historical perspectives
- **PSHE**: Employing role play to navigate social and moral dilemmas
- **English**: Exploring character motivation through improvisation
- **Science**: Using conscience alley to debate ethical issues in scientific development

### Assessment Challenges and Opportunities

Assessing drama, particularly process drama, requires consideration of:

- Contribution to group work and collaboration
- Risk-taking and creative exploration
- Ability to sustain and develop roles
- Understanding of dramatic form and conventions
- Reflection and evaluation skills

At GCSE and A-Level, assessment criteria are explicit, but at KS1-3, teachers must develop appropriate assessment frameworks aligned with school policies and drama-specific learning objectives.

### Resources and Professional Development

The removal of detailed drama guidance from the National Curriculum has increased reliance on subject associations and commercial resources. Key organisations include:

- **National Drama** (formerly National Drama): provides CPD, advocacy, and resources
- **Drama UK**: connects youth theatre, training providers, and educators
- **Trinity College London** and **LAMDA**: offer graded examinations providing alternative assessment frameworks

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### The Status Question

Drama educators continue to debate whether drama should become a statutory National Curriculum subject. Proponents argue that statutory status would ensure equity of access and recognise drama's educational value. Critics suggest that integration within English and cross-curricular use actually extends drama's reach.

### The EBacc and Arts Education

The English Baccalaureate (EBacc) performance measure does not include arts subjects, creating pressures on curriculum time for drama at KS3 and affecting GCSE uptake. Research by the Education Policy Institute and others has documented declining arts participation linked to EBacc accountability measures.

### Inclusive Practice

Research by Andy Kempe and others highlights drama's particular value for learners with SEND, those learning English as an additional language, and pupils who struggle with traditional academic approaches. Drama's kinaesthetic, collaborative, and imaginative nature provides alternative access points to curriculum content.

### Cultural Diversity and Representation

Contemporary drama education increasingly addresses questions of whose stories are told and which theatrical traditions are valued. Teachers must ensure that drama work reflects diverse cultural perspectives and challenges rather than reinforces stereotypes.

## Conclusion

Drama's position in the National Curriculum reflects broader tensions in English education between prescription and professional autonomy, between subjects as discrete disciplines and as integrated learning tools. Effective drama education requires teachers to understand both the statutory minimum requirements and the rich pedagogical traditions articulated by Heathcote, Bolton, Neelands, O'Neill, Kempe, and others.

Whether delivered as a distinct subject or integrated within and across other curriculum areas, high-quality drama provision develops essential skills: creativity, collaboration, communication, critical thinking, and cultural understanding. The challenge for curriculum leaders is to ensure that all pupils access these benefits, regardless of their school's organisational structures or examination priorities.
