# Drama at Key Stages 1 and 2

## Statutory Framework and National Curriculum Position

Drama at Key Stages 1 and 2 occupies a unique position within the English National Curriculum for England (2014). Unlike at Key Stages 3 and 4, Drama is not a discrete, statutory subject but is instead embedded within the English curriculum under 'Spoken Language'. The National Curriculum states that pupils should be taught to "participate in role play and improvisations" (DfE, 2013) as part of developing their speaking and listening skills.

Despite its non-statutory status as a standalone subject, Drama remains a powerful pedagogical tool across the primary curriculum. The National Society for Education in Drama and the Arts (NSEAD) has consistently advocated for Drama's broader educational value beyond its English curriculum placement, emphasising its contribution to personal, social, and emotional development.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Curriculum Expectations

At Key Stage 1, the National Curriculum for English requires pupils to "participate in discussions, presentations, performances, role play, improvisations and debates" (DfE, 2013). The emphasis is on developing confidence in speaking and listening through playful, imaginative contexts.

Drama at this stage builds upon the natural propensity of young children for imaginative play. Dorothy Heathcote described this as "the dramatic playing that children do naturally" which teachers can harness for educational purposes (Heathcote & Bolton, 1995). The focus should be on:

- Simple role-play activities
- Teacher-in-role interactions
- Story dramatisation
- Hot-seating with familiar characters
- Freeze-frames depicting story moments
- Movement and mime work

### Practical Approaches for Teachers

**Teacher-in-Role**: This technique, pioneered by Dorothy Heathcote, involves the teacher adopting a role to engage children in dramatic contexts. At KS1, teachers might become story characters, community helpers, or fantastical beings to stimulate language and imagination. Heathcote argued that teacher-in-role "gives the teacher power to shape the drama from within" whilst maintaining control of learning objectives (Bolton, 1984).

**Story Drama**: Using familiar tales as frameworks for dramatic exploration allows children to inhabit characters and explore narrative. Gavin Bolton's work emphasises how drama creates an "as if" world where children can safely explore emotions and situations beyond their immediate experience (Bolton, 1992).

**Physical Theatre and Movement**: At this stage, non-verbal communication through gesture, facial expression, and whole-body movement develops children's dramatic vocabulary before sophisticated verbal articulation emerges.

### Learning Outcomes

By the end of Key Stage 1, children should:
- Speak audibly and fluently with growing confidence
- Participate in role-play with sustained focus
- Begin to understand that performance requires an audience
- Use voices and bodies expressively
- Work collaboratively in small groups

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Curriculum Expectations

The Key Stage 2 English curriculum maintains the requirement for "participation in performances, role play, improvisations and debates" (DfE, 2013) but expectations deepen significantly. Pupils should develop:

- More sophisticated characterisation
- Understanding of dramatic tension and narrative structure
- Ability to maintain roles over extended periods
- Critical reflection on their own and others' work
- Integration of drama across the curriculum

### Conventions and Techniques

Jonothan Neelands' seminal work "Structuring Drama Work" (1990, revised 2000) provides a framework of dramatic conventions particularly valuable at KS2:

**Hot-Seating**: Questioning characters to explore motivation, backstory, and perspective. This develops both questioning skills and empathetic understanding.

**Conscience Alley**: Pupils form two lines representing conflicting viewpoints, through which a character walks whilst hearing arguments. This convention, detailed in Neelands & Goode (2015), develops critical thinking and moral reasoning.

**Mantle of the Expert**: Developed by Dorothy Heathcote, this approach positions pupils as experts (archaeologists, town planners, scientists) tackling real-world problems through drama. Heathcote argued this created "responsible teams" where children's expertise is genuinely valued (Heathcote & Bolton, 1995).

**Forum Theatre**: Adapted from Augusto Boal's work, pupils can stop and restart scenes, suggesting alternative actions and exploring consequences—valuable for PSHE and citizenship education.

**Tableaux and Still Image**: Creating frozen pictures develops understanding of symbolism, composition, and visual communication. Andy Kempe's work emphasises how still images allow "time for reflection and detailed observation" (Kempe & Ashwell, 2000).

### Process Drama

Cecily O'Neill's concept of "process drama" is particularly applicable at KS2. Unlike performance-focused work, process drama emphasises the "dramatic exploration of a theme or situation" without predetermined scripts or audience requirements (O'Neill, 1995). O'Neill argued that in process drama, "the emphasis is on the experience of the participants rather than the communication to an audience."

Process drama at KS2 might involve:
- Extended dramatic inquiry lasting multiple sessions
- Building fictional contexts collaboratively
- Exploring moral dilemmas and social issues
- Developing critical thinking through in-role decision-making

### Cross-Curricular Applications

Drama at KS2 powerfully supports learning across subjects:

**History**: Historical enactment allows children to explore different perspectives on past events. Hot-seating historical figures, creating tableaux of significant moments, or using conscience alley for historical dilemmas develops historical empathy and source interpretation skills.

**PSHE and Citizenship**: Drama provides safe spaces to explore relationships, emotions, and social scenarios. Forum theatre enables children to rehearse responses to bullying, peer pressure, or moral dilemmas.

**Literacy**: Drama deepens textual understanding. "Getting inside" characters through role-play, exploring alternative plot developments, or performing Reader's Theatre enhances comprehension and inference skills.

**Science**: Scientific processes can be dramatised (becoming particles in different states, or cells during mitosis), making abstract concepts concrete through embodied learning.

### Developing Dramatic Intelligence

Gavin Bolton distinguished between "drama for learning" and "drama for theatre" (Bolton, 1984). At KS2, both have value, but Bolton emphasised that educational drama prioritises "the development of understanding through the drama experience" rather than polished performance.

Key elements of dramatic intelligence developing at KS2 include:

**Symbolic Representation**: Understanding that objects, spaces, and actions can represent other things (a chair becomes a throne; walking in place represents a journey).

**Dramatic Irony**: Grasping that participants and audiences can hold different knowledge, creating tension and engagement.

**Genre Awareness**: Recognising conventions of different dramatic forms (comedy, tragedy, melodrama) and how they shape meaning.

**Critical Reflection**: Articulating what happened in drama, why choices were made, and what was learned—what Jonothan Neelands calls "reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action" (Neelands, 2009).

### Progression in Skills

**Lower Key Stage 2 (Years 3-4)**:
- Sustaining roles for extended periods
- Beginning to develop distinct character voices and physicality
- Understanding basic dramatic structure (beginning, middle, end)
- Working collaboratively in groups of 4-6
- Simple devising from stimuli
- Responding to teacher-in-role with appropriate language

**Upper Key Stage 2 (Years 5-6)**:
- Sophisticated characterisation incorporating motivation and emotion
- Understanding and using dramatic conventions consciously
- Devising structured performances with clear intentions
- Evaluating drama using appropriate terminology
- Leading and facilitating drama in peer groups
- Integrating technical elements (lighting, sound, costume) conceptually

## Assessment and Progression

Unlike secondary Drama (where GCSE specifications provide clear assessment criteria), KS1 and KS2 Drama assessment must align with broader curriculum objectives. The 'Spoken Language' Programme of Study provides some guidance, but many schools develop their own progression frameworks.

Assessment should consider:

**Participation**: Engagement, risk-taking, sustained concentration
**Collaboration**: Listening, negotiating, building on others' ideas
**Communication**: Clarity of expression, appropriate register, non-verbal communication
**Creation**: Originality, development of ideas, structural coherence
**Reflection**: Articulating learning, evaluating choices, suggesting improvements

Andy Kempe and Marigold Ashwell, in "Progression in Secondary Drama" (2000), propose assessment should value both the process and product, recognising that "understanding develops through the drama experience itself."

## Practical Classroom Management

### Space and Resources

Primary Drama requires flexible space. Whilst dedicated drama studios are ideal, most KS1 and KS2 Drama occurs in classrooms or halls. Key considerations:

- Clear floor space with furniture moved aside
- Defined performance/working areas
- Signal systems for attention (drum, chime, hand signals)
- Simple costume pieces and props stored accessibly
- Visual stimuli (images, objects, artefacts)

### Behaviour Management in Drama

Drama's active, collaborative nature requires specific management approaches. Dorothy Heathcote emphasised the importance of "contract" between teacher and pupils—establishing clear expectations about focus, respect, and commitment (Wagner, 1979).

Effective strategies include:

**Clear Signals**: Agreed methods for gaining attention (hand up, freeze, "curtain")
**Defined Spaces**: Distinguishing between "in role" and "out of role" spaces
**Reflection Time**: Building in moments to step back and discuss
**Structured Activities**: Particularly important for groups new to Drama
**Positive Framing**: Presenting challenges rather than restrictions ("Show me you can freeze completely still" rather than "Stop moving")

### Inclusion and Differentiation

Drama is inherently inclusive, offering multiple entry points for diverse learners. Gavin Bolton argued that drama "has the capacity to involve all children regardless of ability" (Bolton, 1992) because it values different intelligences and modes of expression.

Considerations include:

- Offering roles requiring different skills (movement-based, verbal, organisational)
- Paired work supporting less confident pupils
- Visual supports for understanding dramatic structure
- Alternative communication methods (gesture, tableau, drawing)
- Cultural responsiveness in selecting themes and approaches

## Key Theorists and Their Contributions

### Dorothy Heathcote (1926-2011)

Heathcote revolutionised educational drama through concepts including teacher-in-role and Mantle of the Expert. Her work emphasised drama as a learning medium rather than an art form to be taught. Key texts include "Drama for Learning: Dorothy Heathcote's Mantle of the Expert Approach to Education" (Heathcote & Bolton, 1995).

### Gavin Bolton (1927-2021)

Bolton developed Heathcote's work academically, articulating theoretical frameworks for educational drama. His distinction between "drama" and "theatre" and emphasis on "living through" experiences shaped primary practice. Essential reading: "New Perspectives on Classroom Drama" (Bolton, 1992).

### Jonothan Neelands

Neelands codified dramatic conventions in accessible frameworks for teachers. "Structuring Drama Work" (first published 1990, revised with Tony Goode in 2015) remains the definitive guide to conventions applicable at KS2. His work makes sophisticated techniques accessible to non-specialist primary teachers.

### Cecily O'Neill

O'Neill's concept of "process drama" emphasises dramatic exploration without performance pressure—particularly valuable in primary contexts. Her work "Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama" (O'Neill, 1995) provides theoretical grounding for extended dramatic inquiry.

### Andy Kempe

Kempe's work bridges primary and secondary practice, with particular attention to progression and assessment. "The GCSE Drama Coursebook" (with various co-authors) and "Progression in Secondary Drama" (with Marigold Ashwell, 2000) offer frameworks applicable to upper KS2.

## Links to Secondary Transition

Effective KS2 Drama provides foundation for GCSE Drama (available from AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, and WJEC). Students arriving at KS3 with strong KS2 Drama experience bring:

- Confidence in performance and collaboration
- Understanding of basic conventions and techniques
- Ability to devise from stimuli
- Vocabulary for discussing and evaluating drama
- Experience of both process and performance work

However, secondary colleagues should not assume consistent primary Drama experience. Some pupils will have extensive exposure; others minimal. Secondary schemes of work should include foundational experiences whilst providing extension for experienced practitioners.

## Current Context and Future Directions

The 2014 National Curriculum's positioning of Drama within English has been controversial. Whilst ensuring all primary pupils encounter drama-based activities, it potentially marginalises Drama's distinctive contribution to creativity, emotional intelligence, and cultural understanding.

Recent educational priorities intersect productively with Drama:

**Oracy**: The increased focus on speaking and listening skills, championed by organisations like Voice 21, recognises Drama's contribution to articulacy and confidence.

**Character Education**: Drama's capacity to explore moral dilemmas and develop empathy aligns with schools' character education priorities.

**Cultural Capital**: Drama exposes children to theatrical traditions, cultural narratives, and artistic expression—contributing to cultural capital development emphasised in Ofsted frameworks.

**Wellbeing**: Drama provides emotional expression opportunities and safe spaces for exploring feelings—increasingly valued post-pandemic.

## Conclusion

Drama at Key Stages 1 and 2, whilst not a statutory standalone subject, offers powerful learning experiences across the curriculum. Drawing on the work of theorists like Heathcote, Bolton, Neelands, O'Neill, and Kempe, primary teachers can employ drama as both an art form and a learning medium. Effective primary Drama develops confident communicators, collaborative workers, creative thinkers, and empathetic citizens—outcomes valuable far beyond any single subject's boundaries.

Quality Drama provision in primary schools requires committed teachers (not necessarily specialists), flexible space, and institutional recognition of Drama's value. Where these exist, Drama transforms learning, making the abstract concrete, the distant immediate, and the theoretical experiential. As Dorothy Heathcote observed, "Drama is not just another way of teaching—it's a way of understanding the world."
