# Drama at Key Stage 3

## National Curriculum Framework and Statutory Requirements

Drama at Key Stage 3 occupies a unique position within the National Curriculum for England. Since the 2014 revision, Drama is no longer a statutory subject in its own right but is instead embedded within the English Programme of Study. The National Curriculum states that students should be taught to "participate in discussions, presentations, performances, role play/improvisations and debates" as part of their spoken language development.

However, many schools continue to deliver discrete Drama provision at KS3, recognising its distinct pedagogical value beyond English. Where schools offer Drama as a separate subject, they typically deliver one or two lessons per fortnight, though provision varies significantly across institutions. The subject's non-statutory status has created both challenges for resourcing and opportunities for curriculum innovation.

## Developmental Context: Ages 11-14

Key Stage 3 spans Years 7-9, covering students aged 11-14. This developmental period is characterised by significant cognitive, emotional and social changes. Dorothy Heathcote's concept of the "mantle of the expert" is particularly powerful at this stage, as students are developing their capacity for abstract thinking and can engage with complex fictional contexts and perspectives.

Gavin Bolton emphasised that early adolescence represents a critical period for dramatic development, where students move from simple enactment towards understanding theatre as a symbolic art form. Students at this stage can begin to appreciate the relationship between dramatic action and meaning-making, developing what Bolton termed "metaxis" - the capacity to exist simultaneously in the real and fictional worlds.

## Core Learning Areas at KS3

### Performance Skills

Students at KS3 should develop fundamental performance competencies including:

- **Physical skills**: spatial awareness, gesture, posture, movement, facial expression and stillness
- **Vocal skills**: projection, articulation, tone, pitch, pace, pause and accent
- **Characterisation**: creating believable roles through physical and vocal choices, understanding motivation, exploring status and relationships
- **Ensemble work**: developing timing, focus, trust and collaborative creativity

Jonothan Neelands' work on ensemble-building is particularly relevant here. His "Structuring Drama Work" (1990, co-authored with Tony Goode) identifies key conventions that scaffold collaborative performance, including still image, thought-tracking, and forum theatre techniques.

### Creating and Devising

KS3 students should develop the capacity to create original dramatic work through:

- Improvisation skills: spontaneous response, offer and acceptance, maintaining fictional frames
- Structured devising: developing work from stimuli including text, objects, images, music and themes
- Scriptwriting fundamentals: understanding dialogue, stage directions, dramatic structure
- Exploration of dramatic forms: naturalism, physical theatre, forum theatre, verbatim theatre

Cecily O'Neill's concept of "process drama" is foundational at this stage. Her work, particularly "Drama Worlds" (1995), emphasises how students construct meaning through dramatic exploration rather than working towards polished performance. Process drama allows KS3 students to engage with complex issues through sustained fictional inquiry.

### Responding and Evaluating

Critical engagement develops significantly during KS3:

- Using subject-specific vocabulary to discuss drama work
- Analysing effectiveness of dramatic techniques and conventions
- Offering constructive peer feedback using success criteria
- Reflecting on the relationship between intention and impact
- Beginning to contextualise work within theatrical traditions

Andy Kempe's work on teaching drama in secondary schools emphasises the importance of developing "critical vocabularies" that allow students to articulate their understanding of dramatic processes and products.

## Key Pedagogical Approaches

### Teacher-in-Role

Dorothy Heathcote pioneered this technique, which remains central to KS3 practice. The teacher adopts a role within the drama to:

- Challenge students' thinking from within the fictional frame
- Model dramatic commitment and risk-taking
- Control pace and deepen engagement
- Introduce information and complications
- Elevate the register of language and interaction

Heathcote's work demonstrated how teacher-in-role shifts power dynamics, positioning students as experts whose contributions drive the drama forward.

### Drama Conventions and Structures

Neelands and Goode's taxonomy of conventions provides essential scaffolding for KS3 work:

- **Context-building conventions**: creating the dramatic world (hot-seating, role-on-the-wall, mapping)
- **Narrative conventions**: advancing the story (narration, flashback/flash-forward, ritual)
- **Poetic conventions**: creating symbolic meaning (still image, soundscape, marking the moment)
- **Reflective conventions**: stepping out to analyse (forum theatre, conscience alley, thought-tracking)

These conventions allow teachers to structure increasingly sophisticated dramatic explorations, appropriate to students' developing capabilities.

### Whole-Class Drama vs. Small-Group Work

KS3 programmes typically balance whole-class process drama with small-group devising projects. Gavin Bolton's distinction between "making" drama and "performing" drama is useful here. Process drama emphasises meaning-making through collective engagement, while small-group devising develops autonomy and performance craft.

## Curriculum Planning and Progression

### Year 7 Curriculum Focus

Year 7 typically emphasises transition and foundation-building:

- Establishing safe, creative studio culture and protocols
- Core physical and vocal skill development through games and exercises
- Introduction to key conventions (still image, improvisation, hot-seating)
- Exploring familiar contexts before moving to more challenging themes
- Short-form work building confidence and ensemble trust

Common schemes of work include identity and belonging, fairytales reimagined, and community narratives.

### Year 8 Curriculum Focus

Year 8 builds complexity and sophistication:

- Extended devising projects requiring sustained collaboration
- Introduction to scripted work and text-based approaches
- Exploration of social and historical themes through drama
- Development of directorial and design thinking
- More sophisticated use of theatrical conventions and forms

Typical schemes explore social issues, historical events (evacuation, civil rights, conflict), or cultural stories from diverse traditions.

### Year 9 Curriculum Focus

Year 9 prepares students for potential GCSE study:

- Pre-1956 and contemporary script extracts
- Sophisticated devising incorporating multiple theatrical styles
- Introduction to practitioner approaches (Brecht, Stanislavski, Berkoff)
- Technical theatre awareness (lighting, sound, design concepts)
- Extended written and verbal evaluation

Many schools use Year 9 to introduce GCSE-style terminology and assessment frameworks, particularly where students may progress to AQA, Edexcel, OCR or WJEC Eduqas Drama GCSE.

## Assessment at Key Stage 3

Without statutory Drama assessment frameworks, schools develop varied approaches. Best practice typically includes:

### Formative Assessment

- Ongoing observation of practical work against clear criteria
- Peer and self-assessment structured through success criteria
- Teacher feedback during process, not just on final outcomes
- Use of video recording for reflection and analysis
- Regular low-stakes opportunities to demonstrate skills

### Summative Assessment

- End-of-unit practical performances assessed against skill descriptors
- Written evaluations of own and others' work
- Portfolio evidence including scripts, designs, research
- Progress measured against personalised targets

Assessment frameworks often reference GCSE assessment objectives to ensure progression, particularly:
- **Creating**: generating and developing ideas
- **Performing**: demonstrating physical and vocal skills
- **Responding**: evaluating work using subject terminology

## Cross-Curricular Links and Wider Learning

### Literacy Development

Drama makes distinctive contributions to literacy beyond standard English provision:

- Oracy and spoken language confidence in varied contexts
- Understanding of dialogue, subtext and dramatic language
- Reading and analysing play scripts as a distinct text type
- Vocabulary acquisition through contextualised language use

### Social and Emotional Learning

KS3 Drama addresses PSHE and SMSC (spiritual, moral, social and cultural) education through:

- Developing empathy through role-taking and perspective-shifting
- Exploring ethical dilemmas in safe fictional contexts
- Building collaborative skills and collective responsibility
- Addressing sensitive topics including discrimination, conflict, identity

Heathcote's work particularly emphasises how drama allows students to explore difficult social realities through the "protection of the frame" - the fictional context creates psychological safety for engaging with challenging material.

### Cultural Capital and Theatre Access

KS3 Drama should broaden cultural horizons through:

- Live theatre attendance and theatre trip programmes
- Exposure to diverse theatrical traditions and global practices
- Engagement with professional practitioners through workshops
- Understanding theatre as a living art form and potential career pathway

## Inclusive Practice and Differentiation

### Supporting SEND Students

Drama's multimodal nature makes it particularly accessible:

- Non-written forms of expression suit varied learning profiles
- Physical and visual learning alongside verbal communication
- Structured conventions provide scaffolding for participation
- Opportunities for different types of contribution within ensemble

Neelands emphasises how drama conventions democratise participation, allowing every student to contribute meaningfully regardless of confidence or prior attainment.

### Stretching High Attainers

Challenge is provided through:

- Leadership roles within devising processes
- Complex characterisation requiring nuanced choices
- Abstract or challenging themes and stimuli
- Extended written analysis requiring subject-specific terminology
- Introduction to demanding practitioner approaches and theory

### Cultural Responsiveness

KS3 Drama should reflect diverse experiences through:

- Schemes exploring multiple cultural narratives and perspectives
- Avoiding stereotypical casting or tokenistic representation
- Selecting texts and stimuli representing varied voices
- Creating space for students to bring their own stories into drama work

## Resources and Professional Development

### Key Texts for Teachers

Essential reading for KS3 Drama practitioners includes:

- Neelands, J. and Goode, T. (1990/2015) "Structuring Drama Work" - foundational conventions and planning frameworks
- Kempe, A. and Winkelmann, C. (2010) "Drama Education and Special Needs" - inclusive approaches
- Baldwin, P. (2012) "With Drama in Mind" - linking drama pedagogy to cognitive development
- Woolland, B. (2008) "Teaching Primary Drama" - while focused on primary, includes valuable KS3 transition material

### Professional Associations

- **National Drama**: the UK subject association offering CPD, publications and advocacy
- **National Association for the Teaching of Drama (NATD)**: regional networks and conferences
- Regional theatre education departments offering teacher workshops

### Digital Resources

- National Theatre Learning resources and digital theatre broadcasts
- Drama Resource website offering scheme planning and conventions
- National Drama's publications including "Drama" magazine

## Challenges and Current Context

### Curriculum Time and Status

The 2014 curriculum changes reduced Drama's visibility and statutory protection. Many schools have reduced or removed KS3 Drama provision, particularly where accountability measures prioritise EBacc subjects. However, schools maintaining strong Drama programmes report benefits including improved student wellbeing, attendance and whole-school culture.

### Transition to GCSE

Where KS3 Drama is limited or absent, students selecting Drama GCSE arrive with minimal prior learning. This creates challenges for delivering demanding GCSE specifications from AQA, Edexcel, OCR or WJEC Eduqas. Three years of KS3 provision allows proper skill development and informed GCSE option choices.

### Assessment Without Levels

Post-levels assessment requires schools to develop meaningful progression frameworks. Best practice involves clear skill descriptors showing development from Year 7 through Year 9, aligned with eventual GCSE criteria where appropriate.

## Links to Key Stage 2 and Key Stage 4

### Transition from Primary

Year 7 Drama should acknowledge and build upon KS2 experiences, which vary considerably between primary schools. Students arrive with diverse drama backgrounds from extensive process drama work to occasional nativity performances. Initial assessment and foundation units establish shared starting points.

### Progression to GCSE

Strong KS3 provision prepares students for GCSE Drama by:

- Establishing foundational performance, devising and evaluation skills
- Introducing script work and theatrical vocabulary
- Developing collaborative and independent working capacities
- Building cultural capital through theatre attendance
- Fostering understanding of drama as both practical and intellectual discipline

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) require students to demonstrate performance, devising and written analysis skills that should be progressively developed across KS3.

## Conclusion: The Distinctive Value of KS3 Drama

Despite its non-statutory status, Drama at Key Stage 3 offers irreplaceable educational value. As Gavin Bolton argued throughout his career, drama develops unique capabilities: thinking from within experience, collaborative creativity, and understanding human behaviour through embodied exploration. Dorothy Heathcote's legacy demonstrates how drama enables young people to examine their world through powerful fictional lenses, developing both critical consciousness and creative agency.

For the adolescent learner navigating complex identity formation and social positioning, KS3 Drama provides essential opportunities for exploration, expression and encounter with diverse perspectives. When taught with rigour, imagination and pedagogical expertise drawing on the rich tradition of UK drama education, it remains a transformative element of secondary education.
