# Drama in EYFS

## Introduction to Drama in Early Years Foundation Stage

Drama in the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) is not a discrete curriculum subject but rather a pedagogical approach that permeates all areas of learning and development. Drama activities in EYFS support children's natural propensity for imaginative play, which forms the foundation for all subsequent dramatic work. The EYFS statutory framework (DfE, 2021) recognises drama as a crucial vehicle for development across the prime and specific areas of learning, particularly within Communication and Language, Personal, Social and Emotional Development, and Expressive Arts and Design.

Gavin Bolton's seminal work emphasises that drama with young children should be "living through" experiences rather than performance-focused activities. This aligns perfectly with EYFS principles, where process takes precedence over product and child-initiated learning is paramount.

## EYFS Framework and Drama

### Statutory Framework References

The EYFS statutory framework (September 2021) provides several explicit references to dramatic play and imaginative activities:

**Prime Areas:**
- **Communication and Language**: "Children should be supported to develop their communication skills through role play and storytelling" (DfE, 2021, p. 8)
- **Personal, Social and Emotional Development**: "Through adult modelling and guidance, children learn how to look after themselves, manage their emotions and develop positive relationships through collaborative play" (DfE, 2021, p. 9)

**Specific Areas:**
- **Expressive Arts and Design**: "The development of children's artistic and cultural awareness supports their imagination and creativity. It is important that children have regular opportunities to engage with the arts, enabling them to explore and play with a wide range of media and materials" (DfE, 2021, p. 10)

### Early Learning Goals

The Early Learning Goal for Expressive Arts and Design specifically states: "Children at the expected level of development will invent, adapt and recount narratives and stories with peers and their teacher" and "perform songs, rhymes, poems and stories with others, and – when appropriate – try to move in time with music" (DfE, 2021, p. 11).

## Theoretical Foundations

### Dorothy Heathcote's "Teacher in Role"

Dorothy Heathcote's pioneering work on "teacher in role" has particular significance in EYFS settings. This technique involves the practitioner taking on a role within the dramatic play, not to direct or control, but to deepen children's engagement and challenge their thinking. In EYFS contexts, teacher in role might involve:

- A practitioner becoming a character who needs help (e.g., a giant who has lost something)
- Taking on a low-status role to empower children's decision-making
- Using role to introduce problems that require collaborative solutions

Heathcote argued that drama "cracks open the everyday" and allows children to explore situations beyond their immediate experience. For three- to five-year-olds, this might involve exploring emotions, social scenarios, or fantastical situations within the safety of fiction.

### Gavin Bolton on Drama with Young Children

Bolton's work in "Drama as Education" (1984) emphasises that drama in early years should focus on "concrete experiences" rather than abstract concepts. He argues that young children learn best through:

- Direct engagement with materials and situations
- Physical embodiment of ideas
- Immediate, sensory experiences
- Play that emerges from their interests

Bolton suggests that practitioners should "go with" children's play rather than imposing adult-conceived structures, whilst still providing provocations that deepen engagement.

## Developmental Appropriateness

### Ages and Stages

**Three-year-olds** typically engage in:
- Parallel play with emerging collaborative elements
- Simple role-taking (mummy, daddy, baby, familiar characters)
- Brief dramatic sequences lasting a few minutes
- Heavy reliance on props and costumes to sustain roles
- Physical, whole-body engagement with stories

**Four-year-olds** generally demonstrate:
- More sustained collaborative play scenarios
- Increasingly complex narratives that can last 15-20 minutes
- Negotiation of roles and rules within play
- Use of language to create imaginary scenarios without props
- Beginning to distinguish between "real" and "pretend"

**Five-year-olds** (Reception) often show:
- Sophisticated collaborative play with multiple participants
- Extended narrative sequences that may continue across days
- Clear understanding of the dramatic frame ("we're just pretending")
- Ability to plan play scenarios in advance
- Integration of literacy elements (writing signs, tickets, menus for role play)

## Drama Strategies for EYFS

### Process Drama Approaches

Process drama, as articulated by Cecily O'Neill in "Drama Worlds" (1995), can be adapted for EYFS with careful scaffolding. O'Neill emphasises that drama is not about performing rehearsed scripts but about exploring situations collaboratively. In EYFS, this might involve:

**Story exploration:** Taking a familiar story (e.g., "The Three Little Pigs") and exploring it from different perspectives. What did the pigs' mother think? How did the builder who sold the straw feel?

**Mantle of the Expert:** Heathcote's "Mantle of the Expert" approach positions children as experts with knowledge and responsibility. In EYFS, children might become:
- Vets caring for sick toy animals
- Builders constructing homes for story characters
- Gardeners growing magic beans
- Museum curators looking after precious objects

### Practical Drama Techniques

**1. Freeze Frames (Still Images)**
Even very young children can create freeze frames showing moments from stories or emotions. This technique:
- Supports understanding of body language
- Develops spatial awareness
- Provides a concrete way to explore abstract concepts
- Can be used as a starting point for discussion

**2. Hot Seating**
A simplified version where a practitioner in role answers children's questions. For EYFS:
- Keep sessions brief (5-10 minutes)
- Use familiar characters initially
- Encourage children to ask questions
- Model appropriate questioning techniques

**3. Thought Tracking**
Touching a freeze-frame character on the shoulder to hear their thoughts. This helps children:
- Develop theory of mind (understanding others' perspectives)
- Articulate emotions
- Make connections between feelings and situations

**4. Soundscaping**
Creating sound effects for stories or environments. Children might:
- Make storm sounds for "Whatever Next!"
- Create jungle sounds for "Walking Through the Jungle"
- Develop listening and responding skills

## Planning Drama in EYFS

### Adult-Led vs. Child-Initiated Drama

The EYFS framework emphasises the balance between adult-led and child-initiated learning. Drama provision should reflect this:

**Adult-Led Drama Activities:**
- Whole-class story dramatisations
- Focused drama conventions exploring specific concepts
- Modelling of dramatic play scenarios
- Introduction of new vocabulary and concepts through role
- Typically 10-15 minutes for Nursery, 15-20 minutes for Reception

**Child-Initiated Drama:**
- Continuous provision areas (role play areas, small world resources)
- Adult responsiveness to emerging play themes
- Enhancement of spontaneous play through practitioner involvement
- Documentation and celebration of children's dramatic play

Jonothan Neelands emphasises that drama conventions should be "lightly structured" with young children, providing enough framework to deepen engagement without constraining creativity.

### Continuous Provision for Drama

**Role Play Areas:**
Research by the British Educational Research Association (BERA) indicates that well-resourced role play areas significantly enhance language development and social skills. Effective role play areas should:

- Change regularly to reflect children's interests (typically every 3-4 weeks)
- Include authentic resources rather than plastic replicas where possible
- Provide open-ended materials that support multiple scenarios
- Include literacy resources relevant to the theme
- Reflect diverse cultures and family structures

**Suggested themes:**
- Home corner (culturally diverse homes)
- Shops and cafés
- Hospitals and vets
- Space stations or castles
- Garden centres or post offices
- Story-based areas (Goldilocks' cottage, Giant's castle)

**Small World Play:**
Small world resources provide opportunities for narrative exploration on a smaller scale. Children develop:
- Narrative construction skills
- Dialogue and characterisation
- Problem-solving through play scenarios
- Fine motor skills

### Planning for Stories and Texts

Cecily O'Neill's work on "text as pretext" suggests using quality literature as a springboard for drama. In EYFS, effective texts for drama work include:

**Traditional Tales:**
- "The Three Billy Goats Gruff" – exploring bravery, negotiation
- "Little Red Riding Hood" – safety, trust, character perspective
- "Goldilocks and the Three Bears" – consequences, empathy

**Contemporary Picture Books:**
- "We're Going on a Bear Hunt" (Michael Rosen) – physical theatre, soundscaping
- "Where the Wild Things Are" (Maurice Sendak) – emotion regulation, imagination
- "The Gruffalo" (Julia Donaldson) – problem-solving, confidence
- "Lost and Found" (Oliver Jeffers) – friendship, helping others

When planning drama from texts, practitioners should:
1. Read the story multiple times before drama exploration
2. Identify key moments or questions for exploration
3. Choose age-appropriate drama conventions
4. Allow children to revisit and extend their dramatic work
5. Make connections to other areas of learning

## Assessment and Observation

### Observing Drama in EYFS

The EYFS Profile (2021) requires practitioners to make "best fit" judgements about children's learning. Drama activities provide rich opportunities for observation across multiple areas:

**Communication and Language:**
- Vocabulary use in role
- Listening and responding to others in dramatic play
- Following and giving instructions within play scenarios
- Using talk to organise and extend play narratives

**Personal, Social and Emotional Development:**
- Managing emotions within the safety of role
- Negotiating roles and rules with peers
- Showing sensitivity to others' feelings
- Demonstrating confidence in new situations

**Expressive Arts and Design:**
- Creating and developing narratives
- Taking on different characters
- Using imagination to transform objects
- Responding to stories through drama

Andy Kempe's work on assessment in drama emphasises that in early years, assessment should focus on process rather than product. Practitioners should document:
- The development of play narratives over time
- Increasing sophistication in role-taking
- Growing collaborative skills
- Language development within dramatic contexts

### Documentation Strategies

Effective documentation of drama in EYFS includes:
- Photographs of dramatic play with adult commentary
- Video recordings of significant dramatic moments
- Children's dictated accounts of their play
- Written observations noting language use and interactions
- Learning stories that capture extended play sequences

## Supporting All Learners

### SEND Considerations

Drama is an inherently inclusive pedagogy when thoughtfully implemented. For children with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities:

**Children with communication difficulties:**
- Drama provides context for language development
- Physical and visual elements support understanding
- Repetition within play scenarios reinforces language
- Low-pressure environment for communication attempts

**Children with social communication differences (including autism):**
- Structured drama activities provide clear frameworks for interaction
- Role play areas offer predictable social scenarios to practice
- Visual supports (props, costumes) clarify expectations
- Drama allows exploration of social situations with adult support

**Children with physical disabilities:**
- Adapt drama activities to ensure full participation
- Use inclusive approaches (e.g., everyone sitting for freeze frames)
- Provide alternative ways to contribute (sounds, ideas, directions)
- Ensure role play areas are physically accessible

Bolton emphasises that drama should be about "making meaning" rather than "making theatre," ensuring that all children can participate meaningfully regardless of ability.

### English as an Additional Language (EAL)

Drama is particularly powerful for supporting children learning English as an Additional Language:
- Physical and contextual clues support comprehension
- Repetitive language within play scenarios aids acquisition
- Low-anxiety environment for language experimentation
- Opportunities to hear and use English in meaningful contexts
- Cultural stories and role play can celebrate home languages and cultures

## Practitioner Development

### Confidence in Using Drama

Many early years practitioners report low confidence in using drama (Walsh-Bowers & Basso, 1999). To develop practitioner expertise:

**Initial Steps:**
- Begin with story dramatisation of familiar tales
- Use simple conventions (freeze frames, soundscaping)
- Observe children's spontaneous dramatic play and build from it
- Work alongside more confident colleagues
- Attend professional development sessions on drama in early years

**Developing Practice:**
- Experiment with teacher in role in low-risk situations
- Plan collaborative drama experiences with colleagues
- Reflect on what works and why
- Read professional literature (journals such as "Early Years Educator")
- Join networks such as Drama UK or National Drama

Neelands' "Structuring Drama Work" (2010) provides accessible conventions suitable for EYFS, though practitioners must adapt these for developmental appropriateness.

## Links to Key Stage 1 and Beyond

### Transitions and Progression

Drama experiences in EYFS provide essential foundations for the National Curriculum at Key Stage 1. The English programmes of study for Key Stage 1 state that pupils should "participate in role play and improvisations" (DfE, 2013).

**Progression from EYFS to KS1:**
- From spontaneous dramatic play → structured improvisation
- From adult-supported role work → more independent role-taking
- From exploring familiar situations → creating original scenarios
- From physical responses to stories → scripted drama experiences

**Continuity principles:**
- Year 1 teachers should continue to value process over performance
- Child-initiated drama should remain part of provision
- Drama should continue to be integrated across the curriculum
- Playful approaches should continue into Key Stage 1

Dorothy Heathcote's concept of "dramatic playing" versus "playing dramatically" is useful here. EYFS focuses on dramatic playing (using drama to explore and understand), which gradually develops into playing dramatically (consciously crafting drama for effect) in later key stages.

## Research and Evidence Base

### Impact of Drama in Early Years

Research evidence demonstrates significant benefits of drama in early years:

**Language Development:**
A longitudinal study by the Education Endowment Foundation (EEF) exploring the impact of arts education found that drama activities in early years positively impact oral language development, particularly for disadvantaged children (EEF, 2019).

**Social and Emotional Development:**
Research by Dunn and Cutting (2006) in the British Journal of Developmental Psychology found that pretend play, including dramatic role play, significantly predicts later social understanding and emotion regulation.

**Cognitive Development:**
Vygotsky's (1978) seminal work on play emphasises that dramatic play creates a "zone of proximal development" where children perform "a head taller than themselves," demonstrating capabilities beyond their everyday functioning.

**School Readiness:**
The Effective Provision of Pre-School Education (EPPE) project (Sylva et al., 2004) identified high-quality dramatic play provision as a characteristic of excellent early years settings that promote school readiness.

## Conclusion

Drama in EYFS is not an additional subject to fit into an already crowded curriculum, but rather a pedagogical approach that enhances learning across all areas of the EYFS framework. By recognising and extending children's natural dramatic play, practitioners create powerful contexts for language development, social learning, emotional understanding, and creative thinking.

The work of key drama educators – Heathcote's teacher in role, Bolton's emphasis on living through experiences, O'Neill's process drama, Neelands' drama conventions, and Kempe's accessible approaches – all find practical application in early years settings when adapted appropriately for very young children.

Effective drama in EYFS is characterised by:
- Child-initiated play supported by thoughtful adult involvement
- Process-focused exploration rather than performance outcomes
- Integration with all areas of learning and development
- Inclusive practice that celebrates all children's contributions
- Documentation that captures the richness of dramatic play
- Practitioners who are willing to play,
