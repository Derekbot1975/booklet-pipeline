# Drama at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview and Curriculum Framework

Key Stage 5 Drama represents the apex of secondary drama education in the UK, typically studied by students aged 16-18 pursuing A Level qualifications. Unlike Key Stages 1-4, KS5 Drama is not governed by the National Curriculum but by individual awarding bodies: AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. Each specification has been designed to meet Ofqual's requirements for GCE qualifications and builds upon skills developed in GCSE Drama whilst introducing higher-level theoretical, analytical, and practical competencies.

The A Level specifications share common aims: developing students as informed, critical practitioners who can create, perform, and analyse drama with sophistication. Students engage with theatre as an art form, exploring its social, cultural, historical, and political contexts whilst developing their own artistic voice.

## Key Components Across Exam Boards

### Practical Performance

All specifications require substantial practical work, typically comprising 40-50% of the final assessment. Students must demonstrate performance or design skills in two distinct contexts:

**Devising Theatre** (AQA Component 1, Edexcel Component 1, OCR Component 1, WJEC Component 1): Students create original theatre pieces, often working collaboratively in small groups. This process embodies Cecily O'Neill's concept of 'process drama', where meaning-making occurs through structured improvisation and experimentation. Students must document their creative journey through working notebooks or portfolios, demonstrating how they have researched, explored, developed, and refined their work. The devising process typically requires engagement with a practitioner, text, or stimulus, encouraging students to explore theatrical styles ranging from naturalism to physical theatre.

**Text-Based Performance** (examined or visiting examiner assessment): Students perform extracts from published plays, demonstrating their understanding of character, style, genre, and directorial interpretation. This component connects to Dorothy Heathcote's emphasis on 'making meaning' through embodied practice, requiring students to analyse texts as both literary works and blueprints for live performance.

### Written Examination

The written components (typically 40% of qualification) assess students' ability to analyse and evaluate live theatre, demonstrating knowledge of theatrical conventions, practitioners, and social-historical contexts.

**Theatre Analysis**: Students must see and analyse live professional productions, applying critical vocabulary and theoretical frameworks. This requirement recognises Gavin Bolton's assertion that drama education must connect classroom practice with the wider theatrical landscape. Students develop as informed audience members, considering how directors, designers, and performers communicate meaning through theatrical choices.

**Textual Analysis**: Students study set texts as performance texts rather than literary works, considering multiple interpretative possibilities. Jonothan Neelands' concept of 'conventions' becomes crucial here, as students must understand how theatrical devices create meaning for audiences. Questions typically require students to outline how they would direct or design specific scenes, demonstrating practical understanding alongside theoretical knowledge.

## Practitioner Study

A distinguishing feature of A Level Drama is the requirement to study theatrical practitioners in depth. Each specification mandates engagement with influential figures such as:

- **Konstantin Stanislavski**: Naturalistic acting methodology, psychological realism
- **Bertolt Brecht**: Epic theatre, alienation effect, political theatre
- **Antonin Artaud**: Theatre of Cruelty, visceral performance
- **Jerzy Grotowski**: Poor Theatre, actor training
- **Steven Berkoff**: Physical theatre, Total Theatre
- **Frantic Assembly**: Contemporary physical theatre, collaborative devising
- **Kneehigh Theatre**: storytelling, playful theatricality
- **Complicité**: visual theatre, ensemble work

Students must understand how practitioners' philosophies translate into practical application. Andy Kempe's work on 'Drama Schemes, Themes and Dreams' (2000) emphasises that practitioner study should be experiential: students must work practically in the style of their chosen practitioner, not merely study them theoretically.

## Exam Board Specifications

### AQA Drama and Theatre (7262)

**Component 1: Devising (40%)**: Students create original theatre influenced by a practitioner, working notebook (60 marks) and practical performance (20 marks).

**Component 2: Text in Performance (20%)**: Monologue or duologue performance plus group performance of full text extract.

**Component 3: Theatre Makers in Practice (40%)**: Written examination including live theatre analysis and directorial interpretation of texts. Students study one complete performance text and one complete text from a different historical period.

### Edexcel Drama and Theatre (9DR0)

**Component 1: Devising (40%)**: Portfolio documenting process and final performance, influenced by specified practitioner.

**Component 2: Text in Performance (20%)**: Performance of two extracts from one play (monologue/duologue and group piece).

**Component 3: Theatre Makers in Practice (40%)**: Written examination requiring analysis of one complete performance text and live theatre evaluation.

### OCR Drama and Theatre (H459)

**Component 1: Drama and Theatre (40%)**: Written examination including practical exploration of texts and live theatre analysis.

**Component 2: Creating Original Drama (30%)**: Devised performance with accompanying reflective report.

**Component 3: Drama Performance (30%)**: Performance of extract from published play, with separate designer option available.

### WJEC Eduqas Drama and Theatre (A680QS)

**Component 1: Theatre Workshop (40%)**: Reinterpretation of extract from text in two different styles, with creative log.

**Component 2: Text in Action (40%)**: Written examination including analysis of two complete texts and live theatre review.

**Component 3: Text in Performance (20%)**: External assessment of group performance from complete text.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Process-Led Learning

Drawing on the legacy of Dorothy Heathcote and Gavin Bolton, effective A Level Drama teaching balances process and product. Bolton's distinction between 'drama for understanding' and 'theatre for communication' (1984) remains relevant: students must experience authentic creative processes whilst developing skills to communicate effectively with audiences.

Workshop-based exploration should precede performance preparation. When studying Brecht, for instance, students might experiment with gestus, placards, and direct address before applying these devices to assessed work. This experiential approach, advocated by Neelands and Goode in 'Structuring Drama Work' (2015, 3rd edition), ensures students develop embodied understanding rather than superficial imitation.

### Critical Reflection

A Level students must develop metacognitive awareness of their practice. Working notebooks and portfolios should document not merely what students created, but why they made specific choices and how these evolved. This reflective practice connects to Cecily O'Neill's concept of 'dramatic inquiry', where learning emerges through questioning and refinement.

Teachers should establish regular structured reflection opportunities using frameworks such as:
- What? So what? Now what?
- Description → Analysis → Evaluation → Synthesis
- IDEA (Identify, Discuss, Evaluate, Apply)

### Live Theatre as Core Text

All specifications require students to see multiple live performances. This is non-negotiable and reflects the fundamental truth that drama is a live art form. Teachers must programme theatre visits strategically, selecting productions that complement curriculum texts, practitioners, or styles.

The theatre analysis component requires students to evaluate directorial interpretation, design elements, and performance skills with sophistication. Andy Kempe and Marigold Ashwell's 'Progression in Secondary Drama' (2000) emphasises that students should attend performances with clear analytical frameworks, using specialist vocabulary to articulate how meaning is constructed through theatrical choices.

## Assessment Requirements and Standards

### Performance Assessment Criteria

Across all boards, practical performance is assessed against criteria including:

- **Communication with audience**: clarity, engagement, presence
- **Realisation of artistic intentions**: coherence, creativity, appropriateness
- **Technical control**: vocal and physical skills, spatial awareness
- **Understanding of style/genre**: appropriate conventions and techniques
- **Collaborative skills** (where applicable): ensemble work, responsiveness

Teachers must provide students with board-specific assessment criteria and exemplar materials. Moderation processes ensure national standards are maintained, with visiting examiners or recorded performances subject to external marking.

### Portfolio/Working Notebook Requirements

Written accompaniments to devised performances must demonstrate:

- Research and exploration of stimulus materials and practitioner influence
- Development of ideas through experimentation and refinement
- Analysis of creative decisions using specialist terminology
- Evaluation of outcomes with reference to artistic intentions
- Understanding of dramatic elements: form, structure, language, space, time

Portfolios typically range from 2,500-4,000 words depending on specification. These documents are not retrospective essays but contemporaneous records of creative process, including annotations, diagrams, and images alongside written reflection.

## Set Texts and Literary Canon

A Level specifications feature diverse repertoire spanning historical periods and theatrical movements:

**Classical/Renaissance**: Greek tragedy (Sophocles, Euripides), Shakespeare, Marlowe, Webster

**Restoration/18th-19th Century**: Congreve, Sheridan, Wilde, Ibsen, Chekhov

**20th Century**: Brecht, Miller, Williams, Beckett, Pinter, Churchill, Wesker

**Contemporary**: Kane, Stephens, Ravenhill, Walsh, Bean, Eldridge

Students must understand texts as products of their social-historical contexts whilst exploring contemporary relevance. Neelands' concept of 'making theatre' emphasises that texts are starting points for creative interpretation, not fixed entities to be faithfully reproduced.

## Supporting Diverse Learners

### Differentiation Strategies

A Level Drama attracts students with varied strengths: some excel in performance whilst others demonstrate strong analytical or design capabilities. Specifications accommodate this diversity through:

- **Performance/Design pathways**: OCR and some other boards allow students to specialise in design rather than performance
- **Solo/Group work balance**: Students can showcase individual artistry in monologues whilst developing collaborative skills in ensemble pieces
- **Multiple intelligences**: Assessment encompasses kinaesthetic, linguistic, interpersonal, and intrapersonal domains

Teachers should employ Kempe and Ashwell's 'layered objectives' approach, where tasks contain core requirements accessible to all, with extension opportunities for high-attaining students.

### Inclusive Practice

Drama's collaborative nature and emphasis on multiple modes of communication makes it inherently accessible. However, teachers must consider:

- **Physical access**: Adaptations for students with mobility impairments; design pathways as alternative to performance
- **Neurodiversity**: Clear structures, visual supports, and predictable routines benefit students with autism or ADHD; practical work provides alternative to written assessments
- **Confidence building**: Heathcote's 'teacher in role' technique, where teacher works alongside students within dramatic frame, reduces performance anxiety

## Transition and Progression

### From GCSE to A Level

Students experience significant step-up in expectations:

- **Theoretical sophistication**: Deeper engagement with practitioners, requiring independent research beyond taught content
- **Analytical complexity**: Live theatre analysis demands nuanced evaluation using specialist vocabulary
- **Creative independence**: Less scaffolding in devising; students must generate and develop original concepts
- **Portfolio rigour**: Extended written reflection with academic conventions

Teachers should explicitly teach study skills including: effective note-making, time management, independent research, and academic writing. Early intervention in Year 12 establishes strong foundations.

### Beyond A Level

A Level Drama prepares students for diverse progression routes:

**Higher Education**: Drama/Theatre Studies degrees, Performing Arts conservatoires, English Literature, Creative Arts

**Vocational Training**: Drama schools (RADA, Central, Guildhall), physical theatre companies, technical theatre

**Transferable Skills**: Collaboration, creativity, confidence, critical thinking, communication—valued across all sectors

Bolton's assertion that drama develops 'life skills' alongside subject-specific competencies remains pertinent. A Level Drama produces articulate, empathetic, culturally literate individuals capable of working collaboratively and thinking critically.

## Quality Assurance and Professional Development

### Effective Teaching Practice

Research by the National Drama Teachers Association and educational practitioners emphasises:

- **Regular practical work**: Minimum two practical sessions per week maintaining physical skills
- **Workshop before performance**: Extensive exploration precedes polished performance work
- **Integrated theory-practice**: Practitioner study occurs through doing, not abstract study
- **Professional theatre engagement**: Regular theatre attendance with structured post-show discussion
- **Peer assessment**: Developing critical vocabulary through giving and receiving feedback

### Teacher Development Resources

- **National Drama** (formerly National Drama): Professional association providing CPD, publications, and networking
- **Exam Board Resources**: Training events, specimen materials, examiners' reports
- **Drama UK**: Advocacy organisation connecting education with professional theatre
- **Subject-Specific Publications**: 'Drama' magazine, 'Teaching Drama' journal

Neelands' work emphasises that drama teachers must be reflective practitioners, continuously developing their own understanding through engagement with professional theatre, current research, and pedagogical innovation.

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### Cultural Diversity and Decolonisation

Contemporary discourse questions the Eurocentric canon dominating A Level specifications. Teachers and exam boards increasingly recognise need to include:

- Plays by Global Majority writers (Kwame Kwei-Armah, debbie tucker green, Roy Williams)
- Non-Western theatrical traditions (Noh, Kathakali, African storytelling)
- Diverse practitioner voices beyond white European males

This reflects broader educational imperative to provide culturally responsive curriculum relevant to diverse student populations.

### Digital Theatre and Technology

Post-pandemic education recognises digital platforms as legitimate theatrical spaces. National Theatre Live, Digital Theatre+, and livestreamed performances expand access but raise questions about liveness and theatrical experience. Specifications increasingly acknowledge hybrid forms whilst maintaining commitment to live theatre attendance.

### Assessment Validity

Ongoing debate considers whether current assessment models adequately capture drama's collaborative, process-oriented nature. Some practitioners argue for greater emphasis on formative assessment and portfolio evidence over summative performance snapshots.

## Conclusion

Drama at Key Stage 5 represents sophisticated arts education combining theoretical rigour with practical creativity. Building on foundations established in earlier key stages, it develops students as informed theatre-makers and culturally literate citizens. Effective provision requires teachers who are themselves passionate about theatre, committed to experiential learning, and reflective about pedagogical practice. When taught well, A Level Drama transforms students' capacity for creative thinking, collaborative working, and critical analysis—skills valuable far beyond the drama studio.
