# Drama Conventions and Strategies

## Introduction and Pedagogical Context

Drama conventions and strategies form the foundational toolkit of process drama pedagogy in the UK. These structured teaching approaches enable pupils to explore narratives, themes and human experiences through active, embodied learning. As Jonothan Neelands defines them, conventions are "agreed codes of practice that create and govern dramatic meaning" (Neelands and Goode, *Structuring Drama Work*, 2015). They provide the architectural framework through which teachers can construct meaningful dramatic encounters that develop understanding, empathy and critical thinking.

Dorothy Heathcote pioneered the pedagogical use of drama conventions through her development of 'Mantle of the Expert' and 'Teacher in Role' approaches in the 1970s and 1980s. Cecily O'Neill extended this work through her conception of 'process drama' as a distinct educational form, separate from theatre-making, in her seminal text *Drama Worlds* (1995). Gavin Bolton's research established the intellectual rigour of educational drama, positioning conventions as tools for creating what he termed "the dramatic universe" where fictional contexts enable genuine learning (*Acting in Classroom Drama*, 1998).

The National Curriculum for England (2014) requires that Drama teaching should enable pupils to "improvise, devise and script drama for one another and a range of audiences" whilst developing "spoken language through discussion and performance". Drama conventions provide the specific pedagogical mechanisms through which these statutory requirements can be achieved across Key Stages 1-4.

## Defining Drama Conventions versus Drama Strategies

### Conceptual Distinction

Whilst often used interchangeably in schools, Neelands makes an important pedagogical distinction between **conventions** and **strategies**:

**Conventions** are the theatrical devices and forms through which dramatic meaning is created and communicated. These include still image, thought-tracking, forum theatre, and role-play. They are the "language" of drama education.

**Strategies** refer to the broader pedagogical approaches teachers employ to structure learning sequences using conventions. These include 'Teacher in Role', 'Mantle of the Expert', and 'hot-seating'. Strategies orchestrate multiple conventions into coherent learning experiences.

Andy Kempe and Marigold Ashwell, in *Progression in Secondary Drama* (2000), describe conventions as "building blocks" whilst strategies represent the "architectural plan" that determines how those blocks are assembled for educational purpose.

## Core Drama Conventions

### Still Image (Tableau, Freeze Frame)

Still image involves participants creating frozen, physical representations of moments, ideas or themes. This convention, extensively documented in Neelands' work, enables multiple literacies: visual composition, spatial awareness, and symbolic representation.

**Pedagogical applications across Key Stages:**

- **KS1-2**: Creating images of story moments; representing emotions; simple tableau work
- **KS3**: Multi-layered images exploring themes; abstract concept representation; transition sequences
- **KS4**: Brechtian gestus; symbolic imagery; examination devising (AQA requires "use of space and levels")
- **KS5**: Theoretical application (Brecht's *Lehrstücke*; Boal's Image Theatre from *Theatre of the Oppressed*)

**Teaching implications**: Still image provides immediate access for all abilities, requires no specialist resources, and generates rich discussion material. Teachers should employ **thought-tracking** (see below) to deepen understanding of frozen images. Examination specifications across AQA, Edexcel and OCR Component 2 (Devising) frequently require students to demonstrate "use of physical theatre techniques" where still image features prominently.

### Thought-Tracking (Voices in the Head)

Participants speak aloud the internal thoughts of characters frozen in still images or during role-play. Bolton identified this convention as particularly powerful for developing perspective-taking and emotional literacy (*Drama as Education*, 1984).

Heathcote used thought-tracking extensively to slow down action and deepen reflection, creating what she termed "pressing moments" where significance could be examined. The convention enables pupils to:

- Articulate subtext and motivation
- Explore multiple perspectives simultaneously
- Develop inference and empathy skills
- Access character psychology without extended improvisation

**Cross-curricular applications**: Thought-tracking serves History (exploring historical perspectives), PSHE (understanding decision-making), and English (character analysis). The National Curriculum's speaking and listening requirements are directly addressed through structured thought-tracking activities.

### Teacher in Role (TiR)

Developed extensively by Heathcote, Teacher in Role involves the teacher adopting a fictional role within the drama to guide, challenge, and deepen learning. Bolton described TiR as "the most significant teaching innovation in drama education" (*Towards a Theory of Drama in Education*, 1979).

**Key functions of TiR (Heathcote and Bolton, 1995):**

1. **Information-giver**: Providing narrative context and stimulus
2. **Questioner**: Challenging pupils' thinking and assumptions
3. **Devil's advocate**: Creating productive tension
4. **Subservient role**: Empowering pupils through status reversal
5. **Authority figure**: Generating urgency and consequence

**Practical considerations**: Effective TiR requires clear signalling of role entry/exit, commitment to the fictional frame, and careful calibration of challenge. Teachers must balance being "in" the drama whilst maintaining pedagogical oversight. Kempe emphasises the importance of "low-status" roles that position pupils as experts, thereby maximising engagement and cognitive demand (*The GCSE Drama Coursebook*, 2001).

### Hot-Seating

A character (portrayed by teacher or pupil) is questioned by others, revealing information, motivation and perspective. This convention bridges drama and oracy development, directly supporting National Curriculum requirements for "articulating and justifying answers" and "speaking audibly and fluently".

**Progressive development:**

- **KS1-2**: Simple questioning of familiar characters (story-based)
- **KS3**: Complex interrogation exploring moral dilemmas; multiple hot-seats revealing different perspectives
- **KS4**: Examination preparation for Component 3 (AQA, Edexcel) where "understanding of character" must be demonstrated
- **KS5**: Stanislavskian character analysis; application of practitioner methodologies

### Forum Theatre

Derived from Augusto Boal's *Theatre of the Oppressed* (1979), forum theatre enables participants to stop action and intervene, offering alternative choices for characters facing dilemmas. Neelands adapted Boal's politically-oriented technique for educational contexts, emphasising its power for exploring consequence and agency.

**Educational applications**: PSHE scenarios (bullying, peer pressure), citizenship education (democratic participation), and safeguarding contexts. The convention embodies the 2014 National Curriculum's requirement for pupils to "develop their understanding of important issues".

The technique requires a "Joker" (facilitator) who manages interventions. In classroom contexts, teachers typically assume this role initially, gradually transferring responsibility to pupils as confidence develops.

### Role-on-the-Wall

A visual convention where the outline of a character is drawn (or projected), with factual information recorded outside the outline and emotional/psychological information inside. O'Neill describes this as a "shared text" that the group collectively constructs (*Drama Words*, 1995).

**Pedagogical benefits:**

- Provides visible, evolving record of character understanding
- Differentiates between objective facts and subjective interpretation
- Supports pupils with additional needs through visual scaffolding
- Creates reference material for subsequent drama work or written tasks

Role-on-the-Wall bridges drama and literacy, supporting English curriculum requirements for character analysis whilst maintaining active, collaborative approaches.

### Conscience Alley (Thought Tunnel)

Participants form two lines facing each other, creating an "alley" through which a character walks whilst hearing spoken thoughts representing conflicting perspectives or internal debate. Neelands identifies this convention as particularly effective for exploring moral dilemmas and decision-making (*Structuring Drama Work*, 2015).

**Curriculum connections**: Supports RE/Ethics (moral reasoning), English Literature (character motivation), PSHE (decision-making frameworks). The convention makes abstract ethical concepts concrete and experientially understood.

## Key Drama Strategies

### Mantle of the Expert

Heathcote's most sophisticated pedagogical strategy, Mantle of the Expert positions pupils as a "community of experts" responsible for tasks within a fictional enterprise. The teacher, often in role, frames tasks that require genuine curriculum learning whilst maintaining the fictional context.

**Defining characteristics (Heathcote and Bolton, *Drama for Learning*, 1995):**

- Pupils work in "commission" with real responsibility within fiction
- Tasks require authentic research, problem-solving and curriculum knowledge
- The fictional frame generates purpose and audience for work
- Learning occurs through needing to accomplish expert tasks

**Example structure**: A KS2 class becomes "museum curators" responsible for creating an exhibition about Ancient Egypt (History curriculum), requiring research, artefact analysis, and public communication skills.

**Practical implications**: Mantle requires sustained commitment (typically multi-lesson sequences), careful task design ensuring curriculum learning is essential for success within fiction, and teacher confidence in maintaining the fictional frame. The approach is particularly effective for engaging reluctant learners and developing intrinsic motivation.

### Collective Role and Whole Group Drama

Drawing on Heathcote's work, this strategy involves all participants sharing responsibility for role and narrative development. Unlike traditional improvisation where individuals play separate characters, collective role creates a "corporate character" where the group thinks and acts as one entity.

Bolton emphasised how collective role reduces performance anxiety whilst maximising participation (*Drama as Education*, 1984). The approach particularly suits:

- Pupils new to drama
- Groups with varied confidence levels
- Exploring abstract concepts or historical movements
- Creating ensemble pieces for examination devising

### Drama Loops and Ritual

Neelands and Goode describe rituals as "pattern[s] of formal action that carr[y] symbolic meaning" (*Structuring Drama Work*, 2015). Ritualistic conventions create rhythm, significance and theatrical power within educational drama.

**Applications include:**

- Creating ceremonies marking significant fictional events
- Establishing rhythmic patterns supporting narrative structure
- Developing ensemble cohesion through shared physical/vocal patterns
- Accessing mythic and archetypal dimensions of human experience

Drama loops—repeated sequences that can be entered at any point—provide structures for ensemble devising, particularly valuable for GCSE/A-Level Component 2 (Devising) across all examination boards.

## Progression in Drama Conventions

### Key Stage Progression Framework

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7):**
- Simple role-play and imaginative play structures
- Basic still images representing story moments
- Teacher in Role using clear, accessible characters
- Narrative-based conventions supporting story comprehension

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11):**
- Multiple conventions combined in sequences
- Thought-tracking revealing character perspective
- Hot-seating familiar and researched characters
- Conscience alley for simple dilemmas
- Introduction to Forum Theatre with teacher facilitation

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14):**
- Sophisticated combination of conventions creating complex meaning
- Pupil-led facilitation of conventions
- Abstract and symbolic use of still image
- Extended improvisation structured through conventions
- Documentary and verbatim techniques
- Understanding conventions as theatrical language

**Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16):**
- Conscious application of conventions for examination devising
- Theoretical understanding of practitioners' use of conventions (Brecht, Boal, Berkoff)
- Evaluative writing about convention effectiveness
- AQA Component 2 requires "analysis of how drama and theatre techniques were used"
- Edexcel Component 1 assesses "effective use of performance skills"

**Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18):**
- Critical engagement with convention theories
- Application of practitioner methodologies (Stanislavski's units and objectives, Brechtian Verfremdungseffekt)
- Original convention creation within devising
- Scholarly analysis of conventions in professional practice

## Research Evidence and Impact

### Cognitive and Social Development

Bolton's research established that drama conventions develop what Vygotsky termed the "zone of proximal development" by creating scaffolded fictional contexts where pupils can attempt more sophisticated thinking than in non-fictional contexts (*Acting in Classroom Drama*, 1998). The fictional frame provides safety for risk-taking and experimentation.

O'Neill's research demonstrated that process drama using conventions develops:

- Metacognitive awareness (thinking about thinking)
- Multiple perspective-taking
- Tolerance for ambiguity
- Symbolic and metaphorical thinking

### Literacy and Oracy Impact

Research by the National Literacy Trust (Baldwin, 2012) found that drama conventions significantly improve speaking and listening skills, particularly for disadvantaged pupils. Structured conventions like hot-seating and conscience alley provide frameworks that support articulate expression.

The Cambridge Primary Review (Alexander, 2010) identified dialogic teaching approaches, of which drama conventions are exemplary, as crucial for developing reasoning and communication. Still image combined with thought-tracking, for example, requires pupils to construct coherent spoken explanations of visual compositions, integrating multiple literacy skills.

### Emotional Literacy and PSHE

Heathcote argued that drama conventions create "productive tension" where pupils engage with challenging content at an appropriate emotional distance. The fictional frame enables exploration of difficult topics (loss, conflict, injustice) without personal vulnerability. This "metaxis" (Boal's term for existing simultaneously in reality and fiction) is psychologically protective whilst educationally powerful.

## Examination Specifications and Drama Conventions

### GCSE Level (Key Stage 4)

**AQA Drama (8261):**
- Component 1 (Understanding Drama): Requires knowledge of "how meaning is interpreted through theatrical conventions"
- Component 2 (Devising Drama): Students must "use drama strategies and conventions effectively"
- Assessment Objective 1 specifically examines "use of performance space and spatial relationships"

**Edexcel Drama (1DR0):**
- Component 1 (Devising): Requires "use of drama techniques and theatre conventions"
- Component 3 (Theatre Makers in Practice): Students analyse "how performance conventions create meaning"

**OCR Drama and Theatre (J316):**
- Component 2 (Devising Drama): Assessment criteria include "effective use of theatrical techniques"
- Specific reference to "physical theatre conventions" and "non-naturalistic techniques"

**WJEC Eduqas Drama (C690U):**
- Component 1 (Devising Theatre): Requires demonstration of "drama techniques and strategies"
- Welsh-medium specifications maintain identical convention requirements

### A-Level (Key Stage 5)

All A-Level specifications require sophisticated understanding of how practitioners employ conventions theoretically and practically. AQA A-Level Drama and Theatre (7262) Component 3 requires analysis of practitioners including Brecht (whose use of conventions like gestus, placards, and breaking the fourth wall must be understood theoretically and applied practically).

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning with Conventions

Neelands advocates the "dramatic playing" model where teachers select conventions that create appropriate aesthetic distance and cognitive challenge for specific learning objectives. Effective planning requires:

1. **Clear learning intentions**: What understanding should conventions reveal?
2. **Appropriate convention selection**: Which conventions best serve these intentions?
3. **Sequencing**: How do conventions build toward deeper understanding?
4. **Reflection structures**: How will learning be articulated and consolidated?

### Classroom Management Considerations

Drama conventions require particular management approaches:

- **Clear signals** for entering/exiting fiction (Kempe recommends consistent verbal and physical markers)
- **Established protocols** for discussion following convention work
- **Physical safety** parameters, particularly for dynamic conventions
- **Emotional safety** through contracts establishing respectful engagement

### Differentiation through Conventions

Conventions provide inherent differentiation opportunities:

- **Still image**: Non-verbal access for EAL pupils; visual-spatial learning styles
- **Thought-tracking**: Scaffolds articulation for less confident speakers through structured prompts
- **Role-on-the-Wall**: Visual reference supporting working memory challenges
- **Collective role**: Removes individual performance pressure whilst maintaining participation

### Assessment for Learning

Conventions generate rich formative assessment opportunities. Teachers can observe:

- Quality of symbolic representation in still images (abstract thinking)
- Sophistication of perspective-taking in thought-tracking (empathy development)
- Questioning depth in hot-seating (critical engagement)
- Collaborative effectiveness in ensemble conventions (social skills)

Bolton emphasised that assessment in drama should focus on the "quality of engagement" rather than theatrical polish (*Towards a Theory of Drama in Education*, 1979). Conventions make learning processes visible, enabling teachers to identify and address misconceptions immediately.

## Conclusion: The Contemporary Relevance of Drama Conventions

Drama conventions and strategies represent a refined pedagogical toolkit developed through decades of research and practice by Heathcote, Bolton, O'Neill, Neelands and others. They provide structured approaches to active learning that address National Curriculum requirements whilst developing capabilities beyond statutory minimums—empathy, critical thinking, collaborative problem-solving, and articulate communication.

In an educational landscape emphasising knowledge acquisition, drama conventions offer powerful structures for knowledge construction through embodied, social learning. They transform abstract concepts into experiential understanding whilst maintaining intellectual rigour.
