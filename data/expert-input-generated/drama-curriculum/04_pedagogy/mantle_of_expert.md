# Mantle of the Expert

## Overview and Origins

Mantle of the Expert (MoE) is a process drama approach developed by **Dorothy Heathcote** at Newcastle University during the 1980s. It represents one of the most sophisticated and educationally rigorous applications of drama as a learning medium. The approach positions students as expert consultants or specialists working collaboratively to solve authentic problems, thereby creating a powerful context for curriculum learning and personal development.

Heathcote, often referred to as the "mother of process drama", developed MoE as a refinement of her earlier "teacher in role" work. The methodology was extensively documented by **Luke Abbott**, Heathcote's research associate, and has been further developed by **Tim Taylor** through Mantle of the Expert.com. The approach is fundamentally different from traditional drama teaching because learning about drama is secondary to learning through drama, positioning it as a powerful cross-curricular pedagogy.

## Theoretical Framework

### Core Principles

The methodology rests on several interconnected principles that distinguish it from conventional educational approaches:

**Expert Positioning**: Students adopt the "mantle" or responsibility of experts rather than engaging in make-believe play. This creates what Heathcote termed "the productive tension" between the student's actual knowledge and the knowledge their expert role demands, generating authentic motivation for learning.

**Commission Structure**: Learning is organised around commissions or authentic tasks that the expert team must complete. These commissions create narrative coherence and purposeful activity, moving beyond isolated drama exercises toward sustained enquiry. As **Gavin Bolton** observed in *Acting in Classroom Drama* (1998), this structure provides "protection into learning" by creating psychological distance that allows students to engage with challenging material.

**Curriculum Integration**: MoE functions as a methodology for delivering National Curriculum content rather than existing as a discrete subject. The approach creates what Heathcote called "a living curriculum" where knowledge acquisition becomes functionally necessary rather than externally imposed.

### The Mantle Framework

The approach operates through what Taylor identifies as five key "frames":

1. **Drama Frame**: The fictional context and roles
2. **Curriculum Frame**: Subject content and skills being addressed
3. **Responsibility Frame**: The authentic tasks students must complete
4. **Language Frame**: Register, vocabulary and communication forms required
5. **Social Frame**: Collaborative working and decision-making processes

## Pedagogical Components

### Teacher in Role

Central to MoE is sophisticated use of teacher in role, where the educator adopts a carefully chosen position within the fiction. Unlike simple characterisation, this strategic role-taking serves specific pedagogical functions. **Jonothan Neelands**, in *Structuring Drama Work* (2000), identifies multiple possible teacher role positions including:

- **The Client**: Who commissions the expert team's work
- **The Messenger**: Who brings information or challenges
- ****The Press Officer**: Who requires experts to communicate their work
- **The Antagonist**: Who creates productive obstacles

The teacher role must be carefully calibrated to maintain students' expert status whilst introducing curriculum content, complications, and the need for deeper enquiry.

### Establishing the Fiction

MoE requires meticulous preparation of what Heathcote called "the enterprise". This involves:

**Context Building**: Creating a believable organisational structure (museum, consultancy firm, archaeological team, etc.) with authentic artefacts, documentation, and working spaces. This physical and conceptual environment signals the seriousness of the work.

**Contracts and Commissions**: Written documentation establishing the expert team's responsibilities. These texts model functional writing whilst creating authentic reading purposes, supporting **English National Curriculum** objectives around reading for purpose and formal writing genres.

**Slow Building of Belief**: Rather than immediate immersion, MoE typically begins with low-risk activities like examining credentials, discussing expertise, or preparing the workspace. This gradual entry allows students to inhabit their expert roles confidently.

### Inquiry and Curriculum Content

The commission structure creates what **Cecily O'Neill** describes in *Drama Worlds* (1995) as "productive constraints" that necessitate curriculum learning. For example:

- **History**: Victorian sewage engineers researching cholera outbreaks (linking to KS2 History - Victorian Britain)
- **Science**: Marine biologists investigating coral reef degradation (supporting KS3 Science - ecosystems and biodiversity)
- **Geography**: Urban planning consultants examining sustainable development (addressing KS3 Geography - human geography)
- **Mathematics**: Heritage site managers calculating conservation budgets (applying KS2/3 Mathematics - percentages, ratio, financial literacy)

The expert role demands research, application of subject knowledge, and creation of authentic outcomes (reports, presentations, exhibitions) that demonstrate understanding.

## Application Across Key Stages

### Early Years Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1

At EYFS and KS1, MoE must be carefully adapted to developmental capabilities. Young learners can successfully adopt simplified expert roles:

- **Museum Curators** preparing exhibitions about toys/homes/transport (History - changes within living memory)
- **Book Makers** creating texts for younger children (supporting EYFS Communication and Language; KS1 English - writing for purpose)
- **Park Rangers** caring for plants and animals (Science - identifying and naming plants/animals)

The teacher's role becomes particularly crucial at this stage, providing substantial scaffolding whilst maintaining the fiction that children's contributions are valued expertise.

### Key Stage 2

KS2 represents the optimal developmental stage for MoE, where students possess sufficient subject knowledge to contribute meaningfully whilst remaining highly engaged by the imaginative frame. Successful KS2 applications include:

- **Anglo-Saxon Historians** (supporting History - Britain's settlement by Anglo-Saxons and Scots)
- **Tudor Portraitists** examining power and propaganda (History - significant turning points in British history)
- **Rainforest Ecologists** (Geography - locational and place knowledge; Science - living things and habitats)

At this stage, MoE effectively addresses **National Curriculum English** requirements around formal speech, explanatory writing, and vocabulary development through the authentic language demands of expert roles.

### Key Stage 3

KS3 students benefit from more complex, sustained enquiries addressing sophisticated curriculum content:

- **Elizabethan Theatre Companies** examining Shakespeare's cultural context (English - Shakespeare study requirements)
- **Medieval Guild Members** exploring feudal systems (History - Medieval Britain)
- **Climate Scientists** analysing human/physical geography interactions (Geography - climate change)

The approach naturally supports **Drama National Curriculum** requirements around creating and performing devised work, evaluating live theatre, and understanding theatrical conventions, though it extends beyond these into genuine cross-curricular territory.

### Key Stage 4 and Beyond

Whilst MoE is less commonly applied at GCSE level, the methodology's principles inform devising work required by all major exam boards (**AQA Drama GCSE**, **Edexcel Drama GCSE**, **OCR Drama and Theatre GCSE**, **WJEC Eduqas Drama GCSE**). The approach to:

- Research-based devising (Component 2 across all specifications)
- Collaborative creation processes
- Working with stimuli and developing authentic contexts
- Creating believable characters with clear motivations

These directly draw upon MoE principles, even if the full methodology is not employed.

## Pedagogical Benefits and Research Evidence

### Engagement and Motivation

Research into MoE consistently demonstrates exceptional engagement across diverse learners. **Andy Kempe**, in *The GCSE Drama Coursebook* (2013) and other writings, notes how the expert frame creates what Heathcote termed "the dignity of the expert position", particularly benefiting students who struggle with conventional academic positioning.

Taylor's research (published through Mantle of the Expert.com) demonstrates sustained engagement over weeks or months, contrasting sharply with typical lesson-by-lesson curriculum delivery. The narrative coherence and authentic purpose create intrinsic motivation for learning.

### Language and Literacy Development

MoE creates exceptional opportunities for language development across modes:

**Oracy**: The expert role demands formal register, subject-specific vocabulary, persuasive speech, and collaborative discussion. This directly addresses **Spoken Language National Curriculum** requirements around formal presentations, Standard English, and giving well-structured explanations.

**Reading**: Authentic documents (commissions, research materials, historical sources) create genuine reading purposes. Students read to extract information they need, developing comprehension strategies organically.

**Writing**: Experts must produce reports, proposals, letters, explanatory texts, and documentation. These authentic writing purposes generate significantly more sophisticated outcomes than decontextualised literacy exercises.

Research by academics including **David Booth** and **Kathleen Gallagher** demonstrates measurable literacy gains, particularly for disadvantaged learners and EAL students who benefit from the contextualised language learning MoE provides.

### Inclusive Practice

MoE functions as inherently differentiated pedagogy. Within the expert team:

- Students contribute according to their capabilities
- Multiple intelligences are valued (visual, spatial, interpersonal alongside linguistic/logical)
- The collaborative structure provides peer scaffolding
- The teacher in role can subtly differentiate support

Students with SEND often flourish in MoE contexts because expertise can be demonstrated through diverse means, and the protected fictional frame reduces anxiety around "getting it wrong" in conventional academic terms.

### Thinking Skills and Problem-Solving

The commission structure necessitates higher-order thinking. Students must:

- Analyse problems and information
- Synthesise knowledge from multiple sources
- Evaluate options and make justified decisions
- Create original solutions and outcomes

This addresses **National Curriculum aims** around developing "responsible, competent, independent and creative" learners who can apply knowledge to solve authentic problems.

## Practical Implementation for Teachers

### Planning a Mantle of the Expert Sequence

Successful MoE requires substantially more preparation than conventional lessons but delivers learning across multiple curriculum areas over extended periods (typically 8-15 sessions):

**1. Identify Curriculum Opportunities**: Begin with curriculum content requiring deep understanding rather than surface coverage. MoE works optimally with material benefiting from multiple perspectives, enquiry, and application.

**2. Design the Commission**: Create an authentic scenario where expert knowledge is genuinely needed. The commission should be:
- Challenging enough to require sustained investigation
- Open-ended enough to allow student agency
- Connected to real-world contexts students can understand
- Capable of generating multiple curriculum connections

**3. Establish the Expert Team**: Decide what kind of experts students will become. Consider historical authenticity (Victorian engineers, Tudor advisors) versus contemporary roles (museum curators, documentary makers). The expert identity should be specific rather than generic.

**4. Prepare Documentation and Artefacts**: Create physical evidence of the enquiry: official letters, contracts, research materials, tools of the trade. These materials signal seriousness and provide concrete reference points.

**5. Plan Teacher Role(s)**: Determine your position within the fiction and how it will evolve. Consider multiple roles that might introduce complications, require accountability, or necessitate communication of learning.

**6. Structure the Narrative Arc**: Plan the sequence of activities building toward a culminating outcome (presentation, exhibition, report). Include moments of complication that deepen enquiry.

### Managing the Learning Space

MoE transforms classroom organisation:

**Physical Environment**: Designate the space as the expert workplace - studio, headquarters, laboratory. Even minimal adjustments (rearranged tables, signage, specific "office" areas) signal the fictional frame.

**Documentation**: Display working materials, records of decisions, research findings. This creates visible learning whilst maintaining the fiction that this is an authentic workspace.

**Time Management**: MoE sessions require extended timeframes (60-90 minutes minimum) to achieve depth. The methodology resists fragmentation into 20-minute activities.

### Assessment Within MoE

Assessment operates differently in MoE because learning outcomes span multiple curriculum areas:

**Formative Assessment**: Observe collaborative working, language use, problem-solving approaches, and application of knowledge. Use teacher in role to pose questions revealing understanding.

**Authentic Outcomes**: The commission naturally generates assessable products: written reports, presentations, created artefacts, performances. These demonstrate applied understanding more validly than conventional tests.

**Reflection**: Build reflection points where students step out of role to consider learning. Heathcote termed these "mantle reflections", distinct from drama reflection about performance quality.

### Common Challenges and Solutions

**Maintaining Belief**: If students disengage from the fiction, revisit documentation, introduce complications through teacher in role, or have the expert team face consequences of their decisions.

**Coverage Anxiety**: Teachers worry about "getting through" curriculum content. MoE requires trust that deep learning in authentic contexts creates more secure understanding than rapid coverage. Plan curriculum mapping across the sequence.

**Student Resistance**: Older students occasionally resist the imaginative frame. Emphasise the professional simulation aspect rather than "pretend play". Give students agency in shaping their expert organisation.

**Managing Open-Ended Enquiry**: Balance student agency with curriculum objectives by carefully designing the commission parameters and using teacher in role to introduce necessary information.

## Relationship to Drama as an Art Form

An important consideration for drama educators is MoE's relationship to drama as a discrete discipline. Bolton, in *Drama as Education* (1984), distinguishes between drama for learning (where drama is the medium) and drama for theatre understanding (where drama is the object). MoE firmly inhabits the former category.

This positioning creates both opportunities and tensions:

**Strengths**: MoE demonstrates drama's capacity as a learning medium, validates drama in cross-curricular and primary contexts, and develops transferable skills prized in **Drama National Curriculum** (collaboration, improvisation, understanding context).

**Limitations**: MoE rarely prioritises theatrical craft, performance skills, or aesthetic understanding. Students in MoE sequences aren't typically learning about stagecraft, dramatic form, or theatre history.

**Balance**: Drama departments must balance MoE (drama as learning medium) with work developing theatrical literacy and performance craft required for qualifications and appreciating drama as an art form. Both approaches are valuable; neither is complete alone.

Neelands, in various writings, advocates for drama education encompassing both dimensions - drama as artistic practice and drama as learning methodology - rather than artificial binary choices.

## Contemporary Developments and Resources

### Current Practice and Training

**Mantle of the Expert.com**: Tim Taylor's organisation provides extensive resources, case studies, and training. Their international conference showcases contemporary practice across educational contexts.

**International Primary Curriculum (IPC)**: Incorporates MoE principles within its cross-curricular units, demonstrating mainstream educational adoption.

**Research Networks**: Universities including Durham, Warwick, and Newcastle maintain research interests in process drama and MoE, producing ongoing evidence about pedagogical effectiveness.

### Integration with Other Methodologies

Contemporary practice often combines MoE with complementary approaches:

- **Process Drama** (O'Neill): MoE represents one form within broader process drama methodology
- **Drama Conventions** (Neelands & Goode): MoE sequences incorporate conventions like meetings, documentation, and ritual to deepen engagement
- **Inquiry-Based Learning**: MoE's questioning approach aligns with constructivist pedagogies emphasising student enquiry

### Digital Extensions

Contemporary MoE practice increasingly incorporates digital dimensions:

- Expert teams creating websites, blogs, or presentations
- Research using online archives and databases
- Communication through "authentic" emails or video calls
- Documentation through photography and film

These extensions enhance authenticity for contemporary students whilst developing digital literacy within meaningful contexts.

## Critical Perspectives

### Pedagogical Critiques

Some educators question aspects of MoE:

**Time Investment**: The extended commitment required may seem disproportionate when curriculum coverage pressures intensify. However, advocates argue deep learning in one area exceeds superficial coverage of many.

**Teacher Expertise Required**: MoE demands sophisticated facilitation, thinking on one's feet in role, and managing complex group dynamics. This represents significant professional development need.

**Assessment Clarity**: The diffuse, cross-curricular learning can challenge conventional assessment frameworks expecting discrete, measurable objectives.

### Theoretical Debates

Academic discussions consider:

**Authenticity Questions**: Can school-based fictional expertise ever be truly authentic? Critics suggest the premise contains inherent contradictions. Advocates respond that the "as if" frame creates psychological authenticity generating real learning.

**Power Dynamics**: Does the teacher in role maintain traditional authority under superficial redistribution? Careful practice ensures genuine student agency, but poor implementation can simply disguise teacher control.

**Subject Knowledge**: Can students genuinely develop disciplinary understanding through MoE, or does it risk shallow engagement with complex subjects? Quality practice ensures expert roles necessitate deep subject learning rather than replacing it with theatrical surface.

## Conclusion and Practical Implications

Mantle of the Expert represents one of the most sophisticated applications of drama as a learning medium. For UK drama educators, it offers:

- **Primary Settings**: A powerful vehicle for delivering cross-curricular National Curriculum content through engaging, memorable contexts
- **Secondary Drama**: An approach to devising work drawing upon research, collaboration, and authentic contexts
- **Whole-School Impact**: A methodology demonstrating drama's contribution beyond discrete subject provision

Successful implementation requires:

1. **Thorough Planning**: Substantial preparation of commissions, documentation, and curriculum mapping
2. **Skilled Facilitation**: Sophisticated use of teacher in role and responsive teaching
3. **Sustained Commitment**: Extended sequences rather than one-off lessons
4. **Clear Purpose**: Understanding when MoE serves learning objectives better than alternatives
