# Process Drama

## Definition and Core Principles

Process drama is a teaching methodology and dramatic form in which participants explore content, issues, themes and relationships through improvised action in role. Unlike conventional performance-based drama, process drama emphasises the journey of exploration rather than producing a polished product for an audience. The work exists in the present tense, with teacher and students working together to create a fictional world that is explored from within.

Cecily O'Neill, who coined the term in her seminal work *Drama Worlds: A Framework for Process Drama* (1995), defines it as "an improvised dramatic exploration in which both teacher and students together in role create an improvised world which is explored but never performed for an outside audience." This distinction between 'making' and 'performing' is fundamental to understanding process drama's pedagogical value.

The approach differs significantly from traditional script-based work or drama-as-performance. Gavin Bolton in *Acting in Classroom Drama* (1998) emphasises that process drama creates "living through" experiences where meaning is constructed collaboratively and knowledge is generated through dramatic inquiry rather than merely reproduced.

## Historical Development and Key Thinkers

### Dorothy Heathcote

Dorothy Heathcote (1926-2011) pioneered much of what became process drama through her development of "drama for learning" at Newcastle University. Her most significant contribution was the concept of "mantle of the expert" and "teacher in role." Heathcote demonstrated that drama could be a powerful medium for learning across the curriculum, not merely an arts subject. Her work, documented extensively by Gavin Bolton in *Dorothy Heathcote's Story: The Biography of a Remarkable Drama Teacher* (2003), showed how teachers could use dramatic tension and role to create authentic contexts for learning.

Heathcote's concept of the "rolling role" allowed teachers to shift between different functions within the drama, modelling commitment whilst maintaining pedagogical control. Her emphasis on "brothering" - working alongside students rather than directing them - fundamentally shaped process drama's collaborative ethos.

### Gavin Bolton

Gavin Bolton's theoretical articulation in *Towards a Theory of Drama in Education* (1979) provided intellectual rigour to Heathcote's practical innovations. Bolton distinguished between "drama for understanding" and "drama for theatre," arguing that educational drama's primary purpose was meaning-making rather than aesthetic performance. His concept of "dramatic playing" versus "performing" helped teachers understand how students could work authentically within fiction without the self-consciousness of acting.

Bolton's later work, particularly *Acting in Classroom Drama* (1998), explored how different levels of engagement operate within process drama, from simple role-play to complex symbolic action.

### Jonothan Neelands

Jonothan Neelands' *Structuring Drama Work* (1990, with Tony Goode, third edition 2015) remains the most widely used practical resource in UK drama teaching. Neelands categorised dramatic conventions into accessible structures, providing teachers with a "toolkit" for building process drama experiences. His taxonomy includes conventions such as "teacher in role," "hot-seating," "conscience alley," "forum theatre," and "still image," each serving different pedagogical functions.

Neelands' work at the University of Warwick emphasised drama's capacity for exploring citizenship, social issues and ethical dilemmas. His concept of "ensemble" and collaborative meaning-making directly influenced the National Curriculum for Drama.

### Cecily O'Neill

Cecily O'Neill's theoretical framework in *Drama Worlds* (1995) synthesised previous work into a coherent model. She identified key elements that distinguish process drama: pre-text (the starting stimulus), context (the fictional situation), roles, frame (the perspective from which the drama is viewed), and focus (the central tension or question). O'Neill emphasised how "dramatic tension" drives engagement and authentic investment in the fictional world.

Her concept of "process drama" as distinct from both theatre and simple role-play helped establish this approach as a sophisticated pedagogical methodology worthy of academic study.

### Andy Kempe

Andy Kempe's work, particularly *The GCSE Drama Coursebook* (with Lionel Warner, various editions) and *Drama Education and Special Needs* (with Marigold Ashwell, 2000), made process drama approaches accessible to secondary teachers and applicable to examination contexts. Kempe demonstrated how process drama strategies could develop the skills assessed in GCSE Drama, particularly improvisation, devising and character work.

## Relationship to the UK National Curriculum

### Key Stage 3 National Curriculum for Drama

The National Curriculum in England's Drama component (within Art and Design subjects) explicitly supports process drama approaches. It requires that pupils should be taught to:

- Improvise, devise and script drama for performance
- Use dramatic techniques to explore ideas and texts
- Work collaboratively to generate, develop and communicate ideas
- Develop theatrical skills and apply them in performance

Process drama directly addresses these requirements through its emphasis on improvisation, collaborative creation and exploration of themes and issues.

### Key Stage 1 and 2 Foundation

Whilst drama is not statutory at primary level, the *Curriculum for Primary Schools* encourages drama as a teaching methodology. Process drama supports literacy objectives by creating purposeful contexts for speaking and listening, reading and writing. Many primary schools use process drama to explore historical periods, moral dilemmas and story structures.

## Key Conventions and Structures

### Teacher in Role

Perhaps the most significant convention in process drama, teacher in role involves the educator stepping into the fictional world alongside students. This achieves multiple purposes: modelling commitment to the fiction, introducing information, creating tension, and challenging students' thinking from within the drama. The teacher may adopt various status positions - from authority figures to supplicants - to shape the dramatic inquiry.

Heathcote identified different functional uses of teacher in role, from "messenger" (bringing information) to "devil's advocate" (challenging assumptions). The key pedagogical advantage is that the teacher maintains control whilst appearing to relinquish it.

### Mantle of the Expert

Developed by Dorothy Heathcote, this approach positions students as experts within a fictional context (archaeologists, historians, scientists, community planners). This "mantle" creates responsibility and purpose, elevating students' engagement and the sophistication of their inquiry. Luke Abbott's *Mantle of the Expert: Setting Up the Dramatic Playing Company for Learning* (2014) provides contemporary applications of this approach.

The mantle creates what Heathcote called "productive constraints" - the responsibilities and pressures that generate authentic problem-solving and decision-making.

### Still Image/Tableau

Students create frozen images representing moments, relationships, or abstract concepts. This convention slows down the action, allowing analysis and interpretation. Neelands describes this as "making meaning visible" - abstract ideas become concrete and discussable.

### Thought Tracking

Participants speak aloud the internal thoughts of characters in role, often whilst in still image. This convention develops empathy and explores multiple perspectives on situations.

### Hot-Seating

A character (student or teacher in role) is questioned by others outside the fiction. This develops questioning skills, deepens characterisation and explores motivation and consequence.

### Conscience Alley

A character walks between two lines of students who speak their thoughts, advice or the character's internal debate. This explores decision-making and moral complexity.

### Forum Theatre

Derived from Augusto Boal's Theatre of the Oppressed but adapted for process drama, participants can stop the action to suggest alternative choices or outcomes, exploring consequences and possibilities.

## Pedagogical Framework

### Pre-text

O'Neill's concept of the "pre-text" refers to the initial stimulus that launches the drama. Effective pre-texts are ambiguous enough to invite interpretation but concrete enough to suggest action. Examples include: a mysterious letter, an artefact requiring investigation, a newspaper article, an image, or a reported event.

The pre-text creates what O'Neill calls the "dramatic world" - a coherent fictional context with its own rules, relationships and tensions.

### Building Belief

Process drama requires what Coleridge termed "willing suspension of disbelief." Teachers build belief through consistent use of conventions, attention to detail, and serious commitment to the fiction. Bolton emphasised that authenticity of engagement matters more than theatrical skill.

### Protection into Role

Heathcote recognised that students need "protection" to take risks within drama. This protection comes from the fiction itself - students can explore challenging ideas whilst understanding "this is not real." The role provides distance that paradoxically enables closer engagement with difficult content.

### Dramatic Tension

All effective process drama contains tension - the engine that drives inquiry and maintains engagement. Neelands identifies different types: tension of mystery (what will happen?), tension of task (can we achieve this?), tension of relationship (how will people react?), and tension of time (pressure of deadline).

### Reflection

Critical to process drama's educational value is structured reflection both within and outside the fiction. Reflection transforms experience into learning. This may occur through discussion, writing in role, or further dramatic exploration.

## Applications Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

Process drama at this level often uses simple narratives and familiar contexts. Teachers might use stories as pre-texts, with the class helping characters solve problems. For example, exploring the Three Little Pigs from the perspective of building inspectors creates purposeful language use and problem-solving.

Role typically involves whole-class collective roles (we are all helpers, we are all villagers) with teacher in role guiding the exploration.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Process drama becomes more sophisticated, exploring historical periods, moral dilemmas and community issues. Students can sustain more complex roles and understand layered fictional contexts.

Cross-curricular applications flourish at this stage - history topics become immersive inquiries, science problems are explored through expert roles, and citizenship issues are examined through multiple perspectives.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum's emphasis on exploring themes and issues through drama directly supports process drama. Students develop meta-awareness, understanding how dramatic conventions function and selecting appropriate strategies for their own devised work.

Process drama explores increasingly complex ethical and social issues: prejudice, power, identity, justice. The fictional frame allows safe exploration of challenging content.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE, Ages 14-16)

Process drama underpins much devising work in GCSE Drama specifications. All major exam boards require devising skills:

**AQA GCSE Drama (8261)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) assesses students' ability to create and develop ideas collaboratively. Process drama conventions provide structures for this work.

**Edexcel GCSE Drama (1DR0)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) similarly requires collaborative creation. The specification explicitly references "dramatic conventions and techniques" including many process drama strategies.

**OCR GCSE Drama (J316)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) emphasises exploration of stimulus material through practical experimentation - precisely process drama's methodology.

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Drama (C690U)**: Component 1 (Devising Theatre) requires documented creative processes, evidencing how process drama conventions generate and develop material.

### Key Stage 5 (A-Level, Ages 16-18)

A-Level specifications emphasise theoretical understanding and sophisticated devising. Process drama provides both methodology and content for exploration:

**AQA A-Level Drama and Theatre (7262)**: Component 2 (Creating Original Drama) values process and collaborative creation. Students must document their explorative strategies.

**Edexcel A-Level Drama and Theatre (9DR0)**: Component 2 (Theatre Makers in Practice) requires centres to choose between devising or performing published text, with process drama supporting both pathways.

**OCR A-Level Drama and Theatre (H459)**: Component 2 (Devising Drama) explicitly requires "explorative strategies" documentation - process drama's conventions and reflective practice directly address this.

**WJEC Eduqas A-Level Drama and Theatre (A690U)**: Component 2 requires creation of original work with documented process, rewarding sophisticated use of dramatic strategies.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning Process Drama

Effective process drama requires careful planning whilst maintaining flexibility for student agency. Teachers should consider:

1. **Clear learning objectives**: What understanding, skills or knowledge will this drama develop?
2. **Appropriate pre-text**: What stimulus will engage students and suggest action?
3. **Tension and challenge**: What problem or question will sustain engagement?
4. **Convention selection**: Which conventions will best serve the learning objectives?
5. **Teacher role**: How will teacher in role shape the inquiry?
6. **Reflection opportunities**: When and how will students step back to analyse and articulate learning?

Neelands and Goode's *Structuring Drama Work* (2015) provides detailed planning frameworks and examples.

### Classroom Management Considerations

Process drama can appear chaotic to observers unfamiliar with its methodology. Teachers establish clear protocols:

- **Signals for entering/exiting role**: Consistent cues (light changes, positioning, verbal signals) distinguish fiction from reality
- **Establishing ground rules**: Safe participation requires mutual respect and commitment
- **Building gradually**: Complex process dramas develop over multiple sessions; initial work establishes conventions and trust
- **Physical space**: Flexible space allows different configurations; however, skilled teachers achieve powerful work in conventional classrooms

### Assessment in Process Drama

Since process drama emphasises journey over product, assessment focuses on:

- **Engagement and commitment**: Willingness to take risks within role
- **Collaborative skills**: Contributing ideas, building on others' suggestions, negotiating meaning
- **Understanding of conventions**: Appropriate use of dramatic strategies
- **Depth of exploration**: Moving beyond obvious responses to nuanced understanding
- **Reflective capacity**: Articulating learning and making connections

GCSE and A-Level specifications provide detailed assessment criteria for devising work rooted in process drama approaches.

### Common Pitfalls and Solutions

**Over-direction**: Teachers controlling every moment undermines student agency. Solution: Plan key moments but allow space for genuine inquiry.

**Lack of tension**: Without authentic challenge, engagement dissipates. Solution: Build genuine problems requiring difficult decisions.

**Breaking belief**: Inconsistent commitment to fiction destroys the dramatic world. Solution: Model absolute commitment; address disruption through in-role strategies where possible.

**Unclear objectives**: Drama as mere activity without learning purpose. Solution: Ensure clear connection between dramatic exploration and intended learning.

## Research Evidence and Impact

### Academic Research

John O'Toole's *The Process of Drama* (1992) provides theoretical analysis of how process drama generates meaning. His concept of "the dramatic context" explains how participants negotiate between the real context (classroom), the dramatic context (fictional world), and the theatrical context (awareness of form).

Kathleen Gallagher's research at the University of Toronto, particularly *The Theatre of Urban* (2007), demonstrates process drama's capacity for exploring complex social issues with diverse student populations.

Michael Fleming's *Starting Drama Teaching* (2011, fourth edition) synthesises research evidence supporting drama's educational value, emphasising process drama's unique contributions to speaking and listening, collaborative problem-solving, and empathy development.

### UK-Specific Evidence

The National Drama/London Drama (now part of National Theatre Learning) published extensive case studies demonstrating process drama's impact on literacy, particularly for boys and reluctant writers. Creating purposeful contexts for writing through drama significantly improved engagement and quality.

Research by the Royal Shakespeare Company's education department shows that process drama approaches to exploring Shakespeare texts improved comprehension and engagement compared to traditional teaching methods.

Ofsted reports consistently identify high-quality drama teaching as characterised by active, exploratory learning - essentially describing process drama pedagogy. The *Making a Mark* report (2009) highlighted drama's contribution to personal development and cultural understanding.

### Cognitive and Social Benefits

Process drama's collaborative nature develops what the OECD identifies as "21st-century skills": collaboration, communication, critical thinking and creativity. The methodology requires continuous negotiation, perspective-taking and problem-solving.

Research in embodied cognition supports process drama's effectiveness - physically enacting ideas and emotions enhances understanding and retention. Psychologist Jerome Bruner's work on narrative intelligence aligns with process drama's use of story structures to make meaning.

## Contemporary Debates and Developments

### Applied Theatre Connections

Process drama shares methodology with applied theatre practices: drama in education overlaps with drama in health, community drama, and theatre for development. James Thompson's *Applied Theatre* (2003) explores these connections.

### Digital and Hybrid Applications

Contemporary practitioners explore process drama in digital spaces. Zoom and Teams enable synchronous process drama with geographically dispersed participants, though requiring adaptation of spatial conventions. Some practitioners experiment with hybrid approaches combining physical and digital presence.

### Inclusive Practice

Andy Kempe and Marigold Ashwell's *Drama Education and Special Needs* (2000) demonstrates process drama's accessibility for students with SEND. The methodology's emphasis on diverse entry points (visual, kinaesthetic, verbal) supports differentiation. The fictional frame provides protection for students anxious about personal exposure.

### Decolonising Process Drama

Contemporary critical perspectives question process drama's Western, individualistic assumptions. Educators explore how process drama might honour collective cultural traditions and non-Western dramatic forms. This involves recognising that Heathcote's and O'Neill's frameworks emerged from specific cultural contexts and may require adaptation for diverse communities.
