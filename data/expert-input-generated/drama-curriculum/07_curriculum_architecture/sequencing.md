# Sequencing Drama Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Drama Curriculum Sequencing

Sequencing drama effectively across Key Stages 1-5 requires careful consideration of both progressive skill development and deepening conceptual understanding. The National Curriculum for England (2013) positions drama within English at KS1-3, whilst at KS4-5 it becomes a distinct qualification pathway. This knowledge file examines how to construct a coherent drama curriculum that builds systematically from early years through to post-16 study.

Dorothy Heathcote's concept of 'signing' – where students learn to read and create meaning through dramatic form – provides a fundamental principle for sequencing: students must first experience drama before they can analyse and evaluate it. This experiential-to-analytical progression underpins effective curriculum architecture.

## Foundational Principles for Sequencing

### Progressive Skill Development

Gavin Bolton's work in *Drama as Education* (1984) emphasises that drama learning moves from implicit understanding through practical experience toward explicit knowledge. A well-sequenced curriculum therefore structures encounters with dramatic elements in order of complexity:

**Physical skills progression**: Control of body in space → use of gesture and movement → physical characterisation → ensemble physicality → stylised movement vocabulary

**Vocal skills progression**: Volume control and clarity → tone and pitch variation → accent and dialect → vocal characterisation → voice in performance styles

**Cognitive skills progression**: Understanding role → maintaining role → developing role → analysing character motivation → applying performance theory

### Conceptual Development Through Key Stages

Cecily O'Neill's work on process drama and dramatic inquiry suggests that students' capacity for sustained dramatic exploration develops alongside their cognitive maturity. Sequencing must account for:

- **Concrete to abstract**: Moving from everyday situations (KS1-2) to metaphorical and symbolic drama (KS3-4)
- **Personal to universal**: Beginning with students' own experiences before exploring distant contexts and themes
- **Simple to complex narratives**: Single-scene work progressing to multi-layered, non-linear structures

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Core Focus: Drama as Imaginative Play

At KS1, drama work builds upon natural play instincts. The National Curriculum requires that students participate in 'performances, plays, role play and improvisations'. Sequencing should prioritise:

**Foundation experiences**:
- Teacher-in-role with clearly defined fictional contexts
- Simple whole-class drama structures (magic carpet journeys, meetings with characters)
- Movement and sound-making activities
- Basic understanding of 'pretending together'

**Progressive elements**:
1. Following simple dramatic conventions (freeze-frame, thought-tracking)
2. Contributing ideas within whole-class drama
3. Sustaining belief in role for short periods
4. Using voice and body to create simple characters

### Practical Implications

Teachers should establish 'drama rules' that distinguish dramatic behaviour from everyday behaviour. Jonothan Neelands' conventions provide accessible structures: begin with 'teacher-in-role' and 'still image' before introducing more complex conventions requiring sustained concentration.

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Core Focus: Developing Dramatic Literacy

KS2 represents a significant expansion in students' capacity for dramatic exploration. Sequencing should develop both practical skills and critical vocabulary.

**Year 3-4 focus**:
- Extended role-play scenarios with defined characters
- Introduction to basic dramatic tension and conflict
- Simple script work and performance for audience
- Understanding of drama vocabulary (character, scene, audience, performance)

**Year 5-6 focus**:
- Devising short original scenes using stimulus materials
- Exploring different theatrical styles (naturalistic vs non-naturalistic)
- Structured improvisations with clear objectives
- Beginning to evaluate dramatic work using specific criteria

### Building on Heathcote's Mantle of the Expert

Dorothy Heathcote's 'Mantle of the Expert' approach works particularly well in upper KS2, where students can sustain expert roles and engage with complex scenarios. Sequencing might progress:

1. Taking on roles with status and responsibility
2. Making decisions within fictional contexts
3. Researching to support role authenticity
4. Reflecting on learning achieved through drama

### Practical Implications

By Year 6, students should regularly use drama conventions independently. Andy Kempe's *The GCSE Drama Coursebook* (though written for KS4) identifies foundational skills that should be secure by the end of KS2: spatial awareness, voice projection, character gesture, and collaborative creation.

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### Core Focus: Establishing Theatrical Knowledge and Practice

KS3 is where drama can develop as a discrete subject, though it remains within English in the National Curriculum. This key stage establishes the foundation for GCSE study and must sequence systematically toward the demands of examination specifications.

### Year 7: Establishing Fundamentals

**First half-term priorities**:
- Establishing safe ensemble practice
- Understanding physical and vocal warm-ups
- Key conventions revisited and extended (still image, thought-tracking, forum theatre, hot-seating)
- Introduction to theatrical terminology

**Subsequent sequencing**:
- Exploring script extracts from practitioners (Stanislavski, Brecht)
- Devising work using more complex stimuli
- Understanding the role of director, designer, performer
- Introduction to theatre history (Greek theatre, Shakespeare)

### Year 8: Deepening Practice

Building on Year 7 foundations:

- Sustained character development across multiple sessions
- Script interpretation and performance
- Introduction to design elements (lighting, sound, set, costume)
- Exploring texts from diverse cultures and historical periods
- Understanding social and historical context in theatre

### Year 9: Pre-GCSE Bridge Year

This critical year must sequence learning toward GCSE requirements whilst maintaining breadth:

**Core Components**:
- Extended devising projects (3-4 weeks minimum)
- Detailed script study including annotating texts
- Introduction to practitioner approaches (Stanislavski, Brecht, Artaud, Berkoff)
- Understanding of evaluation and analysis vocabulary
- Regular performance opportunities with structured feedback

### Neelands' Structuring Drama Work

Jonothan Neelands' *Structuring Drama Work* (1990, revised 2000) provides essential frameworks for KS3 sequencing. His taxonomy of conventions should be introduced progressively:

1. **Context-building conventions** (Year 7): Still image, caption making, defining space
2. **Narrative conventions** (Year 7-8): Narration, forum theatre, flashback
3. **Poetic conventions** (Year 8-9): Ritual, symbolic objects, soundscape
4. **Reflective conventions** (Year 8-9): Conscience alley, hot-seating in depth, thought-tracking extended

### Practical Implications for KS3

Teachers must balance experiential learning with developing analytical skills. Bolton's concept of the 'dramatic paradigm' suggests that students should regularly move between doing, watching, and reflecting. A typical KS3 unit might sequence:

1. Practical exploration (2-3 lessons)
2. Performance/showing work (1 lesson)
3. Watching professional work (live or recorded)
4. Evaluating and connecting learning (1 lesson)

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16): GCSE Drama

### Core Focus: Qualification-Specific Sequencing

At KS4, sequencing must align with examination board specifications whilst maintaining artistic integrity. All major specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) share common components requiring careful sequencing.

### Component Structure Across Boards

**Practical components**:
- AQA: Component 1 (Understanding Drama – 40%) and Component 2 (Devising Drama – 40%)
- Edexcel: Component 1 (Devising – 40%) and Component 2 (Performance from Text – 20%)
- OCR: Component 1 (Devising – 30%) and Component 2 (Presenting and Performing Texts – 30%)
- WJEC Eduqas: Component 1 (Devising Theatre – 40%) and Component 2 (Performing from a Text – 20%)

**Written examination**:
All boards include written assessment (20-40%) covering live theatre evaluation and practitioner knowledge

### Year 10 Sequencing Strategy

**Autumn Term**:
- Establishing working methods and ensemble principles
- Introduction to all required practitioners (depth to follow)
- Baseline script work establishing performance skills
- First live theatre experience and evaluation practice

**Spring Term**:
- Begin devising component (if early entry strategy)
- OR continued skill development across devising and scripted work
- Deep dive into one practitioner approach
- Portfolio/written documentation skills

**Summer Term**:
- Complete devising component documentation
- OR second practitioner study
- Script exploration for performance component
- Second live theatre experience

### Year 11 Sequencing Strategy

**Autumn Term**:
- Complete devising work if not already finished
- Script rehearsal and development
- Intensive practitioner study for examination
- Written examination technique

**Spring Term**:
- Perform scripted work
- Live theatre evaluation practice
- Revision of key practitioners and terminology
- Mock examinations

**Summer Term** (pre-exam period):
- Final examination preparation
- Consolidation of theatrical knowledge

### Practitioner Sequencing

Andy Kempe and Marigold Ashwell's work emphasises that practitioner study must connect theory to practice. Suggested progression:

1. **Year 10**: Stanislavski (naturalism as foundation) → Brecht (contrast to naturalism)
2. **Year 11**: Additional practitioners specific to board (Artaud, Berkoff, Frantic Assembly, Kneehler, etc.)

Each practitioner study should follow this sequence:
- Historical/cultural context
- Key theoretical concepts
- Practical workshops exploring techniques
- Application to text or devising
- Analysis and evaluation using practitioner's terminology

### Practical Implications for KS4

Teachers must manage the tension between creative exploration and assessment requirements. Cecily O'Neill's concept of 'process drama' can still inform devising work, ensuring artistic depth whilst meeting criteria. Consider:

- **Portfolio deadlines**: Build in draft-review-redraft cycles
- **Performance pressure**: Schedule multiple 'showing' opportunities before final assessment
- **Written skills**: Integrate evaluation writing throughout, not just at examination period
- **Live theatre**: Sequence visits to align with schemes of work and examination requirements

## Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18): A-Level Drama and Theatre Studies

### Core Focus: Academic Rigour and Artistic Maturity

Post-16 drama study requires sophisticated sequencing that develops:
- Independent research and analysis
- Complex practical application
- Cultural and historical understanding
- Directorial and design thinking

### A-Level Specification Structures

**AQA Drama and Theatre Studies**:
- Component 1: Drama and Theatre (40% written examination)
- Component 2: Creating Original Drama (30% practical)
- Component 3: Making Theatre (30% practical)

**Edexcel Drama and Theatre**:
- Component 1: Devising (40%)
- Component 2: Text in Performance (20%)
- Component 3: Theatre Makers in Practice (40% written)

**WJEC Eduqas Drama and Theatre**:
- Component 1: Theatre Workshop (40%)
- Component 2: Text in Action (40%)
- Component 3: Text in Performance (20%)

### Year 12 Sequencing

**Autumn Term**:
- Establishing A-Level expectations and working methods
- Introduction to set texts and theatre practitioners
- Beginning of devising/workshop component
- First theatre visits (including diverse practice)

**Spring Term**:
- Sustained practitioner study with practical application
- Historical period study (typically Greek, Renaissance, or modern period)
- Continued devising/workshop development
- Portfolio and research skills

**Summer Term**:
- Complete first practical component
- Second set text study
- Intensive analysis and evaluation skills
- Design specialism development (for relevant specifications)

### Year 13 Sequencing

**Autumn Term**:
- Final practical component rehearsal
- Examination text analysis
- Live theatre review consolidation
- Directorial approaches and concepts

**Spring Term**:
- Performance of final practical component
- Examination preparation intensified
- Practitioner application to unseen material
- Theoretical essay writing practice

**Summer Term**:
- Final examination preparation
- Revision of all set texts and practitioners

### Practitioner Depth at A-Level

Bolton and Heathcote's influence extends to A-Level, where students must understand pedagogical approaches as well as performance methodologies. Sequencing should develop:

1. **Foundation understanding**: Stanislavski, Brecht (Year 12 Autumn)
2. **Extended range**: Artaud, Grotowski, Brook (Year 12 Spring/Summer)
3. **Contemporary practice**: Frantic Assembly, Complicité, Mitchell (Year 12/13)
4. **Specialist areas**: Boal (participatory theatre), Punchdrunk (immersive), specific to texts studied

### Set Text Sequencing

Set texts require careful ordering. Consider:

- **Genre progression**: Naturalism → non-naturalism → contemporary experimental
- **Historical chronology**: Ancient Greek → Renaissance → Modern → Contemporary
- **Thematic connections**: Pairing texts that dialogue with each other
- **Practical demands**: Sequence to build technical capacity required

For example, studying Miller's *The Crucible* before Churchill's *Top Girls* allows students to understand naturalistic foundations before encountering contemporary form-breaking.

### Practical Implications for KS5

A-Level sequencing must balance:
- **Academic rigour**: Students should engage with scholarly texts (not just textbooks)
- **Creative freedom**: Time for experimental and risk-taking work
- **Assessment preparation**: Clear understanding of markschemes and expectations
- **Cultural breadth**: Exposure to diverse theatrical traditions and contemporary practice

Teachers should integrate professional theatre experiences throughout, ideally 6-8 productions across two years, spanning historical periods, practitioners, and forms.

## Cross-Key Stage Progression Mapping

### Skills Progression Overview

| Skill Area | KS1-2 | KS3 | KS4 | KS5 |
|------------|-------|-----|-----|-----|
| **Physical** | Basic control and gesture | Characterisation, ensemble work | Style application, precision | Directorial physical concepts |
| **Vocal** | Clarity and volume | Character voice, projection | Accent/dialect, technical skill | Vocal analysis and coaching |
| **Textual** | Simple scripts | Scene study | Full text interpretation | Dramaturgical analysis |
| **Creative** | Ideas contribution | Basic devising | Structured devising with stimuli | Complex devising with research |
| **Analytical** | Simple description | Evaluation using keywords | Practitioner application | Theoretical critique |

### Conceptual Progression

Dorothy Heathcote's concept of 'universal themes accessed through particular contexts' suggests that curriculum sequencing should revisit core dramatic concepts with increasing sophistication:

- **Power and status**: Playground hierarchies (KS1) → historical oppression (KS3) → political theatre analysis (KS5)
- **Identity**: Family roles (KS2) → social identity (KS3) → theoretical constructions of self (KS5)
- **Conflict**: Simple disagreements (KS1) → dramatic tension (KS3) → structural dramaturgical analysis (KS5)

### Assessment Progression

Assessment literacy must develop across key stages:

- **KS1-2**: Teacher observation, self-assessment against simple criteria
- **KS3**: Peer and self-assessment, introduction to levels/grades, written evaluation begins
- **KS4**: Formal assessment against published criteria, portfolio documentation, examination writing
- **KS5**: Academic writing standards, independent research, sophisticated self-evaluation

## Transition Points and Bridging Strategies

### Primary to Secondary Transition

The move from KS2 to KS3 represents significant shift. Andy Kempe's work on drama transitions emphasises:

- **Summer term Year 6**: Introduction to secondary drama expectations, vocabulary consolidation
- **Autumn term Year 7**: Assessment of prior learning, establishment of new expectations without assuming knowledge
- **Communication**: Primary-secondary liaison regarding students' drama experience

### KS3 to KS4 Transition

Year 9 into Year 10 requires careful bridge-building:

- **Options process**: Clear information about GCSE demands and differences from KS3
- **Summer term Year 9**: Introduction to GCSE-style devising and script work
- **Early Year 10**: Explicit teaching of GCSE success criteria and portfolio requirements

### KS4 to KS5 Transition

The academic demands of A-Level require preparation:

- **Year 11 extension activities**: Reading plays independently, seeing diverse theatre
- **Summer bridging work**: Set text reading, practitioner research, theatre visits
- **Early Year 12**: Study skills for extended writing, research methodologies
