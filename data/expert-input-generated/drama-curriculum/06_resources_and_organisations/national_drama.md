# National Drama

## Overview and Context

National Drama, formerly known as the National Association for the Teaching of Drama (NATD), represents a cornerstone organisation in UK drama education. Founded in 1975, it emerged during a pivotal period when drama was establishing itself as a discrete curriculum subject, moving beyond its earlier association purely with English literature and school productions. The organisation has been instrumental in shaping drama pedagogy, supporting practitioners, and advocating for drama's place within the National Curriculum.

The organisation changed its name from NATD to National Drama in 2015 to reflect its broader remit and more inclusive approach to all aspects of drama in education, from early years through to professional practice and community settings.

## Historical Development and Influence

### The NATD Legacy (1975-2015)

The National Association for the Teaching of Drama was established during what Dorothy Heathcote termed the "golden age" of drama in education. This period saw drama recognised as having distinct educational value beyond theatrical production. Gavin Bolton, in his seminal work *Towards a Theory of Drama in Education* (1979), articulated the pedagogical underpinnings that NATD would champion: drama as a medium for learning across the curriculum, not merely a performance art.

The organisation provided a professional home for practitioners influenced by key thinkers including:

- **Dorothy Heathcote** (1926-2011): Her mantle of the expert approach and teacher-in-role techniques became foundational to UK drama pedagogy
- **Gavin Bolton**: His work on dramatic playing and the concept of "living through" drama experiences shaped thinking about process drama
- **Cecily O'Neill**: Her development of process drama theory, particularly in *Drama Worlds* (1995), influenced how drama was conceptualised in educational settings
- **Jonothan Neelands**: His taxonomies of dramatic conventions, particularly in *Structuring Drama Work* (1990, co-authored with Tony Goode), provided practical frameworks widely adopted in UK schools

### Transition to National Drama (2015-Present)

The rebranding to National Drama reflected the organisation's evolution to encompass:

- Applied theatre and drama therapy contexts
- Community and youth theatre
- Early years drama practice
- Professional theatre education departments
- Drama in special educational needs settings

## National Curriculum Connections

### Key Stage 1 and 2

National Drama supports the statutory requirements for drama within the English National Curriculum (2014), where drama appears within the English programmes of study. At Key Stages 1 and 2, pupils should be taught to:

- Participate in role play and improvisations
- Discuss and evaluate drama they have participated in and watched
- Develop understanding of character, motivation and plot

The organisation has consistently advocated for drama's cross-curricular applications, aligning with Heathcote's belief that drama serves as a "laboratory for human behaviour" applicable across subjects. This resonates with current emphasis on oracy (as detailed in the *Oracy Quality Framework* by Voice 21, 2021) and personal, social, and emotional development.

### Key Stage 3

At KS3, National Drama supports teachers implementing drama as either a discrete subject or within English. The organisation's resources address:

- Devising skills and collaborative creation
- Script work and textual analysis
- Technical theatre introduction
- Evaluation and critical response skills

This aligns with Neelands' conventions-based approach, where students learn specific dramatic techniques (such as hot-seating, thought-tracking, and forum theatre) that deepen engagement and understanding.

### Key Stages 4 and 5

National Drama provides support for teachers delivering GCSE and A-Level drama qualifications across all major examination boards:

**GCSE Drama (Key Stage 4):**
- **AQA Drama (8261)**: 60% practical, 40% written examination
- **Edexcel Drama (1DR0)**: Similar weighting with emphasis on performance and design
- **OCR Drama (J316)**: Integrated approach to devising, performance and written analysis
- **WJEC Eduqas Drama (C690U)**: Welsh examination board provision

**A-Level Drama and Theatre (Key Stage 5):**
- **AQA Drama and Theatre (7262)**: Research-based performance, textual study
- **Edexcel Drama and Theatre (9DR0)**: Professional practice orientation
- **OCR Drama and Theatre (H459)**: Strong emphasis on theatre-making processes
- **WJEC Eduqas Drama and Theatre (A620U)**: Includes Welsh language provision

The organisation supports teachers in developing assessment literacy, particularly around subjective practical assessment, through moderation guidance and exemplar materials.

## Membership and Professional Development

### Teacher Support Networks

National Drama provides networking opportunities crucial for drama teachers, who may be sole specialists within their schools. This isolation was identified by Andy Kempe in *The GCSE Drama Coursebook* (2016) as a particular challenge for the profession. The organisation addresses this through:

- Regional support networks and meetings
- Annual conferences featuring leading practitioners
- Online forums and resource sharing
- Mentoring schemes for newly qualified teachers

### Continuing Professional Development (CPD)

National Drama offers CPD aligned with the Department for Education's *Standard for Teachers' Professional Development* (2016), including:

- Practical workshops on Heathcote's mantle of the expert
- Devising techniques drawing on Cecily O'Neill's process drama structures
- Assessment and moderation training for examination specifications
- Safeguarding in drama practice (particularly relevant when using Neelands' more challenging conventions like hot-seating on sensitive topics)
- Inclusive practice for SEND students in drama

## Theoretical Foundations and Pedagogical Approaches

### Process Drama and Product Drama

National Drama has historically championed the process drama movement, influenced heavily by Cecily O'Neill's work. Process drama emphasises:

- Exploration and understanding over performance outcomes
- Collaborative meaning-making
- Teacher as facilitator and co-participant (teacher-in-role)
- Improvisational and devised work

However, the organisation also recognises the value of "product drama" (performance-oriented work), advocating for balanced approaches. This reflects Gavin Bolton's later work (*Acting in Classroom Drama*, 1998) where he acknowledged both approaches serve different educational purposes.

### Conventions-Based Teaching

Jonothan Neelands' influential frameworks provide structured approaches that National Drama promotes through its training. These conventions include:

**Narrative conventions:**
- Storytelling and narration
- Flashback and flash-forward
- Thought-tracking

**Poetic conventions:**
- Still image/tableau
- Movement and mime
- Soundscape

**Rhetorical conventions:**
- Hot-seating
- Conscience alley
- Forum theatre (adapted from Augusto Boal)

These techniques align with the current National Curriculum's emphasis on spoken language development and analytical skills.

## Advocacy and Policy Influence

### Curriculum Campaigns

National Drama has been vocal in advocating for drama's statutory status. Following the 2013 National Curriculum review, which reduced drama's visibility within English, the organisation:

- Submitted evidence to the Department for Education
- Collaborated with the Cultural Learning Alliance
- Produced research summaries demonstrating drama's impact on student outcomes

The organisation cites research including the *Durham Commission on Creativity and Education* (2019) and the Education Endowment Foundation's findings on arts education's impact on attainment.

### EBacc and Curriculum Narrowing

National Drama has actively campaigned against the English Baccalaureate (EBacc) accountability measure, which excludes arts subjects. The organisation argues this has led to:

- Reduced curriculum time for drama at Key Stage 3
- Declining GCSE Drama entries in some regions
- Teacher recruitment and retention challenges

This advocacy work connects with broader campaigns by the National Society for Education in Drama (also known as NSEAD's drama section) and the Incorporated Society of Musicians.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Starting Out with National Drama

**For Newly Qualified Teachers (NQTs/ECTs):**

2. **Resource bank**: Lesson plans and schemes of work reducing planning burden during induction
3. **Regional networks**: Connection with experienced practitioners for informal support

**For Experienced Practitioners:**

1. **Leadership resources**: Guidance on establishing drama departments and securing resources

3. **Research engagement**: Opportunities to contribute to practice-based research
4. **Policy consultation**: Voice in national drama education debates

### Integrating National Drama Resources into Practice

#### Scheme of Work Development

National Drama provides exemplar schemes aligned to specific learning objectives. Teachers should:

- Adapt resources to school context and student cohort
- Ensure progression across key stages using Neelands' conventions hierarchy (simple to complex)
- Integrate assessment opportunities reflecting exam board criteria
- Include opportunities for student voice and choice, following O'Neill's principles of ownership

#### Assessment and Progression

The organisation's assessment guidance reflects current thinking on formative assessment, drawing on Dylan Wiliam's work. Drama-specific considerations include:

- **Process assessment**: Using teacher observation, video recording, and reflective journals
- **Product assessment**: Performance assessment criteria aligned to exam board mark schemes
- **Peer and self-assessment**: Developing critical vocabularies using professional theatre terminology
- **Portfolio development**: Documentary evidence for coursework components

### Safeguarding and Ethical Practice

National Drama provides guidance on maintaining safe practice, particularly relevant when using emotionally engaging techniques advocated by Heathcote and Bolton:

- De-roling procedures following intense dramatic work
- Managing disclosure when drama addresses sensitive topics
- Risk assessment for physical theatre and movement work
- Consent and boundaries in performance work

This aligns with *Keeping Children Safe in Education* (DfE, 2023) statutory guidance.

## Research and Evidence Base

### Impact Studies

National Drama disseminates research demonstrating drama's educational value:

- **Cognitive development**: Enhanced problem-solving and creative thinking (reference: *The Arts and Achievement in At-Risk Youth*, Catterall, 2012)
- **Social and emotional learning**: Improved empathy and relationship skills (supporting PSHE curriculum)
- **Literacy development**: Particularly for reluctant readers and EAL students
- **Academic attainment**: Correlation between arts participation and improved outcomes across subjects

### Contemporary Research Directions

Current research promoted through National Drama includes:

- **Digital drama**: Integrating technology into dramatic practice
- **Drama and mental health**: Therapeutic applications in school settings
- **Inclusive practice**: Adapting Heathcote and Neelands' approaches for SEND students
- **Drama and oracy**: Contributing to the Oracy Framework development

## Connections with Other Organisations

National Drama collaborates extensively with complementary organisations:

- **National Association of Youth Theatres (NAYT)**: Linking curricular and extra-curricular provision
- **Equity**: Professional actors' union, particularly regarding child performance licensing
- **Theatre Royal Stratford East, Royal Shakespeare Company Education**: Professional theatre education partnerships
- **Drama UK (formerly NCDT)**: Higher education drama training quality assurance
- **Arts Council England**: Funding and advocacy for arts education

## Future Directions and Challenges

### Current Challenges (2024)

1. **Teacher recruitment**: Shortage of specialist drama teachers, particularly outside urban centres
2. **Curriculum time**: Competition for timetable space within crowded curriculum
3. **Funding**: Reduced budgets impacting resources, theatre visits, and visiting practitioners
4. **Performance spaces**: Many schools lack dedicated drama studios following building programmes prioritising STEM facilities

### Strategic Priorities

National Drama's current strategic plan emphasises:

- **Diversity and inclusion**: Ensuring drama education reflects contemporary British society
- **Career pathways**: Articulating routes from school drama to creative industries
- **Evidence gathering**: Building robust research base for drama's educational impact
- **Digital innovation**: Supporting teachers adapting to post-pandemic hybrid learning

### The Post-Pandemic Context

COVID-19 significantly impacted drama education, and National Drama has supported recovery through:

- Guidance on maintaining dramatic integrity while observing health protocols
- Resources for digital drama-making
- Mental health support recognising drama teachers' unique pastoral role
- Rebuilding performance confidence in students

## Conclusion

National Drama remains the pre-eminent professional association for drama educators in the UK, bridging theoretical traditions established by Heathcote, Bolton, O'Neill, and Neelands with contemporary classroom realities. For curriculum leaders and teachers, engagement with National Drama provides access to a community of practice, authoritative resources aligned to National Curriculum and examination requirements, and advocacy support ensuring drama's continued place in the educational landscape.

The organisation embodies the tension between drama as an academic discipline (with formal assessment and qualifications) and drama as a creative, exploratory medium for learning. This duality, far from being problematic, reflects the richness of drama education and its capacity to develop students' intellectual, creative, social, and emotional capabilities simultaneously.
