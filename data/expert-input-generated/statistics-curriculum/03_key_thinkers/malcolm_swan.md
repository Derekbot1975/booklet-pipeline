# Malcolm Swan

**Full Name:** Malcolm Swan (1944–2017)

**Former Roles:** Director of the Shell Centre for Mathematical Education at the University of Nottingham; Senior Research Fellow at the Centre for Research in Mathematics Education (CRME), University of Nottingham

**Known For:** Revolutionary work in formative assessment in mathematics education; designing collaborative learning tasks; developing diagnostic teaching materials; leadership of the Mathematics Assessment Project (MAP); pioneering research on misconceptions in mathematics learning, particularly in statistics and probability

---

## Biography and Career History

Malcolm Swan was one of the UK's most influential mathematics educators, whose work profoundly shaped classroom practice across mathematics and statistics education from the 1980s until his death in 2017. Based at the University of Nottingham's Shell Centre for Mathematical Education throughout his career, Swan dedicated his professional life to understanding how students learn mathematics and statistics, and how teachers might better support that learning through carefully designed tasks and assessment practices.

Swan joined the Shell Centre in the early 1970s, working initially under the directorship of Hugh Burkhardt. The Shell Centre had been established in 1968 as a research and curriculum development centre, and became internationally renowned for its work on problem-solving, mathematical modelling, and assessment design. Swan's early work focused on developing teaching materials that made mathematical concepts more accessible whilst maintaining intellectual challenge.

During the 1980s and 1990s, Swan developed his distinctive approach to mathematics teaching, characterised by diagnostic assessment, collaborative group work, and tasks designed to expose and address common misconceptions. His 1983 work "Teaching Decimal Place Value" exemplified this approach, presenting carefully sequenced activities that revealed students' thinking patterns and provided teachers with strategies to address underlying conceptual difficulties.

In statistics education specifically, Swan made significant contributions through materials addressing probability, data handling, and statistical reasoning. He recognised that statistics posed particular pedagogical challenges because students often held strong intuitive beliefs that conflicted with statistical principles. His teaching materials in this domain focused on creating cognitive conflict and discussion opportunities to help students reconstruct their understanding.

From 2000 onwards, Swan led numerous large-scale development projects, most notably serving as principal investigator for the Mathematics Assessment Project (MAP), a collaboration between the Shell Centre and the University of California, Berkeley. This project, funded by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, produced formative assessment lessons used by hundreds of thousands of teachers internationally.

Swan was appointed as a Senior Research Fellow at the Centre for Research in Mathematics Education (CRME) at Nottingham and continued working productively until shortly before his death in January 2017. His legacy continues through the widespread use of his materials and the continued influence of his pedagogical principles.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Diagnostic Teaching and Formative Assessment

Swan's most fundamental contribution was his development of "diagnostic teaching" approaches that integrated assessment seamlessly into instruction. Unlike traditional assessment-for-learning, which often focused on teacher questioning techniques, Swan designed structured tasks and lessons that systematically revealed student thinking patterns and common misconceptions.

His diagnostic approach involved three key phases: exposing current thinking (often through carefully designed diagnostic questions or ranking activities), creating cognitive conflict through discussion and exploration, and consolidating learning through reflection. Swan's 1993 publication "Improving Learning in Mathematics: Challenges and Strategies" laid out this framework comprehensively, providing both theoretical justification and practical exemplification.

In statistics education, Swan applied this approach to concepts like probability where misconceptions are particularly prevalent. His materials on probability often began with scenarios designed to elicit common errors (such as the gambler's fallacy or representativeness heuristic), then structured collaborative activities to help students recognise and resolve these errors through peer discussion and teacher facilitation.

### 2. Collaborative Learning Through Structured Tasks

Swan pioneered the design of mathematical tasks specifically structured to promote productive collaborative learning. Unlike generic group work, Swan's tasks incorporated specific features: multiple entry points, requirement for explanation and justification, elements of ambiguity or conflict, and necessity for students to compare and evaluate different approaches.

His "Improving Learning in Mathematics" project (2005), funded by the Department for Education and Skills, exemplified this approach. The materials included card-sorting activities, ranking exercises, and matching tasks that required genuine collaboration rather than simple task division. For statistics education, Swan developed activities where students would sort data representations, match graphs to contexts, or evaluate statistical claims – all designed to generate mathematical discussion.

Swan's collaborative tasks were underpinned by social constructivist learning theory, particularly influenced by the work of Vygotsky. However, Swan's distinctive contribution was not theoretical but practical: he demonstrated precisely how to design tasks that would generate the "zones of proximal development" where peer collaboration was genuinely productive.

### 3. Addressing Misconceptions Through Cognitive Conflict

Swan developed sophisticated approaches to addressing student misconceptions, moving beyond simple correction towards creating situations where students would recognise and resolve contradictions in their own thinking. This work drew on cognitive psychology research, particularly the conceptual change literature, but translated these ideas into practical classroom strategies.

His 2001 paper "Dealing with Misconceptions in Mathematics" (published in the Handbook of Research on Mathematics Teaching and Learning) provided comprehensive analysis of different types of errors and appropriate pedagogical responses. Swan distinguished between procedural errors (often addressed through practice) and conceptual misconceptions (requiring deeper intervention through cognitive conflict).

In statistics, Swan applied this framework to common misconceptions such as confusion between theoretical and experimental probability, misinterpretation of correlation, and errors in comparing data sets. His teaching materials typically presented students with plausible but incorrect reasoning, asking them to identify flaws and articulate correct reasoning – a more powerful approach than simply presenting correct methods.

### 4. Mathematical Classroom Cultures

Swan's later work increasingly focused on the broader classroom culture necessary for effective mathematics learning. His research demonstrated that diagnostic teaching and collaborative learning required fundamental shifts in classroom norms: students needed to view errors as learning opportunities, feel safe offering tentative ideas, and recognise that mathematical reasoning was more valuable than quick answers.

The "Improving Learning in Mathematics" project included professional development materials addressing these cultural dimensions. Swan developed specific protocols for teachers to establish productive classroom norms, including expectations for mathematical discussion, strategies for handling incorrect responses constructively, and techniques for distributing participation equitably.

This work influenced thinking about mathematical "discourse communities" in UK classrooms, complementing parallel work by researchers such as Mercer and Dawes on exploratory talk. Swan's contribution was to show how task design and classroom culture must develop together for effective mathematics learning.

### 5. Design Principles for Assessment Tasks

Throughout his career, Swan developed and refined design principles for assessment tasks that would genuinely reveal student understanding rather than simply testing procedural fluency. These principles informed both formative assessment tasks used during teaching and summative assessment items.

Swan advocated for tasks requiring explanation and justification, questions with multiple valid approaches, items where common misconceptions would generate plausible incorrect answers, and scenarios requiring application rather than recognition of algorithms. His work influenced assessment design in GCSE mathematics and A-level Statistics, moving these examinations gradually towards greater emphasis on reasoning and problem-solving.

In statistics specifically, Swan criticised traditional assessment items that required only procedural calculation without interpretation. His alternative assessment tasks required students to make decisions about appropriate statistical methods, interpret results in context, critique flawed statistical reasoning, or evaluate statistical claims – all better aligned with genuine statistical literacy.

## Key Publications

### Books and Major Reports

**"The Language of Functions and Graphs"** (1985, with the Shell Centre team) – Revolutionary teaching materials using real-world contexts to develop understanding of graphical relationships. Included substantial sections on interpreting statistical graphs and understanding data relationships. Published by Joint Matriculation Board and Shell Centre, this became one of the most widely used supplementary resources in UK secondary mathematics.

**"The Meaning and Use of Decimals"** (1983, Shell Centre) – Pioneering diagnostic teaching materials addressing decimal misconceptions. Demonstrated Swan's emerging methodology of exposing errors, creating conflict, and consolidating understanding.

**"Improving Learning in Mathematics: Challenges and Strategies"** (2005, Department for Education and Skills Standards Unit) – Perhaps Swan's most influential publication, this comprised teaching materials for post-16 mathematics with detailed guidance on formative assessment and collaborative learning. Modules included statistical content on interpreting data, understanding risk and probability, and analysing statistical reports.

**"Designing Multiple-Choice Items to Measure Higher-Order Thinking"** (2014, with H. Burkhardt and R. Pead, Educational Leadership) – Demonstrated how careful design could make multiple-choice formats reveal sophisticated mathematical reasoning, challenging assumptions about assessment formats.

### Journal Articles and Book Chapters

**"Dealing with Misconceptions in Mathematics"** (2001, in P. Gates (Ed.) *Issues in Mathematics Teaching*, Routledge) – Comprehensive theoretical and practical analysis of addressing mathematical misconceptions, drawing on Swan's extensive empirical work.

**"Collaborative Learning in Mathematics: A Challenge to Our Beliefs and Practices"** (2006, with the Shell Centre team, NIACE/NRDC) – Detailed exposition of Swan's collaborative learning principles with extensive exemplification from classroom practice.

**"A Designer Speaks: Designing Assessment of Performance in Mathematics"** (1993, in M. Niss (Ed.) *Investigations into Assessment in Mathematics Education*, Kluwer Academic) – Swan's articulation of design principles for performance assessment tasks, influential beyond mathematics education.

**"Making Sense of Algebraic Expressions and Equations"** (multiple versions, Shell Centre) – Teaching materials addressing common errors in algebra, exemplifying Swan's diagnostic teaching approach.

### Mathematics Assessment Project (MAP) Materials

From 2007-2015, Swan led the development of extensive MAP materials, freely available online:

**"Formative Assessment Lessons"** (over 100 lessons for grades 6-12) – Complete lesson designs incorporating pre-assessment, collaborative activities, and post-assessment, with detailed teacher guidance. Several lessons addressed statistical concepts including data handling, probability, and statistical problem-solving.

**"Classroom Challenges"** – Extended problem-solving tasks designed for collaborative work, with materials for teachers to analyse student work and provide formative feedback.

**"Professional Development Modules"** – Resources for mathematics departments to develop formative assessment practices, based on Swan's research at Nottingham.

These MAP materials represented the culmination of Swan's career-long development of teaching approaches and became internationally influential, translated into multiple languages and adapted for various curricula.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Swan's work influenced UK mathematics and statistics education policy and practice across multiple dimensions, though often indirectly rather than through formal curriculum committees.

### Impact on GCSE Mathematics

While not directly involved in writing GCSE specifications, Swan's emphasis on problem-solving, reasoning, and addressing misconceptions aligned with and influenced reforms to GCSE Mathematics from 2000 onwards. The increased emphasis on Handling Data and statistical reasoning in GCSE Mathematics reflected pedagogical principles Swan championed. His diagnostic assessment materials were widely used by teachers preparing students for GCSE, making his pedagogical approaches part of mainstream practice.

The 2015 GCSE Mathematics reform, with its greater emphasis on problem-solving and mathematical reasoning, embodied many principles Swan had advocated. Assessment objectives requiring students to "reason, interpret and communicate mathematically" reflected Swan's long-standing argument that mathematics assessment should test thinking rather than procedure.

### Influence on A-level Statistics

Swan's work on statistical reasoning and addressing misconceptions in probability influenced pedagogical approaches to A-level Statistics teaching, particularly through professional development materials. His emphasis on interpretation and evaluation of statistical claims anticipated the reform emphasis on statistical literacy.

The use of card-sorting activities, matching exercises, and discussion-based tasks in A-level Statistics teaching often derived directly from Swan's materials or was inspired by his pedagogical principles. His work demonstrated that statistics education should develop critical evaluation of statistical arguments, not merely calculation skills.

### Impact on Teacher Education and Professional Development

Perhaps Swan's greatest policy influence came through professional development rather than formal curriculum change. The "Improving Learning in Mathematics" project (2005) was explicitly commissioned by the DfES to support teachers in further education colleges, and the materials were distributed nationally. Many teachers encountered Swan's pedagogical approaches through these materials and adapted them across different contexts.

The National Centre for Excellence in the Teaching of Mathematics (NCTEM), established in 2006, drew substantially on Swan's work in designing professional development programmes. The emphasis on formative assessment and collaborative learning in NCTEM materials reflected Swan's influence.

Swan's MAP materials, though developed for international audiences, were extensively used in UK professional development from 2010 onwards. The free availability of these high-quality materials democratised access to research-informed pedagogy, allowing teachers nationwide to implement Swan's approaches.

### Contribution to Assessment Reform

Swan was deeply involved in international discussions about mathematics assessment reform, particularly through his work with the Shell Centre on projects examining assessment practices worldwide. His writings critiqued over-reliance on procedural testing and advocated for assessment better aligned with mathematical thinking and problem-solving.

His work influenced Ofqual's thinking about assessment validity in mathematics, particularly the principle that assessment should test the constructs claimed by curriculum objectives. Swan's demonstration that well-designed tasks could assess higher-order thinking challenged assumptions that only extended written responses could test reasoning.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Theoretical Foundations

Swan's pedagogical approach synthesised multiple theoretical traditions whilst remaining fundamentally pragmatic and evidence-based. He drew on social constructivist learning theory, particularly Vygotskian concepts of mediation and zones of proximal development, but interpreted these through the practical question: "What tasks and teaching moves will help students develop mathematical understanding?"

Swan was influenced by cognitive psychology research on misconceptions and conceptual change, particularly the work of researchers like Laurinda Brown and Dietmar Küchemann in mathematics education. He translated findings about common error patterns into practical diagnostic tools and teaching strategies.

The "didactical phenomenology" tradition from the Freudenthal Institute influenced Swan's use of carefully chosen contexts and representations. Swan's tasks typically moved between multiple representations (verbal, symbolic, graphical, tabular) because research showed this supported conceptual understanding.

### Key Pedagogical Principles

**Start with Student Thinking**: Swan insisted that effective teaching must begin by understanding students' current conceptions and misconceptions. His lessons typically started with diagnostic activities revealing student thinking patterns before introducing new concepts or procedures.

**Create Productive Cognitive Conflict**: Rather than simply presenting correct methods, Swan designed situations where students would recognise inadequacies in their current understanding, creating motivation for conceptual reconstruction. In statistics, this often involved presenting counter-intuitive scenarios or data patterns that challenged intuitive reasoning.

**Structure Collaborative Exploration**: Swan's tasks required students to articulate reasoning, compare approaches, and evaluate alternatives – all supporting deeper conceptual understanding. The collaborative structure was not incidental but central to the learning process.

**Emphasise Connections and Representations**: Swan consistently designed tasks requiring students to move between different representations (graphs, tables, symbols, words) and to make explicit connections between mathematical concepts. This reflected research showing that conceptual understanding involves rich networks of connections.

**Support Metacognitive Reflection**: Swan's lessons incorporated structured reflection phases where students considered what they had learned, what approaches worked well, and where difficulties remained. This metacognitive component distinguished Swan's formative assessment approach from simple feedback.

### Classroom Implementation

Swan provided unusually detailed guidance for teachers implementing his approaches, recognising that pedagogical innovation required supporting changes in teacher practice. His materials included:

- Anticipated student responses and misconceptions, with suggested teaching moves for each
- Sample questions to guide classroom discussion towards mathematical points
- Strategies for managing collaborative group work productively
- Guidance on creating classroom cultures where errors were learning opportunities
- Videos showing implementation in real classrooms (particularly in MAP materials)

This level of detail reflected Swan's research showing that providing teachers with exemplary tasks alone was insufficient; teachers needed support in recognising learning opportunities arising during lessons and responding productively.

### Assessment Philosophy

Swan viewed formative assessment not as a set of techniques (questioning, feedback) but as an epistemological orientation: teaching should continuously attend to student understanding and adapt accordingly. His assessment tasks were simultaneously learning activities, erasing traditional boundaries between assessment and instruction.

Swan was critical of assessment practices that tested procedural fluency without conceptual understanding. In statistics, he objected to questions requiring calculation of measures without interpretation, or selection of charts without justification. His alternative assessment tasks required explanation, comparison, evaluation, and application – genuine statistical reasoning.

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

Malcolm Swan's influence on UK mathematics and statistics education continues strongly after his death, with his materials still widely used and his pedagogical principles increasingly mainstream.

### Ongoing Use of Materials

Swan's published materials remain in active use across UK schools and colleges. The MAP formative assessment lessons are particularly widely adopted, available free online and regularly cited in teacher forums and professional development sessions. His earlier materials like "The Language of Functions and Graphs" continue to inform teaching approaches to graphical interpretation in statistics.

Many examination boards and textbook publishers have incorporated Swan-influenced activities (card sorts, matching exercises, evaluation tasks) into their resources, even without explicit attribution. His design principles have become part of the shared repertoire of mathematics teaching.

### Influence on Current Practice

Contemporary emphasis on mathematical reasoning, problem-solving, and mastery learning in UK curriculum frameworks aligns closely with principles Swan advocated throughout his career. The current emphasis on depth rather than breadth, conceptual understanding rather than procedural fluency, and assessment of reasoning all reflect pedagogical principles Swan championed.

In statistics education specifically, current emphasis on statistical literacy and critical evaluation of data-based claims reflects Swan's long-standing argument that statistics education should develop critical reasoning about statistical arguments, not merely calculation competence.

### Research Programme Continuation

The Centre for Research in Mathematics Education at Nottingham continues research programmes influenced by Swan's work, particularly on formative assessment, collaborative learning, and addressing misconceptions. Researchers including Clare Lee, Colin Foster, and Matt Inglis have extended Swan's approaches into new domains.
