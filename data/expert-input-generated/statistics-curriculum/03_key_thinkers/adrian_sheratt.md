# Adrian Sherratt

**Full Name:** Adrian Sherratt

**Current Roles:** 
- Statistics education consultant and trainer
- Former Chair of the Royal Statistical Society's Centre for Statistical Education (RSSCSE)
- Independent educational consultant specialising in statistics and data literacy

**Known For:** 
- Pioneering work in statistics education reform in UK schools
- Development of practical, context-driven approaches to teaching statistics
- Leadership in the Royal Statistical Society's educational initiatives
- Advocacy for data literacy as a core competency across the curriculum
- Development of teaching resources that bridge secondary and tertiary statistics education

---

## Biography and Career History

Adrian Sherratt has been a influential figure in UK statistics education for over three decades, working at the intersection of secondary education, teacher training, and curriculum development. His career has been characterised by a commitment to making statistics accessible, relevant, and intellectually rigorous for learners at all levels.

Sherratt began his career as a mathematics and statistics teacher in secondary education before moving into teacher training and educational consultancy. He has held various leadership positions within the Royal Statistical Society (RSS), most notably serving as Chair of the RSS Centre for Statistical Education, where he was instrumental in shaping the organisation's strategy for improving statistical literacy across UK education.

Throughout his career, Sherratt has worked extensively with examination boards, curriculum authorities, and educational organisations to improve the teaching and assessment of statistics. He has been particularly active in the period of significant curriculum reform following the 2014 National Curriculum changes in England, which saw statistics given greater prominence in mathematics education.

As an educational consultant, Sherratt has delivered professional development courses to hundreds of teachers across the UK, focusing on building confidence in teaching statistics and developing pedagogical approaches that emphasise statistical thinking over procedural competence. His work has extended beyond mathematics departments, advocating for statistics education within science, geography, and other data-rich subjects.

Sherratt has also been involved in international statistics education communities, contributing to discussions about best practice in statistics pedagogy and curriculum design at conferences organised by the International Association for Statistical Education (IASE) and other bodies.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. Context-Driven Statistics Teaching

Sherratt has consistently advocated for teaching statistics through meaningful, real-world contexts rather than abstract mathematical procedures. He argues that students should encounter statistics as a tool for answering genuine questions about the world, not simply as a collection of techniques to be mastered. This approach emphasises the importance of formulating statistical questions, considering data collection methods, and interpreting results in context before introducing formal procedures.

In his teaching resources and professional development work, Sherratt promotes the use of authentic datasets from sources such as the Office for National Statistics, scientific research, and social surveys. He contends that when students work with real data addressing questions they find meaningful—such as climate change, sports performance, or social inequalities—they develop both technical skills and statistical literacy simultaneously.

### 2. The Statistical Enquiry Cycle

Central to Sherratt's pedagogical framework is the statistical enquiry cycle, which he has adapted and promoted as a structure for learning statistics at secondary level. This cycle encompasses: problem formulation, planning data collection, data analysis, and interpretation and communication of findings. Sherratt emphasises that statistics education should not begin with techniques but with questions, and that students should experience the entire enquiry process, not just the analysis phase.

This approach aligns with the PPDAC cycle (Problem, Plan, Data, Analysis, Conclusion) used in international statistics education but is adapted for the UK curriculum context. Sherratt has developed numerous teaching sequences that guide students through complete statistical investigations, ensuring they understand how each technical skill (such as calculating summary statistics or creating visualisations) serves the broader purpose of answering questions with data.

### 3. Statistical Literacy as a Citizenship Competency

Sherratt has been a vocal advocate for statistical literacy as an essential competency for informed citizenship in the 21st century. He argues that the ability to critically evaluate statistical claims in media, policy documents, and everyday discourse is as fundamental as traditional literacy and numeracy. This perspective has informed his work with the RSS on public engagement and education policy.

He emphasises that statistical literacy involves not just understanding statistical concepts but also developing a critical stance towards data: questioning data sources, recognising potential bias, understanding uncertainty, and distinguishing between correlation and causation. Sherratt has developed teaching approaches that explicitly address common statistical misconceptions and help students become more discerning consumers of quantitative information.

### 4. Integration of Technology in Statistics Teaching

Sherratt has promoted the appropriate use of technology in statistics education, arguing that software and digital tools should be used to enhance statistical thinking rather than simply to automate calculations. He has worked with various statistical software packages and has been particularly interested in tools that allow students to visualise data dynamically and explore distributions interactively.

However, Sherratt maintains that technology should not replace understanding. He advocates for a balanced approach where students learn to perform some calculations manually to understand what they represent, while using technology for more complex analyses and to work with larger, more realistic datasets. This position has influenced guidance on calculator and software use in GCSE and A-level specifications.

### 5. Distinction Between Mathematics and Statistics

A recurring theme in Sherratt's work is the importance of recognising statistics as a distinct discipline with its own modes of thinking, not merely as a branch of mathematics. Whilst acknowledging that statistical work requires mathematical skills, he argues that statistical thinking involves additional competencies: dealing with variability and uncertainty, thinking about data collection and study design, and interpreting results in context.

This perspective has influenced his advocacy for changes in how statistics is positioned within the curriculum. Sherratt has argued that statistics deserves dedicated curriculum time and that mathematics teachers require specific professional development to teach statistics effectively. He has highlighted that many mathematics teachers have limited formal training in statistics and may unconsciously teach it with an overly mathematical emphasis, missing the distinctive features of statistical reasoning.

### 6. Assessment for Statistical Understanding

Sherratt has contributed significantly to discussions about assessment in statistics education, arguing that traditional examination questions often test procedural knowledge rather than genuine statistical understanding. He has worked with examination boards to develop assessment approaches that better evaluate students' ability to think statistically, including questions that require interpretation, justification of methods, and critical evaluation of conclusions.

He advocates for assessment tasks that include extended investigations or project work, where students can demonstrate their ability to conduct a complete statistical enquiry. Sherratt recognises the practical challenges of such assessment within the constraints of the UK examination system but continues to push for greater emphasis on coursework or portfolio elements in statistics qualifications.

### 7. Professional Development for Statistics Teachers

Recognising that many teachers lack confidence in teaching statistics, Sherratt has devoted considerable effort to developing and delivering professional development programmes. His approach to teacher education emphasises not just updating teachers' content knowledge but also addressing pedagogical content knowledge specific to statistics: understanding common student misconceptions, knowing effective representations and examples, and developing appropriate questioning strategies.

Sherratt's professional development work often involves teachers engaging in statistical enquiries themselves, experiencing the approach they are being asked to adopt with students. He provides practical resources and lesson structures that teachers can adapt for their own contexts, recognising the pressures of curriculum coverage and examination preparation.

### 8. Advocating for Data Handling Across the Curriculum

Beyond mathematics classrooms, Sherratt has advocated for developing data handling and statistical literacy skills across curriculum subjects. He has worked with science, geography, and social studies educators to identify opportunities for statistical learning within their subjects, arguing that students develop richer statistical understanding when they encounter statistical concepts in multiple contexts.

This cross-curricular perspective has informed Sherratt's contributions to whole-school approaches to numeracy and data literacy. He has developed guidance for school leaders on creating coherent progression in statistical learning across subjects and key stages.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Principles of Effective Statistics Teaching

Sherratt's pedagogical approach is characterised by several key principles:

**1. Start with Questions, Not Techniques**: Lessons should begin with a genuine question that requires data to answer, rather than with a statistical technique to be learned. This ensures students understand the purpose of statistical methods and develop judgement about when different approaches are appropriate.

**2. Use Messy, Real Data**: While recognising that beginners may need some structured datasets, Sherratt advocates progressively introducing the complexity of real-world data. Students should learn to handle missing values, outliers, and data that doesn't fit neat patterns, as this is essential for developing robust statistical thinking.

**3. Emphasise Variability and Uncertainty**: Statistics exists because of variability in data. Sherratt emphasises that teaching should help students develop intuitions about variation, sampling variability, and uncertainty, rather than treating these as inconvenient complications. This includes using repeated sampling activities and simulation to develop understanding.

**4. Develop Critical Thinking**: Students should be encouraged to question data sources, consider alternative explanations, and recognise limitations of analyses. Sherratt incorporates critiquing poorly conducted or misleadingly reported studies as a regular part of statistics teaching.

**5. Build on Informal Understanding**: Before introducing formal procedures, teachers should elicit and build on students' informal statistical reasoning. Sherratt advocates discussion-based approaches where students articulate their thinking about data and justify their interpretations.

### Classroom Techniques

Sherratt promotes specific teaching strategies:

- **Think-Pair-Share for Data Interpretation**: Students first consider an interpretation question individually, then discuss with a partner, before class discussion. This ensures all students engage with challenging interpretive questions.

- **Data Debates**: Students work in groups to analyse the same dataset to address competing claims, then present and defend their findings. This develops understanding that statistical analysis involves judgement and that different valid approaches may lead to nuanced conclusions.

- **Gradual Introduction of Formality**: Beginning with exploratory data analysis and graphical methods before introducing summary statistics and formal inference procedures. Students develop conceptual understanding before learning algorithms.

- **Connecting Representations**: Explicitly linking different representations of data (raw data, graphs, summary statistics) to help students understand what different representations reveal and conceal.

- **Question Formulation Activities**: Dedicated time for students to generate their own statistical questions about datasets or contexts, developing ownership and understanding of what makes a question amenable to statistical investigation.

### Addressing Common Misconceptions

Sherratt has identified and developed approaches to address common misconceptions:

- Confusion between population and sample
- Believing that a larger sample eliminates bias
- Misinterpreting correlation as causation
- Treating summary statistics as exact descriptions rather than summaries of variable data
- Inappropriate use of statistical techniques without considering underlying assumptions

His teaching resources include specific activities designed to challenge these misconceptions through cognitive conflict and discussion.

## Contemporary Relevance

### Statistics in the Data Age

Sherratt's work has taken on increased relevance in the era of big data, data science, and widespread data use:

- His emphasis on critical data literacy is particularly important when statistical claims proliferate in media and social media
- His advocacy for understanding data collection and potential bias is essential in an age of algorithmic decision-making
- His focus on interpretation skills prepares students for future careers where data literacy is increasingly valuable

### COVID-19 Pandemic

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the importance of statistical literacy for public understanding of health data, epidemiological modelling, and policy decisions. Sherratt's long-standing work on helping citizens understand and critique statistical information proved prescient, as the pandemic revealed widespread misunderstanding of concepts such as exponential growth, uncertainty in projections, and the difference between models and predictions.

During the pandemic, Sherratt contributed to discussions about using COVID-19 data in education and developed resources to help teachers address statistical concepts arising from the pandemic.

### Future Directions

Current directions in Sherratt's work include:

- Exploring connections between traditional statistics education and emerging data science
- Addressing the challenges and opportunities of artificial intelligence and machine learning for statistics education
- Developing approaches to teach about data ethics and responsible data use
- Supporting teachers in navigating the evolving landscape of data tools and technologies
