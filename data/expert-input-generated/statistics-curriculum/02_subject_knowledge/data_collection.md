# Data Collection and Sampling

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Data collection and sampling form foundational elements of statistical literacy in the UK curriculum, progressing from simple data gathering exercises at Key Stage 1 through to sophisticated sampling methods at A-level. The National Curriculum for Mathematics (DfE, 2014) positions these concepts within the Statistics strand, emphasising both the practical skills of gathering data and the critical understanding of how sampling affects the reliability of statistical conclusions.

David Spiegelhalter, Winton Professor of the Public Understanding of Risk at Cambridge, has repeatedly emphasised that understanding sampling is fundamental to statistical literacy in modern society. His work with the Royal Statistical Society's teaching resources highlights that students must develop "a healthy scepticism about data sources and collection methods" to become statistically literate citizens.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 and 2: Foundations of Data Collection

At KS1, pupils begin by collecting and recording data in simple formats (lists, tables, pictograms). The focus is on first-hand data collection from their immediate environment. By KS2, the National Curriculum requires pupils to "solve comparison, sum and difference problems using information presented in a line graph" and to "complete, read and interpret information in tables, including timetables."

Malcolm Swan's research at the University of Nottingham emphasises the importance of starting with meaningful contexts that connect to pupils' experiences. His seminal work on formative assessment in mathematics education (Swan, 2006) demonstrates that effective data collection activities at primary level should involve genuine questions where the answer is not predetermined, encouraging authentic statistical thinking.

### Key Stage 3: Introduction to Sampling Concepts

The KS3 Programme of Study requires pupils to "describe, interpret and compare observed distributions of a single variable through appropriate graphical representation involving discrete, continuous and grouped data." This stage introduces crucial sampling vocabulary: population, sample, bias, and representative samples.

Anne Watson's work on variation theory (Watson & Mason, 2006) provides valuable pedagogical insight here. She argues that students need carefully designed variation in examples to understand what makes a sample representative. Teachers should present multiple samples from the same population, varying sampling methods systematically so students can observe the consequences of different approaches.

Adrian Sheratt's work with the Association of Teachers of Mathematics emphasises that KS3 students often hold naïve conceptions about sampling, believing that any sample will automatically represent the population or that larger samples are always necessary regardless of population size. Addressing these misconceptions requires explicit teaching and carefully chosen counterexamples.

### Key Stage 4: GCSE Requirements

All major GCSE specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) include substantial content on data collection and sampling methods. The requirements typically include:

**Foundation Tier:**
- Understanding and identifying populations
- Recognising different sampling methods (simple random, systematic, stratified)
- Identifying possible sources of bias
- Designing simple data collection sheets and questionnaires
- Understanding primary vs secondary data

**Higher Tier (additional content):**
- Applying stratified sampling calculations
- Evaluating the effectiveness of different sampling methods for given contexts
- Critiquing data collection methods in real contexts
- Understanding sampling frames and their limitations

The AQA GCSE Statistics qualification (distinct from Mathematics) provides particularly detailed treatment, requiring students to "understand and use sampling methods including simple random sampling, stratified sampling, systematic sampling, quota sampling and opportunity sampling" and to "evaluate sampling methods in the context of the data collection process."

## Types of Sampling Methods

### Simple Random Sampling

Simple random sampling ensures every member of the population has an equal probability of selection. In UK education contexts, this is often introduced through "names from a hat" scenarios or random number generators. 

Practical implementation requires a complete sampling frame (list of the population). David Spiegelhalter's work with Understanding Uncertainty emphasises teaching students that obtaining a true sampling frame is often problematic in real-world contexts—a crucial insight often overlooked in textbook treatments.

### Systematic Sampling

Systematic sampling selects every kth member from an ordered list. The Edexcel GCSE Mathematics specification explicitly requires students to "understand the advantages and disadvantages of systematic sampling." Teachers should emphasise that systematic sampling can introduce bias if there are periodic patterns in the population list—a concept Adrian Sheratt highlights as requiring concrete examples to understand.

### Stratified Sampling

Stratified sampling divides the population into groups (strata) and samples proportionally from each. This method appears prominently in all GCSE Higher tier specifications. The key formula is:

**Sample size from stratum = (Stratum size / Population size) × Total sample size**

Anne Watson's research suggests students often struggle with proportional reasoning in this context. Effective teaching requires multiple worked examples showing the calculation process explicitly, followed by carefully structured variation in practice problems.

### Quota Sampling and Opportunity Sampling

These non-random methods appear particularly in GCSE Statistics and A-level specifications. OCR's GCSE Statistics specification requires students to "recognise the advantages and disadvantages of different sampling techniques" including these methods.

Quota sampling sets targets for specific subgroups but allows non-random selection within groups. Opportunity sampling uses the most convenient available subjects. Both introduce bias but are often more practical and economical than random methods—a nuance that Malcolm Swan emphasises should be explored through real-world case studies rather than purely theoretical treatment.

## Key Stage 5: A-level Requirements

### AS and A-level Mathematics

The A-level Mathematics specifications (all boards) include sampling within the Statistics components (typically AS Paper 2 and A2 Paper 3). MEI's specification, known for its applied focus, requires students to "understand and use sampling techniques including simple random sampling and opportunity sampling" and to "select or critique sampling techniques in the context of solving a statistical problem."

### A-level Further Mathematics

Edexcel's Further Statistics options extend to cluster sampling and systematic sampling from large datasets. Students must understand sampling distributions—the distribution of a statistic (like the mean) across all possible samples of a given size.

### A-level Statistics (Distinct Qualification)

AQA offers a standalone A-level Statistics qualification requiring comprehensive understanding of sampling distributions, standard errors, and confidence intervals arising from sampling variation. This specification uniquely requires analysis of two-stage sampling and the design of complex sampling schemes for research scenarios.

## Sources of Bias in Data Collection

### Selection Bias

Selection bias occurs when the sampling method systematically excludes or under-represents certain groups. David Spiegelhalter's public engagement work frequently uses examples from medical research and opinion polling to illustrate how selection bias affects conclusions—cases that resonate with A-level students.

UK GCSE specifications require students to identify potential bias in given scenarios. Common examples include:
- Telephone surveys (excluding those without landlines)
- Voluntary response samples (self-selection bias)
- Convenience samples from limited locations

### Response Bias

Response bias arises from how questions are asked or data is recorded. The AQA GCSE Statistics specification explicitly requires understanding of:
- Leading questions
- Unclear question wording
- Inappropriate response options
- Social desirability bias in responses

Malcolm Swan's Improving Learning in Mathematics project provides excellent resources showing how students can design questionnaires with biased questions, then critique each other's work—a powerful pedagogical approach that develops critical evaluation skills.

### Non-response Bias

Non-response occurs when selected participants don't provide data. This is particularly important at A-level, where specifications require understanding that high non-response rates can invalidate sample representativeness even when initial selection was random.

## Questionnaire Design

All GCSE specifications require students to design and critique data collection sheets and questionnaires. Key principles include:

**Question Types:**
- Closed questions with pre-specified options (enabling quantitative analysis)
- Appropriate use of Likert scales
- Avoiding open-ended questions where categorical data is needed

**Response Categories:**
- Exhaustive and mutually exclusive categories
- Appropriate ranges for numerical data (avoiding overlaps like "10-20, 20-30")
- Including "Other" or "Prefer not to say" where appropriate

**Avoiding Bias:**
- Neutral wording
- Avoiding assumptions
- Appropriate time frames for recall
- Logical question ordering

Anne Watson's work on example spaces emphasises that students benefit from seeing multiple examples of both good and poor questionnaire design, with explicit comparison highlighting the critical features. Teachers should build collections of authentic questionnaires from various sources for analysis.

## Primary and Secondary Data

### Definitions and Distinctions

The distinction between primary data (collected first-hand for a specific purpose) and secondary data (obtained from existing sources) appears throughout KS3 and KS4 curricula. 

**Primary data advantages:**
- Collected for the specific research question
- Known data collection methods
- Control over timing and variables

**Secondary data advantages:**
- Often larger datasets
- More cost and time effective
- May cover periods or populations not otherwise accessible

### UK Secondary Data Sources

Students at GCSE and A-level should be familiar with major UK data sources:
- Office for National Statistics (ONS) datasets
- UK Data Service
- Census data
- Government department statistics (DfE, NHS Digital, etc.)

David Spiegelhalter's resources through Cambridge Mathematics particularly emphasise using authentic UK data sources to develop students' capability with real-world statistical practice. His "Numbercraft" approach advocates that students regularly work with messier, authentic datasets rather than sanitised textbook examples.

## Practical Teaching Approaches

### Experiential Learning

Malcolm Swan's research strongly supports beginning sampling topics with practical activities where students physically take samples and observe variation. Effective activities include:

**The Sweet Jar Investigation:** Students estimate the proportion of different coloured sweets by taking samples of different sizes using different methods, comparing results to the true population proportion.

**School Survey Simulation:** Students design sampling schemes to survey pupil opinions on school issues, implement them, then analyse whether different sampling methods produce different conclusions.

### Critical Analysis of Media Statistics

Adrian Sheratt's work with the ATM recommends regular analysis of statistics in news media, with explicit focus on:
- Who was sampled and how?
- What was the sample size?
- What might the sampling frame have been?
- What potential biases exist?
- Are conclusions justified given the sampling method?

The Royal Statistical Society's CensusAtSchool project provides excellent structured resources for this approach, aligned with UK curriculum requirements.

### Technology Integration

Modern GCSE and A-level teaching should incorporate:
- Random number generation in spreadsheets and calculators
- Statistical software (Autograph, GeoGebra, R) for sampling from distributions
- Online surveys and data collection tools
- Large Dataset exploration (all A-level boards now require work with large datasets)

OCR's Large Data Set, used in their A-level Mathematics specification, provides authentic weather data requiring students to consider sampling temporal data—an often-overlooked aspect of sampling theory.

## Common Misconceptions

### The Representativeness Misconception

Research by Watson and Moritz (2000) identified that many students believe small samples can never be representative or that samples must include at least one of every type in the population. Anne Watson's teaching materials specifically address this through probability simulations showing how random samples of modest size often represent populations well.

### The Larger Is Always Better Misconception

Students often fail to understand that sample quality (randomness, lack of bias) matters more than size beyond a certain point. David Spiegelhalter's work emphasises teaching that "a small random sample beats a large biased sample"—a principle crucial for statistical literacy.

### Confusing Census with Sample

The distinction between census (surveying the entire population) and sampling requires explicit teaching. GCSE specifications require understanding when census is appropriate (small populations, need for complete accuracy) versus sampling (large or inaccessible populations, destructive testing, cost constraints).

## Assessment Considerations

### GCSE Examination Questions

Typical GCSE questions on sampling require students to:
- Calculate stratum sizes for stratified samples (Higher tier)
- Identify sampling methods from descriptions
- Critique sampling methods for given contexts
- Design data collection sheets
- Identify bias sources

Mark schemes typically reward:
- Correct calculations with working shown
- Context-specific evaluation (not generic lists of advantages/disadvantages)
- Multiple relevant points about bias or limitations

### A-level Examination Questions

A-level questions extend to:
- Justifying choice of sampling method for research scenarios
- Analysing sampling distributions
- Understanding sampling error versus bias
- Commenting on validity of conclusions given sampling methods

AQA's A-level Statistics papers include extended response questions requiring synthesis of sampling theory with statistical inference—representing the highest level of sampling understanding in school mathematics.

## Links to Other Statistical Topics

Sampling connects fundamentally to:

**Statistical Inference:** Understanding sampling variation is prerequisite to confidence intervals and hypothesis testing at A-level.

**Probability:** Random sampling relies on probability concepts; systematic misconceptions about randomness affect sampling understanding.

**Data Representation:** The choice of sample affects which graphs and summary statistics are meaningful.

**Experimental Design:** Sampling principles extend to selecting experimental and control groups.

Anne Watson and John Mason's work on "structure of attention" suggests explicitly teaching these connections rather than treating sampling as isolated content.

## Conclusion: Statistical Literacy Goals

The ultimate goal of teaching data collection and sampling in UK schools is developing statistically literate citizens who can critically evaluate claims based on data. As David Spiegelhalter argues, this requires moving beyond procedural competence with sampling calculations to genuine understanding of how sampling affects the trustworthiness of statistical conclusions.

Malcolm Swan's principles of formative assessment apply particularly well to sampling: students need opportunities to make and revise judgements about sampling quality, to compare different approaches, and to articulate their reasoning about bias and representativeness.
