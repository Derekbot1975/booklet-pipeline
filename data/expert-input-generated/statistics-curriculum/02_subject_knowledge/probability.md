# Probability

## Overview and Curriculum Position

Probability is a fundamental strand of statistics that enables learners to quantify uncertainty and make informed predictions about future events. In the UK National Curriculum for Mathematics, probability appears as a distinct domain from Key Stage 3 onwards, though foundational ideas about chance and likelihood are introduced informally in primary education.

The National Curriculum (2014) specifies that pupils should "develop their mathematical knowledge, in part through solving problems and evaluating the outcomes, including multi-step problems" with probability providing rich contexts for such reasoning. David Spiegelhalter, Winton Professor of the Public Understanding of Risk at Cambridge, emphasises that probability education should connect to real-world risk assessment and decision-making, not merely abstract mathematical calculation.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Though not explicitly named in the Key Stage 2 curriculum, preparatory work includes:

- Using vocabulary of chance (impossible, unlikely, even chance, likely, certain)
- Conducting simple probability experiments with coins, dice, and spinners
- Beginning to recognise that some outcomes are more likely than others
- Recording outcomes systematically

Anne Watson's research on mathematical reasoning highlights that these early experiences should focus on qualitative reasoning before introducing numerical probabilities, allowing pupils to develop intuitive understanding of randomness and fairness.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum specifies that pupils should be taught to:

- Record, describe and analyse the frequency of outcomes of simple probability experiments involving randomness, fairness, equally and unequally likely outcomes, using appropriate language and the 0-1 probability scale
- Understand that the probabilities of all possible outcomes sum to 1
- Enumerate sets and unions/intersections of sets systematically, using tables, grids and Venn diagrams
- Generate theoretical sample spaces for single and combined events with equally likely, mutually exclusive outcomes and use these to calculate theoretical probabilities

Malcolm Swan's work on cognitive conflict in mathematics learning suggests that Key Stage 3 is crucial for addressing common misconceptions, particularly the gambler's fallacy and confusion between experimental and theoretical probability. His research emphasises the importance of tasks that expose pupils to sequences of random events to challenge their expectations.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE specifications across all major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) include:

**Foundation Tier:**
- Understanding and using the probability scale from 0 to 1
- Calculating theoretical probabilities from equally likely outcomes
- Calculating experimental probabilities from relative frequencies
- Using two-way tables and frequency trees
- Understanding mutually exclusive and exhaustive outcomes
- Using the addition rule: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) for mutually exclusive events
- Expected frequency calculations

**Higher Tier (additional content):**
- Tree diagrams for combined events (with and without replacement)
- Conditional probability
- The multiplication rule: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
- Venn diagrams for calculating probabilities
- Set notation (∩, ∪)
- Systematic listing strategies

Adrian Sherratt, former Chief Examiner for A-level Statistics, notes that the transition from Foundation to Higher tier probability represents a significant cognitive leap, moving from additive thinking to multiplicative reasoning in combined events.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-level Mathematics and Further Mathematics specifications include:

**AS/A-level Mathematics:**
- Probability distributions (discrete and continuous)
- Statistical distributions (binomial, normal)
- Hypothesis testing using normal distribution
- Correlation and regression (with probability concepts)

**A-level Further Mathematics:**
- Discrete random variables and their distributions
- Poisson distribution
- Geometric and negative binomial distributions
- Continuous random variables
- Hypothesis testing with various distributions

**A-level Statistics (standalone):**
All exam boards offer comprehensive probability coverage including combinatorics, probability generating functions, and advanced hypothesis testing. The MEI specification is particularly noted for its emphasis on real-world applications.

## Fundamental Concepts and Progression

### Theoretical vs Experimental Probability

A critical distinction that develops across key stages. David Spiegelhalter's "The Art of Statistics" (2019) emphasises that students must understand both approaches and recognise when each is appropriate. Experimental probability (relative frequency) provides empirical evidence, whilst theoretical probability relies on assumptions of equally likely outcomes.

**Teaching implications:** Malcolm Swan's research demonstrates that pupils benefit from extended experiments (e.g., rolling dice 600 times collaboratively) to observe the law of large numbers in action. This addresses the misconception that theoretical probability guarantees specific outcomes in small samples.

### Sample Space and Systematic Enumeration

The ability to identify all possible outcomes underpins probability calculation. Anne Watson's work on mathematical structure highlights that sample space enumeration develops spatial and systematic thinking.

**Progression:**
- KS3: Using lists, tables, and simple grids
- KS4: Two-way tables, tree diagrams, Venn diagrams
- KS5: Combinatorial methods, permutations, combinations

### Independence and Conditionality

The distinction between independent events and conditional probability represents a significant threshold concept. Research by Kahneman and Tversky on cognitive biases shows that even adults struggle with conditional probability, making careful pedagogical attention essential.

**Key Stage 4 example:** Distinguishing between sampling with and without replacement. Tree diagrams with changing probabilities on branches make the conditional nature visible.

**Teaching implications:** Adrian Sherratt recommends using contexts before abstract notation. Medical testing scenarios (false positives/negatives) provide meaningful contexts for conditional probability at KS5.

## Common Misconceptions and Pedagogical Approaches

### The Gambler's Fallacy

Believing that past outcomes affect future probabilities in independent events. Malcolm Swan's "Improving Learning in Mathematics" (2005) suggests using extended sequences to demonstrate that "runs" of outcomes occur naturally in random sequences.

**Classroom activity:** Pupils generate fake random coin sequences and genuine random sequences, then identify which is which. Genuine sequences typically contain longer runs than pupils expect.

### Representativeness Heuristic

Expecting small samples to mirror population proportions. Anne Watson's research on example generation shows that pupils often reject perfectly valid random outcomes as "not looking random enough."

**Teaching approach:** Conduct class experiments with large numbers of trials, graphing cumulative relative frequency to show convergence towards theoretical probability.

### Outcome vs Event Confusion

Confusing the probability of an outcome with the probability of an event (set of outcomes). This is particularly problematic with non-equiprobable outcomes.

**Example misconception:** Believing P(sum of 7 on two dice) = P(sum of 2) because both are "one sum."

**Teaching approach:** Explicit attention to sample spaces. The National STEM Centre's "Bowland Maths" resources provide rich tasks that require careful outcome enumeration.

### Conjunction Fallacy

Believing that P(A and B) > P(A) alone, particularly in narrative contexts. This connects to Spiegelhalter's work on public understanding of risk.

**Teaching approach:** Use set diagrams to show that intersections must be subsets, making their probability necessarily smaller.

## Assessment Considerations

### Exam Board Approaches

**AQA:** Known for contextual probability questions, often embedding probability within problem-solving scenarios. Assessment objectives emphasise interpretation and communication.

**Edexcel:** Traditionally includes multi-step probability problems requiring systematic working. Tree diagrams feature prominently in Higher tier assessments.

**OCR:** The MEI specification particularly emphasises statistical modelling and real-data contexts, with probability integrated into authentic scenarios.

**WJEC Eduqas:** Welsh specification includes good balance of procedural and conceptual questions, with emphasis on justification and reasoning.

### Common Assessment Pitfalls

Adrian Sherratt's examiner reports consistently identify:
- Confusion between "and" (multiplication) and "or" (addition)
- Failure to recognise conditional probability situations
- Arithmetic errors in fraction calculations
- Incomplete sample space enumeration
- Not recognising when outcomes are not equally likely

**Teaching response:** Explicit attention to probability language, using stem sentences: "The probability of A AND B means both happen, so we multiply..." Regular practice with fraction arithmetic in probability contexts.

## Practical Classroom Approaches

### Physical Experiments

Malcolm Swan emphasises that physical experimentation builds intuition before formalisation. Recommended experiments include:

- Coin tossing (fairness and independence)
- Dice rolling (equally likely outcomes, sum distributions)
- Drawing from bags (sampling with/without replacement)
- Spinner experiments (non-uniform probabilities)
- Card dealing (conditional probability, combinatorics)

**Key principle:** Sufficient trials to observe convergence. Individual pupils rolling one die six times rarely get each number once; class data of 600 rolls shows much clearer patterns.

### Simulation and Technology

The National Centre for Excellence in the Teaching of Mathematics (NCETM) recommends:
- Spreadsheet simulations for large-scale experiments
- Dynamic geometry software (GeoGebra) for visual probability
- Online random generators for class experiments
- Coding (Python, R) at KS5 for distribution modelling

David Spiegelhalter's "Understanding Uncertainty" website provides excellent interactive resources demonstrating probability concepts through real-world scenarios.

### Rich Tasks and Investigations

Anne Watson's "Questions and Prompts for Mathematical Thinking" suggests open investigations such as:

- "Design a fair game" (requires understanding of expected value)
- "What's the most likely total when rolling two dice?" (sample space exploration)
- "How many people do you need in a room before there's a 50% chance two share a birthday?" (counter-intuitive probability)

The "Nrich" project (Cambridge University) provides carefully designed probability investigations suitable across key stages.

### Context and Application

David Spiegelhalter's public engagement work emphasises connecting probability to:
- Medical decision-making (screening test interpretation)
- Criminal justice (DNA evidence, Sally Clark case)
- Weather forecasting (probability statements)
- Insurance and risk assessment
- Quality control in manufacturing

These contexts make probability meaningful whilst developing critical citizenship skills for evaluating probabilistic claims in media and public discourse.

## Differentiation and Inclusion

### Supporting Struggling Learners

Malcolm Swan's research on low-attaining pupils suggests:
- Extensive use of concrete materials and physical experiments
- Focus on qualitative reasoning before numerical probabilities
- Visual representations (particularly area models for probability)
- Chunking multi-step problems with intermediate questions

### Extending High Attainers

- Introducing combinatorial thinking earlier (permutations, combinations)
- Expected value and game theory concepts
- Bayesian reasoning and inference
- Monte Carlo simulation methods
- Historical development of probability theory (Pascal, Fermat, Cardano)

## Mathematical Language and Vocabulary

Precise language is essential in probability. Key terms requiring explicit teaching:

**KS3:** outcome, event, sample space, equally likely, mutually exclusive, exhaustive, relative frequency, theoretical probability, experimental probability

**KS4:** independent, conditional probability, with/without replacement, expected frequency, union, intersection

**KS5:** random variable, probability distribution, cumulative distribution function, discrete, continuous, combinatorics

Anne Watson emphasises that pupils should use and understand mathematical language in meaningful contexts, not merely memorise definitions. Sentence stems support this: "These events are mutually exclusive because...", "This is a conditional probability because..."

## Research Evidence and Expert Opinion

### Key Research Findings

**Fischbein and Schnarch (1997)** demonstrated that intuitive probability reasoning often conflicts with formal probability knowledge, requiring explicit reconciliation through teaching.

**Kahneman and Tversky's** heuristics and biases research shows systematic errors in probability judgement persist even among educated adults, emphasising the need for robust probability education.

**Jones, Langrall et al. (2007)** identified developmental frameworks for probability thinking, showing progression from subjective to transitional to informal quantitative reasoning before formal probability.

### UK-Specific Pedagogical Research

Malcolm Swan's "Collaborative Learning in Mathematics" (2006) demonstrates that group work on probability investigations, particularly those involving cognitive conflict, significantly improves understanding and retention.

The ICCAMS project (Increasing Competence and Confidence in Algebra and Multiplicative Structures) found that proportional reasoning is foundational for probability understanding, suggesting explicit connections between ratio work and probability calculations.

## Future Directions and Contemporary Relevance

David Spiegelhalter's "COVID-19 Communicating Risk" work demonstrates probability's critical role in contemporary society. He argues that probability education should enable citizens to:

- Interpret probability statements in news and policy
- Understand the difference between absolute and relative risk

- Evaluate statistical evidence critically

The Royal Statistical Society's "Data Science for Everyone" initiative emphasises that modern probability education should include:

- Computational approaches to probability

## Conclusion

Probability education in the UK curriculum progresses from informal qualitative reasoning about chance to sophisticated formal probability theory capable of supporting statistical inference and real-world decision-making. Effective teaching requires attention to common misconceptions, extensive use of physical and simulated experiments, precise mathematical language, and meaningful real-world contexts. The work of Watson, Swan, Sherratt, and Spiegelhalter provides robust pedagogical frameworks for developing both procedural fluency and conceptual understanding across all key stages.
