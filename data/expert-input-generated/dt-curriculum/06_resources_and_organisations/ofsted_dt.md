# Ofsted and D&T

## Overview of Ofsted's Role in D&T Education

The Office for Standards in Education, Children's Services and Skills (Ofsted) serves as the independent inspectorate for education in England, with significant influence over the delivery, quality and perception of Design & Technology education. Since Ofsted's establishment in 1992, its inspection frameworks and subject-specific guidance have shaped curriculum priorities, pedagogical approaches and resource allocation for D&T across primary and secondary schools.

The relationship between Ofsted and D&T has been particularly significant—and at times problematic—given the subject's practical nature, resource requirements and historical vulnerability within the curriculum. David Barlex and Stephanie Atkinson have both critiqued how inspection frameworks sometimes fail to capture the distinctive pedagogical value of D&T, particularly the iterative design process and development of designerly thinking.

## Historical Context: Ofsted's Evolving Approach to D&T

### The Technology Curriculum (1990s-2000)

During the initial National Curriculum implementation, Ofsted subject reports for Design and Technology (initially called 'Technology') provided detailed commentary on subject-specific teaching quality. The 1995 Ofsted report 'Design and Technology: A Review of Inspection Findings' highlighted inconsistencies in delivery but recognised the subject's potential for developing problem-solving capabilities.

### The 'Making' Debate (2000-2015)

Richard Kimbell's research, particularly the 'Assessment of Performance Unit' work and subsequent critiques, emphasised the importance of practical capability. Ofsted reports from this period often highlighted concerns about the balance between designing and making, with the 2011 subject survey report 'Meeting Technological Challenges?' identifying that students' designing skills were frequently weaker than their making skills—a finding that influenced subsequent curriculum reforms.

### Post-2014 National Curriculum

Following Michael Gove's curriculum reforms, Ofsted's approach increasingly focused on curriculum intent, implementation and impact. The 2019 Education Inspection Framework marked a significant shift, with implications for how D&T departments articulate their curriculum sequencing and knowledge progression.

## Current Ofsted Inspection Framework (2019-Present)

### The Three 'I's and D&T

**Intent:**
Ofsted inspectors evaluate whether the D&T curriculum is ambitious and coherent. For D&T, this requires departments to articulate:
- How knowledge progresses from Key Stage 3 through to GCSE and beyond
- The sequencing of technical knowledge, designing skills and material competencies
- How the curriculum prepares students for Level 2 and Level 3 qualifications

Kay Stables' work on 'designerly capability' provides a framework that many departments use to articulate curriculum intent, demonstrating how iterative designing develops across key stages.

**Implementation:**
Inspectors assess pedagogical approaches, including:
- The balance between teacher demonstration, guided practice and independent application
- Use of iterative design processes that reflect authentic practice
- Quality of teacher subject knowledge and technical expertise
- Assessment practices that capture both process and outcome

Matt McLain's research on teacher expertise in D&T contexts has influenced thinking about what effective implementation looks like, particularly regarding the specialist knowledge required for high-quality D&T teaching.

**Impact:**
For D&T, impact evaluation considers:
- Students' development of design thinking and technical competence
- Quality of outcomes in coursework and practical work
- Progression to further study or careers in design, engineering and technology
- Students' ability to critique and evaluate existing products and systems

### Deep Dive Methodology

Ofsted's 'deep dive' approach involves subject specialists examining work scrutiny, lesson observations and discussions with staff and students. For D&T, this typically includes:
- Reviewing progression of technical knowledge through students' portfolios and practical outcomes
- Examining design folders to assess development of designing skills
- Observing workshop practice and health & safety protocols
- Discussing curriculum rationale with subject leaders
- Evaluating assessment practices, including both formative and summative approaches

## Key Ofsted Publications Relevant to D&T

### Subject Reports and Research Reviews

**'Design and Technology in Schools' (2004):** This subject report highlighted that the best D&T teaching involved authentic design challenges with meaningful contexts. It criticised excessive focus on assessment at the expense of creative designing.

**'Making Things: Developing the Use of Information and Communication Technology in Design & Technology' (2003):** Examined integration of CAD/CAM and digital technologies, finding inconsistent application across schools.

**'Design and Technology: Realising the Potential' (2011):** This significant report identified key weaknesses including:
- Limited use of resistant materials in primary schools
- Insufficient links between designing and making activities
- Weak understanding of iterative design processes
- Over-reliance on template-based approaches limiting creativity

**Research Review Series – Design and Technology (Expected 2024-25):** Ofsted has indicated intention to develop subject-specific research reviews following the model established for other subjects. This would provide evidence-based guidance on effective D&T pedagogy.

### Generic Guidance with D&T Implications

**'Education Inspection Framework: Overview of Research' (2019):** While not D&T-specific, this document's emphasis on curriculum coherence and knowledge progression has implications for how D&T departments structure their schemes of work.

**Commentary on Curriculum (Ongoing Blog Posts):** Ofsted's subject leads occasionally publish commentary on curriculum subjects. Amanda Spielman and Chris Jones have addressed practical subjects, though D&T-specific guidance remains less developed than for some other subjects.

## Common Inspection Findings in D&T

### Strengths Often Identified

Based on published reports and sector knowledge:
- Strong practical skills development in specialist facilities
- Effective use of iterative designing in some schools
- Good integration of STEM knowledge in engineering contexts
- Quality outcomes at GCSE and A-Level where specialist teachers are present

### Areas for Development Frequently Cited

**Primary Phase:**
- Limited subject knowledge among non-specialist primary teachers
- Insufficient progression from Early Years Foundation Stage through Key Stage 2
- Over-reliance on construction kits rather than authentic designing and making
- Inadequate coverage of different material areas, particularly textiles and food

**Secondary Phase:**
- Weak curriculum sequencing, particularly in Key Stage 3
- Insufficient emphasis on technical knowledge underpinning practical skills
- Limited opportunities for authentic designing with real clients/contexts
- Poor retention and recruitment of specialist D&T teachers affecting curriculum breadth
- Inadequate workshop facilities and material resources in some schools

## Implications for D&T Departments

### Preparing for Inspection

**Curriculum Documentation:**
Subject leaders should prepare clear documentation articulating:
- Long-term curriculum planning showing knowledge and skills progression
- Medium-term schemes of work with explicit learning objectives
- Assessment frameworks that capture both designing and making capabilities
- Evidence of curriculum review informed by examination results, destinations data and subject-specific research

David Barlex's 'Nuffield Design & Technology' resources provide exemplar curriculum frameworks that demonstrate effective sequencing and progression.

**Evidence of Quality:**
Maintain accessible evidence including:
- Student portfolios showing iterative design development
- Photographic records of practical outcomes across year groups
- Assessment data demonstrating progress in both technical knowledge and design capability
- Examples of projects providing authentic contexts for designing
- Records of extra-curricular enrichment (competitions, industry links, exhibitions)

### Subject Leadership Requirements

Ofsted expects subject leaders to demonstrate:
- Clear vision for high-quality D&T education aligned with National Curriculum requirements
- Understanding of progression from Key Stage 2 through to Key Stage 4/5
- Quality assurance processes including lesson observation, work scrutiny and moderation
- Strategies for maintaining teacher expertise through CPD
- Awareness of contemporary practice in design, engineering and manufacturing

Stephanie Atkinson's research on D&T teacher identity and professionalism provides theoretical frameworks for developing subject leadership.

### Addressing the Evidence Base

Inspectors increasingly reference research evidence. D&T leaders should be familiar with:
- Richard Kimbell's work on assessment and practical capability
- Kay Stables' research on designerly thinking and capability
- E&T Board research on progression and assessment (published by DATA/D&T Association)
- International comparative studies (PISA, TIMSS) relating to design and technology education
- Subject-specific pedagogy research published in 'International Journal of Technology and Design Education' and 'Design and Technology Education: An International Journal'

## Key Stage Specific Considerations

### Key Stage 2 (Primary)

Ofsted findings consistently indicate that D&T is the weakest foundation subject in primary schools. Effective primary D&T demonstrates:
- Coverage of all material areas (textiles, construction materials, food, mechanical/electrical components)
- Clear design briefs with evaluation against design criteria
- Development of cutting, shaping, joining and finishing techniques
- Age-appropriate use of tools with robust health and safety procedures

**National Curriculum Requirements (2014):**
The KS2 programme of study requires pupils to design purposeful, functional products based on design criteria, generate and develop ideas, select from materials and components, and evaluate their products against design criteria.

### Key Stage 3

This is the critical stage for establishing D&T capability before GCSE options. Inspectors look for:
- Systematic building of technical knowledge in materials, structures, mechanisms and electronics
- Regular opportunities for iterative designing
- Introduction to CAD/CAM and digital manufacturing
- Links to mathematics and science curriculum
- Exploration of wider design contexts (social, cultural, environmental)

**National Curriculum Requirements (2014):**
KS3 D&T should develop creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate in an increasingly technological world. Students should build and apply a repertoire of knowledge about materials and techniques.

### Key Stage 4

Ofsted evaluates how effectively schools prepare students for GCSE examinations across all awarding bodies:

**AQA Design & Technology (8552):** Substantial iterative project (50% NEA) requiring contextual challenges and user-centred design. Inspectors assess whether students receive adequate time and support for portfolio development.

**Eduqas/WJEC Design & Technology (C560/C660):** Similar structure with emphasis on Welsh contexts in WJEC version. Quality of technical knowledge application in NEA is scrutinised.

**OCR Design & Technology (J310):** Iterative design challenge requiring 30-35 hours. Inspectors examine whether teaching supports both theoretical understanding and practical application.

**Pearson Edexcel Design & Technology (1DT0):** Integrated approach across material areas. Assessment of how schools manage the breadth of material knowledge required.

### Key Stage 5

At A-Level, Ofsted considers:
- Whether schemes of work extend beyond GCSE content
- Quality of substantial design and make projects (typically 50 hours NEA)
- Academic rigour in understanding design movements, sustainability and commercial practice
- Preparation for degree-level study or design/engineering careers

## Recent Challenges and Controversies

### Subject Status and EBacc

The English Baccalaureate's exclusion of D&T has significantly impacted the subject's status. Ofsted has acknowledged this issue in various reports, noting declining entries at GCSE. The D&T Association and subject experts including Barlex and McLain have critiqued how inspection frameworks sometimes inadvertently reinforce EBacc prioritisation by focusing inspection time disproportionately on EBacc subjects.

### Qualification Reform and Assessment

The 2017 GCSE reforms introduced substantial non-examined assessment (NEA) components requiring 30-35 hours teaching time. Ofsted's approach to evaluating NEA quality has evolved, with current frameworks focusing on authenticity and independent student work rather than teacher intervention. This aligns with awarding body requirements but creates pressure on curriculum time.

### Teacher Recruitment and Retention

Ofsted reports have consistently identified specialist teacher shortages in D&T. The 2021 report 'Research Review Series: Science' noted similar issues in practical subjects. Inspectors now routinely consider whether subject deployment is appropriate and whether non-specialist teaching compromises curriculum quality.

### Workshop Safety and Resources

Following several high-profile accidents in D&T workshops, Ofsted pays increased attention to health and safety procedures. The DATA Health & Safety Guide (now published by D&T Association) provides the sector standard that inspectors reference. Resource limitations—particularly in resistant materials and electronic components—frequently appear in inspection feedback.

## Best Practice Examples from Ofsted Reports

While individual school reports are not always publicly accessible, anonymised good practice includes:

**Curriculum Design:**
- Year 7-9 rotating curriculum exposing students to all material areas before GCSE options
- Explicit knowledge organisers for technical terminology and manufacturing processes
- Progressive complexity in design briefs from Year 7 to GCSE
- Integration of mathematical and scientific principles within authentic design contexts

**Pedagogy:**
- Teacher demonstrations followed by guided practice in skill development
- Regular design critiques encouraging peer evaluation and iterative improvement
- Use of exemplar materials showing progression in design portfolios
- Industry partnerships providing authentic design briefs and professional feedback

**Assessment:**
- Clear success criteria for both designing and making outcomes
- Regular formative assessment during iterative design process
- Moderation processes ensuring consistency across classes and year groups
- Assessment frameworks capturing both product quality and design thinking

## Future Directions

### Anticipated Developments

**Subject-Specific Research Review:**
The D&T sector anticipates Ofsted will eventually produce a research review similar to those published for English, mathematics and science. This would provide evidence-based guidance on effective D&T pedagogy, curriculum sequencing and assessment.

**Digital Technology Integration:**
Ofsted is likely to increase focus on how schools integrate CAD/CAM, laser cutting, 3D printing and other digital manufacturing technologies. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated some schools' adoption of digital design tools for home learning.

**STEM Integration:**
Following the government's STEM strategy and engineering skills shortages, Ofsted may scrutinise more closely how D&T contributes to STEM education and progression routes to engineering careers.

**Sustainability and Environmental Awareness:**
With the 2014 National Curriculum's emphasis on designing for sustainability, and broader policy focus on climate change, inspectors increasingly expect D&T curriculum to address environmental impact, circular economy principles and sustainable design.

### Sector Response and Advocacy

The D&T Association continues to engage with Ofsted, providing subject-specific expertise and challenging inspection approaches that inadequately recognise D&T's distinctive pedagogy. Recent advocacy has focused on:
- Recognition of D&T's contribution to creative and technical literacy
- Appropriate time allocation for practical subjects within inspection schedules
- Specialist subject knowledge among inspectors conducting deep dives
- Curriculum flexibility allowing schools to develop contextually appropriate D&T provision

## Conclusion

Ofsted's influence on D&T education extends beyond formal inspections to shape curriculum priorities, resource allocation and subject status within schools. While the current inspection framework's emphasis on curriculum quality potentially benefits D&T by requiring schools to articulate clear progression in designerly thinking and technical capability, challenges remain around subject time, specialist staffing and parity of esteem with EBacc subjects.

Effective D&T departments respond to inspection requirements not through superficial compliance but by developing genuinely coherent curricula that reflect the subject's distinctive contributions: developing students' capability to identify needs, generate creative solutions, and realise ideas through practical action. This aligns with the vision articulated by Kimbell, Stables, Barlex and other leading D&T educators while meeting Ofsted's expectations for ambitious, well-sequenced and effectively implemented curriculum provision.
