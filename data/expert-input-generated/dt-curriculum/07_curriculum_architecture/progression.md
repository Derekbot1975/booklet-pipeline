# Progression in Design and Making

## Introduction

Progression in Design and Making represents a fundamental principle in UK Design & Technology education, articulated through the sequential development of capability from Key Stage 1 through to Key Stage 5. As Richard Kimbell established in his seminal work *Assessing Technology* (1997), D&T capability emerges through the dynamic interplay between thinking and making, where progression cannot be understood through linear skill acquisition alone but through increasingly sophisticated integration of cognitive and practical dimensions.

The National Curriculum for England (DfE, 2013) establishes clear expectations that pupils should develop "the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world." Understanding how this capability develops progressively is essential for curriculum design and assessment.

## Theoretical Frameworks for Progression

### The APU Model of Design Capability

Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research (1991) established progression in D&T as the development of interaction between 'mind' and 'hand'. This model identifies progression occurring through:

- **Increasing autonomy** in decision-making within design activity
- **Growing sophistication** in the interaction between conceptual development and practical realisation
- **Enhanced ability** to hold and manipulate multiple variables simultaneously
- **Deeper understanding** of the relationship between materials, processes and outcomes

Kimbell argued that progression is not simply about making more complex products but developing more sophisticated capability to navigate design challenges.

### Stables' Progression Model

Kay Stables' research into progression (documented in *The Dynamics of Learning in Design and Technology*, 1998) emphasised the importance of understanding progression across different dimensions simultaneously:

- **Procedural progression**: developing more sophisticated design and making processes
- **Conceptual progression**: building deeper understanding of materials, systems and principles
- **Social progression**: engaging with increasingly complex contexts and user needs
- **Reflective progression**: developing critical evaluation and metacognitive awareness

Stables identified that effective progression requires explicit teaching of the connections between these dimensions, not isolated skill development.

## Progression Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At KS1, the National Curriculum expects pupils to learn to "design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users based on design criteria."

**Characteristic progression indicators include:**

- Moving from teacher-directed making to increased independence in selecting materials and techniques
- Developing from unplanned making to simple design-before-make approaches
- Building vocabulary to describe products, materials and processes
- Beginning to evaluate against simple stated criteria

**Practical implications for teachers:**

David Barlex, in *Nuffield Design & Technology* resources (2007), emphasises that KS1 progression requires careful scaffolding of design activities through concrete contexts. Teachers should provide structured design sheets with visual prompts while gradually reducing support as pupils develop independence.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The KS2 curriculum expects pupils to "apply their understanding of how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures" and "understand and use mechanical systems in their products."

**Progression characteristics:**

- Development from intuitive making to systematic planning involving multiple steps
- Growing ability to work from design specifications with several criteria
- Increased technical vocabulary and understanding of material properties
- Progression from basic tools to more complex equipment requiring skill development
- Enhanced ability to modify designs in response to evaluation

**Research evidence:**

Stephanie Atkinson's research (*Technology Education in Schools*, 2003) demonstrated that KS2 represents a critical period where pupils develop "design fixation" if not properly taught to generate and develop multiple ideas. Effective progression requires explicit teaching of ideation strategies including:

- Morphological analysis appropriate to age
- Structured exploration of alternatives before committing to solutions
- Use of modelling to test and refine ideas
- Systematic evaluation against multiple criteria

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The KS3 National Curriculum requires pupils to "develop and communicate design ideas using annotated sketches, detailed plans, 3-D and mathematical modelling, oral and digital presentations and computer-based tools."

**Progression involves:**

- Sophisticated iterative design processes where designing and making inform each other dynamically
- Ability to work with increasingly abstract concepts (e.g., sustainability, user-centred design)
- Development of specialised technical knowledge across material areas
- Competence with computer-aided design and manufacture
- Critical evaluation drawing on wider contextual knowledge

**Key progression challenges:**

Matt McLain's research (*Pedagogy in Design and Technology Education*, 2017) identified that transition from KS2 to KS3 often shows regression rather than progression, particularly where:

- Increased content coverage prevents depth of iterative development
- Assessment focuses on presentational rather than thinking quality
- Making becomes divorced from authentic design contexts
- Technical skill teaching is isolated from application

Effective KS3 progression requires what Barlex terms "authentic design contexts" where technical capability develops through purposeful application rather than decontextualised exercises.

## Progression in Designing

### Visual Communication Progression

Richard Kimbell and Kay Stables' work on graphicacy (*Understanding Practice in Design and Technology*, 1996) established that progression in visual communication involves:

**KS1-2 progression:**
- Simple labelled drawings to communicate basic ideas
- Development of plan and side view concepts
- Beginning to add dimensions and material specifications

**KS3-4 progression:**
- Orthographic projection understanding
- Sophisticated annotation linking design decisions to criteria
- Integration of sketching with CAD modelling
- Use of rendering to explore aesthetic qualities

**KS5 progression:**
- Technical drawings to professional standards
- Strategic use of different communication modes for different purposes
- Sophisticated use of digital tools for concept development and communication

### Iterative Development Progression

David Barlex's concept of "designing as a conversation with materials" (DATA Research Papers, 2003) provides a framework for understanding progression:

**Early stages:**
- Design-then-make with limited iteration
- Modification primarily corrective rather than developmental

**Intermediate stages:**
- Planned points for evaluation and modification
- Use of modelling to test specific design features
- Systematic comparison of alternatives

**Advanced stages:**
- Fluid movement between designing and making
- Strategic use of prototyping and modelling
- Sophisticated adaptation responding to testing and user feedback

## Progression in Making

### Technical Skills Development

Progression in making skills represents a complex journey documented by Kay Stables in DATA's *Minimum Competencies for Students* (1998). This framework establishes:

**Foundation level:**
- Safe, basic use of hand tools and materials
- Following instructions to produce outcomes
- Simple joining and finishing techniques

**Developing level:**
- Selection of appropriate tools and materials for specific purposes
- Accurate measurement and marking out
- Competence with machine tools under supervision
- Understanding of quality control in making

**Advanced level:**
- Precision working to demanding tolerances
- Independent troubleshooting and problem-solving in making
- Efficient workflow planning and resource management
- Sophisticated finishing techniques for professional-standard outcomes

### Progression in Manufacturing Understanding

Richard Kimbell's work on technological literacy (*PROJECT E, TERU*, 2004) emphasises that progression includes understanding industrial manufacturing:

**KS2-3 progression:**
- Awareness of volume production vs one-off making
- Understanding of basic manufacturing processes
- Introduction to quality control concepts

**KS4-5 progression:**
- Detailed knowledge of commercial manufacturing processes
- Understanding of production planning and systems
- Analysis of products using manufacturing knowledge
- Application of commercial practices in project work

Current GCSE specifications (AQA 8552, OCR J310, Edexcel 1DT0, WJEC Eduqas) all require explicit teaching of manufacturing industry, representing approximately 35-40% of examined content.

## Progression in Technical Knowledge

### Materials and Their Properties

Stephanie Atkinson's research (*Learning to Teach Design and Technology in the Secondary School*, 2nd ed., 2009) established that progression in materials knowledge moves through distinct phases:

**Experiential understanding (KS1-2):**
- Recognition of basic materials (wood, metal, plastic, textiles)
- Intuitive understanding developed through handling and testing
- Simple property vocabulary (strong, flexible, waterproof)

**Systematic understanding (KS3):**
- Classification systems for materials
- Understanding of structure-property relationships
- Conscious selection based on functional requirements
- Introduction to material origins and environmental impact

**Applied technical knowledge (KS4-5):**
- Detailed knowledge of material specifications and standards
- Understanding of material performance under different conditions
- Lifecycle analysis and environmental considerations
- Integration of materials knowledge with manufacturing processes

### Systems and Control Progression

The National Curriculum specifies progression in understanding mechanical and electrical systems. Research by David Barlex and Torben Steeg (*Nuffield Design & Technology Plus*, 2008) identified progression as:

**KS2:**
- Basic mechanical systems (levers, wheels, axles, simple gears)
- Simple electrical circuits with switches and components

**KS3:**
- Mechanical advantage and gear ratios
- Complex circuits with multiple components
- Introduction to programmable control systems

**KS4-5:**
- Sophisticated mechanical and electronic systems
- Microcontroller programming (Arduino, PIC, Raspberry Pi)
- Integration of multiple system types
- Mathematical analysis of system performance

## Assessment and Progression

### Formative Assessment for Progression

Kay Stables' work on assessment (*Assessment in Technology Education*, 2008) emphasised that progression requires formative assessment that:

- **Makes progression criteria explicit** to pupils through exemplification
- **Provides feedback** focused on developing capability rather than grading products
- **Uses dialogue** to develop pupils' understanding of quality
- **Involves pupils** in self and peer assessment against progression frameworks

The DATA publication *Assessing without Levels* (2015) provides practical frameworks for tracking progression across the D&T curriculum that many schools continue to use post-National Curriculum levels.

### Summative Assessment Structures

Current GCSE and A-Level specifications structure progression through:

**GCSE (KS4):**
All major exam boards structure assessment around 50% examined content and 50% non-examined assessment (NEA). Progression is demonstrated through:

- Increasingly sophisticated technical knowledge in examined papers
- Complex, sustained NEA projects demonstrating iterative development
- Integration of designing and making with contextual understanding

**A-Level (KS5):**
Specifications from AQA (7552), OCR (H405), Eduqas (A680QS) structure progression through:

- 50% examined content requiring detailed technical understanding
- 50% NEA demonstrating high-level independent capability
- Extended analysis integrating technical, social, environmental and economic factors

## Barriers to Progression

### The Making-Led Trap

Matt McLain's research (*Rethinking D&T Pedagogies*, 2015) identified that progression often stalls where:

- Making activities are selected for their intrinsic appeal rather than progression potential
- Projects become increasingly complex through addition rather than sophistication
- Assessment rewards presentation over thinking quality
- Time constraints prevent iterative development

McLain argues that effective progression requires "strategic restraint" where projects are carefully selected and scaled to allow depth rather than superficial complexity.

### The Knowledge-Practice Gap

David Barlex (*Beyond the Classroom*, DATA, 2011) documented persistent challenges where technical knowledge remains disconnected from application:

- Theoretical content taught separately from design projects
- Assessment structures that separate knowledge from practice
- Insufficient time for pupils to experiment and apply learning

Progression requires what Barlex terms "knowledge-in-use" approaches where technical learning is contextualised within authentic design challenges.

## Practical Strategies for Supporting Progression

### Curriculum Sequencing

Effective progression requires careful sequencing documented in schemes of work. Research-informed approaches include:

**Spiral curriculum approaches:**
Following Stephanie Atkinson's recommendations, revisit key concepts with increasing sophistication:
- KS2: Introduction to structures through simple frame construction
- KS3: Development through shell and reinforcement principles
- KS4: Application in complex products with mathematical analysis

**Building block approaches:**
Systematically develop prerequisite capabilities:
- Master basic joining before complex construction
- Develop design communication before complex project requirements
- Build technical vocabulary progressively

### Scaffolding and Fading

Kay Stables' research emphasises that progression requires:

- **Initial scaffolding:** Design sheets, process guides, technical demonstrations
- **Gradual fading:** Reducing support as capability develops
- **Challenge matching:** Appropriate difficulty level maintaining engagement
- **Metacognitive development:** Making progression explicit to pupils

## Progression in Focus Material Areas

### Textiles Progression

Specific progression in textiles involves:

**KS1-2:** Basic cutting, joining, decorating fabric; simple pattern use
**KS3:** Pattern adaptation, understanding fabric properties, introduction to CAD/CAM
**KS4-5:** Complex construction techniques, sophisticated surface decoration, integration of electronic textiles

### Food Progression

Following practical cooking requirements (National Curriculum 2014):

**KS1-2:** Basic food hygiene, simple preparation techniques, understanding healthy eating
**KS3:** Understanding nutrition scientifically, complex cooking techniques, menu planning
**KS4-5:** Food science application, commercial food production, dietary analysis

### Resistant Materials Progression

**KS2:** Basic wood and plastic construction
**KS3:** Introduction to metals, developing precision in working
**KS4-5:** Advanced processes, commercial manufacturing knowledge, CAD/CAM integration

## Conclusion

Progression in Design and Making represents a sophisticated developmental journey that cannot be reduced to simple skill ladders. As Richard Kimbell's work consistently demonstrates, authentic progression emerges through increasingly sophisticated integration of conceptual understanding with practical capability, developed through purposeful design activity.

For curriculum leaders and teachers, supporting progression requires:

- **Clear progression frameworks** that articulate development across dimensions
- **Careful project selection** that enables rather than constrains progression
- **Strategic assessment** that makes progression visible and guides development
- **Sufficient time** for iterative development and application of knowledge
- **Explicit teaching** of both processes and technical content

The challenge for UK D&T education, as Matt McLain and David Barlex have consistently argued, is maintaining focus on genuine capability development rather than superficial product complexity, ensuring that pupils' progression represents authentic technological literacy appropriate for our increasingly complex technological world.
