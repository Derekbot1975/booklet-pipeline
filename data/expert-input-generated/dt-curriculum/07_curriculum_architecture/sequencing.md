# Sequencing D&T Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in D&T

Sequencing in Design & Technology education refers to the deliberate organisation of learning experiences that enable pupils to develop increasingly sophisticated design and technological capability across Key Stages 1-4. As David Barlex (2007) emphasises in his work for the Nuffield Design & Technology Project, effective sequencing requires careful consideration of both the horizontal integration (within year groups) and vertical progression (across key stages) of knowledge, skills and understanding.

The challenge, as articulated by Richard Kimbell and Kay Stables in their landmark Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research (1991), is that D&T capability is not simply hierarchical but rather develops through iterative cycles of increasing complexity, where conceptual understanding and practical capability develop symbiotically.

## National Curriculum Framework for Sequencing

### Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology in England (DfE, 2013) establishes distinct purposes and expectations for each key stage:

**Key Stage 1**: Pupils should be taught to design purposeful, functional, appealing products based on design criteria; generate, develop, model and communicate ideas; select from and use a range of tools and equipment; explore and evaluate existing products and their own ideas.

**Key Stage 2**: Building on KS1, pupils should apply their understanding of computing and use mechanical systems in their products; understand how key events and individuals in design and technology have shaped the world.

**Key Stage 3**: Pupils should develop their design thinking and technical knowledge through iterative processes; apply computing and use electronics in products; undertake creative projects involving a range of materials; and develop a critical understanding of the impact of design and technology.

**Key Stage 4**: Through GCSE study, pupils demonstrate capability in identifying and investigating design possibilities; producing design solutions; analysing and evaluating design decisions and wider issues in design and technology.

## Principles for Effective Sequencing

### The Kimbell Model of Capability Development

Richard Kimbell's interaction model (1997, 2000) demonstrates that design capability develops through the dynamic interaction between 'imaging' (the mind's eye) and 'confronting reality' (hands-on making). Sequencing must therefore ensure regular opportunities for pupils to:

- Generate and develop ideas through drawing and modelling
- Test ideas against reality through practical work
- Refine understanding through reflection and iteration

Stephanie Atkinson's research (2000, 2006) on formative assessment in D&T reinforces that progression occurs when pupils encounter appropriately challenging design contexts that require them to synthesise prior learning whilst acquiring new knowledge and skills.

### Vertical Progression: Building Complexity

Matt McLain's work on curriculum progression (2012, 2017) identifies several dimensions along which complexity should increase:

**Design Context Complexity**: From familiar, single-user products (KS1) → products for specific user groups (KS2) → products considering multiple stakeholders (KS3) → complex problems involving competing requirements (KS4).

**Technical Demand**: From simple cuts, joins and finishes (KS1) → controlled mechanisms and circuits (KS2) → integrated mechanical/electrical systems (KS3) → sophisticated programmable systems (KS4).

**Material Range and Specificity**: From exploring basic properties (KS1) → understanding material categories (KS2) → selecting materials based on functional properties (KS3) → specifying materials with detailed technical understanding (KS4).

**Independence and Project Management**: From highly scaffolded tasks (KS1) → guided projects with teacher checkpoints (KS2) → substantial independent projects (KS3) → autonomous iterative development (KS4).

## Sequencing Knowledge Components

### Core Knowledge Categories

The D&T Association's progression framework (DATA, 2017) identifies knowledge components requiring systematic sequencing:

**Design Thinking Knowledge**: Understanding of how designers work, from simple evaluation of existing products to sophisticated application of design movements, ergonomics and anthropometrics.

**Technical Principles**: Properties of materials, structural integrity, mechanical advantage, electrical principles, programmable components - each requiring careful sequencing from phenomenological understanding to theoretical knowledge.

**Manufacturing and Production**: From basic hand tools to CAD/CAM systems, requiring developmental sequencing that ensures safe practice whilst progressively building capability.

**Wider Design Issues**: Sustainability, social responsibility, technological change - concepts introduced simply but revisited with increasing sophistication.

### Threshold Concepts in D&T

Kay Stables' research (2008) on threshold concepts in D&T identifies critical junctures where pupils must integrate understanding:

- **Fitness for Purpose**: Initially understood as 'working', progressing to sophisticated matching of design specifications to user needs
- **Iteration**: Moving from linear 'plan-make-evaluate' to understanding design as inherently iterative
- **Systems Thinking**: Developing from understanding components to grasping integrated systems
- **User-Centred Design**: Progressing from empathy to systematic application of ergonomic and human factors data

## Horizontal Integration: The 'Focused Practical Task' to 'Design and Make Assignment' Continuum

### Key Stage 3 Sequencing Models

The traditional KS3 model, refined through projects like 'Dissemination of a Structured Approach to Design and Technology in Schools' (Barlex & Steeg, 2017), sequences learning within key stages through:

**Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)**: Short, skill-focused activities developing specific techniques or knowledge. These should be deliberately sequenced to build towards more complex applications.

**Design and Make Assignments (DMAs)**: Extended projects requiring integration of multiple skills and knowledge domains. Effective sequencing ensures DMAs draw upon recently taught FPTs whilst introducing new challenges.

**Resource Tasks (RTs)**: Activities developing specific knowledge about materials, components or design approaches without necessarily producing a product. These provide essential contextual knowledge supporting DMAs.

Barlex argues (2003) that successful curriculum sequences alternate between these modes, ensuring pupils acquire necessary knowledge and skills through focused activities before applying them in integrated design contexts.

## Sequencing Across Primary-Secondary Transition

### The KS2-KS3 Bridge

Stephanie Atkinson's research on transition (2003) reveals significant discontinuity between primary and secondary D&T. Effective sequencing requires:

**Transition Auditing**: Secondary departments must understand what pupils have experienced in primary schools, recognising that provision varies considerably.

**Bridging Projects**: Year 7 sequences should include activities that simultaneously assess prior learning and introduce secondary workshop practices and expectations.

**Material and Tool Progression**: Many pupils arrive at KS3 with limited experience in resistant materials. Sequences must not assume uniform prior experience whilst maintaining appropriate challenge.

The DATA Transition Guide (2016) recommends Year 7 sequences begin with projects utilising familiar materials (card, corrugated plastic) before progressing to more demanding materials, whilst simultaneously introducing new technical knowledge.

## Subject Specialisation Sequencing (KS4)

### GCSE Pathways and Prerequisite Knowledge

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure specifications requiring careful KS3 sequencing:

**AQA Design and Technology (8552)**: Requires substantial prior knowledge in core technical principles, designing and making principles, and specialist knowledge in either timber, metals, polymers, textiles, electronic/mechanical systems, or combinations thereof. KS3 sequences must expose pupils to sufficient breadth to make informed specialisation choices whilst developing adequate depth.

**Edexcel Design and Technology (1DT0)**: Emphasises iterative design processes and commercial manufacturing. Effective sequencing ensures KS3 develops understanding of batch and volume production alongside craft-based making.

**OCR Design and Technology (J310)**: Structured around substantial iterative design and make tasks. KS3 sequencing should progressively increase project scale and independence.

**WJEC Eduqas Design and Technology (C680QS)**: Integrates technical principles with design and making skills. Requires careful sequencing of electronics and mechanical systems knowledge from KS3.

### Preparing for NEA Requirements

The Non-Exam Assessment component (typically 50% of GCSE) requires sophisticated capability developed across KS3:

**Research and Investigation Skills**: Must sequence from simple product analysis (Y7) through user research methods (Y8) to systematic design research (Y9).

**Iterative Development**: Progression from teacher-directed iteration to self-initiated refinement across KS3.

**Prototype Development**: From appearance models to functional prototypes to manufacturing specifications.

**Testing and Evaluation**: From subjective assessment to objective testing against measurable criteria.

## Sequencing Practical Skills Development

### Safe Progression in Workshop Practices

The British Standards Institution's framework for risk assessment in schools (BS 4163:2014) informs sequencing of practical work. Matt McLain's guidance (DATA, 2015) on safe progression recommends:

**Tool and Process Introduction**: Sequences should introduce hand tools before machine tools; subtraction before addition; simple mechanisms before complex systems.

**Progressive Autonomy**: From demonstration-and-replication (KS1-2) through guided practice with supervision (early KS3) to independent selection and application (later KS3-4).

**Material Hierarchy**: Consider inherent safety and difficulty - expanded polystyrene and corrugated plastic before MDF and acrylic; pre-manufactured electronic modules before soldering.

### CAD/CAM Sequencing

The integration of Computer-Aided Design and Manufacture requires deliberate sequencing:

**KS1-2**: Introduction to digital tools for communication and simple control.

**KS3 Years 7-8**: 2D CAD for design development; introduction to laser cutting and vinyl cutting.

**KS3 Year 9**: 3D modelling; understanding parametric design; introduction to CNC milling/routing.

**KS4**: Integration of CAD in iterative design processes; understanding commercial CAM applications; advanced features and simulation.

Barlex and Steeg's research (2008) demonstrates that CAD/CAM capability develops most effectively when digital and physical modelling are integrated within design processes rather than taught as separate skills.

## Sequencing Theoretical Knowledge

### Material Science Progression

Kay Stables' materials education framework (2010) suggests progression from:

**Phenomenological Understanding** (KS1-2): Direct sensory experience - materials feel, look, behave in certain ways.

**Categorical Knowledge** (Lower KS3): Understanding material families (metals, polymers, woods, textiles) and typical properties.

**Functional Understanding** (Upper KS3): Matching materials to purposes based on working properties; understanding how processing affects properties.

**Technical Specification** (KS4): Detailed knowledge of specific materials, commercial forms, British Standards, stock forms, and manufacturing implications.

### Electronics and Systems Knowledge

Progression in electronics requires careful sequencing from concrete to abstract:

**KS1-2**: Simple circuits with batteries, switches, buzzers - understanding that circuits must be complete.

**KS3 Year 7-8**: Series and parallel circuits; input-process-output systems; introduction to microcontrollers (BBC micro:bit, Arduino).

**KS3 Year 9**: Programmable systems; sensor integration; understanding gain and amplification.

**KS4**: Detailed understanding of electronic components; circuit design; integrated mechanical/electronic systems.

The DATA Electronics Guidance (2018) emphasises that progression requires regular practical application - theoretical knowledge of Ohm's Law or transistor function develops most effectively through design contexts requiring these concepts.

## Sequencing Design Thinking and Creativity

### From Structured to Autonomous Design Processes

Richard Kimbell's work on design capability (2000) demonstrates that effective designers move flexibly between thinking and doing. Sequencing must develop this flexibility:

**KS1-2**: Highly structured design processes with clear stages - 'Design-Make-Evaluate' provides initial framework.

**KS3 Years 7-8**: Introduction to iterative approaches; understanding that designs develop through making; formalisation through design folders documenting process.

**KS3 Year 9**: Greater autonomy in managing design processes; self-initiated iteration; understanding of diverse design strategies.

**KS4**: Independent management of extended projects; strategic selection of appropriate design methods; sophisticated documentation.

### Sketching and Communication Progression

Visual communication capability requires systematic development:

**KS1-2**: Freehand drawing showing general arrangements; use of templates and simple guides.

**KS3 Years 7-8**: Introduction to formal drawing conventions (isometric, oblique projection); annotation of ideas; exploded diagrams.

**KS3 Year 9**: Perspective sketching; technical drawing with dimensions; integration of CAD.

**KS4**: Strategic use of drawing types for different purposes; presentation techniques; use of British Standards conventions where appropriate.

## Assessment-Informed Sequencing

### Using Assessment to Validate Sequences

Stephanie Atkinson's research on formative assessment (2000, 2006) emphasises that effective sequences incorporate regular assessment opportunities revealing whether pupils are developing capability as intended:

**Diagnostic Assessment**: Early in sequences to establish prior learning and identify misconceptions.

**Formative Assessment**: Embedded throughout sequences, enabling responsive teaching and pupil awareness of progress.

**Summative Assessment**: At sequence endpoints, validating that intended learning has occurred and informing subsequent sequence planning.

The DATA Assessment Guidance (2019) recommends sequences include planned 'hinge point' assessments - critical junctures where teachers determine whether classes are ready to progress or require additional consolidation.

### Differentiation Within Sequences

Matt McLain's work on inclusive practice (2016) demonstrates that effective sequences provide:

**Core Entitlement**: All pupils experience fundamental knowledge and processes.

**Extension Opportunities**: Built into sequences rather than added afterwards - opportunities for greater depth, complexity or sophistication.

**Support Structures**: Pre-prepared scaffolds for pupils requiring additional support - writing frames, templates, exemplars - enabling participation in the same fundamental sequence.

## Subject Leadership Considerations

### Auditing and Developing Schemes of Work

Subject leaders ensuring effective sequencing should:

**Map Knowledge Progression**: Create explicit documentation showing when specific technical knowledge is introduced, reinforced and extended across key stages.

**Identify Dependencies**: Recognise where later learning depends on prior knowledge or skill development, ensuring prerequisites are secured.

**Review Transition Points**: Examine KS2-3 and KS3-4 transitions for gaps or unnecessary repetition.

**Monitor Curriculum Time**: Ensure sequences are realistic given actual curriculum time, including interruptions and practical work pace.

**Resource Audit**: Verify that required materials, tools, equipment and software are available to deliver sequences as planned.

### Whole-School Curriculum Integration

Effective D&T sequences connect with broader curriculum development:

**STEM Connections**: Explicit planning of where D&T reinforces and applies mathematical and scientific knowledge.

**Literacy Development**: Sequenced development of technical vocabulary and report-writing capabilities.

**Computing Integration**: Planned progression in computational thinking, programming and digital design.

The Barlex and Steeg model (2017) of 'projects that really work' emphasises authentic connections rather than forced integration - sequences should identify genuine opportunities where D&T contexts enable application of knowledge from other subjects.

## Contemporary Challenges in Sequencing

### Curriculum Time Constraints

Research by the Design and Technology Association (2021) reveals significant variation in KS3 D&T time allocation - from one hour weekly to two hours fortnightly or less. This directly impacts viable sequencing:

**Limited Contact Time**: Sequences must prioritise essential knowledge and core capability over comprehensive coverage.

**Interrupted Learning**: Projects spanning multiple weeks face challenges when lessons are fortnightly or interrupted by school events.

**Practical Implications**: Extended making activities require either blocking timetables or designing projects completable in short episodes.

### Teacher Expertise and Sequencing

The evolving nature of D&T teacher expertise affects sequencing decisions:

**Material Specialism**: Many secondary D&T teachers have deep expertise in specific materials. Sequences may reflect departmental strengths whilst acknowledging this may not provide balanced curriculum entitlement.

**Iterative Design Understanding**: Not all practitioners are confident with open-ended, iterative project sequences, potentially favouring more linear, predetermined outcomes.

**Emerging Technologies**: Rapid technological change (3D printing, laser cutting, programmable electronics) requires sequences responsive to new possibilities whilst maintaining foundational capability.

## Evidence-Based Sequence Development

### Research Evidence for Effective Sequences

David Barlex's extensive curriculum development work identifies characteristics of effective sequences:

**Authentic Contexts**: Projects with genuine purpose and real users engage pupils more effectively than artificial tasks.

**Appropriate Challenge**: Sequences pitched in Vygotsky's 'zone of proximal development' - achievable with effort but not without learning.

**Regular Making**: Theoretical knowledge becomes meaningful through application in practical contexts - sequences integrate rather than separate theory and practice.

**Visible Progression**: Pupils can articulate what they're learning and how it builds on prior learning.

### Practitioner Inquiry and Sequence Refinement

Subject leaders should establish cycles of curriculum evaluation:

**Monitoring Implementation**: Observing lessons to verify sequences work as intended.

**Pupil Voice**: Gathering feedback on challenge, engagement and perceived progress.

**Work Scrutiny**: Examining outcomes to identify where sequences successfully develop capability and where gaps appear.

**Assessment Analysis**: Using formative and summative assessment data to validate progression assumptions.
