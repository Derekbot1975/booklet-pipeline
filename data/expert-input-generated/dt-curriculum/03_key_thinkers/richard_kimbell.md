# Richard Kimbell

**Full Name:** John Richard Kimbell  
**Career Positions:** Emeritus Professor of Design and Technology Education, Goldsmiths, University of London  
**Known For:** Leading the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) Design and Technology project, developing the concept of 'design capability', creating the e-scape digital assessment system, and fundamentally shaping assessment theory and practice in Design & Technology education in the UK

---

## Biography and Career History

Richard Kimbell has been one of the most influential figures in Design & Technology education in the UK over the past four decades. His career has combined theoretical development, empirical research, and practical innovation in ways that have fundamentally shaped the subject.

Kimbell began his career as a secondary school teacher before moving into teacher education and research. He joined Goldsmiths, University of London, where he spent the majority of his academic career and established the Technology Education Research Unit (TERU). At Goldsmiths, he developed a reputation as both a rigorous researcher and a practical innovator, committed to understanding how young people design and how their capabilities might be authentically assessed.

His most significant early contribution came through his leadership of the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) Design and Technology project (1985-1991), commissioned by the Department of Education and Science. This groundbreaking national research project explored how to assess practical capability in design and technology across a representative sample of 15-year-olds in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. The APU project established many of the foundational principles that continue to underpin assessment thinking in the subject.

Following the APU work, Kimbell continued to research and write extensively about assessment, capability, creativity, and pedagogy in Design & Technology. He was appointed Professor of Design and Technology Education at Goldsmiths and later became Emeritus Professor following his formal retirement, though he has remained active in research and development.

From 2000 onwards, Kimbell led the development of e-scape (electronic solutions for creative assessment in portfolio environments), a revolutionary digital assessment system that fundamentally challenged traditional examination approaches. This work demonstrated how technology could enable more authentic, portfolio-based assessment at scale.

Throughout his career, Kimbell has supervised numerous doctoral students, many of whom have become influential figures in Design & Technology education themselves. He has also acted as a consultant to examination boards, government departments, and international education systems.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Design Capability

Kimbell's most enduring conceptual contribution is the notion of 'design capability'. Developed through the APU project, this concept emphasises that designing is not merely about acquiring discrete skills or knowledge, but rather about developing an integrated capability to operate effectively in design contexts.

In the seminal APU publication *The Assessment of Performance in Design and Technology* (1991), Kimbell and his colleagues argued that capability emerges through the "interaction of mind and hand" - a phrase that has become central to Design & Technology discourse. This formulation rejected artificial separations between thinking and making, mental and manual, or conception and execution.

Design capability, as Kimbell conceptualised it, involves:
- The ability to work with uncertainty and ambiguity
- Combining thought and action iteratively
- Moving fluidly between concrete and abstract thinking
- Developing and refining ideas through modelling and making
- Managing the complex interplay between different forms of knowledge and understanding

This holistic view directly challenged assessment approaches that attempted to measure 'design process' as a linear sequence of stages, or that separated 'designing' from 'making' in artificial ways.

### 2. The Thought and Action Model

Central to Kimbell's work is what has become known as the 'thought and action' model of designing, sometimes represented as an intertwined helix or spiral. This model, first articulated in the APU research, portrays designing as a constant oscillation between thinking (imaging, speculating, hypothesising, evaluating) and action (modelling, making, testing).

The model explicitly rejects the linear, stage-based representations of 'the design process' that were prevalent in earlier curriculum materials. Instead, Kimbell demonstrated through empirical research that effective young designers move rapidly and iteratively between thought and action, with each informing and developing the other.

This conceptualisation has had profound implications for both pedagogy and assessment, suggesting that authentic design activity cannot be captured through written work alone, nor through making divorced from conceptual development. The model emphasises that knowing emerges through doing, and doing is guided by knowing - they are inseparable aspects of design capability.

### 3. Authentic Assessment in Design & Technology

Kimbell has been perhaps the most influential critic of traditional written examination approaches in Design & Technology, arguing that such methods fundamentally misrepresent the nature of design capability. His research has consistently demonstrated that written examinations privilege verbal-linguistic intelligence and recall of declarative knowledge, while failing to capture the procedural, tacit, and material forms of knowing that are central to designing.

Through the APU project and subsequent research, Kimbell advocated for assessment approaches that:
- Are situated in authentic design contexts
- Allow extended time for iterative development
- Capture both process and outcome
- Enable students to demonstrate capability through multiple modes (drawing, modelling, making, writing)
- Assess integrated capability rather than atomised skills

This critique has been particularly pointed regarding the increasing examination emphasis in GCSE Design & Technology, which Kimbell has argued produces "dancing dogs" - students who learn to perform examination routines rather than developing genuine capability.

### 4. Portfolio-Based Assessment

Building directly from his critique of written examinations, Kimbell has championed portfolio assessment as a more authentic approach. His work has demonstrated how portfolios can capture the developmental journey of design thinking, the iterative nature of designing, and the integration of different forms of knowledge and representation.

However, Kimbell has also been acutely aware of the practical challenges of portfolio assessment at scale, particularly regarding:
- Reliability and comparability across different contexts
- Manageability for teachers and students
- The risk of portfolios becoming formulaic and process-driven
- The time and expertise required for quality assessment

The e-scape system emerged directly from Kimbell's attempts to address these challenges while preserving the authenticity advantages of portfolio approaches.

### 5. e-scape: Digital Innovation in Assessment

The e-scape project (2003-2010, with ongoing development) represents Kimbell's most ambitious attempt to resolve the tension between authentic, portfolio-based assessment and the demands of high-stakes, summative assessment at national scale.

e-scape uses digital technology to:
- Capture student work through photographs, audio commentary, and digital annotation
- Create chronologically-organised digital portfolios automatically
- Enable collaborative online moderation using comparative judgement techniques
- Reduce bureaucratic burden while increasing assessment validity

The system asks students to work on authentic design challenges over extended time periods (typically one day of intensive work). Students photograph their developing work, add audio commentary explaining their thinking, and the system automatically compiles this into a multimedia portfolio showing the developmental journey.

Crucially, e-scape moved away from criteria-based marking schemes toward comparative judgement, where assessors make holistic judgements by comparing pairs of portfolios. This approach, Kimbell argued, is more reliable, more valid, and better reflects how expert judgement actually operates.

The e-scape work has been taken up internationally and has influenced thinking about digital assessment well beyond Design & Technology, particularly regarding authentic assessment of creative and practical subjects.

### 6. The Primacy of Task Design

Throughout his work, Kimbell has emphasised that the quality of assessment depends fundamentally on the quality of the tasks students are asked to tackle. He has demonstrated that:
- Tasks must be sufficiently open to allow genuine designing to occur
- They must provide appropriate structure without over-determining outcomes
- The context must be meaningful and engaging to students
- Tasks should enable students to demonstrate the full range of their capability

Kimbell has been critical of examination tasks that reduce designing to formulaic procedure-following, or that constrain student response so tightly that genuine design thinking cannot emerge. His own assessment tasks, developed through APU and e-scape, have been characterised by:
- Real-world contexts that connect to students' experience
- Problems that genuinely need solving rather than artificial examination scenarios
- Opportunities for multiple valid solutions
- Requirements for both thinking and making

### 7. The Relationship Between Formative and Summative Assessment

Kimbell has consistently argued that summative assessment should not be regarded as separate from learning, but rather as part of a continuum of assessment that includes formative, diagnostic, and summative purposes. He has criticised assessment systems where summative assessment becomes detached from learning, leading to "teaching to the test" behaviours.

His work suggests that well-designed summative tasks should themselves be learning experiences, and that the evidence gathered through authentic design activity serves both learning and certification purposes. This integrated view challenges traditional boundaries between formative and summative assessment.

## Key Publications

### Major Books and Research Reports

**Kimbell, R., Stables, K., Wheeler, T., Wozniak, A., & Kelly, V. (1991).** *The Assessment of Performance in Design and Technology: The Final Report of the APU Design and Technology Project 1985-91.* London: Schools Examination and Assessment Council / HMSO.

This landmark publication reported the findings from the APU Design and Technology project. It established the theoretical framework of design capability, introduced the thought and action model, and presented empirical evidence from assessing thousands of students. The report fundamentally challenged prevailing assumptions about design process and assessment, and its influence continues to the present day.

**Kimbell, R., & Stables, K. (2007).** *Researching Design Learning: Issues and Findings from Two Decades of Research and Development.* Dordrecht: Springer.

This comprehensive volume synthesised Kimbell's research over two decades, from the APU project through to the early e-scape work. It presented detailed theoretical arguments about the nature of design capability, critiqued current curriculum and assessment practices, and offered evidence-based proposals for improvement. The book remains an essential reference for researchers in Design & Technology education.

**Kimbell, R. (2012).** *Evolving Project E-scape for National Assessment.* International Journal of Technology and Design Education, 22(2), 135-155.

This key paper documented the development and validation of the e-scape system, presenting evidence of its reliability and validity compared to traditional assessment methods. It articulated the theoretical principles underpinning the system and reported findings from pilot implementations.

### Significant Journal Articles and Chapters

**Kimbell, R. (1994).** *Progression in Learning and the Assessment of Children's Attainments in Art and Design.* In G. Banks (Ed.), *Assessment and Evaluation in the Arts.* London: Falmer Press.

This influential chapter extended Kimbell's thinking about assessment beyond Design & Technology to consider creative and practical subjects more broadly.

**Kimbell, R. (2000).** *Creativity in Crisis.* Journal of Design and Technology Education, 5(3), 206-211.

A powerful critique of government policy and examination practice, arguing that narrow assessment approaches were undermining genuine creativity and capability development in schools.

**Kimbell, R. (2007).** *E-assessment in Project e-scape.* Design and Technology Education: An International Journal, 12(2), 66-76.

This paper outlined the technical and pedagogical innovation of the e-scape approach, explaining how digital technology could transform assessment practice.

**Kimbell, R., Wheeler, T., Miller, S., & Pollitt, A. (2007).** *e-scape Portfolio Assessment Phase 2 Report.* London: Technology Education Research Unit, Goldsmiths, University of London.

A detailed technical report on the development and validation of e-scape, including statistical analysis of reliability and validity evidence.

**Kimbell, R., & Perry, D. (2001).** *Design and Technology in the Knowledge Economy.* London: Engineering Council.

This report examined the role of Design & Technology education in preparing students for a knowledge-based economy, arguing for the subject's centrality in developing creative and practical capability.

**Kimbell, R. (2011).** *Wrong...But Right: Design Assessment Based on Kantian Aesthetics.* In E. W. L. Norman & K. Seery (Eds.), *PATT 25: CRIPT 8 Conference Proceedings.* London: Goldsmiths, University of London.

This theoretical paper explored philosophical underpinnings of assessment judgement in design, drawing on Kantian aesthetics to explain why comparative judgement approaches are more valid than criteria-based marking.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Kimbell's work has profoundly influenced Design & Technology curriculum development in the UK since the late 1980s. The APU research findings were available during the development of the first Technology National Curriculum (1990), and the concept of 'capability' became central to curriculum formulations throughout the 1990s.

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology has consistently reflected Kimbell's emphasis on:
- Designing and making as integrated activity
- The importance of iterative development
- Learning through practical action
- The development of capability rather than merely skills acquisition

However, Kimbell has also been a persistent critic of aspects of curriculum development, particularly:
- The fragmentation of the subject into separate material areas
- Over-specification of content that constrains authentic designing
- Insufficient emphasis on designing relative to technical knowledge
- The dominance of examination requirements over educational principles

### Assessment and Examination Policy

Kimbell's influence on assessment policy has been both profound and contested. His critique of written examinations and advocacy for portfolio assessment helped shape the introduction of coursework components in GCSE Design & Technology in the late 1980s and 1990s.

However, the relationship between Kimbell's assessment philosophy and actual examination practice has often been problematic. While his ideas influenced the introduction of coursework, he became increasingly critical of how coursework was implemented, arguing that it became:
- Over-specified and formulaic
- Process-focused rather than outcome-focused
- A means of reproducing examination requirements rather than developing capability
- Vulnerable to malpractice concerns

The e-scape project represented an explicit attempt to influence national assessment policy by demonstrating that authentic, valid, and reliable assessment was possible at scale. While e-scape has been used in several contexts, including by awarding organisations, it has not achieved the systemic transformation Kimbell hoped for.

His critique of the reduction in coursework weighting and the increased emphasis on written examination (particularly from 2017 onwards) has been sharp. He has argued that these policy changes fundamentally undermine the educational purposes of Design & Technology and prioritise administrative convenience over authentic assessment.

### International Influence

Kimbell's work has influenced Design & Technology education internationally, particularly in countries with educational systems similar to the UK. The APU research has been widely cited internationally, and the thought and action model has been adopted in curriculum frameworks in several countries.

The e-scape system has been piloted and adopted in several international contexts, including Scotland, Ireland, Singapore, and various Australian states. This international take-up demonstrates the wider applicability of Kimbell's assessment principles beyond the specific UK policy context.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Principles of Effective Design & Technology Teaching

While Kimbell's work has focused primarily on assessment and capability rather than classroom pedagogy per se, his research has clear pedagogical implications that have influenced teaching practice.

Effective teaching, in Kimbell's framework, should:
- **Provide authentic design contexts** that require genuine problem-solving rather than following predetermined procedures
- **Enable iterative development** with time and resources for students to develop, test, and refine ideas
- **Balance guidance and autonomy** - providing sufficient structure while allowing genuine design decisions
- **Value multiple forms of representation** - encouraging students to work through drawing, modelling, making, and explaining
- **Connect thought and action** - ensuring that conceptual development and practical activity are integrated
- **Focus on capability development** rather than merely skills acquisition or knowledge accumulation

### The Role of Modelling and Making

Kimbell's research consistently emphasised that modelling and making are not merely ways of realising pre-formed ideas, but are fundamental modes of thinking. Practical action generates insights, reveals problems, suggests solutions, and develops understanding.

This has clear implications for teaching:
- Students should be encouraged to begin practical exploration early rather than spending extended time on paper-based planning
- Quick, rough modelling should be privileged over highly finished presentation work
- Making should be understood as part of designing, not something that happens after designing is complete
- Teachers should help students recognise and articulate the learning that emerges through practical action

### Assessment for Learning

While critical of much assessment practice, Kimbell strongly supported formative assessment approaches that genuinely support learning. His work suggests that effective formative assessment in Design & Technology:
- Focuses on developing students' capability to self-evaluate and refine their work
- Uses authentic design challenges that themselves drive learning
- Provides feedback on integrated capability rather than atomised skills
- Helps students develop metacognitive awareness of their design thinking

## Critical Reception and Ongoing Debates

### Support and Adoption

Kimbell's work has been enormously influential among Design & Technology educators, researchers, and teacher educators. The concept of design capability and the thought and action model have become foundational ideas in the field, widely taught in initial teacher education and cited in curriculum guidance.

His critique of narrow assessment practices has resonated with many teachers who have experienced the constraining effects of examination requirements on authentic design activity. The e-scape project has been celebrated as an innovative attempt to resolve longstanding assessment problems.
