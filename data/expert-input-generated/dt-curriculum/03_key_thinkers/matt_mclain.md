# Matt McLain

**Full Name:** Matt McLain

**Current Role:** Senior Lecturer in Design and Technology Education, Edge Hill University

**Known For:** Pioneering work in maker pedagogy, collaborative design approaches, teacher education in D&T, and advocating for inclusive and creative design practices in secondary education

---

## Biography

Matt McLain is a Senior Lecturer in Design and Technology Education at Edge Hill University, where he has established himself as a leading voice in contemporary D&T pedagogy and teacher education. His work bridges the gap between traditional craft-based D&T teaching and emerging maker culture approaches, providing practical frameworks for teachers whilst maintaining rigorous attention to the subject's educational value.

McLain's career in education began as a secondary D&T teacher, where he developed expertise across the broad range of material areas within the subject. His classroom experience informs his research and teacher education work, giving him particular credibility when addressing the practical realities of D&T teaching in resource-constrained environments. This grounding in classroom practice has enabled him to develop pedagogical approaches that are both theoretically sound and practically applicable.

At Edge Hill University, McLain has been instrumental in developing initial teacher education programmes that prepare the next generation of D&T teachers. He works extensively with trainee teachers, mentor teachers, and school partnerships, giving him unique insights into the current state of D&T education across the North West region and beyond. His work encompasses both undergraduate and postgraduate teacher education routes.

McLain has become particularly associated with research into collaborative design practices, maker pedagogy, and the integration of digital technologies within D&T. His work frequently explores how teachers can develop learners' design thinking whilst maintaining the hands-on, material-focused nature of the subject that has historically defined it.

He is an active member of the Design and Technology Association (DATA) and has contributed to various working groups examining curriculum development and teacher professional development. McLain regularly presents at conferences, including the DATA Research Conference, where he shares findings from his ongoing research projects.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Maker Pedagogy in Design & Technology

McLain has been at the forefront of articulating what maker pedagogy means specifically within the context of secondary D&T education. Unlike some interpretations that see maker culture as replacement for traditional D&T, McLain positions making as complementary to established practice. He argues that maker pedagogy can revitalise D&T by emphasising experimentation, iteration, and collaborative problem-solving whilst retaining the subject's focus on designing and making quality outcomes.

His conception of maker pedagogy in D&T emphasises several principles: rapid prototyping as a design strategy; cross-material experimentation; embracing failure as a learning opportunity; collaborative rather than purely individual project work; and integration of digital fabrication tools alongside traditional techniques. Crucially, McLain maintains that maker pedagogy must serve educational aims rather than simply replicating hobby-focused making activities.

McLain has developed practical frameworks for teachers wanting to integrate maker approaches without abandoning the rigour and progression that characterises effective D&T teaching. This includes guidance on structuring projects that balance open-ended exploration with clear skill development and design process understanding.

### 2. Collaborative Design in Secondary D&T

Much of McLain's research focuses on collaborative design practices in secondary schools. He has investigated how group design projects can develop students' communication skills, negotiation abilities, and design thinking whilst also producing higher-quality outcomes than purely individual work. This research challenges the traditional emphasis on individual project work that has dominated D&T assessment structures.

McLain's work identifies specific pedagogical strategies that enable productive collaboration in D&T classrooms. These include structured design critiques, role allocation within teams, and assessment approaches that recognise both individual and group contributions. He has explored how collaborative projects can be managed within the constraints of the National Curriculum and GCSE specifications.

His research demonstrates that collaborative design approaches can be particularly beneficial for certain learner groups, including students with lower confidence in practical skills who can contribute design ideas and research within group contexts. This has implications for inclusion and differentiation within D&T teaching.

### 3. Digital Integration Without Digital Dominance

McLain advocates for thoughtful integration of digital technologies within D&T without allowing them to dominate or define the subject. His work examines how CAD/CAM, laser cutting, 3D printing, and programmable systems can enhance rather than replace traditional making skills. He argues that digital tools should be positioned as additional techniques within a broad technological repertoire rather than seen as inherently superior to hand tools and conventional processes.

This perspective is particularly important in addressing concerns that D&T is becoming overly focused on computer-based work at the expense of hands-on making. McLain's research provides evidence that blended approaches—combining digital design with hand finishing, or using digital fabrication for components within traditionally constructed projects—can produce both higher-quality outcomes and deeper learning.

### 4. Teacher Knowledge and Confidence in D&T

Through his teacher education work, McLain has developed insights into how trainee and early-career teachers develop the multi-faceted knowledge base required for effective D&T teaching. His research examines pedagogical content knowledge specific to D&T, including how teachers learn to scaffold design thinking, manage complex practical classrooms, and assess iterative design work.

McLain has identified particular challenges facing new D&T teachers, including developing confidence across multiple material areas, managing health and safety in workshop environments, and balancing curriculum demands with creative project work. His work with initial teacher education students has informed development of more effective preparation programmes that address these challenges systematically.

He has also contributed to understanding of continuing professional development needs for established D&T teachers, particularly regarding new technologies and changing curriculum requirements. His research highlights the importance of subject-specific CPD rather than generic teaching and learning approaches.

### 5. Inclusive Practice in D&T

McLain's work addresses inclusion and equity within D&T education, examining how the subject can be made accessible to all learners whilst maintaining its distinctive character. This includes research into supporting students with SEND in practical environments, addressing gender imbalances in material area preferences, and ensuring D&T is valued across the full ability range rather than positioned as vocational provision for less academic students.

His research has explored how differentiation can be achieved through project structure, tool choice, and collaborative working arrangements rather than simply producing "easier" versions of projects for some students. This approach maintains high expectations whilst providing appropriate support.

### 6. Design Process Teaching

McLain has contributed significantly to understanding how design processes can be taught effectively in secondary D&T. His work builds on established models like those of Kimbell and Stables but provides practical classroom strategies for helping students develop genuine design thinking rather than simply completing documentation exercises.

He emphasises the importance of authentic design contexts, real-world connections, and iterative making processes where designing and making are interleaved rather than sequential. McLain's research has examined how sketch modelling, quick prototyping, and design testing can be embedded within project work to develop students' design capabilities.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Teacher Education Philosophy

McLain's approach to teacher education in D&T emphasises the development of reflective practitioners who can make informed pedagogical decisions rather than simply following prescribed methods. He prepares trainee teachers to navigate the complexities of D&T teaching, including balancing curriculum requirements with creative project work, managing diverse learner needs, and developing students' design thinking.

His teacher education practice models the collaborative, iterative approaches he advocates for school D&T. Trainee teachers engage in design projects themselves, developing material knowledge and design capabilities whilst simultaneously analysing the pedagogical strategies being employed. This meta-cognitive approach helps trainee teachers understand learning processes from student perspectives.

McLain emphasises the importance of developing confidence across multiple material areas and technologies rather than specialising narrowly. This reflects the broad requirements of secondary D&T teaching and the importance of maintaining the subject's distinctive multi-material character.

### Research-Informed Practice

McLain's work exemplifies research-informed practice, where classroom investigations directly inform pedagogical recommendations. His research questions emerge from practical teaching challenges, ensuring relevance to the teaching community. This practitioner-research orientation characterises much of his work.

He collaborates extensively with teachers and schools, conducting research in authentic classroom contexts rather than controlled experimental settings. This approach produces findings with high ecological validity and immediate applicability, though it requires careful attention to research design to maintain rigour.

McLain's dissemination strategy prioritises accessibility, ensuring research findings reach practising teachers through channels they regularly access. This commitment to impact beyond academic publications reflects understanding that educational research must influence practice to justify its existence.

### Inclusive and Creative Practice

Throughout his work, McLain maintains that D&T should be both inclusive and creatively challenging. He rejects false dichotomies between technical skill development and creative expression, between traditional and digital techniques, or between academic and vocational purposes. His pedagogy seeks to hold multiple purposes in productive tension.

This inclusive approach extends to his conception of what counts as valuable making. McLain values both beautiful craft outcomes and functional problem-solving, both individual skill mastery and collaborative project work, both traditional workshop practice and experimental making with novel materials.

His pedagogical recommendations consistently consider how approaches work for diverse learners rather than assuming all students will respond identically. This attention to differentiation and inclusion reflects both ethical commitments and practical understanding of contemporary comprehensive school contexts.

## Contribution to Design & Technology Theory

McLain's work contributes to theoretical understanding of D&T education in several ways. His research on collaborative design extends understanding of how design thinking develops in social contexts rather than purely individual cognition. This has implications for theories of design learning and capability development.

His articulation of maker pedagogy within the specific context of formal D&T education helps distinguish school-based making from hobby making or higher education fabrication practices. This theoretical clarification is important as terms like "maker education" are used increasingly loosely in educational discourse.

McLain's work on teacher knowledge in D&T contributes to broader understanding of pedagogical content knowledge in practical subjects. His research helps articulate what teachers need to know and be able to do specifically for D&T teaching, adding to the more general teacher knowledge frameworks developed by educational researchers like Shulman.

By maintaining attention to both practical making and design thinking, both individual skill development and collaborative working, McLain's theoretical position reflects the complexity and multi-faceted nature of D&T as a school subject. This resist reductionist tendencies that would narrow the subject to either vocational skills training or purely conceptual design education.

## Current and Future Directions

McLain continues to investigate collaborative design practices in secondary D&T, with particular attention to how such approaches can be implemented within current assessment frameworks. This work addresses a key challenge for teachers who recognise the value of collaborative projects but face individual assessment requirements in GCSE and A-Level specifications.

His ongoing work with initial teacher education students provides continuing insights into the development of teacher knowledge and confidence in D&T. As the subject faces teacher recruitment and retention challenges nationally, understanding how to prepare and support teachers effectively becomes increasingly important.

McLain remains engaged with questions about digital technology integration in D&T, particularly as accessible tools like 3D printing and laser cutting become more common in schools. His work continues to advocate for balanced approaches that maintain material diversity rather than allowing digital fabrication to dominate.

The development of maker pedagogy approaches applicable in mainstream secondary schools remains a focus. As the initial enthusiasm for maker education matures, questions about sustainability, assessment, and progression become more pressing. McLain's work provides grounded perspectives based on classroom realities rather than idealised makerspaces.

## Significance for UK D&T Education

Matt McLain represents a generation of D&T educators who bridge traditional craft-based practice and contemporary digital making cultures. His work provides practical pathways for teachers seeking to evolve their practice whilst maintaining the essential character of D&T as a hands-on, material-focused subject.

His contribution to teacher education is particularly significant at a time when D&T faces challenges in teacher recruitment and subject status. By preparing confident, reflective new teachers with strong pedagogical understanding, McLain's work supports the sustainability of the subject into the future.

McLain's balanced perspective on contentious issues within D&T—avoiding both uncritical technophilia and defensive traditionalism—models the thoughtful, evidence-informed approach the subject community needs. His work demonstrates that D&T can evolve and embrace new approaches whilst maintaining its distinctive educational value.

Through accessible dissemination and engagement with practising teachers, McLain ensures his research influences classroom practice. This commitment to impact reflects understanding that educational research must ultimately serve learners and teachers rather than purely academic audiences.
