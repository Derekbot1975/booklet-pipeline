# Stephanie Atkinson

**Full Name:** Stephanie Atkinson

**Current Role:** Professor Emerita of Design and Technology Education, University of Sunderland

**Known For:** Pioneering work in food technology and textiles technology education; development of health and safety frameworks in D&T; leadership in teacher education and curriculum development; advocacy for practical capability and design thinking in schools

---

## Biography and Career History

### Academic Career

Professor Stephanie Atkinson spent the majority of her distinguished career at the University of Sunderland, where she became a leading figure in Design and Technology education. She served as Professor of Design and Technology Education and led the D&T teacher education programmes at Sunderland for over two decades. Her work at Sunderland established the institution as a significant centre for D&T pedagogy and research, particularly in the areas of food technology and textiles technology.

Atkinson's academic career was characterised by a commitment to bridging theory and practice in D&T education. She worked extensively with schools, teachers, and curriculum bodies to ensure that D&T provision remained relevant, rigorous, and safe. Her influence extended beyond Sunderland through her involvement with national bodies and her extensive publication record.

### Professional Involvement

Throughout her career, Atkinson held numerous influential positions:

- She was actively involved with the Design and Technology Association (DATA), the professional body for D&T teachers, contributing to policy discussions and teacher support materials
- She served on various national committees examining D&T curriculum development and assessment
- She was a regular contributor to D&T professional development programmes across England
- She worked closely with examination boards on qualification development, particularly in food technology and textiles technology
- She supervised numerous doctoral students who have themselves become influential in D&T education

### Areas of Expertise

Atkinson developed particular expertise in:

1. **Food Technology Education**: Developing pedagogical approaches that combined practical skills with scientific understanding, nutrition education, and consumer awareness
2. **Textiles Technology**: Advancing textiles as a technological subject area with equal status to resistant materials and electronic systems
3. **Health and Safety**: Creating comprehensive frameworks for risk management in D&T workshops and practical rooms
4. **Teacher Education**: Designing and delivering Initial Teacher Education (ITE) programmes that produced confident, competent D&T teachers
5. **Curriculum Development**: Contributing to national curriculum revisions and qualification reforms

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. The Integration of Theory and Practice in Food Technology

Atkinson argued consistently that food technology education must go beyond simply teaching cooking skills. She developed a conceptual framework that positioned food technology as a design-based subject where students engage with:

- The science of food (including nutrition, food chemistry, and microbiology)
- Systems thinking in food production and supply chains
- Cultural and social dimensions of food choices
- Sustainability and ethical considerations in food technology
- Design processes applied to food product development

This framework influenced how food technology was taught in Initial Teacher Education programmes and shaped curriculum guidance documents. Atkinson emphasised that practical capability must be underpinned by conceptual understanding, enabling students to make informed decisions as designers and consumers.

### 2. Textiles Technology as a Design and Technology Subject

Atkinson was instrumental in reconceptualising textiles education within the D&T framework. She challenged outdated perceptions of textiles as solely a 'craft' or 'domestic' subject, instead positioning it as:

- A technologically sophisticated area involving smart materials, CAD/CAM applications, and industrial processes
- A vehicle for developing design thinking and creative problem-solving
- An area where sustainability and ethical manufacture could be explored meaningfully
- A context for understanding material science and engineering principles

Her work in this area helped ensure that textiles technology retained its place within the D&T curriculum during periods when it was under threat. She published extensively on textiles pedagogy and worked with teachers to develop innovative approaches to textiles teaching that emphasised technological understanding alongside practical skills.

### 3. Health and Safety Frameworks for D&T

Atkinson recognised that effective health and safety management was essential for maintaining D&T as a practical subject in schools. She developed comprehensive frameworks that:

- Distinguished between hazards and risks, promoting dynamic risk assessment rather than risk avoidance
- Provided clear guidance on supervision ratios, room layouts, and equipment maintenance
- Addressed specific safety concerns in food rooms (allergens, cross-contamination, temperature control) and textiles rooms (machinery guards, electrical safety, chemical handling)
- Emphasised the educational value of teaching students to work safely and assess risks themselves
- Balanced safety requirements with the need for authentic, engaging practical experiences

Her work in this area was characterised by pragmatism and a commitment to enabling rather than restricting practical work. She contributed to DATA publications on safe practice and influenced CLEAPSS (Consortium of Local Education Authorities for the Provision of Science Services) guidance specific to D&T contexts.

### 4. The 'Complete Designer' Concept in Teacher Education

Atkinson developed the concept of the 'complete designer' in her work with student teachers. This framework identified the knowledge, skills, and attributes that D&T teachers needed to develop in their trainees:

- **Technical capability**: The ability to work with materials, tools, and processes competently and safely
- **Design capability**: Understanding and applying design processes, including research, ideation, development, and evaluation
- **Technological understanding**: Knowledge of how materials, systems, and products function, and the scientific principles underlying them
- **Critical awareness**: The ability to evaluate existing products and design solutions critically
- **Social and environmental responsibility**: Understanding the broader impacts of design and technology decisions

This holistic framework influenced ITE curricula at Sunderland and beyond, ensuring that trainee teachers developed comprehensive capabilities rather than narrow technical skills.

### 5. Formative Assessment in Practical Subjects

Atkinson contributed significantly to understanding how formative assessment works in D&T contexts. She explored:

- How teachers can provide effective feedback on practical work and design processes
- The role of modelling, demonstration, and exemplars in supporting student learning
- Strategies for making design thinking visible and open to assessment
- The challenges of assessing creativity, innovation, and problem-solving
- How to balance process and product in D&T assessment

Her work in this area drew on research from the Assessment for Learning movement but applied it specifically to the unique challenges of D&T. She emphasised that effective assessment in D&T must capture the iterative, often messy nature of design work, not just the finished outcomes.

### 6. Specialist Subject Knowledge in D&T Teacher Education

Atkinson was a strong advocate for D&T teachers having deep specialist knowledge in at least one material area while maintaining broader competence across the subject. She argued that:

- The breadth of D&T made it impossible for teachers to be equally expert in all areas
- Initial Teacher Education should enable trainees to develop one or two specialisms deeply (such as food technology or textiles technology)
- Schools needed to recognise and value specialist expertise within D&T departments
- Continuing Professional Development (CPD) should support teachers in maintaining and extending their specialist knowledge
- Collaboration within departments was essential, with teachers sharing their specialist expertise

This position influenced how many ITE programmes structured their D&T provision and contributed to debates about whether D&T should be taught as a unified subject or through distinct specialisms.

### 7. Capability and 'Learning by Doing'

Drawing on the work of earlier D&T theorists, Atkinson championed the importance of practical capability in D&T education. She argued that:

- Practical work was not merely a motivational 'add-on' but central to how students develop technological understanding
- 'Learning by doing' in D&T involved sophisticated cognitive processes, including problem-solving, decision-making, and evaluation
- Practical activities should be intellectually challenging, not just manually engaging
- The development of practical capability had broader educational value, developing transferable skills and attributes

Atkinson's work in this area helped counter narratives that devalued practical subjects and contributed to maintaining D&T's status within the secondary curriculum.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Learning Through Design Projects

Atkinson advocated for project-based learning in D&T, where students engage in authentic design challenges. Her approach emphasised:

- **Real-world contexts**: Projects should connect to students' lives and broader societal issues
- **Open-ended challenges**: Problems should allow for multiple solutions and creative responses
- **Iterative processes**: Students should experience the non-linear nature of design work, with opportunities to test, evaluate, and refine
- **Integration of knowledge and skills**: Projects should require students to draw on theoretical understanding while developing practical capabilities

### Scaffolded Practical Work

In food and textiles technology particularly, Atkinson promoted scaffolded approaches to developing practical skills:

- **Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)**: Short, focused activities to develop specific skills or knowledge
- **Progressive skill development**: Carefully sequenced activities building from foundational skills to complex techniques
- **Demonstration and modelling**: Teachers making their own thinking and processes visible to students
- **Independent practice**: Opportunities for students to apply skills in their own design work

### Critical Engagement with Products and Systems

Atkinson emphasised that D&T students should develop critical awareness of the designed world:

- **Product analysis**: Examining existing products to understand design decisions, materials, processes, and impacts
- **Systems thinking**: Understanding how products fit into larger systems (supply chains, user networks, environmental systems)
- **Evaluation of social and environmental impacts**: Questioning who benefits from design decisions and what the broader consequences are
- **Consumer education**: Developing informed, critical consumers who can make reasoned choices

### Inclusive Practice in D&T

Atkinson promoted inclusive approaches to D&T that enabled all students to succeed:

- **Differentiation in practical work**: Providing multiple entry points and routes through projects
- **Adaptation for diverse learners**: Ensuring that practical activities were accessible to students with varying needs
- **Cultural responsiveness**: Particularly in food technology, recognising and valuing diverse food cultures and practices
- **Gender equality**: Challenging stereotypes in both food and textiles technology and promoting these areas as valuable for all students

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

### Impact on Teacher Education

The programmes Atkinson led at Sunderland produced generations of D&T teachers who have gone on to leadership roles in schools and the wider D&T community. Many of her former students credit her with:

- Developing their confidence in teaching food and textiles technology
- Providing frameworks for understanding D&T as a pedagogical discipline
- Instilling high standards for health and safety in practical teaching
- Fostering a commitment to maintaining breadth and balance in D&T provision

### Influence on Professional Community

Atkinson was a respected voice in the D&T professional community, known for:

- Clear, pragmatic guidance grounded in classroom realities
- Advocacy for maintaining specialist expertise in food and textiles
- Commitment to safety without sacrificing authentic practical experiences
- Supporting early-career teachers through accessible publications and advice

### Contribution to D&T Scholarship

Atkinson's scholarly work helped establish D&T education as a legitimate field of academic inquiry. She:

- Published research that bridged educational theory and D&T practice
- Supervised doctoral students whose work advanced understanding of D&T teaching and learning
- Contributed to international conferences and publications, sharing UK perspectives on D&T education
- Demonstrated how practice-based research could inform policy and curriculum development

## Relevance to Current D&T Education

### Contemporary Challenges

Atkinson's work remains relevant to current challenges in D&T:

**Curriculum Pressures**: Her arguments for maintaining breadth in D&T, including food and textiles, continue to inform debates about subject content as schools face pressure to narrow their curriculum offer.

**Teacher Supply**: Her frameworks for developing subject knowledge in ITE are relevant to ongoing concerns about D&T teacher recruitment and retention, particularly in specialist areas.

**Practical Work in Schools**: Her health and safety frameworks provide essential guidance for schools maintaining practical facilities in an era of budget constraints and increasing regulation.

**Food Education**: With renewed policy focus on food education, nutrition, and cooking skills, Atkinson's work on integrating practical capability with scientific understanding offers valuable pedagogical models.

**Sustainability and Ethics**: Her emphasis on critical engagement with products and systems aligns with current curriculum priorities around sustainability, ethical design, and social responsibility.

### Enduring Principles

Several principles from Atkinson's work have enduring value:

1. **The integration of theory and practice**: D&T learning should develop both conceptual understanding and practical capability
2. **Specialist expertise matters**: Teachers need deep knowledge in at least one area of D&T to teach effectively
3. **Safety enables learning**: Effective health and safety management makes authentic practical work possible
4. **Design thinking is disciplined thinking**: D&T develops rigorous cognitive processes, not just manual skills
5. **Context matters**: Learning in D&T should connect to students' lives and broader social issues
