# Kay Stables

**Full Name:** Professor Kay Stables

**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Design and Technology Education, Goldsmiths, University of London

**Known For:** Pioneering research in design cognition, assessment in Design & Technology, primary D&T education, and establishing theoretical frameworks that have shaped UK D&T curriculum policy. Leading figure in understanding how children learn through designing and making.

---

## Biography and Career History

### Academic Career

Professor Kay Stables established her career at Goldsmiths, University of London, where she became one of the most influential researchers in Design & Technology education in the UK. Her work at Goldsmiths spanned several decades, during which she built an internationally recognised research profile focusing on design cognition, assessment practices, and primary education.

Stables' academic trajectory positioned her at the forefront of D&T educational research during critical periods of curriculum development in England and Wales. She served as Director of the Technology Education Research Unit (TERU) at Goldsmiths, a centre that became instrumental in shaping evidence-based policy for D&T education.

### Contributions to Professional Bodies

Throughout her career, Stables worked closely with the Design and Technology Association (DATA), subsequently the Design and Technology Association (DATA) and later absorbed into other professional bodies. She contributed significantly to professional development materials and frameworks used by teachers across the UK.

Her work has been recognised internationally, with invitations to contribute to curriculum development projects and conferences across Europe, North America, and Australasia.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The APU Framework and Design Capability

One of Stables' most significant contributions was her involvement with and development from the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) framework. Building on the original APU Design & Technology work (1987-1991), Stables extended the conceptualisation of 'design capability' as a holistic construct comprising:

- **Conceptual capability:** Understanding design contexts, purposes, and principles
- **Active capability:** Physical and cognitive skills in designing and making
- **Executive capability:** Managing design activities, decision-making, and project progression

This framework moved D&T assessment beyond simple skills checklists towards recognising design as an integrated cognitive activity. Stables argued that authentic assessment in D&T must capture the interplay between thinking and doing, rather than separating them into discrete components.

### 2. Design & Technology in Primary Education

Stables became a leading advocate for establishing D&T as a rigorous subject in primary schools. Her research challenged the perception that primary D&T was merely 'craft work' or unsystematic making activities. Key arguments included:

- Primary children are capable of sophisticated design thinking when appropriately scaffolded
- Design projects develop transferable cognitive skills including problem-solving, evaluation, and systems thinking
- Integration of D&T with other subjects (particularly science and mathematics) enhances learning across the curriculum
- Teacher confidence and subject knowledge are critical barriers requiring targeted professional development

Her work with primary teachers emphasised practical approaches to implementing meaningful D&T education with limited resources and time.

### 3. Design Cognition and Children's Learning

Stables' research into design cognition examined how children think whilst designing. Drawing on cognitive psychology and observational studies, she identified characteristic patterns in children's design behaviour:

- **Iterative thinking:** Children naturally move between different aspects of a problem rather than following linear design processes
- **Concrete reasoning:** Young designers often think through making, using materials as cognitive tools
- **Imaging and modelling:** The role of drawing, modelling, and prototyping as thinking strategies rather than merely recording outcomes
- **Contextual influences:** How task setting, materials, and social environments shape design thinking

This work challenged simplified 'design process' models that presented designing as a series of discrete, sequential steps. Stables argued for teaching approaches that recognised and supported the fluid, complex nature of authentic design activity.

### 4. Assessment Authenticity in D&T

A central theme throughout Stables' work was assessment authenticity. She critiqued assessment practices that reduced D&T to measurable but trivial outcomes, arguing that:

- **Authentic assessment** must capture the processes of designing, not just final products
- **Quality indicators** should reflect real-world design practice rather than artificial classroom criteria
- **Formative assessment** is particularly crucial in D&T, where ongoing feedback shapes developing ideas
- **Self and peer assessment** develop metacognitive awareness essential for design capability

Stables contributed to several national projects developing assessment frameworks, including work that influenced National Curriculum assessment arrangements.

### 5. The Role of Critique and Evaluation

Stables emphasised evaluation and critique as central to design capability, not as end-stage activities but as continuous aspects of designing. She developed frameworks for teaching evaluative thinking that included:

- Distinguishing between different types of evaluation (ongoing, developmental, final, authentic testing)
- Teaching explicit criteria development with pupils
- Using comparison and benchmarking against existing products
- Developing critical literacy about design in the made world

This work influenced how evaluation is conceptualised in UK curriculum documentation.

### 6. Gender and Design & Technology

Stables contributed to research examining gender differences in D&T participation and achievement. Her work identified:

- Curriculum materials and project contexts that inadvertently favour particular gender stereotypes
- Differences in confidence rather than capability between boys and girls in certain D&T domains
- The importance of diverse role models and contexts in engaging all students
- Assessment practices that may advantage particular approaches to designing

This research informed efforts to make D&T more inclusive and equitable.

### 7. Technology Education and Values

Stables argued that D&T education must address values and ethics, not merely technical capability. Her work explored how curriculum could develop critical awareness of:

- Environmental sustainability in design decisions
- Social and cultural impacts of technological development
- User-centred versus market-driven design priorities
- The designer's responsibility to multiple stakeholders

This emphasis on values helped establish D&T as contributing to broader citizenship education.

## Key Publications

### Books and Monographs

**Stables, K. (1997).** *Critical Issues in Design and Technology.* Buckingham: Open University Press.
This edited collection brought together leading researchers to examine contested issues in D&T education, including curriculum balance, progression, and assessment.

**Stables, K., & Kimbell, R. (2000).** *Designerly Thinking and Learning: Design & Technology in the Primary Curriculum.* London: Goldsmiths, University of London.
A seminal text examining how young children engage with design activities and implications for primary curriculum.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

**Stables, K. (1992).** "The Assessment of Design & Technology: Problems and Possibilities." *Design and Technology Teaching*, 24(3), pp. 146-150.
An early exploration of assessment challenges specific to D&T, identifying tensions between validity and reliability.

**Stables, K. (1995).** "Discontinuity in Transition: Pupils' Experience of Technology in Year 6 and Year 7." *International Journal of Technology and Design Education*, 5(2), pp. 157-169.
Research highlighting progression issues at the primary-secondary transition, showing significant discontinuity in D&T experiences.

**Kimbell, R., Stables, K., Wheeler, T., Wozniak, A., & Kelly, V. (1991).** *The Assessment of Performance in Design and Technology: The Final Report of the APU Design and Technology Project 1985-1991.* London: School Examinations and Assessment Council (SEAC).
The landmark APU report that fundamentally shaped subsequent D&T curriculum and assessment policy.

**Stables, K., & Kimbell, R. (2007).** "Evidence through the Looking Glass: Developing Performance in Design & Technology." In J. Dakers (Ed.), *Defining Technological Literacy: Towards an Epistemological Framework* (pp. 155-170). New York: Palgrave Macmillan.
Theoretical examination of what constitutes evidence of learning in D&T contexts.

**Stables, K. (2008).** "Educating for Environmental Sustainability and Educating for Creativity: Actively Compatible or Missed Opportunities?" *Design and Technology Education: An International Journal*, 13(2), pp. 85-94.
Exploring connections between sustainability education and creative design capability.

### Research Reports

**Stables, K., & Rogers, M. (2000).** *Designing and Making at Key Stage 2: A Report of the Nuffield Primary Design & Technology Project.* Wellesbourne: Nuffield Foundation.
Influential research into primary D&T practice, identifying effective teaching strategies and common challenges.

**Stables, K. (1997).** *The Portfolio as an Assessment Tool in Technology Education.* Loughborough: Loughborough University.
Examination of portfolio assessment, influential in coursework assessment at GCSE and A-level.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Stables' research directly influenced several iterations of the National Curriculum for Design & Technology in England. Her work on design capability informed the 1995 and 2000 curriculum revisions, particularly in:

- The articulation of design capability as comprising investigation, development, and evaluation
- Assessment level descriptors that recognised process as well as outcome
- Greater emphasis on evaluation as ongoing rather than summative activity

### Assessment Policy

The APU project that Stables contributed to fundamentally reshaped how D&T was assessed in England and Wales. The framework influenced:

- GCSE examination board specifications for D&T
- Key Stage 3 assessment arrangements
- Teacher assessment guidance materials
- The concept of 'capability' entering official curriculum language

### Primary D&T

Stables' advocacy for rigorous primary D&T contributed to:

- Increased profile of D&T in primary initial teacher education
- Development of subject leadership materials for primary schools
- Professional development resources through organisations like DATA
- Research-informed guidance for Ofsted inspection frameworks

### International Influence

Stables' work informed technology education curriculum development internationally, particularly in:

- Australia (through contributions to Technology Mandatory curriculum in New South Wales)
- New Zealand (Technology curriculum development)
- Scotland (Technological Education curriculum)
- Various European national curricula through presentations and consultancy

## Pedagogical Approach

### Research-Informed Practice

Stables consistently advocated for evidence-based teaching in D&T, arguing that professional practice should be informed by research into learning and cognition. This included:

- Using knowledge of children's design thinking to plan appropriate scaffolding
- Selecting contexts and materials based on evidence of engagement and learning
- Assessment practices grounded in understanding of progression in design capability

### Constructivist Approaches

Her pedagogical philosophy aligned with constructivist learning theory, emphasising:

- Learners actively constructing understanding through design activity
- Social learning through collaborative project work
- Authentic problem contexts that connect to learners' experiences
- Teacher as facilitator of inquiry rather than deliverer of predetermined knowledge

### Authentic Design Practice

Stables argued that teaching should reflect authentic design practice, including:

- Real clients and users where possible
- Iterative development with prototyping and testing
- Constraint-based problem-solving
- Drawing on contextual research and existing products
- Working to realistic quality standards

### Reflective Practice

A strong emphasis on developing reflective practitioners, both for pupils and teachers:

- Structured reflection on design processes and outcomes
- Metacognitive awareness of decision-making
- Continuous professional development for teachers grounded in reflective practice
- Action research approaches to improving classroom practice

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

### Theoretical Frameworks

Stables' conceptual frameworks continue to underpin D&T education research and practice:

- The tripartite model of design capability (conceptual, active, executive) remains influential in assessment design
- Her work on design cognition informs teacher education programmes
- Frameworks for authentic assessment are referenced in examination specifications

### Professional Development

Many of Stables' publications and frameworks have been adapted into professional development materials used by:

- The Design and Technology Association
- Local authority advisory services
- Initial teacher education providers
- Subject leader training programmes

### Research Agenda

Stables' work established research priorities that continue to shape the field:

- Understanding progression in design capability
- Effective pedagogies for developing design thinking
- Assessment validity and authenticity
- Primary D&T subject knowledge and pedagogy
- Technology education and sustainability

### Current Relevance

In contemporary D&T education debates, Stables' work remains highly relevant to discussions about:

- GCSE and A-level reform and the balance between knowledge and capability
- Primary curriculum crowding and the role of foundation subjects
- STEM education and the distinctive contribution of design
- Assessment without levels and alternative progression models
- Digital technologies and continuity with traditional D&T values

## Critical Perspectives

### Assessment Debates

Whilst Stables' work on authentic assessment was influential, debates continue about:

- Practical feasibility of process-focused assessment in high-stakes examinations
- Reliability versus validity tensions in portfolio assessment
- Workload implications of her proposed assessment approaches
- Whether authentic design assessment is compatible with standardised testing regimes

### Curriculum Breadth

Some practitioners questioned whether Stables' frameworks adequately addressed:

- The role of technical knowledge and skills development
- Progression in specific material areas (resistant materials, textiles, electronics)
- The relationship between design capability and making quality
- Whether emphasis on process undervalued craftsmanship

### Implementation Challenges

Research-practice gaps meant that Stables' sophisticated frameworks were sometimes:

- Over-simplified in translation to classroom practice
- Misunderstood as advocating unstructured 'discovery learning'
- Difficult to implement without substantial teacher subject knowledge
- Resource-intensive in terms of time and materials
