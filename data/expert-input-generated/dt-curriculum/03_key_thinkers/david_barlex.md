# David Barlex

**Full Name:** David Barlex

**Current Roles:** Independent Consultant in Design & Technology Education; Visiting Professor at Brunel University London

**Known For:** Leading the Nuffield Design & Technology Project; pioneering 'design without make' pedagogy; developing Young Foresight; championing capability-driven approaches to D&T curriculum; extensive work on assessment, progression, and the intellectual rigour of designing

---

## Biography

David Barlex is one of the most influential figures in UK Design & Technology education over the past three decades. His career spans classroom teaching, curriculum development, research, and policy advisory work, establishing him as a thought leader who has consistently pushed for greater intellectual rigour and pedagogical innovation within the subject.

Barlex began his career as a secondary school physics and science teacher before moving into Design & Technology education. This scientific background profoundly influenced his approach to D&T, emphasising systematic thinking, modelling, and evidence-based decision-making within designing and making activities.

His major breakthrough came in the mid-1990s when he was appointed Project Director of the **Nuffield Design & Technology Project** (1995-2000), funded by the Nuffield Foundation. This project produced comprehensive teaching resources and a pedagogical framework that fundamentally reshaped how D&T was taught in many UK schools. The Nuffield approach emphasised 'capability' over content knowledge alone, focusing on developing students' ability to design and make products that met genuine human needs.

Following the Nuffield Project, Barlex established himself as an independent consultant, working with examination boards, government agencies, schools, and international organisations. He has been a Visiting Professor at Brunel University London's Centre for Learning Innovation and Professional Practice, where he has supervised doctoral research and contributed to initial teacher education.

Throughout the 2000s and 2010s, Barlex worked extensively on curriculum reform, contributing to government reviews and consultations on the National Curriculum for Design & Technology. He has been a vocal advocate for maintaining D&T's distinct identity whilst ensuring it responds to technological change and economic needs.

Barlex has maintained an international profile, working with curriculum developers in countries including Australia, New Zealand, Hong Kong, and across Europe. His work has influenced design and technology education frameworks worldwide, particularly in contexts seeking to balance academic rigour with practical capability.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Design & Technology as Capability Development

Barlex's fundamental contribution has been theorising D&T as primarily about developing **capability** rather than merely transmitting knowledge or skills. Drawing on the work of John Eggleston and others, he articulated capability as the synthesis of knowledge, skills, understanding, and attitudes that enable students to identify needs and opportunities and respond through designing and making.

In the **Nuffield Design & Technology framework**, Barlex identified five key capabilities:
- Imaging and exploring ideas
- Clarifying tasks
- Critical evaluation
- Developing proposals
- Planning and organising production

This framework moved beyond linear design process models, recognising that designing involves iterative, non-linear thinking where designers move fluidly between different types of activity. This represented a significant departure from the rigid 'design folders' that had dominated practice.

### 2. Design Without Make

Barlex pioneered **'design without make'** approaches, recognising that not all designing need culminate in physical making, particularly when teaching complex systems or when resource constraints exist. This concept, developed through various projects and publications from the early 2000s onwards, challenged the assumption that D&T must always involve workshop production.

In his paper "Design-without-make: challenging the conventional approach to teaching and learning in design and technology" (with John Steeg), Barlex argued that designing for contexts like electronics, systems and control, or product development could be taught through simulation, computer-aided design, virtual prototyping, and other modelling techniques. This approach:
- Allowed exploration of more complex products and systems
- Enabled students to work at a higher conceptual level
- Reflected authentic industrial practice
- Made efficient use of curriculum time
- Reduced resource costs whilst maintaining rigorous learning

This work was particularly influential during the 2000s when schools faced increasing pressure on curriculum time and resources.

### 3. Young Foresight

**Young Foresight** is Barlex's innovative approach to teaching designing through futures thinking. Developed in the mid-2000s and extensively refined since, Young Foresight engages students in considering how emerging technologies, social trends, and environmental factors might create future design opportunities.

The Young Foresight methodology involves:
- Researching drivers of change (technological, social, environmental, economic, political)
- Developing scenarios for possible futures
- Identifying design opportunities within these scenarios
- Creating design proposals responsive to anticipated needs
- Evaluating designs against future contexts

This approach draws on professional foresight methodologies used in industry and policy-making, adapted for educational contexts. Barlex developed extensive teaching resources, including the **Young Foresight Online** platform, which provides structured activities, case studies, and assessment frameworks.

Young Foresight addresses several pedagogical challenges:
- It provides authentic, engaging contexts for designing
- It develops higher-order thinking skills including analysis, synthesis, and evaluation
- It connects D&T with contemporary issues like sustainability and technological change
- It mirrors professional design practice where anticipating future needs is crucial

### 4. Authentic Contexts and Client-Centred Design

Throughout his work, Barlex has emphasised the importance of **authentic contexts** for design activity. He has critiqued approaches where students design for hypothetical users or without genuine need, arguing this produces superficial learning.

His development of **client-centred design** approaches, particularly in the Nuffield materials, requires students to:
- Work with real clients or well-researched user groups
- Conduct systematic user research
- Test proposals with users
- Evaluate outcomes against user needs

This approach was radical in the 1990s when much school D&T involved students designing for themselves or vaguely defined 'someone'. Barlex demonstrated that even young learners could engage with real clients, producing more meaningful learning and more innovative outcomes.

### 5. Modelling and Iterative Development

Barlex has consistently advocated for **modelling** as central to designing, drawing parallels with scientific and mathematical thinking. He distinguishes between different types of models:
- Conceptual models (ideas and principles)
- Graphical models (drawings, CAD representations)
- Mathematical models (calculations, simulations)
- Physical models (prototypes, mock-ups)
- Virtual models (CAD simulations, animations)

His work emphasises that expert designers use modelling to explore ideas, test assumptions, and refine proposals before committing to final production. This challenges the 'make it and see' approach that can dominate school practice.

The **Nuffield Project's emphasis on iterative development** introduced structured approaches to prototyping, testing, and refinement. Barlex developed assessment frameworks that credited this developmental work rather than only valuing final outcomes, helping shift assessment practice across GCSE and A Level specifications.

### 6. Resource Tasks and Focused Practical Tasks

Barlex was instrumental in developing and promoting **Resource Tasks** and **Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)** as pedagogical tools. These structured activities, developed during the Nuffield Project, provide:

- **Resource Tasks**: Short activities that build specific knowledge or skills needed for design contexts (e.g., researching materials properties, learning about mechanisms, exploring user needs in specific contexts)
- **Focused Practical Tasks**: Practical activities that develop particular making techniques or allow exploration of materials and processes without the complexity of full design projects

This framework provided a systematic alternative to the 'learn by doing projects' approach, enabling teachers to build students' capability progressively. The distinction between these task types and full **Capability Tasks** (extended design-and-make projects) became influential in curriculum planning and assessment design.

### 7. Progression in Design & Technology

Barlex has contributed significantly to understanding **progression** in D&T. His work identifies progression across multiple dimensions:
- Complexity of tasks and contexts
- Independence in working
- Sophistication of designing strategies
- Technical demand of making
- Depth of evaluation and critical thinking

In various publications, including work with Torben Steeg and others, Barlex has critiqued simplistic progression models based solely on technical difficulty. He argues for progression frameworks that recognise:
- The iterative nature of capability development
- The importance of metacognitive awareness
- The role of domain-specific knowledge
- The need for increasingly sophisticated design thinking

This work influenced the development of level descriptors and assessment criteria in examination specifications and school curriculum frameworks.

### 8. Assessment for Learning in D&T

Barlex has been a consistent advocate for **formative assessment** practices in D&T. He has critiqued summative assessment regimes that focus narrowly on final products, arguing they fail to capture the full range of student learning.

His work on assessment includes:
- Developing frameworks for assessing design thinking processes
- Creating mark schemes that value developmental work
- Promoting peer and self-assessment strategies
- Advocating for assessment tasks that mirror authentic design practice

In publications and professional development work, Barlex has demonstrated how assessment can support learning rather than merely measuring it, showing teachers practical strategies for providing feedback that advances students' designing capability.

### 9. Data-Rich Tasks

Barlex developed the concept of **data-rich tasks** to support rigorous designing. These tasks provide students with extensive information about contexts, users, materials, components, and manufacturing processes, enabling more sophisticated design decisions.

Data-rich tasks typically include:
- Detailed context descriptions
- User research data
- Technical specifications
- Material and component information
- Manufacturing constraints
- Cost parameters

This approach addresses the problem of students making uninformed design decisions based on limited knowledge. By providing relevant data, teachers enable students to engage in more authentic design thinking whilst managing time constraints.

Data-rich tasks have been particularly influential in examination contexts, where examination boards have adopted this approach in controlled assessment and NEA (Non-Examined Assessment) tasks.

### 10. Technology Push and Market Pull

Barlex introduced **technology push and market pull** as conceptual tools for understanding design opportunities. This framework, drawn from innovation studies, helps students recognise that design opportunities arise from:
- **Technology push**: New technologies or materials creating possibilities for novel products or solutions
- **Market pull**: Identified user needs or market demands requiring design responses

This framework appears throughout the Nuffield materials and in Barlex's subsequent teaching resources. It provides students with a structured way to identify and evaluate design opportunities, moving beyond personal preferences or arbitrary briefs.

The technology push/market pull framework has become embedded in D&T curriculum planning and is explicitly referenced in many GCSE and A Level specifications.

## Key Publications

### Books and Major Reports

**Nuffield Design & Technology (1995-2000)**
The comprehensive teaching resource produced under Barlex's direction, including:
- *Nuffield Design & Technology: Teacher's Guide*
- *Nuffield Design & Technology: Student Books* (multiple volumes covering different material areas and age ranges)
- *Nuffield Design & Technology: Resource Tasks*
- *Nuffield Design & Technology: Capability Tasks*

These materials, published by Longman (later Pearson), became the most widely used D&T teaching resources in UK schools during the late 1990s and 2000s.

**"Assessing Capability in Design and Technology"** (2007)
Co-authored with Torben Steeg and published by Routledge, this book provides comprehensive guidance on assessment practices in D&T, including frameworks for assessing design thinking, making skills, and evaluative capability.

**"Longitudinal Study of Design and Technology in Schools"** (various reports, 2003-2007)
Barlex contributed to this significant research project examining D&T provision, teaching quality, and student outcomes across England.

### Journal Articles and Papers

**"Design-without-make: challenging the conventional approach to teaching and learning in design and technology"** (with John Steeg), published in *The Journal of Design and Technology Education* (2002)
This influential paper articulated the rationale and pedagogical approach for design-without-make activities.

**"Creativity in Crisis: Design and Technology at KS3 and KS4"** (2003)
Published by the Nuffield Foundation, this report examined the state of D&T teaching and identified challenges to maintaining creative, rigorous learning experiences.

**"Nuffield Design & Technology: Perspectives on Pupil Performance"** (1998)
This research report documented the impact of the Nuffield approach on student learning outcomes and teacher practice.

**"Considering the Creation and Use of Design Briefs"** (2005)
Published in *The Journal of Design and Technology Education*, this paper examined how design briefs are constructed and used in teaching, advocating for more authentic, research-based approaches.

**"Design-without-make: a case for electronics"** (2003)
An exploration of how electronics education can be effectively taught through simulation and modelling approaches.

**"Young Foresight: Building Futures Thinking in Design and Technology"** (various dates)
A series of papers and resources explaining the Young Foresight methodology and its application in schools.

**"Assessing Design and Technology in the Secondary School"** (with Alison Hardy, 2013)
Published in *The Handbook of Technology Education*, this chapter provides comprehensive guidance on assessment approaches and challenges.

### Professional Resources

**Young Foresight Online Platform** (ongoing)
A comprehensive web-based resource providing teaching materials, student activities, and assessment frameworks for futures-based designing. Available at youngforesight.org (though URL may have changed).

**D&T Network Resources** (2000s-2010s)
Barlex contributed extensively to the online resources produced by the Design and Technology Association and the former Secondary National Strategy, including guidance on progression, assessment, and curriculum planning.

**DATA Research Papers** (various dates)
Multiple papers published by the Design and Technology Association examining aspects of teaching, curriculum design, and assessment.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Barlex has been actively involved in National Curriculum reviews and consultations throughout his career. During the 2000s, he provided expert advice to the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA) on D&T curriculum frameworks and programmes of study.

During the 2013-2014 National Curriculum review, Barlex was a prominent voice in debates about D&T's content and structure. He contributed written evidence to the Department for Education and engaged in public debate about the proposed changes. Whilst critical of some aspects of the final 2014 National Curriculum, particularly its knowledge-heavy emphasis, Barlex acknowledged improvements in the curriculum's recognition of rigorous designing processes.

His influence is evident in several aspects of the current National Curriculum for D&T, including:
- Recognition of iterative designing processes
- Emphasis on modelling and testing
- Focus on designing for users and purposes
- Integration of emerging technologies

### Examination Specifications

Barlex has worked extensively with examination boards (AQA, OCR, Edexcel/Pearson, WJEC) as a consultant and advisor. His influence is particularly evident in:

**GCSE Design & Technology specifications** (various iterations):
- Introduction of controlled assessment tasks using data-rich formats
- Assessment criteria that value design thinking processes
- Recognition of design-without-make approaches in coursework
- Emphasis on iterative development and testing

**A Level Design & Technology specifications**:
- Integration of futures thinking and trend analysis
- Assessment frameworks for complex design projects
- Emphasis on user-centred research
- Recognition of multiple modelling approaches

Several examination boards have explicitly drawn on Nuffield Project materials and Barlex's conceptual frameworks in developing specimen assessment materials and teaching guidance.

### Professional Development and Teacher Education

Barlex has been a leading figure in D&T professional development for over three decades. He has:
- Led national training programmes for the Nuffield Project
- Delivered keynote addresses at Design and Technology Association conferences regularly
- Provided training for examination board moderators and examiners
- Contributed to initial teacher education programmes, particularly at Brunel University
- Delivered CPD sessions for local authority advisory services and teaching school alliances

His accessible communication style and practical focus have made him particularly effective in translating research and theory into classroom practice.

### Policy Advocacy

Barlex has been a consistent advocate for D&T within education policy debates. He has:
- Submitted evidence to Parliamentary Select Committee inquiries on STEM education
- Contributed to policy reports examining D&T provision and status
- Engaged in public debate through articles in *TES* and other professional publications
- Worked with subject associations and professional bodies to promote D&T

He has been particularly vocal about:
- The importance of maintaining D&T as a distinct, compulsory subject
- The need for adequate curriculum time
- The importance of specialist teacher training
- The risks of narrow, skills-focused approaches that neglect designing capability
- The value of D&T in developing transferable thinking skills

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructivist Foundations

Barlex's pedagogy is rooted in **constructivist learning theory**, particularly the work of Piaget and Vygotsky. He emphasises that students construct understanding through active engagement with design problems, materials, and technologies. This is evident in:
- The emphasis on student-centred investigation and research
- The use of practical activity to develop conceptual understanding
- The importance of social interaction and collaborative design
- The role of scaffolding through Resource Tasks and structured activities
