# D&T at Key Stage 2

## Overview and National Curriculum Framework

Design and Technology at Key Stage 2 (ages 7-11) represents a critical phase in children's technological capability development. The National Curriculum for England (DfE, 2013) establishes Design and Technology as a foundation subject, requiring pupils to develop creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world.

At KS2, the statutory requirements build upon Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1 experiences, expecting pupils to develop more sophisticated design thinking, technical knowledge, and making skills. The programme of study is structured around three core attainment targets: designing, making, and evaluating, supplemented by technical knowledge requirements.

## Statutory Requirements

### Design

Pupils should be taught to:
- Use research and develop design criteria to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products that are fit for purpose, aimed at particular individuals or groups
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through discussion, annotated sketches, cross-sectional and exploded diagrams, prototypes, pattern pieces and computer-aided design (CAD)

The progression from KS1 to KS2 represents a significant cognitive leap. As Richard Kimbell has argued in his seminal work 'Assessing Technology' (1997), Key Stage 2 pupils move from intuitive making towards more conscious design thinking, developing what he terms 'procedural capability' alongside their 'conceptual capability'.

### Make

Pupils should be taught to:
- Select from and use a wider range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks accurately
- Select from and use a wider range of materials and components, including construction materials, textiles and ingredients, according to their functional properties and aesthetic qualities

The emphasis on accurate practical work reflects research by Stephanie Atkinson and colleagues at Sunderland University, demonstrating that primary-aged children can develop sophisticated making skills when appropriately scaffolded and when technical vocabulary is explicitly taught.

### Evaluate

Pupils should be taught to:
- Investigate and analyse a range of existing products
- Evaluate their ideas and products against their own design criteria and consider the views of others to improve their work
- Understand how key events and individuals in design and technology have helped shape the world

Kay Stables' research on designerly thinking (2008) emphasises the importance of evaluation as an iterative process throughout the design and make process, not merely as an end-point activity. At KS2, pupils should be encouraged to develop critical evaluation skills that inform ongoing refinement.

## Technical Knowledge Requirements

### Structures

Pupils should:
- Apply their understanding of how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures
- Understand that mechanical systems such as levers and linkages or pneumatic systems create movement

David Barlex's work on structures, particularly through the Nuffield Design & Technology resources, provides excellent frameworks for teaching structural principles. Pupils should explore concepts including triangulation, corrugation, lamination, and cross-bracing through practical making activities.

### Mechanisms

The curriculum requires understanding of:
- Mechanical systems: levers, linkages, pulleys, gears, cams
- Electrical systems: simple circuits incorporating switches, bulbs, buzzers and motors

Matt McLain's research (2016) on primary technology emphasises the importance of focused practical tasks (FPTs) to develop specific technical knowledge before applying this within design and make assignments (DMAs). For mechanisms, this might involve exploring existing products (product analysis) before designing solutions incorporating these systems.

### Electrical and Electronic Systems

Pupils should:
- Apply their understanding of computing to program, monitor and control their products

The integration with computing represents a significant development in the 2014 National Curriculum. Pupils might use systems such as Crumble controllers, Micro:bits, or LEGO WeDo to add programmable elements to their products.

## Subject-Specific Contexts

The KS2 programme of study specifies five contexts through which design and technology should be taught:

### Cooking and Nutrition

Pupils should be taught to:
- Understand and apply the principles of a healthy and varied diet
- Prepare and cook a variety of predominantly savoury dishes using a range of cooking techniques
- Understand seasonality, and know where and how a variety of ingredients are grown, reared, caught and processed

The promotion of cooking and nutrition to a statutory requirement reflects the government's concern with childhood obesity and life skills. Schools must teach food education throughout KS2, developing from simple food preparation at KS1 to more complex cooking techniques including use of heat, understanding of seasonality, and food provenance.

### Textiles

Textile projects at KS2 should move beyond simple decoration to functional products. Pupils should:
- Understand pattern pieces and template use
- Apply a range of joining techniques including running stitch, back stitch, blanket stitch, over-sewing
- Develop understanding of fabric properties and appropriate selection
- Explore decoration techniques including appliqué, embroidery, dyeing and printing

Kay Stables' work on capability in textiles emphasises the importance of understanding fabric behaviour and properties, not merely decorative techniques.

### Construction Materials

Working with resistant materials including wood, card, plastic and metal requires:
- Accurate measuring and marking out (using rulers, try-squares, templates)
- Appropriate cutting techniques (saws, junior hacksaws, craft knives with safety cutting mats)
- Joining methods (appropriate adhesives, mechanical fixings, temporary and permanent joins)
- Finishing techniques (painting, varnishing, sanding)

### Electrical and Mechanical Components

Pupils should incorporate:
- Simple circuit components (batteries, wires, bulbs, switches, buzzers, motors)
- Mechanical components (wheels, axles, cams, pulleys, gears, levers)
- Programmable components (simple control systems)

### Digital World

The integration of CAD and CAM at KS2 level might include:
- 2D Design software for planning
- Digital photography for recording and evaluation
- Word processing for design proposals
- Simple programming for control
- Laser cutting for component manufacture (where facilities exist)

## Pedagogical Approaches for KS2

### The Iterative Design Process

Effective KS2 teaching follows the iterative design model rather than linear progression. Research by Richard Kimbell in the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) project established that capable young designers constantly move between 'imaging' (thinking about ideas) and 'modelling' (representing ideas), whether through sketches, card models, or discussions.

### Focused Practical Tasks

David Barlex's advocacy for focused practical tasks (FPTs) provides a robust pedagogical approach. Before undertaking a design and make assignment, pupils should experience:
- Product analysis and evaluation of existing solutions
- Skill-building activities developing specific techniques
- Knowledge-building activities exploring relevant technical principles

### Design and Make Assignments

The substantial design and make assignment (DMA) represents the culmination of learning. Effective DMAs at KS2:
- Present authentic design contexts with clear client/user needs
- Allow for multiple design solutions
- Require application of technical knowledge taught through FPTs
- Provide opportunities for iterative development
- Enable genuine evaluation against fitness for purpose

### Resource Tasks and Capability Tasks

Stephanie Atkinson's research framework distinguishes between:
- **Resource Tasks**: activities building specific skills, knowledge or understanding
- **Capability Tasks**: holistic design and make assignments requiring integration of learning

Both are essential at KS2, with resource tasks providing scaffolding for more complex capability tasks.

## Assessment Approaches

### Formative Assessment

Assessment at KS2 should focus on:
- Design thinking (generation of ideas, development, communication)
- Making skills (accuracy, appropriate tool use, quality of outcome)
- Technical knowledge (understanding of materials, components, systems)
- Evaluation capability (critical analysis, improvement suggestions)

Matt McLain's research (2018) on assessment in primary D&T emphasises the importance of assessing the process as well as the product. Teachers should document design thinking through photographs, annotation of pupils' work, and observational assessment.

### Progression from KS1 to KS2

Kay Stables' research on designerly behaviour identifies clear progression indicators:
- Increasing independence in design decision-making
- Growing sophistication in communicating ideas (moving from simple drawings to annotated sketches, models, prototypes)
- Development of technical vocabulary
- Enhanced ability to analyse existing products
- Greater accuracy in making
- More sophisticated understanding of user needs and contexts

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Time Allocation

Research by the Design and Technology Association consistently identifies insufficient curriculum time as a significant challenge. Many primary schools allocate only one hour weekly, insufficient for substantial making activities. Solutions include:
- Blocking time into half-day or full-day sessions
- Cross-curricular links (particularly with mathematics, science, computing)
- Careful planning of focused practical tasks to maximise skill development efficiency

### Teacher Confidence and Subject Knowledge

Matt McLain's research (2019) identifies that many primary teachers lack confidence in teaching D&T, particularly technical aspects. Professional development strategies include:
- Subject leader expertise development through DATA membership and training
- Collaborative planning within schools and Multi-Academy Trusts
- Use of published schemes of work (Projects on a Page, Reach Out CPD)
- Partnership with secondary D&T departments

### Resource Provision

Adequate resources are essential for quality D&T teaching. Schools should provide:
- Construction materials in sufficient quantity (card, wood strips, wheels, axles)
- Textiles and consumables (fabrics, thread, decorative materials)
- Tools appropriate for age (safe cutting tools, measuring equipment)
- Electrical and mechanical components
- Food ingredients and equipment for cooking

### Health and Safety

The Association for Science Education's 'Be Safe!' publication and DATA's 'Primary Design and Technology – A Guide for Teacher Assessment' provide essential health and safety guidance. Key principles include:
- Risk assessment for all activities
- Age-appropriate tool use with proper instruction
- Supervision appropriate to risk level
- Specific attention to food hygiene when cooking
- Clear rules for workshop areas and equipment use

## Links to Other Curriculum Areas

### Mathematics

D&T provides authentic contexts for:
- Measurement (length, mass, volume, temperature)
- Geometry (2D and 3D shapes, nets, angles)
- Ratio and proportion (scaling recipes, mixing colours)
- Data handling (testing materials, surveys of user preferences)

### Science

Technical knowledge in D&T builds upon scientific understanding:
- Forces (structures, mechanisms)
- Electricity (circuits, switches, components)
- Materials properties (selection for purpose)
- Nutrition (healthy eating, digestion)

### Computing

The 2014 National Curriculum explicitly links D&T and computing:
- Programming control systems
- Digital design tools (CAD)
- Research and communication
- Data logging and monitoring

### English

D&T develops literacy through:
- Technical vocabulary
- Instructional writing (recipes, making instructions)
- Persuasive writing (product promotion, design proposals)
- Report writing (evaluation)

## Planning for Progression

### Year 3/4 Expectations

Lower KS2 pupils should:
- Design with increasing independence, considering user and purpose
- Develop ideas through discussion and simple sketches
- Use a wider range of materials and tools with growing accuracy
- Begin to use mechanical systems (levers, linkages)
- Understand basic electrical circuits
- Follow simple recipes, using appropriate cooking techniques
- Evaluate against simple design criteria

### Year 5/6 Expectations

Upper KS2 pupils should:
- Research and develop detailed design criteria
- Communicate ideas through annotated sketches, exploded diagrams, and prototypes
- Select tools and materials appropriately, explaining choices
- Demonstrate accuracy and quality of finish in making
- Incorporate more complex mechanical and electrical systems
- Use control programming to enhance products
- Prepare and cook savoury dishes confidently
- Evaluate thoroughly, suggesting realistic improvements

## Current Developments and Future Directions

### Digital Technologies

David Barlex's recent work on smart materials and emerging technologies suggests primary schools should begin introducing:
- Programmable components (Micro:bit, Crumble, Arduino)
- 3D design software (Tinkercad, 2D Design)
- Laser cutting and 3D printing (where available)

### Sustainability and Environmental Awareness

Contemporary D&T education increasingly emphasises:
- Sustainable material choices
- Design for disassembly and recycling
- Energy efficiency in products
- Life cycle thinking
- Ethical sourcing and manufacture

Kay Stables' recent work highlights how even young children can engage with these concepts through carefully structured activities.

### STEM Integration

The promotion of STEM education creates opportunities and challenges for D&T. While integration can strengthen learning, research by Richard Kimbell warns against D&T being subsumed into science or becoming merely the 'making' element of STEM. The unique contribution of designerly thinking must be preserved.

## Conclusion

Design and Technology at Key Stage 2 represents a crucial phase in developing children's technological literacy and capability. Effective teaching requires subject knowledge, pedagogical understanding, adequate resources, and sufficient curriculum time. When taught well, D&T develops creative, critical, and practical capabilities that prepare pupils for life in an increasingly technological world. The research and frameworks provided by key thinkers including Kimbell, Barlex, Stables, Atkinson, and McLain offer robust foundations for curriculum planning and teaching that enables all pupils to develop design and technology capability.
