# D&T at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview of A Level Design & Technology

Key Stage 5 Design & Technology represents the pinnacle of pre-university D&T education in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. Unlike Key Stages 3 and 4, A Level D&T is not governed by the National Curriculum but by exam board specifications regulated by Ofqual (England), Qualifications Wales, and CCEA (Northern Ireland). The typical age range is 16-18 years.

A Level D&T specifications were reformed in 2017 to align with new government requirements emphasising mathematical skills, core technical principles, and greater rigour. As David Barlex notes in his analysis of the reformed specifications, the changes represent "a significant shift towards more demanding technical content whilst maintaining the creative, iterative design processes that characterise the subject" (DATA Research Paper, 2016).

## Exam Board Specifications

### AQA Design & Technology (7552)

AQA offers a unified A Level specification covering multiple material categories within a single qualification. The specification comprises:

- **Paper 1: Technical Principles (30%)** - 2.5 hours, 120 marks
- **Paper 2: Designing and Making Principles (20%)** - 1.5 hours, 80 marks
- **NEA: Practical Application (50%)** - Substantial design and make project

Students must work primarily within one material category: Fashion and Textiles, Product Design, or Engineering. The specification emphasises mathematics (minimum 15% of overall assessment) and core technical principles including materials, systems, and manufacturing processes.

### Pearson Edexcel Design & Technology (9DT0)

Edexcel's specification similarly combines theory and practice:

- **Paper 1: Principles of Design and Technology (50%)** - 3 hours, 150 marks
- **NEA: Independent Design and Make Project (50%)** - Substantial coursework

Edexcel emphasises contemporary designers, design movements, and sustainability more explicitly than other boards. Matt McLain's research on vocational and academic pathways highlights how Edexcel's approach bridges traditional craft knowledge with contemporary design thinking (Journal of Design and Technology Education, 2018).

### OCR Design & Technology (H404/H405/H406)

OCR offers three distinct routes:

- **H404: Product Design**
- **H405: Fashion and Textiles**
- **H406: Design Engineering**

Each comprises:
- **Component 1: Principles of Design and Technology (50%)** - 2.5 hours
- **Component 2: Problem Solving in Design and Technology (50%)** - NEA project

OCR's Design Engineering pathway has particular academic credibility for university progression in engineering disciplines.

### WJEC Eduqas Design & Technology (A680)

Available in England and Wales, the WJEC specification includes:

- **Component 1: Design and Technology in the 21st Century (25%)** - 2 hours
- **Component 2: Design and Technology Core Content (25%)** - 2 hours
- **Component 3: Design and Make Task (50%)** - NEA project

WJEC places stronger emphasis on commercial manufacture and systems thinking than other boards.

## Core Content Areas

### Technical Principles

All specifications require substantial technical knowledge across:

1. **Materials and their properties** - mechanical, physical, and working properties of woods, metals, polymers, textiles, papers and boards, including smart and modern materials
2. **Manufacturing processes** - processes, quality control, scale of production
3. **Digital technologies** - CAD/CAM, rapid prototyping, enterprise resource planning
4. **Forces and stresses** - calculations involving force, stress, strain, moments, and material selection
5. **Electrical and mechanical systems** - circuits, programmable components, mechanical advantage, gear ratios
6. **Mathematical applications** - minimum 15% mathematical content including calculations, data analysis, and modelling

Richard Kimbell's longitudinal research emphasises that technical knowledge in isolation is insufficient; students must apply principles within "authentic design contexts where knowledge serves purposeful making" (Kimbell & Stables, 2007, *Researching Design Learning*).

### Design Theory and Processes

Kay Stables' work on designerly thinking (Stables, 2009) underpins the pedagogical approach required at A Level:

- **Iterative design development** - moving between conceptualisation, prototyping, and refinement
- **Design strategies** - user-centred design, systems thinking, circular economy principles
- **Design analysis** - critical evaluation of existing products and design movements
- **Design communication** - sophisticated presentation of ideas using industry-standard conventions
- **Contextual understanding** - social, cultural, environmental and economic influences on design

Stephanie Atkinson's research on capability emphasises that A Level students should demonstrate "holistic design capability integrating procedural, conceptual and dispositional knowledge" (Atkinson, 2013).

## The Non-Exam Assessment (NEA)

### Project Requirements

The NEA constitutes 50% of the A Level qualification across all boards (30-35 hours for AS Level where still offered). Students must:

1. Investigate a design context and identify a genuine need or want
2. Develop a detailed design brief and specification
3. Generate, develop and refine multiple design ideas
4. Produce a high-quality prototype that demonstrates technical competence
5. Test, evaluate and refine their solution
6. Present comprehensive documentation throughout

### Assessment Criteria

Whilst specific mark schemes vary by board, all assess:

- **Investigation and specification** (approximately 10-15%)
- **Design development and refinement** (approximately 15-20%)
- **Technical competence in making** (approximately 30-40%)
- **Analysis and evaluation** (approximately 10-15%)
- **Communication throughout** (embedded across criteria)

David Barlex's E-scape research project demonstrated that valid and reliable assessment of design capability requires both product quality and process documentation: "Assessment must recognise that designing is a cognitive activity made visible through representation and realisation" (Barlex, 2007).

### Teacher Guidance and Authentication

Teachers may provide:
- General guidance on project management
- Technical instruction on tools, processes and equipment
- Feedback on design development at specified review points

Teachers must not:
- Provide specific solutions to design problems
- Correct work beyond initial feedback
- Complete practical work for students

Authentication procedures are stringent, requiring candidate and supervisor signatures, plus centre authentication forms. Matt McLain's research on assessment authenticity highlights the professional judgement required: "Teachers must balance support for learning with maintaining assessment integrity" (McLain, 2019).

## Pedagogy and Teaching Approaches

### Project-Based Learning

A Level D&T teaching should integrate knowledge acquisition with application. Richard Kimbell advocates for "task-centred learning where theoretical understanding emerges from confronting authentic design challenges" (*The Assessment of Design and Technology*, 1997).

Effective approaches include:

- **Focused practical tasks** building specific technical skills
- **Case studies** of commercial products, designers and manufacturers
- **Modelling activities** using CAD and iterative prototyping
- **Collaborative peer critique** developing evaluative capability
- **Industry links** providing authentic contexts and technical insight

### Mathematical and Scientific Rigour

The reformed specifications demand greater mathematical competence. Teachers must explicitly teach:

- Structural calculations (stress, strain, factor of safety)
- Electrical calculations (Ohm's Law, power, resistance in series/parallel)
- Mechanical advantage calculations (gear ratios, velocity ratios)
- Statistical analysis (standard deviation, reliability testing)
- Scale and proportion in technical drawings

Stephanie Atkinson's research on cross-curricular links emphasises that mathematical application in D&T develops different understanding than mathematics lessons alone: "Mathematics becomes meaningful when applied to tangible design problems" (Atkinson, 2006).

### Workshop Management and Safety

A Level students require access to:

- **Traditional hand tools and machinery** for working resistant materials
- **Computer-aided design software** (SolidWorks, Fusion 360, 2D Design)
- **CNC manufacturing equipment** (laser cutters, 3D printers, CNC mills/lathes)
- **Electronics equipment** (soldering stations, microcontrollers, testing equipment)
- **Textiles equipment** where appropriate (industrial sewing machines, heat presses, CAD software)

Health and safety remains paramount. Risk assessments must be comprehensive, and students should operate only equipment for which they have received specific instruction. The DATA *Safe Practice in Design & Technology* guidance applies equally at Key Stage 5.

## Progression and University Pathways

### University Destinations

A Level D&T provides excellent preparation for:

- **Design degrees** - product design, industrial design, fashion design, graphic design
- **Engineering degrees** - particularly from OCR Design Engineering route
- **Architecture** - developing spatial awareness and design thinking
- **Creative industries** - theatre design, exhibition design, model making
- **Business and entrepreneurship** - understanding product development and manufacturing

Russell Group universities increasingly value D&T A Level alongside STEM subjects. Kay Stables' research on progression demonstrates that D&T develops "transferable design capabilities valuable across disciplines" (Stables & Kimbell, 2000).

### Facilitating Subject Status

Whilst D&T A Level is not classified as a "facilitating subject" by the Russell Group, it demonstrates:

- Problem-solving capability
- Creative thinking
- Technical competence
- Project management skills
- Communication abilities

David Barlex argues that this classification undervalues D&T's contribution: "Design capability represents sophisticated cognitive activity integrating knowledge from multiple domains – precisely what universities seek" (Barlex, 2019, *Design and Technology Education: An International Journal*).

## Subject Knowledge Development for Teachers

### Technical Competence

Teaching A Level D&T demands:

- Advanced understanding of materials science and manufacturing
- Competence in CAD/CAM and digital technologies
- Knowledge of contemporary design practice and industry
- Mathematical confidence to teach technical calculations
- Understanding of systems (mechanical, electrical, pneumatic)

### Professional Development

Key organisations supporting A Level D&T teachers:

- **Design and Technology Association** - subject association providing CPD, resources, and networking
- **National STEM Learning Centre** - courses on electronics, programming, mathematics in D&T
- **Institution of Engineering and Technology** - technical support and industry links
- **Exam board training** - mandatory standardisation and specification updates

Matt McLain's research on teacher expertise emphasises that "effective A Level teaching requires both disciplinary knowledge and pedagogical content knowledge – understanding how students learn design thinking" (McLain, 2015).

## Current Challenges and Future Directions

### Recruitment and Retention

A Level D&T faces challenges:

- **Declining entries** - down approximately 40% since 2010 (JCQ statistics)
- **Facilitating subject debate** - perceived lower status compared to "pure" STEM subjects
- **Resource intensity** - high costs of equipment, materials and technician support
- **Teacher supply** - shortage of qualified specialist teachers

### Opportunities

However, contemporary developments provide opportunities:

- **Industry 4.0 technologies** - additive manufacturing, IoT, artificial intelligence
- **Sustainability imperatives** - circular economy, life cycle analysis
- **Maker movement** - renewed interest in craft and making
- **Design thinking** - growing recognition in business and innovation contexts

David Barlex's recent writing emphasises that "D&T must articulate its unique contribution – developing holistic design capability essential for addressing complex 21st century challenges" (Barlex, 2020).

## Practical Recommendations for Departments

1. **Establish clear progression** from Key Stage 4 GCSE to smooth transition
2. **Develop strong industry links** providing authentic contexts and technical expertise
3. **Invest in contemporary CAD/CAM technologies** maintaining currency with industry practice
4. **Build complementary curriculum** with mathematics, physics, and art departments
5. **Plan extended project timelines** allowing iterative development and refinement
6. **Create exemplar portfolios** demonstrating assessment criteria at different levels
7. **Maintain rigorous health and safety protocols** with comprehensive risk assessments
8. **Promote cross-specification collaboration** where multiple A Levels run concurrently
9. **Engage with educational research** through D&T Association publications and conferences
10. **Celebrate student achievement** showcasing high-quality outcomes to wider school community and potential students

## Conclusion

A Level Design & Technology represents sophisticated, demanding study requiring integration of theoretical knowledge, technical competence, and creative design capability. As Richard Kimbell argues, it develops "designerly ways of knowing" – holistic problem-solving abilities valuable across academic disciplines and professional contexts. Effective teaching demands subject expertise, pedagogical skill, and commitment to supporting students through substantial, authentic design challenges that prepare them for further study and professional practice in design, engineering, and creative industries.
