# D&T in EYFS

## Overview and Statutory Framework

Design and Technology in the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) is not a discrete curriculum subject but is embedded within the statutory framework through the Prime and Specific Areas of Learning. The **EYFS Statutory Framework** (DfE, 2021) provides the foundation for design and technology capability through **Expressive Arts and Design** (EAD) and **Understanding the World** (UW), with significant contributions from **Physical Development** (PD).

Whilst D&T becomes a statutory National Curriculum subject at Key Stage 1, the EYFS provides crucial developmental groundwork. As Kay Stables has argued, early years experiences establish fundamental design behaviours including "making decisions, evaluating outcomes and developing resilience" (Stables, 2008), which underpin later capability in the subject.

## The EYFS Framework and D&T Capabilities

### Expressive Arts and Design: Creating with Materials

The **Creating with Materials** strand of Expressive Arts and Design provides the most direct connection to D&T. The Early Learning Goals (ELGs) state that children should:

- Safely use and explore a variety of materials, tools and techniques, experimenting with colour, design, texture, form and function
- Share their creations, explaining the process they have used
- Make use of props and materials when role playing characters in narratives and stories

These goals align with Richard Kimbell's concept of "modelling ideas" as a fundamental D&T capability. Kimbell's research with the Assessment of Performance Unit in the 1980s and 1990s emphasised that young children naturally engage in iterative cycles of "imaging and modelling" (Kimbell et al., 1991), which forms the basis of design thinking.

### Understanding the World: The Natural World and People, Culture and Communities

Understanding the World contributes to D&T capability through:

- Exploring the natural world and how things work
- Talking about the differences and similarities between materials
- Understanding some important processes and changes in the natural world
- Knowing that there are different countries in the world and talking about the differences they have experienced or seen in photos

David Barlex has emphasised the importance of early material exploration, arguing that "young children's tactile and sensory experiences with materials form the basis for later conceptual understanding of material properties" (Barlex, 2007).

### Physical Development: Fine Motor Skills

The Physical Development area is essential for developing the manipulative skills required for D&T:

- Holding a pencil effectively in preparation for fluent writing
- Using a range of small tools, including scissors, paintbrushes and cutlery
- Beginning to show accuracy and care when drawing

Stephanie Atkinson's work on primary D&T emphasises that "tool use and fine motor control are prerequisites for realising design ideas" (Atkinson, 2010), making Physical Development outcomes critical for later D&T success.

## Key Design and Technology Capabilities Developed in EYFS

### 1. Exploring and Investigating

Young children develop investigation skills through:
- **Sensory exploration** of materials (texture, weight, flexibility, strength)
- **Comparative experiences** (which is stronger? which sticks better?)
- **Cause and effect** understanding through construction play
- **Taking things apart** safely to see how they work

**Practical Implication**: Provide continuous provision areas with collections of materials to compare, dismantle toys (with supervision), and exploration stations with different types of joining methods (tape, glue, split pins, staplers).

### 2. Designing and Communicating Ideas

EYFS design communication is predominantly through:
- **Verbal explanation** of what they want to make
- **Drawing and mark-making** to represent intentions
- **Gesture and demonstration** to show ideas
- **Simple modelling** with construction materials or playdough

Matt McLain's research on primary D&T identifies that "graphical communication develops from concrete three-dimensional modelling towards abstract two-dimensional representation" (McLain, 2015). EYFS practitioners should therefore prioritise 3D modelling over drawing.

**Practical Implication**: Accept and value oral design proposals. Photograph children's constructions and compile these into class design portfolios. Use "talk time" before making to articulate intentions.

### 3. Making and Construction

Core making capabilities include:
- **Selecting materials** based on properties (even if criteria are simple)
- **Using tools safely and with increasing control** (scissors, hole punches, staplers, glue spreaders)
- **Joining techniques** (adhesive tape, PVA glue, split pins, treasury tags)
- **Assembling components** (construction kits, loose parts)
- **Developing persistence** when things don't work first time

Kay Stables emphasises that "resilience in making is learned through experience of overcoming setbacks" (Stables, 2008), making a supportive environment critical.

**Practical Implication**: Model thinking aloud when you encounter problems. Create a "workshop area" with accessible materials and tools where children can work on projects over multiple sessions.

### 4. Evaluating and Improving

Early evaluation skills include:
- **Testing whether something works** for its intended purpose
- **Talking about what they like** about their product
- **Comparing their outcome** with their original intention
- **Suggesting simple improvements** or making modifications

Richard Kimbell's work emphasises that evaluation must be purposeful: children need authentic contexts where their products actually serve a function (Kimbell, 2011).

**Practical Implication**: Create genuine purposes for making (cards for family members, props for role-play, containers for resources). Ask "Does it do what you wanted?" rather than "Do you like it?"

## Progression Within EYFS

### Nursery (3-4 years)

**Typical D&T-related behaviours**:
- Explore materials freely with minimal intended outcome
- Join materials randomly or through simple repetition
- Talk about what they are making as they work (often changing)
- Use one-handed tools with adult support
- Build simple structures that may be more about process than product

**Teacher support**: Focus on exploration and skill development. Provide open-ended materials. Model vocabulary for materials and processes. Support tool use through hand-over-hand guidance.

### Reception (4-5 years)

**Typical D&T-related behaviours**:
- Articulate intentions before starting (though these may evolve)
- Select materials with simple criteria in mind ("I need strong card")
- Use increasing range of joining techniques independently
- Persist with problems for longer periods
- Evaluate against simple success criteria
- Plan sequences of actions ("First I'll cut, then I'll stick")

**Teacher support**: Introduce explicit design-before-making opportunities. Provide real design briefs linked to play themes or stories. Encourage peer discussion and collaborative making. Introduce technical vocabulary systematically.

## Characteristics of Effective Teaching and Learning

The EYFS framework identifies three **Characteristics of Effective Teaching and Learning** which are particularly pertinent to D&T:

### Playing and Exploring
- Children learn D&T capabilities through exploratory play with construction materials, junk modelling resources, and malleable materials
- The "workshop area" or "making table" provides continuous provision for design-make activities

### Active Learning
- Sustained shared thinking during making activities
- Persistence when constructions collapse or materials don't behave as expected
- Satisfaction from completing functional products

### Creating and Thinking Critically
- Having own ideas about what to make and how
- Making links between materials and their properties
- Choosing ways to approach problems and finding new ways when first attempts fail

David Barlex emphasises that these characteristics align with "designerly behaviour" - the cognitive approaches that professional designers employ (Barlex, 2003).

## Planning for D&T in EYFS

### Continuous Provision Areas

**Construction Area**: 
- Large wooden blocks, hollow blocks
- Small construction kits (Duplo, Mobilo, Lego)
- Connecting materials (cardboard tubes, boxes, guttering)
- Planning tools (clipboards, pencils, simple templates)

**Workshop/Making Area**:
- Recycled materials (boxes, tubes, fabric scraps, bubble wrap)
- Tools (scissors, hole punches, staplers, glue spreaders, spreaders)
- Joining materials (PVA glue, glue sticks, masking tape, sellotape, split pins)
- Decorative materials (buttons, sequins, tissue paper, stickers)

**Malleable Materials**:
- Playdough with tools and embellishments
- Clay or air-dry clay
- Plasticine and modelling tools

### Enhanced Provision and Focused Activities

While continuous provision allows child-initiated exploration, enhanced provision and adult-led activities develop specific capabilities:

**Example focused activities**:
- Making puppets for storytelling (textiles focus)
- Designing vehicles for small-world play (wheels and axles)
- Creating packaging for a special object (nets and structures)
- Building bridges for toy characters (structures, testing)
- Making moving pictures (mechanisms: sliders, levers)

Stephanie Atkinson's research recommends that "even in EYFS, design activities benefit from authentic contexts and clear purposes" (Atkinson, 2010).

### Links to Topics and Books

D&T activities integrate effectively with:
- **Traditional tales**: Making houses for Three Little Pigs (structures, materials)
- **Food topics**: Designing and making fruit salads, sandwich choices
- **Transport themes**: Creating vehicles with moving wheels
- **Seasonal celebrations**: Making cards, decorations, gifts
- **Growth and planting**: Designing pots, planters, or gardens

## Vocabulary Development

Systematic vocabulary teaching is essential for D&T capability. Key vocabulary for EYFS includes:

**Materials vocabulary**: 
card, cardboard, paper, fabric, plastic, wood, metal, smooth, rough, hard, soft, flexible, stiff, strong, weak, waterproof, shiny, dull

**Tools vocabulary**:
scissors, glue, tape, stapler, split pin, hole punch, cutting, sticking, joining, fixing, attaching

**Process vocabulary**:
design, make, plan, decide, choose, improve, change, test, problem, solution

**Evaluative vocabulary**:
better, stronger, easier, works, doesn't work, stable, wobbly, neat, careful

## Links to Key Stage 1

Strong EYFS provision creates smooth transition to the KS1 National Curriculum for D&T (DfE, 2013), which requires pupils to:
- Design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas
- Select from and use a range of tools and equipment
- Explore and evaluate existing products

The progression from EYFS involves:
- **More explicit design-make-evaluate structure** (though still maintaining some fluidity)
- **Introduction of technical vocabulary** with greater precision
- **Use of templates, patterns and joining techniques** taught systematically
- **Evaluation against design criteria** articulated before making

Kay Stables argues that this transition is most successful when KS1 teachers "build on rather than restart" design behaviours established in EYFS (Stables, 2008).

## Assessment in EYFS D&T

Assessment is through **ongoing observational assessment** aligned with Development Matters (2021) and the Early Learning Goals. Look for evidence of:

- **Increasing confidence** with tools and materials
- **More sophisticated selection** of materials for properties
- **Greater persistence** when facing setbacks
- **More detailed communication** of intentions and processes
- **Beginning to evaluate** against purpose

**Assessment approaches**:
- Photograph products with brief notes on process
- Record verbal design explanations
- Observe collaborative making and problem-solving
- Note skill development with specific tools
- Track vocabulary acquisition

Matt McLain emphasises that "assessment in early D&T must focus on process and development of designerly thinking, not just product quality" (McLain, 2017).

## Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND)

D&T activities in EYFS can be highly inclusive when adapted appropriately:

**Physical needs**:
- Provide adapted scissors, easy-grip tools, and larger components
- Use materials requiring less fine motor control (tearing rather than cutting)
- Provide hand-over-hand support as needed

**Communication needs**:
- Accept non-verbal communication of intentions (pointing, gesturing)
- Use visual prompts and Makaton signing for key vocabulary
- Photograph stages of process for reference

**Cognitive needs**:
- Break making tasks into smaller achievable steps
- Provide adult support to sustain attention
- Use pre-prepared components if necessary to ensure success

**Sensory needs**:
- Offer alternative materials if textures are aversive
- Provide sensory-rich exploration opportunities
- Create quieter making spaces if needed

## Health and Safety in EYFS D&T

The **CLEAPSS** organisation provides guidance for practical activities in early years settings. Key considerations include:

- **Scissors**: Introduce safety scissors appropriate for child's hand size and grip strength
- **Glue**: Use non-toxic, washable PVA or glue sticks; supervise glue gun use by adults only
- **Small parts**: Monitor for choking hazards with children who mouth objects
- **Tools**: Introduce staplers, hole punches with close supervision; teach safe carrying
- **Food activities**: Check allergy information; follow good hygiene practices
- **Risk-benefit approach**: Allow children to encounter manageable challenges

David Barlex argues that "overprotection in early years limits development of crucial practical competencies" (Barlex, 2007), advocating for supervised, scaffolded risk-taking.

## Parental Engagement

D&T provides excellent opportunities for home-school partnership:

- Send home "junk modelling" requests specifying useful materials
- Photograph and share making processes via learning journals or apps
- Invite parents to "making mornings" where they create alongside children
- Send home "weekend making challenges" linked to current topics
- Create lending libraries of construction equipment or tools

## Conclusion

D&T in EYFS establishes foundational capabilities that will develop throughout a child's educational journey. Through exploration, construction, problem-solving, and evaluation within authentic contexts, young children develop both practical skills and designerly thinking. As Richard Kimbell concludes, "the capability to imagine possibilities and realise them through action is developed from the earliest years" (Kimbell, 2011), making high-quality EYFS D&T provision essential for developing capable, creative, and resilient designers and makers.
