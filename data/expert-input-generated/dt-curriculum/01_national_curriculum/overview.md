# D&T National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction to Design & Technology in the National Curriculum

Design & Technology (D&T) is a statutory foundation subject within the National Curriculum for England, established through the Education Reform Act 1988 and maintained through subsequent curriculum reforms. The current framework, introduced in 2014, represents a significant shift towards emphasising the iterative design process, making skills, and technological understanding, whilst reducing prescriptive content requirements.

As David Barlex has consistently argued, D&T provides "a distinctive contribution to the education of young people through developing their capability to design and make products that solve real and relevant problems within a variety of contexts" (Barlex, 2007). This positions D&T as uniquely placed to develop practical competence alongside theoretical knowledge.

## Statutory Requirements and Legal Framework

The National Curriculum for Design & Technology is mandatory for maintained schools at Key Stages 1-3 (ages 5-14). Academy schools are not required to follow the National Curriculum but must provide a "broad and balanced curriculum" that includes D&T. At Key Stage 4 (ages 14-16), D&T becomes optional, though students must study at least one creative or technical subject.

The statutory Programme of Study is remarkably concise compared to earlier iterations, deliberately allowing schools flexibility in content whilst maintaining clear expectations for skill development and knowledge acquisition.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Programme of Study Requirements

At KS1, pupils must develop their designing and making skills through practical activities. The Programme of Study specifies that pupils should:

**Design:**
- Design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users based on design criteria
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through talking, drawing, templates, mock-ups and, where appropriate, information and communication technology

**Make:**
- Select from and use a range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks (cutting, shaping, joining and finishing)
- Select from and use a wide range of materials and components, including construction materials, textiles and ingredients, according to their characteristics

**Evaluate:**
- Explore and evaluate a range of existing products
- Evaluate their ideas and products against design criteria

**Technical Knowledge:**
- Build structures, exploring how they can be made stronger, stiffer and more stable
- Explore and use mechanisms (levers, sliders, wheels and axles) in their products

### Practical Implications for KS1 Teachers

Richard Kimbell's research on capability in young children emphasises the importance of "hands-on, minds-on" learning. At KS1, this translates into creating opportunities where children physically manipulate materials whilst simultaneously developing their conceptual understanding of design principles.

Teachers should establish a culture of "safe risk-taking" where mistakes are viewed as learning opportunities. Stephanie Atkinson's work on early years design education highlights that young children naturally engage in iterative design processes when given appropriate scaffolding and encouragement.

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Programme of Study Requirements

At KS2, the curriculum builds upon KS1 foundations with increased complexity and independence:

**Design:**
- Use research and develop design criteria to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products fit for purpose, aimed at particular individuals or groups
- Generate, develop, model and communicate ideas through discussion, annotated sketches, cross-sectional and exploded diagrams, prototypes, pattern pieces and computer-aided design

**Make:**
- Select from and use a wider range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks accurately
- Select from and use a wider range of materials and components, including construction materials, textiles and ingredients, according to their functional properties and aesthetic qualities

**Evaluate:**
- Investigate and analyse a range of existing products
- Evaluate their ideas and products against their own design criteria and consider the views of others to improve their work
- Understand how key events and individuals in design and technology have helped shape the world

**Technical Knowledge:**
- Apply their understanding of how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures
- Understand and use mechanical systems (gears, pulleys, cams, levers and linkages)
- Understand and use electrical systems (series circuits incorporating switches, bulbs, buzzers and motors)
- Apply their understanding of computing to program, monitor and control their products

### Subject-Specific Areas at KS2

The Programme of Study identifies three specific technical areas:

**Cooking and Nutrition:**
- Understand and apply principles of a healthy and varied diet
- Prepare and cook a variety of predominantly savoury dishes using a range of cooking techniques
- Understand seasonality and know where and how ingredients are grown, reared, caught and processed

**Textiles:**
Though not explicitly separated, textile work should include pattern-making, fabric selection, and joining techniques appropriate to purpose.

**Structures and Mechanisms:**
Development of understanding about structural integrity, mechanical advantage, and electrical control systems.

### Practical Implications for KS2 Teachers

Kay Stables' research on design capability emphasises the importance of developing pupils' ability to manage the "dialectic" between their intended outcomes and what is achievable. At KS2, teachers should increasingly encourage pupils to reconcile ambitious design ideas with practical constraints.

Matt McLain's work on primary D&T highlights the importance of meaningful contexts that connect to pupils' lives. Projects should address authentic problems rather than contrived scenarios, enabling pupils to develop genuine design empathy.

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### Programme of Study Requirements

The KS3 Programme of Study represents a significant step up in complexity and expectation:

**Design:**
- Use research including analysis of existing products to inform design decisions
- Develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional products
- Use a variety of approaches to generate creative ideas and avoid stereotypical responses
- Develop and communicate design ideas using annotated sketches, detailed plans, 3D and mathematical modelling, oral and digital presentations and computer-based tools

**Make:**
- Select from and use specialist tools, techniques, processes, equipment and machinery precisely, including computer-aided manufacture
- Select from and use a wider, more complex range of materials, components and ingredients, taking into account their properties

**Evaluate:**
- Analyse the work of past and present professionals to develop and broaden understanding
- Test, evaluate and refine ideas and products against specification, taking into account views of intended users
- Understand developments in design and technology, their impact on individuals, society and the environment, and the responsibilities of designers, engineers and technologists

**Technical Knowledge:**
- Understand and use the properties of materials and the performance of structural elements
- Understand how more advanced mechanical systems used in products enable changes in movement and force
- Understand how more advanced electrical and electronic systems can be powered and used in products
- Apply computing and use electronics to embed intelligence in products
- Understand how mechanical, electrical and electronic systems respond to inputs and control outputs

### Curriculum Organisation at KS3

David Barlex's "Nuffield Design & Technology" project provides influential guidance on KS3 curriculum organisation. He advocates for a "project-based" approach where pupils engage with substantial design-and-make tasks that integrate knowledge from multiple material areas.

Schools typically organise KS3 D&T through:

**Rotation Model:** Pupils rotate through focused units in different material areas (resistant materials, textiles, food, graphics, electronics, systems and control). Each rotation typically lasts 6-12 weeks.

**Integrated Model:** Pupils work on projects that draw upon multiple material areas as needed, with specialist teaching accessed flexibly.

**Hybrid Model:** Combination of focused skill-building modules followed by integrated design projects.

### Practical Implications for KS3 Teachers

Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research established that capability in D&T requires the integration of "mind and hand." At KS3, teachers must ensure that theoretical knowledge about materials, systems, and processes is always connected to practical application.

Stephanie Atkinson's work on design thinking emphasises the importance of developing pupils' ability to handle "ill-defined problems" – challenges without single correct solutions that require creative problem-solving and iterative refinement.

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16) – GCSE Specifications

### Overview of GCSE D&T

At KS4, D&T becomes optional but remains part of the English Baccalaureate creative and technical subject grouping. The current GCSE specifications (introduced 2017) reflect significant changes from previous iterations, with increased emphasis on core technical principles and iterative designing.

All major exam boards (AQA, OCR, Eduqas/WJEC, and formerly Edexcel/Pearson) offer GCSE Design & Technology, having moved from multiple material-specific qualifications to a single unified qualification.

### Common Elements Across Exam Boards

**Assessment Structure:**
- Written examination: 50% (typically 2 hours)
- Non-exam assessment (NEA)/Coursework: 50% (substantial design-and-make project)

**Core Content Areas:**
All specifications must cover:
- Core technical principles (materials and their properties, systems, mechanisms)
- Specialist technical principles (detailed knowledge in chosen material areas)
- Designing and making principles (investigation, design development, making, evaluation)

### Exam Board Specific Approaches

**AQA Design & Technology (8552):**
AQA's specification emphasises "designing and making principles" as a distinct category. The written paper includes multiple-choice, short-answer, and extended-response questions. The NEA requires a substantial project (approximately 35 hours) addressing a context set by AQA, with regular rotation of themes.

**OCR Design & Technology (J310):**
OCR structures content around "Iterative Design Challenge" methodology. Their specification particularly emphasises systems thinking and encourages use of specialist material areas within integrated projects. The NEA includes specific requirements for iterative development and user-centred design.

**Eduqas/WJEC Design & Technology (C660):**
The WJEC/Eduqas specification is popular in Wales and some English schools. It maintains stronger connections to material-specific knowledge whilst meeting the unified qualification requirements. Assessment includes emphasis on mathematical and scientific principles applied to design contexts.

### Practical Implications for KS4 Teachers

Matt McLain's research on GCSE D&T emphasises the challenge of balancing breadth (covering multiple material areas) with depth (developing genuine expertise). He advocates for schools to develop "signature projects" that enable pupils to demonstrate capability whilst building upon the school's specialist resources and staff expertise.

The shift to unified GCSE has generated significant debate. Kay Stables has written extensively about ensuring that the breadth required doesn't result in superficial coverage, arguing that "authentic capability requires deep engagement with materials, processes and design thinking within meaningful contexts."

## Wider Context: The 6Rs Framework and Sustainable Design

Contemporary D&T curriculum thinking increasingly emphasises sustainable and responsible design. The "6Rs" framework has become influential in structuring environmental considerations:

- **Rethink:** Questioning whether products are necessary
- **Refuse:** Rejecting unsustainable design solutions
- **Reduce:** Minimising material and energy use
- **Reuse:** Designing for multiple lifecycles
- **Recycle:** Enabling material recovery
- **Repair:** Creating products that can be maintained

This framework must be integrated throughout all key stages, reflecting the Programme of Study requirement to "understand developments in design and technology, their impact on individuals, society and the environment, and the responsibilities of designers, engineers and technologists."

## Assessment and Progression

### Assessment Principles in D&T

Richard Kimbell's groundbreaking work on D&T assessment, particularly through the APU projects and subsequent research for Goldsmiths' College, established that assessment must capture both process and product. His critique of traditional assessment methods highlighted that D&T capability cannot be adequately measured through written tests alone.

The DATA (Design and Technology Association, now the Design and Technology Association) provides extensive guidance on assessment, advocating for:

- Formative assessment embedded within project work
- Photographic/video evidence of making processes
- Design portfolios showing iterative development
- Evaluation against user needs and original specifications

### Progression Framework

Progression in D&T involves increasingly sophisticated:

**Conceptual Understanding:**
From simple cause-and-effect (KS1) to complex systems thinking (KS3/4)

**Technical Skills:**
From basic tool use to precise, controlled manufacturing processes

**Design Thinking:**
From teacher-structured approaches to independent, strategic problem-solving

**Communication:**
From simple drawings to sophisticated multi-modal presentations incorporating CAD, technical drawings, and prototyping

## Cross-Curricular Links and STEM Integration

### Natural Curriculum Connections

D&T naturally connects with multiple curriculum areas:

**Mathematics:** Measurement, geometry, scaling, ratios, data handling in testing
**Science:** Material properties, forces, electrical circuits, nutrition
**Computing:** Programming, control systems, CAD/CAM, digital design tools
**Art & Design:** Aesthetics, visual communication, cultural context

### The STEM Agenda

David Barlex has written extensively about D&T's role in STEM education, arguing that D&T provides the "application context" for scientific and mathematical principles. However, he cautions against D&T being subsumed into generic STEM, emphasising that design thinking and making capability represent distinct educational objectives.

The "E4E" (Engineering for Education) programme and similar initiatives have promoted engineering-focused D&T projects. Whilst valuable, Stephanie Atkinson and others argue for maintaining D&T's broader focus on user-centred design rather than purely engineering challenges.

## Current Challenges and Debates

### Subject Viability and Uptake

GCSE D&T entries have declined significantly since the introduction of the EBacc, which doesn't include D&T in its core measures. The Design and Technology Association reports that many secondary schools have reduced D&T provision, particularly at KS3, raising concerns about subject sustainability.

### Teacher Supply and Expertise

Teacher recruitment in D&T remains challenging. The broad nature of the unified GCSE requires teachers with expertise across multiple material areas, yet most D&T teachers have specialist backgrounds. Continued professional development (CPD) is crucial but often inadequately funded.

### Resource Implications

Quality D&T education requires specialist facilities, tools, materials, and equipment. Maintained schools receive no ring-fenced funding for D&T resources, creating significant inequity. Kay Stables has highlighted that inadequate resourcing fundamentally constrains what pupils can achieve.

### Curriculum Time Allocation

Research by Matt McLain indicates that many schools provide insufficient curriculum time for D&T, particularly at KS3. Meaningful design-and-make projects require sustained engagement – fragmented short lessons severely limit educational outcomes.

## Future Directions and Innovations

### Digital Technologies

Emerging technologies continue to transform D&T practice:
- CAD/CAM integration from KS2
- 3D printing and rapid prototyping
- Laser cutting and CNC machining
- Microcontrollers (BBC micro:bit, Arduino, Raspberry Pi)
- Virtual and augmented reality for design visualisation

David Barlex advocates for these technologies to enhance rather than replace traditional making skills, arguing that "haptic" (touch-based) knowledge remains fundamental to design capability.

### Pedagogical Developments

Current pedagogical innovation includes:
- **Design fixation** research influencing how teachers present stimuli
- **Critique culture** adapted from architecture and art education
- **User-centred design** methodologies from professional practice
- **Systems thinking** approaches addressing complexity
- **Sustainable design** embedding environmental considerations throughout

## Conclusion

The National Curriculum for Design & Technology provides a framework for developing pupils' capability to design and make products that address authentic human needs. From early exploration with materials at KS1 through to sophisticated design-and-make projects at GCSE, the curriculum enables progressive development of technical knowledge, practical skills, and design thinking.

Effective implementation requires adequate resourcing, specialist teaching, sufficient curriculum time, and pedagogical approaches that honour D&T's distinctive contribution to education. As Richard Kimbell observed, D&T uniquely develops "the interplay between hand and mind" – capability that remains essential in an increasingly complex, technological world.
