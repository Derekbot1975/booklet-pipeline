# D&T at Key Stage 1

## Introduction and National Curriculum Context

Key Stage 1 Design & Technology encompasses Years 1 and 2 (ages 5-7) and represents children's first formal encounter with the subject within the National Curriculum for England. The statutory requirements are outlined in the Department for Education's *National Curriculum in England: Design and Technology Programmes of Study* (2013), which establishes the foundation for developing practical capabilities and design thinking in young learners.

The Key Stage 1 programme of study is deliberately concise, focusing on developing children's capability to create products through designing, making, and evaluating. As Richard Kimbell has argued in his seminal work on assessment in design and technology, even at this early stage, children are capable of engaging in sophisticated design thinking when provided with appropriate contexts and scaffolding (*Assessing Technology*, 1997).

## Statutory Requirements

### Programme of Study Overview

The National Curriculum (2013) specifies that Key Stage 1 pupils should be taught to:

**Design:**
- Design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users based on design criteria
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through talking, drawing, templates, mock-ups and, where appropriate, information and communication technology

**Make:**
- Select from and use a range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks (for example, cutting, shaping, joining and finishing)
- Select from and use a wide range of materials and components, including construction materials, textiles and ingredients, according to their characteristics

**Evaluate:**
- Explore and evaluate a range of existing products
- Evaluate their ideas and products against design criteria

**Technical Knowledge:**
- Build structures, exploring how they can be made stronger, stiffer and more stable
- Explore and use mechanisms (for example, levers, sliders, wheels and axles), in their products

### Additional Subject Areas

The programme of study also specifies that pupils should be taught about:

**Cooking and Nutrition:**
- Use the basic principles of a healthy and varied diet to prepare dishes
- Understand where food comes from

## Developmental Appropriateness

### Cognitive Development at Key Stage 1

Stephanie Atkinson's research on young children's design and technology capability highlights that 5-7 year olds are in a critical period of cognitive development where concrete, hands-on experiences are essential (*'Young Children's Capability with Information and Communications Technology'*, 2000). Children at this stage are transitioning from pre-operational to concrete operational thinking (following Piagetian theory), meaning they learn most effectively through direct manipulation of materials and real-world contexts.

Kay Stables emphasises in her research that even very young children can engage meaningfully with design activities when these are presented through narrative contexts and linked to their own experiences (*'The Design Process'*, DATA publication, 2008). The use of stories, role-play scenarios, and familiar contexts helps children understand the purpose behind their designing and making.

### Physical Development Considerations

Fine motor skills are still developing significantly during Key Stage 1. Teachers must recognise that children at this stage:

- Are developing hand-eye coordination and manual dexterity
- May struggle with tools requiring sustained grip strength
- Benefit from repeated practice with basic techniques
- Need appropriately sized tools and equipment
- Require careful supervision for safety whilst being encouraged towards independence

## Pedagogical Approaches

### The Design and Make Assignment (DMA)

Richard Kimbell's Design and Make Assignment framework, whilst originally developed for secondary assessment, has influenced Key Stage 1 practice through its emphasis on holistic project work. At this level, projects should typically last 2-4 sessions and maintain a clear narrative thread throughout.

**Effective DMA characteristics at KS1:**
- Clear user and purpose established at the outset
- Simple design criteria co-constructed with children
- Opportunities for iterative development
- Balance between teacher guidance and child autonomy
- Celebration of outcomes through sharing and reflection

### Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)

Before children can successfully complete design and make assignments, they require skill development through Focused Practical Tasks. David Barlex has written extensively about the importance of sequencing skill development (*'Assessing Capability in Design & Technology'*, 2007).

**Essential FPTs for Key Stage 1:**

*Construction skills:*
- Measuring and marking (using rulers, templates)
- Cutting (scissors, saws with appropriate supervision)
- Joining (glue, tape, split pins, temporary fixings)
- Strengthening structures (folding, corrugating, using braces)

*Textile skills:*
- Threading needles (large plastic needles, thick thread)
- Running stitch and basic joining
- Decorating fabrics (fabric crayons, simple appliqué)
- Pattern recognition and template use

*Food preparation:*
- Hygiene practices (handwashing, clean surfaces)
- Basic cutting skills (bridge and claw grip)
- Mixing and combining ingredients
- Following simple recipes

### Investigative and Evaluative Activities (IEAs)

Evaluation begins with exploration. Before designing, children should explore existing products to understand:

- How products work (mechanisms, structures)
- Who uses them and why
- What makes them appealing or fit for purpose
- How they are constructed

This aligns with Stephanie Atkinson's emphasis on 'product analysis' as a vehicle for developing both technical understanding and critical thinking from an early age.

## Subject Knowledge for Teachers

### Materials and Their Properties

Teachers must develop confident understanding of materials commonly used at Key Stage 1:

**Construction materials:**
- Card and corrugated card (strength, fold-ability, joining methods)
- Reclaimed materials (boxes, tubes, containers)
- Malleable materials (clay, playdough, Modroc)
- Wood strips (safe cutting and joining for young children)
- Plastics (bottles, containers for reclaimed work)

**Textiles:**
- Felt (doesn't fray, easy to cut and join)
- Cotton fabrics (plain weave for first stitching)
- Hessian (open weave for developing skills)
- Wadding and stuffing materials
- Fastenings (buttons, Velcro, poppers)

### Structures and Mechanisms

**Structures:**
Children should explore the concepts of:
- Stability (wide base, low centre of gravity)
- Strength (material thickness, folding, laminating)
- Stiffness (corrugating, using edge bracing, triangulation)

Kay Stables' work emphasises that children can grasp these concepts through exploratory play and guided investigation rather than formal instruction.

**Mechanisms:**
At Key Stage 1, children should experience:
- Levers and pivots (simple see-saws, moving parts on models)
- Sliders (in greetings cards, information books)
- Wheels and axles (vehicles, moving toys)
- Simple linkages (connecting card mechanisms)

Matt McLain's research on primary design and technology highlights that mechanisms teaching is most effective when embedded in meaningful contexts rather than taught as isolated skills (*'Cross-Curricular Approaches to Teaching and Learning'*, 2015).

## Planning and Progression

### Progression Framework

**Year 1 expectations:**

*Design:*
- Generate ideas from own experiences and from existing products
- Develop ideas through talk and rudimentary drawings
- Use simple design criteria (colour, size, basic function)

*Make:*
- Select from familiar tools with teacher support
- Use basic techniques with increasing control (cutting, joining, finishing)
- Work in a safe and organised manner with adult supervision

*Evaluate:*
- Talk about existing products with prompts
- Discuss their own work informally during making
- Identify what went well

*Technical knowledge:*
- Recognise basic structures in the environment
- Explore simple mechanisms through play and investigation
- Name common materials and tools

**Year 2 expectations:**

*Design:*
- Generate ideas from a range of stimuli and begin to communicate in different forms
- Develop ideas through drawing, templates and mock-ups
- Use more detailed design criteria including user needs

*Make:*
- Select appropriate tools with increasing independence
- Demonstrate improved accuracy and attention to finish
- Begin to suggest own improvements during making

*Evaluate:*
- Describe existing products with growing vocabulary
- Evaluate against simple design criteria
- Suggest how products could be improved

*Technical knowledge:*
- Understand how structures can be strengthened
- Use mechanisms with purpose in own designs
- Make simple connections between materials and their uses

### Long-term Planning Considerations

A balanced Key Stage 1 D&T curriculum should include:

- **At least 3-4 substantial D&T projects per year** (each lasting multiple sessions)
- **Coverage of all material areas** across the key stage (construction, textiles, food)
- **Progression in technical skills** from Year 1 to Year 2
- **Cross-curricular links** where meaningful (but ensuring D&T learning objectives remain clear)
- **Regular skill-building sessions** between major projects

David Barlex emphasises the danger of 'tokenism' in primary D&T where subjects are marginalised by insufficient curriculum time or integration that dilutes learning objectives (*'Creativity in Crisis'*, Design and Technology Association, 2003).

## Cross-Curricular Connections

### Literacy Links

D&T provides authentic contexts for developing literacy skills:

- Following and writing instructions (recipes, construction methods)
- Labelling diagrams and designs
- Storytelling as design context (designing products for characters)
- Persuasive writing (explaining why their design is effective)
- Technical vocabulary development

### Mathematics Links

Design and technology naturally incorporates mathematical thinking:

- Measurement (length, mass, capacity)
- Shape recognition and properties (2D and 3D shapes in construction)
- Counting and ordering (steps in processes, quantities in recipes)
- Problem-solving (how many wheels needed? will it fit?)

### Science Links

The material properties explored in D&T complement science learning:

- Material characteristics (waterproof, strong, flexible)
- Forces (push, pull, twist in mechanisms)
- Healthy eating and nutrition (linking to science of the body)

**Important caveat:** Matt McLain warns against allowing D&T to become merely a 'service subject' for other areas. Each project must have clear D&T learning objectives that are explicitly taught and assessed.

## Cooking and Nutrition

### Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum (2013) made cooking and nutrition a specific requirement within D&T. At Key Stage 1, this includes:

- Understanding basic principles of healthy eating
- Knowing where food comes from (growing, farming, shops)
- Preparing simple savoury and sweet dishes
- Developing safe and hygienic practices

### Practical Implementation

**Appropriate dishes for Key Stage 1:**

*Year 1 might include:*
- Fruit salads (washing, peeling, cutting soft fruit)
- Sandwich making (spreading, simple fillings)
- Simple biscuits (mixing, shaping)
- Smoothies (combining ingredients, safe use of blenders with supervision)

*Year 2 might progress to:*
- Salads with multiple ingredients
- Simple cooked items (pizza faces, simple pasta dishes)
- Wrapped items (wraps, simple sushi rolls)
- Baking with weighing and following recipes

### Health and Safety Considerations

- All cooking must follow school food safety policies
- Consider allergies and dietary requirements
- Teach handwashing and clean surfaces as routine
- Introduce age-appropriate knife skills (bridge and claw grip)
- Ensure adequate supervision ratios
- Use appropriate equipment (junior hacksaws for food, smaller utensils)

## Assessment at Key Stage 1

### Approaches to Assessment

Richard Kimbell's research emphasises that design and technology assessment should focus on capability demonstrated through practical activity rather than written or verbal responses alone (*'Assessing Technology', 1997*). At Key Stage 1, this is particularly important given children's developing literacy skills.

**Effective assessment strategies:**

*During projects (formative):*
- Observation of practical skills development
- Photographs of work in progress (particularly important as products may not survive)
- Simple self-assessment using visual prompts (smiley faces, thumbs up/down)
- Verbal discussions recorded through teacher notes
- Annotation of children's drawings by adults

*End of projects (summative):*
- Comparison against design criteria
- Photographic evidence of completed products
- Simple written/drawn evaluations appropriate to age
- Teacher assessment against National Curriculum statements

### Key Performance Indicators

**Working towards expected standard (Year 2):**
- Generates ideas from own experiences with support
- Uses basic techniques with adult guidance
- Talks about their work with prompts
- Recognises simple structures and mechanisms

**Working at expected standard (Year 2):**
- Designs products based on simple design criteria
- Selects appropriate tools and materials
- Evaluates against design criteria
- Understands how to strengthen structures and use mechanisms

**Working at greater depth (Year 2):**
- Shows creativity in generating and developing ideas
- Works with increasing precision and independence
- Makes thoughtful evaluations and suggests improvements
- Applies technical knowledge to solve problems

## Classroom Organisation and Resources

### Essential Equipment for Key Stage 1 D&T

**Basic toolkit (per group of 4-6 children):**
- Scissors (left and right-handed, various sizes)
- Glue sticks and PVA glue
- Sticky tape and masking tape
- Rulers and pencils
- Split pins (brass fasteners)
- Staplers (appropriate size for children)

**Construction materials:**
- Range of card (thin card, mounting board)
- Reclaimed materials (systematically collected)
- Wood strips or lollipop sticks
- Dowel and wheels (pre-purchased)
- Various joining materials

**Textiles resources:**
- Plastic needles and bodkins
- Thread (thick thread, wool, embroidery thread)
- Range of fabrics (felt, cotton, hessian)
- Wadding and stuffing
- Basic decorating materials

**Food technology:**
- Child-safe knives
- Chopping boards (colour-coded)
- Mixing bowls and measuring equipment
- Appropriate utensils and equipment

### Health and Safety

Kay Stables emphasises that risk assessment should focus on enabling children to develop independence within safe parameters rather than eliminating all risk (*'Risk and Primary Design and Technology'*, 2000).

**Key safety considerations:**

- **Tool use:** Introduce tools systematically with clear demonstrations; children should understand safe carrying and storage
- **Supervision levels:** Increased adult support for tools requiring more skill or presenting greater risk
- **Safe behaviours:** Establish routines (walking with scissors pointing down, using cutting mats, organised workspaces)
- **Allergies and dietary requirements:** Comprehensive information maintained and communicated
- **Risk-benefit analysis:** Allow children developmentally appropriate challenges

The Design and Technology Association (DATA, now the Design and Technology Association) publishes *'Primary School Guidance Materials - Health and Safety'* which provides specific guidance for Key Stage 1 contexts.

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge: Limited Curriculum Time

**Solutions:**
- Protect discrete D&T time in the timetable
- Use extended project sessions (morning or afternoon) rather than fragmenting into short periods
- Consider D&T days or afternoons where multiple sessions occur
- Link to other subjects meaningfully but maintain clear D&T objectives

### Challenge: Teacher Confidence with Technical Content

**Solutions:**
- Access subject-specific CPD (Design and Technology Association offers courses)
- Develop subject knowledge through practice before teaching
- Use reliable published schemes (e.g., Projects on a Page, D&T Association schemes)
- Collaborate with colleagues to share expertise
- Reference David Barlex's materials on developing teacher capability

### Challenge: Managing Practical Sessions

**Solutions:**
- Prepare materials in advance (templates, pre-cut components where appropriate)
- Use teaching assistants strategically for skill demonstration stations
- Establish clear routines for equipment distribution and clearing away
- Consider whether whole-class making is appropriate or whether groups work on rotation
- Take photographs for assessment as work progresses

### Challenge: Differentiation

**Solutions:**
- Provide materials of varying difficulty (easier to cut/join)
- Offer templates alongside opportunities to create own
- Adult support targeted to those needing technical assistance
- Open-ended design briefs allowing for different levels of sophistication
- Celebrate diverse outcomes that meet design criteria in different ways

## Supporting All Learners

### SEND Considerations

Stephanie Atkinson's research highlights that design and technology can be particularly enabling for children with special educational needs when appropriately differentiated (*'Developing Technical and Vocational Skills'*, 2001).

**Strategies include:**
- Modified tools (adapted grips, left-handed equipment)
- Visual instructions and task boards
- Pre-teaching technical vocabulary
- Additional adult support for fine motor difficulties
- Extended time allowances
- Alternative recording methods (photographs, video, oral explanations)

### Gifted and Talented Pupils

Higher attaining pupils benefit from:
- More complex design criteria
- Opportunities to work more independently
- Challenges to improve efficiency or aesthetics
- Introduction to more sophisticated mechanisms or techniques
- Design problems requiring creative problem-solving
