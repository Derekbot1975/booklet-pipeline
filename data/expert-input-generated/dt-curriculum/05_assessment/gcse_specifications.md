# GCSE D&T Specifications

## Overview of GCSE Design & Technology in England

GCSE Design & Technology represents the culmination of Key Stage 3 learning and provides students aged 14-16 with a unified understanding of design, materials, manufacturing processes, and technological systems. Following the 2017 curriculum reforms led by the Department for Education, all major awarding organisations (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) introduced revised specifications that took effect from September 2017, with first examinations in Summer 2019.

The reformed GCSE D&T specifications were influenced significantly by the work of David Barlex and Richard Kimbell, who argued for a return to authentic design-and-make activities whilst ensuring appropriate technical knowledge. As Barlex (2015) emphasised in his critiques of earlier iterations, the specifications needed to balance "knowledge about materials and manufacturing with knowledge for creative designing."

## Common Framework Across Awarding Organisations

All GCSE D&T specifications share a common structure mandated by Ofqual's Subject Level Conditions for Design & Technology (2016). These specify:

### Assessment Structure

**Non-Exam Assessment (NEA): 50% of qualification**
- A substantial design-and-make project (approximately 30-35 hours)
- Contextual challenges released annually by each awarding organisation
- Assessment against four distinct sections: investigation, design, make, and evaluate
- Maximum raw marks: typically 100 marks

**Written Examination: 50% of qualification**
- Single written paper lasting 2 hours
- 100 marks available
- Mixture of short answer, structured, and extended response questions
- Core technical principles (approximately 35-40 marks)
- Specialist technical principles (approximately 30-35 marks)
- Designing and making principles (approximately 30-35 marks)

### Subject Content Requirements

The Department for Education's (2016) GCSE Design & Technology Subject Content document specifies three principal areas of knowledge and understanding:

1. **Core Technical Principles** (universal technical knowledge)
2. **Specialist Technical Principles** (material category specific knowledge)
3. **Designing and Making Principles** (iterative design processes)

## Material Specialisms and Knowledge Domains

Following the 2017 reforms, GCSE D&T became a single qualification encompassing all material areas. Unlike previous Resistant Materials, Textiles, or Food Technology GCSEs, students now study across multiple material categories. Kay Stables (2017) noted this represented "a significant philosophical shift toward design capability across contexts rather than material specialism."

### Core Technical Principles

All students must develop understanding across:

- **New and emerging technologies**: Industry 4.0, artificial intelligence in design, biometric data, crowdfunding
- **Energy generation and storage**: fossil fuels, nuclear power, renewable sources, battery technologies
- **Developments in new materials**: smart materials, modern materials, composite materials, technical textiles
- **Systems approach to designing**: inputs, processes, outputs, feedback loops
- **Mechanical devices**: gears, pulleys, cams, levers, linkages
- **Materials and their working properties**: physical and mechanical properties, material selection

### Specialist Technical Principles

Students develop deeper knowledge in at least one material category:

- **Timber-based materials**: hardwoods, softwoods, manufactured boards, joining methods
- **Metal-based materials**: ferrous and non-ferrous metals, alloys, forming processes
- **Polymers**: thermoplastics, thermosetting plastics, elastomers, manufacturing processes
- **Textiles-based materials**: natural, synthetic, mixed fibres, construction techniques
- **Electronic and mechanical systems**: programmable components, microcontrollers, sensor applications
- **Papers and boards**: paper types, printing processes, die-cutting, creasing

Richard Kimbell's research (2012) on e-scape assessment influenced how specifications structure practical capability, arguing that "judgements of designing capability must emerge from the interactions between mind and hand that characterise authentic project work."

## Individual Awarding Organisation Approaches

### AQA (8552)

AQA's specification emphasises the iterative nature of designing through its NEA structure. The contextual challenges typically provide broad starting points allowing student interpretation. Assessment Objectives weight:
- AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding): 25%
- AO2 (Application of Knowledge): 25%
- AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation): 30%
- AO4 (Technical Skills): 20%

AQA provides explicit guidance on mathematical requirements, with approximately 15% of marks requiring mathematical skills at Level 2 (GCSE Mathematics).

### Pearson Edexcel (1DT0)

Edexcel places particular emphasis on sustainability and environmental issues throughout both examination and NEA components. Their written paper includes specific marks allocated to commercial manufacturing and quality control systems. Matt McLain's work with Edexcel on defining 'design fixation' in GCSE students influenced their guidance on divergent thinking strategies.

### OCR (J310)

OCR adopts a problem-solving approach aligned with engineering contexts. Their specification explicitly references the work of Stephanie Atkinson on inclusive design and user-centred approaches. The NEA tasks often incorporate specific user profiles requiring accessibility considerations. OCR provides extensive guidance on iterative development cycles and the use of modelling throughout designing.

### WJEC Eduqas

WJEC Eduqas specifications serve schools in Wales and some English centres. Their approach integrates Welsh industry case studies and emphasises the role of designers in society. The written examination includes specific questions requiring students to critique existing products using ACCESS FM principles (Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Safety, Size, Function, Materials/Manufacture).

## Non-Exam Assessment (NEA) Detailed Requirements

### The Contextual Challenge

Each awarding organisation releases NEA contextual challenges annually, typically in June for teaching from September. These challenges provide thematic starting points whilst allowing student interpretation. Barlex and Steeg (2017) describe effective challenges as those creating "bounded freedom – sufficient constraint to provide direction whilst enabling personalised responses."

Contextual challenges must enable students to:
- Identify authentic user needs or wants
- Research contextual information
- Develop design criteria
- Generate multiple design ideas
- Develop and refine design solutions
- Manufacture a prototype to appropriate quality standards
- Test and evaluate outcomes against user requirements

### Assessment Criteria Structure

Whilst specific marking varies, all specifications assess:

**Investigation (approximately 20 marks)**
- Analysis of contextual challenge and user requirements
- Research into existing products, user needs, materials, and manufacturing methods
- Development of design criteria/specification

**Design (approximately 40 marks)**
- Generation of multiple design ideas
- Communication techniques including sketching, CAD, modelling
- Development and refinement of design solution
- Use of feedback from stakeholders
- Technical detail including dimensions, materials, manufacturing methods

**Make (approximately 30 marks)**
- Appropriate tools, equipment, and processes
- Technical skill and accuracy
- Quality of construction and finish
- Functionality and fitness-for-purpose

**Evaluate (approximately 10 marks)**
- Testing against design criteria
- Evaluation of fitness-for-purpose
- Consideration of modifications and improvements

Kay Stables' research (2008) on assessment authenticity influences this structure, arguing that "assessment must mirror the reality of design practice whilst remaining manageable within educational contexts."

## Knowledge Depth Requirements

### Core Technical Principles: Breadth of Knowledge

The specifications require students to understand technical knowledge applicable across material contexts. This represents a significant departure from previous material-specific qualifications. Teachers must ensure coverage of:

**Materials and Their Properties**
- Classification systems for materials
- Physical properties: density, electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, absorbency
- Mechanical properties: hardness, toughness, malleability, ductility, elasticity
- Working properties relevant to designing and manufacturing

**Forces and Stresses**
- Tension, compression, shear, torsion, bending
- Application to product analysis and design decisions

**Ecological and Social Footprint**
- Product life cycles (extraction, manufacturing, use, disposal)
- 6Rs: Rethink, Refuse, Reduce, Reuse, Repair, Recycle
- Continuous improvement approaches (Kaizen)
- Life Cycle Analysis methodologies

Richard Kimbell's advocacy for contextualised knowledge application is evident in examination questions requiring students to "apply understanding to unfamiliar situations rather than merely recall facts" (Kimbell, 2016).

### Specialist Technical Principles: Depth of Knowledge

Students must develop detailed understanding of at least one material category, though most specifications encourage broader coverage. For polymers, this includes:

- Thermoplastic properties and applications (acrylic, polystyrene, ABS, polypropylene, PET)
- Thermosetting plastic characteristics (urea formaldehyde, epoxy resin, polyester resin)
- Manufacturing processes: vacuum forming, line bending, injection moulding, blow moulding, extrusion
- Finishing techniques: sanding, polishing, painting, printing

For electronic systems:
- Programmable components and microcontrollers (PIC, Arduino platforms)
- Input devices: switches, sensors (temperature, light, moisture, proximity)
- Output devices: LEDs, buzzers, motors, displays
- Programming using flowcharts or block-based languages

## Designing and Making Principles

This knowledge domain integrates theoretical understanding with practical capability. Specifications require knowledge of:

### Investigation and User-Centred Design

- Primary and secondary research methods
- Anthropometric and ergonomic data application
- User profiles and personas
- Task analysis techniques
- Product analysis frameworks (ACCESS FM)

Stephanie Atkinson's extensive work on inclusive design (2012) informs specification requirements that students "consider diverse user needs including physical, sensory, and cognitive capabilities."

### Design Strategies and Communication

- Iterative design processes
- Divergent and convergent thinking approaches
- Communication techniques: orthographic projection, isometric drawing, perspective drawing, exploded diagrams
- CAD/CAM applications and parametric modelling
- Physical modelling and prototyping strategies

### Manufacturing Processes and Techniques

- Wasting processes: cutting, drilling, milling, turning, laser cutting
- Addition processes: joining, fastening, assembling
- Reforming processes: bending, folding, laminating, moulding
- Commercial manufacturing: batch production, mass production, continuous flow
- Quality control: tolerance, quality assurance systems, testing protocols

Matt McLain's research (2015) on student misconceptions about manufacturing emphasises that "students often possess naive understandings of industrial processes, requiring explicit teaching of scale, efficiency, and automation."

## Mathematical Requirements

Specifications mandate approximately 15% of available marks require mathematical skills. These include:

- Calculate areas, volumes, and quantities of materials
- Scale drawings and ratios
- Unit conversions
- Interpretation of graphs and charts
- Percentages for material composition and costings
- Use of formulae (mechanical advantage, velocity ratio, gear ratios)

Teachers must explicitly teach mathematical applications within D&T contexts rather than assuming transfer from Mathematics GCSE.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Curriculum Planning

**Two-Year Course Structure**: Most centres adopt a three-term approach:
- **Year 10, Terms 1-2**: Core and specialist technical principles teaching through focused practical tasks
- **Year 10, Term 3 to Year 11, Term 1**: NEA project completion
- **Year 11, Terms 2-3**: Revision, examination preparation, and retrospective NEA analysis

Barlex (2018) recommends "front-loading technical knowledge through purposeful practical activities that develop skills whilst building conceptual understanding."

### Integration of Theory and Practice

The specifications require seamless integration of theoretical knowledge with practical capability. Effective approaches include:

- **Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)**: Short projects developing specific skills whilst embedding technical knowledge
- **Technical Demonstrations**: Teacher-led sessions exploring materials processing or manufacturing techniques
- **Product Disassembly**: Analysis of existing products to understand material selection, construction methods, and functionality
- **Case Studies**: Examination of real-world design problems, designer approaches, and industrial manufacturing

Kay Stables and Richard Kimbell's E-scape research (2007) demonstrated that "iterative practical exploration develops deeper conceptual understanding than theory-first approaches."

### Managing the NEA

Centres face significant challenges in managing the NEA component within time and resource constraints:

**Timing and Project Management**
- Most centres allocate 30-35 hours of supervised time
- Students require explicit project management training
- Milestone deadlines prevent rushed final submissions
- Authentication procedures ensure student ownership

**Contextual Challenge Selection**
- Annual release creates planning challenges
- Some centres run trial projects with previous year's challenges
- Student choice within challenge parameters increases engagement

**Standardisation and Moderation**
- Internal standardisation essential across multiple teaching groups
- Understanding mark scheme application through exemplar materials
- Preparation for external moderation visits or postal sampling

David Barlex's analysis of NEA assessment (2019) emphasises "the critical role of formative feedback throughout project development whilst maintaining summative assessment integrity."

### Resource Implications

GCSE D&T specifications place substantial demands on workshop facilities, equipment, and materials:

- Multi-material workshops enabling polymer, timber, and metal work
- CAD/CAM facilities including 2D cutters and 3D printers
- Electronic components and programmable systems
- Textile equipment where centres offer this specialism
- Health and safety provisions compliant with CLEAPSS guidance

Budget constraints require strategic planning. Barlex and Steeg (2017) advocate for "making strategic investments in technologies that enable rather than prescribe design outcomes."

## Assessment Objectives and Mark Distribution

All specifications align with Ofqual's mandated Assessment Objectives:

**AO1: Identify, investigate and outline design possibilities**
- Typically 10-15% of qualification
- Demonstrated through NEA investigation and examination questions requiring research application

**AO2: Design and make prototypes that are fit for purpose**
- Typically 45-50% of qualification
- Predominantly assessed through NEA design development and practical manufacture

**AO3: Analyse and evaluate design decisions and wider issues**
- Typically 20-25% of qualification
- Assessed through NEA evaluation and examination questions requiring critical analysis

**AO4: Demonstrate and apply knowledge and understanding of technical principles**
- Typically 20-25% of qualification
- Assessed through written examination and application of knowledge in NEA

Richard Kimbell argues that "assessment architecture fundamentally shapes pedagogical approaches – specifications that over-emphasise content knowledge risk creating technician-students rather than designer-technologists" (Kimbell, 2017).

## Examining Technical Knowledge

The written examination typically contains 15-20 questions with varying mark allocations:

**Short Answer Questions (1-4 marks)**
- Define terminology
- Identify materials, tools, or processes
- State properties or characteristics

**Structured Questions (5-9 marks)**
- Explain reasons for material/process selection
- Describe manufacturing sequences
- Compare alternative approaches

**Extended Response Questions (8-12 marks)**
- Analyse design problems considering multiple factors
- Evaluate commercial products against criteria
- Justify design decisions with detailed reasoning

Matt McLain's research on examination performance (2018) reveals "students frequently struggle with application questions requiring synthesis of knowledge from multiple domains – explicit teaching of transfer strategies improves performance."

## Quality of Written Communication

All specifications assess quality of written communication, particularly in extended response questions. Students must:

- Use specialist technical terminology accurately
- Structure extended answers logically
- Communicate ideas clearly using appropriate annotation and labelling
- Demonstrate literacy appropriate to GCSE level

Marks are specifically allocated in 8-12 mark questions for communication quality, typically 1-3 additional marks.

## Differentiation and Accessibility

GCSE D&T specifications serve students across the full ability range with grades 9-1 available. The reformed specifications removed Foundation and Higher tiers, creating a single tier of entry.

### Supporting Lower Attaining Students

- Scaffold NEA projects with templates and structured frameworks
- Provide technical knowledge organisers for revision
- Simplify manufacturing processes without compromising design quality
- Use accessible contexts for design briefs

### Stretching Higher Attaining Students

- Encourage sophisticated material combinations and manufacturing techniques
- Require detailed technical analysis and justification
- Introduce advanced concepts (parametric CAD, programmable systems)
- Explore complex user needs and inclusive design considerations

Stephanie Atkinson's research (2015) on differentiation argues that "authentic design problems naturally enable multiple entry points – the key is matching project scope to student capability."

## Links to Key Stage 3 and Post-16 Pathways

### Building from Key Stage 3

The National Curriculum for Design & Technology (2013) at Key Stage 3 establishes foundations including:
- Iterative design processes
- Materials knowledge and practical skills
- Technical understanding of mechanical and electronic systems
- Cooking and nutrition (for centres offering this emphasis)

GCSE specifications build systematically on this foundation, requiring deeper technical knowledge and more sophisticated practical capability.
