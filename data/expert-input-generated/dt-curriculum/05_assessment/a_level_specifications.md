# A Level D&T Specifications

## Overview of A Level Design & Technology

A Level Design & Technology represents the culmination of secondary design and technology education in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. The specifications were reformed in 2017 to align with broader curriculum changes, resulting in linear qualifications that emphasise both theoretical knowledge and practical capability. As David Barlex has noted, the reformed A Levels marked a significant shift towards "a more academically rigorous approach whilst maintaining the practical essence of the subject."

The current A Level specifications are offered by four main examination boards: AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. Each board offers different material specialisms and approaches, though all must meet the Department for Education's content requirements outlined in the A Level Design & Technology subject content document (DfE, 2016).

## Regulatory Framework and Content Requirements

### DfE Subject Content Requirements

The DfE's subject content document establishes that all A Level D&T specifications must enable learners to demonstrate knowledge and understanding in three core areas:

1. **Technical principles** (approximately 50% weighting) - covering technical knowledge and understanding of materials, systems, and processes
2. **Designing and making principles** (approximately 25% weighting) - including user-centred design, design theory, and innovation
3. **Practical application** (approximately 25% weighting) - demonstrated through a substantial design and make project

Richard Kimbell's research on design capability has significantly influenced this framework, particularly the emphasis on the iterative relationship between designing and making. His work on the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) established that authentic design capability must be assessed through extended practical activities rather than solely through written examination.

### Ofqual Regulatory Requirements

Ofqual's AS and A Level Design and Technology: Guidance on Assessment Objectives (2015) specifies three assessment objectives that all specifications must address:

- **AO1**: Identify, investigate and outline design possibilities to address needs and wants
- **AO2**: Design and make prototypes that are fit for purpose
- **AO3**: Analyse and evaluate design decisions and prototypes for commercial or educational purposes
- **AO4**: Demonstrate and apply knowledge and understanding of technical principles and designing and making principles

Kay Stables' research on authentic assessment in D&T emphasises that these objectives must be assessed in contexts that mirror professional design practice, maintaining what she terms "situated cognition" - learning that occurs within meaningful, real-world contexts.

## Examination Board Specifications

### AQA Design & Technology (7552)

AQA offers a single unified specification covering multiple material areas: timber-based, metal-based, polymers, textiles, and electronic/mechanical systems. The specification comprises:

**Component 1: Technical Principles (120 marks, 30% of A Level)**
- A 2.5-hour written examination
- Mixture of short and extended response questions
- Covers all technical principles including materials and their applications, performance characteristics of materials, enhancement of materials, forming processes, digital design and manufacture

**Component 2: Designing and Making Principles (120 marks, 20% of A Level)**
- A 1.5-hour written examination  
- Mixture of short and extended response questions
- Covers design methods, design theory, technology and cultural change, design for manufacturing, and sustainability

**Component 3: Non-Exam Assessment (100 marks, 50% of A Level)**
- Substantial design and make project
- Approximately 100 hours
- Students identify and investigate a contextual challenge
- Assessment criteria aligned with David Barlex's work on design fixation and iteration

The AQA specification draws heavily on Stephanie Atkinson's research into iterative design processes, requiring students to demonstrate ongoing evaluation and refinement rather than linear progression through design stages.

### Pearson Edexcel Design & Technology (9DT0)

Edexcel's specification offers three distinct pathways: Product Design, Fashion and Textiles, and Design Engineering. Each pathway maintains common core content whilst specialising in specific knowledge areas.

**Paper 1: Principles of Design and Technology (120 marks, 40% of A Level)**
- 2.5-hour written examination
- Section A: Product Analysis (30 marks) - analysis of existing products
- Section B: Core Technical Principles (70 marks)
- Section C: Specialist Technical Principles (20 marks) specific to chosen pathway

**Paper 2: Problem Solving in Design and Technology (80 marks, 20% of A Level)**
- 1.5-hour written examination
- Structured questions based on contextual challenges
- Tests application of technical and design principles to novel situations

**Component 3: Iterative Design Project (100 marks, 40% of A Level)**
- Non-exam assessment
- Approximately 100 hours
- Four assessment objectives covering investigation, design, making, and analysis/evaluation

The Edexcel approach reflects Matt McLain's research on design cognition, particularly his emphasis on problem-scoping and the importance of sustained engagement with design challenges.

### OCR Design & Technology (H404/H405/H406)

OCR offers three separate qualifications: Product Design (H404), Fashion and Textiles (H405), and Design Engineering (H406). Each is a standalone qualification with specialised content.

**Component 1: Principles of Design and Technology (120 marks, 45% of A Level)**
- 2.5-hour written examination
- Common content across all three specifications
- Covers designing processes, materials and applications, manufacturing processes, digital technologies, and broader social influences

**Component 2: Problem Solving in Design and Technology (80 marks, 15% of A Level)**
- 1.5-hour written examination
- Scenario-based problem solving
- Application of knowledge to unfamiliar contexts

**Component 3: Iterative Design Project (100 marks, 40% of A Level)**
- Non-exam assessment with defined stages: Investigation, Design, Making, Evaluation
- Approximately 100 hours
- Moderator training emphasises Richard Kimbell's APU framework for assessing design capability

OCR's approach incorporates Kay Stables' research on designerly thinking, particularly her work on how students develop and refine design ideas through making and prototyping activities.

### WJEC Eduqas Design & Technology (A650QS)

Available in Wales and some English centres, the Eduqas specification offers Product Design, Fashion and Textiles, with Design Engineering available from 2025.

**Component 1: Design and Technology in the 21st Century (90 marks, 22.5% of A Level)**
- 1.25-hour written examination
- Focuses on contemporary design issues, sustainability, enterprise, and user-centred design

**Component 2: Design and Technology - Core and Specialist Knowledge (90 marks, 22.5% of A Level)**
- 1.75-hour written examination
- Structured questions on technical principles

**Component 3: Design and Make Task (110 marks, 55% of A Level)**
- Non-exam assessment
- Contextual challenge set by WJEC
- Controlled assessment conditions for investigation phase
- Extended making project

The Eduqas specification places particular emphasis on contemporary practice and follows Stephanie Atkinson's recommendations for authentic design contexts that connect to students' lived experiences.

## Assessment Approaches and Moderation

### Non-Exam Assessment (NEA) Structure

All specifications require students to complete a substantial design and make project, typically representing 40-50% of the total A Level marks. The structure follows principles established by Richard Kimbell's research into authentic assessment:

**Investigation Phase**: Students must identify and investigate a genuine design need. David Barlex emphasises the importance of avoiding "design fixation" at this stage - students should explore multiple possibilities rather than immediately committing to a solution.

**Design Development Phase**: Iterative development through prototyping, testing, and refinement. Kay Stables' work on designerly behaviour highlights that this phase should demonstrate genuine development rather than predetermined outcomes illustrated through staged drawings.

**Making Phase**: Production of a functioning prototype. Assessment criteria recognise both technical skill and design thinking during manufacture, reflecting Stephanie Atkinson's research showing that significant design decisions continue during making.

**Evaluation and Analysis Phase**: Critical reflection on the project, process, and outcomes. Matt McLain's research on metacognition in D&T informs this requirement, emphasising students' ability to articulate their design thinking.

### Moderation and Standardisation

Examination boards implement rigorous moderation processes following Ofqual requirements:

**Centre Assessment**: Teachers assess NEA work using board-specific criteria, typically organised into four assessment strands aligned with the assessment objectives.

**Standardisation Activities**: Online standardisation exercises ensure consistent interpretation of assessment criteria. These typically include marked exemplar materials with commentary from senior moderators.

**Moderation Visits or Postal Moderation**: External moderators review centre marking. AQA, Edexcel, and Eduqas primarily use postal moderation; OCR employs visiting moderation for larger centres. Moderators receive extensive training based on Kimbell's assessment frameworks.

**Scaling and Adjustment**: Where centres show systematic over- or under-marking, adjustments ensure national standards are maintained whilst recognising teacher professional judgement.

## Technical Knowledge Content

### Core Technical Principles

All specifications require substantial technical knowledge across several domains:

**Materials Science**: Properties, characteristics, and working properties of materials including woods, metals, polymers, textiles, modern and smart materials. Content draws on materials science curricula and industrial practice.

**Processes and Manufacture**: Commercial manufacturing processes including casting, moulding, fabrication, addition and wastage processes. Specifications emphasise scales of production and the relationship between design decisions and manufacturing methods.

**Digital Technologies**: CAD/CAM systems, digital manufacturing including 3D printing and CNC machining, electronic systems, and programmable components. David Barlex's work on technology-mediated learning influences this content, particularly regarding how digital tools enhance design capability.

**Engineering Principles**: Mechanical systems, electronic circuits, pneumatics, structural integrity, and forces analysis. The Design Engineering pathways include substantial mathematics and physics content.

### Specialist Technical Knowledge

Different pathways and specialisms require additional depth:

**Product Design**: Focuses on resistant materials and manufactured products, including furniture, consumer products, and packaging design.

**Fashion and Textiles**: Textile science, pattern construction, garment manufacture, surface decoration, and fashion marketing.

**Design Engineering**: Advanced mechanical and electronic systems, mathematics for engineering, control systems, and structures. This pathway has particular overlap with A Level Physics and Mathematics content.

## Design Theory and Principles

### User-Centred Design

All specifications emphasise design approaches that prioritise user needs. Content includes:

**Anthropometrics and Ergonomics**: Human factors in design, working with data about body dimensions and capabilities. Kay Stables' research emphasises that students must apply this knowledge contextually rather than merely memorising data tables.

**Inclusive Design**: Designing for diverse user groups, accessibility considerations, and the social model of disability. Influenced by professional practice standards including BS 8300.

**User Research Methods**: Primary and secondary research techniques including surveys, interviews, observation studies, and product analysis. Reflects professional methodologies from interaction design and human-centred design disciplines.

### Design Movements and Contextual Influences

Students must understand design history and contemporary practice:

**Historical Context**: Arts and Crafts, Bauhaus, Modernism, Post-Modernism, and contemporary movements. Matt McLain's research on visual literacy informs pedagogical approaches to teaching design history.

**Cultural and Social Influences**: How design both shapes and is shaped by society, technology, and culture. Connects to broader humanities and social sciences content.

**Contemporary Designers and Companies**: Case studies of significant practitioners and organisations. Specifications reference companies like Dyson, Apple, and designers like Dieter Rams and Jonathan Ive.

### Design for Manufacture and Enterprise

Commercial realities of design practice feature prominently:

**Design for Manufacture (DFM)**: Principles ensuring designs can be efficiently manufactured at scale. Includes considerations of material selection, tolerances, assembly methods, and cost implications.

**Enterprise and Marketing**: Business contexts for design including market analysis, intellectual property, consumer protection, and product lifecycle management. Stephanie Atkinson's work on authentic learning contexts emphasises connecting classroom activity with commercial realities.

**Sustainability**: Environmental considerations throughout the product lifecycle, circular economy principles, and sustainable material selection. Increasingly prominent following climate awareness and regulatory changes.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Curriculum Planning

**Teaching Time Allocation**: Schools typically allocate 240-300 hours annually (approximately 7-9 hours weekly) for A Level D&T. David Barlex recommends approximately 60% teaching time for theory content in Year 12, shifting to 70-80% NEA focus in Year 13.

**Sequencing Considerations**: Teachers must balance content delivery with NEA project time. Most effective approaches introduce technical principles through small practical activities before students commence major NEA projects.

**Resource Requirements**: A Level D&T demands significant workshop facilities, materials budgets (typically £3000-5000+ annually), and specialist equipment. OCR and AQA provide guidance on minimum facility standards.

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Bridging from GCSE**: Significant step-up in theoretical knowledge, mathematical content, and independent project management. Kay Stables' research on progression highlights the importance of explicitly teaching design decision-making processes.

**Flipped Learning**: Many teachers use pre-recorded content to cover theoretical knowledge, maximising face-to-face time for practical activities and individual project support. Particularly effective for technical principles content.

**Project-Based Learning**: Embedding technical content within practical contexts. David Barlex advocates for "just-in-time" teaching where technical knowledge is introduced when students need it for their projects.

**Collaborative Learning**: Group projects for skills development (not assessment) helps build technical capability and mirrors professional practice. Matt McLain's research on design cognition emphasises the value of peer critique and discussion.

### Supporting the NEA

**Context Setting**: Teachers can provide broad contexts but must not dictate specific design solutions. Ofqual guidance emphasises student autonomy in defining problems and developing solutions.

**Formative Assessment**: Regular feedback crucial for project development. Richard Kimbell's research demonstrates that effective feedback focuses on design thinking processes rather than aesthetic judgements.

**Managing Time**: Project management is frequently challenging for students. Explicit teaching of planning, milestone setting, and time management supports successful completion.

**Ensuring Authenticity**: Centres must maintain evidence that work is students' own. Authentication statements, progress photographs, and teacher observations help verify authenticity for moderators.

### Professional Development Needs

**Subject Knowledge Enhancement**: Rapidly evolving technologies (particularly digital manufacture, smart materials, and sustainable practices) require ongoing CPD. Examination boards provide free CPD programmes.

**Assessment Literacy**: Understanding assessment criteria and moderation standards. Attending board-provided standardisation events significantly improves marking accuracy.

**Networking**: Subject associations including the Design and Technology Association (DATA) provide communities of practice for sharing approaches and resources.

## Current Issues and Future Directions

### Recruitment and Participation

A Level D&T has experienced declining entries nationally, from approximately 18,000 entries in 2013 to around 13,000 in 2023. Research by Matt McLain and others identifies multiple factors including timetable clashes with STEM subjects, resource constraints, and perceptions of difficulty.

**Strategies for Reversing Decline**: Professional bodies advocate for:
- Better articulation of progression routes to creative industries, engineering, and architecture
- Enhanced funding for practical subjects recognising resource costs
- Strengthened design education at Key Stages 3-4 building pipeline to A Level

### Gender Balance

Fashion and Textiles pathways show strong female participation (approximately 85%), whilst Design Engineering skews male (approximately 75%). Product Design shows more balanced gender representation. Stephanie Atkinson's research explores how curriculum design and pedagogy can address these disparities.

### Technological Evolution

Specifications must balance current technologies with rapidly evolving practice:

**Emerging Technologies**: AI-assisted design tools, advanced digital manufacturing, smart/responsive materials, and sustainable innovations challenge traditional curriculum boundaries.

**Digital Competency**: David Barlex argues for enhanced digital literacy, including generative design, parametric modelling, and simulation tools increasingly standard in professional practice.

### Sustainability Imperative

Climate emergency and circular economy principles increasingly central to design practice. Kay Stables advocates for embedding sustainability as core design principle rather than supplementary consideration.

### Interdisciplinary Connections

Stronger connections emerging between D&T, Computer Science (particularly in Design Engineering), Art and Design, and Business Studies. Some centres develop complementary teaching approaches across these subjects.

## Assessment Outcomes and Standards

### Grade Distributions

Historical data (2019, pre-pandemic) showed:
- Approximately 77% achieving A*-C grades across all D&T A Levels
- Product Design specifications showing marginally higher attainment than Fashion and Textiles
- Design Engineering (introduced 2017) showing comparable outcomes to Physics A Level

### Standards Maintenance

Ofqual's regular monitoring ensures:
- Comparable outcomes between examination boards accounting for cohort differences
- Standards maintenance over time despite specification changes
- Alignment between GCSE and A Level progression expectations

### Value-Added Analysis

Research by Kimbell and others demonstrates that D&T A Level students frequently exceed predictions based on prior attainment, suggesting the subject enables diverse learners to excel through its combination of practical and theoretical assessment.
