# Formative Assessment in D&T

## Introduction and Theoretical Foundation

Formative assessment in Design & Technology represents one of the most complex and nuanced aspects of pedagogy within the subject. Unlike many curriculum areas where knowledge can be assessed through written responses, D&T requires assessment of thinking processes, practical capability, design decisions, and the ability to work with materials and tools. As Richard Kimbell has consistently argued throughout his career, assessment in D&T must capture both the 'hand' and the 'mind' – the interplay between thinking and making that characterises authentic design and technological activity.

The Assessment Reform Group (1999) defined formative assessment as "the process of seeking and interpreting evidence for use by learners and their teachers to decide where the learners are in their learning, where they need to go and how best to get there." In D&T, this definition takes on particular significance because learning pathways are rarely linear and students must navigate uncertain, iterative design processes.

Kay Stables' research has demonstrated that formative assessment in D&T serves multiple functions: it supports metacognitive development, helps students understand their own capability, guides design decisions in real-time, and develops the critical judgment necessary for successful designing and making. Her work emphasises that assessment in D&T should be integral to learning rather than bolted on.

## Distinctive Nature of Formative Assessment in D&T

### The Design Process as Assessment Opportunity

David Barlex has articulated how the iterative nature of design work creates unique assessment opportunities. Unlike subjects where progression is hierarchical, D&T progression is often spiral, with students revisiting concepts at increasing levels of sophistication. Formative assessment must therefore:

- Capture learning across cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains simultaneously
- Recognise that 'failure' in designing (such as a prototype that doesn't work) is often evidence of sophisticated thinking and risk-taking
- Value the process as much as the product, understanding that different routes to solutions demonstrate different types of capability
- Accommodate multiple intelligences and learning styles, recognising that some students think through making whilst others design conceptually first

Richard Kimbell's Portfolio Assessment work (Kimbell et al., 1991) established that authentic D&T assessment must capture design thinking as it happens, not merely evaluate finished outcomes. This has profound implications for formative assessment practice.

### Real-Time Assessment During Making

Stephanie Atkinson's research into practical capability has highlighted the importance of observing students during making activities. Teachers must develop what she terms "workshop literacy" – the ability to read the signs of learning through observation of:

- Tool handling and material manipulation
- Problem-solving strategies when things go wrong
- Health and safety awareness demonstrated through actions
- Quality control decisions and self-correction
- Persistence and resilience when facing technical challenges

This real-time assessment requires teachers to circulate effectively, using questioning and observation to understand thinking behind actions.

## Strategies for Effective Formative Assessment in D&T

### Questioning Techniques

Effective questioning in D&T moves beyond simple recall to probe design thinking and decision-making. Matt McLain's work on dialogic teaching in D&T emphasises the importance of questions that:

**Diagnostic questions** that reveal current understanding:
- "Why did you choose that material?"
- "What problem are you trying to solve here?"
- "How does your design meet the user's needs?"

**Reflective questions** that promote metacognition:
- "What would you do differently if you started again?"
- "What was the biggest challenge you faced and how did you overcome it?"
- "How has your understanding changed through making?"

**Predictive questions** that develop forward planning:
- "What might happen if you join these materials this way?"
- "How will you ensure accuracy when cutting?"
- "What could go wrong and how will you prevent it?"

### Feedback Strategies

Dylan Wiliam's principles of effective feedback apply particularly well to D&T, where feedback must be:

**Timely**: Given during the design and making process when students can act upon it, not only at summative points. This might mean intervening as a student is about to make a critical error, or asking them to pause and explain their thinking before proceeding.

**Specific**: Related to particular design decisions, technical skills, or quality issues. Generic praise ("good work") is less effective than specific feedback ("your edge finishing here shows good attention to detail, but this joint needs additional support").

**Actionable**: Providing clear next steps. In D&T this might involve demonstration, provision of additional resources, or guided experimentation.

Research by Kimbell and Stables (2007) demonstrates that effective feedback in D&T often involves:
- Physical demonstration alongside verbal explanation
- Showing examples of quality (both student work and professional products)
- Asking students to identify their own areas for improvement before teacher input
- Using technical vocabulary to build subject-specific literacy

### Assessment for Learning Strategies in D&T Context

The following Assessment for Learning strategies, adapted from Black and Wiliam's work, have particular application in D&T:

**Sharing Learning Intentions and Success Criteria**: At Key Stage 3, this might involve displaying specifications and criteria for design tasks. Students should understand not just what they're making but why, and what good looks like. Kay Stables emphasises the importance of co-constructing success criteria with students, particularly for open-ended design tasks where multiple solutions are possible.

**Self and Peer Assessment**: Particularly valuable during development and evaluation phases. Students might assess each other's design proposals against specifications, test each other's prototypes, or critique iterations. This develops critical evaluation skills required by the National Curriculum. At Key Stage 4, peer assessment helps students internalise the assessment criteria used by examination boards.

**Traffic Lighting and Self-Assessment Tools**: Students might use traffic light systems to indicate their confidence with specific technical skills or understanding of concepts. This helps teachers identify where additional support is needed and allows students to take ownership of their learning.

**Learning Portfolios**: Following Kimbell's portfolio assessment methodology, students maintain ongoing records of their design thinking, decisions, and reflections. These are not merely presentation portfolios but working documents that capture authentic learning processes.

## Key Stage Specific Approaches

### Key Stage 3

The National Curriculum (2013) requires students to "develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world." Formative assessment at KS3 must:

- Build foundational understanding of materials, systems, and processes through regular, low-stakes assessment
- Develop design thinking capability through iterative projects with built-in assessment checkpoints
- Use making activities to assess and develop practical skills progressively
- Encourage experimentation and risk-taking, assessing the thinking behind decisions rather than only outcomes

David Barlex's work on "learning to teach D&T" emphasises that KS3 assessment should focus on developing capability rather than merely acquiring knowledge. Students should understand that designing involves uncertainty and that assessment captures their journey through this uncertainty.

### Key Stage 4

At GCSE level, formative assessment must prepare students for terminal examination while maintaining authentic D&T practice. All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) include substantial Non-Examination Assessment (NEA) components, typically worth 50% of the GCSE.

**AQA Design and Technology (8552)**: The NEA requires students to produce a substantial design and make project. Formative assessment should focus on:
- Developing sophisticated design thinking captured through portfolio work
- Technical skills required for high-quality making
- Analysis and evaluation skills
- Understanding of how assessment objectives translate to marks

**Pearson Edexcel (1DT0)**: Similar structure but with emphasis on iterative design and development. Formative assessment strategies include:
- Regular review points aligned with NEA assessment criteria
- Practice at responding to contexts and briefs
- Development of annotation and communication skills

**OCR Design and Technology (J310)**: Features an Iterative Design Challenge. Teachers must formatively assess:
- Ability to respond to user needs through research
- Selection and use of appropriate materials and techniques
- Quality of design communication
- Evaluation throughout the process

Matt McLain's research on GCSE D&T assessment suggests that effective formative assessment at KS4 involves making assessment criteria transparent and providing exemplar materials that help students understand quality standards.

### Key Stage 5

At A-Level, formative assessment must develop independence and sophisticated design capability. Specifications from AQQ, Edexcel, OCR and WJEC all require substantial design and make projects worth 50% or more of the qualification.

Students must develop:
- Sustained investigation and research skills
- Advanced technical capability with materials and processes
- Critical analysis and evaluation throughout designing and making
- Professional standards of communication and presentation

Stephanie Atkinson's work on developing expertise suggests that A-Level formative assessment should increasingly involve students assessing their own work against professional standards, moving beyond school-based criteria to understand industry expectations.

## Practical Implementation for Teachers

### Creating an Assessment-Rich Environment

The D&T workshop or classroom should visually communicate standards and expectations:

- **Display boards** showing exemplar work (annotated to explain quality features)
- **Success criteria** visible for current projects
- **Technical vocabulary** walls that build subject-specific literacy
- **Process documentation** showing the messy reality of designing, not just polished outcomes
- **Student voice** capturing reflections on learning and design decisions

### Effective Use of Planning Time

David Barlex emphasises that effective formative assessment must be planned, not incidental. Teachers should:

- **Identify key assessment opportunities** within projects (e.g., initial research phase, concept development, prototype testing)
- **Prepare focused observation schedules** to ensure all students receive attention during practical work
- **Design checkpoint activities** where students must demonstrate understanding before progressing
- **Build in reflection time** where students step back from making to evaluate progress
- **Use starter and plenary activities** to assess understanding of technical knowledge

### Managing Assessment During Practical Work

The challenge of assessing 30 students engaged in diverse practical activities requires systematic approaches:

**Focused observation**: Rather than trying to see everything, focus on specific students or specific skills each lesson. Keep simple records of observations.

**Structured interventions**: Create deliberate assessment moments, such as asking students to show you three things they're proud of and one thing they're going to improve.

**Student documentation**: Require students to photograph work in progress and annotate images, explaining decisions. This creates assessment evidence while developing metacognition.

**Mini-plenaries**: Stop practical work periodically for mini-plenaries where students share problems, solutions, or techniques. This provides assessment insight while building classroom community.

### Record Keeping and Documentation

Kay Stables' research suggests that effective record keeping in D&T should:

- Capture both process and product assessment
- Record technical skills development over time
- Note design thinking capability and decision-making quality
- Identify misconceptions or areas requiring intervention
- Inform planning and differentiation

Digital tools (photographs, video, online portfolios) can make this more manageable than traditional written records alone.

## Assessment of Design Thinking

### Capturing Thinking Through Graphic Communication

Richard Kimbell's research has consistently shown that students' graphic communication (sketches, diagrams, annotations) provides windows into their thinking. Formative assessment should examine:

- **Idea generation**: Quantity and diversity of initial concepts
- **Development**: Evidence of iterative refinement and problem-solving
- **Communication quality**: Appropriate use of drawing types (isometric, orthographic, exploded diagrams)
- **Annotation**: Explanation of decisions, materials, processes, and user needs
- **Critical evaluation**: Honest assessment of strengths and weaknesses

Teachers should model high-quality design communication and provide regular feedback on students' graphic literacy.

### Modelling and Testing as Assessment Evidence

Physical and digital modelling provides rich formative assessment opportunities. When students create prototypes, mock-ups, or test pieces, teachers can assess:

- **Planning ability**: Whether students think ahead about what needs testing
- **Technical skill**: Appropriate selection and use of modelling materials and processes
- **Analytical thinking**: How students evaluate what their models tell them
- **Iterative development**: Whether students use testing to inform design refinement

Kay Stables emphasises that modelling activities should be structured to maximise learning, with clear assessment of what students discover through making.

## Assessment of Technical Knowledge and Skills

### Diagnostic Assessment of Prior Learning

At the start of new topics or projects, teachers should diagnose existing capability:

- **Practical skills audits**: Students self-assess confidence with specific tools and processes
- **Knowledge checks**: Quick quizzes on material properties, systems, or technical terminology
- **Practical tasks**: Simple making activities that reveal current skill levels
- **Discussion**: Questioning to uncover understanding and misconceptions

This diagnostic information should inform differentiation and support planning.

### Formative Assessment of Skill Development

The development of practical skills requires particular assessment approaches:

**Direct observation** during practical work, noting:
- Safe working practices
- Appropriate tool selection and use
- Accuracy and quality control
- Independence and problem-solving

**Practical tests**: Focused activities assessing specific skills (e.g., "produce three different edge finishes to show quality")

**Test pieces**: Requiring students to practice techniques before applying them to projects, allowing assessment and improvement before high-stakes making

**Student self-assessment**: Using technical criteria or video analysis to evaluate their own skill development

Stephanie Atkinson's work emphasises that skill assessment must be ongoing and developmental, recognising that expertise develops over time through deliberate practice.

## Digital Technologies and Formative Assessment

The integration of digital technologies in D&T creates new assessment challenges and opportunities:

### CAD/CAM Assessment

Students working with Computer-Aided Design and Computer-Aided Manufacturing require assessment of:
- Digital design capability (software skills, 3D thinking, parametric modelling)
- Understanding of manufacturing constraints and possibilities
- Ability to optimise designs for digital manufacture
- Integration of digital and traditional processes

Matt McLain's research on digital making emphasises that assessment should focus on design thinking enabled by digital tools, not merely software proficiency.

### Electronics and Programming

When students work with electronics and control systems:
- Assess understanding of systems thinking and input-process-output models
- Evaluate programming logic and problem-solving
- Examine testing and debugging strategies
- Consider integration of electronic/mechanical elements

### Digital Portfolio Work

Many schools now use digital portfolios (Google Classroom, OneNote, Microsoft Teams, or specialist D&T platforms). These enable:
- Easy capturing of work in progress through photography
- Quick feedback cycles through digital annotation
- Sharing of exemplar work and success criteria
- Student reflection through video or audio recording
- Tracking of iterative development over time

## Challenges and Solutions in Formative Assessment

### Challenge: Assessment Workload

The intensive nature of D&T teaching, with high contact time during practical work, makes comprehensive formative assessment demanding.

**Solutions**:
- Focus assessment efforts strategically on key learning moments
- Use peer and self-assessment to develop student metacognition while managing workload
- Employ digital tools efficiently (voice recording of feedback, photographic evidence)
- Build assessment into activities rather than adding it on (e.g., students photograph and annotate work as standard practice)

### Challenge: Assessing Diverse Project Outcomes

When students produce different products, comparing achievement is complex.

**Solutions**:
- Establish clear assessment criteria applicable across different contexts (as exam boards do)
- Focus on design capability and process quality rather than only product success
- Use exemplars showing quality across different project types
- Assess against learning intentions specific to each project phase

### Challenge: Balancing Process and Product

Overemphasis on portfolio documentation can detract from practical work, while focus only on finished products misses learning journey.

**Solutions**:
- Integrate documentation naturally into workflow (photograph as you go, quick annotations)
- Value different forms of evidence (verbal explanations, physical models, digital records)
- Assess specific stages with criteria appropriate to that phase
- Follow Richard Kimbell's principle of assessing authentic activity, not artificial documentation

### Challenge: Subjectivity in Practical Work Assessment

Practical capability assessment can appear subjective, particularly with creative or aesthetic dimensions.

**Solutions**:
- Develop clear technical criteria for practical skills (accuracy tolerances, finish quality, joint strength)
- Use exemplar materials to calibrate judgment
- Moderate assessments with colleagues, particularly at KS4 and KS5
- Involve students in defining quality criteria
- Separate technical skill assessment from creative or aesthetic judgments where appropriate

## Research Evidence and Impact

Kay Stables and Richard Kimbell's longitudinal research demonstrates that when formative assessment is integral to D&T teaching:

- Students develop stronger metacognitive awareness of their own designing and making capability
- Practical skills develop more rapidly when students receive timely, specific feedback
- Design thinking becomes more sophisticated as students learn to evaluate their own work critically
- Engagement and motivation increase when students understand expectations and see their progress

The Technology Enhanced Learning in STEM subjects project found that formative assessment strategies in D&T had greatest impact when:
- Learning intentions were explicit and co-constructed with students
- Feedback focused on design decisions and technical choices, not merely outcomes
- Students regularly engaged in self and peer assessment using clear criteria
- Teachers structured deliberate opportunities for formative assessment within project timelines
