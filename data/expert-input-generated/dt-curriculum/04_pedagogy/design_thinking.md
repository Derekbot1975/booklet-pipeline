# Design Thinking Approaches

## Introduction to Design Thinking in D&T Education

Design thinking represents a distinctive pedagogical approach within Design & Technology that emphasises iterative, user-centred problem-solving. In UK D&T education, design thinking has evolved from the subject's inception, shaped significantly by the work of Richard Kimbell, whose seminal research "Assessing Technology" (1991) established thinking through design as central to technological capability.

Kay Stables defines design thinking as "the cognitive processes employed when designing" and argues that it encompasses "the interplay between imaging and modelling" (Stables & Kimbell, 2000). This conceptualisation moves beyond linear problem-solving models towards understanding design as an iterative dialogue between mind and hand, thought and representation.

The National Curriculum for Design & Technology in England (2013) embeds design thinking throughout, requiring pupils to develop "the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world." This positions design thinking not as an isolated methodology but as fundamental to technological literacy.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Kimbell's Interaction Model

Richard Kimbell's interaction model (1991, 2000) remains foundational to UK D&T pedagogy. Kimbell proposed that designing involves constant interaction between two domains:

- **The domain of the mind (thought)**: including imaging, speculating, exploring ideas
- **The domain of the hand (action)**: including modelling, making, confronting reality

This model challenged earlier linear design process models (e.g., identify problem → research → generate ideas → make → evaluate) by recognising that designers move fluidly between thinking and doing. Kimbell's research demonstrated that effective designers maintain "task appraisal" throughout, constantly reviewing and refining their approach.

### Stables' Imaging and Modelling Framework

Kay Stables extended Kimbell's work by examining how pupils develop ideas through different forms of representation. Her research (Stables, 1992; Kimbell & Stables, 2007) identified that design thinking involves:

- **Imaging**: internal mental representations that may be visual, verbal, or kinaesthetic
- **Modelling**: external representations including sketches, prototypes, diagrams, and digital models

Stables emphasised that modelling is not simply about communicating finished ideas but is itself a thinking tool. Sketching, making mock-ups, and CAD modelling all enable designers to explore possibilities that cannot be fully imagined mentally.

### Cognitive Apprenticeship and Situated Learning

David Barlex has consistently advocated for making design thinking visible through pedagogical approaches informed by cognitive apprenticeship theory (Collins, Brown & Newman, 1989). Barlex argues that effective D&T teaching requires:

- **Modelling**: teachers demonstrating design thinking processes explicitly
- **Coaching**: providing scaffolded support as pupils develop capability
- **Articulation**: requiring pupils to explain their design thinking
- **Reflection**: encouraging metacognitive awareness of design strategies

Barlex's Nuffield Design & Technology resources exemplify this approach, providing structured activities that expose and develop design thinking capabilities.

## Design Thinking Frameworks in UK D&T

### The Iterative Design Process

While the National Curriculum deliberately avoids prescribing a specific design process, most UK examination specifications reference iterative designing. The key distinction from linear models is the acceptance—indeed expectation—of循環 movement between stages:

**AQA GCSE Design & Technology (2017)** references: "designing and making principles" including iterative design processes where "initial ideas are developed through investigation, modelling, modification and refinement."

**Pearson Edexcel GCSE (9-1) Design and Technology (2017)** requires understanding of "iterative design processes, including the work of others."

**OCR GCSE (9-1) Design and Technology (J310)** specifies that learners should "understand how an iterative design process can lead to innovative, well designed products."

### Human-Centred Design

Contemporary UK D&T increasingly emphasises human-centred or user-centred design thinking. This approach, influenced by design consultancies like IDEO but adapted for educational contexts by practitioners including Matt McLain and Stephanie Atkinson, prioritises:

1. **Empathy**: understanding users through observation, interview, and immersion
2. **Definition**: framing the design problem from user perspectives
3. **Ideation**: generating multiple potential solutions
4. **Prototyping**: creating low-fidelity models for testing
5. **Testing**: gathering user feedback and iterating

Stephanie Atkinson's research (2005) on user-centred design in schools demonstrated that when pupils engage meaningfully with real users, the quality and appropriateness of design outcomes improves significantly. However, she cautioned that superficial engagement (e.g., single questionnaires) provides limited benefit—sustained user involvement is necessary.

### Systems Thinking

Systems thinking represents an increasingly important dimension of design thinking in D&T. Matt McLain's work on sustainable design (McLain et al., 2020) argues that pupils must develop capacity to understand products within broader ecological, social, and economic systems.

Systems thinking in D&T involves:

- Understanding interconnections and feedback loops
- Recognising unintended consequences
- Considering life-cycle impacts
- Appreciating stakeholder perspectives
- Identifying leverage points for intervention

This approach aligns with the National Curriculum's requirement that pupils understand "how more advanced materials and systems can be used" and consider "environmental and sustainability issues."

## Key Stage Progression in Design Thinking

### Key Stage 1 and 2

At primary level, design thinking emerges through structured yet playful exploration. The National Curriculum requires pupils to:

**KS1**: "design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users based on design criteria"

**KS2**: "use research and develop design criteria to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products that are fit for purpose"

Effective primary practice, as documented by DATA (now Design and Technology Association), involves:

- **Focused practical tasks**: developing specific skills and knowledge
- **Design and make assignments**: applying skills in purposeful contexts
- **Evaluating existing products**: analysing how things work and how they meet needs

Research by Stephanie Atkinson and colleagues (2004) demonstrated that even young children can engage in sophisticated design thinking when appropriately scaffolded. However, they require explicit teaching of evaluation criteria and metacognitive prompts to reflect on their design decisions.

### Key Stage 3

KS3 represents critical development in design thinking capability. The National Curriculum requires that pupils:

- "identify and solve their own design problems"
- "develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products"
- "use a variety of approaches [to generate ideas], for example biomimicry and user-centred design"

David Barlex's "Nuffield Design & Technology" project provides exemplary KS3 resources that develop design thinking through:

- **Resource tasks**: building knowledge of materials, processes, and existing solutions
- **Capability tasks**: applying knowledge in increasingly complex design challenges
- **Case studies**: analysing professional design practice

Research by Kimbell and Perry (2001) on technology education at KS3 revealed that effective design thinking development requires sustained projects (6-8 weeks minimum) rather than fragmented short tasks. Pupils need time to iterate, reflect, and refine their thinking.

### Key Stage 4 and 5

GCSE and A Level specifications formalise design thinking requirements whilst allowing diverse approaches:

**GCSE Level** (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas):

All specifications require coursework/NEA components where pupils demonstrate iterative design thinking. Common requirements include:

- Contextual research and analysis of user needs
- Development of design specifications/criteria
- Generation of multiple design ideas using different techniques
- Development and refinement of chosen solution(s)
- Prototyping and testing with stakeholders
- Critical evaluation throughout

**A Level** (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC):

Advanced level work demands more sophisticated design thinking:

- Independent identification of authentic design opportunities
- Systematic research including primary user research
- Critical analysis of wider contextual factors (commercial, cultural, environmental)
- Advanced development work showing refined iteration
- Justification of design decisions with explicit reasoning

Kay Stables' research on A Level project work (2008) emphasised that students struggle most with the transition from generating initial ideas to systematic development. Many students repeat ideas rather than genuinely refining them through prototyping and testing. Effective teaching makes this development process explicit through worked examples and structured scaffolding.

## Pedagogical Approaches for Teaching Design Thinking

### Making Thinking Visible

Richard Kimbell's principle that "capability is thought in action" necessitates pedagogical strategies that externalise and make visible the normally invisible cognitive processes of designing. Practical approaches include:

**Think-aloud protocols**: Teachers demonstrating design thinking by verbalising their thought processes whilst sketching, modelling, or problem-solving. Research by McLain (2018) showed that teachers who regularly modelled design thinking had pupils who demonstrated more sophisticated design strategies.

**Design journals**: Structured reflection tools where pupils document not just what they did but why they made particular decisions and what they learned. Kay Stables advocates for design journals that combine visual and written reflection.

**Peer critique sessions**: Structured protocols where pupils present work-in-progress and receive feedback. David Barlex recommends "design reviews" modelled on professional practice, where pupils present to peers using specific questions to guide feedback.

**Metacognitive prompts**: Questions that develop pupils' awareness of their own thinking: "What strategies are you using?", "What would you do differently next time?", "How do you know this solution addresses the problem?"

### Structured Ideation Techniques

While creativity is central to design thinking, research demonstrates that creativity flourishes within structure rather than unstructured "free thinking". Effective techniques documented in UK D&T literature include:

**SCAMPER** (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse): A systematic framework for generating variations. Particularly effective at KS3 when pupils struggle to move beyond initial ideas.

**Morphological analysis**: Breaking products into component attributes and systematically exploring combinations. David Barlex's resources include excellent examples applying this to various product categories.

**Biomimicry**: Using nature as inspiration for design solutions. Particularly powerful for sustainable design challenges. The National Curriculum explicitly mentions this approach.

**Co-design workshops**: Collaborative ideation with users. Stephanie Atkinson's research demonstrated enhanced outcomes when pupils conducted structured co-design sessions with genuine end-users rather than designing "for" imagined users.

### Prototyping as Thinking

Contemporary understanding of design thinking emphasises that prototyping is not about testing finished ideas but is itself an ideation tool. Matt McLain advocates for "rapid iterative prototyping" approaches where:

- Pupils create multiple low-fidelity prototypes quickly (card, foam, digital)
- Each prototype tests specific aspects (ergonomics, mechanism, aesthetics)
- Prototypes are created to answer questions, not to impress
- Failing quickly and cheaply is valued

This approach contrasts with traditional D&T pedagogy where pupils often created single, highly finished models that represented solutions rather than explored possibilities.

## Assessment of Design Thinking

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Assessing design thinking requires approaches that capture process as well as outcome. Richard Kimbell's APU (Assessment of Performance Unit) research established that effective assessment in D&T must evaluate pupils' capability to:

- Clarify tasks through investigation
- Generate and develop ideas
- Plan and make products
- Evaluate processes and outcomes

Practical formative assessment strategies include:

**Design portfolios**: Collected evidence of thinking development including sketches, photographs of models, test results, and reflective annotations. All GCSE specifications require portfolio evidence in NEA components.

**Interim reviews**: Formal assessment points during extended projects where pupils present work-in-progress. David Barlex recommends minimum three review points: initial research/specification, design development, and final evaluation.

**Success criteria co-construction**: Involving pupils in defining what successful design thinking looks like for particular challenges. Kay Stables' research demonstrated that this develops metacognitive awareness and self-regulation.

### Summative Assessment in Examinations

GCSE and A Level specifications assess design thinking through both examined and non-examined components:

**Written examinations** typically assess:
- Knowledge of iterative design processes
- Analysis of others' design decisions
- Application of design thinking to novel scenarios

**NEA/Coursework** assesses:
- Application of design thinking to sustained projects
- Quality of iterative development
- Justification of design decisions
- User-centred research and testing

**AQA GCSE D&T**: NEA worth 50%, assessed against criteria including "Design – Investigation", "Design – Design & Development", with specific marks for showing "development through modelling and reviewing."

**Edexcel GCSE D&T**: Similar weighting with assessment objectives requiring candidates to "investigate, analyse and evaluate design decisions and wider issues in design and technology."

**OCR GCSE D&T**: Iterative Design Challenge (NEA) worth 50%, with specific requirements for "iterative designing" and "testing and evaluation with the client or third party."

### Assessment Challenges and Considerations

Research by Kimbell and colleagues (2009) through the E-scape project explored technology-enhanced assessment of design thinking. Key findings included:

- Digital portfolios enabled richer capture of iterative processes
- Assessment focused on "design behaviour" (how pupils designed) rather than just outcomes
- Holistic assessment by trained assessors proved more valid than atomised mark schemes
- However, digital assessment introduced access and equity challenges

Matt McLain (2019) has cautioned that current GCSE assessment structures may inadvertently discourage authentic design thinking by:

- Rewarding comprehensive documentation over quality of thinking
- Limiting genuine iteration through time constraints
- Assessing against predetermined criteria that may not suit all design contexts
- Separating "designing" and "making" in ways that contradict Kimbell's interaction model

## Contemporary Issues and Developments

### Design Thinking and Digital Technologies

The integration of CAD/CAM technologies has profound implications for design thinking in D&T. As David Barlex (2007) observed, digital technologies can:

**Enhance design thinking** by:
- Enabling rapid iteration through virtual prototyping
- Facilitating complex calculations and simulations
- Providing access to extensive design databases and resources
- Supporting collaborative design across distances

**Constrain design thinking** by:
- Software-imposed limitations on what can be modelled
- Reduced tactile feedback compared to physical making
- Technical difficulties that interrupt design flow
- Template-based thinking rather than original conceptualisation

Effective pedagogy requires explicit teaching of digital design thinking strategies, not just software skills. This includes understanding when CAD is appropriate versus when physical modelling serves thinking better.

### Design Thinking for Sustainability

Contemporary D&T increasingly emphasises sustainable design thinking. Research by McLain and colleagues (2020) argues that traditional design thinking approaches can inadvertently promote unsustainable practices by:

- Focusing on product replacement rather than repair or longevity
- Emphasising novelty and innovation over appropriateness
- Treating environmental considerations as "add-ons" rather than fundamental

Alternative approaches include:

**Circular economy thinking**: Designing for disassembly, remanufacturing, and material recovery from inception.

**Sufficiency-based design**: Questioning whether new products are needed or whether alternative solutions (services, behaviour change, repair) might be more appropriate.

**Life-cycle thinking**: Systematically considering environmental, social, and economic impacts across entire product lifespans.

The Design and Technology Association has developed resources supporting sustainable design thinking appropriate for different key stages, emphasising systems perspectives and critical evaluation of consumption patterns.

### Inclusive Design Thinking

Stephanie Atkinson's work (2006) on inclusive design emphasises that genuine user-centred design thinking must actively engage diverse users, including those with disabilities, different cultural backgrounds, and varied ages. This requires:

- Moving beyond "mainstream" assumptions about users
- Involving diverse stakeholders throughout design processes
- Understanding that designing for specific needs often produces solutions beneficial for all
- Recognising cultural dimensions of design appropriateness

GCSE specifications increasingly reference inclusive design, with examination questions requiring analysis of how products meet diverse user needs.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Developing Teacher Design Thinking Capability

Research consistently demonstrates that teachers' own design thinking capabilities directly influence teaching effectiveness. The Design and Technology Association recommends that teachers:

- Engage regularly in personal design projects to maintain and develop capability
- Participate in professional networks that share design thinking pedagogy
- Access CPD focused on contemporary design practice, not just curriculum content
- Analyse professional design case studies to understand authentic design thinking

David Barlex has repeatedly emphasised that D&T teachers must be "informed commentators" on design practice, able to articulate design thinking processes explicitly and situate pupils' work within broader design contexts.

### Structuring Project Work

Effective project work that develops design thinking requires careful structure:

**Initial phase (investigation and specification)**:
- Authentic design contexts that engage pupils' interests
- Structured research including user investigation, not just internet searches

- Clear constraints that focus rather than limit thinking
