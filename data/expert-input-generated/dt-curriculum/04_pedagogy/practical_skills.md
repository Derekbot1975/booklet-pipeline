# Teaching Practical Skills Safely

## Introduction

Teaching practical skills safely is fundamental to effective Design & Technology education in the UK. As Richard Kimbell has long argued, the subject's unique contribution to the curriculum lies in its integration of 'hand and mind' – the cognitive and practical working together. However, this practical dimension brings inherent risks that must be managed systematically. David Barlex emphasises that safety education is not merely about avoiding accidents; it represents a pedagogical opportunity to develop students' capability to assess and manage risk independently – a key life skill.

The Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 places clear legal responsibilities on schools and teachers, whilst the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 require specific risk assessments for all activities. The DATA (Design and Technology Association) publication 'Safety in Practice' provides the most authoritative guidance for D&T departments in England, Wales and Northern Ireland.

## Legislative and Regulatory Framework

### Core Legal Requirements

Teachers must understand their responsibilities under several key pieces of legislation:

- **Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974**: Places duties on employers (schools/local authorities) and employees (teachers) to ensure safety
- **Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999**: Requires risk assessment and appropriate training
- **Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) Regulations 2002**: Mandates assessment and control of hazardous materials
- **Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations (PUWER) 1998**: Sets standards for equipment maintenance and use
- **Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992**: Requires provision of appropriate PPE where risks cannot be eliminated

### Professional Guidance

The Design and Technology Association (DATA), now transformed into the D&T Association, publishes the definitive 'Safety in Practice' guide, regularly updated to reflect current regulations and best practice. This document should be the cornerstone of every department's safety policy.

CLEAPSS (Consortium of Local Education Authorities for the Provision of Science Services) provides extensive guidance on materials, equipment and processes relevant to D&T, including student safety sheets and risk assessments.

## Risk Assessment and Management

### The Risk Assessment Process

Matt McLain's research on practical capability emphasises that risk assessment must be both a teacher responsibility and a learning opportunity for students. A systematic approach includes:

1. **Hazard identification**: Recognising potential sources of harm (machinery, tools, materials, processes)
2. **Risk evaluation**: Assessing likelihood and severity of potential harm
3. **Control measures**: Implementing hierarchy of controls (elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE)
4. **Recording and review**: Documenting assessments and reviewing after incidents or changes

### Dynamic Risk Assessment

Kay Stables' work on designerly thinking highlights that D&T activities are inherently fluid and iterative. This requires teachers to employ 'dynamic risk assessment' – ongoing evaluation as activities unfold and students pursue individual design solutions. Teachers must develop the professional judgement to:

- Recognise when standard risk assessments no longer apply
- Intervene appropriately when students' designs introduce new hazards
- Balance safety with the pedagogical need for student autonomy and creativity

### Student Involvement in Risk Assessment

Stephanie Atkinson's research on capability development demonstrates that students should progressively take responsibility for their own safety. This progression might look like:

**Key Stage 3**: Students identify obvious hazards with teacher guidance, follow clear safety instructions, and understand basic PPE requirements

**Key Stage 4**: Students conduct simple risk assessments for their projects, justify their choice of materials and processes considering safety, and demonstrate independence in safe working practices

**Key Stage 5**: Students produce detailed risk assessments for complex projects, research safety requirements for unfamiliar processes, and critically evaluate safer alternative methods

## Teaching Strategies for Safety Education

### Embedding Safety in Practical Pedagogy

Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research demonstrated that practical capability develops through authentic, purposeful activity. Safety teaching must therefore be contextualised within meaningful making activities rather than isolated 'safety lessons'.

**Effective approaches include**:

- **Demonstration and modelling**: Teachers explicitly model safe techniques whilst thinking aloud about risk management decisions
- **'Just-in-time' instruction**: Teaching specific safety procedures immediately before students need them, maintaining relevance and retention
- **Peer teaching**: Experienced students demonstrating safe techniques to peers, reinforcing their own understanding
- **Safety audits**: Students conducting workshop audits, developing critical observation skills
- **Incident analysis**: Discussing (anonymised) accidents and near-misses to develop hazard awareness

### Progressive Skill Development

David Barlex's work on curriculum progression emphasises the importance of building technical skills systematically. For safety, this means:

**Lower Key Stage 3** (Years 7-8):
- Safe use of hand tools (saws, chisels, files, hand drills)
- Basic workshop behaviour and discipline
- Understanding of PPE (safety glasses, aprons)
- Supervised use of simple machines (pillar drill with appropriate guards)

**Upper Key Stage 3** (Year 9):
- Introduction to basic machinery with multiple safety features (sanding disc, scroll saw)
- Safe handling of resistant materials and basic heat processes
- Independent tool selection and safe storage
- Understanding of workshop zoning and traffic flow

**Key Stage 4** (Years 10-11):
- Extended machine repertoire including controlled access equipment (band saw, CAM equipment, heat processes)
- Material-specific safety knowledge (composite materials, finishing products)
- Certification schemes where appropriate (British Wood Machining Federation 'Safe Use of Woodworking Machinery')
- Complex process planning incorporating safety considerations

**Key Stage 5** (Years 12-13):
- Advanced manufacturing techniques (casting, welding, CNC machinery where available)
- Independent research into material and process safety
- Responsibility for maintaining safe working environments
- Mentoring younger students in safe practices

## Material-Specific Safety Considerations

### Resistant Materials

Wood, metal, and plastics each present distinct hazards requiring specific pedagogical approaches:

**Timber**: Dust extraction becomes critical, particularly with certain hardwoods and manufactured boards containing formaldehyde. Teachers must ensure machinery has appropriate extraction fitted and regularly maintained. The relationship between cutting speed, feed rate and safety must be explicitly taught.

**Metals**: Introduction should progress from soft metals (aluminium) to harder alloys, with explicit teaching about appropriate tool selection, cutting speeds and lubricants. Hot metalwork requires specific training, controlled access, and understanding of heat treatment processes.

**Plastics**: The DATA guidance distinguishes between thermoplastics and thermosetting plastics, each requiring different approaches to heating, cutting and finishing. Acrylic forming requires careful temperature control; students must understand crack propagation and appropriate annealing techniques.

### Textiles and Resistant Materials Integration

Modern D&T increasingly involves smart materials and electronics integration. This requires teachers to address:

- Safe use of sewing machines and overlockers (finger guard awareness, appropriate speed control)
- Fabric cutting equipment (rotary cutters, die-cutting machines)
- Heat application (irons, heat presses, dyeing processes)
- Electronic component handling (soldering safety, battery management, laser cutting of fabric-reinforced materials)

### Food Materials

Whilst often separated organisationally, food technology requires equally rigorous safety management:

- Cross-contamination prevention
- Temperature control (danger zone awareness)
- Allergen management (critical in UK following Natasha's Law - requiring full ingredient labelling)
- Equipment safety (mandolins, food processors, heat sources)
- Personal hygiene protocols

## Workshop Organisation and Management

### Physical Environment

Effective workshop design supports safe practice. Key considerations include:

**Layout and zoning**: Distinct areas for different activities (machine area, hand tool areas, finishing areas, design areas) with clear sight lines allowing teacher supervision. The British Standard BS 4163:2014 'Health and safety for design and technology in schools and similar establishments' provides specific guidance.

**Storage systems**: Tools and materials must be stored systematically, enabling students to develop independent capability whilst ensuring sharps, hazardous materials and specialist equipment are appropriately secured.

**Maintenance regimes**: Regular inspection schedules documented in department safety logs, with clear protocols for removing faulty equipment from service.

**Environmental controls**: Appropriate lighting (particularly for detailed work and machine operations), ventilation/extraction, and temperature control.

### Class Organisation and Supervision

Kay Stables' research on D&T pedagogy highlights the tension between individual student autonomy and necessary teacher control. Effective strategies include:

**Induction procedures**: Every Key Stage 3 cohort should complete comprehensive workshop induction before independent work begins. This should be documented (students sign to confirm understanding) and revisited regularly.

**Differentiated access systems**: Tiered certification for equipment use (e.g., 'machine licence' systems where students demonstrate competence before independent access). This approach, advocated by Matt McLain, develops student capability whilst maintaining safety.

**Maximum ratios**: Whilst no statutory maximum class size exists for D&T, the D&T Association recommends maximum ratios of 20:1 for practical work, reduced to 16:1 when introducing new techniques or working with higher-risk equipment.

**Strategic positioning**: Teachers must position themselves to maintain visual oversight whilst supporting individual students. This 'lighthouse' approach involves regular scanning even during focused individual interactions.

## Specific Processes and Equipment

### Machinery Teaching

The introduction of machinery represents a critical pedagogical moment. Richard Kimbell's concept of 'informed opportunism' suggests students need sufficient technical capability to realise their design intentions, but machinery teaching must be carefully sequenced:

**Demonstration protocols**:
1. Show the complete process at normal speed (building mental model)
2. Repeat slowly with explicit commentary on safety features
3. Identify all safety mechanisms and emergency stops
4. Demonstrate common errors and recovery strategies
5. Student verbal rehearsal before physical practice
6. Supervised first attempts with immediate feedback

**Machine-specific considerations**:

- **Pillar drill**: Secure workpiece (never hand-held), appropriate speed for material, awareness of drilling breakthrough, chuck key removal
- **Belt sander/disc sander**: Appropriate side of disc, workpiece support, dust extraction, avoiding loose clothing/jewellery
- **Scroll saw**: Blade selection and fitting, feed rate control, blade breakage response
- **Band saw**: Crown guard adjustment, appropriate fence use, blade tracking, avoiding backing out of cuts
- **CNC equipment**: Software simulation before cutting, material securing, emergency stop familiarisation, supervision during operation

### Heat Processes

David Barlex emphasises that heat processes (soldering, plastic forming, metal brazing, enamelling) offer valuable learning opportunities but require stringent controls:

- Designated heat-work areas with appropriate heat-resistant surfaces
- Mandatory PPE (safety glasses, heat-resistant gloves where appropriate)
- Clear protocols for hot object management and cooling
- Extraction where fumes are produced
- Fire safety equipment accessible and regularly maintained
- Student understanding of appropriate extinguisher types

### CAD/CAM and Digital Technologies

The increasing prominence of laser cutters, 3D printers and CNC routers introduces contemporary safety challenges:

**Laser cutters**: Material restrictions (no PVC due to chlorine gas), extraction requirements, interlocks, appropriate supervision ratios, focused beam awareness

**3D printers**: Ventilation for FDM printers (ultra-fine particle awareness), temperature hazards, appropriate material selection, understanding of support material removal

**CNC routers**: Substantial guard systems, acoustic protection, programmed simulation before cutting, controlled access protocols

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

### The Hierarchy of Control

PPE represents the final line of defence in risk management. Teachers must emphasise that PPE is required only after hazards cannot be eliminated, substituted, or controlled through engineering or administrative means. However, in D&T workshops, certain PPE is standard:

**Safety glasses/goggles**: Mandatory for all machine use, recommended for hand tool work in resistant materials. Students must understand different types (standard safety glasses, face shields for turning, full goggles for chemical resistance).

**Aprons**: Protection from sharp edges, splinters, and contamination. Different types appropriate for different materials (leather for metalwork, cotton for general work, disposable for messy processes).

**Hearing protection**: Required when noise levels exceed 85dB (typical for routing operations, extended machine use). Stephanie Atkinson's work on capability highlights this as an opportunity to discuss long-term health implications.

**Dust masks/respirators**: Appropriate ratings for different hazards (FFP2/FFP3 for fine dust, specific cartridges for solvents and spray finishing).

### PPE Management

Practical implications for departments:

- Adequate supplies in multiple sizes (particular attention to fit for diverse student physiology)
- Regular inspection and replacement protocols
- Storage systems ensuring cleanliness and accessibility
- Student responsibility for personal PPE (where feasible)
- Accommodation for students wearing religious head coverings (securing loose fabric, appropriate safety glasses over hijab)

## Inclusion and Differentiation in Safety Teaching

### SEND Considerations

Students with special educational needs and disabilities have equal entitlement to D&T education. Reasonable adjustments might include:

- Modified tools (ergonomic handles, adapted grips, lighter alternatives)
- Additional supervision or peer support
- Simplified or visual safety instructions
- Alternative techniques achieving similar learning outcomes
- Extended induction and practice time

Matt McLain's research on practical capability emphasises that lower academic attainment doesn't correlate with reduced practical capability, but processing speed or working memory limitations may require additional safety scaffolding.

### Cultural and Language Considerations

In diverse UK schools, teachers must ensure safety information is genuinely understood:

- Visual safety signage supplementing written instructions
- Demonstration-based teaching reducing language barriers
- Translation of key safety documents where appropriate
- Awareness that previous educational experiences may not have included practical workshop work
- Explicit teaching of UK safety culture and expectations

## Assessment of Safe Working

### Formative Assessment

Kay Stables' research on designerly behaviour demonstrates that assessment in D&T should support learning. For safety, formative assessment includes:

- Observation of working practices during practical sessions
- Verbal questioning ("Why have you chosen safety glasses for this process?")
- Risk assessment documentation reviewed and improved iteratively
- Photographic evidence of safe workshop practice
- Peer assessment of safety practices (developing critical observation)

### Summative Assessment

All GCSE and A-Level specifications include assessment of safe working, though approaches vary:

**AQA**: Non-exam assessment (NEA) marking criteria explicitly assess "safe and accurate use of tools and equipment" within the 'Making' section. Students can access higher mark bands only by demonstrating safe practices consistently.

**OCR**: The iterative design challenge requires students to "demonstrate safe working practices throughout the making process" with photographic evidence and annotation.

**Edexcel**: Safety features prominently in the practical assessment criteria, with specific marks allocated for risk assessment quality and safe working demonstration.

**WJEC**: The 'Making' element includes explicit assessment of "working safely with minimal risk to self and others" with progression descriptors.

### Recording and Evidencing Safety

Practical implications include:

- Photographic evidence protocols (students photographing safe working setups, appropriate PPE use, completed risk assessments)
- Teacher observation records (dated, specific, focused on safety practices)
- Student logbooks/design journals including safety reflections
- Completion certificates for specific machine training
- Department safety records (induction completion, incident reports, safety communications)

## Incident Management and Learning

### Response Protocols

Despite best practice, incidents occur. Effective management requires:

**Immediate response**: First aid provision (trained staff, accessible equipment), incident scene management, ensuring remaining students are safe and supervised.

**Recording**: Accident books (legal requirement), internal incident reporting systems, near-miss recording (proactive safety culture).

**Investigation**: Root cause analysis (not blame allocation), involvement of department leadership, review of risk assessments.

**Learning**: Staff briefings, curriculum adjustments, resource or procedural changes, student cohort communication where appropriate.

### Fostering a Positive Safety Culture

David Barlex argues that effective D&T education develops responsible, capable individuals. A positive safety culture means:

- Safety presented as enabling creativity (not restricting it)
- Student voice in safety improvements (suggestions schemes, safety representatives)
- Celebrating safe working (positive recognition not just incident response)
- Teacher modelling (consistent adherence to safety protocols)
- Open discussion of errors and learning (psychological safety)

Richard Kimbell's work on authentic assessment suggests students should understand that professional designers and makers prioritise safety, making it an integral part of designerly behaviour rather than an externally imposed restriction.

## Professional Development and Teacher Capability

### Initial Teacher Education

D&T teacher training programmes must include substantial safety education, though the DATA report 'Ensuring D&T's Place in the Curriculum' (2017) identified this as variable across providers. Effective ITE includes:

- Comprehensive workshop induction before teaching practice
- Specific training in risk assessment and management
- Observed teaching of safety procedures with expert feedback
- Understanding of legal responsibilities and liability
- Regular updates reflecting technological change

### Continuing Professional Development

Safety knowledge requires regular updating. Matt McLain's research on subject knowledge development emphasises that expertise is maintained through:

- DATA/D&T Association conferences and regional networks
- CLEAPSS updates and training courses
- British Standards revisions (particularly BS 4163:2014)
- Manufacturer training for new equipment
- INSET focused on new materials and processes
