# Materials and Components

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Materials and Components form a foundational element of Design & Technology education in the UK, underpinning the technical knowledge required across all material areas. The National Curriculum for Design and Technology (2014) explicitly states that pupils should develop "a thorough knowledge of which materials and components to use and how they have been shaped" and understand "how more advanced mechanical systems used in their products enable changes in movement and force."

This knowledge domain has been consistently emphasised by key D&T educators. David Barlex's work on "designerly thinking" highlights that material knowledge must be contextualised within design problems rather than taught in isolation. Richard Kimbell's seminal research on "technological capability" (1991, 1997) demonstrated that understanding materials involves both propositional knowledge (knowing that) and procedural knowledge (knowing how).

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 3

At KS3, pupils must develop knowledge of:
- The working characteristics of materials and components
- How materials and components can be combined and processed
- The functional and aesthetic properties of a range of materials
- How materials can be reinforced and strengthened

The National Curriculum specifies that students should work with "a range of materials and components, including construction materials, textiles and ingredients." This breadth requirement distinguishes UK D&T from narrower technical education approaches found in some international contexts.

Kay Stables' research on capability and progression (2008) emphasises that KS3 students must develop what she terms "material literacy" – the ability to make informed selections based on understanding material behaviour under different conditions.

### Key Stage 4

GCSE specifications from all major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) require detailed technical knowledge organised around six material categories:
- Papers and boards
- Natural and manufactured timber
- Ferrous and non-ferrous metals
- Thermosetting and thermoplastic polymers
- Textiles fibres and fabrics
- Electronic and mechanical components and systems

Matt McLain's work on curriculum progression (2017) notes that KS4 represents a significant step-change, requiring students to understand material science concepts including stress, strain, elasticity, ductility, malleability, and toughness.

## Classification of Materials

### Material Categories and Properties

**Papers and Boards**: Students must understand different paper weights (gsm), finishes (matte, gloss, embossed), and compositions (virgin pulp, recycled content). Specifications include layout paper (50gsm), cartridge paper (120-150gsm), corrugated card (variable flute sizes), and duplex board with its distinctive grey reverse.

**Timber**: The distinction between hardwoods (deciduous, slow-growing, generally denser) and softwoods (coniferous, faster-growing, generally less dense) must be taught alongside exceptions (balsa as a hardwood, yew as a dense softwood). Manufactured boards (MDF, plywood, chipboard) represent engineered solutions to timber's natural limitations including movement, weakness in certain directions, and limited sizes.

**Metals**: Ferrous metals (containing iron) exhibit magnetic properties and susceptibility to corrosion, whilst non-ferrous metals (aluminium, copper, brass) resist corrosion but may oxidise differently. Alloys combine elements to achieve specific properties – mild steel (0.15-0.3% carbon) for formability, high carbon steel (0.6-1.4% carbon) for hardness and tool manufacture.

**Polymers**: The fundamental distinction between thermoplastics (can be repeatedly reformed with heat) and thermosetting polymers (undergo irreversible chemical change when heated) underpins material selection decisions. AQA's GCSE specification specifically requires knowledge of acrylic (PMMA), polystyrene (HIPS), polyethylene (HDPE/LDPE), polypropylene (PP), PVC, urea-formaldehyde, epoxy resin, and polyester resin.

### Electronic and Mechanical Components

Stephanie Atkinson's research on systems and control (2000) emphasises that components must be understood within systems thinking frameworks. Students require knowledge of:

**Input devices**: Sensors (LDR, thermistor, pressure pad, reed switch, tilt switch) and manual inputs (switches, potentiometers, variable resistors)

**Process components**: Microcontrollers (particularly Arduino and BBC micro:bit platforms in UK schools), transistors as switching devices, timing circuits

**Output devices**: Motors (DC, servo, stepper), solenoids, LEDs, buzzers, displays

**Mechanical components**: Gears (spur, bevel, worm, rack and pinion), cams and followers, cranks, linkages, pulleys and belts, chains and sprockets

OCR's GCSE specification uniquely requires understanding of mechanical advantage calculations and velocity ratios, representing a more technical approach to mechanical systems.

## Material Properties and Selection

### Physical and Working Properties

Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research identified that successful designers exhibit "interactive thinking" between materials knowledge and design intentions. This requires understanding:

**Mechanical properties**: Tensile strength, compressive strength, bending strength, torsional strength, hardness, toughness, elasticity, plasticity, ductility, malleability

**Aesthetic properties**: Colour, texture, finish potential, grain pattern (timber), lustre (metals)

**Functional properties**: Electrical conductivity/resistance, thermal conductivity/insulation, density, fusibility, corrosion resistance, absorbency (textiles/papers)

David Barlex's "Nuffield Design & Technology" project materials (2000s) pioneered the use of material property cards and decision matrices to scaffold student selection processes, now widely adopted in UK secondary schools.

### Material Testing and Analysis

Edexcel's specification explicitly requires knowledge of standard material tests:
- Tensile testing to determine ultimate tensile strength
- Bend testing for flexibility measurement
- Impact testing (Izod/Charpy) for toughness
- Hardness testing (Brinell, Vickers, Rockwell scales)

Practical implications: Teachers should provide opportunities for students to conduct comparative tests using accessible equipment – drop tests for impact resistance, hanging weights for tensile testing, deflection measurements for stiffness comparisons.

## Sources and Origins

### Stock Forms and Standard Components

Industrial terminology must be accurately taught. Metals are supplied in:
- Round bar, square bar, flat bar, angle section, channel section, tube (seamless or welded)
- Sheet metal (specified by gauge thickness or millimetres)

Timber standard sizes include:
- PAR (planed all round) with nominal vs actual dimensions
- Sawn timber sold in imperial sizes despite metric measurements
- Sheet materials in 8×4 ft (2440×1220mm) standard sheets

Kay Stables' work emphasises that understanding industrial stock forms enables students to design "with manufacturing in mind," considering material wastage and cutting optimisation.

### Sustainability and Environmental Impact

The National Curriculum states students should understand "how materials and components can be combined and processed sustainably." All GCSE specifications now include substantial weighting (typically 10-15% of marks) on sustainability considerations.

**Life Cycle Analysis (LCA)** frameworks require evaluation across:
- Raw material extraction and processing
- Manufacture and fabrication
- Transportation and distribution
- Use phase energy consumption
- End-of-life disposal or recycling

The Ellen MacArthur Foundation's circular economy principles have increasingly influenced UK D&T curricula, with WJEC's specification specifically referencing the 6Rs: Rethink, Refuse, Reduce, Reuse, Repair, Recycle.

**FSC (Forest Stewardship Council)** certification for timber products and **embodied energy** calculations for material comparisons represent practical sustainability tools students must understand.

## Processing and Forming Techniques

### Material Processing Requirements

Different material categories require specialist processes:

**Timber**: Cutting (sawing, drilling, routing, turning), shaping (planing, filing, sanding), joining (adhesives, mechanical fixings, traditional joints), finishing (staining, varnishing, painting, oiling, waxing)

**Metals**: Cutting (sawing, shearing, drilling, laser/plasma cutting), forming (bending, folding, rolling, forging, casting), joining (welding, brazing, soldering, riveting, nuts/bolts), finishing (filing, grinding, polishing, painting, powder coating, galvanising, anodising)

**Polymers**: Cutting (sawing, laser cutting, die cutting), forming (line bending, vacuum forming, blow moulding, injection moulding, rotational moulding), joining (adhesives, solvent welding, mechanical fixings, ultrasonic welding), finishing (sanding, polishing, painting, printing)

Matt McLain's research on practical capability development (2015) demonstrates that students require repeated exposure to processes with increasing complexity to develop genuine competence rather than superficial familiarity.

### Heat Treatment and Material Modification

AQA and OCR specifications both require understanding of heat treatment processes:

**Annealing**: Heating metal to specific temperature (steel: 723°C+) and cooling slowly to soften and relieve internal stresses

**Hardening**: Heating high carbon steel above critical temperature and rapid cooling (quenching) in water or oil

**Tempering**: Reheating hardened steel to intermediate temperature (230-300°C) to reduce brittleness whilst maintaining hardness

**Case hardening**: Adding carbon to low carbon steel surface through heating in carbon-rich environment

Practical implications: School workshops rarely achieve temperatures for steel heat treatment, but teachers can effectively demonstrate principles using tin (60°C melting point) or demonstrate work-hardening of copper through repeated bending and annealing cycles.

## Smart and Modern Materials

### Contemporary Material Technologies

All UK exam boards now require knowledge of smart and modern materials representing recent innovations:

**Smart materials** respond to environmental stimuli:
- Shape Memory Alloy (SMA) such as Nitinol – returns to predetermined shape when heated above transition temperature
- Thermochromic pigments/films – change colour with temperature variation
- Photochromic materials – darken in sunlight, return to clear indoors
- Phosphorescent pigments – absorb and re-emit light
- Quantum Tunnelling Composite (QTC) – changes from insulator to conductor under pressure

**Modern materials** represent recent developments:
- Graphene – single-layer carbon atoms, exceptional strength and conductivity
- Carbon fibre reinforced polymer – high strength-to-weight ratio
- Metal foam – lightweight cellular structures
- Cornstarch-based biopolymers – biodegradable alternatives to conventional plastics
- Kevlar – aramid fibre with exceptional tensile strength

David Barlex's recent work on emerging technologies (2018) cautions against superficial coverage, arguing that students should understand underlying principles rather than memorising material names.

### Composite Materials

Stephanie Atkinson's research emphasises that understanding composites develops systems thinking – recognising how combining materials creates properties unavailable in constituents alone.

**Matrix materials** (polymer resins, metal matrices) provide the bulk structure, whilst **reinforcement materials** (carbon fibre, glass fibre, Kevlar) provide strength in specific directions. Students must understand:
- Fibre orientation and its impact on directional strength
- Resin-to-fibre ratios affecting weight and strength
- Manufacturing processes (hand lay-up, resin transfer moulding, pre-preg autoclave curing)

GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic/fibreglass) remains most accessible for school workshops using polyester resin and chopped strand mat or woven glass cloth.

## Component Systems Integration

### Electronic System Design

The proliferation of Arduino-based learning and physical computing platforms has transformed component knowledge requirements. OCR's specification particularly emphasises system design approaches:

**System architecture**: Input → Process → Output models with feedback loops

**Interfacing considerations**: Voltage/current matching, signal conditioning, pull-up/pull-down resistors, transistor switching for high-current loads

**Programmable components**: Microcontrollers require understanding of digital/analogue pins, PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) for motor speed control, serial communication protocols

Practical implications: Kay Stables' research on technological learning environments suggests that component knowledge develops most effectively through authentic problem-solving contexts rather than isolated skills teaching.

### Mechanical Systems and Movement

Richard Kimbell's work on "thinking and doing" emphasises that mechanical systems understanding develops through iterative making and testing. Students require knowledge of:

**Rotary motion transmission**: Gear trains calculating speed ratios, torque multiplication, direction reversal. Spur gears for parallel shafts, bevel gears for perpendicular shafts, worm gears for high reduction ratios with self-locking properties.

**Rotary to linear conversion**: Rack and pinion for continuous linear motion, cams for reciprocating motion with designed dwell periods, cranks and sliders for reciprocating motion

**Linear motion guidance**: Bearings (ball, roller, plain bush), slides, linkages (parallel, reverse, bell crank)

WJEC's specification uniquely requires calculations of mechanical advantage in lever systems and velocity ratios in pulley systems, supporting mathematical application within D&T contexts.

## Quality Assurance and Testing

### Manufacturing Tolerances

Industrial awareness requires understanding that components are manufactured to specified tolerances:
- **Nominal size**: The intended dimension
- **Tolerance**: Permissible variation (e.g., ±0.5mm)
- **Clearance fit**: Component slightly smaller than housing
- **Interference fit**: Component slightly larger, requiring force to assemble

British Standards (BS) and ISO standards govern tolerancing conventions. GCSE specifications reference these indirectly through quality control requirements.

### Material Specification Communication

Technical communication requirements include:
- Material specifications using standard abbreviations (e.g., "50mm × 50mm SPAR 3m" for sawn timber)
- Component specifications with part numbers and suppliers
- Finishes specified using recognised terminology
- Assembly instructions with torque specifications for fixings

David Barlex's work on design communication emphasises that specification literacy enables students to engage with authentic industrial practices.

## Teaching Approaches and Pedagogical Considerations

### Material-Focused Learning Vs Context-Driven Learning

Richard Kimbell's long-standing critique of decontextualised knowledge teaching challenges traditional "theory lessons" on material properties. His APU research demonstrated that students taught materials in isolation struggled to apply knowledge in design contexts.

Effective approaches identified by Kay Stables (2008) include:
- **Focused practical tasks** introducing specific materials through structured making
- **Material investigations** where students test and compare properties hands-on
- **Contextual challenges** requiring material selection as an integral design decision
- **Disassembly activities** examining how products use materials and components

Stephanie Atkinson's research on systems thinking suggests that components should be taught within "control projects" where students design functional systems rather than learning component names abstractly.

### Assessment of Material Knowledge

Matt McLain's research on assessment validity (2019) identifies tensions between examining material knowledge through written papers versus practical application. All UK exam boards now employ hybrid approaches:

**AQA**: 50% examination (including material properties questions), 50% NEA (Non-Examined Assessment) where material selection justification appears

**OCR**: 50% examination (Paper 1 includes material-specific questions), 50% iterative project requiring material specification

**Edexcel**: 50% written examination with dedicated materials section, 50% substantial design-and-make requiring evidenced material decisions

**WJEC Eduqas**: Similar 50/50 split with particularly detailed material specification requirements in NEA

Effective classroom assessment should mirror these approaches – combining retrieval practice on factual knowledge with application within design scenarios.

### Practical Workshop Implications

Schools face challenges maintaining breadth of material experience. The DATA (Design and Technology Association) guidance recommends:
- Maintaining adequate stock of diverse materials for experimentation
- Regular machinery maintenance and safety training for staff
- Risk assessments covering all processes (CLEAPSS guidance for secondary schools)
- Building relationships with local suppliers for industrial waste materials

David Barlex's Nuffield project emphasised that restricted material access inevitably constrains design thinking – students unconsciously limit ideas to available resources.

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### Material Innovation and Curriculum Lag

The rapid pace of material development creates curriculum challenges. Graphene, despite discovery in 2004, appeared in GCSE specifications only in 2017. Bio-based polymers, metamaterials, and programmable materials represent emerging areas insufficiently addressed in current specifications.

Matt McLain (2021) argues for developing "material learning capabilities" rather than encyclopaedic knowledge of specific materials, enabling students to research and evaluate novel materials independently.

### Sustainability Imperative

Kay Stables' recent work emphasises that material education must foreground environmental impact. Current GCSE specifications address sustainability, but critics argue treatment remains superficial – students memorising recycling symbols without deep understanding of material lifecycles or circular economy principles.

The RSA's "Designing Our Tomorrow" report (2021) calls for D&T curricula centring sustainability, potentially restructuring material teaching around environmental frameworks rather than traditional categories.
