# The Design Process

## Introduction

The design process is fundamental to Design & Technology education in the UK, providing a structured yet flexible framework for pupils to identify needs, generate solutions, and create functional outcomes. As Richard Kimbell has articulated through decades of research, authentic design activity involves the "interaction of mind and hand" where thinking and doing are inseparable. The design process is not linear but iterative, involving continuous cycles of development, evaluation, and refinement.

The National Curriculum for England (2014) positions "design, make, evaluate and technical knowledge" as the core content of D&T, with the design process serving as the pedagogical vehicle through which pupils develop both conceptual understanding and practical capability.

## Historical Context and Key Thinkers

### Richard Kimbell's Contribution

Richard Kimbell's seminal work, particularly through the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research in the 1980s and 1990s, established the theoretical foundation for understanding design activity in educational contexts. His concept of "designerly thinking" emphasises that designing is fundamentally about managing the interaction between what Kimbell terms the "mind" (thought, reflection, evaluation) and the "hand" (modelling, making, testing). This is not sequential but occurs simultaneously, with making serving as a form of thinking.

Kimbell's research demonstrated that assessment of design capability must capture both the process and the product, leading to the development of portfolio-based assessment approaches still used in GCSE and A-Level Design & Technology specifications.

### David Barlex's Practical Framework

David Barlex, through his extensive work with the Nuffield Design & Technology Project and ongoing curriculum development initiatives, has provided practical interpretations of the design process for classroom application. Barlex emphasises the importance of "authentic design contexts" and argues against formulaic, tick-box approaches to designing. His work on "designerly decision-making" highlights that pupils must learn to make and justify choices based on competing criteria rather than follow prescribed steps.

Barlex has been particularly influential in developing approaches to teaching iterative development, arguing that pupils need structured opportunities to test, evaluate, and refine ideas through modelling and prototyping activities.

### Kay Stables' Research on Design Thinking

Kay Stables' research has focused on how young people learn to design and the cognitive processes involved in design activity. Her work emphasises the importance of "imaging" – the ability to visualise and manipulate ideas mentally and through external representations. Stables argues that effective design teaching must develop pupils' ability to generate, communicate, and evaluate multiple ideas, moving beyond first solutions to more innovative outcomes.

Her research has informed understanding of progression in design capability, demonstrating that younger pupils often exhibit "concrete thinking" whilst older pupils develop capacity for more abstract conceptualisation and systems thinking.

### Stephanie Atkinson's Pedagogical Insights

Stephanie Atkinson's research has explored the pedagogical strategies that effectively support design learning. Her work emphasises the role of teacher modelling, structured critique, and the importance of creating classroom cultures where risk-taking and iteration are valued. Atkinson has contributed significantly to understanding how pupils develop design "fluency" through repeated engagement with design challenges of increasing complexity.

## The Design Process in the National Curriculum

### Key Stage 3 Requirements

The National Curriculum for England (2014) states that Key Stage 3 pupils should "use research and exploration, such as the study of different cultures, to identify and understand user needs" and "identify and solve their own design problems and understand how to reformulate problems given to them."

The curriculum emphasises that pupils should "develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products that respond to needs in a variety of situations" and "use a variety of approaches to generate creative ideas and avoid stereotypical responses."

### Key Stage 4 and Examination Specifications

At GCSE level, all major examination boards structure their specifications around iterative designing, making, and evaluating. The weighting given to design process work (coursework/NEA) reflects its centrality:

**AQA Design & Technology (8552)**: The Non-Exam Assessment (50% of qualification) requires pupils to demonstrate "investigating, designing, making and evaluating" skills. The specification explicitly requires iterative development shown through "a range of design ideas" and "the development and refinement of design proposals."

**Eduqas/WJEC Design & Technology (601/8451/2)**: Similarly structures NEA around investigation, design development, making, and evaluation. The specification emphasises that design development must show "modifications as a result of on-going evaluation against the design specification."

**OCR Design & Technology (J310)**: The Iterative Design Challenge (NEA, 50%) requires pupils to demonstrate "an iterative design process" with evidence of "development of design ideas" and "review and refinement."

**Edexcel Design & Technology (1DT0)**: Requires pupils to demonstrate "iterative design processes" that show "analysis, development and refinement of design ideas."

## Models of the Design Process

### Linear vs Iterative Models

Early models of design process, often criticised by contemporary researchers, presented designing as linear stages: identify problem → research → generate ideas → select solution → make → evaluate. Matt McLain's recent work has highlighted how such models can lead to superficial engagement, with pupils treating each stage as a discrete task to be completed rather than as interconnected activities.

Contemporary understanding, informed by Kimbell, Barlex, and others, emphasises iterative models where designers move fluidly between activities, with evaluation occurring continuously rather than only at the end. The "design loop" or "spiral" models better represent authentic design activity.

### The DATA Design Process Model

The Design and Technology Association (DATA, now the Design and Technology Association) has promoted various iterations of design process models. Current best practice emphasises:

1. **Exploring contexts and identifying opportunities**: Understanding user needs, market contexts, and identifying genuine problems worth solving
2. **Designing and developing ideas**: Generating multiple concepts, developing selected ideas through modelling and prototyping
3. **Making functional prototypes**: Creating outcomes that can be tested and evaluated
4. **Evaluating processes and products**: Continuous and summative evaluation against criteria

This framework allows for flexibility and iteration whilst providing structure for teaching and assessment.

## Key Stages of Design Activity

### Investigation and Research

Effective investigation goes beyond superficial internet research. Pupils should engage in:

- **Primary research**: User interviews, surveys, observation, product analysis through disassembly
- **Secondary research**: Expert sources, existing solutions, technical specifications, market analysis
- **Contextual analysis**: Cultural, social, environmental, and economic factors affecting design decisions

Barlex emphasises that investigation should generate "design drivers" – key insights that genuinely inform subsequent design decisions rather than serving as disconnected background information.

### Developing Design Specifications

Design specifications translate user needs and investigation insights into measurable criteria against which solutions can be evaluated. Stables' research demonstrates that effective specifications are:

- **Specific and measurable**: Rather than "attractive," specify "appeals to 16-25 year old demographic through contemporary aesthetic"
- **Prioritised**: Distinguishing essential requirements from desirable features
- **Flexible**: Allowing refinement as understanding develops through prototyping

The National Curriculum requires that pupils "develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products."

### Idea Generation and Development

Research by Kimbell and Stables emphasises that creativity in design education requires structured approaches to divergent and convergent thinking:

**Divergent phase**: Generating multiple, diverse ideas through:
- Sketching and drawing (2D exploration)
- 3D modelling with card, foam, clay
- Digital exploration using CAD
- Morphological analysis and SCAMPER techniques

**Convergent phase**: Evaluating ideas against specifications, combining best features, developing selected concepts through:
- Detailed development drawings showing alternative solutions to specific design aspects
- Prototyping critical features to test function, ergonomics, aesthetics
- Seeking user feedback on prototypes and models

Kay Stables argues that pupils often struggle to move beyond "first ideas," requiring explicit teaching of strategies for development and refinement.

### Prototyping and Modelling

Kimbell's concept of "modelling as thinking" is central to contemporary understanding. Prototypes serve multiple purposes:

- **Exploring ideas**: Quick mock-ups to visualise concepts
- **Testing functionality**: Working models to verify mechanisms, structures
- **Communicating proposals**: Presentation models to gather feedback
- **Developing details**: Testing specific aspects like ergonomics, finishes, assembly methods

Barlex distinguishes between "looks-like" prototypes (appearance models) and "works-like" prototypes (functional testing), arguing that pupils need experience creating both.

### Making and Manufacturing

The National Curriculum emphasises that pupils should "select from and use specialist tools, techniques, processes, equipment and machinery precisely, including computer-aided manufacture." Making is not separate from designing but is part of the iterative process.

Matt McLain's research on "making as learning" demonstrates that practical activity develops both technical skills and conceptual understanding. The quality of making affects whether designs function as intended, making practical capability essential to design capability.

### Evaluation and Testing

Evaluation occurs throughout the design process, not just at the end:

**Formative evaluation**: Ongoing testing and refinement during development
- Testing prototypes against specifications
- Gathering user feedback
- Identifying improvements needed

**Summative evaluation**: Final assessment of outcome quality
- Testing finished product performance
- User testing and feedback
- Analysis of success against original intentions
- Reflection on process and identification of further developments

Atkinson's research emphasises that pupils need explicit teaching of evaluation strategies and should develop critical awareness of their own work and that of others.

## Pedagogical Approaches to Teaching the Design Process

### Scaffolding and Support

Research by Stables and Atkinson demonstrates that pupils require carefully structured support, gradually withdrawn as capability develops:

**Early Key Stage 3**: 
- Focused design tasks with clear constraints
- Teacher-led investigation with structured recording templates
- Exemplar materials showing quality outcomes and process work
- Regular critique and feedback sessions

**Later Key Stage 3 and Key Stage 4**:
- More open-ended design briefs requiring problem identification
- Independent research with guidance on source evaluation
- Expectation of self-directed iteration and refinement
- Peer critique and self-evaluation skills

### Avoiding Formulaic Approaches

Barlex has repeatedly warned against treating the design process as a rigid formula to be followed. Problems with formulaic approaches include:

- Pupils completing stages superficially to "tick boxes"
- Separation of thinking (designing) from doing (making)
- Lack of genuine iteration and development
- Designing becoming a paper exercise disconnected from practical reality

Effective teaching embeds design process naturally within authentic project work rather than teaching it as abstract stages.

### Design Fixation and Moving Beyond First Ideas

Research by Stables and others identifies "design fixation" – where pupils commit prematurely to initial ideas – as a common barrier. Strategies to overcome this include:

- Requiring minimum numbers of initial concepts (whilst avoiding quantity over quality)
- Structured development activities focusing on specific design aspects
- Regular critique sessions where pupils must justify decisions
- Teaching ideation techniques like SCAMPER, morphological analysis, biomimicry
- Celebrating iteration and revision rather than treating changes as mistakes

### Integration with Technical Knowledge

The National Curriculum positioning of technical knowledge alongside the design process is deliberate. McLain's research emphasises that technical knowledge enables design capability – understanding materials, structures, mechanisms, electronics, and systems allows pupils to generate feasible, innovative solutions.

Effective teaching integrates technical knowledge acquisition with design activity:
- Teaching new technical content through focused practical tasks
- Applying technical knowledge immediately in design contexts
- Encouraging exploration of technical possibilities during idea generation
- Using technical analysis of existing products to inform design decisions

## Assessment of the Design Process

### Portfolio-Based Assessment

GCSE and A-Level specifications require evidence of design process through portfolios (often digital). Quality portfolios demonstrate:

- **Genuine investigation** that generates insights informing design decisions
- **Multiple initial ideas** showing exploration of different approaches
- **Iterative development** with evidence of testing, evaluation, and refinement
- **Clear links** between investigation findings, specifications, and design decisions
- **Technical understanding** applied appropriately to solve problems
- **Critical evaluation** with specific reference to success criteria and user feedback

Assessment criteria across all examination boards reward iteration, justification of decisions, and evidence of development rather than quantity of pages.

### Formative Assessment During Projects

Atkinson's research highlights the importance of ongoing assessment and feedback:

- **Design reviews**: Regular presentations where pupils explain and justify developing work
- **Critique sessions**: Structured peer and teacher feedback using specific criteria
- **Learning checkpoints**: Pausing project work to teach needed skills or knowledge
- **Exemplar analysis**: Comparing pupil work against quality examples to develop success criteria understanding

### Common Weaknesses and Misconceptions

Research by Kimbell, Barlex, and others identifies recurring issues in pupil design work:

- Superficial investigation with no clear connection to subsequent design decisions
- Generating single ideas then presenting minor variations as multiple concepts
- Lack of genuine development – ideas remain at initial concept level
- Evaluation that merely describes rather than critically analyses
- Disconnection between designed outcome and what is actually made
- Retrospective design portfolios created after making is complete

Teachers must explicitly address these through modelling quality work, structured feedback, and clear success criteria.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Project Planning and Curriculum Sequencing

Effective curriculum sequencing builds design process capability progressively:

**Year 7**: Short, focused tasks with clear constraints; teacher-structured investigation; emphasis on generating multiple ideas through sketching and modelling.

**Year 8**: Longer projects with increasing independence; introduction of formal specifications; focus on iterative development of selected ideas.

**Year 9**: Open-ended projects requiring problem identification; user-centred design approaches; sophisticated evaluation including testing.

**Years 10-11**: Extended NEA projects demonstrating full design process capability; authentic contexts; significant independence with teacher as consultant rather than director.

### Time Allocation and Project Duration

Barlex argues that meaningful design activity requires sufficient time for iteration. However, extended projects can lead to loss of momentum. Effective approaches include:

- **Mixed project lengths**: Combining short focused tasks (2-4 weeks) with extended projects (8-12 weeks)
- **Structured milestones**: Breaking projects into stages with defined outcomes preventing drift
- **Parallel strands**: Developing process capability alongside building technical knowledge through focused practical tasks

### Resources and Tools

Supporting effective design process requires:

- **Modelling materials**: Card, foam board, clay, construction kits for rapid prototyping
- **Digital tools**: CAD software, digital portfolios, research tools
- **Communication tools**: Quality sketchbooks, drawing equipment, photography for documentation
- **Exemplar materials**: Examples of quality process work at different levels
- **Assessment tools**: Clear rubrics, self-assessment checklists, peer feedback structures

### Cross-Curricular Connections

The design process connects with:

- **Art**: Visual communication, drawing skills, aesthetic evaluation
- **Science**: Investigation methods, systematic testing, evidence-based conclusions
- **Mathematics**: Measurement, calculation, proportion, modelling
- **Computing**: Digital tools, systems thinking, algorithmic approaches
- **English**: Communication, persuasion, analysis and evaluation

Teachers should make these connections explicit, helping pupils transfer skills and understanding.

## Contemporary Developments and Future Directions

### User-Centred and Human-Centred Design

Contemporary professional practice emphasises empathy and deep understanding of user needs. This is increasingly reflected in curriculum materials and examination specifications. Approaches include:

- Ethnographic research methods
- User journey mapping
- Iterative testing with users throughout development
- Inclusive and universal design considerations

### Sustainable Design Thinking

The climate emergency has foregrounded sustainability considerations. The design process now increasingly incorporates:

- Lifecycle analysis from material sourcing through disposal
- Circular economy principles (design for disassembly, repair, recycling)
- Evaluation of environmental impact alongside function and aesthetics
- Systems thinking considering broader consequences of design decisions

### Digital Tools and Manufacturing

CAD/CAM integration affects how pupils engage with the design process:

- Digital modelling allows rapid iteration and testing
- Simulation can replace physical prototyping for some functions
- CNC manufacturing enables complex outcomes but may distance pupils from making processes
- Balance needed between digital and physical modelling

McLain's research suggests that digital tools should enhance rather than replace hands-on prototyping and making.

## Conclusion

The design process remains central to Design & Technology education in the UK, providing a pedagogical framework that develops creative, technical, and evaluative capabilities. Contemporary understanding, informed by decades of research by Kimbell, Barlex, Stables, Atkinson, McLain and others, recognises designing as inherently iterative, with thinking and making inseparable. Effective teaching avoids formulaic approaches, instead embedding design process naturally within authentic, challenging projects that require genuine problem-solving and innovation.

Teachers must provide structured support whilst fostering independence, explicitly teach design strategies and technical knowledge, and assess the quality of design thinking and decision-making rather than mere completion of prescribed stages. As the subject continues to evolve in response to contemporary challenges including sustainability and digital technologies, the fundamental principles of the design process – understanding needs, generating solutions, developing through iteration, and evaluating outcomes – remain essential to developing capable, creative designers.
