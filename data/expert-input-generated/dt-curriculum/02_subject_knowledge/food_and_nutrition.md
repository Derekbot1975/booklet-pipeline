# Food and Nutrition

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Food and Nutrition forms a substantial component of Design & Technology in the English National Curriculum, representing one of the three material categories alongside textiles and resistant materials. The 2014 National Curriculum for Design & Technology explicitly requires that pupils understand and apply the principles of nutrition and learn how to cook. This marks a significant policy shift, emphasising both the practical and scientific dimensions of food education.

The National Curriculum states that pupils should be taught to "understand and apply the principles of nutrition and health" and to "cook a repertoire of predominantly savoury dishes so that they are able to feed themselves and others a healthy and varied diet" (DfE, 2013). This dual emphasis on nutritional knowledge and practical cookery skills distinguishes the contemporary curriculum from previous iterations.

## Key Stage 3 Requirements

At Key Stage 3, the National Curriculum specifies that all pupils should be taught to:

- Understand the source, seasonality and characteristics of a broad range of ingredients
- Understand and apply the principles of nutrition and health
- Cook a repertoire of predominantly savoury dishes
- Become competent in a range of cooking techniques including controlling temperature, portioning, and timing
- Measure accurately using weighing scales and measuring jugs

David Barlex has argued that food technology at KS3 must balance practical capability with conceptual understanding, emphasising that "cooking is both a practical skill and an intellectual activity requiring decision-making and problem-solving" (Barlex, 2007). This perspective challenges teachers to ensure that food lessons are not merely instructional cookery demonstrations but genuine design activities involving evaluation, adaptation, and creative thinking.

### Practical Implications for KS3 Teachers

Teachers should structure schemes of work that progress from basic techniques (knife skills, measuring, mixing methods) towards more complex processes requiring temperature control and timing coordination. The DATA (Design and Technology Association) guidance suggests a minimum of one hour per week for food education at KS3, though many schools struggle to achieve this allocation.

Assessment should encompass both practical skills and theoretical understanding. Richard Kimbell's research on performance assessment emphasises the importance of evaluating pupils' thinking processes alongside their practical outcomes (Kimbell & Stables, 2007). In food technology, this means assessing recipe adaptation, sensory evaluation skills, and nutritional analysis as well as the finished dish.

## Key Stage 4: GCSE Food Preparation and Nutrition

The reformed GCSE Food Preparation and Nutrition (first teaching 2016) represents a significant curriculum development. All four major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) offer specifications that follow the same core structure mandated by Ofqual.

### Content Areas

The GCSE specifications require study of:

1. **Food, nutrition and health**: macronutrients (proteins, fats, carbohydrates), micronutrients (vitamins and minerals), nutritional needs across life stages, diet-related health conditions
2. **Food science**: functional and chemical properties of food, heat transfer methods, raising agents, protein denaturation, gelatinisation
3. **Food safety**: bacterial contamination, enzyme action, food poisoning organisms, temperature control, cross-contamination
4. **Food choice**: factors influencing food choice including cultural, religious, ethical, and economic considerations
5. **Food provenance**: environmental impact, food miles, food security, farming methods

### Assessment Structure

All specifications include:
- **Written examination** (50%): typically 1 hour 45 minutes
- **Non-examination assessment** (50%): comprising a Food Investigation (30%) and a Food Preparation Assessment (20%)

The Food Investigation requires pupils to undertake scientific investigation into the functional or chemical properties of ingredients. This assessment explicitly connects food technology with scientific methodology, requiring hypothesis formation, controlled testing, and evidence-based conclusions.

Matt McLain's work on assessment in D&T emphasises that effective food investigations should demonstrate "genuine enquiry rather than recipe-following," requiring pupils to manipulate variables and analyse results systematically (McLain, 2018).

## Core Knowledge: Nutritional Science

### Macronutrients

Teachers must ensure pupils understand the three macronutrients in detail:

**Proteins**: Composed of amino acids; eight essential amino acids must be obtained from diet. High Biological Value (HBV) proteins contain all essential amino acids (meat, fish, eggs, dairy, soya); Low Biological Value (LBV) proteins lack one or more essential amino acids (cereals, pulses, nuts). Protein complementation allows combination of LBV sources to provide complete amino acid profiles.

**Carbohydrates**: Simple sugars (monosaccharides and disaccharides) provide quick energy; complex carbohydrates (polysaccharides including starch and non-starch polysaccharides/dietary fibre) provide sustained energy release. Current UK dietary guidance recommends at least 50% of energy from carbohydrates, with emphasis on wholegrain sources.

**Fats**: Essential for hormone production, vitamin absorption, and insulation. Saturated fats (primarily animal sources) should comprise no more than 11% of energy intake; unsaturated fats (monounsaturated and polyunsaturated) should be prioritised. Essential fatty acids (omega-3 and omega-6) cannot be synthesised by the body.

### Micronutrients

Pupils should understand key vitamins and minerals, their functions, deficiency symptoms, and dietary sources. Priority micronutrients include:

- **Vitamin A**: vision, immune function; found in liver, dairy, orange/yellow vegetables (as beta-carotene)
- **Vitamin D**: calcium absorption, bone health; limited food sources (oily fish, fortified products); primarily synthesised via sunlight exposure
- **Vitamin C**: collagen formation, immune function, iron absorption; destroyed by heat, light, and alkali
- **Iron**: haemoglobin formation; haem iron (meat) more readily absorbed than non-haem iron (plant sources)
- **Calcium**: bone and teeth formation, blood clotting, muscle function

Stephanie Atkinson's research emphasises that nutritional knowledge must be contextualised within real-world scenarios: "Abstract nutritional facts become meaningful only when pupils apply them to actual dietary planning and recipe modification" (Atkinson, 2009).

## Core Knowledge: Food Science

### Protein Denaturation and Coagulation

Heat, mechanical action, and acids cause protein molecules to unfold (denature) and form new bonds (coagulate). This principle explains egg setting, meat cooking, and gluten development. Practical applications include:

- Eggs coagulate at 60-65°C (yolk) and 65-70°C (white)
- Overcooking causes excessive water loss and rubbery texture
- Whisking egg whites denatures proteins, trapping air bubbles to form foam

### Gelatinisation and Dextrinisation

**Gelatinisation**: Starch granules absorb water and swell when heated in liquid (typically 60-80°C), causing thickening. Complete gelatinisation produces a paste or gel. This principle underpins sauce-making, custard, and roux-based preparations.

**Dextrinisation**: Dry heat breaks down starch into sweeter-tasting dextrins, causing browning on bread surfaces, toasted products, and roasted foods.

### Caramelisation and Maillard Reaction

**Caramelisation**: Sugars break down at high temperatures (160°C+), producing brown colour and complex flavours. Occurs in caramel, toffee, and roasted vegetables.

**Maillard Reaction**: Chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars at temperatures above 140°C, producing browned surfaces and complex flavours in roasted meats, baked goods, and fried foods. Named after French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard.

### Raising Agents

Pupils must understand the science behind leavening:

- **Biological**: yeast fermentation produces carbon dioxide; requires warmth, moisture, and time
- **Chemical**: bicarbonate of soda (requires acid activation), baking powder (contains acid component)
- **Mechanical**: air incorporation through creaming, whisking, rubbing-in, or folding
- **Steam**: expansion of water molecules during heating (choux pastry, Yorkshire puddings)

## Food Safety and Microbiology

Understanding food safety represents a statutory requirement and forms substantial content across all GCSE specifications. The Food Standards Agency provides authoritative guidance that teachers should reference.

### Bacterial Growth Requirements

Bacteria require specific conditions for multiplication, remembered by the acronym FATTOM:
- **Food**: particularly high-risk foods (proteins and moist foods)
- **Acid**: most bacteria prefer pH 5-8 (neutral conditions)
- **Temperature**: danger zone 5-63°C; optimal growth 37°C
- **Time**: bacteria multiply through binary fission every 10-20 minutes in optimal conditions
- **Oxygen**: some bacteria require oxygen (aerobic), others do not (anaerobic)
- **Moisture**: bacteria require water activity above 0.85

### Key Food Poisoning Organisms

Pupils should understand pathogenic bacteria including:

- **Salmonella**: found in raw poultry, eggs; causes vomiting and diarrhoea; onset 12-36 hours
- **Campylobacter**: most common cause of food poisoning in UK; raw poultry; onset 2-5 days
- **E. coli O157**: severe illness possible; undercooked minced beef; onset 1-3 days
- **Staphylococcus aureus**: transferred from human handlers; produces toxin; rapid onset 1-6 hours
- **Clostridium perfringens**: spore-forming; survives initial cooking; insufficient reheating; onset 12-18 hours

### Temperature Control

Critical temperatures include:
- **-18°C**: freezer storage temperature
- **0-5°C**: refrigerator storage temperature
- **5°C**: maximum cold storage temperature
- **63°C**: minimum hot holding temperature
- **75°C**: minimum core cooking temperature for most foods

## Food Provenance and Sustainability

Contemporary food education must address environmental and ethical dimensions. Kay Stables argues that design and technology education should develop "socially and environmentally conscious citizens who understand the wider implications of their choices" (Stables, 2008).

### Environmental Considerations

Topics include:
- **Food miles**: distance food travels from production to consumer; carbon footprint implications
- **Seasonal eating**: environmental and economic benefits of consuming produce in season
- **Farming methods**: organic vs. conventional; free-range vs. intensive; environmental impacts
- **Food waste**: UK households waste approximately 6.6 million tonnes annually (WRAP data)
- **Packaging**: environmental impact of different materials; recycling considerations

### Ethical and Cultural Factors

Pupils should understand diverse dietary requirements:
- **Religious**: Halal (Islamic), Kosher (Jewish), Hindu vegetarianism
- **Ethical**: vegetarianism, veganism, Fairtrade considerations
- **Medical**: coeliac disease (gluten-free), lactose intolerance, allergies
- **Lifestyle**: dietary preferences, weight management, sports nutrition

## Sensory Analysis and Evaluation

Systematic sensory evaluation represents a crucial skill distinguishing food technology from basic cookery. Teachers should employ structured methodologies:

### Sensory Testing Methods

**Preference testing**: ranking or rating products; identifies consumer preference
**Discrimination testing**: triangle test (identify odd sample), paired comparison (identify difference)
**Profiling**: rating product attributes using descriptive scales; produces sensory profiles

Kay Stables and Richard Kimbell's research emphasises that evaluation in design and technology must be analytical and evidence-based rather than superficial (Kimbell et al., 1991). In food contexts, this means moving beyond "it tastes nice" towards specific sensory vocabulary: umami, mouthfeel, texture contrast, flavour balance, visual appeal.

### Vocabulary Development

Teachers should develop pupils' sensory vocabulary across five senses:
- **Appearance**: colour intensity, gloss, texture, symmetry, garnish
- **Aroma**: identifying specific scent notes (herb profiles, spice characteristics)
- **Taste**: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami
- **Texture**: crisp, crunchy, tender, chewy, smooth, grainy
- **Sound**: crunch, snap, sizzle (often overlooked but significant)

## Practical Skills Progression

The reformed GCSE specifications include detailed lists of technical skills. Teachers should ensure systematic skills development:

### Foundation Skills (typically Year 7-8)
- Knife skills: bridge hold, claw grip, different cutting techniques (dice, slice, julienne)
- Weighing and measuring accurately
- Basic mixing methods: rubbing-in, melting, creaming, all-in-one
- Temperature control: boiling, simmering, frying
- Simple food preparation: fruit salad, simple salads, sandwiches

### Intermediate Skills (typically Year 9)
- Sauce-making: roux-based sauces, reduction, emulsification
- Pastry: shortcrust, rubbed-in method
- Bread-making: basic yeast dough, proving, shaping
- Protein cooking: managing different cooking methods (grilling, roasting, poaching)
- Multi-component dishes requiring timing coordination

### Advanced Skills (typically Years 10-11)
- Complex pastry: choux, enriched dough, puff pastry
- Advanced techniques: filleting fish, preparing poultry, complex knife skills
- Complex sauce work: hollandaise, reduction sauces
- Intricate presentation and garnishing
- Multi-process dishes demonstrating technical competence

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Demonstrations

David Barlex emphasises that demonstrations should be interactive rather than passive: "The demonstration is not a performance but a shared exploration of technique and reasoning" (Barlex, 2007). Teachers should:

- Position demonstrations so all pupils can see clearly (mirrors, cameras, raised platforms)
- Involve pupils in decision-making during demonstrations
- Pause to check understanding and elicit predictions
- Use questioning to develop conceptual understanding alongside technique

### Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)

FPTs develop specific skills through structured activities. Effective FPTs should:
- Target one or two specific techniques
- Provide clear success criteria
- Allow for immediate feedback and correction
- Progress systematically in complexity

### Design and Make Activities (DMAs)

DMAs provide opportunities for extended project work where pupils demonstrate creativity, problem-solving, and application of knowledge. Kay Stables's research on iterative design emphasises that pupils should trial, test, modify, and refine products through multiple cycles rather than following fixed recipes (Stables & Kimbell, 2000).

## Assessment and Feedback

Matt McLain's work on assessment emphasises formative approaches that develop capability rather than merely measuring it. In food technology contexts, this includes:

### Formative Assessment Strategies

- **Live feedback during practical work**: immediate correction of technique
- **Photographic evidence**: documenting work stages for later reflection
- **Peer assessment**: structured sensory evaluation by classmates
- **Self-evaluation**: reflective writing about modifications and improvements

### Summative Assessment

GCSE specifications provide clear assessment criteria. The Food Preparation Assessment (NEA2) marks pupils on:
- **Technical skills demonstrated** (30 marks)
- **Dish selection demonstrating skills** (15 marks)
- **Planning and preparation** (15 marks)

Teachers should ensure pupils understand that the assessment rewards skill complexity and technical competence, not simply producing attractive final dishes.

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Obesity and Public Health

The UK faces significant public health challenges related to diet. Public Health England's Eatwell Guide provides the foundation for dietary education, recommending:
- Base meals on starchy carbohydrates (wholegrain where possible)
- Consume at least 5 portions of varied fruit and vegetables daily
- Include protein sources (beans, pulses, fish, eggs, meat)
- Choose unsaturated oils and spreads
- Limit foods high in fat, salt, and sugar

Stephanie Atkinson argues that food education must address these public health concerns without becoming "preachy or moralistic" (Atkinson, 2016). Teachers should present nutritional information factually whilst acknowledging the complexity of food choices influenced by economic, cultural, and social factors.

### Skills vs. Knowledge Debate

An ongoing debate concerns the balance between practical cooking skills and nutritional knowledge
