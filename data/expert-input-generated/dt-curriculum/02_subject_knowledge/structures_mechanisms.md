# Structures and Mechanisms

## Overview

Structures and mechanisms form a fundamental component of Design & Technology education in the UK, providing students with essential knowledge about how objects maintain their form, withstand forces, and create movement. This area bridges theoretical understanding of forces, materials and engineering principles with practical making skills. According to David Barlex (2007), understanding structures and mechanisms is crucial for developing students' capability to design products that are both functional and fit for purpose.

The National Curriculum for England (2014) explicitly requires students to "apply their understanding of how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures" and to understand and use "mechanical systems in their products." This knowledge area appears throughout Key Stages 1-4, with progressive complexity.

## Key Concepts in Structures

### Types of Structures

**Shell structures** are hollow, three-dimensional forms that derive strength from their curved shape (e.g., eggs, bottles, helmets). Richard Kimbell's research (1997) emphasised how understanding natural shell structures helps students appreciate efficient design solutions.

**Frame structures** consist of rigid components joined together to form a stable skeleton (e.g., chairs, buildings, bicycles). These distribute loads through individual members working in tension or compression.

**Solid structures** are made from solid materials throughout, relying on material mass for strength (e.g., bricks, stone walls). While strong, they are typically heavy and material-intensive.

### Forces Acting on Structures

Kay Stables (2008) emphasised the importance of students understanding the invisible forces that structures must resist:

- **Compression** – pushing forces that squeeze or crush materials
- **Tension** – pulling forces that stretch materials
- **Torsion** – twisting forces that rotate one part against another
- **Shear** – sliding forces acting in opposite directions
- **Bending** – combination of compression and tension forces

The National Curriculum requires students at Key Stage 3 to "understand how more advanced mechanical systems used in their products enable changes in movement and force."

### Structural Strengthening Techniques

**Triangulation** – Using triangular configurations to create rigid structures that resist deformation. Stephanie Atkinson (2009) highlighted this as a critical concept that students often struggle to apply practically.

**Cross-bracing** – Adding diagonal members to rectangular frames to prevent racking.

**Material selection** – Choosing materials with appropriate properties for the forces encountered.

**Corrugation and folding** – Creating rigidity in sheet materials through geometric manipulation.

**Lamination** – Building up layers to increase thickness and strength.

**Webbing and ribbing** – Adding internal structures to distribute loads (common in injection mouldings).

## Key Concepts in Mechanisms

### Types of Motion

Matt McLain's work on primary D&T (2015) emphasised the importance of establishing clear understanding of motion types from Key Stage 1:

- **Linear motion** – movement in a straight line
- **Reciprocating motion** – back-and-forth linear movement
- **Rotary motion** – circular movement around an axis
- **Oscillating motion** – back-and-forth curved movement (pendulum)

### Mechanical Systems

**Levers** form the foundation of mechanical advantage understanding. The three classes of lever should be taught with real-world examples:
- **Class 1**: Fulcrum between effort and load (scissors, crowbar, see-saw)
- **Class 2**: Load between fulcrum and effort (wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener)
- **Class 3**: Effort between fulcrum and load (tweezers, fishing rod, forearm)

**Linkages** convert or modify motion and force. Common types include:
- Reverse motion linkages (changing direction)
- Parallel motion linkages (maintaining direction)
- Bell crank linkages (changing motion through 90°)
- Treadle linkages (converting reciprocating to rotary motion)

**Gears and gear trains** transmit rotary motion and modify speed and torque:
- Simple gear trains (spur gears, bevel gears, worm gears)
- Compound gear trains for greater speed changes
- Gear ratios and velocity ratio calculations

**Cams and followers** convert rotary motion into reciprocating motion with various profiles:
- Pear cam (gentle rise and fall)
- Snail cam (gradual continuous rise)
- Heart-shaped cam (uniform rise and sudden drop)

**Pulleys and belt drives** transmit rotary motion over distances and can modify speed through different pulley diameters.

**Crank and slider mechanisms** convert rotary motion to reciprocating linear motion (engines, sewing machines).

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

Students should explore simple mechanisms including sliders, levers, wheels and axles through making projects. Focus on understanding that mechanisms make things move and investigating how existing products use these principles.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Students progress to understanding and using more complex mechanisms including linkages, cams, gears and pulleys. They should begin to understand mechanical advantage and how mechanisms can change the direction and magnitude of forces. Projects might include moving toys, pop-up books, or simple fairground rides.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum requires deeper understanding of mechanical systems and their application. Students should calculate gear ratios, analyse forces in structures, and apply strengthening techniques systematically. OCR's Design & Technology specifications emphasise practical application through projects requiring mechanical systems integration.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE specifications across all major exam boards include significant structures and mechanisms content:

**AQA Design & Technology (8552)** includes mechanical devices within "Core technical principles," requiring understanding of systems, forces, moments, and mechanical advantage calculations.

**Edexcel Design & Technology (1DT0)** assesses "Engineering and manufacturing" including structural integrity, forces analysis, and mechanical systems integration.

**OCR Design & Technology (J310)** includes "Selection of materials and components" requiring understanding of how materials respond to forces and how mechanisms achieve required functions.

**WJEC Design & Technology (C560)** requires knowledge of "forces and stresses" and "learning about mechanisms" within their technical principles.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Disassembly and Investigation

David Barlex (2007) advocated strongly for focused practical tasks (FPTs) involving systematic disassembly of products to understand their mechanical systems. Students should investigate real products including toys, household appliances, and tools to identify mechanisms in context.

### Modelling and Prototyping

Richard Kimbell's research at Goldsmiths, University of London (1991, 1997) demonstrated that iterative modelling is crucial for developing understanding of structures and mechanisms. Students should create low-fidelity card and paper models before progressing to more permanent materials. This approach allows rapid testing and modification.

Kay Stables' work on modelling (1992) emphasised that mechanisms are particularly suitable for exploratory modelling as motion and function can be immediately tested and refined.

### Mathematical Integration

Calculations of mechanical advantage, gear ratios, and force analysis provide excellent opportunities for applying mathematics. Stephanie Atkinson (2009) identified this as an area where cross-curricular links are particularly valuable but often underutilised.

For example, velocity ratio (VR) = number of teeth on driven gear ÷ number of teeth on driver gear. Students should apply these calculations to real design scenarios.

### Testing and Evaluation

Structures can be tested systematically through load testing, deflection measurement, and failure analysis. Matt McLain (2015) emphasised the importance of testing as a learning opportunity, not merely as assessment. Students develop deeper understanding when they can observe and measure structural performance.

## Common Misconceptions

Research by Stephanie Atkinson and others has identified recurring misconceptions:

1. **Force and weight confusion** – Students often conflate mass with force and struggle to understand that gravity creates the downward force.

2. **Stability and strength** – Many students believe heavy structures are automatically stable, without understanding centre of gravity principles.

3. **Gear direction** – Students frequently make errors in predicting rotation direction in gear trains.

4. **Mechanical advantage** – The concept that machines make work easier (not reduce work done) requires careful teaching.

5. **Material properties** – Students often assume materials behave uniformly under different forces without understanding concepts like brittleness, ductility, and hardness.

## Practical Resources and Activities

### Structures Activities

**Spaghetti bridge challenge** – Students design and build bridges from dried spaghetti and adhesive, then test to failure. This demonstrates triangulation, compression, tension, and efficient material use.

**Paper tower construction** – Building the tallest freestanding tower from limited paper sheets develops understanding of folding, stability, and material efficiency.

**Beam testing** – Creating and testing different cross-section beams (I-beam, hollow box, solid rectangular) demonstrates how shape affects stiffness and strength.

### Mechanisms Activities

**Mechanical systems kits** – Construction kits such as LEGO Technic, K'NEX, or Meccano allow rapid experimentation with gears, linkages, and other mechanisms.

**Cardboard automata** – Creating hand-cranked automata using cams and followers teaches motion conversion and timing while producing engaging outcomes.

**Gear train design** – Students design gear trains to achieve specific speed reductions or increases, applying calculations then testing physical models.

## Links to Industry and Real-World Applications

David Barlex's work on 'Nuffield Design & Technology' (2005) emphasised connecting classroom learning to industrial practice. Structures and mechanisms are fundamental to:

- **Automotive engineering** – Chassis structures, suspension linkages, transmission systems
- **Aerospace** – Lightweight frame structures, flight control mechanisms, retractable landing gear
- **Architecture** – Building frames, roof trusses, opening mechanisms for doors and windows
- **Consumer products** – Packaging structures, toy mechanisms, ergonomic handles using lever principles
- **Robotics** – Frame structures, gripper mechanisms, gear trains for motors

Inviting engineers as speakers or arranging factory visits helps students understand professional application of these principles.

## Assessment Considerations

When assessing student understanding of structures and mechanisms:

**Knowledge assessment** should include identification of mechanism types, naming structural forms, and explaining how forces act.

**Application assessment** requires students to select appropriate structural solutions or mechanical systems for given design contexts, justifying choices with reference to forces, materials, and function.

**Analysis assessment** involves calculating mechanical advantage, gear ratios, or predicting motion output from given input, as required by all GCSE specifications.

**Practical assessment** through NEA (Non-Exam Assessment) should demonstrate students can design, make, and test products incorporating appropriate structures and mechanisms that function effectively.

## Health and Safety

When teaching structures and mechanisms, teachers must consider:

- **Testing to failure** – Ensure adequate eye protection and containment when deliberately breaking structures
- **Sharp edges** – Mechanisms with gears or linkages may create pinch points
- **Rotating machinery** – Long hair and loose clothing hazards with moving mechanisms
- **Tools** – Appropriate use of saws, drills, and cutting tools when fabricating components

The DATA (Design and Technology Association) provides comprehensive health and safety guidance specific to D&T education.

## Conclusion

Structures and mechanisms represent essential technical knowledge that enables students to design and make functional products. As David Barlex argues, this knowledge must be "learned in context, through purposeful activity, rather than simply transmitted as abstract theory." Effective teaching combines theoretical understanding with practical investigation, systematic testing, and real-world application, progressively building capability from Key Stage 1 through to GCSE and beyond.
