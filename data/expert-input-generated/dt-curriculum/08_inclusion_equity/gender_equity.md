# Gender Equity in D&T

## Introduction and Context

Gender equity in Design & Technology remains one of the most persistent challenges in UK secondary education. Despite D&T being positioned as an inclusive, holistic subject since the introduction of the National Curriculum in 1989, significant gender disparities persist in participation rates, subject choices, and attainment patterns. The challenge is particularly acute at Key Stage 4, where students encounter material-area specialisms that carry historical gender associations—Resistant Materials and Engineering attracting predominantly male students, whilst Food Preparation and Nutrition and Textiles attract predominantly female students.

Kay Stables' research at Goldsmiths, University of London has consistently highlighted how these patterns are established early, with gendered attitudes towards making, materials, and design contexts evident from Key Stage 2 onwards. The subject community has long recognised that achieving genuine gender equity requires systematic intervention addressing curriculum design, pedagogy, assessment, and the cultural positioning of D&T within schools.

## Historical Context and Policy Framework

### National Curriculum Development

The 1989 National Curriculum Technology Orders attempted to break down traditional craft subject boundaries by creating a unified Technology curriculum. David Barlex has documented how the original vision aimed to eliminate gendered subject choices by making technology education universal and capability-focused rather than material-specific. However, implementation challenges meant many schools maintained separate material-focused departments, perpetuating historical divisions.

The 1995 Dearing Review fragmented Technology into distinct subject areas, inadvertently reinforcing gendered choices. Richard Kimbell's critique of this period emphasised how the subject lost its original integrative vision, allowing schools to offer narrow, material-specific pathways that attracted gender-imbalanced cohorts.

The 2014 National Curriculum attempted to reassert design and technology as a unified discipline, stating that D&T should enable all pupils to "develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world." However, the reality in many schools remains that gender-segregated options persist, particularly at Key Stage 4.

### Current Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology (2014) contains no explicit gender equity statements, operating under the assumption that the subject is inherently inclusive. The Programme of Study requires that pupils work across "a variety of contexts" including "the wider historical, social, cultural, environmental and economic contexts." However, the curriculum does not mandate specific interventions to address gender imbalances.

## Research Evidence on Gender Disparities

### Participation Patterns

Stephanie Atkinson's extensive research at Sunderland University has quantified the scale of gender disparities in D&T. Her longitudinal studies demonstrate:

- At Key Stage 3, where D&T is compulsory, participation is balanced but engagement patterns differ significantly
- At Key Stage 4, severe gender segregation occurs across material specialism choices
- Design Engineering/Engineering (typically 85-90% male)
- Resistant Materials/Product Design (typically 70-80% male)
- Food Preparation and Nutrition (typically 75-85% female)
- Fashion and Textiles (typically 90-95% female)

Analysis of GCSE entry data from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas consistently confirms these patterns. The Engineering GCSE, introduced by several exam boards from 2017, has struggled to attract female students despite explicit attempts to position it as gender-neutral.

### Attainment Patterns

Matt McLain's research at Liverpool John Moores University has examined attainment data across D&T specifications, revealing complex patterns:

- In Food Preparation and Nutrition, female students typically outperform male students by 0.3-0.5 grades
- In Resistant Materials and Engineering, attainment gaps are minimal, but the small number of female participants makes generalisations problematic
- In Textiles, male students are so under-represented that meaningful comparison is impossible
- Coursework components generally show smaller gender gaps than examined components

These patterns suggest that when students do select non-stereotypical pathways, they can achieve well, indicating that capability is not the barrier—choice architecture and cultural factors are paramount.

### Attitudes and Aspirations

Research by Kay Stables and Richard Kimbell through the Centre for Technology Education Studies identified that gender differences in D&T attitudes emerge early:

- By Year 5, boys express greater confidence with tools, machinery, and construction
- Girls demonstrate equal or superior design thinking capabilities but often lack confidence in making activities
- Boys typically prefer contexts involving vehicles, mechanisms, and electronics
- Girls typically prefer contexts involving fashion, food, and social/environmental themes
- These preferences are culturally constructed rather than innate, as demonstrated by international comparisons

David Barlex's work on "design-without-make" pedagogies has shown that when physical making is de-emphasised, gender confidence gaps narrow, suggesting that workshop culture and tool access are significant barriers.

## Barriers to Gender Equity

### Curriculum and Subject Structure

**Material-area specialisms**: The persistence of traditional craft-based pathways creates immediate gendered associations. The terminology itself carries baggage—"Resistant Materials" evokes masculine associations with hardness and strength, whilst "Textiles" is culturally feminised. Even contemporary rebranding attempts (Product Design, Fashion and Textiles) struggle to overcome these deep-seated associations.

**Context selection**: Stephanie Atkinson's research demonstrates that project contexts significantly influence student engagement. Contexts involving childcare, fashion, or domestic activities attract female students, whilst automotive, sports, or military contexts attract male students. Balanced curriculum design requires deliberate diversification of contexts across all material areas.

**Assessment weighting**: The balance between designing and making components affects gender equity. Where physical making using traditional workshop equipment dominates assessment, female students often feel disadvantaged due to limited prior experience and workshop confidence.

### Pedagogical Factors

**Teacher modelling**: David Barlex has highlighted how teacher demographics influence subject culture. D&T departments with exclusively or predominantly male staff teaching Resistant Materials and female staff teaching Food and Textiles reinforce stereotypical associations. Teacher enthusiasm and expertise areas unconsciously shape student perceptions of "who belongs" in each specialism.

**Workshop culture**: The workshop environment can be unwelcoming or intimidating for students who lack prior experience with tools and machinery. Research indicates that boys are more likely to have home-based making experience with construction toys, DIY exposure, and encouragement to use tools. The workshop can therefore function as a space where existing privilege is consolidated rather than equity achieved.

**Language and imagery**: Kay Stables' work on design communication has shown how teacher language, exemplar materials, and visual resources frequently perpetuate stereotypes. Examples dominated by male designers, gendered pronouns in project briefs, and stereotypical product imagery all contribute to implicit messaging about who D&T is "for."

### Institutional Factors

**Options structures**: School options systems often position D&T pathways in direct competition with each other or with other subjects. When students must choose between Food Preparation and Nutrition and Engineering, gendered choices are almost inevitable. The "guided choices" processes used at Key Stage 3/4 transition frequently reinforce rather than challenge stereotypes.

**Careers education**: Links between D&T and career pathways often emphasise traditionally gendered professions—engineering and construction (implicitly male), nutrition and fashion design (implicitly female). Failure to showcase diverse role models and counter-stereotypical career paths perpetuates limited aspirations.

**Extra-curricular provision**: Enrichment activities in D&T often reflect gendered interests—robotics clubs attracting boys, Young Fashion Designer competitions attracting girls. Limited mixed-gender, diverse-interest enrichment misses opportunities to broaden participation.

## Strategies for Promoting Gender Equity

### Curriculum Design Strategies

**Integrated project contexts**: David Barlex advocates for project briefs that explicitly integrate multiple material areas and appeal to diverse interests. For example, designing assistive technology devices requires textiles (comfort, wearability), resistant materials (structure, mechanisms), and electronics (function), avoiding narrow material-focus.

**Rotating material experiences**: At Key Stage 3, structured rotation systems ensuring all students experience all material areas with equal time and emphasis prevent early specialisation based on stereotype. The DATA (now Design and Technology Association) guidance recommends minimum six-week rotations across at least four material areas.

**Contemporary contexts**: Using contexts from contemporary design practice helps overcome historical associations. Projects addressing sustainability, universal design, medical devices, and smart textiles present D&T as forward-looking and relevant to all students.

**Explicit diversity showcasing**: Kay Stables recommends deliberate inclusion of diverse designers as case studies—female engineers, male fashion designers, and designers from varied cultural backgrounds. The curriculum should actively counter stereotypical representations.

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Confidence-building scaffolding**: Stephanie Atkinson's research supports progressive skill development with extensive modelling, peer support, and low-stakes practice opportunities. When all students receive thorough tool inductions and making support, gender confidence gaps narrow.

**Design-led approaches**: Emphasising design thinking, research, and development before making activities allows all students to demonstrate capability. Richard Kimbell's research on "capability" foregrounded problem-solving and decision-making over craft skill, potentially reducing gendered barriers.

**Collaborative making**: Pairing or grouping students intentionally (avoiding friendship groups that may be gender-segregated) for making activities can support confidence development and skill-sharing. However, teachers must monitor that gendered labour divisions don't emerge within groups.

**Language awareness**: Conscious use of inclusive language, gender-neutral pronouns in examples, and challenging stereotypical assumptions when they arise is essential. Matt McLain's work emphasises the cumulative impact of seemingly minor language choices.

### Assessment Considerations

**Diverse assessment modes**: Using varied assessment approaches—written design work, CAD/CAM, physical making, presentation, and evaluation—ensures students can demonstrate capability through multiple channels. Exam boards offer different assessment balances:

- **AQA Design and Technology GCSE**: 50% written examination, 50% NEA (Non-Examined Assessment), allowing substantial design work alongside making
- **Edexcel Design and Technology GCSE**: Similar 50/50 split with flexibility in material focus within NEA
- **OCR Design and Technology GCSE**: 50% examined, 50% iterative design project
- **WJEC Eduqas**: Offers separate Design and Technology and Engineering specifications with different emphases

Teachers can guide project selections within NEA briefs to ensure students aren't constrained by stereotypical assumptions about appropriate projects.

**Inclusive exemplar materials**: Moderator reports and grade boundary materials from exam boards should be reviewed for gender representation. Teachers should actively seek and display exemplar work from students of all genders across all project types.

### Institutional Approaches

**Options guidance**: Careers and options guidance at Key Stage 3/4 transition should explicitly address gender stereotypes in D&T. Research-informed guidance materials from the Gatsby Foundation and the Design and Technology Association provide frameworks for equitable options processes.

**Timetabling considerations**: Where possible, avoiding direct competition between D&T pathways in options blocks allows students to select based on genuine interest rather than forced choice. Some schools have successfully positioned Food Preparation and Nutrition outside the "D&T" option block to increase accessibility.

**Professional development**: D&T departments should engage with gender equity research and practice through CPD. The Design and Technology Association offers guidance, and research networks like PATT (Pupils' Attitudes Towards Technology) provide evidence-based resources.

**Department culture audit**: Regular review of department practices, displays, language, resources, and assumptions can identify areas requiring attention. Stephanie Atkinson recommends structured departmental self-review processes examining gender equity explicitly.

## Subject-Specific Considerations

### Food Preparation and Nutrition

This specification area faces particular challenges as the most gender-imbalanced (female-dominated) and often marginalised within D&T departments. Strategies include:

- Positioning food as sophisticated technological design (fermentation, emulsification, thermal properties)
- Using contexts beyond domestic cooking—food product development, commercial catering, food science
- Actively recruiting male students through showcasing male chefs, food scientists, and product developers
- Challenging assumptions that food is "easier" or less technical than other D&T areas

The AQA Food Preparation and Nutrition GCSE and similar specifications from other boards include substantial scientific content, yet this is often under-emphasised in school marketing.

### Engineering and Resistant Materials

The most male-dominated areas require particular attention:

- Showcasing female engineers and product designers as standard practice, not tokenistic additions
- Using contexts addressing social needs, sustainability, and user-centred design rather than mechanical problem-solving exclusively
- Ensuring workshop induction processes are genuinely inclusive, with no assumptions about prior experience
- Addressing "shop floor" culture that may exclude or marginalise female students

The new Engineering GCSE specifications (available from AQA, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) attempt to present engineering more broadly, but uptake patterns suggest limited success in achieving gender balance without deliberate intervention.

### Textiles

Overwhelmingly female-dominated, Textiles faces different equity challenges:

- Positioning textiles as high-technology (smart fabrics, technical textiles, sustainability innovation)
- Using contexts beyond fashion—medical textiles, sports engineering, aerospace applications
- Recruiting male students through showcasing diverse textile applications and designers
- Ensuring the subject isn't marginalised within D&T provision

The Fashion and Textiles specifications offered by exam boards vary in their emphasis, with OCR's Cambridge Nationals offering more explicitly technical pathways.

## Role Models and Case Studies

Effective gender equity work requires consistent use of diverse role models. Key examples include:

- **Dame Wendy Hall** (computer scientist, showing computing-technology integration)
- **Jony Ive** (product designer, but balanced with female designers like Fiona Fairhurst or Becca McCharen-Tran)
- **Marcus Samuelsson** (chef, countering food as exclusively female domain)
- **Stella McCartney** (fashion, but emphasised for sustainability technology innovation)
- **Helen Hamlyn** (industrial designer specialising in inclusive design)

David Barlex emphasises that role models should be integrated naturally throughout teaching, not segregated into tokenistic "women in engineering" lessons that paradoxically highlight otherness.

## Monitoring and Evaluation

Schools should systematically monitor:

- **Participation data**: Numbers of students by gender in each D&T pathway at Key Stages 3 and 4
- **Attainment data**: Performance by gender across specifications
- **Progression data**: Post-16 and post-18 pathways by gender
- **Enrichment participation**: Gender balance in clubs, competitions, and extension activities
- **Student voice**: Regular consultation on experiences, barriers, and suggestions

The Design and Technology Association publishes annual data on national participation patterns, allowing schools to benchmark against sector trends.

## Links to Careers and Progression

Gender equity in D&T connects directly to broader STEM equity challenges. The Engineering UK reports consistently document severe gender imbalances in engineering professions (currently around 16% female). Similarly, the fashion and food industries, whilst numerically female-dominated in some roles, show significant gender stratification with male chefs and designers disproportionately in prestigious positions.

Matt McLain's work emphasises that D&T education represents a critical intervention point. Students who experience genuinely inclusive, diverse D&T education are more likely to consider non-stereotypical career pathways. Conversely, reinforcing gender segregation in D&T contributes to pipeline problems in engineering, technology, and design industries.

## Practical Classroom Strategies: A Summary

1. **Audit all resources** for gender representation and stereotyping
2. **Diversify project contexts** systematically across all material areas
3. **Showcase diverse designers** consistently, not as special topics
4. **Use inclusive language** consciously and consistently
5. **Monitor participation and attainment** by gender explicitly
6. **Build making confidence** through scaffolding and peer support
7. **Challenge stereotypes** when they emerge from students
8. **Collaborate with careers education** to address progression pathways
9. **Ensure equitable workshop access** and induction processes
10. **Engage with research evidence** through professional development

## Conclusion

Gender equity in Design & Technology represents unfinished business for the subject community. Despite decades of awareness and effort, significant disparities persist. As Kay Stables has argued, this is not a peripheral issue but central to the subject's educational validity. A subject that claims to develop capability and prepare students for participation in technological society cannot fulfil that mission whilst perpetuating exclusionary practices.

Research evidence from David Barlex, Richard Kimbell, Stephanie Atkinson, Matt McLain, and others provides clear direction: equity requires systematic, deliberate intervention addressing curriculum, pedagogy, assessment, and institutional culture. The National Curriculum's assumption of inherent inclusivity is insufficient—teachers and curriculum leaders must actively construct equitable provision.

The stakes extend beyond D&T itself to broader questions of social justice, economic participation, and national capability in design and technology. Every teacher and department has responsibility for ensuring that all students, regardless of gender, experience D&T as a subject "for them," opening rather than closing future possibilities.
