# SEND in D&T

## Introduction

Special Educational Needs and Disability (SEND) provision in Design & Technology represents both a significant challenge and an exceptional opportunity within the UK curriculum. D&T's inherently practical, multimodal nature makes it particularly well-suited to inclusive pedagogy, yet the subject's technical demands and safety requirements necessitate careful consideration of access arrangements and differentiation strategies.

The 2014 SEND Code of Practice (DfE/DoH) establishes that all teachers are teachers of SEND pupils. In D&T, this principle acquires particular significance given the subject's requirement for manipulative skills, spatial reasoning, sustained concentration, and collaborative working. Richard Kimbell's work on 'modelling ideas' demonstrates that D&T uniquely allows pupils to demonstrate capability through practical means rather than solely through written or verbal communication, making it potentially more accessible than many other curriculum areas.

## Legal and Statutory Framework

### National Curriculum Requirements

The National Curriculum for Design & Technology (DfE, 2013) states that teachers should set high expectations for every pupil, including those with SEND. The curriculum explicitly requires teachers to plan for pupils who need additional support and to ensure full access to the curriculum through appropriate adaptations.

The Equality Act 2010 places three key duties on schools relevant to D&T provision:
- Not to treat disabled pupils less favourably
- To make reasonable adjustments for disabled pupils
- To plan to increase access to education for disabled pupils

### SEND Code of Practice Application

The graduated approach (Assess, Plan, Do, Review) applies directly to D&T teaching. Teachers must work with SENCOs to identify barriers to learning within practical contexts and implement targeted interventions. Kay Stables' research on design capability emphasises the importance of understanding individual cognitive profiles when supporting design thinking processes.

## Categories of Need in D&T Contexts

### Communication and Interaction Needs

Pupils with speech, language and communication needs (SLCN) or autism spectrum conditions (ASC) may experience specific challenges in D&T:

**Design Communication**: The requirement to explain design intentions verbally can present barriers. However, David Barlex's work on 'design methods' demonstrates that visual communication, modelling, and digital tools can provide alternative means of expression. Computer-aided design (CAD) software, annotated photographs, and video diaries offer multimodal communication opportunities.

**Collaborative Working**: Group projects can be particularly challenging for pupils with ASC. Stephanie Atkinson's research on technology education emphasises the value of structured roles within collaborative work. Providing visual timetables, clear role cards, and defined workstation boundaries supports pupils who struggle with social communication.

**Technical Vocabulary**: D&T introduces extensive specialist terminology. Pre-teaching vocabulary, providing visual glossaries, and using Widgit symbols or Makaton signs alongside technical terms supports comprehension.

### Cognition and Learning Needs

Pupils with moderate learning difficulties (MLD), severe learning difficulties (SLD), or specific learning difficulties such as dyslexia face particular challenges:

**Working Memory Demands**: Design processes require holding multiple variables in mind simultaneously. Breaking projects into smaller steps, providing visual process charts, and using physical modelling to 'externalise' thinking reduces working memory load. Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research demonstrated that hands-on modelling supports cognitive processing for all pupils, particularly those with learning difficulties.

**Sequencing and Organisation**: Multi-step manufacturing processes can overwhelm pupils with sequencing difficulties. Photographic step-by-step guides, colour-coded process cards, and one-step-at-a-time instruction sheets provide essential scaffolding.

**Reading and Writing Barriers**: Design folios traditionally require extended writing. Alternative recording methods include:
- Voice recording and transcription software
- Photographic evidence with minimal annotation
- Video documentation
- Pictorial design sheets
- Scribed responses with pupil verification

Matt McLain's work on assessment practices in D&T supports the validity of these alternative evidence forms, arguing that they can demonstrate design capability as effectively as traditional written submissions.

### Social, Emotional and Mental Health Needs

Pupils with ADHD, attachment disorders, or anxiety conditions require specific considerations:

**Impulsivity and Safety**: Workshop environments require sustained attention and impulse control. Strategies include:
- Designated 'thinking spaces' away from machinery
- Traffic light systems for equipment access
- Peer buddies trained in safety protocols
- Reduced class sizes for practical sessions
- Additional adult supervision

**Sustained Attention**: Longer project timelines can be difficult. Chunking projects into achievable sub-tasks with regular completion points maintains engagement and provides success opportunities.

**Anxiety Management**: The performance aspect of presenting design ideas can trigger anxiety. Providing alternative presentation formats (recorded presentations, written submissions, small group rather than whole-class sharing) reduces stress whilst maintaining assessment validity.

### Sensory and Physical Needs

Pupils with visual impairment (VI), hearing impairment (HI), physical disabilities, or sensory processing difficulties require environmental and task modifications:

**Physical Access**: Workshop layouts must accommodate wheelchair users and those with mobility difficulties. The Design and Technology Association (DATA, now part of the Specialist Schools and Academies Trust) provides specific guidance on accessible workshop design, including:
- Adjustable-height workbenches
- Wheelchair-accessible machinery with appropriate guards
- Adapted hand tools with modified grips
- Jigs and fixtures to stabilise work
- One-handed operation equipment

**Visual Impairment**: Pupils with VI benefit from:
- High-contrast marking on tools and materials
- Tactile templates and guides
- Verbal descriptions of visual demonstrations
- Enlarged technical drawings with bold lines
- Good task lighting without glare

**Hearing Impairment**: In noisy workshop environments, pupils with HI require:
- Visual safety signals alongside audible alarms
- Preferential seating to see teacher demonstrations
- British Sign Language (BSL) interpretation for technical content
- Written instructions to supplement verbal guidance
- Radio aid systems compatible with workshop noise

**Sensory Processing**: Some pupils experience sensory overload in workshops. Considerations include:
- Ear defenders for noise sensitivity
- Alternative materials for texture aversions
- Gradual exposure to machinery noise
- Sensory breaks during extended practical sessions

## Key Stage Specific Considerations

### Key Stage 3

At KS3, D&T introduces foundational technical knowledge and processes. The programme of study requires pupils to develop design capability across multiple material areas and technological systems.

**Baseline Assessment**: Early identification of SEND needs in D&T contexts is crucial. Kay Stables' work on capability assessment provides frameworks for identifying specific barriers within design and make activities.

**Scaffolded Design Processes**: The Nuffield Design & Technology Project (Barlex & Steeg) offers structured design activities with built-in differentiation, particularly valuable for pupils requiring additional support.

**Material Area Rotation**: Traditional rotating material areas (resistant materials, textiles, food, graphics) can be disorienting for pupils with organisational difficulties or those who thrive on routine. Some schools now offer extended units within single material areas to provide consistency.

**Homework and Independent Learning**: Extended project work requires executive function skills many SEND pupils find challenging. Providing structured homework planners, parental guidance sheets, and optional lunchtime support sessions improves completion rates.

### Key Stage 4

GCSE D&T courses from all major exam boards (AQA Design & Technology 8552, Pearson Edexcel Design & Technology 1DT0, OCR Design & Technology J310, WJEC Eduqas Design & Technology C660) include significant NEA (Non-Examined Assessment) components requiring sustained independent working.

**Access Arrangements**: The Joint Council for Qualifications (JCQ) guidance on access arrangements applies to both NEA and written examinations. Common arrangements in D&T include:
- Extra time (25% typically)
- Rest breaks
- Separate room invigilation
- Scribe or word processor for written content
- Practical assistant for physical tasks
- Prompter for organisation and focus

**NEA Adaptations**: The contextual challenges and iterative design requirements of NEA projects can be particularly demanding. Stephanie Atkinson's research on project-based learning emphasises the importance of regular checkpoint reviews and structured reflection points.

Exam boards permit reasonable adjustments to NEA provision, including:
- More frequent supervisory support (within permitted contact time)
- Pre-populated design context analysis sections
- Simplified client brief scenarios
- Modified marking criteria focusing on pupil's achieved outcomes
- Alternative recording formats with prior exam board approval

**Portfolio Development**: Extended design portfolios require sustained writing and organisation. Acceptable modifications include:
- Digital portfolios with assistive technology
- Voice-to-text software
- Reduced word count requirements (with evidence)
- Photographic evidence with concise annotation
- Structured pro-forma sheets

### Post-16 Provision

A-level Design & Technology (AQA 7552, Pearson Edexcel 9DT0, OCR H406, WJEC Eduqas A660) and vocational qualifications (BTEC Engineering, BTEC Applied Science) require higher-order design thinking and technical capability.

**Bridging Support**: The transition to advanced-level study requires explicit teaching of independent learning skills. Matt McLain's work on progression in D&T highlights the need for structured support in developing autonomous working patterns.

**Specialist Pathways**: Some pupils with SEND benefit from more focused vocational routes rather than broad A-level content. BTEC Engineering or Construction qualifications offer practical emphasis with incremental assessment.

**University and Employment Preparation**: D&T teachers play a crucial role in developing self-advocacy skills. Teaching pupils to articulate their needs and access workplace reasonable adjustments prepares them for higher education and employment.

## Pedagogical Approaches and Differentiation Strategies

### Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

The UDL framework, whilst developed in the USA, aligns well with UK inclusive practice guidance. In D&T contexts, UDL principles suggest:

**Multiple Means of Representation**: Present design briefs, technical information, and processes through varied formats (written, visual, verbal, video, physical demonstration).

**Multiple Means of Engagement**: Offer choice in project contexts, materials, and outcomes within learning objectives. Richard Kimbell's 'authentic contexts' research demonstrates that personal relevance increases engagement across all ability levels.

**Multiple Means of Expression**: Accept varied forms of evidence for design capability - not solely written portfolios but also video documentation, oral presentations, physical prototypes, and digital formats.

### Adaptive Teaching Strategies

Rather than preparing entirely separate lessons for SEND pupils, adaptive teaching involves building flexibility into mainstream planning:

**Tiered Challenges**: Provide basic, developing, and advanced versions of design challenges sharing core learning objectives. All pupils access age-appropriate technical content but complexity and independence levels vary.

**Flexible Grouping**: Strategic grouping supports peer learning whilst avoiding fixed ability groupings. David Barlex's work on collaborative design emphasises the cognitive benefits of explaining design decisions to peers.

**Responsive Teaching**: Continuous formative assessment allows real-time adjustments. In practical subjects, circulating and observing work provides immediate insight into pupil progress and difficulties.

### Metacognitive Approaches

Pupils with SEND particularly benefit from explicit teaching of thinking strategies. The Education Endowment Foundation identifies metacognition as high-impact, low-cost intervention.

In D&T contexts, metacognitive support includes:
- Thinking aloud whilst demonstrating techniques
- Providing sentence starters for design evaluation
- Teaching specific design strategies (morphological analysis, SCAMPER)
- Regular reflection activities using structured prompts
- Self-assessment against clear success criteria

Kay Stables' research on design fixation demonstrates that teaching pupils to recognise and overcome cognitive barriers improves outcomes, particularly for those with learning difficulties.

## Assessment and Evidence of Learning

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Continuous assessment is particularly important for SEND pupils who may struggle to demonstrate learning in high-stakes summative assessments.

**Practical Observation**: Direct observation of practical capability provides evidence written assessments may not capture. Richard Kimbell's APU research demonstrated that practical capability often exceeded pupils' ability to articulate their understanding.

**Photographic Evidence**: Documenting work-in-progress captures transient practical skills and provides evidence for pupils whose finished products may not fully represent their capability.

**Verbal Assessment**: Questioning pupils about their design decisions during practical work provides insight into their thinking processes. Recording these conversations creates evidence for SEND pupils who struggle with written expression.

### Summative Assessment Adaptations

**GCSE and A-level**: Access arrangements ensure SEND pupils can demonstrate their capability under examination conditions. However, Matt McLain's research on assessment validity in D&T raises important questions about whether written examinations fully capture design and technological capability.

**Alternative Accreditation**: Some pupils with significant SEND may access D&T through:
- Entry Level qualifications (Entry Level 1-3)
- ASDAN programmes incorporating design and technology
- BTEC Vocational qualifications with continuous assessment
- Component awards recognising specific technical skills

The Design and Technology Association guidance emphasises that accreditation should be aspirational whilst realistic, with careful consideration of progression routes.

## Resource and Equipment Considerations

### Adapted Tools and Equipment

Specialist equipment suppliers (such as Taskmaster, TTS, and Rompa) provide adapted tools including:
- Easy-grip scissors and cutting tools
- One-handed clamps and vices
- Bench-mounted jar openers and mixing equipment
- Weighted tools for tremor reduction
- Extended handles for reduced grip strength
- Colour-contrasted measuring equipment

### Assistive Technology

Digital tools can significantly enhance access:
- Computer-aided design (CAD) software with voice commands
- 3D printing for pupils unable to undertake traditional manufacturing
- Laser cutters with accessible software interfaces
- Text-to-speech for reading technical information
- Speech-to-text for recording design ideas
- Tablet-based design apps with accessibility features

David Barlex's work on technological capability emphasises that using assistive technology is itself a valuable design skill, not a deficit approach.

### Environmental Modifications

Workshop design should anticipate diverse needs:
- Dual-height workbenches or adjustable surfaces
- Clear circulation routes for wheelchair access
- Good lighting without glare or flicker
- Sound-absorbing materials to reduce noise levels
- Clear safety signage with symbols and text
- Designated quiet areas for planning or evaluation work

## Working with Specialists

### SENCO Collaboration

Effective SEND provision in D&T requires close partnership between subject teachers and SENCOs:

**Information Sharing**: SENCOs provide SEND registers, individual education plans (IEPs), and specific strategy recommendations. D&T teachers contribute subject-specific observations about pupils' practical capability and design thinking.

**Strategy Development**: SENCOs may suggest general strategies requiring adaptation for workshop contexts. D&T teachers provide expertise on safety implications and practical feasibility.

**Assessment Planning**: Early discussion about examination access arrangements ensures necessary evidence gathering and applications submission within JCQ deadlines.

### External Specialists

Various specialists contribute to comprehensive SEND support:

**Educational Psychologists**: Provide cognitive profiles informing differentiation strategies. Understanding working memory capacity, processing speed, and spatial reasoning ability directly informs D&T teaching approaches.

**Occupational Therapists (OTs)**: Assess physical capability and recommend adapted equipment, seating, and task modifications. OT input is particularly valuable for pupils with physical disabilities or coordination difficulties.

**Specialist Teachers**: Qualified Teachers of the Visually Impaired (QTVIs) or Qualified Teachers of the Deaf (QTODs) provide subject-specific advice for adapting D&T teaching. The National Sensory Impairment Partnership (NatSIP) offers D&T-specific guidance.

## Parental and Pupil Engagement

### Communication and Partnership

The SEND Code of Practice emphasises parental partnership and pupil voice as central to effective provision.

**Design Project Contexts**: Involving parents in identifying meaningful design contexts can increase motivation and provide home support for extended projects. Care must be taken to ensure equity for pupils from varied family circumstances.

**Progress Communication**: Parents of SEND pupils particularly value regular, specific feedback on practical capability and progress. Photographic evidence helps parents understand their child's achievements in D&T's practical domain.

**Transition Planning**: Parents are key partners in ensuring appropriate equipment, support, and access arrangements transfer between key stages or schools.

### Pupil Self-Advocacy

Teaching pupils to understand and articulate their needs develops independence:
- Explicit discussion of SEND as variation, not deficit
- Teaching pupils to request specific reasonable adjustments
- Involving pupils in reviewing support strategies
- Building independence in using assistive technology
- Preparing pupils to advocate for workplace adjustments

## Staff Development and Whole-School Approaches

### Professional Learning

Effective SEND provision requires ongoing professional development:

**Subject-Specific Training**: Generic SEND training requires translation into D&T contexts. Subject associations (such as the Design and Technology Association) provide D&T-specific SEND guidance.

**Collaborative Practice**: Observing colleagues supporting SEND pupils in practical contexts, joint planning, and team-teaching builds collective expertise.

**Action Research**: Systematic evaluation of differentiation strategies and reasonable adjustments develops evidence-informed practice. Matt McLain's work emphasises the value of practitioner research in D&T education.
