# Royal Economic Society and Economics Education

## Overview and Historical Context

The Royal Economic Society (RES), founded in 1890 as the British Economic Association, stands as the United Kingdom's oldest and most prestigious learned society dedicated to the advancement of economics. The Society received its Royal Charter in 1902 and has maintained its position as a central institution in British economic thought and education for over 130 years. The RES serves approximately 3,500 members worldwide, with a significant proportion being UK-based economists working in universities, schools, government departments, and the private sector.

The Society's mission extends beyond academic research to encompass the broader dissemination of economic understanding throughout UK education. This commitment manifests through various initiatives that directly impact economics teaching from Key Stage 4 through to undergraduate and postgraduate levels.

## The Economics Network and Educational Resources

### The Economics Network Partnership

The RES works closely with the Economics Network, formerly hosted by the University of Bristol and now part of the Higher Education Academy's subject centre provision. This partnership has produced substantial resources for economics educators, particularly those teaching A-Level Economics across AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC specifications.

The collaboration addresses the critical challenge identified by researchers including Peter Smith, who has extensively documented the transition difficulties students face between A-Level and undergraduate economics, particularly regarding mathematical rigour and analytical depth.

### Teacher Training and Professional Development

The RES supports continuous professional development (CPD) for economics teachers through:

- Annual conferences featuring sessions specifically designed for secondary school educators
- Workshops on pedagogy and assessment aligned with current examination requirements
- Resources addressing the teaching of quantitative skills, responding to the enhanced mathematical content introduced across all major exam boards from 2015 onwards

Jim Riley, founder of tutor2u and a prominent figure in A-Level Economics education, has collaborated with RES initiatives to bridge academic economics and classroom practice, ensuring that research-informed teaching methods reach practicing teachers.

## Publications and Academic Resources

### The Economic Journal

The *Economic Journal*, published by the RES since 1891, represents one of the world's leading peer-reviewed economics publications. While primarily aimed at academic researchers, it provides valuable material for advanced A-Level students and serves as an essential resource for teachers maintaining their subject knowledge.

The journal's accessibility has increased significantly with digital publication, and selected articles are recommended for use with high-attaining Year 13 students, particularly those considering economics degrees.

### Economic Outlook Series

The RES regularly publishes accessible analyses of current economic conditions, policy debates, and structural changes in the UK economy. These publications prove invaluable for teachers needing contemporary case studies and real-world applications of economic theory, directly supporting the requirements of specifications across all exam boards that emphasise contextual understanding.

## Impact on UK Economics Curriculum

### National Curriculum and Key Stage 4

Whilst economics is not a statutory National Curriculum subject, the RES influences the development of GCSE Economics through its consultation responses to Ofqual and examination boards. The Society has consistently advocated for:

- Rigorous treatment of fundamental economic concepts including scarcity, opportunity cost, and market mechanisms
- Introduction of quantitative methods appropriate to Key Stage 4 students
- Real-world applications connecting economic theory to students' lived experiences

Nancy Wall, a former Chief Examiner and prominent economics educator, has emphasised the importance of RES guidance in maintaining academic standards whilst ensuring accessibility for the 14-16 age range.

### A-Level Economics Specifications

The 2015 reforms to A-Level Economics across all examination boards reflected significant RES input. The Society advocated successfully for:

**Enhanced Mathematical Content**: All current specifications (AQA 7135/7136, Edexcel 9EC0, OCR H060/H460, WJEC Eduqas A-Level Economics) now require students to demonstrate mathematical skills equivalent to at least Higher Tier GCSE Mathematics, including:
- Calculation and interpretation of index numbers
- Construction and interpretation of graphs including complex supply and demand diagrams
- Percentage calculations and percentage changes
- Understanding of statistical concepts including mean, median, and correlation

**Increased Emphasis on Analysis and Evaluation**: The RES supported the move toward assessment objectives rewarding higher-order thinking skills, with AO3 (Analysis) and AO4 (Evaluation) now constituting 50% of available marks across specifications.

**Synoptic Assessment**: The requirement for Paper 3 synoptic assessments reflects RES advocacy for integrated economic thinking, requiring students to draw connections across microeconomics, macroeconomics, and (where applicable) development economics.

## Research and Evidence-Based Practice

### The Economics Teaching Research Evidence

The RES has supported and disseminated research examining effective economics pedagogy. Key findings influencing UK classroom practice include:

**Active Learning Approaches**: Research published through RES channels demonstrates that active learning strategies—including simulations, debates, and problem-based learning—produce superior outcomes compared to traditional lecture-based instruction, particularly for complex concepts such as market failure and macroeconomic policy trade-offs.

**Threshold Concepts**: The Society has promoted research on threshold concepts in economics education, identifying that certain ideas (opportunity cost, marginalism, equilibrium, and elasticity) represent "portals" to economic thinking. Peter Smith's work on threshold concepts has influenced how these fundamental ideas are sequenced and reinforced throughout A-Level specifications.

**Quantitative Skills Development**: Given persistent evidence of student difficulties with mathematical content, the RES has championed explicit, scaffolded teaching of quantitative methods integrated within economic contexts rather than taught separately.

## Ha-Joon Chang and Pluralist Economics

The RES, whilst maintaining its position as a professional society for economists across methodological perspectives, has engaged with debates surrounding pluralism in economics education. Ha-Joon Chang's influential work, particularly *Economics: The User's Guide* (2014), has challenged conventional approaches to teaching introductory economics.

Chang argues for exposing students to multiple schools of economic thought—classical, Keynesian, behavioural, institutional, and developmental economics—rather than presenting a single neoclassical orthodoxy. The RES's position acknowledges the value of methodological diversity whilst maintaining that students require solid grounding in core analytical frameworks.

This debate has practical implications for A-Level teaching. Whilst examination specifications remain primarily rooted in neoclassical microeconomics and Keynesian-neoclassical synthesis macroeconomics, effective teachers increasingly incorporate:

- Behavioural economics (now explicitly included in AQA, Edexcel, and OCR specifications)
- Critical evaluation of economic assumptions (homo economicus, perfect information, rationality)
- Historical context for economic theories
- Alternative perspectives on development, inequality, and environmental sustainability

## Resources for Teachers

### Alain Anderton's Economics Textbooks

Alain Anderton's comprehensive textbooks, particularly *Economics for A Level*, have achieved near-ubiquitous status in UK economics education. Anderton has collaborated with the RES in developing supplementary materials and has incorporated RES research findings into successive editions of his texts.

Anderton's approach exemplifies RES principles:
- Extensive use of real-world data and case studies
- Clear exposition of economic theory with progressive complexity
- Integration of evaluation skills throughout
- Comprehensive coverage of all major examination specifications

### Digital Resources and Online Platforms

The RES has embraced digital technology to extend its educational reach:

**Research Digest**: Regular summaries of significant economics research translated for classroom use, enabling teachers to incorporate cutting-edge findings without requiring specialist knowledge.

**Data Resources**: Curated links to reliable economic data sources including the Office for National Statistics, Bank of England, and international organisations, supporting the data skills requirements across all A-Level specifications.

**Video Content**: Partnerships with organisations including the Institute for Fiscal Studies and Centre for Economic Policy Research to produce accessible video explanations of current economic issues.

## Policy Influence and Educational Advocacy

### Government Consultation Responses

The RES regularly submits evidence-based responses to government consultations affecting economics education:

- **Treasury Select Committee**: Evidence on economic literacy and the case for broader economics education
- **Department for Education**: Input on curriculum reforms, assessment practices, and teacher recruitment
- **Ofqual**: Technical advice on examination standards and assessment validity

### Promoting Economics Education in Schools

The Society actively campaigns to increase economics uptake at GCSE and A-Level, addressing the significant regional and socio-economic disparities in access. Research shows that students from disadvantaged backgrounds and those attending schools outside the South East and London have substantially reduced access to economics qualifications.

RES initiatives addressing this inequality include:

- Supporting taster sessions and enrichment activities in underserved areas
- Providing teaching resources accessible to non-specialist teachers
- Advocating for economics inclusion in initial teacher training programmes

## Teacher Subject Knowledge and Qualification Routes

### The Economics Teacher Shortage

A persistent challenge in UK economics education is the shortage of qualified specialist teachers. Unlike mathematics or English, economics is not a National Curriculum subject, resulting in limited initial teacher training provision. The RES has documented that approximately 40% of A-Level economics teachers lack a degree in economics or a closely related discipline.

### RES Support for Subject Knowledge Enhancement

The Society addresses this gap through:

**Subject Knowledge Resources**: Comprehensive guides covering fundamental economic concepts, graphical analysis, and quantitative methods, specifically designed for non-specialist teachers entering economics education.

**Academic-Teacher Partnerships**: Initiatives connecting serving teachers with university economics departments for mentoring, research access, and subject knowledge updating.

**Conference Strand for Teachers**: The annual RES Conference includes dedicated sessions where academic economists present research findings in formats directly applicable to classroom teaching.

## International Perspectives and Comparative Education

The RES maintains connections with equivalent organisations globally, including the American Economic Association and European Economic Association, facilitating comparative study of economics education approaches. This international perspective proves valuable for UK teachers preparing students for increasingly globalised higher education and employment.

Research shared through RES channels highlights distinctive features of UK economics education:

- Earlier specialisation compared to broad-based approaches in continental Europe and North America
- Greater emphasis on written evaluation skills compared to multiple-choice assessment prevalent elsewhere
- Stronger integration of current policy debates into examination content

## Practical Implications for Classroom Teachers

### Effective Use of RES Resources

Teachers can maximise RES resources by:

1. **Regular Review of *Economic Journal* Abstracts**: Even without full article access, abstracts provide insight into current research directions, informing teaching that extends beyond textbooks.

2. **Incorporating RES Public Engagement Content**: Using accessible RES publications and media appearances to provide authoritative perspectives on current economic debates, supporting evaluation skills development.

3. **Attending RES Events**: Where possible, participating in RES conferences or regional events provides invaluable networking opportunities and exposure to cutting-edge economic thinking.

4. **Utilising RES Data Resources**: Incorporating data from RES-recommended sources enhances quantitative skills teaching and provides current, reliable evidence for economic arguments.

### Addressing Common Misconceptions

RES-supported research has identified persistent student misconceptions that teachers should explicitly address:

- **Conflation of wealth and income**: Research shows students frequently confuse stock and flow concepts
- **Misunderstanding of inflation**: Many students believe inflation means prices are high rather than increasing
- **Confusion about government debt and deficit**: The distinction requires explicit teaching and reinforcement
- **Oversimplified understanding of unemployment**: Students often fail to grasp different unemployment types and measurement challenges

## Assessment and Examination Practice

### Mark Scheme Development

The RES contributes expertise to examination board mark scheme development, ensuring that economic literacy standards remain rigorous and consistent. This involvement particularly benefits:

- **Essay Questions**: Ensuring mark schemes reward sophisticated economic analysis rather than mechanical application of mark-scheme terms
- **Data Response Questions**: Validating that questions test genuine analytical skills rather than simple data description
- **Quantitative Questions**: Confirming mathematical demands are appropriate and economically meaningful

### Supporting Student Progression

The RES research programme on student progression from A-Level to undergraduate study provides actionable insights for teachers:

**Mathematical Preparation**: Students require explicit practice with algebraic manipulation, not merely arithmetic, to succeed in undergraduate economics. A-Level teachers should extend beyond specification minima where students show aptitude.

**Independent Study Skills**: Research indicates A-Level students accustomed to structured resources (revision guides, model answers) struggle with the independent reading and research required at university. Progressive development of independent study throughout Years 12 and 13 supports transition.

**Critical Thinking**: Undergraduate success correlates strongly with ability to critique economic arguments and recognise limitations of models—skills requiring systematic development throughout A-Level study.

## Future Directions and Emerging Issues

### Digital Economics Education

The RES increasingly focuses on digital innovation in economics teaching, particularly following accelerated technology adoption during COVID-19 disruption. Emerging priorities include:

- Validating effectiveness of online learning platforms
- Developing interactive simulations of economic concepts
- Creating digital assessment methods complementing traditional examinations

### Responding to Contemporary Economic Challenges

The RES supports teachers addressing evolving economic realities including climate change economics, digitalisation impacts, and post-pandemic policy challenges. These contemporary issues increasingly feature in examination questions, requiring teacher subject knowledge to remain current.

### Diversity and Inclusion in Economics

The RES has prioritised increasing diversity within the economics profession, recognising that limited diversity among economists potentially narrows the range of questions asked and perspectives considered. Educational initiatives supporting this goal include:

- Highlighting contributions of economists from underrepresented groups
- Addressing unconscious bias in teaching materials and case study selection
- Supporting access to economics education for students from all backgrounds

## Conclusion

The Royal Economic Society represents an indispensable resource for UK economics education, bridging academic research and classroom practice. For teachers, engagement with RES resources, research, and initiatives provides access to authoritative economic thinking, evidence-based pedagogical approaches, and professional development opportunities that enhance teaching quality and student outcomes across Key Stage 4, Key Stage 5, and into higher education.

The Society's influence permeates UK economics education through examination specification development, textbook quality assurance, teacher professional development, and advocacy for economics education accessibility. Teachers who actively engage with RES resources demonstrate enhanced subject knowledge, employ evidence-based teaching strategies, and better prepare students for both examination success and economic literacy as engaged citizens.
