# International Economics and Trade

## Overview and Curriculum Context

International Economics and Trade forms a critical component of A-level Economics curricula across all UK examination boards. This area examines how nations interact economically through trade in goods and services, capital flows, exchange rates, and international economic institutions. The subject bridges microeconomic principles of comparative advantage with macroeconomic concerns about balance of payments, globalisation, and economic development.

### National Curriculum and Specification Coverage

All major UK exam boards include substantial coverage of international economics:

- **AQA A-level Economics (7136)**: Section 2.4 covers 'The International Economy', including globalisation, trade policy, balance of payments, and exchange rates
- **Edexcel A-level Economics A (9EC0)**: Theme 4 addresses 'A Global Perspective' with specific focus on international trade and trading blocs
- **OCR A-level Economics (H460)**: Component 2.4.5 covers 'The Global Context' including trade theories and protectionism
- **WJEC/Eduqas A-level Economics**: Unit 4 examines 'The Global Economy' including comparative advantage and globalisation

This topic typically represents 20-25% of A-level examination content, making it essential for comprehensive subject knowledge development.

## Theoretical Foundations of International Trade

### Absolute and Comparative Advantage

The principle of **comparative advantage**, originally developed by David Ricardo in 1817, remains the cornerstone of international trade theory. Peter Smith, in his authoritative textbooks used widely in UK schools, emphasises that comparative advantage demonstrates how nations benefit from specialisation even when one country has absolute advantage in producing all goods.

**Alain Anderton**, whose textbooks are endorsed by multiple UK exam boards, presents comparative advantage through clear numerical examples that help students understand opportunity cost calculations. Teachers should ensure students can:

- Calculate opportunity cost ratios from production possibility data
- Identify which country should specialise in which good
- Explain the gains from trade using pre-trade and post-trade consumption points
- Evaluate assumptions underlying the model (constant costs, no transport costs, perfect factor mobility)

**Practical teaching implications**: Use contemporary examples such as UK-EU trade in financial services versus German manufacturing, or Bangladesh garment production versus UK pharmaceutical exports. Jim Riley's Tutor2u resources provide excellent real-world case studies regularly updated with current trade data.

### Limitations and Extensions of Trade Theory

**Ha-Joon Chang**, the Cambridge economist, offers important critical perspectives on traditional trade theory that UK teachers should incorporate. In "Bad Samaritans" (2008) and "Kicking Away the Ladder" (2002), Chang argues that developed nations achieved prosperity through protectionist policies, contradicting simplistic free trade advocacy. This provides valuable evaluation material for higher-level students preparing A-level essays.

Modern trade theory extends beyond comparative advantage to include:

- **Heckscher-Ohlin theory**: Trade patterns based on factor endowments (land, labour, capital)
- **New trade theory** (Paul Krugman): Economies of scale and network effects explain intra-industry trade
- **Porter's Diamond Model**: Competitive advantage stems from domestic conditions, supporting industries, and firm strategy

Nancy Wall's examination materials frequently test student understanding of how these theories explain real-world trade patterns that simple comparative advantage cannot address, such as why similar countries trade similar products.

## Terms of Trade and Trading Relationships

### Understanding Terms of Trade

The **terms of trade** index measures the ratio of export prices to import prices (Export Price Index ÷ Import Price Index × 100). Improvement in terms of trade means a country can purchase more imports for each unit of exports.

Anderton's textbooks emphasise that changes in terms of trade affect:

- Real national income and living standards
- Current account balance
- Distribution of income between trading partners

**Practical teaching application**: Use UK Office for National Statistics (ONS) data showing how sterling depreciation after the 2016 referendum worsened UK terms of trade. Students should practise calculating terms of trade indices and interpreting their movements.

The **Prebisch-Singer hypothesis** suggests developing countries face declining terms of trade over time because primary commodity prices fall relative to manufactured goods. This controversial theory remains relevant for evaluating development strategies and appears regularly in exam questions about trade and development.

## Barriers to Trade and Protectionism

### Types of Protectionist Measures

UK exam specifications require detailed knowledge of protectionist instruments:

1. **Tariffs**: Taxes on imports raising domestic price and creating deadweight welfare loss
2. **Quotas**: Physical limits on import quantities
3. **Subsidies**: Government payments to domestic producers reducing costs
4. **Non-tariff barriers**: Regulations, standards, administrative procedures

Jim Riley's teaching resources effectively demonstrate tariff analysis using supply and demand diagrams showing:

- Consumer surplus loss (area under demand curve)
- Producer surplus gain (area above supply curve)
- Government revenue (tariff rate × quantity imported)
- Deadweight welfare loss (triangular areas representing inefficiency)

### Arguments For and Against Protection

**Arguments supporting protectionism** that students must understand:

- **Infant industry protection**: Temporary support allows emerging industries to achieve economies of scale (strongly advocated by Ha-Joon Chang)
- **Strategic industries**: National security requires domestic production capacity
- **Prevention of dumping**: Protection against predatory pricing from foreign firms
- **Protection of employment**: Maintaining jobs in declining industries
- **Correction of market failure**: Addressing environmental or social costs not reflected in prices

**Arguments against protectionism**:

- Allocative inefficiency from resource misallocation
- Productive inefficiency from reduced competitive pressure
- Retaliation risks triggering trade wars
- Higher prices for consumers reducing real incomes
- Rent-seeking behaviour and government failure

Peter Smith's textbooks provide excellent case study material examining EU Common Agricultural Policy, US steel tariffs (2002 and 2018), and China-US trade tensions, all valuable for contextualising theoretical arguments.

**Practical teaching recommendation**: Debate activities work well for this topic. Assign students different stakeholder perspectives (consumers, domestic producers, foreign exporters, government) to argue protectionist policies' merits.

## Trading Blocs and Regional Integration

### Types of Economic Integration

UK specifications distinguish between integration levels:

1. **Free Trade Area**: Members eliminate tariffs between themselves (e.g., EFTA)
2. **Customs Union**: Free trade area plus common external tariff (EU)
3. **Common Market**: Customs union plus free movement of labour and capital
4. **Economic Union**: Common market plus harmonised economic policies (EU)
5. **Monetary Union**: Economic union with single currency (Eurozone)

### Trade Creation versus Trade Diversion

Students must understand Viner's distinction:

- **Trade creation**: Production shifts from high-cost domestic producers to lower-cost partner producers (welfare-enhancing)
- **Trade diversion**: Production shifts from low-cost external producers to higher-cost partner producers (welfare-reducing)

The UK's departure from the EU provides contemporary relevance. Nancy Wall's recent examination papers frequently assess understanding of Brexit's trade implications through questions requiring application of these concepts.

### Regional Trade Agreements

Key trading blocs for UK curriculum consideration:

- **European Union**: The UK's former membership and current Trade and Cooperation Agreement
- **USMCA** (formerly NAFTA): North American integration
- **ASEAN**: Southeast Asian cooperation
- **African Continental Free Trade Area**: Emerging integration
- **CPTPP**: Comprehensive and Progressive Trans-Pacific Partnership (UK joined 2023)

Anderton's textbooks examine how these arrangements affect trade patterns, FDI flows, and economic development, providing essential case study material.

## Balance of Payments

### Structure and Components

The UK's balance of payments records all transactions between residents and non-residents. All exam boards require understanding of:

**Current Account** comprising:
- Trade in goods (visible trade)
- Trade in services (invisible trade)
- Primary income (investment income, compensation of employees)
- Secondary income (transfers without quid pro quo)

**Capital Account**: Capital transfers and non-produced, non-financial assets

**Financial Account**:
- Foreign direct investment (FDI)
- Portfolio investment
- Other investment
- Reserve assets

The **balancing item** accounts for errors and omissions, necessary because sources differ for recording credits and debits.

### Current Account Deficits and Surpluses

The UK has run persistent current account deficits since 1984, particularly relevant for teaching context. Peter Smith emphasises that current account positions require careful interpretation:

**Causes of current account deficits**:
- Low international competitiveness
- Strong exchange rate making exports expensive
- High consumer spending on imports
- Strong domestic growth relative to trading partners
- Declining comparative advantage in certain sectors

**Consequences of deficits**:
- Downward pressure on exchange rates
- Rising foreign ownership of domestic assets
- Potential debt sustainability issues if persistent
- Reduced aggregate demand (import leakage)

**Evaluation considerations** that Jim Riley emphasises:
- Deficits financed by FDI inflows may be sustainable and beneficial
- Investment-driven deficits differ from consumption-driven deficits
- Deficit size relative to GDP matters more than absolute value
- Current account balances globally must sum to zero

### Policies to Address Imbalances

**Expenditure-reducing policies**: Deflationary fiscal or monetary policy decreasing income and import demand

**Expenditure-switching policies**: Exchange rate depreciation or supply-side measures improving competitiveness

Anderton's texts provide detailed analysis of Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve effects, essential for evaluating exchange rate policy effectiveness.

## Exchange Rates

### Determination and Systems

UK specifications require understanding of:

**Floating exchange rates**: Market-determined by supply and demand for currency
- Clean float: No government intervention
- Managed float: Occasional intervention (UK system since 1992)

**Fixed exchange rates**: Government maintains specific rate through intervention
- Requires foreign exchange reserves
- Historical examples: Bretton Woods (1944-1971), ERM (1990-1992)

**Factors affecting exchange rate**:
- Interest rate differentials (covered and uncovered interest parity)
- Inflation rate differentials (purchasing power parity)
- Current account position
- Capital flows and speculation
- Government intervention
- Political stability and confidence

### Exchange Rate Effects on Economy

**Appreciation effects**:
- Exports more expensive (quantity demanded falls)
- Imports cheaper (quantity demanded rises)
- Reduces inflationary pressure (imported raw materials cheaper)
- Worsens current account (subject to Marshall-Lerner condition)
- Reduces international competitiveness

**Depreciation effects**: Reverse of above, but effectiveness depends on price elasticities of demand for exports and imports.

The **Marshall-Lerner condition** states depreciation improves current account only if sum of export and import price elasticities exceeds one. Nancy Wall's mark schemes frequently award high marks to students who reference this condition when evaluating exchange rate policy.

The **J-curve effect** describes short-run current account worsening following depreciation (contracts already agreed at old prices) before eventual improvement (volumes adjust). Teachers should use graphical illustration with UK data following 2016 sterling depreciation.

## Globalisation

### Characteristics and Drivers

**Ha-Joon Chang** defines globalisation as increasing interconnectedness through trade, investment, people, and ideas. Key characteristics include:

- Increased international trade as proportion of GDP
- Growth of multinational corporations (MNCs)
- International capital mobility
- Technology transfer and knowledge flows
- Cultural convergence and global brands

**Drivers of globalisation** that Anderton identifies:

- **Technological change**: Containerisation, internet, communications
- **Political change**: Collapse of Soviet Union, China's market reforms
- **Trade liberalisation**: WTO negotiations, regional agreements
- **Financial deregulation**: Removal of capital controls
- **Transport costs**: Declining costs enabling complex supply chains

### Consequences of Globalisation

**Benefits argued by mainstream economists**:
- Exploitation of comparative advantage raises global output
- Increased competition drives efficiency and innovation
- Access to larger markets enables economies of scale
- Technology and knowledge transfer accelerates development
- Consumer benefits from lower prices and greater variety
- Foreign direct investment brings capital to developing countries

**Criticisms from economists including Ha-Joon Chang**:
- Increased inequality within and between countries
- Vulnerability to global shocks (2008 financial crisis, COVID-19)
- Environmental degradation from increased production and transport
- Loss of policy autonomy for national governments
- Exploitation of workers in developing countries
- Dominance of Western cultural values
- "Race to the bottom" in labour and environmental standards

Peter Smith's textbooks effectively present both perspectives, essential for developing evaluative skills. Teachers should encourage students to use evidence from specific countries and sectors rather than generalised assertions.

## Multinational Corporations

### Role and Impact

MNCs are businesses operating in multiple countries through FDI. Jim Riley's resources emphasise their significant global economic role:

- World's largest 500 MNCs account for over 50% of global trade
- Top MNCs have revenues exceeding many countries' GDP
- Control global supply chains and value chains

**Benefits to host countries**:
- Capital investment and job creation
- Technology transfer and skills development
- Increased tax revenue
- Improved infrastructure
- Export earnings and current account improvement
- Multiplier effects through local supply chains

**Costs to host countries**:
- Profit repatriation reducing domestic income
- Tax avoidance through transfer pricing
- Monopoly power exploitation
- Environmental damage
- Cultural imperialism
- Vulnerability to relocation decisions
- Dependency reducing domestic enterprise

**Evaluation**: Ha-Joon Chang argues MNC impact depends critically on host country policies. Successful developing countries (South Korea, Taiwan) imposed performance requirements on MNCs rather than accepting unconditional entry.

## International Competitiveness

### Measurement and Determinants

**International competitiveness** refers to a country's ability to sell goods and services in international markets. Nancy Wall's examination materials assess understanding through:

**Price competitiveness measures**:
- Relative unit labour costs
- Relative export prices
- Real exchange rate indices

**Non-price competitiveness factors**:
- Product quality and design
- Branding and marketing
- After-sales service
- Reliability and delivery times
- Innovation and technological sophistication

**Policies to improve competitiveness** align with supply-side economics:

- **Education and training**: Improving labour productivity
- **Infrastructure investment**: Reducing business costs
- **R&D support**: Encouraging innovation
- **Deregulation**: Reducing bureaucratic burdens
- **Labour market flexibility**: Enabling efficient resource allocation
- **Corporate tax competition**: Attracting FDI

Peter Smith emphasises the **Porter Diamond Model** for A-level students, explaining competitive advantage through factor conditions, demand conditions, related and supporting industries, and firm strategy/rivalry.

## World Trade Organization and Global Governance

### WTO Functions and Principles

The WTO (established 1995, successor to GATT) promotes trade liberalisation through:

- **Non-discrimination**: Most Favoured Nation and National Treatment principles
- **Reciprocity**: Mutual tariff reductions
- **Binding commitments**: Tariff ceilings cannot be exceeded
- **Transparency**: Publication of trade policies
- **Dispute settlement**: Arbitration mechanism

**Doha Development Round** (launched 2001) remains incomplete due to disagreements over agricultural subsidies and developing country exemptions. Anderton's textbooks explain how this illustrates tensions between developed and developing countries.

### Other International Economic Institutions

**International Monetary Fund (IMF)**: 
- Promotes exchange rate stability and monetary cooperation
- Provides loans with conditionality to countries facing balance of payments crises
- Criticized for imposing austerity conditions (Ha-Joon Chang)

**World Bank**:
- Provides development loans for infrastructure and poverty reduction
- Shifted focus towards governance and institutional development

**United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD)**:
- Represents developing country interests
- Advocates policy space for industrial development

## Practical Teaching Strategies

### Data Skills and Application

International economics provides excellent opportunities for quantitative skills development required by all UK exam boards:

- Calculating terms of trade indices from ONS data
- Interpreting exchange rate graphs using Bank of England databases
- Analysing balance of payments components from UK economic statistics
- Using OECD data to compare trade openness across countries
- Evaluating World Bank development indicators

Jim Riley's Tutor2u resources include regularly updated economic data presentations, particularly useful for current examples.

### Evaluation Skills Development

Strong evaluation distinguishes high-achieving A-level students. Nancy Wall's mark schemes reward:

- **Contextual evaluation**: Answers depending on country development level, size, resource endowment
- **Time period consideration**: Short-run versus long-run effects (especially exchange rates)
- **Elasticity-dependent conclusions**: Marshall-Lerner condition application
- **Policy objective conflicts**: Trade-offs between growth, inflation, employment
- **Stakeholder analysis**: Winners and losers from policy changes
