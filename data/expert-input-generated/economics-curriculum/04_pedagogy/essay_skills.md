# Extended Writing and Evaluation Skills

## Overview and Importance in Economics Education

Extended writing and evaluation represent the pinnacle of economic thinking in UK secondary education. These skills enable students to demonstrate sophisticated understanding of economic concepts, synthesise complex arguments, and make reasoned judgements about economic issues. As Alain Anderton notes in his *Economics* textbook series, evaluation separates students who can recall economic theory from those who can apply it meaningfully to real-world situations.

The ability to evaluate economic arguments is explicitly required across all UK examination specifications at GCSE and A-Level. AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas all allocate significant marks—typically 25-40% of total assessment—to evaluation skills, making this a critical area for teacher focus.

## National Curriculum and Examination Framework

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

At GCSE level, extended writing typically manifests in questions worth 6-12 marks. Students are expected to:

- Explain economic concepts with developed chains of reasoning
- Apply knowledge to unfamiliar contexts
- Make judgements supported by economic analysis
- Consider different stakeholder perspectives

The GCSE Economics specifications (revised 2017) emphasise the importance of "making reasoned judgements" and "weighing up alternative views." Edexcel's GCSE specification explicitly requires students to "evaluate economic arguments and use qualitative and quantitative evidence to support their own reasoned arguments and conclusions."

### Key Stage 5 (A-Level)

A-Level Economics demands considerably more sophisticated evaluation. Peter Smith, in his influential *AQA Economics* teaching resources, identifies evaluation as accounting for approximately 30% of available marks at A-Level. Essay questions (typically 25 marks) and data response evaluation questions (15-20 marks) require students to:

- Construct sustained arguments over extended responses (typically 400-600 words)
- Integrate multiple economic concepts and theories
- Assess the validity of economic models and their assumptions
- Consider time lags, unintended consequences, and dynamic effects
- Make prioritised judgements with clear criteria

## Theoretical Foundations of Evaluation in Economics

### What Constitutes Evaluation?

Jim Riley, through his influential *tutor2u* materials widely used across UK schools, defines economic evaluation as "making a reasoned judgement about the significance or importance of something." This extends beyond mere description or explanation to require critical assessment.

Nancy Wall's work on assessment in economics education emphasises that evaluation involves:

1. **Critical distance**: Standing back from economic arguments to assess their validity
2. **Consideration of alternatives**: Examining competing viewpoints or policy options
3. **Contextual awareness**: Recognising how circumstances affect conclusions
4. **Reasoned judgement**: Reaching defensible conclusions based on evidence

### Taxonomies of Thinking in Economics

Evaluation sits at the apex of Bloom's Revised Taxonomy, requiring students to judge, critique, and synthesise. In economics specifically, this translates to assessing:

- The validity of economic models and their assumptions
- The appropriateness of policy interventions
- Trade-offs between competing objectives
- Short-run versus long-run effects
- Equity implications alongside efficiency considerations

Ha-Joon Chang's work, particularly *Economics: The User's Guide*, provides valuable perspective for UK teachers by emphasising that economic evaluation must recognise multiple schools of thought and the contested nature of economic theory—an approach increasingly reflected in reformed A-Level specifications.

## Pedagogical Approaches to Teaching Extended Writing

### Building Foundations: Key Stage 4

**Structured Frameworks**

At GCSE level, teachers should introduce structured evaluation frameworks explicitly. The widely-used **PEEL** structure (Point, Evidence, Explain, Link) helps students construct coherent paragraphs, whilst the **DEED** approach (Define, Explain, Example, Develop) supports economic analysis.

For evaluation specifically, Alain Anderton's resources suggest the **IDEA** framework:
- **I**dentify the argument or policy
- **D**evelop analysis of its effects
- **E**valuate through considering limitations, alternatives, or context
- **A**ssess overall by making a reasoned judgement

**Scaffolding Techniques**

Research from the Economics and Business Education Association (EBEA) demonstrates that explicit scaffolding significantly improves student performance in extended writing. Effective approaches include:

- **Sentence starters**: Providing evaluative stems such as "However, this depends on...", "In contrast...", "This would be more effective if..."
- **Writing frames**: Templates that model evaluation structure whilst allowing student independence
- **Model answers**: Teacher exemplars with explicit annotation of evaluation phrases and techniques

### Developing Sophistication: Key Stage 5

**Chains of Reasoning**

Peter Smith emphasises the importance of extended chains of reasoning at A-Level. Rather than isolated points, students must develop arguments through multiple logical steps. For example, when evaluating supply-side policies:

"Tax cuts may increase incentives to work (Level 2 analysis) → leading to increased labour supply (Level 3 analysis) → potentially lowering wage inflation (Level 4 analysis) → **however** (evaluation) this depends on the elasticity of labour supply, which may be relatively inelastic in the short run due to skill constraints and geographical immobility."

**Types of Evaluation at A-Level**

Jim Riley's taxonomy identifies several distinct evaluation types that students must master:

1. **Assumption-based evaluation**: Questioning the assumptions underlying economic models (e.g., perfect information, rational behaviour)

2. **Context-dependent evaluation**: Recognising how conclusions vary with circumstances (elasticity values, stage of business cycle, development level)

3. **Time-frame evaluation**: Distinguishing short-run from long-run effects (particularly crucial in macroeconomics)

4. **Stakeholder evaluation**: Considering winners and losers from economic changes

5. **Comparative evaluation**: Weighing alternative policies or approaches

6. **Priority evaluation**: Establishing criteria for judgement and applying them

### The Role of Economic Frameworks

**Evaluative Concepts**

Certain economic concepts inherently support evaluation. Teachers should explicitly teach these as evaluative tools:

- **Elasticity**: Determines magnitude of effects and policy effectiveness
- **Time lags**: Recognition lag, implementation lag, impact lag
- **Trade-offs**: Particularly equity-efficiency and macroeconomic policy objectives
- **Externalities and market failure**: Questioning whether markets achieve optimal outcomes
- **Ceteris paribus**: Recognising what happens when "other things" don't remain equal

**Data and Evidence**

Nancy Wall's research on economics assessment emphasises that high-quality evaluation at A-Level must engage with evidence. The data response questions now mandatory in all A-Level specifications require students to:

- Extract relevant information from data sources
- Identify trends, patterns, and anomalies
- Use evidence to support evaluative arguments
- Recognise limitations of data (reliability, validity, timeliness)

## Examination Board Requirements

### AQA Specifications

AQA A-Level Economics explicitly assesses evaluation through:
- **Essay questions** (25 marks): Assessment Objective 3 (AO3) accounts for 12 marks specifically for evaluation
- **Context data response** (25 marks): Requiring evaluation in parts (d) and (e)
- Mark schemes reward "sustained evaluation" with well-developed evaluative themes

AQA's mark scheme descriptors indicate Level 5 evaluation (the highest) requires:
- Supported judgements
- Effective use of evidence
- Prioritised consideration of alternative viewpoints
- Recognition of the significance of key factors

### Edexcel Specifications

Edexcel (Pearson) emphasises evaluation through:
- **Theme essays** (25 marks each): Final 10-12 marks awarded for evaluation quality
- Integration of "discuss" and "assess" command words requiring explicit evaluation
- Emphasis on chains of reasoning that extend analysis before evaluation

Edexcel mark schemes particularly reward students who evaluate throughout their response rather than treating evaluation as a separate concluding section.

### OCR Specifications

OCR Economics A-Level requires:
- **Extended response questions** assessing all Assessment Objectives simultaneously
- Explicit evaluation of economic arguments and policies
- Recognition of competing perspectives in economic debate

OCR's approach particularly emphasises Ha-Joon Chang's point about methodological pluralism—students are rewarded for acknowledging that different economic perspectives yield different policy conclusions.

### WJEC Eduqas

WJEC specifications for Wales specifically require:
- Essay questions worth 24 marks with substantial evaluation component
- Integration of Welsh and UK economic examples where appropriate
- Assessment of "judgements about the significance of economic issues"

## Practical Teaching Strategies

### Sequencing Extended Writing Instruction

**Year 12 (First Year of A-Level)**

1. **Term 1**: Introduce evaluation concepts explicitly; use structured frameworks; focus on single evaluative points
2. **Term 2**: Develop chains of reasoning; require multiple evaluative points; introduce comparative evaluation
3. **Term 3**: Expect sustained evaluation throughout responses; remove scaffolding gradually

**Year 13 (Second Year of A-Level)**

1. **Term 1**: Emphasise prioritised evaluation; require judgement with explicit criteria
2. **Term 2**: Integrate multiple types of evaluation; expect synthesis of micro and macroeconomics
3. **Term 3**: Full independent extended writing with sophisticated, nuanced evaluation

### Marking and Feedback Strategies

Research from the Economics Network (part of the Higher Education Academy) demonstrates that effective feedback on extended writing should:

**Focus on Improvement Not Just Assessment**

Rather than simply awarding marks, teachers should:
- Identify the highest level achieved for each Assessment Objective
- Provide one specific target for improvement in evaluation
- Model how the student could reach the next level

**Use Comparative Judgement**

Alain Anderton's research suggests that having students compare pairs of responses (with teacher guidance) develops their understanding of evaluation quality more effectively than written feedback alone.

**Provide Formative Practice**

Nancy Wall advocates for regular low-stakes extended writing opportunities:
- Paragraph-level evaluation practice (10 minutes)
- Mini-essays (one side of A4) focussing solely on evaluation
- Peer assessment using simplified mark schemes
- Self-assessment against exemplar responses

### Vocabulary Development

Effective evaluation requires precise economic vocabulary and evaluative language. Teachers should explicitly teach:

**Evaluative Phrases**
- "However, this depends upon..."
- "In contrast..."
- "Alternatively..."
- "This is particularly significant because..."
- "The extent of this effect depends on..."
- "In the short run...but in the long run..."
- "On balance..."
- "The most important factor is...because..."

**Hedging Language**

Jim Riley emphasises that sophisticated economics requires qualified claims:
- "may", "might", "could" (rather than "will")
- "likely", "probably", "potentially"
- "tends to", "generally"
- "in most cases", "typically"

This recognises the conditional nature of economic analysis and demonstrates evaluative thinking.

## Common Difficulties and Misconceptions

### Student Challenges

**Evaluation as Afterthought**

Many students treat evaluation as a separate final paragraph rather than integrating it throughout their response. Peter Smith's research indicates this significantly limits marks available. Teachers should model evaluation interwoven with analysis.

**Assertion Without Support**

Students often state evaluative points without justification (e.g., "This policy might not work in developing countries"). Teachers must emphasise that evaluation requires **because** statements—explaining **why** conclusions might differ.

**Generic Evaluation**

Overuse of phrases like "it depends on many factors" without specifying which factors demonstrates weak evaluation. Alain Anderton emphasises specificity—naming particular elasticities, identifying relevant time periods, quantifying where possible.

**Failure to Judge**

Students frequently present balanced arguments but fail to reach a conclusion. All UK examination boards require explicit judgement at A-Level. Teachers should use questions demanding: "What is the most important factor?" or "Which policy would be most effective?"

### Teacher Challenges

**Over-Scaffolding**

Whilst frameworks support learning, Nancy Wall warns against excessive structure that prevents independent thinking. Teachers should plan to gradually remove scaffolds.

**Insufficient Modelling**

Research consistently shows that teacher modelling of extended writing improves student outcomes. Teachers should regularly demonstrate live writing, verbalising their thinking process, particularly around evaluation.

**Lack of Practice Volume**

Extended writing skills develop through practice. EBEA research suggests students need at least one substantial extended writing task per fortnight across Year 12 and Year 13.

## Integration with Economic Theory

### Microeconomics Evaluation

In microeconomics, evaluation frequently considers:

- **Elasticity values**: Determining policy effectiveness
- **Externality size**: Assessing significance of market failure
- **Government failure**: Questioning whether intervention improves outcomes
- **Dynamic effects**: How markets adjust over time

Ha-Joon Chang's emphasis on institutional context is particularly relevant here—students should evaluate how property rights, legal systems, and cultural factors affect microeconomic conclusions.

### Macroeconomics Evaluation

Macroeconomic evaluation typically involves:

- **Trade-offs between objectives**: Phillips curve, current/future living standards
- **Time lags**: Recognition, implementation, and impact lags affecting policy
- **Demand-side versus supply-side approaches**: Comparative policy evaluation
- **External shocks**: Recognising exogenous factors affecting outcomes
- **State of the economy**: How business cycle position affects policy appropriateness

Peter Smith particularly emphasises that macroeconomic evaluation must consider the real-world context—what works during recession may fail during boom.

## Assessment and Progression

### Levels of Evaluation

Based on analysis of all four major examination boards, evaluation quality can be characterised across five levels:

**Level 1**: Simple, unsupported statements (e.g., "This might not work")

**Level 2**: Identification of relevant evaluative factors without development (e.g., "This depends on elasticity")

**Level 3**: Developed evaluative points with supporting reasoning (e.g., "This depends on elasticity, and if demand is inelastic the policy will be less effective because...")

**Level 4**: Multiple developed evaluative points with some prioritisation (e.g., considering several factors and indicating relative importance)

**Level 5**: Sustained, integrated evaluation with prioritised judgement using explicit criteria (e.g., evaluating throughout, considering alternatives, reaching justified overall conclusion)

### Tracking Student Progress

Departments should track extended writing performance systematically:

- Regular assessment of extended responses using examination board criteria
- Recording highest level achieved for each Assessment Objective
- Identification of common weaknesses requiring intervention
- Intervention strategies for students struggling with evaluation

## Conclusion and Recommendations

Extended writing and evaluation skills represent essential outcomes of economics education in the UK. As Jim Riley observes, these skills extend beyond examination success to develop critical thinking applicable across higher education and employment.

**Key Recommendations for Teachers:**

1. Make evaluation expectations explicit from the start of GCSE and A-Level courses
2. Teach evaluation frameworks systematically, removing scaffolds gradually
3. Model extended writing regularly, particularly the integration of evaluation
4. Provide frequent, low-stakes practice opportunities
5. Give feedback focusing on progression between levels
6. Develop students' evaluative vocabulary deliberately
7. Recognise different types of evaluation and teach them explicitly
8. Integrate evaluation with subject content—make it intrinsic not additional
9. Use real-world economic events as contexts for evaluative discussion
10. Emphasise that economics involves contested judgements, not single correct answers

As Ha-Joon Chang argues, genuine economic understanding requires recognising assumptions, considering alternatives, and making reasoned judgements—precisely what extended writing and evaluation skills develop. These capabilities represent the ultimate aim of economics education: developing informed, critical participants in economic debate who can navigate complexity and reach defensible conclusions.
