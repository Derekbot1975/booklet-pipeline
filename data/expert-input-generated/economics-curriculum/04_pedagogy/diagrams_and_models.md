# Teaching Economic Diagrams and Models

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Economic diagrams and models form the analytical backbone of economics education in the UK. From GCSE through to A-level, students are required to construct, interpret, and evaluate graphical representations of economic relationships. The DfE's subject content for A-level Economics (2014) explicitly requires students to "use and understand relevant economic terminology, concepts and theories, and think as an economist" through the application of diagrammatic analysis. Similarly, the GCSE subject content (2016) emphasises that students must "apply, analyse and evaluate economic concepts" using appropriate models.

## Key Principles for Teaching Economic Diagrams

### The Progressive Development Approach

Alain Anderton's *Economics* textbook series demonstrates the importance of scaffolding diagrammatic learning from simple supply and demand curves through to complex AS-AD models. Research by Peter Smith (2009) in the *Economics Network* journal emphasises that students must master basic graphical concepts before progressing to multi-shift analysis. The "building blocks" approach begins with single curves (demand or supply in isolation) before introducing market equilibrium, then multiple shifts, and finally integrated models like circular flow diagrams or Phillips curves.

### The Six-Step Diagram Construction Method

Jim Riley's *tutor2u* teaching resources advocate a systematic approach to diagram construction that has become standard practice in many UK economics departments:

1. **Label axes clearly** with variables and units
2. **Draw and label curves** with appropriate notation (D₁, S₁, etc.)
3. **Identify equilibrium points** clearly marked (E₁, E₂)
4. **Show shifts or movements** with directional arrows
5. **Identify new equilibrium positions** with consistent labelling
6. **Annotate changes** in key variables (P₁→P₂, Q₁→Q₂)

This structured approach addresses the common student error of unlabelled diagrams, which all UK exam boards penalise heavily in assessment.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE Economics)

At GCSE level, all major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) require competency in fundamental diagrams:

- **Supply and demand analysis**: market equilibrium, shifts versus movements
- **Production possibility frontiers** (PPFs): opportunity cost and efficiency
- **Cost and revenue curves**: total, average and marginal relationships
- **Labour market diagrams**: wage determination and employment levels

Nancy Wall's research on GCSE Economics pedagogy (2015) highlights that students struggle most with distinguishing movements along curves from shifts of curves. She recommends using physical activities where students physically move along a line versus shifting the line itself to embed this conceptual distinction.

The AQA GCSE specification (8136) allocates approximately 30% of assessment objectives to "apply knowledge and understanding in various economic contexts, including through use of data". This necessitates regular practice in diagram application rather than mere memorisation.

### Key Stage 5 (A-level Economics)

A-level specifications dramatically expand the range and complexity of required diagrams:

**Microeconomics:**
- **Elasticity representations**: PED, YED, XED geometric interpretation
- **Welfare analysis**: consumer and producer surplus triangles
- **Market failures**: externality diagrams with MSC/MSB curves
- **Market structures**: perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly models
- **Labour market analysis**: MRP=MC diagrams, trade union models

**Macroeconomics:**
- **Aggregate demand and supply**: both Keynesian and classical LRAS representations
- **Multiplier effects**: injections and withdrawals diagrams
- **Phillips curve**: short-run and long-run relationships
- **Exchange rate determination**: supply and demand for currency
- **Growth and development models**: Harrod-Domar, Lewis model

The Edexcel (Pearson) A-level specification (2015) Theme 2 and Theme 4 particularly emphasise diagrammatic analysis, with mark schemes typically allocating 4-6 marks for accurate, well-explained diagrams within 15 or 25-mark questions.

## Common Student Misconceptions and Teaching Strategies

### Misconception 1: Confusing Shifts and Movements

Peter Smith's research identifies this as the most persistent student error. Effective teaching strategies include:

- **Colour coding**: using different colours for shifts (curve moving) versus movements (points sliding along curves)
- **Ceteris paribus emphasis**: explicitly stating "all other factors remain constant" when explaining movements
- **Cause-and-effect analysis**: requiring students to identify whether the factor affects demand, supply, or both before drawing

### Misconception 2: Incorrect Curve Slopes

Students frequently draw demand curves sloping upwards or supply curves incorrectly. Jim Riley recommends:

- **Mnemonic devices**: "Demand downwards, Supply skywards"
- **Real-world anchoring**: always starting with concrete examples (iPhone prices and quantity demanded) before abstract representation
- **Regular retrieval practice**: starter activities requiring quick sketch diagrams without notes

### Misconception 3: Failure to Show Adjustment Processes

Ha-Joon Chang's *Economics: The User's Guide* (2014) emphasises that diagrams should illustrate dynamic processes, not just static equilibria. Students often draw only final positions without showing:

- The direction of change (arrows indicating movement from E₁ to E₂)
- Intermediate disequilibrium states (excess demand or supply)
- The market mechanism that restores equilibrium

Teaching should incorporate "storytelling" through diagrams, where students narrate the economic adjustment process whilst drawing.

## Integrating Diagrams with Written Analysis

### The PEE(D) Framework

Alain Anderton's pedagogical approach advocates integrating diagrams into analytical writing using:

- **Point**: make the economic claim
- **Evidence**: reference the diagram specifically ("As shown in the diagram, the rightward shift of AD from AD₁ to AD₂...")
- **Explain**: interpret the diagrammatic changes in economic terms
- **Diagram**: a clearly constructed, fully labelled graphical representation

This framework directly addresses AQA's "Analysis" assessment objective (AO3), which requires students to "examine economic problems, issues, and institutions" through diagrammatic and written reasoning.

### Annotation Techniques

Effective diagram pedagogy teaches students to annotate diagrams with:

- **Directional arrows** showing causation (e.g., "increased consumer confidence →")
- **Numerical labels** where data is provided (ΔQ = +15%)
- **Textual references** linking to real-world contexts ("2008 financial crisis")

The OCR A-level specification (H460) mark schemes reward "well-annotated" diagrams with additional marks in higher-order questions.

## Technology-Enhanced Teaching Approaches

### Digital Tools for Diagram Construction

Modern economics teaching increasingly incorporates:

- **GeoGebra**: free graphing software allowing interactive manipulation of curves
- **Microsoft Excel**: creating supply and demand schedules that automatically generate diagrams
- **Padlet/OneNote**: collaborative digital spaces for diagram peer assessment
- **Tablet annotation**: teachers drawing diagrams in real-time during online or projected teaching

Jim Riley's *tutor2u* platform provides extensive interactive diagram libraries that allow students to practice at their own pace with immediate feedback.

### Formative Assessment Through Diagrammatic Analysis

Nancy Wall's research (2017) on effective economics assessment recommends:

- **Exit tickets**: students draw and explain a single diagram from the lesson
- **Spot-the-error activities**: providing deliberately flawed diagrams for correction
- **Diagram races**: competitive team activities to correctly construct complex diagrams
- **Think-pair-share**: students explain diagrams to peers, developing articulation skills

## Advanced Diagrammatic Techniques for A-level

### Combined Diagrams

Higher-achieving students must master integrated diagrammatic analysis:

- **AD-AS with Phillips Curve**: showing simultaneous changes in inflation, unemployment, and output
- **Money market with AD-AS**: linking interest rates to aggregate demand
- **International trade diagrams**: combining comparative advantage with tariff analysis

Peter Smith emphasises that these should only be introduced after mastery of component diagrams, typically in the second year of A-level study.

### Dynamic versus Static Analysis

Ha-Joon Chang's institutional economics perspective reminds us that most economic diagrams represent simplified, static relationships. Advanced students should critically evaluate:

- Time lags between cause and effect
- Feedback loops not captured in simple models
- Institutional factors that modify theoretical predictions

The Edexcel Theme 4 specification explicitly requires evaluation of economic models, including recognition of their limitations.

## Exam Board Specific Guidance

### AQA (Specification 7136)

AQA mark schemes typically award:
- 2-3 marks for accurate, labelled diagrams in 9-mark questions
- 4-6 marks for diagrams with detailed annotation in 25-mark essays
- Additional marks for diagrams that directly address the question context

### Edexcel (Pearson)

Edexcel emphasises application through "context-specific" diagrams. Generic diagrams receive lower marks than those adapted to the question scenario (e.g., showing "UK housing market" rather than "market X").

### OCR

OCR's "Breadth in Economics" paper (H060/01) particularly rewards synthesised diagrammatic analysis connecting microeconomic and macroeconomic concepts.

### WJEC Eduqas

The Welsh specification places greater emphasis on Welsh and UK-specific examples. Diagrams should reference Welsh industries (steel, agriculture) or policy contexts (Welsh Government initiatives).

## Professional Development Recommendations

Economics departments should:

1. **Standardise diagram conventions** across teachers (consistent notation, colour schemes)
2. **Create diagram mark schemes** for internal assessments mirroring exam board standards
3. **Develop progression maps** showing which diagrams are introduced at each stage
4. **Share best practice** through Economics Network resources and subject associations
5. **Use lesson observations** focused specifically on diagram teaching quality

The Economics, Business and Enterprise Association (EBEA) provides CPD workshops specifically addressing diagrammatic pedagogy.

## Conclusion

Effective teaching of economic diagrams requires systematic progression, explicit instruction in construction techniques, integration with written analysis, and regular formative assessment. By following evidence-based approaches from key thinkers like Anderton, Smith, Riley, Wall, and Chang, whilst adhering to exam board requirements, teachers can ensure students develop the diagrammatic competency essential for economics qualifications and economic literacy.
