# A Level Economics Specifications

## Overview of A Level Economics in the UK

A Level Economics specifications in England, Wales and Northern Ireland underwent significant reform in 2015, with first teaching from September 2015 and first examinations in summer 2017. These reforms were driven by the Department for Education's objectives to increase rigour, remove modular assessment structures, and ensure specifications prepared students adequately for undergraduate Economics study. As Alain Anderton notes in his *Economics* (2015 onwards editions), the reformed specifications represent "the most substantial change to Economics qualifications in a generation", requiring teachers to reconsider pedagogical approaches and assessment preparation strategies.

## The Four Main Examination Boards

### AQA (Assessment and Qualifications Alliance)

AQA's A Level Economics specification (7136) is the most widely adopted in England. The specification divides content into microeconomics and macroeconomics, with quantitative skills explicitly integrated throughout. The assessment structure comprises:

**Paper 1: Markets and Market Failure** (33.3% of A Level, 2 hours)
- Data response questions and essay questions
- Covers microeconomic theory, market failure, and government intervention

**Paper 2: National and International Economy** (33.3% of A Level, 2 hours)
- Data response questions and essay questions
- Addresses macroeconomic theory, measurement and policy

**Paper 3: Economic Principles and Issues** (33.3% of A Level, 2 hours)
- Multiple choice questions, case study and essay questions
- Synoptic assessment requiring integration of microeconomic and macroeconomic principles

Peter Smith, formerly Chief Examiner for AQA, emphasises that the specification requires "explicit demonstration of quantitative skills worth at least 20% of marks", including calculation, interpretation of data, and use of quantitative evidence in argument construction.

### Edexcel (Pearson)

Edexcel's A Level Economics specification (9EC0) adopts four themes that integrate micro and macro content:

**Theme 1: Introduction to Markets and Market Failure**
**Theme 2: The UK Economy – Performance and Policies**
**Theme 3: Business Behaviour and the Labour Market**
**Theme 4: A Global Perspective**

Assessment structure:
- Paper 1: Markets and Business Behaviour (35%, 2 hours)
- Paper 2: The National and Global Economy (35%, 2 hours)
- Paper 3: Microeconomics and Macroeconomics (30%, 2 hours) – synoptic paper

Nancy Wall, Edexcel's former Subject Advisor for Economics, highlights that this thematic approach "encourages students to make connections between microeconomic and macroeconomic concepts from the outset", rather than treating them as discrete units.

### OCR (Oxford, Cambridge and RSA)

OCR offers two A Level Economics specifications:

**OCR Economics A (H460)** follows a traditional structure:
- Paper 1: Microeconomics (33.3%, 2 hours)
- Paper 2: Macroeconomics (33.3%, 2 hours)
- Paper 3: Themes in Economics (33.3%, 2 hours) – synoptic

**OCR Economics B (H630)** takes a contextual approach, examining economic systems and issues through real-world applications. This specification appeals to teachers seeking to emphasise contemporary economic challenges and behavioural economics perspectives.

Jim Riley, founder of tutor2u and experienced OCR examiner, suggests that OCR Economics B "offers particular value for students who engage better with applied contexts than abstract theory", though uptake remains considerably lower than Economics A.

### WJEC (Welsh Joint Education Committee)

WJEC Eduqas A Level Economics (specification for Wales and England) provides:
- Component 1: Markets, Consumers and Firms (40%, 2 hours 15 minutes)
- Component 2: The National and Global Economy (40%, 2 hours 15 minutes)
- Component 3: Principles and Issues (20%, 1 hour 30 minutes)

The specification requires Welsh centres to include specific references to the Welsh economy where applicable, providing contextualisation opportunities.

## Core Content Requirements Across Specifications

### Microeconomics Content

All specifications mandate coverage of:
- Economic methodology and the economic problem
- Price determination in competitive markets (demand, supply, elasticity)
- Production, costs and revenue
- Perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly and monopoly
- Labour markets
- Market failure (externalities, public goods, information asymmetries)
- Government intervention and failure

Ha-Joon Chang's critique of neoclassical assumptions, articulated in *Economics: The User's Guide* (2014), has influenced how specifications frame market structures. Whilst mainstream theory dominates, specifications increasingly require students to evaluate theoretical assumptions critically.

### Macroeconomics Content

Common macroeconomic requirements include:
- Measurement of economic performance (GDP, inflation, unemployment, balance of payments)
- Aggregate demand and aggregate supply analysis
- Fiscal policy and monetary policy
- Supply-side policies
- International trade theory and protectionism
- Exchange rates and competitiveness
- Role of financial markets and monetary policy implementation
- Inequality and poverty measurement

### Quantitative Skills Requirements

Following reforms, all specifications mandate that "at least 20% of marks" require quantitative skills. These include:

1. **Calculations**: percentage changes, index numbers, ratios, averages, marginal concepts
2. **Graphical analysis**: demand/supply diagrams, cost curves, AD/AS models, Phillips curves, production possibility frontiers
3. **Data interpretation**: time series analysis, identifying trends, making inferences from tables and charts
4. **Using quantitative evidence**: selecting appropriate data to support arguments

Peter Smith's research on A Level Economics assessment (2017) found that many students struggled with quantitative requirements in early examination series, particularly "applying mathematical skills within economic contexts rather than as standalone calculations". This has implications for teaching approaches.

## Assessment Objectives Across Specifications

All specifications assess four Assessment Objectives (AOs):

**AO1: Demonstrate knowledge of terms, concepts, theories and models** (20-25% weighting)

**AO2: Apply knowledge and understanding to various economic contexts** (25-30% weighting)

**AO3: Analyse economic problems and issues** (25-30% weighting)

**AO4: Evaluate economic arguments and evidence, making informed judgements** (20-25% weighting)

Alain Anderton emphasises that the increased weighting towards AO3 and AO4 represents "a deliberate shift towards higher-order thinking skills", requiring teachers to dedicate substantial time to developing analytical and evaluative capabilities beyond content acquisition.

## Synoptic Assessment Requirements

All specifications include synoptic assessment, typically in Paper 3. Synopticity requires students to:

- Draw together knowledge, understanding and skills from different parts of the specification
- Apply microeconomic and macroeconomic concepts to unfamiliar contexts
- Demonstrate connections between different economic theories and policies

Nancy Wall's guidance on synoptic assessment (Economics Review, 2017) recommends that teachers "integrate micro and macro content throughout the course rather than teaching them as separate blocks", facilitating synoptic thinking from the outset.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Curriculum Planning

**Sequential vs Integrated Approaches**: Teachers must decide whether to teach microeconomics followed by macroeconomics (sequential) or integrate topics throughout. Jim Riley's research suggests "no significant difference in outcomes between approaches", though student preference varies.

**Quantitative Skills Embedding**: Given the 20% minimum requirement, successful departments embed quantitative practice throughout teaching rather than treating it separately. This includes regular low-stakes calculation practice and explicit teaching of graph construction.

**Contemporary Context Integration**: Specifications require application to current economic issues. Regular incorporation of recent data, policy announcements and economic developments maintains relevance and supports AO2 development.

### Assessment Preparation Strategies

**Command Word Familiarity**: Each board uses specific command words with defined expectations. "Evaluate" at A Level requires consideration of alternative viewpoints, weighing evidence, and reaching supported judgements. Students must understand these distinctions.

**Data Response Technique**: All specifications include substantial data response components. Students require explicit teaching of data extraction, inference-making and integration into analytical paragraphs.

**Essay Structure**: Extended writing at A Level demands clear introduction of theoretical frameworks, application to context, development of analytical chains, and balanced evaluation. Alain Anderton's *Economics* textbooks provide structured approaches to essay planning that align with mark scheme requirements.

**Diagram Accuracy**: Economic diagrams must be accurately labelled with correctly positioned and identified curves. Common errors include unlabelled axes, missing equilibrium points and inappropriate curve shapes. Regular diagram practice with peer and self-assessment supports accuracy development.

## Distinguishing AS from A Level

AS Economics, whilst decoupled from A Level since 2015 reforms, covers approximately half the A Level content. Key differences include:

- **Depth of evaluation**: AS evaluation is less sophisticated, often comparing two perspectives rather than requiring multi-dimensional judgement
- **Synoptic requirements**: AS has limited or no synoptic assessment
- **Quantitative demands**: whilst present, quantitative analysis is less complex at AS
- **Essay length and development**: AS essays typically require shorter responses with less extended chains of reasoning

Peter Smith notes that AS "provides useful formative assessment for students continuing to A Level but should not be treated as merely half an A Level", as the evaluative demands increase disproportionately at full A Level.

## Current Debates and Future Developments

### Curriculum Content Debates

Ha-Joon Chang and other heterodox economists argue that A Level specifications remain "excessively focused on neoclassical theory at the expense of alternative perspectives". The Post-Crash Economics Society and Rethinking Economics campaign for greater pluralism in economic education. Whilst specifications have introduced some critical evaluation requirements, mainstream theory remains dominant.

### Skills vs Content Balance

Ongoing debate exists regarding the balance between content coverage and skill development. Some teachers argue that the breadth of content leaves insufficient time for deep conceptual understanding and skill mastery. Jim Riley's surveys of Economics teachers consistently identify "time pressure and content overload" as primary concerns.

### Digital Assessment Possibilities

Examination boards are exploring digital assessment possibilities, potentially enabling dynamic data manipulation and more sophisticated quantitative assessment. However, implementation remains distant given equity and accessibility concerns.

## Conclusion

A Level Economics specifications require teachers to develop students' theoretical understanding, quantitative capabilities, and evaluative skills within challenging timeframes. Successful teaching necessitates strategic curriculum planning, explicit skill development, contemporary contextualisation, and thorough familiarisation with board-specific assessment requirements. As Alain Anderton observes, "the reformed specifications demand that students think like economists rather than simply recall economic content" – a challenge requiring sophisticated pedagogical approaches and sustained focus on higher-order cognitive development.
