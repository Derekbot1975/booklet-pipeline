# Nancy Wall

**Full Name:** Nancy Wall

**Current Role:** Senior Curriculum Developer and Education Consultant; formerly Lead Developer for Nuffield Economics

**Known For:** Pioneering the development of the Nuffield Economics curriculum, integrating critical thinking and ethical reasoning into A-level Economics teaching, and advocating for a more pluralist and pedagogically innovative approach to economics education in the UK secondary sector.

---

## Biography

Nancy Wall emerged as a significant figure in UK economics education during the 2010s, particularly through her instrumental role in developing the Nuffield Economics curriculum. Her background combines both academic economics and practical teaching experience, which informed her understanding of the gap between traditional economics teaching methods and the pedagogical approaches needed to engage contemporary students with complex economic issues.

Wall joined the Nuffield Foundation's economics education initiative during its formative stages, working alongside subject specialists, teachers, and curriculum designers to create an entirely new approach to A-level Economics. Her work was directly informed by post-2008 financial crisis critiques of economics education, particularly concerns raised by student movements such as Rethinking Economics and Post-Crash Economics Society, who argued that mainstream economics teaching was too narrow, mathematically abstract, and disconnected from real-world economic problems.

Throughout her career with Nuffield Economics (approximately 2014-2021), Wall served as a bridge between academic economists, educational researchers, and classroom practitioners. She has worked extensively with schools piloting the Nuffield Economics curriculum, providing teacher training, developing assessment materials, and refining pedagogical resources based on classroom feedback. Her influence extends beyond curriculum design into broader debates about the purpose of economics education and its role in developing informed, critical citizens.

Since her primary work with Nuffield Economics, Wall has continued to contribute to economics education through consultancy, teacher professional development, and advocacy for curriculum reform. She has been a frequent speaker at economics education conferences, including those organised by the Economics, Business and Enterprise Association (EBEA) and the Royal Economic Society's Centre for Economic Learning and Teaching (CELT).

## Key Ideas

### 1. Threshold Concepts in Economics Education

Wall has championed the application of threshold concepts theory to economics teaching, building on the work of educational theorists Jan Meyer and Ray Land. She argues that economics education should be structured around key threshold concepts—such as opportunity cost, market equilibrium, and externalities—that represent transformative moments in student understanding. Rather than covering vast amounts of content superficially, Wall advocates for deep engagement with these conceptual gateways that fundamentally change how students see economic phenomena.

In the Nuffield Economics curriculum, this approach manifests through extended engagement with core concepts, using multiple contexts and real-world applications to help students cross these conceptual thresholds. Wall emphasises that threshold concepts in economics are often "troublesome knowledge"—they contradict everyday intuition or previous understanding, requiring careful pedagogical scaffolding and time for students to work through conceptual difficulties.

### 2. Enquiry-Led Economics Teaching

Wall has been a leading proponent of enquiry-based learning in economics education. The Nuffield Economics curriculum, which she helped design, is structured entirely around "Big Questions" such as "How can we make the economy fairer?" and "How can we use markets to solve climate change?" rather than traditional topic-based units. This approach positions students as investigators of economic problems rather than passive recipients of economic theory.

Wall argues that enquiry-led teaching develops deeper conceptual understanding, enhances student motivation by connecting economics to issues students care about, and better reflects how economists actually work. Students learn economic concepts and methods as tools for investigating real questions, rather than abstract content to be memorised. This pedagogical approach requires teachers to adopt a more facilitative role, guiding student investigation whilst maintaining conceptual rigour.

### 3. Critical Thinking and Evaluative Judgement

Wall has emphasised the central importance of developing students' critical thinking capabilities in economics. She distinguishes between lower-order evaluation (weighing up pros and cons within a given framework) and higher-order critical thinking (questioning assumptions, recognising limitations of models, and considering alternative perspectives). The Nuffield Economics curriculum explicitly teaches students to identify and question the assumptions underlying economic models and arguments.

Wall's approach to critical thinking includes teaching students to recognise that economic models are simplifications with limitations, that positive and normative economics cannot always be cleanly separated, and that different schools of economic thought offer different insights. She has developed classroom activities that help students identify hidden assumptions, consider whose perspectives are included or excluded in economic analysis, and evaluate evidence critically. This approach responds to criticisms that traditional economics teaching presents economic theory as settled truth rather than contested knowledge.

### 4. Ethical Reasoning in Economics

Wall has been particularly influential in integrating ethical reasoning into economics education. She argues that economics teaching has historically avoided explicit discussion of values, presenting economics as a value-neutral science whilst implicitly embedding particular normative assumptions. Wall advocates for making ethical questions explicit and giving students frameworks for thinking through them systematically.

The Nuffield Economics curriculum includes explicit treatment of distributive justice, wellbeing economics, and environmental ethics. Wall has developed resources that help students distinguish between efficiency and equity, understand different philosophical approaches to fairness (utilitarian, Rawlsian, libertarian), and apply ethical frameworks to economic policy questions. She emphasises that economics teachers need not impose particular values, but should equip students to reason clearly about value-laden choices and recognise where value judgements enter economic arguments.

### 5. Pluralism in Economics Education

Wall has been an advocate for methodological and theoretical pluralism in economics education. Influenced by the Rethinking Economics movement and academic work on pluralist economics education, she argues that A-level students should be exposed to insights from different schools of economic thought, including behavioural economics, institutional economics, and ecological economics, rather than exclusively neoclassical approaches.

However, Wall's approach to pluralism is pragmatic and pedagogically grounded. She argues for "structured pluralism"—introducing alternative perspectives at appropriate moments when they illuminate particular questions or challenges to mainstream theory, rather than systematically teaching multiple schools of thought, which would be overwhelming for A-level students. The Nuffield curriculum incorporates behavioural insights, institutional analysis, and ecological perspectives whilst maintaining a coherent core structure.

### 6. Contextualised Learning and Contemporary Economic Issues

Wall advocates strongly for connecting economics teaching to contemporary economic issues and real-world contexts. She argues that traditional economics teaching often presents decontextualised theory first, then applies it superficially to examples, whereas effective learning moves between concrete contexts and abstract concepts iteratively.

The Nuffield Economics curriculum embeds economic learning in rich, contemporary contexts—from labour markets and automation, to climate change policy, to digital platform economics. Wall has developed case studies and teaching materials that use real data, actual policy debates, and current economic challenges as vehicles for learning economic concepts. This contextualised approach not only enhances motivation but also helps students understand economics as a discipline that responds to evolving social and economic realities.

### 7. Assessment for Deep Understanding

Wall has contributed to rethinking assessment in economics education, arguing that traditional examinations often incentivise superficial learning and memorisation rather than deep conceptual understanding. She has worked on developing assessment approaches that reward genuine understanding of threshold concepts, application of economic reasoning to novel situations, and critical evaluation of economic arguments.

The Nuffield Economics qualification includes innovative assessment formats such as extended project work, pre-released material requiring sustained analysis, and questions that explicitly assess students' ability to identify assumptions and evaluate models. Wall emphasises alignment between pedagogical approaches and assessment—if teaching emphasises enquiry and critical thinking, assessment must reward these capabilities rather than recall.

### 8. Teacher Professional Development and Pedagogical Content Knowledge

Wall recognises that implementing innovative economics pedagogy requires significant teacher professional development. She has worked extensively on building teachers' pedagogical content knowledge—not just subject knowledge but understanding of how students learn economics, common misconceptions, and effective teaching strategies for threshold concepts.

Wall has developed professional development programmes that model enquiry-based teaching, provide teachers with conceptual frameworks for understanding student learning difficulties, and build communities of practice among economics teachers. She emphasises that curriculum reform cannot succeed through materials alone; it requires sustained investment in teacher development and ongoing support.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on A-level Economics

Wall's most significant curriculum influence has been through the Nuffield Economics qualification, which was introduced for first teaching in 2019. Whilst remaining a small alternative to mainstream examination board offerings (Edexcel, AQA, OCR), Nuffield Economics has influenced broader thinking about economics education. Several examination boards have subsequently incorporated elements such as:
- Greater emphasis on real-world contexts and contemporary issues
- More explicit assessment of evaluation and critical thinking
- Introduction of ethical considerations into specification content
- Reference to behavioural economics and alternative perspectives

The Nuffield Economics curriculum has been adopted by a growing number of schools and colleges, particularly those seeking a more engaging and intellectually rigorous approach to economics education. Wall's accompanying teacher development programmes have been crucial to implementation success.

### Contribution to Policy Debates

Wall has contributed to broader policy debates about economics education through:
- Evidence submissions to curriculum reviews and consultations
- Participation in Royal Economic Society discussions on economics education
- Engagement with the Government Economic Service on economic literacy
- Contributions to Office for Students (OfS) discussions about transition from A-level to university economics

Her work has been cited in academic studies of economics education reform, including research by the Centre for Curriculum Redesign and international comparative studies of economics pedagogy.

### Influence Beyond A-level

Wall's ideas have influenced economics teaching at other levels:
- GCSE Economics teachers have adopted enquiry-based approaches
- Undergraduate economics teaching has drawn on Nuffield pedagogical innovations
- International curricula have examined Nuffield Economics as a reform model
- Business and enterprise education has incorporated ethical reasoning frameworks

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructivist Foundations

Wall's pedagogical approach is grounded in constructivist learning theory, particularly the idea that students actively construct understanding rather than passively receiving knowledge. She draws on the work of Piaget, Vygotsky, and contemporary educational researchers who emphasise the importance of:
- Building on students' existing conceptions and misconceptions
- Social learning through discussion and collaborative enquiry
- Scaffolding that supports students through challenging conceptual territory
- Multiple representations of concepts (graphical, numerical, verbal, contextual)

### Dialogic Teaching

Wall advocates for dialogic teaching in economics classrooms—moving beyond teacher-led exposition to genuine dialogue where student thinking is made visible, ideas are explored collectively, and understanding is negotiated. This approach requires careful questioning techniques, wait time, and creation of classroom cultures where tentative thinking and conceptual struggle are valued.

### Use of Technology and Data

Wall has promoted intelligent use of technology in economics teaching, particularly for:
- Accessing and manipulating real economic data
- Visualising economic relationships through interactive tools
- Researching contemporary economic issues
- Simulating economic scenarios and policy choices

However, she cautions against technology for its own sake, emphasising that technological tools must serve clear pedagogical purposes and support conceptual understanding.

### Formative Assessment

Wall emphasises formative assessment as integral to learning, not merely preparation for summative examinations. She advocates for:
- Regular low-stakes activities that reveal student thinking
- Peer assessment and discussion of economic arguments
- Self-assessment using clear criteria for economic reasoning
- Teacher feedback that advances conceptual understanding

### Classroom Culture

Wall recognises that implementing ambitious pedagogy requires particular classroom cultures. She advocates for:
- Psychological safety where students feel comfortable with uncertainty
- Intellectual respect for student ideas and questions
- High expectations combined with appropriate support
- Emphasis on growth mindset in relation to economic understanding

## Influence on Educational Research

Wall's work has contributed to the growing field of economics education research in several ways:

### Practitioner Research

Wall has modelled engagement with research evidence in curriculum development, drawing on international studies of misconceptions in economics, cognitive science research on learning, and empirical studies of teaching effectiveness. She has also contributed to practitioner research through evaluation of the Nuffield Economics pilots and documentation of implementation experiences.

### Research Questions

Wall's curriculum innovations have generated research questions investigated by academic researchers:
- How do students develop understanding of threshold concepts in economics?
- What pedagogical approaches most effectively develop economic evaluation skills?
- How does enquiry-based economics teaching affect student motivation and uptake?
- What forms of teacher knowledge are required for innovative economics pedagogy?

### Collaboration with Academic Researchers

Wall has collaborated with university-based researchers studying economics education, including colleagues at UCL Institute of Education, University of Bristol, and Manchester Metropolitan University. These collaborations have produced evidence about effective economics teaching and student learning.

## Legacy and Ongoing Influence

Nancy Wall's contribution to UK economics education extends beyond specific curriculum materials to encompass a broader vision of what economics education can and should be. Her work has:

1. **Demonstrated feasibility** of ambitious alternatives to traditional economics teaching, showing that enquiry-based, ethically engaged, and pluralist approaches can work at A-level.

2. **Influenced teaching practice** beyond schools directly using Nuffield Economics, as her ideas have spread through teacher networks, conferences, and professional development.

3. **Contributed to theoretical understanding** of economics education, particularly regarding threshold concepts, critical thinking development, and ethical reasoning.

4. **Provided practical resources** that enable teachers to implement innovative pedagogy, reducing barriers to adoption.

5. **Shaped policy debates** about the purposes of economics education and its role in developing informed citizenship.

6. **Built teacher communities** committed to pedagogical innovation and continuous improvement in economics teaching.

Wall's work represents a significant contribution to the movement for economics education reform that emerged following the 2008 financial crisis. Whilst traditional approaches to economics teaching remain dominant, her influence has created a viable alternative pathway and demonstrated what reimagined economics education can achieve.
