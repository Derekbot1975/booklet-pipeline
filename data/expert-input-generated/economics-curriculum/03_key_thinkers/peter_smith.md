# Peter Smith

**Full Name:** Peter Smith

**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Education at the University of Southampton; formerly Senior Lecturer in Economics Education at the University of Southampton

**Known For:** Leading figure in economics education in the UK; pioneering work in teaching quantitative skills to economics students; extensive contributions to economics textbooks and pedagogical research; influential voice in economics curriculum development at secondary and tertiary levels

---

## Biography

### Early Career and Academic Background

Peter Smith established himself as a prominent figure in UK economics education from the 1980s onwards, working primarily at the University of Southampton where he devoted his career to improving the teaching and learning of economics. His academic work has consistently focused on the pedagogical challenges inherent in economics education, particularly the difficulties students encounter when engaging with quantitative methods and mathematical concepts within economic contexts.

### Career Development

Smith's career trajectory reflects a sustained commitment to economics education research and practice. At the University of Southampton, he combined teaching responsibilities with research into effective pedagogical approaches for economics. His work bridged the gap between economic theory and educational practice, making him a distinctive voice in both communities. Unlike many economics educators who focus primarily on either secondary or tertiary education, Smith maintained engagement across both sectors, understanding the crucial transition points in students' learning journeys.

Throughout his career, Smith has been actively involved with key organisations shaping economics education in the UK, including the Economics Network (part of the Higher Education Academy, now Advance HE) and contributing to various curriculum development initiatives. His practical experience as both an economics teacher and education researcher has informed his empirically grounded approach to pedagogical innovation.

### Retirement and Continuing Influence

Since becoming Emeritus Professor, Smith has continued to influence economics education through publications, consultancy work, and engagement with curriculum reform discussions. His legacy is particularly evident in the ongoing debates about quantitative skills development in economics programmes across UK universities.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The Quantitative Skills Challenge in Economics Education

Peter Smith's most significant contribution lies in his systematic analysis of why students struggle with quantitative methods in economics. He identified that the difficulty is not simply a matter of mathematical ability but rather stems from students' inability to connect mathematical techniques with economic concepts and real-world applications. Smith argued that traditional approaches treating mathematics as a separate 'toolkit' fail because they don't integrate quantitative reasoning with economic thinking.

In his research, Smith demonstrated that successful quantitative learning in economics requires simultaneous development of three interconnected capabilities: mathematical technique, economic interpretation, and contextual application. This framework challenged the prevalent assumption that students simply needed more mathematics preparation before studying economics. Instead, Smith advocated for embedding quantitative skills within economic contexts from the outset, allowing students to develop mathematical understanding through economic problems rather than as a prerequisite to them.

### 2. The 'Threshold Concepts' Framework in Economics

Smith engaged extensively with the threshold concepts framework developed by Meyer and Land, applying it specifically to economics education. He identified several troublesome areas in economics where students encounter conceptual barriers that, once overcome, transform their understanding of the discipline. Key threshold concepts he examined included opportunity cost, elasticity, equilibrium, and marginal analysis.

His contribution to this literature emphasised that quantitative representations of these concepts often constitute the threshold itself—students may grasp qualitative explanations but struggle when encountering mathematical formulations. Smith's pedagogical solutions involved creating 'bridge' activities that gradually introduce mathematical complexity while maintaining economic meaning, helping students traverse these conceptual thresholds more successfully.

### 3. Assessment Design for Conceptual Understanding

Smith made important contributions to assessment practice in economics, arguing that traditional examination methods often test procedural knowledge rather than genuine economic understanding. He advocated for assessment designs that require students to demonstrate integrated knowledge—applying quantitative techniques to analyse economic scenarios, interpreting results in economic terms, and evaluating limitations of analytical approaches.

His work highlighted the formative role of assessment in learning, proposing that carefully structured problems could guide students through conceptual development. Smith developed assessment frameworks that progressively increased complexity, requiring students to move from calculation to interpretation to critical evaluation, thereby using assessment itself as a pedagogical tool.

### 4. Active Learning in Large Economics Classes

Recognising the practical constraints of teaching economics to large cohorts in UK universities, Smith researched effective active learning strategies scalable to substantial class sizes. He championed the use of 'classroom experiments' where students participate in simulated economic scenarios (auctions, public goods games, etc.) and then analyse their own behaviour using economic concepts.

Smith's approach to active learning was pragmatic rather than ideological. He carefully evaluated which techniques produced measurable learning gains relative to implementation costs. His research demonstrated that even simple interventions—such as structured peer discussion of quantitative problems or brief individual reflection tasks—could significantly improve conceptual understanding when strategically deployed.

### 5. Progression from A-Level to Undergraduate Economics

Smith conducted influential research on the transition from A-level Economics to university study, identifying critical discontinuities in content, approach, and expectations. He found that while A-level Economics provides valuable conceptual foundations, the step-change in quantitative demands at university often overwhelms students, particularly those without A-level Mathematics.

His work informed curriculum design recommendations for first-year undergraduate programmes, advocating for explicit 'bridging' content that acknowledges students' diverse mathematical backgrounds while maintaining disciplinary rigour. Smith argued against both 'dumbing down' university economics and assuming mathematical competence, instead proposing differentiated support mechanisms within mainstream teaching.

### 6. The Role of Context in Economics Learning

Throughout his work, Smith emphasised that economics is distinctive in requiring students to understand abstract theoretical models whilst simultaneously recognising their contextual limitations. He explored how students develop the sophisticated understanding that models are simultaneously 'true' (within their assumptions) and 'false' (as complete descriptions of reality).

This focus on contextual understanding influenced his approach to teaching quantitative methods—Smith insisted that every mathematical technique should be introduced alongside discussions of when it is applicable, what assumptions it requires, and what its limitations are. This contrasted with approaches treating quantitative methods as value-free tools applicable across all contexts.

### Academic Papers and Book Chapters

Smith's scholarly output includes numerous peer-reviewed papers in economics education journals. Significant publications include:

**"Teaching and Learning Quantitative Methods in Economics" (co-authored with various colleagues)** – A series of papers examining specific pedagogical approaches to mathematical and statistical methods in economics programmes. These papers reported empirical studies of teaching interventions and their effects on student learning outcomes.

**"Threshold Concepts in Economics Education"** – Contributions to edited collections exploring how threshold concepts framework applies specifically to economics, identifying key conceptual barriers students encounter.

**"Assessment in Economics: Multiple Choice or Essay?"** – Research examining the relationship between assessment format and learning outcomes in economics, questioning conventional assumptions about assessment design.

**Papers in the *International Review of Economics Education* (IREE)** – Smith was a regular contributor to IREE, the leading journal for economics education research, publishing empirical studies and theoretical pieces examining various aspects of economics teaching and learning.

**Economics textbooks for A-level students** – Smith contributed to textbook series aligned with various UK examination board specifications, bringing his pedagogical expertise to bear on structuring content for optimal student learning.

**Workbooks and study guides** – Recognising that textbooks alone are insufficient for developing quantitative skills, Smith produced supplementary materials focusing on practice problems with scaffolded support, helping students develop confidence with mathematical applications in economics.

**Online resources** – Smith was an early adopter of digital resources for economics education, contributing to online learning materials that allowed students to interact with economic models and data.

### Reports and Policy Documents

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on Higher Education Teaching

Smith's work has substantially influenced how quantitative methods are taught in UK economics departments. His research provided empirical evidence supporting integrated approaches to mathematics teaching in economics, influencing curriculum design in numerous universities. The Economics Network disseminated his findings widely, leading to adoption of his recommended practices across the sector.

His emphasis on scaffolded learning and explicit bridging from A-level has informed the design of foundation and first-year economics modules, particularly regarding how mathematical content is introduced and supported. Many UK universities now incorporate the principles Smith advocated—contextualised quantitative teaching, formative assessment, and recognition of diverse mathematical backgrounds.

### Contribution to A-Level Economics

Through his textbook contributions and involvement in curriculum consultations, Smith influenced A-level Economics teaching. His research on the transition to university informed discussions about appropriate mathematical content at A-level, contributing to ongoing debates about whether A-level Economics should include more quantitative methods or focus on conceptual foundations.

Smith's work highlighted the importance of teaching students to interpret and critique quantitative information rather than merely perform calculations—an emphasis reflected in more recent A-level Economics specifications that assess data analysis and evaluation skills.

### Professional Development and Teacher Training

Smith's pedagogical research has been widely used in professional development for economics teachers at both school and university levels. The Economics Network and subject associations have drawn on his work when designing training workshops and resources for teachers. His accessible writing style and practical focus made his research particularly valuable for practitioners seeking evidence-based approaches to teaching challenges.

### Influence on Research Community

Smith helped establish economics education as a legitimate field of academic inquiry in the UK context. His rigorous research methods and theoretical framing demonstrated that pedagogical research could meet high scholarly standards whilst remaining practically relevant. This contributed to growing acceptance of education research within economics departments and encouraged other economists to engage with pedagogical scholarship.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Empirically-Grounded Practice

Smith's pedagogical philosophy was fundamentally empirical. He insisted that teaching approaches should be evaluated based on evidence of learning outcomes rather than intuition or tradition. His research designs typically involved comparing different teaching methods, measuring student learning through appropriate instruments, and drawing conclusions based on data rather than anecdote.

This empirical orientation extended to his own teaching practice. Smith regularly evaluated his teaching innovations, using both quantitative measures (test scores, survey data) and qualitative evidence (student interviews, written reflections) to assess effectiveness and refine approaches.

### Integration of Theory and Application

A hallmark of Smith's approach was simultaneous attention to theoretical rigour and practical application. He rejected false dichotomies between 'pure' and 'applied' economics education, arguing that students need both abstract understanding and contextual application. His teaching materials typically introduced concepts through real-world scenarios, developed theoretical frameworks to analyse them, then returned to practical applications to consolidate learning.

This integration was particularly evident in his treatment of quantitative methods. Rather than teaching mathematical techniques as abstract procedures, Smith always connected them to economic questions and real data, helping students understand why particular methods are valuable and when they should be employed.

### Constructivist Learning Principles

Smith's pedagogy reflected constructivist learning theory, emphasising that students actively construct understanding rather than passively receiving knowledge. He designed learning activities requiring students to engage with concepts, make predictions, test ideas, and revise understanding based on evidence.

His use of classroom experiments exemplified this approach—students experienced economic phenomena directly, then used economic theory to explain their observations. This reversed the traditional sequence of theory-then-application, allowing students to construct theoretical understanding from concrete experience.

### Scaffolded Complexity

Smith was particularly skilled at designing learning sequences that gradually increased complexity. He understood that students needed to master foundational concepts before tackling advanced applications, but also recognised that oversimplified treatments could create misconceptions requiring later unlearning.

His scaffolding approach involved introducing concepts in simplified contexts, ensuring secure understanding, then systematically adding complexity. For quantitative methods, this meant starting with numerical examples, progressing to algebraic formulations, then introducing more sophisticated mathematical treatments. At each stage, economic meaning remained central, preventing mathematics from becoming divorced from its purpose.

### Student-Centred Focus

Throughout his career, Smith maintained focus on student experience and student learning. His research questions emerged from classroom observations of student difficulties rather than abstract theoretical concerns. He listened carefully to students' explanations of their thinking, using their perspectives to identify conceptual barriers and design targeted interventions.

This student-centred approach influenced his assessment design, textbook writing, and professional development work. Smith consistently advocated that curriculum and teaching decisions should be informed by understanding of how students actually learn economics, not just what experts believe they should know.

## Legacy and Continuing Relevance

Peter Smith's contributions remain highly relevant to contemporary debates in UK economics education. Current discussions about mathematical requirements for economics degrees, the role of data science in economics curricula, and effective teaching in large classes all engage with issues Smith examined throughout his career.

His research on threshold concepts continues to influence curriculum design, particularly as universities reconsider economics programmes in light of student diversity and calls for curriculum reform. The pedagogical principles Smith articulated—integration of quantitative and economic thinking, contextualised learning, scaffolded progression—are increasingly recognised as essential elements of effective economics education.

The growth of economics education research in the UK owes much to Smith's pioneering work establishing rigorous, practically-relevant pedagogical scholarship. His legacy is evident in the continuing stream of research examining economics teaching and learning, much of which builds directly on foundations he established.
