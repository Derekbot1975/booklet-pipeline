# Ha-Joon Chang

**Full Name:** Ha-Joon Chang (장하준)

**Current Roles:** 
- Reader in the Political Economy of Development, Faculty of Economics, University of Cambridge
- Fellow, Centre of Development Studies, University of Cambridge
- Life Fellow, Clare Hall, Cambridge

**Known For:** 
Challenging free-market orthodoxy, making economics accessible to general audiences, advocating heterodox and institutional approaches to economic development, and questioning the historical narratives used to justify neoliberal policies.

---

## Biography

### Early Life and Education

Ha-Joon Chang was born in Seoul, South Korea, in 1963. He completed his undergraduate degree in Economics at Seoul National University before moving to the UK to pursue graduate studies. Chang obtained his MPhil (1987) and PhD (1991) in Economics from the Faculty of Economics at the University of Cambridge, where he wrote his doctoral thesis on the role of the state in industrial development.

### Academic Career

Chang has spent his entire academic career at Cambridge, joining the Faculty of Economics in 1990. He was appointed Reader in the Political Economy of Development in 2003, a position he continues to hold. Unlike many economists who progress to full professorships, Chang has maintained his Reader position whilst building an international reputation through his research, writing, and public engagement activities.

He is a Fellow of the Centre of Development Studies at Cambridge and holds a Life Fellowship at Clare Hall. His position at Cambridge has afforded him both academic credibility and institutional independence to pursue heterodox approaches that challenge mainstream economic thinking.

### Recognition and Awards

Chang has received numerous accolades including:
- Wassily Leontief Prize for Advancing the Frontiers of Economic Thought (2005)
- Freeman Prize of the Association for Evolutionary Economics (2003)
- Listed as one of Prospect magazine's World Thinkers (multiple years)
- Honorary degrees from universities including the University of Buenos Aires and FLACSO Argentina

## Key Ideas

### 1. Kicking Away the Ladder

Perhaps Chang's most influential concept, developed extensively in his 2002 book of the same name, challenges the historical narrative that developed countries achieved prosperity through free-market policies. Drawing on meticulous historical research, Chang demonstrates that:

- Britain, often portrayed as the birthplace of free trade, actually employed protectionist policies during its industrial development (18th-19th centuries)
- The United States was one of the most protectionist economies until after World War II
- Germany, Japan, and South Korea all used active industrial policies, trade protection, and state intervention during their developmental phases
- These countries now advocate free-market policies for developing nations whilst 'kicking away the ladder' they themselves used to climb

This historical institutionalist approach has fundamentally challenged the Washington Consensus and influenced development economics education, particularly in showing students that economic history is more complex than simplified narratives suggest.

### 2. The Myth of Free Trade

Chang argues that free trade is not a neutral, universally beneficial policy but rather serves the interests of countries that have already industrialised. His key arguments include:

- Infant industry protection is economically rational and historically proven
- Free trade can lock developing countries into producing low-value commodities
- Trade policy should be considered a tool for industrial development, not an end in itself
- The World Trade Organisation (WTO) rules prevent developing countries from using policies that developed countries historically employed

This critique has influenced how development economics is taught in UK universities, encouraging critical examination of trade liberalisation policies.

### 3. Institutional Economics and Development

Drawing on the institutional economics tradition of Thorstein Veblen, John Commons, and Gunnar Myrdal, Chang emphasises:

- Institutions shape economic behaviour more fundamentally than markets
- There is no single 'best' set of institutions applicable to all countries
- Institution-building must consider local contexts, historical trajectories, and power relations
- Formal institutions (laws, regulations) interact with informal institutions (norms, culture)

Chang's institutional approach has provided UK economics students with frameworks for understanding why identical policies produce different outcomes in different countries.

### 4. The Role of the State in Economic Development

Counter to neoliberal minimalist state theory, Chang argues:

- Successful economic development has always involved active state intervention
- States must coordinate investment in complementary industries (coordination failure problem)
- Public ownership can be more efficient than private ownership in certain sectors
- Industrial policy, when properly designed, can accelerate technological upgrading

His work on developmental states has influenced teaching about East Asian industrialisation in UK universities, moving beyond simplistic 'free market miracle' narratives.

### 5. Critique of Neo-Classical Economics

Chang has been a prominent critic of neo-classical economics methodology and assumptions:

- Questions the realism of assumptions like perfect information and rational economic man
- Challenges the idea that economic theory can be value-neutral or purely scientific
- Argues that mathematical formalism can obscure rather than clarify economic relationships
- Advocates for methodological pluralism in economics education

This critique has strengthened the case for teaching heterodox economics alongside mainstream approaches in UK universities.

### 6. Post-Autistic Economics Movement

Chang has been associated with the post-autistic economics (now 'Rethinking Economics') movement, which emerged from French economics students in 2000. The movement criticises:

- Over-mathematisation of economics at the expense of real-world understanding
- Dominance of a single theoretical paradigm (neo-classical)
- Lack of pluralism in economics curricula
- Disconnect between economics education and actual economic problems

This has influenced debates about economics curriculum reform in UK universities, including at Cambridge itself.

### 7. Capabilities Approach to Economics

Influenced by Amartya Sen (his Cambridge colleague), Chang incorporates capability theory:

- Economic development should be measured by expansion of human capabilities, not just GDP
- Focuses on what people can actually do and be, not just income or consumption
- Emphasises substantive freedoms rather than formal freedoms
- Questions whether market outcomes genuinely reflect individual choices

This approach has enriched teaching of development economics and welfare economics in UK curricula.

### 8. Technology and Economic Development

Chang emphasises technological capabilities as central to development:

- Developing countries must acquire technological capabilities, not just access to technology
- Technology transfer is a learning process requiring active absorption capacity
- Intellectual property rights regimes can hinder technological catch-up
- Education and training systems must align with industrial development strategies

His work has influenced teaching about innovation policy and technology transfer in UK development economics courses.

## Key Publications

### Books for Academic Audiences

**"Kicking Away the Ladder: Development Strategy in Historical Perspective" (2002), Anthem Press**

This seminal work examines the actual policies used by now-developed countries during their industrialisation. Chang analyses trade policy, industrial policy, intellectual property rights, and institutional development across Britain, the United States, Germany, France, Sweden, the Netherlands, Belgium, Switzerland, Japan, Korea, and Taiwan. The book systematically dismantles the historical narrative used to justify the Washington Consensus, showing that virtually all developed countries used protectionist and interventionist policies during their catch-up phases. It has become required reading in many UK development economics courses.

**"The Political Economy of Industrial Policy" (1994, editor), Macmillan**

An edited volume bringing together institutionalist and structuralist perspectives on industrial policy, featuring contributions from leading heterodox economists. This book established Chang as a serious scholar in institutional economics and provided frameworks still used in teaching industrial policy.

**"Globalisation, Economic Development and the Role of the State" (2003), Zed Books/Third World Network**

This collection of essays develops Chang's critique of neo-liberal globalisation and argues for policy space for developing countries. It explores infant industry theory, technology policy, foreign investment regulation, and institutional development. The book is widely used in UK graduate courses on development economics.

**"Institutional Change and Economic Development" (2007, editor with Peter Evans), United Nations University Press**

An edited volume exploring how institutions evolve and how they relate to economic development. Features contributions from leading institutional economists and provides frameworks for understanding institutional diversity. Used in UK courses on institutional economics and comparative political economy.

**"The East Asian Development Experience: The Miracle, the Crisis and the Future" (2006, editor), Zed Books**

Examines East Asian industrialisation, the 1997 financial crisis, and development prospects. Challenges both the 'free market miracle' and 'crony capitalism' narratives. Provides nuanced case studies used in UK teaching about Asian development.

### Books for General Audiences

**"Bad Samaritans: The Myth of Free Trade and the Secret History of Capitalism" (2007), Bloomsbury Press**

Chang's first major book for a general audience, which became an international bestseller. Written in accessible prose, it explains how rich countries became rich through protectionism and intervention, not free markets. The book uses memorable examples (Friedrich List, Alexander Hamilton, American tariffs) and clear arguments to make heterodox economics accessible. It has been widely used in UK A-Level Economics and first-year undergraduate courses to introduce critical perspectives on trade and development.

**"23 Things They Don't Tell You About Capitalism" (2010), Allen Lane/Penguin**

Perhaps Chang's most influential popular work, structured as 23 accessible chapters challenging common economic assumptions:
- Thing 1: 'There is no such thing as a free market'
- Thing 2: 'Companies should not be run in the interest of their owners'
- Thing 13: 'Making rich people richer doesn't make the rest of us richer'
- Thing 23: 'Good economic policy does not require good economists'

Each 'thing' is explained in 10-15 pages using real-world examples, historical evidence, and clear reasoning. The book has sold over half a million copies worldwide and has been translated into 37 languages. In UK education, it is widely used in A-Level Economics to introduce critical thinking, in Access to Higher Education courses, and in first-year undergraduate teaching to expose students to heterodox perspectives. Teachers particularly value its accessibility and evidence-based approach.

**"Economics: The User's Guide" (2014), Pelican/Penguin**

Commissioned as part of Penguin's relaunch of the Pelican imprint, this comprehensive introduction to economics covers:
- History of economic thought (nine schools from Classical to Behaviouralist)
- How the economy works (production, finance, inequality, work, etc.)
- How to use economics in the real world

The book explicitly aims to democratise economics knowledge and promote pluralism. It presents multiple schools of thought fairly rather than privileging one approach. The 'User's Guide' format emphasises practical understanding over technical detail. This book has become particularly influential in UK further education and access courses, providing an alternative to standard neo-classical textbooks. Many economics teachers use it alongside mainstream texts to provide pluralist perspectives.

**"Edible Economics: A Hungry Economist Explains the World" (2022), Allen Lane/Penguin**

Chang's most recent popular book uses food and eating to explain economic concepts:
- Acorn: nature and capitalism
- Okra: colonialism and development
- Coconut: globalisation and fair trade
- Anchovy: welfare state and redistribution
- Prawn: financial markets and crisis

The book demonstrates Chang's evolving pedagogical approach, using familiar experiences (eating) to explain abstract concepts. It reflects growing interest in embodied and experiential learning in economics education. UK teachers have begun incorporating chapters into GCSE and A-Level teaching to make economics more relatable and engaging.

### Journal Articles and Book Chapters

**"The Economic Theory of the Developmental State" (1999), in Woo-Cumings, M. (ed.), The Developmental State, Cornell University Press**

A systematic theoretical defence of state intervention in development, synthesising neo-classical market failure theory, institutionalist analysis, and structuralist development economics. This chapter is widely cited in UK graduate courses on development economics and political economy.

**"Understanding the Relationship between Institutions and Economic Development: Some Key Theoretical Issues" (2006), in Chang, H.J. (ed.), Institutional Change and Economic Development, UNU Press**

Clarifies theoretical debates about institutions and development, distinguishing between form and function of institutions, and arguing against institutional monocropping. Used in UK teaching on institutional economics.

**"Industrial Policy: Can We Go Beyond an Unproductive Confrontation?" (2009), in Annual World Bank Conference on Development Economics**

A pragmatic assessment of industrial policy debates, arguing that the question is not whether to have industrial policy but how to design it well. This paper has influenced how industrial policy is taught in UK development courses, moving beyond ideological positions.

**"Institutions and Economic Development: Theory, Policy and History" (2011), Journal of Institutional Economics**

Develops Chang's institutional framework, arguing that institutions must be understood in historical context and that there are multiple viable institutional configurations for development. Widely assigned in UK institutional economics courses.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on University Economics Curricula

Chang's work has significantly influenced UK economics education, particularly in:

**Promoting Pluralism:** Chang has been a vocal advocate for teaching multiple schools of economic thought rather than neo-classical monoculture. His influence is evident in:
- The Post-Crash Economics Society movement at Manchester and other UK universities (2012 onwards)
- The Rethinking Economics campaign, which has pushed for curriculum reform across UK universities
- Introduction of 'History of Economic Thought' modules at Cambridge and other institutions
- Greater inclusion of institutional, structuralist, and Keynesian perspectives alongside neo-classical theory

**Development Economics:** Chang's historical approach to development has reshaped teaching in this field:
- Students now learn about actual historical policies of developed countries, not just idealised models
- East Asian development is taught with more nuance, acknowledging state intervention
- Trade policy is presented critically rather than as unquestionably beneficial
- The Washington Consensus is contextualised and critiqued rather than taken as given

**Political Economy Modules:** Many UK universities now offer political economy pathways or modules influenced by Chang's work:
- Cambridge's Political Economy and Development specialisation
- SOAS University of London's heterodox economics programmes
- Manchester's Political Economy pathway
- Greenwich's Political Economy modules

### Influence on A-Level and GCSE Economics

While A-Level specifications remain largely neo-classical, Chang's accessible books have provided teachers with resources to:

**Develop Critical Thinking:** Examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR) require students to evaluate economic arguments. Chang's work provides substantive critiques:
- Questioning assumptions behind free trade arguments
- Evaluating evidence for trickle-down economics
- Considering historical evidence alongside theoretical models
- Analysing role of institutions in economic outcomes

**Real-World Examples:** Teachers use Chang's books for case studies:
- Historical examples of protectionism (US, Britain, Germany) for trade theory units
- Asian development models for development economics
- Financial deregulation leading to 2008 crisis for market failure units
- Shareholder vs stakeholder capitalism for business economics

**Extend and Challenge Activities:** For high-attaining students, teachers assign Chang's articles or book chapters as extension reading, encouraging engagement with economic debates beyond the specification.

### Influence on Teacher Training and CPD

Chang's work appears in economics teacher continuing professional development:

**Economics Subject Associations:** The Economics, Business and Enterprise Association (EBEA) has featured Chang's work in conferences and publications, helping teachers incorporate heterodox perspectives.

**University-Schools Partnerships:** Cambridge's Faculty of Economics has partnered with schools through programmes like HE+, where Chang's accessible writing helps teachers understand pluralist approaches.

### Influence on Policy Debates

While not directly involved in UK economic policy-making, Chang's ideas have influenced:

**Industrial Strategy:** The return of industrial strategy discourse in UK policy (2017 Industrial Strategy White Paper) reflects arguments Chang has advanced about active state roles in economic development. Think tanks like the Institute for Public Policy Research (IPPR) and the New Economics Foundation (NEF) cite Chang's work.

**Brexit Debates:** Chang's nuanced position on globalisation—critical of neoliberal aspects whilst recognising benefits of international integration—provided intellectual resources for non-protectionist Remain arguments and critical perspectives on EU economic governance.

**Development Policy:** Chang has influenced UK development policy debates through advisory roles with international organisations (UN, ILO, World Bank) and engagement with parliamentary committees considering development assistance policies.

**Regional Development:** Arguments for place-based industrial policy and regional development strategies in the UK draw on Chang's work about active state coordination of investment and technological upgrading.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Accessibility and Public Engagement

Chang is distinguished by his commitment to making economics accessible to non-specialist audiences:

**Clear, Jargon-Free Writing:** Chang deliberately avoids technical jargon and mathematical formalisation in his popular works. He explains concepts using everyday language and familiar examples. This approach demonstrates that economic arguments can be rigorous without being inaccessible.

**Narrative and Storytelling:** Rather than presenting abstract models, Chang uses historical narratives and concrete examples. In "Bad Samaritans," he tells the story of Korean development; in "Edible Economics," he uses food histories. This narrative approach makes economic history memorable and engaging.

**Challenging Assumptions:** Chang's pedagogical style involves making explicit and questioning assumptions that mainstream economics takes as natural:
- "There is no such thing as a free market" (Thing 1) challenges the concept as given
- Showing historical protectionism challenges inevitability of free trade
- Discussing multiple schools of thought challenges theoretical monopoly
