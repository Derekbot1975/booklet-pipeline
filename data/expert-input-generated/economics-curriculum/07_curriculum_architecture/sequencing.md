# Sequencing Economics Topics

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in Economics

Sequencing Economics topics effectively is fundamental to building students' conceptual understanding and analytical capabilities. As Peter Smith argues in *Teaching Secondary Economics* (2017), Economics is inherently hierarchical in its knowledge structure, requiring careful scaffolding from concrete, observable phenomena to abstract theoretical frameworks. The subject demands that teachers consider both vertical progression (building complexity over time) and horizontal coherence (linking related concepts within and across units).

Unlike some humanities subjects, Economics relies heavily on cumulative knowledge. Students cannot meaningfully engage with market failure without understanding market equilibrium, nor can they analyse monetary policy without grasping inflation and unemployment. This hierarchical nature makes sequencing decisions particularly consequential for student outcomes.

## Key Principles of Economics Sequencing

### The Concrete-to-Abstract Principle

Alain Anderton's *Economics* textbook series (Causeway Press) consistently demonstrates the importance of beginning with familiar, observable economic phenomena before introducing theoretical models. Students should encounter:

1. **Real-world examples** (rising prices at local shops, changes in employment)
2. **Simple analytical frameworks** (supply and demand diagrams)
3. **Abstract theoretical models** (perfect competition, general equilibrium)
4. **Complex policy applications** (evaluating quantitative easing effectiveness)

This progression aligns with cognitive load theory, ensuring students develop secure foundational knowledge before tackling sophisticated analysis.

### The Micro-to-Macro Debate

A fundamental sequencing question concerns whether microeconomics should precede macroeconomics. The major UK exam boards adopt different approaches:

**AQA A-level Economics** (7136) integrates micro and macro throughout both years, whilst **Edexcel** (9EC0) emphasises Theme 1 (Introduction to markets and market failure) before Theme 2 (The UK economy – performance and policies). **OCR** (H460) similarly prioritises microeconomic principles in Component 1.

Ha-Joon Chang, in *Economics: The User's Guide* (2014), critiques the conventional micro-first approach, arguing it can embed assumptions about rational actors and market efficiency that later require unlearning. However, most UK practitioners favour micro-first sequencing because:

- Microeconomic concepts (scarcity, opportunity cost, demand and supply) require less prior knowledge
- Students can relate microeconomics to personal experience (consumer choices, firm behaviour)
- Microeconomic analysis provides analytical tools (elasticity, marginalism) applicable in macroeconomics
- The mathematical demands generally progress more gradually in microeconomics

### Spiral Curriculum Architecture

Nancy Wall's research on Economics pedagogy emphasises revisiting core concepts with increasing sophistication. A spiral approach might introduce:

- **Year 12 Autumn**: Basic demand and supply (market equilibrium)
- **Year 12 Spring**: Elasticity and its applications (refined understanding of demand/supply responsiveness)
- **Year 12 Summer**: Market structures (applying demand/supply to different competitive contexts)
- **Year 13 Autumn**: Labour markets (demand and supply with derived demand)
- **Year 13 Spring**: International trade (comparative advantage using opportunity cost from Year 12)

This approach prevents the "coverage without depth" problem identified by Jim Riley in his tutor2u resources, where students encounter topics once superficially rather than building mastery through repeated engagement.

## Sequencing for Different Key Stages

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Economics rarely appears as a discrete subject at KS3, but elements feature in Citizenship and Geography curricula. When schools offer Economics at KS3, effective sequencing includes:

1. **Introduction to Economic Thinking** (Year 7): Scarcity, choice, opportunity cost through familiar scenarios
2. **How Markets Work** (Year 8): Basic demand and supply, price mechanism, introduction to competition
3. **The Wider Economy** (Year 9): Introduction to unemployment, inflation, government intervention

This sequence moves from individual decision-making to market interactions to economy-wide phenomena, building complexity progressively.

### Key Stage 4: GCSE Economics

All major GCSE specifications (AQA 8136, Edexcel 1EC0, OCR J205, WJEC Eduqas) follow broadly similar sequencing principles:

**Year 10 Foundation:**
- How markets work (demand, supply, equilibrium, elasticity)
- Market failure (externalities, public goods, information gaps)
- Labour markets (wage determination, trade unions)
- Government intervention (taxation, subsidies, regulation)

**Year 11 Development:**
- Economic objectives (growth, employment, price stability, balance of payments)
- Macroeconomic policies (fiscal, monetary, supply-side)
- International economics (trade, globalisation, exchange rates)
- Development economics (living standards, inequality)

This micro-to-macro sequence ensures students possess analytical tools before tackling policy evaluation. The AQA specification explicitly structures assessment around this progression, with Paper 1 focusing on microeconomic concepts and Paper 2 on macroeconomic analysis.

### Key Stage 5: A-level Economics

Peter Smith's work emphasises that A-level sequencing must develop genuine economic thinking rather than topic-by-topic memorisation. The standard A-level sequence typically follows:

**Year 12 (AS-level content):**

*First Term:*
- The economic problem and production possibility frontiers
- Price determination (demand, supply, equilibrium)
- Elasticity concepts and applications
- Market failure (externalities, public goods, information asymmetry)
- Government intervention in markets

*Second Term:*
- Introduction to macroeconomics (circular flow, GDP measurement)
- Aggregate demand and aggregate supply
- Economic performance indicators (unemployment, inflation, growth)
- Basic fiscal and monetary policy

*Third Term:*
- International trade theory (absolute and comparative advantage)
- Balance of payments and exchange rates
- Introduction to development economics

**Year 13 (Full A-level content):**

*First Term:*
- Market structures (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition)
- Labour markets and wage determination
- Financial markets and market failure
- Inequality and poverty

*Second Term:*
- Advanced macroeconomic models (Keynesian and Classical perspectives)
- Phillips curve and policy trade-offs
- Supply-side policies
- European Union economics (particularly relevant for OCR)

*Third Term:*
- Development economics (growth models, trade strategies, aid and institutions)
- Financial sector analysis
- Synoptic connections and essay technique
- Data response mastery

### Vertical Concepts Requiring Careful Sequencing

Several concepts demand particular attention in sequencing:

**Elasticity:** Introduced at GCSE as price elasticity of demand, then expanded to include cross-price elasticity, income elasticity, and price elasticity of supply at A-level. The mathematical applications grow more sophisticated, requiring secure prior understanding.

**Market Failure:** GCSE introduces externalities and public goods; A-level adds information asymmetry, moral hazard, adverse selection, and principal-agent problems. Students must understand efficient market operation before grasping failures.

**Government Intervention:** GCSE covers basic taxation and subsidies; A-level requires cost-benefit analysis, buffer stock schemes, regulatory frameworks, and evaluation of intervention effectiveness using welfare economics.

**Inflation:** GCSE introduces inflation measurement and consequences; A-level requires understanding of demand-pull versus cost-push inflation, expectations-augmented Phillips curve, and monetary policy transmission mechanisms.

## Practical Sequencing Strategies for Teachers

### Threshold Concepts and Bottlenecks

Jim Riley identifies several "threshold concepts" in Economics that students must master before progressing:

1. **Opportunity cost**: Understanding all choices involve trade-offs
2. **Ceteris paribus**: Ability to isolate variables in analysis
3. **Marginalism**: Thinking at the margin rather than in totals
4. **Market equilibrium**: Understanding how markets clear
5. **Real versus nominal values**: Distinguishing price level effects

Teachers should sequence lessons to ensure these concepts are secure before building upon them. For example, margin analysis should precede production theory, which requires understanding marginal cost, marginal revenue, and marginal product.

### Interleaving Economic Content

Research on memory and learning suggests interleaving related topics improves long-term retention compared to blocked practice. Rather than completing all microeconomics before beginning macroeconomics, effective sequencing might alternate:

- Week 1-3: Microeconomic fundamentals (demand and supply)
- Week 4-5: Introduction to macroeconomic indicators (GDP, unemployment, inflation)
- Week 6-8: Elasticity and market applications (micro)
- Week 9-10: Aggregate demand and aggregate supply (macro)
- Week 11-13: Market failure (micro)
- Week 14-15: Macroeconomic policy (macro)

This approach helps students see Economics as an integrated discipline rather than isolated topics.

### Assessment-Informed Sequencing

Alain Anderton's extensive work on exam preparation emphasises aligning sequencing with assessment demands. For AQA A-level, the context-based data response in Paper 1 requires students to apply multiple microeconomic concepts synoptically. Therefore, sequencing should regularly revisit earlier concepts rather than treating them as "finished":

- Teach elasticity in Term 1
- Return to elasticity when analysing tax incidence in Term 2
- Apply elasticity to labour markets in Term 3
- Use elasticity in evaluating government intervention policies in Year 13

### Mathematical Sequencing

Economics' mathematical demands increase substantially from GCSE to A-level. Effective sequencing builds quantitative skills progressively:

**GCSE Level:**
- Percentages and percentage changes
- Simple index numbers
- Reading values from diagrams
- Basic ratio analysis

**AS Level (Year 12):**
- Calculating elasticity coefficients
- Using formulae (e.g., multiplier = 1/(1-MPC))
- Interpreting gradients and areas on diagrams
- Working with index numbers (RPI, CPI)

**Full A-level (Year 13):**
- Using elasticity in calculations of tax incidence
- Working with Gini coefficients
- Calculating exchange rate changes
- Using economic data to calculate AD components

Teachers should audit mathematical prerequisite knowledge before introducing concepts. For instance, understanding price elasticity requires confidence with percentage changes and division of negative numbers.

## Subject-Specific Sequencing Challenges

### The Causality Problem

Economics deals extensively with causation, but students often confuse correlation with causation. Sequencing should explicitly develop causal reasoning:

1. **Single-direction causation**: Price changes cause quantity demanded changes
2. **Mutual causation**: Wage-price spirals, multiplier-accelerator interaction
3. **Reverse causation**: Does growth cause investment or investment cause growth?
4. **Spurious correlation**: Distinguishing genuine relationships from coincidence

Peter Smith recommends introducing simple causal chains before complex feedback loops, and explicitly teaching students to identify causal mechanisms rather than assuming relationships.

### The Evaluation Challenge

Economics assessment at GCSE and A-level heavily weights evaluation skills, but students struggle to move beyond simple "advantages and disadvantages" lists. Ha-Joon Chang's work emphasises that evaluation requires understanding different theoretical perspectives (Classical, Keynesian, Behavioural).

Effective sequencing builds evaluation skills progressively:

**Year 10/Year 12:** Basic two-sided analysis (benefits and drawbacks)
**Year 11/Year 12:** Introducing criteria for judgement (short-run vs long-run, stakeholder impacts)
**Year 13:** Theoretical perspective evaluation (Keynesian vs Classical views on government intervention)
**Year 13:** Contextual evaluation (policy effectiveness depends on economic circumstances)

### Time Lags and Dynamic Analysis

Students initially think in static terms, struggling with time dimensions in Economics. Nancy Wall's pedagogical research suggests introducing dynamics gradually:

1. **Immediate effects**: Price floor creating excess supply
2. **Short-run adjustment**: Firms responding to profit opportunities
3. **Long-run equilibrium**: Entry and exit determining market structure
4. **Time lags in policy**: Recognition lag, implementation lag, impact lag

This sequencing prevents confusion between instant market-clearing models and realistic policy scenarios where effects emerge over months or years.

## Sequencing for Different Learner Needs

### Supporting Lower-Attaining Students

For students struggling with abstract concepts, sequencing should:

- Maintain longer periods on concrete examples before introducing theory
- Use case studies extensively (e.g., specific firms, real policy decisions)
- Introduce one variable change at a time before considering multiple shifts
- Provide worked examples before independent application
- Revisit foundational concepts (opportunity cost, scarcity) regularly

Edexcel's approach in Theme 1 provides accessible entry points through familiar markets (housing, labour, transport).

### Stretching Higher-Attaining Students

Advanced students benefit from:

- Earlier introduction of competing theoretical perspectives
- More sophisticated mathematical applications
- Exploration of current research and policy debates
- Deeper engagement with economists' work (Smith, Keynes, Friedman, Sen)
- Econometric analysis and data interpretation

OCR's synoptic Component 3 particularly rewards students who can synthesise across topic boundaries, suggesting that higher-attaining students should regularly practice making connections between micro and macro concepts.

## Curriculum Leadership Considerations

Curriculum leaders designing Economics sequences should:

1. **Map vertical progression**: Document how concepts develop from introduction to mastery across key stages
2. **Identify prerequisite knowledge**: Ensure earlier units provide foundation for later topics
3. **Build in retrieval practice**: Schedule regular returns to core concepts rather than linear "coverage"
4. **Align with departmental assessment**: Sequence summative assessments to test cumulative knowledge
5. **Consider teaching allocation**: Place most challenging concepts where time allows thorough treatment
6. **Monitor sequencing effectiveness**: Use common assessments to identify whether students possess prerequisite knowledge

Jim Riley's tutor2u resources provide comprehensive topic-by-topic guidance that can inform departmental sequencing decisions, though schools should adapt these to their specific contexts and student needs.

## Conclusion

Effective sequencing in Economics education requires balancing multiple considerations: cognitive development from concrete to abstract, cumulative knowledge building, interleaving for retention, mathematical progression, and examination requirements. The most successful sequences position students to develop genuine economic thinking rather than memorising isolated facts.

As Alain Anderton's enduring textbooks demonstrate, great Economics teaching returns repeatedly to core principles, applying them in increasingly sophisticated contexts. Curriculum leaders should design sequences that allow concepts to mature through multiple encounters, building towards the synoptic, evaluative analysis that characterises high-quality Economics at A-level.

The micro-to-macro approach adopted by most UK exam boards reflects pedagogical consensus, but teachers should recognise this as a starting framework rather than rigid prescription. Ha-Joon Chang's pluralist perspective reminds us that Economics encompasses diverse theoretical traditions, and sequencing should ultimately prepare students to engage critically with economic questions, not simply reproduce standard models.
