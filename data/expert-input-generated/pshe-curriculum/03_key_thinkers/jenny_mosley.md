# Jenny Mosley

**Full Name:** Jennifer (Jenny) Mosley

**Current Role:** Founder and Director, Jenny Mosley Consultancies Ltd

**Known For:** Pioneering Circle Time methodology in UK schools; developing the Quality Circle Time model; establishing whole-school approaches to positive behaviour, self-esteem and relationships; creating frameworks for values-based education and school ethos development

---

## Biography and Career History

Jenny Mosley is one of the most influential figures in UK PSHE and Citizenship education, particularly regarding the social and emotional development of children. Trained originally as a primary school teacher, she worked in mainstream schools before specialising in special educational needs, which profoundly shaped her understanding of how self-esteem and belonging affect learning.

During the 1980s, Mosley began developing what would become her signature contribution to UK education: Quality Circle Time. Initially inspired by humanistic psychology and therapeutic circle work, she adapted these approaches specifically for classroom settings, making them practical and accessible for mainstream teachers. Her work gained significant traction during the late 1980s and throughout the 1990s, coinciding with increasing recognition of the importance of social and emotional learning in schools.

In 1994, Mosley established Jenny Mosley Consultancies, which has since trained thousands of teachers, teaching assistants, and school leaders across the UK and internationally. The consultancy delivers courses, in-service training, and whole-school development programmes focused on creating positive school cultures.

Her approach gained particular prominence during the introduction of the National Curriculum for PSHE and Citizenship, and later when Social and Emotional Aspects of Learning (SEAL) was rolled out by the Department for Education and Skills (2005-2010). Although SEAL was developed by others, Mosley's Circle Time methodology was frequently cited as a compatible and complementary approach.

Mosley has worked extensively with local authorities, academy chains, and individual schools, supporting them in implementing values-based education. Her influence extends beyond PSHE lessons into whole-school behaviour policies, anti-bullying strategies, and the creation of positive learning environments.

## Key Ideas and Concepts

### 1. Quality Circle Time Model

Mosley's most significant contribution is the Quality Circle Time model, a structured approach to circle-based classroom discussion that promotes emotional literacy, self-esteem, and positive relationships. Unlike informal circle activities, Quality Circle Time follows a specific five-phase structure:

- **Meeting Up:** Children enter the circle using a special ritual (such as a handshake or greeting), establishing the circle as a special, safe space
- **Warming Up:** Quick games or activities that build energy, inclusion and positive feelings
- **Opening Up:** The main teaching phase addressing specific PSHE themes, moral issues, or social problems through discussion, role-play, or problem-solving activities
- **Cheering Up:** Celebrating achievements, giving compliments, or engaging in affirming activities
- **Calming Down:** A quiet, reflective closing activity that helps children transition back to regular classroom work

This structure ensures emotional safety, maintains engagement across different learning styles, and addresses both the cognitive and affective aspects of PSHE learning. The approach emphasises that Circle Time should never be used punitively or to "tell children off" about behaviour—it is a proactive space for building skills and relationships.

### 2. Golden Rules and Positive Behaviour Systems

Mosley developed a widely-adopted framework of simple, positively-framed school rules known as "Golden Rules." Rather than focusing on prohibitions, these rules articulate desired behaviours:

- We are gentle (we don't hurt others)
- We are kind and helpful (we don't hurt anybody's feelings)
- We listen (we don't interrupt)
- We are honest (we don't cover up the truth)
- We work hard (we don't waste our own or others' time)
- We look after property (we don't waste or damage things)

This framework has been adopted by thousands of UK primary schools and directly influenced behaviour policy development during the 2000s. The Golden Rules are typically paired with visual reward systems such as "Golden Time"—a period of special privileges earned through following the rules—and consequence systems that are educational rather than punitive.

### 3. Self-Esteem and the Whole Child

Central to Mosley's philosophy is the belief that self-esteem is the foundation of learning, behaviour, and wellbeing. She draws on research from humanistic psychologists such as Abraham Maslow and Carl Rogers, arguing that children cannot learn effectively unless their emotional needs are met first.

Mosley identifies self-esteem as having several components:
- **Self-knowledge:** Understanding one's own feelings, strengths and weaknesses
- **Self-acceptance:** Acknowledging imperfections whilst maintaining self-worth
- **Self-confidence:** Willingness to try new things and take appropriate risks
- **Self-reliance:** Developing independence and problem-solving capacity

Her programmes systematically build these components through structured activities, positive teacher-child relationships, and peer support systems.

### 4. Relationship-Based Pedagogy

Mosley emphasises that the quality of relationships in schools—between adults and children, among children themselves, and between adults—fundamentally determines the effectiveness of teaching and learning. She advocates for:

- **Consistent, warm adult modelling** of respect, empathy and emotional regulation
- **Systems that acknowledge and celebrate** positive behaviour and effort
- **Opportunities for children to contribute** to classroom and school decision-making
- **Conflict resolution approaches** that teach skills rather than simply impose sanctions

This relationship-based approach aligns closely with attachment theory and trauma-informed practice, though Mosley developed her framework before these became mainstream in educational discourse.

### 5. Whole-School Ethos Development

Rather than treating PSHE as isolated lessons, Mosley advocates a "whole-school quality approach" where values, relationships and positive behaviour systems are embedded throughout school life. This includes:

- **Lunchtimes and playtimes:** Training midday supervisors in positive behaviour management; providing structured play activities; creating systems for addressing playground conflicts
- **Physical environment:** Designing welcoming spaces, displaying affirmations and achievements, creating quiet areas for reflection
- **Staff wellbeing:** Establishing Circle Time and support systems for adults, recognising that teachers model emotional literacy
- **Parental engagement:** Communicating values and approaches to families; providing workshops on building self-esteem at home

Her "Quality Standards Framework" provides schools with detailed criteria for evaluating and developing their ethos across these dimensions.

### 6. Moral Development Through Circle Time

Mosley positions Circle Time as a key vehicle for moral education and citizenship learning. Through carefully structured activities, children explore:

- **Moral dilemmas and ethical reasoning:** Using stories, scenarios and discussion to develop moral thinking
- **Rights and responsibilities:** Understanding that privileges depend on responsible behaviour
- **Democratic participation:** Making classroom decisions, voting on issues, understanding fairness
- **Empathy development:** Perspective-taking activities that build understanding of others' feelings and experiences

This approach anticipated many aspects of the Citizenship curriculum introduced in 2002, particularly regarding democratic participation and social responsibility.

### 7. Inclusion and Special Educational Needs

Drawing from her background in SEN, mosley emphasises that Circle Time and positive behaviour systems are inherently inclusive. She argues that:

- All children can participate in Circle Time regardless of ability when activities are carefully differentiated
- Children with behavioural difficulties particularly benefit from the predictable structure, positive reinforcement and opportunities to experience success
- Circle Time provides a non-threatening context for children with communication difficulties to practise social interaction
- The emphasis on emotional literacy supports children with social, emotional and mental health needs

Her publications include specific guidance on adapting approaches for children with autism, ADHD, and attachment difficulties.

### Books

**"Turn Your School Round" (1993)** – One of Mosley's earliest books, presenting a comprehensive guide to implementing whole-school positive behaviour systems. LDA (Learning Development Aids). This text established many of the frameworks later expanded in subsequent publications.

**"Quality Circle Time in the Primary Classroom" (1996)** – The definitive guide to implementing the Quality Circle Time model, including detailed session plans, activities, and guidance on establishing ground rules. **"Helping Children Deal with Bullying" (2004)** – Co-authored with Eileen Gillibrand, this book applies Circle Time approaches specifically to anti-bullying work, providing activities that build assertiveness, empathy and bystander intervention skills. Routledge.

**"Golden Moments for Children with Special Needs" (2005)** – Specific guidance on adapting Quality Circle Time and positive behaviour systems for children with various special educational needs. Positive Press.

**"Effective Circle Time for Young Children" (2005)** – Adapting the model for early years settings, addressing Foundation Stage requirements. Positive Press.

**"Personal, Social and Emotional Development" (2006)** – Linking Circle Time specifically to the EYFS framework, providing activities that support early years PSHE objectives. Positive Press.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on PSHE Framework Development

Mosley's work significantly influenced PSHE curriculum development during the 1990s and 2000s. When the non-statutory PSHE framework was established in 2000, many of its learning outcomes around relationships, emotional literacy, and participation aligned closely with outcomes systematically addressed through Quality Circle Time.

The Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA) cited Circle Time in several guidance documents as an effective pedagogical approach for delivering PSHE outcomes. Local authorities including Hampshire, Wiltshire, and Bristol specifically commissioned Mosley to deliver authority-wide training during this period.

### Relationship with SEAL (Social and Emotional Aspects of Learning)

When the Primary National Strategy introduced SEAL (2005), Mosley's Circle Time methodology was widely recognised as complementary. Many schools implementing SEAL used Quality Circle Time as the primary delivery vehicle for SEAL themes including new beginnings, getting on and falling out, and going for goals.

While SEAL provided curriculum content and learning objectives, Mosley's structured Circle Time approach offered a proven pedagogical framework for delivering this content. Several local authorities produced guidance explicitly mapping SEAL objectives to Quality Circle Time activities.

### Behaviour Policy Influence

Mosley's Golden Rules framework and positive behaviour approaches influenced DfES guidance during the 2000s, particularly around reducing exclusions and creating inclusive school cultures. Her emphasis on rewards over sanctions aligned with the behaviourist principles being promoted through the National Strategies but added a humanistic, relationship-focused dimension.

The "Excellence and Enjoyment" primary strategy (DfES, 2003) emphasised school ethos and values education in ways consistent with Mosley's whole-school approach. Many of the case studies featured in strategy documentation highlighted schools using Mosley's frameworks.

### Anti-Bullying Initiatives

Mosley's work on using Circle Time for anti-bullying directly influenced Anti-Bullying Week initiatives and guidance from Anti-Bullying Alliance. Her emphasis on empowering bystanders, building empathy, and creating emotionally safe environments became mainstream in anti-bullying policy.

The "Safe to Learn" anti-bullying guidance (DCSF, 2007) recommended Circle Time as one approach for preventing and addressing bullying, citing the importance of regular dialogue about relationships and behaviour.

### Continued Relevance

Despite changes in government and policy direction, Mosley's approaches remain embedded in many schools' practice. The 2020 statutory Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education, and Health Education guidance's emphasis on safe environments, respect, and emotional literacy continues to align with Mosley's core principles.

Ofsted framework criteria around personal development, behaviour and attitudes, and school culture reflect concerns that Mosley has championed for decades. Schools implementing her frameworks often perform well against these criteria.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Humanistic Foundations

Mosley's pedagogy is rooted in humanistic educational philosophy, particularly the work of Carl Rogers. Key principles include:

- **Unconditional positive regard:** All children deserve respect and dignity regardless of behaviour
- **Non-judgemental acceptance:** Separating the person from their actions
- **Empowerment:** Helping children develop self-direction and problem-solving capacity
- **Holistic development:** Addressing emotional, social, moral and cognitive domains together

This contrasts with purely behaviourist approaches focused on external rewards and sanctions, though Mosley pragmatically incorporates behavioural techniques within her relational framework.

### Experiential Learning

Quality Circle Time exemplifies experiential learning methodology:

- **Concrete experience:** Activities and games provide direct experience
- **Reflective observation:** Structured discussion helps children process experiences
- **Abstract conceptualisation:** Linking specific situations to general principles and values
- **Active experimentation:** Role-play and problem-solving allow practice of new skills

This cycle, articulated by David Kolb, ensures learning is active, memorable and transferable to real-life situations.

### Safe Space and Ground Rules

Essential to Mosley's approach is creating emotionally safe learning environments through:

- **Clear ground rules:** Everyone has the right to speak; right to pass; listening without interruption; confidentiality within appropriate boundaries
- **Consistent structure:** The five-phase model provides predictability
- **Teacher modelling:** Adults demonstrate active listening, empathy and emotional regulation
- **Graduated challenge:** Starting with low-risk activities before moving to more personal sharing

These conditions enable children to take the emotional risks necessary for genuine PSHE learning—sharing feelings, admitting mistakes, discussing moral complexity.

### Participatory and Democratic Methods

Circle Time embodies democratic participation:

- Physical circle arrangement symbolises equality
- Speaking rights are distributed through objects (e.g., "talking object" passes around circle)
- Decision-making uses voting and consensus-building
- Children contribute topics and issues for discussion
- Solutions to problems come from peer discussion rather than teacher imposition

This approach develops citizenship capabilities while addressing PSHE content.

### Strengths-Based Approach

Rather than focusing on deficits, Mosley's pedagogy emphasises:

- Catching children being good and publicly celebrating this
- Identifying and building on individual strengths
- Reframing difficulties as opportunities for growth
- Using affirmations and positive self-talk
- Creating success experiences for all children

This anticipates more recent developments in positive psychology and solution-focused approaches in education.

### Assessment and Evaluation

Mosley provides frameworks for evaluating impact including:

- **Observation schedules** for noting behaviour changes and relationship quality
- **Self-esteem audit tools** for children to self-assess across different domains
- **Whole-school quality standards** enabling schools to evaluate ethos development
- **Pupil voice mechanisms** capturing children's perceptions of safety, belonging and wellbeing

These tools support evidence-based practice and continuous improvement.

## Critical Perspectives and Debates

### Academic Research Base

While Mosley's approaches are widely used, academic research specifically evaluating Quality Circle Time has been limited. Some researchers have noted:

- Much supporting evidence is practitioner testimony rather than controlled studies
- Claims about self-esteem impact require more rigorous longitudinal research
- The relationship between self-esteem and academic achievement is more complex than early advocates suggested

However, broader research on social and emotional learning (e.g., Durlak et al.'s 2011 meta-analysis) supports the general principles underlying Mosley's work.

### Behaviour Management Debates

Critics from more traditional behaviour management perspectives have questioned:

- Whether positive-only approaches adequately address serious behavioural difficulties
- The evidence base for "golden time" reward systems
- Potential for inconsistent implementation across staff
- Time investment required for whole-school approaches

Mosley's response emphasises that her approach includes consequences but within a relational framework, and that investment in relationships reduces time spent managing crises.

### Sustainability and Consistency

Some schools report difficulties sustaining Circle Time practice due to:

- Curriculum time pressures particularly following 2010 curriculum changes
- Staff turnover disrupting whole-school consistency
- Need for ongoing training investment
- Challenge of maintaining enthusiasm when not externally required

Successful implementation appears to depend on strong leadership commitment and embedding Circle Time within broader school culture rather than treating it as an add-on programme.
