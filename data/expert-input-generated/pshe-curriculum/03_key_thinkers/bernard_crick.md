# Bernard Crick

**Full Name:** Sir Bernard Rowland Crick (1929-2008)

**Most Known For:** Chair of the Advisory Group on Citizenship (1997-1998); author of the seminal Crick Report; establishing citizenship education as a statutory subject in the National Curriculum; pioneering the concept of 'political literacy'

**Key Roles:** Professor of Politics at Birkbeck College, University of London (1971-1984); Professor of Politics at University of Sheffield (1984-1991); Chair of the Advisory Group on Citizenship Education (1997-1998); Advisor to the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA)

---

## Biography and Career History

### Early Life and Academic Formation

Bernard Crick was born in London in 1929 and educated at University College London, where he studied politics. He completed his doctorate at the London School of Economics, focusing on the American political system. His academic career began in the United States, where he taught at Harvard University and the University of California, Berkeley, before returning to the UK.

### Academic Career

Crick's academic reputation was established through his work as a political theorist and biographer. From 1965 to 1971, he held posts at the University of Sheffield before moving to Birkbeck College, University of London, where he became Professor of Politics and served until 1984. He returned to Sheffield as Professor of Politics from 1984 until his retirement in 1991.

Throughout his academic career, Crick was deeply engaged with democratic theory, political education, and the nature of citizenship. His seminal work 'In Defence of Politics' (1962) established him as a leading political philosopher, arguing for politics as a civilising force and a necessary activity in plural societies.

### The Citizenship Education Movement

In the 1970s, Crick became increasingly concerned with political education in schools. He chaired the Programme for Political Education (PPE) from 1974 to 1978, an initiative supported by the Hansard Society and the Politics Association. This work produced the influential document 'Political Education and Political Literacy' (1977), which first articulated many concepts later developed in the Crick Report.

### The Advisory Group on Citizenship (1997-1998)

Following the 1997 election of Tony Blair's Labour government, Education Secretary David Blunkett appointed Crick to chair the Advisory Group on Citizenship. The group was tasked with providing advice on effective education for citizenship in schools. The resulting report, 'Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools' (1998), universally known as the Crick Report, transformed citizenship education in England.

### Later Years and Recognition

Crick was knighted in 2002 for his services to citizenship education. He continued to write and advocate for citizenship education until his death in 2008, remaining actively involved in debates about British identity, immigration, and the nature of democratic citizenship. His final major contribution was his role in developing 'Life in the United Kingdom: A Journey to Citizenship' (2004), though he later distanced himself from how this was implemented.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Political Literacy

Political literacy was Crick's foundational concept, first developed comprehensively in the 1977 PPE report. He defined political literacy as encompassing political knowledge, skills, and attitudes. For Crick, a politically literate person possessed:

- **Knowledge**: Understanding of political institutions, processes, and concepts
- **Skills**: Ability to analyse political issues, evaluate arguments, and participate effectively
- **Attitudes**: Disposition towards active engagement and critical thinking about political matters

Crucially, Crick argued that political literacy was not about indoctrination or promoting particular political views, but about developing the capacity to engage critically with political issues. He emphasised that political literacy required both understanding and action – it was insufficient merely to know about politics without developing the confidence and skills to participate.

### 2. Three Strands of Citizenship Education

The Crick Report articulated three essential and interrelated strands of citizenship education:

**Social and Moral Responsibility**: Children learning from an early age self-confidence and socially and morally responsible behaviour both in and beyond the classroom, towards those in authority and towards each other. Crick emphasised that this was the essential precondition for the other strands.

**Community Involvement**: Learning about becoming helpfully involved in the life and concerns of their communities, including learning through community involvement and service to the community. This strand emphasised active participation and learning by doing.

**Political Literacy**: Pupils learning about the institutions, problems, and practices of our democracy and how to make themselves effective in the life of the nation, locally, regionally, and nationally, through skills and values as well as knowledge. This built on Crick's earlier theoretical work.

Crick insisted these strands were mutually reinforcing and should not be taught in isolation. The genius of this framework was its attempt to balance individual development, community engagement, and political understanding.

### 3. Active Citizenship

Crick championed 'active citizenship' as distinct from merely passive or status-based concepts of citizenship. For Crick, citizenship was not simply about rights or legal status, but fundamentally about participation in civic life. Active citizenship involved:

- **Participation**: Engaging in community activities, democratic processes, and public deliberation
- **Responsibility**: Taking ownership of community problems and contributing to solutions
- **Critical engagement**: Questioning, debating, and challenging existing arrangements
- **Practical involvement**: Not just theoretical understanding but actual engagement in civic activities

This concept represented a republican tradition of citizenship, emphasising duties and participation alongside rights. Crick argued that active citizenship needed to be taught and practised, not assumed to develop naturally.

### 4. Politics as a Civilising Activity

Drawing from his broader political philosophy articulated in 'In Defence of Politics' (1962), Crick viewed politics as an essential civilising force. He argued that politics – the peaceful resolution of conflict through compromise and conciliation – was a noble activity that prevented violence and tyranny. This perspective fundamentally shaped his approach to citizenship education.

Crick believed schools should teach students to appreciate politics as a positive process, countering widespread cynicism. He argued that conflict was natural in diverse societies, but political processes provided civilised means of managing disagreement. This was particularly important in his vision of citizenship education, which sought to develop appreciation for democratic processes.

### 5. Education FOR Citizenship, Not ABOUT Citizenship

Crick made a crucial distinction between education 'about' citizenship and education 'for' citizenship. Education about citizenship might simply transmit knowledge about political institutions and processes. Education for citizenship, however, aimed to develop actual citizens – people equipped and disposed to participate actively in civic life.

This distinction had profound pedagogical implications. It meant citizenship education required:

- Active, participatory pedagogies rather than passive learning
- Development of skills and dispositions, not just knowledge transmission
- Authentic engagement with real issues and communities
- Opportunities to practice citizenship within schools (e.g., school councils)
- Connection between classroom learning and lived experience

### 6. The Inclusive Nature of Citizenship

Crick advocated for an inclusive conception of citizenship that recognised diversity. In his work on immigration and identity, particularly 'Life in the United Kingdom: A Journey to Citizenship' (2004), he argued that British citizenship should accommodate multiple identities and allegiances. He distinguished between nationality (legal status) and national identity (cultural affiliation), arguing that citizenship should not require cultural homogeneity.

However, Crick did believe citizenship required certain common commitments: to democratic values, the rule of law, and mutual respect. His approach attempted to balance diversity with social cohesion, though this remained contested in subsequent debates about citizenship tests and immigration policy.

### 7. Minimalist National Curriculum Requirements

The Crick Report notably recommended a 'light touch' approach to curriculum specification. Crick advocated for 5% of curriculum time (approximately one hour per week) dedicated to citizenship education at secondary level. He deliberately avoided prescribing detailed content, instead outlining essential learning outcomes and broad themes.

This reflected Crick's educational philosophy that citizenship education required flexibility, responsiveness to local contexts, and space for teachers' professional judgment. He was wary of over-prescription that might reduce citizenship education to mere fact-learning or make it vulnerable to political interference.

## Key Publications

### Books and Major Works

**'In Defence of Politics' (1962, with subsequent editions in 1982, 1992, 2000, 2005)**: Crick's most influential work of political philosophy, arguing that politics is a noble activity essential for managing conflict peacefully in diverse societies. This established his reputation as a political theorist and provided the philosophical foundation for his later work on citizenship education.

**'Political Education and Political Literacy' (1977)**: The report of the Programme for Political Education, co-authored with Alex Porter. This document introduced the concept of political literacy and provided the intellectual groundwork for citizenship education in schools, predating the Crick Report by two decades.

**'Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools' (1998)**: The Crick Report, produced by the Advisory Group on Citizenship. This 78-page document transformed citizenship education in England, leading to its establishment as a statutory National Curriculum subject. The report outlined the three strands of citizenship education, recommended curriculum time, and provided detailed guidance on implementation.

**'Essays on Citizenship' (2000)**: A collection of Crick's writings on citizenship, democracy, and political education, bringing together his thinking over several decades.

**'Democracy: A Very Short Introduction' (2002)**: An accessible introduction to democratic theory and practice, aimed at general readers and students.

**'Life in the United Kingdom: A Journey to Citizenship' (2004)**: Report of the advisory group on the naturalisation process, which Crick chaired. This influenced the development of citizenship tests for immigrants, though Crick later criticised how these were implemented.

**'Crossing Borders: Political Essays' (2001)**: A collection exploring themes of identity, nationality, and citizenship in an increasingly interconnected world.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

**'Basic Concepts for Political Education' (1974)**: Published in Teaching Politics, outlining key conceptual foundations for political education.

**'The Presuppositions of Citizenship Education' (1999)**: Published in the Journal of Philosophy of Education, elaborating on the philosophical foundations of the Crick Report.

**'Education for Citizenship: The Citizenship Order' (2002)**: Published in Parliamentary Affairs, reflecting on the implementation of citizenship education following the Crick Report.

**'Citizenship: The Political and the Democratic' (2007)**: Published in British Journal of Educational Studies, one of his final major scholarly contributions.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Statutory Citizenship Education

The most significant impact of Crick's work was the establishment of citizenship as a statutory National Curriculum subject. Following the Crick Report in 1998, the government accepted its recommendations. Citizenship education became:

- A statutory foundation subject at Key Stages 3 and 4 (ages 11-16) from September 2002
- A non-statutory framework at Key Stages 1 and 2 (primary schools)
- Embedded in the National Curriculum with specific Programmes of Study and Attainment Targets

### The 2002 Citizenship Order

The Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA) translated the Crick Report's recommendations into the official Citizenship Order (2002). This established statutory requirements including:

- **Key concepts**: Democracy and justice; rights and responsibilities; identities and diversity
- **Key processes**: Critical thinking and enquiry; advocacy and representation; taking informed and responsible action
- **Range and content**: Political, legal, human rights, and social systems; governance at multiple levels; diverse identities

The influence of Crick's three strands was evident throughout this framework, though the language evolved. Notably, 'political literacy' was somewhat downplayed in favour of broader 'citizenship' terminology, partly to avoid political controversy.

### Teacher Training and Qualification

The Crick Report's implementation required significant investment in teacher development. Initial Teacher Training programmes were required to include citizenship education. Subject associations, particularly the Association for Citizenship Teaching (ACT), founded in 2001, developed partly in response to the need for specialist citizenship educators.

Crick advocated strongly for specialist citizenship teachers with appropriate training, though he recognised the subject would also be taught by non-specialists. The challenge of securing adequate teacher expertise remained a persistent implementation issue.

### Post-Crick Developments

Subsequent curriculum reforms modified but retained the essential framework Crick established:

- The 2007 National Curriculum review maintained citizenship as statutory at KS3-4
- The 2013 National Curriculum under Michael Gove retained statutory citizenship, though with reduced prescription
- The requirement for citizenship education has survived multiple curriculum reforms, testament to the strength of Crick's foundational work

### Beyond England

While the Crick Report specifically addressed England, its influence extended to other UK nations and internationally. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland developed their own citizenship education frameworks, often influenced by Crick's concepts even where their approaches differed. Internationally, the Crick Report has been studied and cited in citizenship education developments in numerous countries.

### Influence on Related Areas

Crick's work influenced developments beyond formal citizenship education:

- PSHE frameworks increasingly incorporated citizenship concepts
- Community cohesion policies drew on Crick's ideas about active citizenship
- Debates about Fundamental British Values (introduced 2014) referenced Crick's earlier work on core civic values
- Youth participation initiatives adopted the language of active citizenship

### Ongoing Debates and Critiques

Crick's influence also shaped ongoing debates and critiques:

- Concerns about whether citizenship education was adequately resourced and timetabled
- Questions about whether political literacy was sufficiently emphasised in practice
- Debates about whether citizenship education should be broader (global citizenship) or more focused (British values)
- Tensions between liberal conceptions of citizenship and more communitarian approaches

## Pedagogical Approach

### Participatory and Active Learning

Crick consistently advocated for active, participatory pedagogies. He argued that citizenship could not be learned purely through didactic instruction – students needed opportunities to practice citizenship. Recommended approaches included:

- **Discussion and debate**: Structured opportunities for students to discuss controversial issues and develop argumentation skills
- **Simulations**: Role-plays, mock elections, and simulations of democratic processes
- **Community projects**: Authentic engagement with local communities and real issues
- **Student voice**: School councils, student representation, and genuine participation in school decision-making

The Crick Report explicitly stated: "We aim at no less than a change in the political culture of this country both nationally and locally: for people to think of themselves as active citizens, willing, able and equipped to have an influence in public life."

### Controversial Issues

Crick strongly defended the importance of addressing controversial political and social issues in citizenship education. He argued in 'In Defence of Politics' and throughout his work that avoiding controversy was itself a form of indoctrination – it suggested there were no legitimate disagreements.

However, Crick emphasised the need for balanced treatment. Teachers should:

- Present multiple perspectives on controversial issues
- Create safe spaces for disagreement and debate
- Help students evaluate evidence and arguments
- Avoid imposing their own political views
- Develop students' critical thinking rather than telling them what to think

This approach was codified in the QCA guidance on teaching controversial issues in citizenship education.

### Cross-Curricular Connections

While advocating for dedicated curriculum time, Crick recognised that citizenship education connected with other subjects. The Crick Report recommended:

- Links with History (understanding historical development of rights and democracy)
- Connections with English (developing communication and argumentation skills)
- Relationship with PSHE (personal and social development)
- Integration with Religious Education (understanding diverse beliefs and values)

However, Crick was clear that citizenship education should not be entirely delivered through other subjects – it required its own distinct space in the curriculum.

### Assessment Challenges

Crick acknowledged the challenges of assessing citizenship education. Traditional examination-based assessment was potentially problematic for a subject emphasising participation, skills, and dispositions. The Crick Report suggested:

- Portfolio-based assessment capturing students' engagement and development
- Recognition of community involvement and practical activities
- Assessment of understanding and skills alongside knowledge
- Formative assessment supporting students' development as citizens

The introduction of citizenship GCSE in 2002 (later revised) attempted to address some of these challenges, though debate continued about appropriate assessment models.

### School Ethos and Organisation

Crick emphasised that citizenship education required a supportive whole-school ethos. The Crick Report argued that schools themselves should model democratic practices:

- Genuine student participation in school governance
- Fair and transparent systems of rules and discipline
- Opportunities for student voice and representation
- Democratic classroom practices
- Teachers modelling citizenship values

This reflected Crick's understanding that the 'hidden curriculum' of school organisation profoundly affected citizenship learning. Students could not become active citizens if they experienced schools as authoritarian institutions without meaningful participation opportunities.

## Legacy and Continuing Relevance

### Institutional Legacy

Crick's most tangible legacy is the continued statutory status of citizenship education in the National Curriculum. Despite multiple curriculum reforms and changing political priorities, citizenship education has been retained, demonstrating the enduring influence of the Crick Report's arguments.

The Association for Citizenship Teaching (ACT), established in 2001, continues to support citizenship educators and advocate for the subject. The Citizenship Foundation (now part of Young Citizens) and other organisations built their work on foundations Crick helped establish.
