# Lee Jerome

**Full Name:** Lee Jerome

**Current Role:** Senior Lecturer in Education at Middlesex University, London

**Known For:** Leading research and practice in citizenship education pedagogy, human rights education (HRE), controversial issues teaching, and critical approaches to political literacy in schools

---

## Biography and Career History

Lee Jerome is a senior lecturer in the Department of Education at Middlesex University, where he has established himself as one of the UK's foremost scholars in citizenship education. His academic career has been characterised by a sustained commitment to developing practical, theoretically-informed approaches to teaching controversial political issues and human rights in schools.

Jerome's background includes experience as a classroom teacher, which has informed his distinctly practice-oriented approach to educational research. This dual perspective—combining classroom realism with academic rigour—has made his work particularly valued by practitioners seeking to navigate the complex challenges of citizenship education.

At Middlesex University, Jerome has been instrumental in developing Initial Teacher Education (ITE) programmes that prioritise citizenship pedagogy, and has supervised numerous doctoral students researching aspects of political education and human rights teaching. He has maintained close links with schools through research partnerships, action research projects, and professional development programmes.

Jerome has held significant roles within the academic citizenship education community, including serving on the executive committee of the Association for Citizenship Teaching (ACT) and contributing to various government consultations on citizenship curriculum development. His work bridges academic research, policy development, and classroom practice in distinctive ways.

## Key Ideas

### 1. **Epistemic Insight and Political Literacy**

Jerome has developed sophisticated theoretical frameworks for understanding how young people develop political knowledge and understanding. His concept of "epistemic insight" refers to students' understanding of how political knowledge is constructed, contested, and validated. Drawing on the work of Michael Young and the "powerful knowledge" debate, Jerome argues that citizenship education must help students understand not just political content, but the nature of political knowledge itself—how claims are made, how evidence is used in political discourse, and how different political perspectives construct reality differently.

This approach challenges superficial approaches to political literacy that focus merely on institutional knowledge (how Parliament works) and instead emphasises critical understanding of political epistemology. Jerome contends that without epistemic insight, students cannot genuinely participate in democratic deliberation or evaluate competing political claims effectively.

### 2. **Teaching Controversial Issues**

Jerome is recognised as a leading expert on the pedagogy of controversial issues in citizenship education. Alongside his colleague Stephen Thornton, he has extensively researched how teachers navigate the challenges of teaching politically contentious topics without indoctrinating students or avoiding genuine political engagement.

His work identifies several key pedagogical approaches:
- **Balanced presentation**: Ensuring multiple perspectives are fairly represented
- **Procedural neutrality**: Teachers facilitating discussion without imposing personal views
- **Committed impartiality**: Teachers making their own position clear while ensuring fair exploration of alternatives
- **Critical thinking pedagogy**: Enabling students to analyse and evaluate competing claims independently

Jerome has documented how teachers' professional identities, institutional contexts, and external political pressures shape their approaches to controversial issues. His research has revealed that many teachers adopt defensive strategies, avoiding genuinely contentious topics or presenting them in sanitised ways that fail to engage students with real political disputes.

### 3. **Human Rights Education (HRE)**

Jerome has made substantial contributions to theorising and developing practical approaches to human rights education in UK schools. His work in this area distinguishes between different models of HRE:
- **About human rights**: Cognitive learning about rights frameworks and documents
- **Through human rights**: Experiencing rights-respecting relationships and environments
- **For human rights**: Developing commitment to defending and promoting rights

Jerome has been particularly critical of superficial approaches to HRE that treat rights as uncontested principles to be memorised rather than as frameworks requiring critical understanding and practical application. His research explores how students develop understanding of rights as political achievements with contested meanings, rather than natural or obvious entitlements.

He has collaborated extensively with organisations including Amnesty International UK and the Centre for the Study of Human Rights at the London School of Economics, developing teacher resources and professional development programmes that promote more sophisticated approaches to HRE.

### 4. **Critical Political Education**

Jerome advocates for what he terms "critical political education"—an approach that positions citizenship education as genuinely political rather than as neutral civics instruction. This perspective draws on critical pedagogy traditions (Paulo Freire, Henry Giroux) while remaining attentive to the specific constraints of the English schooling context.

Critical political education, in Jerome's formulation, involves:
- Examining power relations and structural inequalities
- Connecting personal experiences to political systems
- Developing critical consciousness about social justice issues
- Enabling students to understand themselves as political actors
- Questioning taken-for-granted assumptions about economic and social arrangements

This approach challenges the depoliticised version of citizenship education that focuses primarily on community volunteering and charitable action without addressing underlying political causes of social problems.

### 5. **The "Civic Empowerment Gap"**

Jerome has researched and written extensively about educational inequalities in citizenship education, particularly what he terms the "civic empowerment gap." His work demonstrates how students from disadvantaged backgrounds receive qualitatively different citizenship education from their more privileged peers, with implications for democratic equality.

Research conducted by Jerome and colleagues has shown that:
- Working-class students are more likely to receive citizenship education focused on behaviour management and social cohesion
- Middle-class students more frequently engage with sophisticated political analysis and participation opportunities
- Schools serving disadvantaged communities often deprioritise citizenship education due to accountability pressures around examination results

This research challenges assumptions that equal curriculum entitlement necessarily produces equal educational outcomes, highlighting how citizenship education can reproduce or challenge educational inequalities.

### 6. **Teacher Professional Identity and Citizenship Education**

Jerome has conducted influential research on how teachers construct their professional identities in relation to citizenship education. This work explores the tension between:
- Professional norms emphasising political neutrality
- The inherently political nature of citizenship curriculum content
- Teachers' own political beliefs and commitments
- Institutional expectations and pressures

His research reveals how citizenship teachers develop distinct professional identities that enable them to navigate these tensions, often adopting what Jerome describes as "strategic pragmatism"—making contextual judgements about when and how to address political issues based on their reading of school culture, community expectations, and student needs.

### 7. **Pedagogical Judgement in Citizenship Education**

Jerome's work emphasises the importance of pedagogical judgement—the contextualised, professional decision-making that experienced teachers employ when planning and delivering citizenship education. Against technicist approaches that seek to reduce teaching to prescribed methods, Jerome argues for recognising and developing teachers' capacity for situated judgement.

His research identifies various dimensions of pedagogical judgement in citizenship education:
- Determining appropriate levels of complexity for different age groups
- Deciding when to introduce teacher perspectives versus maintaining facilitator roles
- Selecting which controversial issues to address and when
- Balancing curriculum coverage with depth of critical engagement
- Adapting approaches based on immediate classroom dynamics

This emphasis on judgement recognises citizenship teaching as intellectual work requiring sophisticated professional expertise rather than mere curriculum delivery.

## Key Publications

### Books

**"Teaching Controversial Issues in the Classroom: Key Issues and Debates"** (2017, co-edited with Stephen Thornton), published by Routledge. This collection brings together international scholars to examine theoretical and practical dimensions of teaching controversial issues across different subjects. Jerome's contributions include chapters on teacher identity and political neutrality. The volume has become a key text in citizenship education ITE programmes.

**"Critical Practice in P4C"** (2016, with Lizzy Lewis), published by Routledge. Although focused on Philosophy for Children (P4C), this book connects to citizenship education through exploring how philosophical inquiry can develop critical thinking dispositions essential for democratic participation.

### Journal Articles

**"Fundamental British Values: The Colonial Legacy in English Education"** (2019), published in *British Educational Research Journal*. This article provides critical analysis of the government's Fundamental British Values policy, examining its colonial assumptions and implications for citizenship education pedagogy.

**"Political education and the epistemic crisis"** (2020), published in *The Curriculum Journal*. Jerome examines how citizenship education should respond to contemporary challenges around "fake news," post-truth politics, and political polarisation through developing students' epistemic understanding.

**"Knowledge and Curriculum in Citizenship Education"** (2012), published in *The Curriculum Journal*. This influential article applies social realist curriculum theory (particularly the work of Michael Young) to citizenship education, arguing for the importance of "powerful knowledge" in enabling political literacy.

**"What do citizenship teachers think they are doing? A Q-methodological study of teacher perspectives on aims and purposes"** (2012, with Helen Ramsden), published in *Citizenship Teaching & Learning*. This empirical study reveals the diverse perspectives citizenship teachers hold about their subject's purposes, identifying distinct professional subjectivities.

**"Human Rights Education: rhetoric and reality"** (2016), published in *Education, Citizenship and Social Justice*. Jerome critiques superficial implementations of HRE in schools and proposes a more critical, politically-engaged approach.

**"Teaching about poverty: UK teachers' perspectives on engaging with a controversial issue"** (2014), published in *International Journal of Inclusive Education*. This research examines how teachers navigate teaching about economic inequality and poverty—topics often avoided despite their relevance to students' lives.

### Reports and Policy Documents

**"Teach First or Teach for All?"** (2020), published by the National Foundation for Educational Research (NFER). While not exclusively about citizenship education, this report includes Jerome's analysis of how different teacher education routes address political education and social justice pedagogy.

**"Teaching and Learning Controversial Issues: Policy and Practice Guidance"** (2015, with Thornton), produced for The Citizenship Foundation (now the Association for Citizenship Teaching). This influential guidance document has shaped teacher education and professional development programmes across the UK.

Jerome has also contributed chapters to numerous edited collections on citizenship education, political education, and human rights education, and regularly publishes in practitioner journals including *Teaching Citizenship*.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Jerome's work has significantly influenced both policy debates and curriculum practice in citizenship education, though often in critical dialogue with rather than in direct service of government policy.

### Fundamental British Values

Jerome emerged as one of the most prominent academic critics of the government's Fundamental British Values (FBV) policy, introduced following the Trojan Horse affair in 2014. His research and commentary identified several problems:
- The undefined and potentially contradictory nature of "British values"
- The policy's implicit construction of "risky" communities requiring values instruction
- The tension between critical citizenship education and values transmission
- The colonial assumptions embedded in constructing "British" values as superior

His analysis provided intellectual resources for teachers and school leaders seeking to implement the policy in ways that maintained commitment to critical citizenship education rather than uncritical nationalism. Jerome's work has been cited in union guidance documents and professional association position statements on FBV.

### Curriculum Review Processes

Jerome has contributed to various curriculum review processes, including responses to the 2013 national curriculum review that relegated citizenship education to a non-statutory subject at Key Stage 4. His submissions and public commentary emphasised the importance of maintaining rigorous, knowledge-rich citizenship education with adequate curriculum time.

Through his roles in professional associations, Jerome has advocated for:
- Maintaining citizenship as a statutory curriculum subject across all key stages
- Recognition of citizenship education as requiring specialist subject knowledge
- Protection of curriculum time for substantive political education
- Support for specialist citizenship teacher education

### Teacher Education Standards

Jerome's research on teacher professional identity and pedagogical judgement has influenced thinking about what effective citizenship teacher education requires. His work challenges generic competency frameworks that fail to recognise the distinctive pedagogical demands of citizenship education, particularly around managing controversial issues and developing political literacy.

His publications are widely used in university-based citizenship education ITE programmes, particularly in relation to preparing trainees to teach controversial issues and human rights.

## Pedagogical Approach

Jerome's pedagogical approach is characterised by several distinctive features that distinguish it from more conservative or instrumental approaches to citizenship education.

### Dialogic Pedagogy

Jerome advocates strongly for dialogic approaches to citizenship education—teaching methods that prioritise genuine discussion, deliberation, and collaborative knowledge construction rather than transmission of predetermined content. Drawing on the work of Robin Alexander and Paulo Freire, he emphasises creating classroom environments where:
- Multiple perspectives are genuinely explored rather than superficially surveyed
- Students develop skills in reasoned argumentation
- Political concepts are tested through application to real cases
- Teachers facilitate rather than dominate discussion

His classroom-based research has documented effective practices in dialogic citizenship teaching while also identifying common barriers, including examination pressures, large class sizes, and teachers' anxieties about losing control of discussions.

### Critical Inquiry

Jerome promotes inquiry-based approaches that position students as active investigators of political issues rather than passive recipients of civic knowledge. This involves:
- Starting with authentic political questions rather than predetermined answers
- Engaging with real political texts, speeches, and primary sources
- Developing students' capacity to analyse and evaluate political claims
- Connecting abstract political concepts to concrete cases and controversies

This approach aligns with wider traditions of inquiry-based learning while maintaining focus on the distinctive epistemology of political knowledge.

### Experiential and Participatory Methods

While emphasising intellectual rigour and conceptual understanding, Jerome also recognises the importance of experiential learning and authentic participation in citizenship education. His work discusses the value of:
- School councils and other democratic governance structures
- Community engagement and research projects
- Simulation activities and role-play
- Campaigns and advocacy projects

However, Jerome is critical of approaches that prioritise participation without critical reflection, or that limit students to "safe" forms of participation (charity fundraising, litter collection) while avoiding genuinely political action.

### Assessment for Critical Thinking

Jerome has written about the challenges of assessing citizenship learning in ways that recognise complexity and critical thinking rather than merely rewarding reproduction of taught content. He advocates for assessment approaches that:
- Require application of concepts to novel political situations
- Value argumentation quality over "correct" political positions
- Recognise multiple valid interpretations of political issues
- Assess development of political literacy over time

His work has been critical of assessment regimes that reduce citizenship education to memorisation of facts about political institutions.

## Relationship to Other Key Figures

Jerome's work exists in dialogue with various traditions and scholars in citizenship education:

### David Kerr and Ian Davies

Jerome builds on the foundational work of David Kerr (who led citizenship education research at NFER) and Ian Davies (York St John University) in establishing citizenship education as a distinct curriculum field. However, Jerome tends toward more explicitly critical political orientations than some earlier citizenship education scholarship.

### Michael Young and Social Realism

Jerome has engaged extensively with Michael Young's social realist curriculum theory, applying concepts of "powerful knowledge" to citizenship education. This provides theoretical grounding for defending disciplinary knowledge in citizenship against both progressive approaches emphasising only skills and conservative approaches focused on socialisation.

### Audrey Osler and Hugh Starkey

Jerome's work on human rights education intersects with the significant contributions of Audrey Osler and Hugh Starkey at UCL's Institute of Education. While sharing commitment to HRE as central to citizenship education, Jerome perhaps emphasises more explicitly the contested political nature of rights.

### Bernard Crick

Jerome engages critically with Bernard Crick's foundational work establishing citizenship education in the national curriculum. While recognising Crick's achievement in establishing the subject, Jerome has questioned whether Crick's framework adequately addresses structural inequalities and power relations.

## Contemporary Relevance and Ongoing Work

Jerome's work has become increasingly relevant as citizenship education faces multiple contemporary challenges:

### Post-Brexit Political Education

Following the 2016 EU referendum, Jerome has contributed to debates about how schools should address political divisions and teach about the UK's relationship with Europe. His emphasis on teaching controversial issues through balanced exploration of multiple perspectives offers practical guidance for teachers navigating polarised communities.

### Digital Citizenship and Misinformation

Jerome's work on epistemic understanding in political education addresses urgent contemporary concerns about misinformation, conspiracy theories, and declining trust in institutions. His approach emphasises developing students' capacity to evaluate sources, understand how political claims are constructed, and navigate disagreement.

### Decolonising the Curriculum

Jerome has contributed to discussions about decolonising citizenship education, examining how colonial assumptions persist in constructions of "British values" and political knowledge. His work encourages critical examination of whose knowledge and experiences are centred in citizenship curricula.

### Education Policy Context

Jerome's critical scholarship offers resources for understanding and responding to the increasingly regulated and politically contentious context of English education, including:
- Prevent duty requirements affecting how teachers address terrorism and extremism
- Fundamental British Values mandates
- Restrictions on "political" activity in schools
- Culture wars around topics including empire, racism, and gender

His work helps teachers maintain commitment to critical citizenship education within challenging policy environments.

## Impact on Practice

Jerome's work has practical impact through multiple channels:

**Teacher Education**: His publications are widely used in university-based citizenship education courses, particularly at PGCE and Masters levels. Many citizenship teachers cite Jerome's work on controversial issues as formative in their professional development.

**Professional Development**: Jerome regularly delivers continuing professional development (CPD) for teachers, school leaders, and local authority advisers, translating research insights into practical strategies.

**Resource Development**: Jerome has contributed to developing teaching resources published by organisations including Amnesty International UK, the Association for Citizenship Teaching, and Oxfam Education, ensuring research-informed approaches reach classroom practitioners.

**School Partnerships**: Through action research projects with partnership schools, Jerome works directly with teachers to develop and evaluate innovative citizenship education approaches.
