# Jonathan Charlesworth

**Full Name:** Jonathan Charlesworth

**Current Role:** Chief Executive, PSHE Association (2013–present)

**Known For:** Leading the professionalisation of PSHE education in England; advocacy for statutory Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education; championing evidence-based approaches to mental health education; strategic leadership during the implementation of statutory RSE (2020); establishing quality assurance frameworks for PSHE delivery.

## Biography and Career History

Jonathan Charlesworth has served as Chief Executive of the PSHE Association since 2013, positioning the organisation as the national body for personal, social, health and economic education in England. Under his leadership, the PSHE Association has grown from a small membership organisation into the authoritative voice on PSHE education, providing guidance to over 20,000 schools and influencing government policy at the highest levels.

Prior to joining the PSHE Association, Charlesworth worked in education policy and advocacy roles, though he has maintained a deliberately low public profile regarding his earlier career, focusing attention instead on institutional credibility rather than personal narrative. His appointment came at a critical juncture when PSHE remained non-statutory and highly variable in quality across schools.

Charlesworth's tenure has been characterised by strategic positioning of the PSHE Association as an evidence-led, politically neutral organisation that works across party lines. This approach proved crucial during the campaign for statutory status for relationships and sex education, which culminated in the Children and Social Work Act 2017 and subsequent implementation in September 2020.

His leadership through the COVID-19 pandemic saw the PSHE Association rapidly produce guidance on remote PSHE delivery, mental health support during lockdowns, and the safe return to classroom-based RSE teaching. The organisation published specific frameworks for addressing pandemic-related trauma, bereavement, and social isolation through PSHE curricula.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. PSHE as a Curriculum Entitlement

Charlesworth has consistently articulated PSHE education as a fundamental curriculum entitlement for all pupils, not merely a pastoral add-on. He argues that PSHE provides the curriculum space where children and young people develop the knowledge, skills, and attributes needed to manage their lives now and in the future. Under his leadership, the PSHE Association developed the Programme of Study for PSHE Education (Key Stages 1-5), published in 2014 and updated in 2020, which provides a comprehensive framework organised around three core themes: Health and Wellbeing, Relationships, and Living in the Wider World (which includes economic wellbeing and careers education).

This framework explicitly positions PSHE as academically rigorous, requiring careful curriculum planning, assessment for learning, and qualified teaching—countering the historical perception of PSHE as requiring little expertise or preparation.

### 2. The Case for Statutory PSHE

Charlesworth led a sustained, evidence-based campaign for PSHE to achieve statutory status in the national curriculum. Working with the All-Party Parliamentary Group (APPG) on PSHE Education, he presented research demonstrating that non-statutory status resulted in inadequate curriculum time (often fewer than 20 hours per year), teaching by non-specialist staff with insufficient training, and PSHE being the first subject dropped when timetable pressures emerged.

Whilst the 2017 legislation made only Relationships Education, RSE, and Health Education statutory (leaving economic wellbeing and careers components non-statutory within PSHE), Charlesworth has emphasised this as significant progress whilst continuing to advocate for fully statutory PSHE. His pragmatic approach recognises political realities whilst maintaining long-term ambitions.

### 3. Quality Assurance and the Quality Mark

In 2014, under Charlesworth's direction, the PSHE Association launched the PSHE Association Quality Mark, a rigorous quality assurance scheme that recognises schools demonstrating high-quality PSHE provision. The scheme requires schools to provide evidence across multiple criteria including curriculum planning, staff expertise, assessment processes, leadership commitment, and pupil voice.

By 2024, over 500 schools had achieved the Quality Mark, establishing a network of exemplar practice. Charlesworth positioned this scheme as addressing the "postcode lottery" of PSHE provision, creating demonstrable standards that Ofsted inspectors, governors, and parents could reference.

### 4. Teacher Subject Specialism and Professional Development

Charlesworth has championed the professionalisation of PSHE teaching, arguing that effective PSHE delivery requires specialist knowledge and pedagogical skills. Under his leadership, the PSHE Association developed the PSHE Association Teacher Competency Framework (2015), identifying six core competencies: curriculum knowledge; pedagogy; safeguarding and confidentiality; managing sensitive discussions; assessment for learning; and creating inclusive classrooms.

The Association subsequently established the PSHE Association Training Programme, offering certificates in teaching PSHE at primary and secondary levels. Charlesworth has argued that whilst PSHE can be taught by non-specialists with appropriate training, schools should designate PSHE subject leaders with dedicated time, just as with mathematics or science.

### 5. Evidence-Based Mental Health Education

Charlesworth has been particularly influential in shaping approaches to mental health education in schools. He has consistently cautioned against well-intentioned but potentially harmful approaches, such as encouraging young people to diagnose mental health conditions in themselves or peers, or conflating mental health education with therapeutic intervention.

Instead, he advocates for curricula that: develop understanding of mental health as a continuum; teach evidence-based strategies for maintaining good mental health; reduce stigma; ensure young people know how to access appropriate support; and develop emotional literacy and resilience. The PSHE Association's guidance documents, produced under his leadership, explicitly reference research from organisations including the Anna Freud Centre and evidence reviews from Public Health England.

Following the government's Green Paper on mental health (2017), Charlesworth worked closely with the Department for Education and Department of Health and Social Care to ensure PSHE education was positioned as universal, preventative mental health provision, distinct from but complementary to targeted mental health support services.

### 6. Age-Appropriate, Inclusive RSE

During the development and implementation of statutory RSE, Charlesworth navigated highly contentious territory. He consistently defended the principle that RSE must be inclusive of all pupils, including LGBT+ young people, whilst also respecting the legal right of faith schools to teach RSE within their religious framework (provided statutory content is covered).

The PSHE Association's guidance emphasises that "age-appropriate" does not mean delaying topics until secondary school, but rather teaching content in ways matched to pupils' developmental stage. For example, primary-age children learn about different family structures including same-sex parents, whilst detailed content about sexual orientation and gender identity is introduced during secondary education.

Charlesworth defended schools and teachers during the protests outside primary schools in Birmingham and elsewhere (2019), arguing that professionally planned RSE curricula are designed with child development in mind and that parental concerns should be addressed through dialogue, transparency about curriculum content, and respect for the legal framework.

### 7. PSHE Education as Safeguarding

Charlesworth has positioned high-quality PSHE education as fundamental to safeguarding, providing the curriculum space where schools can proactively address issues including healthy and unhealthy relationships, consent, online safety, child sexual exploitation, so-called "honour-based" violence, female genital mutilation, and radicalisation.

Under his leadership, the PSHE Association developed specific guidance on teaching about consent (2015, updated 2020), which emphasised that consent education begins in primary school through age-appropriate learning about bodily autonomy, permission, and respecting others' boundaries. This framework influenced the inclusion of consent within the statutory RSE curriculum.

He has argued that PSHE provides opportunities for pupils to disclose concerns or recognise they are experiencing harm, provided teachers have appropriate training in safeguarding procedures and confidentiality protocols are clearly established.

### 8. Assessment in PSHE Education

Charlesworth has addressed the complex question of assessment in PSHE, arguing against grading or examining pupils' personal views, values, or circumstances, whilst maintaining that schools must assess whether pupils have learned what was taught. The PSHE Association's guidance, developed under his leadership, distinguishes between assessing knowledge and understanding (which can and should be assessed) and assessing personal attitudes or private information (which is inappropriate).

The recommended approach involves baseline and endpoint assessment, pupil self-assessment, and assessment of pupils' ability to apply learning to scenarios, rather than assessment of their personal choices or circumstances.

### 9. Partnership with Parents and Carers

Throughout his tenure, Charlesworth has emphasised that schools should work in partnership with parents and carers on PSHE education, particularly RSE. The PSHE Association produced detailed guidance on parental consultation and engagement, recommending that schools: communicate curriculum content clearly; offer opportunities for parents to view resources; explain the rationale for topics; and address concerns respectfully.

However, he has also been clear that whilst schools should consult parents, the final decisions on curriculum content rest with schools (within the legal framework), and that the right to withdraw from sex education (though not relationships or health education) applies only until three terms before a pupil turns 16.

### 10. Economic Wellbeing and Financial Education

Charlesworth has consistently advocated for economic wellbeing education as an essential component of PSHE, though this remains non-statutory. Under his leadership, the PSHE Association developed comprehensive guidance on teaching about personal finance, employability, career development, and economic understanding.

He has worked with partners including the Money and Pensions Service (formerly Money Advice Service) and Young Enterprise to promote financial education, arguing that understanding concepts such as budgeting, saving, credit, debt, and financial planning is essential for young people's future wellbeing, particularly given increasing economic precarity.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Statutory RSE and Health Education (2020)

Charlesworth's most significant policy achievement was contributing to the campaign that resulted in Relationships Education, RSE, and Health Education becoming statutory. Whilst multiple organisations and parliamentarians were involved, the PSHE Association, under his leadership, provided the technical curriculum expertise and evidence base that shaped the final statutory guidance.

The PSHE Association's consultation response to the Department for Education's draft guidance (2018) resulted in several amendments, including strengthened content on consent, online safety, and mental health. Charlesworth appeared before parliamentary committees and worked directly with civil servants to ensure the guidance was workable for schools.

### Ofsted Inspection Framework

Charlesworth has worked with Ofsted to ensure PSHE education is appropriately considered within inspection frameworks. The 2019 Education Inspection Framework includes PSHE within the "personal development" judgement, and Ofsted's *Review of Sexual Abuse in Schools and Colleges* (2021) emphasised the importance of high-quality RSE—findings that reflected positions Charlesworth had long advocated.

### National Curriculum Reviews

During the 2013 National Curriculum review, Charlesworth submitted detailed evidence arguing for PSHE's inclusion as a foundation subject. Whilst unsuccessful in achieving statutory status for all PSHE at that time, the submission established positions and evidence bases that later proved influential.

### Public Health and Safeguarding Policy

Charlesworth's framing of PSHE as preventative public health education has influenced policy documents from Public Health England (now Office for Health Improvement and Disparities) and safeguarding guidance from the Department for Education. His emphasis on evidence-based practice has helped counter moral panic-driven policy responses to issues such as knife crime, gang involvement, and substance misuse.

### Youth Violence and Serious Violence Strategy

Following government focus on knife crime and serious violence, Charlesworth contributed to policy discussions emphasising that PSHE education can help young people develop skills and knowledge to resist involvement in violence, understand consequences, and develop positive relationships, but that PSHE cannot compensate for inadequate youth services, policing, or social support.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Ground Rules and Safe Learning Environments

Charlesworth has championed pedagogical approaches that create safe, inclusive learning environments where sensitive topics can be discussed. The PSHE Association's recommended practice includes establishing ground rules (often co-created with pupils) that emphasise respect, confidentiality with safeguarding limits, and the right not to share personal information.

### Distanced Learning Techniques

To enable discussion of sensitive topics without requiring personal disclosure, Charlesworth advocates "distanced" techniques including scenarios, case studies, role play, and discussion of fictional or media examples. This approach allows pupils to engage with content whilst maintaining appropriate boundaries.

### Spiral Curriculum Model

The PSHE Association's Programme of Study, developed under Charlesworth's leadership, employs a spiral curriculum model where topics are revisited across key stages with increasing depth and complexity. For example, healthy relationships are introduced through friendship in Key Stage 1, developed through understanding of different relationship types in Key Stage 2, extended to intimate relationships in Key Stage 3, and explored in depth including sexual relationships and consent in Key Stage 4.

### Assessment for Learning

Charlesworth's approach to PSHE pedagogy emphasises assessment for learning rather than summative assessment. Recommended techniques include baseline assessment (establishing prior knowledge), questioning, pupil self-assessment, and assessment of ability to apply knowledge to scenarios. This approach recognises that PSHE aims to influence future behaviour and decision-making, which cannot be assessed in conventional ways.

### Whole-School Approach

Whilst focusing on curriculum, Charlesworth has consistently argued that effective PSHE requires a whole-school approach including school culture, behaviour policies, pastoral support, and modelling by staff. The Quality Mark criteria reflect this, requiring evidence of PSHE values embedded beyond timetabled lessons.

## Contemporary Relevance and Recent Work

### Online Safety and Digital Literacy

Charlesworth has positioned the PSHE Association at the forefront of online safety education, contributing to development of the UK Council for Internet Safety's guidance and working with organisations including the UK Safer Internet Centre. He has argued for developmental approaches that teach critical evaluation of online content, management of online identity and relationships, and recognition of online risks including exposure to harmful content, cyberbullying, and manipulation.

### Response to Culture Wars

During controversially termed "culture war" debates about RSE content, particularly regarding gender identity and LGBT+ inclusion, Charlesworth has maintained a position emphasising legal requirements, safeguarding of all pupils, and professional curriculum design. He has defended schools and teachers whilst emphasising transparency and parental engagement.

### Cost-of-Living Crisis

Recently, Charlesworth has highlighted the importance of PSHE education in supporting pupils affected by economic hardship, including teaching about financial decision-making, understanding of economic systems, and signposting to support services. The PSHE Association has produced guidance for addressing poverty sensitively within PSHE curricula.

### Teacher Recruitment and Retention

Charlesworth has increasingly emphasised the teacher recruitment crisis's impact on PSHE, as schools struggle to provide adequate time and expertise for PSHE delivery when overall staffing is stretched. He has called for initial teacher training to include PSHE pedagogy for all trainee teachers.
