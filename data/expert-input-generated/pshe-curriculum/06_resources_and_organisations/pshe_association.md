# PSHE Association

## Overview and Purpose

The PSHE Association is the national body for Personal, Social, Health and Economic (PSHE) education in England. Established in 2009, it provides authoritative guidance, quality assurance, and professional development for schools delivering PSHE education. The Association operates as a membership organisation supporting over 11,000 members including teachers, schools, and local authorities across the UK.

The PSHE Association was created in response to the need for consistent, evidence-based guidance following the introduction of the Social and Emotional Aspects of Learning (SEAL) framework and growing recognition that PSHE required specialist expertise. The organisation works closely with the Department for Education (DfE), Ofsted, and other national bodies to shape policy and practice.

## Key Functions and Services

### Programme of Study (2020)

The PSHE Association's flagship resource is its Programme of Study, most recently updated in 2020 following the statutory introduction of Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE), and Health Education. This framework provides comprehensive guidance covering:

- **Core Themes**: Health and Wellbeing, Relationships, and Living in the Wider World (including economic wellbeing and careers)
- **Key Stage Progression**: Detailed learning opportunities from Key Stage 1 through Key Stage 5
- **Statutory Requirements Integration**: Clear mapping to DfE statutory guidance for RSE and Health Education (2019, updated 2020)

The Programme of Study builds upon earlier work by practitioners including Jenny Mosley's circle time approaches and Bernard Crick's citizenship framework, whilst responding to contemporary challenges such as online safety, mental health awareness, and financial capability.

### Quality Assurance Schemes

The PSHE Association operates a Quality Mark scheme, established in 2011, which provides external recognition for schools demonstrating high-quality PSHE provision. The scheme assesses:

- Curriculum planning and coherence
- Teaching and learning approaches
- Assessment, monitoring, and evaluation
- Staff expertise and continuing professional development
- Leadership and whole-school commitment

This quality assurance framework addresses concerns raised by Ofsted inspections that have historically identified PSHE as an area of inconsistent quality across schools.

## Policy Influence and Advocacy

### Campaign for Statutory Status

The PSHE Association, led by Chief Executive Joe Hayman (2015-2021) and subsequently Katharine Adeney (2021-present), campaigned extensively for PSHE education to become statutory. This campaign achieved partial success when Relationships Education (primary), RSE (secondary), and Health Education (all schools) became compulsory in England from September 2020 under the Children and Social Work Act 2017.

Whilst economic wellbeing and careers education remain non-statutory (except in maintained secondary schools where careers guidance is required under the Education Act 2011), the Association continues to advocate for full statutory status for all aspects of PSHE.

### Evidence Base and Research

The Association draws upon research from academics including Lee Jerome (Middlesex University) whose work on controversial issues pedagogy informs guidance on teaching sensitive topics. Nick Boddington's work on values education and Jonathan Charlesworth's research on PSHE curriculum development have shaped the Association's approach to curriculum planning.

The Association's policy positions are informed by evidence reviews including:

- Brook, PSHE Association, and Sex Education Forum's RSE quality standards (2014, updated 2018)
- Research on the impact of PSHE on academic attainment and wider outcomes
- International comparative studies on health and relationships education

## Teacher Guidance and Resources

### Planning Frameworks

The PSHE Association provides detailed planning frameworks that help teachers develop age-appropriate, progressive curricula:

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)**
- Focus on self-awareness, managing feelings, healthy lifestyles
- Introduction to relationships and families
- Basic understanding of rules, fairness, and belonging

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)**
- Developing emotional vocabulary and self-regulation strategies
- Understanding physical and emotional changes
- Introduction to financial decision-making
- Rights, responsibilities, and British values

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)**
- Puberty, sexual health, and consent foundations
- Mental health and wellbeing strategies
- Financial planning including budgeting
- Active citizenship and democracy (linking to Citizenship National Curriculum)

**Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)**
- Sexual health, contraception, and healthy relationships
- Mental health, self-harm, and substance misuse prevention
- Post-16 pathways and financial independence
- Critical thinking about media, radicalisation, and extremism

**Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18/19)**
- Preparing for independent living
- Sexual consent and relationship negotiations in context of adult relationships
- Financial capability including student finance, credit, and debt
- Employability skills and workplace rights

### Teaching Methodologies

The Association advocates for active learning approaches consistent with educational theorists' recommendations. Guidance emphasises:

- **Safe Learning Environment**: Establishing ground rules and distancing techniques when discussing sensitive issues (building on Jenny Mosley's circle time principles)
- **Participatory Methods**: Role play, debate, case studies, and problem-solving activities
- **Spiral Curriculum**: Revisiting topics with increasing depth and complexity
- **Assessment for Learning**: Using formative assessment to understand pupils' starting points and progress

The Association's guidance on controversial issues draws heavily on Lee Jerome's pedagogical framework, distinguishing between:
- **Closed issues** (where legal/scientific consensus exists)
- **Open issues** (where legitimate debate exists)
- **Contested issues** (where values and beliefs differ)

## Professional Development and Training

### CPD Programmes

The PSHE Association offers extensive continuing professional development:

- **Subject Leadership Training**: Programmes for PSHE co-ordinators and leads
- **Specialist Topic Training**: Including RSE, mental health, substance misuse education
- **Whole-School Approaches**: Training for senior leadership teams
- **Regional Networks**: Subject associations and local authority partnerships

Training programmes are particularly crucial given that PSHE rarely features in initial teacher training. Ofsted's subject review of PSHE education (2013) identified lack of specialist training as a significant barrier to quality, a finding reinforced in subsequent school inspection reports.

### Consultancy Services

The Association provides bespoke school consultancy including:

- Curriculum review and development
- Policy writing support (RSE policy requirements, safeguarding integration)
- Stakeholder consultation guidance (statutory parental consultation for RSE policies)
- Staff confidence audits and training needs analysis

## Assessment Guidance

Recognising the challenges of assessing PSHE outcomes, the Association has developed sophisticated assessment frameworks that move beyond knowledge recall to evaluate:

- **Knowledge and Understanding**: What pupils know about health, relationships, and economic matters
- **Skills Development**: Communication, decision-making, risk assessment, critical thinking
- **Attitudes and Attributes**: Self-awareness, empathy, respect, resilience

Assessment guidance explicitly avoids grading personal attitudes or behaviours whilst maintaining academic rigour. This approach responds to concerns raised by Bernard Crick and others about the dangers of personalising assessment in citizenship and values education.

The Association recommends:
- Pre- and post-topic assessments to measure knowledge gain
- Self-assessment and reflection activities
- Practical demonstration of skills (e.g., help-seeking, refusal skills)
- Pupil voice mechanisms to evaluate curriculum relevance

## Exam Board Relationships

### Formal Qualifications

Whilst PSHE education itself is typically ungraded, the PSHE Association liaises with exam boards regarding related qualifications:

**AQA**: Offers Citizenship GCSE (9-1), where PSHE themes intersect with citizenship requirements including rights, responsibilities, and participation

**OCR**: Previously offered Personal Life Skills qualifications; the Association contributed to content development ensuring alignment with PSHE Programme of Study

**WJEC**: Provides Health and Social Care qualifications incorporating PSHE health education content

**Edexcel**: Citizenship GCSE specifications incorporate personal responsibility and health awareness themes

The Association emphasises that whilst qualifications can enhance status, core PSHE education should remain non-assessed at personal level to maintain the safe, exploratory learning environment essential for addressing sensitive topics.

## Relationship with Statutory Guidance

### DfE Statutory Guidance Integration

Following the statutory introduction of relationships, sex, and health education, the PSHE Association provides detailed guidance on implementing DfE requirements:

**Relationships Education (Primary)**
The Association's Programme maps comprehensively to statutory requirements including families, respectful relationships, online safety, and physical health

**RSE (Secondary)**
Guidance covers intimate relationships, sexual health, consent, and online pornography—sensitive areas requiring carefully planned progression

**Health Education (All Phases)**
Mental wellbeing, healthy eating, drugs/alcohol, health prevention, and basic first aid feature throughout the Programme of Study

The Association's materials exceed statutory minima, addressing gaps such as economic wellbeing, careers, and broader citizenship themes that remain non-statutory but pedagogically essential.

### Safeguarding Integration

The PSHE Association emphasises PSHE's crucial safeguarding role, providing guidance on:

- Recognising and responding to disclosures appropriately
- Teaching about abuse, exploitation, and FGM (female genital mutilation)
- Radicalisation and extremism awareness (Prevent duty compliance)
- Online safety and digital resilience (responding to UKCCIS guidance)

This positions PSHE as central to safeguarding policy implementation, drawing connections between curriculum content and Keeping Children Safe in Education (DfE, 2023) requirements.

## Challenges and Critiques

### Curriculum Time and Status

Despite statutory RSE/Health Education, the Association acknowledges ongoing challenges:

- **Curriculum Crowding**: Pressure on timetable space, particularly at GCSE level with EBacc subjects prioritised
- **Variable Quality**: Ofsted continues to identify inconsistent PSHE provision despite improvements
- **Senior Leadership Buy-In**: PSHE's non-statutory elements may be marginalised without strong leadership commitment

### Specialist Expertise

Jonathan Charlesworth's research highlights the "expert generalist" problem—PSHE typically taught by non-specialists across departments. The Association's training programmes attempt to address this but cannot fully substitute for initial teacher training integration.

### Political Sensitivities

The Association navigates politically contested territory, particularly regarding:

- **RSE Content**: Parental right to withdraw (primary) and age-appropriate content decisions
- **British Values**: Balancing critical thinking with statutory requirements to promote fundamental British values
- **LGBTQ+ Inclusion**: Responding to debate about age-appropriate content on gender identity and sexual orientation

Nick Boddington's work on values education informs the Association's approach: acknowledging diverse viewpoints whilst maintaining evidence-based health education and equalities legislation compliance (Equality Act 2010).

## Impact and Evidence

Research evidence suggests quality PSHE provision, as defined by PSHE Association standards, correlates with:

- Enhanced emotional literacy and mental health outcomes
- Reduced risky behaviours (substance use, unsafe sexual activity)
- Improved attendance and behaviour
- Stronger school community cohesion
- Better prepared school leavers (employability, independent living skills)

However, causal relationships remain difficult to establish given PSHE's integration within whole-school approaches. The Association advocates for longitudinal research to strengthen the evidence base.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Getting Started

Schools new to comprehensive PSHE provision should:

1. **Join the Association**: Access to Programme of Study, planning resources, and CPD is cost-effective compared to development from scratch
2. **Audit Current Provision**: Use Association audit tools to identify gaps and strengths
3. **Develop School Policy**: Particularly statutory RSE policy with parental consultation
4. **Establish Subject Leadership**: Designate a trained PSHE lead with appropriate time allocation
5. **Plan Progression**: Ensure spiral curriculum prevents repetition without progression

### Quality Indicators

The Association's quality mark criteria provide benchmarks:
- Dedicated curriculum time (typically 5% of timetable minimum)
- Specialist or trained teachers delivering sessions
- Age-appropriate, evidence-based resources
- Active learning pedagogies predominating
- Clear assessment and monitoring systems
- Regular stakeholder evaluation

### Resource Selection

The Association's resource assessment tool helps teachers evaluate external materials against criteria including:
- Evidence-base and accuracy
- Age-appropriateness
- Alignment with Programme of Study
- Inclusivity and equality
- Safeguarding awareness

This critical curation is essential given the proliferation of PSHE materials of variable quality from commercial and charitable providers.

## Conclusion

The PSHE Association functions as the authoritative voice for PSHE education in England, providing essential infrastructure for a curriculum area that has historically lacked the institutional support available to statutory subjects. Its Programme of Study, quality assurance mechanisms, and professional development offerings represent the gold standard for PSHE provision.

For curriculum leaders and teachers, engagement with the Association's resources and frameworks is highly recommended as the most evidence-informed and pedagogically sound basis for PSHE curriculum development. The Association's work bridges the gap between policy requirements, academic research (including contributions from Jerome, Boddington, and Charlesworth), and practical classroom implementation.

As PSHE continues to evolve in response to societal challenges—from mental health crises to financial literacy needs—the Association's role in maintaining quality, consistency, and professional standards becomes increasingly crucial to preparing young people for successful, healthy lives in modern Britain.
