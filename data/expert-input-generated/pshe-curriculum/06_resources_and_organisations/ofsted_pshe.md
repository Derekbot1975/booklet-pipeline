# Ofsted and PSHE

## Overview and Historical Context

The relationship between Ofsted (Office for Standards in Education, Children's Services and Skills) and PSHE education has been complex and evolving. Unlike core subjects, PSHE (Personal, Social, Health and Economic education) was historically a non-statutory subject, which created particular challenges for inspection frameworks. This changed significantly with the statutory introduction of Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE), and Health Education from September 2020 under the Children and Social Work Act 2017.

Bernard Crick, whose 1998 report fundamentally shaped citizenship education in England, argued that effective citizenship required both knowledge and active participation. However, the inspection framework for these subjects has struggled to balance standardisation with the inherently values-based and contextual nature of PSHE provision.

## Current Inspection Framework (2019 onwards)

### The Education Inspection Framework (EIF) 2019

The current Ofsted framework, introduced in September 2019 and refined in 2021, places significant emphasis on curriculum quality and implementation. Inspectors now evaluate schools through four key judgements:

1. **Quality of education**
2. **Behaviour and attitudes**
3. **Personal development**
4. **Leadership and management**

PSHE is primarily assessed within the **personal development** judgement, though its impact pervades all four areas. The School Inspection Handbook (2023) states that inspectors must consider "the extent to which the education that the provider offers helps learners to develop their character – including their resilience, confidence and independence."

### Personal Development Judgement

Under paragraph 138-144 of the School Inspection Handbook, inspectors evaluate:

- The curriculum for personal development, including PSHE, RSE, and citizenship
- How the curriculum promotes fundamental British values
- Preparation for life in modern Britain
- Development of responsible, respectful and active citizens
- Knowledge to keep physically and mentally healthy
- Recognition of online and offline risks

Nick Boddington, former senior HMI and PSHE specialist, has emphasised that inspectors look for a "well-sequenced and coherently planned" PSHE curriculum that builds knowledge progressively across key stages.

## Statutory Requirements and Inspection Expectations

### Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education

Since September 2020, inspectors have specific statutory grounds to evaluate:

**Primary schools:**
- Relationships Education (statutory)
- Health Education (statutory)
- Sex Education (non-statutory but recommended)

**Secondary schools:**
- Relationships and Sex Education (statutory)
- Health Education (statutory)

The DfE statutory guidance (2019, updated 2020) specifies that RSE must be "age-appropriate" and "sensitive to the needs and religious backgrounds of pupils." Ofsted inspectors check compliance with this guidance and examine how schools consult with parents and develop their policies.

### Citizenship Education

Citizenship remains statutory at Key Stages 3 and 4 as part of the National Curriculum. The Programme of Study (2013) requires teaching about:

- Parliamentary democracy and the UK constitution
- The role of law and the justice system
- Public finances and economic decisions
- Diverse national, regional, religious and ethnic identities
- Volunteering and active citizenship

Lee Jerome (University of London, UCL Institute of Education) has extensively researched citizenship inspection and notes that Ofsted's approach has historically been inconsistent, with citizenship sometimes "lost" within broader personal development judgements.

## What Inspectors Look For

### Curriculum Intent, Implementation and Impact

Drawing from the three I's framework of the EIF, inspectors evaluate PSHE through:

**Intent:**
- Clear rationale for PSHE curriculum choices
- Alignment with school context and pupil needs
- Progression and sequencing across year groups
- Coverage of statutory requirements (RSE, Health Education)
- Integration of protected characteristics under Equality Act 2010

**Implementation:**
- Teacher subject knowledge and confidence
- Quality of teaching and learning
- Resources and materials used (appropriateness and quality)
- Assessment approaches (though not requiring formal grading)
- Time allocation and curriculum space
- Specialist teaching versus form tutor delivery

**Impact:**
- Pupils' knowledge, understanding and vocabulary
- Application of learning to their own lives
- Development of respectful attitudes and behaviours
- Safeguarding awareness and help-seeking behaviours
- Preparation for adult life and future challenges

Jonathan Charlesworth, former PSHE subject lead with extensive inspection experience, emphasises that inspectors conduct "deep dives" into PSHE just as they would with any other subject, examining curriculum plans, observing lessons, scrutinising pupils' work and speaking with pupils and staff.

## Subject-Specific Inspection Guidance

### PSHE Subject Reports

Ofsted has produced several influential subject-focused reports on PSHE:

**"Not Yet Good Enough: Personal, Social, Health and Economic Education in Schools" (2013):**
This landmark report found PSHE required improvement or was inadequate in 40% of schools. Key findings included:

- Insufficient curriculum time
- Lack of specialist training for teachers
- Weak assessment practices
- Limited subject leadership
- Inadequate coverage of sensitive topics including sex and relationships education

**"Exploring the School's Actions to Support All Pupils" (2021):**
Examined personal development provision during COVID-19 recovery, highlighting the importance of mental health support and PSHE's role in rebuilding pupils' confidence.

**RSE-focused reviews (2021-2023):**
Recent thematic reviews have examined implementation of statutory RSE, noting variability in quality and concerns about some schools' reluctance to address LGBTQ+ content despite statutory requirements.

### Common Inspection Findings

Analysis of inspection reports reveals recurring themes:

**Strengths identified:**
- Well-planned, spiral curricula building on prior learning
- Confident, well-trained staff delivering sensitive topics
- Strong links between PSHE and safeguarding
- Active pupil voice in curriculum development
- Integration with wider pastoral support

**Weaknesses identified:**
- Insufficient curriculum time (often below the PSHE Association's recommended minimum of one hour per week per key stage)
- Over-reliance on external providers without quality assurance
- Weak subject leadership with inadequate monitoring
- Limited assessment and evaluation of impact
- Tokenistic or superficial coverage of complex issues
- Inconsistent delivery across year groups or classes

## Key Stage Considerations

### Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS)

While PSHE is not separately identified in the EYFS Framework (2021), inspectors consider personal, social and emotional development (PSED) as one of the prime areas. The curriculum should support:

- Self-regulation and emotional literacy
- Building relationships and cooperation
- Understanding feelings and behaviours

Jenny Mosley's Circle Time approaches and Quality Circle Time materials have influenced many primary settings' approaches to PSED and PSHE, promoting emotional literacy from the earliest years.

### Key Stage 1 and 2

Inspectors expect primary schools to:

- Deliver statutory Relationships Education and Health Education
- Teach age-appropriate content about families, caring friendships, respectful relationships
- Cover physical and mental wellbeing, including basic first aid
- Introduce online safety progressively
- Begin developing economic understanding and financial capability

The PSHE Association's Programme of Study (regularly updated) provides non-statutory guidance that many schools follow and that inspectors may reference.

### Key Stage 3 and 4

Secondary inspection focuses on:

- Statutory RSE and Health Education delivery
- Statutory Citizenship National Curriculum coverage
- Age-appropriate deepening of topics introduced in primary
- Preparation for adult life, including relationships, sexual health, financial decisions
- Understanding consent, the law, and personal safety
- Critical media literacy and online relationships
- Career guidance and economic understanding

At Key Stage 4, some schools enter pupils for qualifications including:

**AQA:** GCSE Citizenship Studies (8100) - full course examining citizenship themes, rights, responsibilities and political engagement

**Pearson Edexcel:** GCSE Citizenship Studies (1CS0) - includes contemporary citizenship issues and active citizenship projects

**OCR:** No current GCSE Citizenship offering (discontinued)

**WJEC Eduqas:** GCSE Citizenship Studies (C690QS) - covering life in modern Britain and active citizenship

Inspectors note whether schools offer accredited citizenship qualifications and evaluate the quality of teaching if they do.

### Key Stage 5

Post-16 PSHE is non-statutory but strongly recommended. Ofsted's "Keeping your top button done up" report (2020) on sexual harassment in schools and colleges specifically highlighted the importance of continuing PSHE in sixth forms to address:

- Consent and healthy relationships
- Sexual harassment and violence prevention
- Independent living skills
- Mental health and stress management
- Financial capability and debt awareness

## Evidence and Deep Dives

### Conducting a PSHE Deep Dive

When PSHE is selected for a deep dive inspection, inspectors typically:

1. **Meet with the subject leader** to discuss curriculum intent, sequencing, and rationale
2. **Review curriculum documentation**, including schemes of work, progression maps, and policy documents
3. **Conduct lesson observations** alongside the subject leader, focusing on pedagogy and pupil engagement
4. **Speak with pupils** about their learning, checking knowledge retention and understanding
5. **Scrutinise pupils' work** (recognising this may include posters, drama work, discussions rather than written work)
6. **Interview teachers** delivering PSHE about their confidence, training, and assessment approaches

Lee Jerome's research (2012, 2017) has consistently shown that inspection effectiveness in PSHE and citizenship improves when inspectors have subject specialist knowledge, though this remains inconsistent across the inspection workforce.

### Types of Evidence

Inspectors accept varied forms of evidence for PSHE:

- Curriculum plans and schemes of work
- Lesson observations and learning walks
- Pupil voice and discussions
- Photographs of displays, activities, and events
- Records of assemblies, themed days, and visitors
- Staff training records
- Parent consultation outcomes for RSE policy
- Links to safeguarding records and pastoral interventions
- Pupil surveys on wellbeing and safety

## Leadership and Management

### Subject Leadership

Effective PSHE leadership is crucial for positive inspection outcomes. Inspectors look for:

- **Strategic vision:** Clear rationale for PSHE curriculum choices linked to school context and pupil needs
- **Curriculum expertise:** Subject knowledge and understanding of progression
- **Monitoring and evaluation:** Regular review of teaching quality and curriculum impact
- **Staff development:** Training provision and support for all staff delivering PSHE
- **Resource management:** Quality-assured materials and appropriate budget allocation
- **Stakeholder engagement:** Consultation with parents, governors, and pupils
- **Policy development:** RSE policy meeting statutory requirements with parental consultation

Nick Boddington has argued that schools should avoid regarding PSHE as a "bolt-on" subject but instead ensure it has parity with other curriculum areas in terms of leadership time, professional development, and monitoring.

### Governor Responsibilities

Under statutory RSE requirements, governing boards must:

- Ensure the school has an RSE policy
- Consult with parents in developing the policy
- Review the policy regularly
- Ensure curriculum compliance with statutory guidance
- Handle parental withdrawal requests for non-statutory sex education components

Inspectors check governors' understanding of their RSE responsibilities and may examine minutes of relevant governor meetings.

## Safeguarding Connections

### PSHE and Safeguarding

Ofsted places significant emphasis on the relationship between PSHE and safeguarding. "Keeping Children Safe in Education" (DfE, 2023) statutory guidance explicitly states that children should be taught about safeguarding through the curriculum.

Inspectors evaluate whether PSHE effectively teaches pupils:

- How to recognise abuse and harmful situations
- Understanding of consent and healthy relationships
- Online safety and digital resilience
- How and where to seek help
- Recognition of peer-on-peer abuse, including sexual harassment
- Understanding of female genital mutilation (FGM), forced marriage, and honour-based violence
- Awareness of radicalisation and extremism risks

The curriculum must be age-appropriate but sufficiently comprehensive. Ofsted's "Review of Sexual Abuse in Schools and Colleges" (2021) found that sexual harassment and online sexual abuse were "normalised" among many young people, highlighting the critical importance of high-quality RSE.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Preparing for Inspection

**For Subject Leaders:**

1. **Document your curriculum clearly:** Create accessible curriculum maps showing progression, coverage of statutory requirements, and rationale for curriculum choices
2. **Evidence monitoring activities:** Keep records of lesson observations, work scrutiny, staff training, and curriculum evaluation
3. **Know your impact data:** Be able to discuss how you know PSHE is making a difference (pupil voice, behaviour data, pastoral referrals, safeguarding concerns)
4. **Understand the policy context:** Be thoroughly familiar with RSE statutory guidance, Keeping Children Safe in Education, and the Equality Act 2010
5. **Prepare subject-specific resources:** Have key documents ready, including schemes of work, assessment frameworks, and staff training records

**For Classroom Teachers:**

1. **Understand progression:** Know what pupils have learned previously and what they will learn next
2. **Use correct terminology:** Teach and use appropriate vocabulary for the topics covered
3. **Create safe learning environments:** Establish ground rules, use distancing techniques, and avoid pressuring pupils to disclose personal information
4. **Connect learning to real life:** Help pupils understand how PSHE learning applies to their own lives and future
5. **Check understanding:** Use assessment strategies that check knowledge retention and understanding without requiring personal disclosure

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Based on inspection evidence and expert commentary, schools should avoid:

- **Relegating PSHE to tutor time without adequate planning or training**
- **Over-relying on drop-down days** that lack coherence and progression
- **Failing to quality-assure external providers** who may deliver inconsistent or inappropriate content
- **Avoiding difficult topics** due to concerns about controversy or parental complaints
- **Neglecting staff training** in sensitive topics, particularly RSE
- **Treating PSHE as primarily safeguarding-reactive** rather than proactive skill and knowledge building
- **Inadequate time allocation** (less than one hour per week per key stage)
- **Lack of subject-specific monitoring** treating PSHE as if it doesn't need pedagogical expertise

## Assessing PSHE

### Inspection Expectations for Assessment

While Ofsted does not require grading or levelling of PSHE, inspectors do expect schools to:

- **Monitor pupil learning:** Know whether pupils are retaining and understanding key knowledge
- **Adapt teaching:** Use formative assessment to address misconceptions and gaps
- **Track curriculum coverage:** Ensure all pupils receive their curriculum entitlement
- **Evaluate impact:** Use a range of qualitative and quantitative measures to assess effectiveness

Jonathan Charlesworth has advocated for assessment approaches that:

- Check knowledge and understanding without requiring personal disclosure
- Use pre- and post-learning activities to measure progress
- Include pupil self-assessment and reflection
- Focus on whether pupils can apply learning to hypothetical scenarios

The PSHE Association guidance suggests baseline assessment, end-of-unit checks, and periodic whole-cohort surveys to evaluate curriculum impact.

## British Values and PSHE

### Statutory Requirements

Under Section 78 of the Education Act 2002 and DfE guidance on promoting British values (2014), schools must actively promote:

- Democracy
- The rule of law
- Individual liberty
- Mutual respect and tolerance of those with different faiths and beliefs

Inspectors explicitly check how schools promote these values, and PSHE provides a natural curriculum space for this. However, critics including Lee Jerome have argued that the British values agenda can be delivered tokenistically or superficially without engaging with genuine democratic participation.

### Practical Delivery

Effective integration includes:

- **Democracy:** School councils, voting processes, understanding electoral systems, engaging with local democracy
- **Rule of law:** Understanding why laws exist, consequences, rights and responsibilities
- **Individual liberty:** Personal autonomy, making informed choices, freedom within the law
- **Mutual respect and tolerance:** Equality and diversity teaching, challenging prejudice, understanding protected characteristics

## Protected Characteristics and Equality Act 2010

### Legal and Inspection Context

The Equality Act 2010 protects people from discrimination based on nine protected characteristics:

- Age
- Disability
- Gender reassignment
- Marriage and civil partnership
- Pregnancy and maternity
- Race
- Religion or belief
- Sex
- Sexual orientation

Schools have a public sector equality duty to:

- Eliminate discrimination and harassment
- Advance equality of opportunity
- Foster good relations between different groups

Ofsted inspectors check whether the PSHE curriculum:

- Teaches pupils about all protected characteristics
- Promotes respect for diversity
- Challenges stereotypes and prejudice
- Prepares pupils for life in diverse modern Britain

### LGBTQ+ Content

The statutory RSE guidance requires teaching about different types of families, including those with same-sex parents, and about LGBTQ+ relationships in age-appropriate ways. Despite concerns from some parent groups, Ofsted has been clear that schools must teach this content.
