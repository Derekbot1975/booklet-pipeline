# LGBTQ+ Inclusion in RSE

## Introduction and Legislative Context

LGBTQ+ inclusion in Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) became a statutory requirement in England from September 2020 under the Children and Families Act 2014 and subsequent DfE guidance. The **Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education and Health Education (England) Regulations 2019** mandate that all schools must teach about different types of families and relationships, including those with LGBTQ+ people, in an age-appropriate manner.

The DfE's statutory guidance *'Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education'* (2019, updated 2021) explicitly states that schools should ensure "teaching is sensitive and age appropriate in approach and content" whilst being "fully integrated into programmes of study" rather than delivered as standalone, marginalised content. This represents a significant shift from previous guidance, where LGBTQ+ content was often omitted or treated as controversial.

The Equality Act 2010 provides the overarching legal framework, protecting individuals from discrimination based on protected characteristics including sexual orientation and gender reassignment. Section 149 establishes the Public Sector Equality Duty, requiring schools to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of opportunity, and foster good relations.

## Curriculum Framework and Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2: Relationships Education

At primary level, the focus is on **diverse families and respectful relationships**. The DfE guidance (2019) requires pupils to understand that:

- Families can include single parents, LGBTQ+ parents, families headed by grandparents, adoptive parents, foster parents and step-parents
- Stable, caring relationships are important for children's wellbeing, regardless of family structure
- Different people have different characteristics, including sexual orientation and gender identity
- Respect for others is fundamental

Jenny Mosley's **Circle Time** approach provides an effective pedagogical framework for exploring difference and similarity at primary level. Mosley's emphasis on inclusive language, positive relationships, and creating safe spaces aligns well with LGBTQ+ inclusive practice. Her work on emotional literacy provides teachers with practical strategies for discussing difference without othering LGBTQ+ identities.

Primary schools must teach that marriage represents a formal commitment (legally available to same-sex couples since the Marriage (Same Sex Couples) Act 2013), though teaching about marriage should not privilege this above other stable relationships.

### Key Stage 3: RSE Development

At KS3, RSE builds complexity around relationships, identity development, and sexual health. The statutory guidance requires teaching about:

- Different types of committed, stable relationships
- The characteristics of healthy relationships, including in same-sex relationships
- How stereotypes, including those based on sex, gender, race, religion, sexual orientation or disability, can cause damage
- That intimate relationships should be entered into freely and not as a result of pressure

Nick Boddington's work on **citizenship education and identity** emphasises the importance of enabling young people to understand their own developing identities within diverse societies. Boddington (2012) in *'Developing Children's Identity and Citizenship Skills through Religious Education'* argues for pedagogies that allow exploration of difference without mandating particular viewpoints, an approach particularly relevant to LGBTQ+ inclusion where family values may differ.

### Key Stage 4: Advanced RSE Content

At KS4, RSE must address:

- Sexual orientation and gender identity, including the coming out process
- The law relating to sexual consent (Sexual Offences Act 2003), which applies equally to same-sex relationships
- The concepts of, and laws relating to, sexual exploitation, abuse, grooming, coercion, harassment, rape, domestic abuse, forced marriage, honour-based violence and FGM
- How to access appropriate health services, including LGBTQ+-inclusive sexual health services

GCSE Citizenship Studies specifications from all major exam boards now include content on equality, diversity, and rights. **AQA GCSE Citizenship Studies (8100)** requires understanding of the Equality Act 2010 and protected characteristics. **Edexcel GCSE Citizenship Studies (1CS0)** includes "diverse national, regional, religious and ethnic identities in the UK" and contemporary equalities debates. **OCR GCSE Citizenship Studies (J270)** explicitly addresses discrimination and the role of law in protecting minorities.

## Pedagogical Approaches and Best Practice

### Creating Inclusive Classroom Environments

Research by **Stonewall's School Report 2017** found that 45% of LGBTQ+ students face bullying, and many LGBTQ+ pupils report that their identities are never positively discussed in lessons. Jonathan Charlesworth's work with the PSHE Association emphasises that LGBTQ+ inclusion must be **normalised and integrated** throughout the curriculum rather than being an optional 'add-on' or one-off lesson.

Effective pedagogical strategies include:

**1. Language Matters**: Use inclusive language consistently. Say "partner" rather than "boyfriend/girlfriend", "parent/carer" rather than assuming heterosexual parenting, and "people" rather than "boys and girls" when discussing relationships.

**2. Representation Throughout Resources**: Ensure teaching materials routinely include LGBTQ+ individuals and relationships in examples, case studies, and images. The *No Outsiders* programme developed by Andrew Moffat demonstrates effective integration of diverse relationships across primary curriculum.

**3. Ground Rules and Safe Spaces**: Establish clear classroom expectations. Lee Jerome's research on controversial issues in citizenship education (Jerome & Starkey, 2021, *'Teaching Controversial Issues in Citizenship Education'*) emphasises the importance of structured discussion protocols. Jerome argues for **proceduralised approaches** where teachers facilitate exploration of issues without imposing views, whilst maintaining clear boundaries against discriminatory language.

### Addressing Challenging Conversations

Bernard Crick's seminal work establishing citizenship education in the UK (the Crick Report, 1998) emphasised **active citizenship and political literacy**. Crick's framework supports teachers addressing LGBTQ+ inclusion as fundamentally about human rights, equality, and democratic participation rather than purely as 'moral' or 'religious' issues.

When facing parental concerns or community resistance:

- **Anchor in statutory requirements**: The teaching is legally mandated, not optional
- **Emphasise educational purpose**: Schools teach *about* diverse relationships factually; they do not promote particular lifestyles
- **Reference the Equality Act**: Schools have legal duties to protect LGBTQ+ pupils and prepare all pupils for life in diverse Britain
- **Use the right to withdraw carefully**: Parents may withdraw children from sex education (not relationships education) but schools should explain what children will miss and the potential impact

The Court of Appeal judgment in *R (Eweida) v British Airways* [2009] and subsequent ECtHR ruling established that manifestation of religious belief must not infringe others' rights. This principle supports schools maintaining LGBTQ+ inclusion even when religious objections are raised.

### Age-Appropriate Content: Practical Guidance

**Reception and Key Stage 1 (Ages 4-7)**:
- Picture books featuring diverse families (e.g., *And Tango Makes Three*, *Mommy, Mama, and Me*)
- Discussion of different family structures in matter-of-fact ways
- Focus on kindness, respect, and identifying trusted adults

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)**:
- Exploring stereotypes around gender and interests
- Understanding that some people are LGBTQ+ and this is part of human diversity
- Addressing bullying language, including homophobic, biphobic, and transphobic language
- Beginning to understand that some children may have LGBTQ+ family members or may be questioning their own identity

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)**:
- Identity development and understanding sexual orientation
- Challenging stereotypes and discrimination
- Healthy relationships across all relationship types
- Understanding homophobia, biphobia, and transphobia as forms of prejudice
- Where to access support

**Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)**:
- Detailed understanding of sexual orientation and gender identity
- Legal rights and protections
- Sexual health information applicable to all orientations
- Support services including LGBTQ+-specific organisations
- Intersectionality and how LGBTQ+ identity interacts with other characteristics

## Evidence Base and Research

Research from **University College London's Centre for Longitudinal Studies** (2019) found that LGBTQ+-inclusive RSE correlates with improved mental health outcomes for LGBTQ+ young people and increased understanding and acceptance among all students.

**Metro Charity's 'Youth Chances' research** (2014), surveying over 7,000 LGBTQ+ young people aged 16-25, found that 49% reported considering self-harm and 42% had sought medical help for anxiety or depression. Inclusive education is identified as a protective factor.

The **LGBT Foundation's 'Hidden Figures' report** (2016) documented significant health inequalities facing LGBTQ+ communities, including higher rates of substance misuse, poor mental health, and barriers to healthcare. Education plays a crucial role in addressing these through building resilience and awareness.

## Assessment and Monitoring

While RSE is non-assessed in external examinations, schools must monitor its effectiveness. Ofsted's *'Education Inspection Framework'* (2019, updated 2024) evaluates how well schools prepare pupils for life in modern Britain, including understanding diversity and developing respectful relationships.

Inspectors will consider:

- The quality of RSE curriculum planning and implementation
- How well the curriculum reflects the school's statutory duties under Equality Act 2010
- Pupils' understanding of healthy relationships and respect for diversity
- The school's response to bullying, including identity-based bullying

GCSE Citizenship Studies assessments include questions requiring understanding of equality legislation and rights. For example, **AQA Paper 2** may include case studies requiring analysis of discrimination scenarios, whilst **OCR's Component 2** assesses understanding of rights and responsibilities in context.

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Staff Training and Confidence Building

Research by Terrence Higgins Trust (2016) found that 77% of secondary school teachers had received no training on teaching LGBTQ+ issues. Effective implementation requires:

- **Whole-staff training**: Building shared understanding of legal requirements and pedagogical approaches
- **Subject-specific development**: Ensuring RSE and citizenship leads have specialist knowledge
- **Regular updates**: LGBTQ+ language and understanding evolves; training must be ongoing
- **Access to specialist organisations**: Partners like the PSHE Association, Stonewall Education, Educate & Celebrate, and Just Like Us provide resources and support

### Curriculum Mapping and Resources

Schools should conduct **curriculum audits** to identify where LGBTQ+ content is currently included and where gaps exist. Jonathan Charlesworth's work with the PSHE Association advocates for **curriculum threading**—ensuring LGBTQ+ inclusion appears consistently across year groups and subjects, not just in designated RSE lessons.

Quality-assured resources include:

- **PSHE Association Curriculum Planning Resources**: Mapped to statutory guidance with age-appropriate lesson plans
- **Stonewall Education Guides**: Evidence-based approaches to LGBTQ+ inclusion
- **Brook Traffic Light Tool**: Assessment of age-appropriate sexual behaviour content
- **Educate & Celebrate Recognition Scheme**: Framework for embedding LGBTQ+ inclusion across whole school

### Working with Governors and Parents

School governors must understand their statutory duties. The **Governance Handbook** (DfE, 2020) clarifies that governors must ensure compliance with statutory RSE requirements whilst managing parental concerns sensitively.

Effective parental engagement strategies include:

- **Consultation (not consent)**: Schools must consult parents on RSE policy but parents cannot veto statutory content
- **Clear communication**: Share curriculum content, lesson plans, and resources in advance
- **Parent workshops**: Allow parents to see teaching approaches and ask questions
- **Written information**: Provide accessible explanations of what is taught and why

The **DfE's 'Plan Your Relationships, Sex and Health Curriculum'** tool (2020) supports schools in managing parental engagement within statutory frameworks.

## Supporting LGBTQ+ Students

Schools must provide **pastoral support** recognising that LGBTQ+ pupils may face specific challenges. This includes:

- **Visible support**: Display LGBTQ+-inclusive materials; promote events like LGBT History Month (February) and Pride
- **Staff allies**: Identify trained staff members whom LGBTQ+ pupils can approach
- **LGBTQ+ student groups**: Where appropriate, establish safe spaces for peer support
- **Anti-bullying policies**: Explicitly address homophobic, biphobic, and transphobic bullying
- **Recording and monitoring**: Track identity-based incidents and take action

The **Allsorts Youth Project** and **LGBT Foundation** provide evidence that school-based support significantly improves outcomes for LGBTQ+ young people.

### Gender Identity and Trans Inclusion

Separate from sexual orientation, schools increasingly address **gender identity** within RSE and broader PSHE provision. While contentious, schools must balance competing rights and safeguards. Current guidance includes:

- **DfE non-statutory guidance on gender questioning children** (expected 2024): Will clarify schools' approach to social transition
- **Equality and Human Rights Commission guidance**: Balances rights of trans pupils with safeguarding for all
- **PSHE Association resources on gender**: Age-appropriate content challenging stereotypes

Teachers should focus on factual, age-appropriate information about gender diversity whilst respecting that this remains an area of ongoing policy development.

## Conclusion: Embedding LGBTQ+ Inclusion

Effective LGBTQ+ inclusion in RSE requires systematic, whole-school approaches combining statutory compliance with educational best practice. Bernard Crick's vision of citizenship education preparing young people to understand diversity and participate in plural societies remains foundational. Lee Jerome's work on controversial issues provides practical frameworks for navigating potentially contentious content whilst maintaining professional standards.

Jenny Mosley's emphasis on emotional literacy and inclusive communities, Nick Boddington's attention to identity development within diverse contexts, and Jonathan Charlesworth's advocacy for integrated, normalised LGBTQ+ content across PSHE collectively inform contemporary best practice.

Schools must recognise that LGBTQ+ inclusion is not about promoting particular lifestyles but about preparing all young people to live respectfully in diverse modern Britain, protecting vulnerable pupils, and fulfilling statutory duties under equalities legislation. The evidence demonstrates that inclusive RSE benefits all students whilst providing essential support to LGBTQ+ young people who face documented health and wellbeing inequalities.
