# SEND in PSHE

## Introduction and Statutory Context

The provision of high-quality PSHE education for pupils with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) is both a statutory requirement and a matter of educational equity. Since September 2020, Health Education and Relationships Education have been statutory for all pupils, including those with SEND, with the Department for Education's guidance explicitly stating that "all pupils should receive teaching on these subjects" (DfE, 2019). The SEND Code of Practice (2015) emphasises that pupils with SEND must have access to a broad and balanced curriculum, including non-statutory subjects like the wider PSHE curriculum.

The Equality Act 2010 places duties on schools to make reasonable adjustments to ensure pupils with SEND can access the curriculum fully. This is particularly pertinent in PSHE education, where content relates directly to personal development, safety, health, and citizenship participation. Nick Boddington has argued that PSHE represents "the curriculum area where inclusion is not merely desirable but essential" (2014), as it addresses the life skills all young people need to navigate relationships, health, and civic life.

## The Unique Importance of PSHE for Pupils with SEND

### Vulnerability and Safeguarding

Research consistently demonstrates that children and young people with SEND face heightened vulnerability across multiple domains. The Council for Disabled Children (2019) notes that disabled children are three times more likely to experience abuse than their non-disabled peers. Pupils with learning disabilities may face particular difficulties recognising inappropriate behaviour, understanding consent, or knowing how to report concerns.

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on safeguarding within PSHE education emphasises that appropriate, accessible RSE and safeguarding education is "not a luxury but a necessity" for SEND pupils (2017). The curriculum must address body autonomy, privacy, appropriate and inappropriate touch, and healthy versus unhealthy relationships using concrete, developmentally appropriate language and resources.

### Social Understanding and Relationships

Pupils with autism spectrum conditions, social communication difficulties, or moderate to severe learning disabilities often require explicit teaching of social skills that neurotypical pupils may acquire incidentally. Jenny Mosley's work on Circle Time and positive relationships has been particularly influential here. Her Quality Circle Time model (1996, 2005) provides structured opportunities for social and emotional learning that can be adapted for pupils with varying needs, emphasising "the deliberate teaching of pro-social skills within a safe, inclusive environment."

PSHE provides the natural curriculum space for teaching about friendship, recognising emotions in oneself and others, conflict resolution, and understanding social norms and boundaries. For pupils with SEND, this content requires careful scaffolding, repetition, and use of visual supports.

### Health and Independence

Pupils with SEND often face specific challenges regarding physical and mental health, puberty, hygiene, and developing independence. The statutory guidance on Relationships and Sex Education acknowledges that for some pupils with SEND, content may need to be "differentiated, and in some cases developed at a more granular level" (DfE, 2019). This might include more explicit teaching about menstrual management, personal hygiene routines, or understanding bodily changes during puberty.

## Curriculum Planning and Adaptation

### The Principle of Entitlement

Lee Jerome, whose research focuses on citizenship education and inclusion, argues that differentiation must be rooted in "curriculum entitlement rather than curriculum reduction" (Jerome, 2018). All pupils, regardless of need, are entitled to learn about relationships, health, wellbeing, and their rights and responsibilities as citizens. The question is not whether to teach PSHE content, but how to make it accessible and meaningful.

This principle challenges deficit models that might exclude pupils with SEND from certain topics deemed "too complex." Instead, it requires teachers to employ creative pedagogical approaches, use appropriate resources, and collaborate with families and specialists.

### Developmental Appropriateness vs Age-Appropriateness

A key tension in SEND PSHE education concerns the balance between developmental and chronological age-appropriateness. A 15-year-old with severe learning disabilities may have the cognitive understanding of a much younger child but experiences the physical changes of puberty and faces age-appropriate social situations and risks.

The National Curriculum Inclusion Statement (2013) requires teachers to set "suitable learning challenges" and "respond to pupils' diverse learning needs." In PSHE, this means considering:

- **Physical maturation**: Teaching about puberty, sexual health, and relationships appropriate to pupils' bodily development
- **Cognitive understanding**: Using concrete language, visual supports, and simplified concepts matched to pupils' comprehension
- **Social context**: Addressing the actual situations pupils encounter in their lives
- **Life stage transitions**: Preparing for key transitions including moving to further education, employment, or adult social care

### Graduated Approach to Differentiation

Effective PSHE education for pupils with SEND employs a graduated approach aligned with the SEND Code of Practice's assess-plan-do-review cycle:

**Universal Quality First Teaching**: All pupils benefit from PSHE delivered through active learning, visual supports, clear language, and opportunities for practice and consolidation. The PSHE Association's "Programme of Study for PSHE Education" (2020) emphasises these principles throughout.

**Targeted Support**: Some pupils may require additional scaffolding such as pre-teaching of vocabulary, use of social stories (developed by Carol Gray), visual timetables showing PSHE lesson structure, or additional adult support during discussions.

**Specialist Approaches**: A smaller number of pupils may require highly individualised programmes, possibly delivered one-to-one or in small groups, using specialist resources such as Makaton signing, symbol-supported materials (e.g., Widgit symbols), or adapted resources from organisations like the Autism Education Trust or Mencap.

## Key Stage Considerations

### EYFS and Key Stage 1

Even in early years, inclusion principles apply to PSHE-related learning. The EYFS framework (2021) emphasises Personal, Social and Emotional Development (PSED) as a prime area of learning. For pupils with SEND, this might involve:

- Extended time and support for developing self-regulation and emotional literacy
- Use of visual emotion tools (e.g., Zones of Regulation)
- Structured small group work for developing friendship skills
- Adapted Circle Time using objects of reference or simplified rules
- Body awareness and privacy teaching using anatomically correct language appropriate to understanding level

### Key Stage 2

Upper primary pupils with SEND require careful preparation for puberty and transition to secondary school. Bernard Crick's emphasis on active citizenship learning "through practical experience and participation" (Crick Report, 1998) is particularly relevant for pupils with learning disabilities who benefit from concrete, experiential learning.

Content considerations include:

- Puberty education delivered over extended timescales with regular revisiting
- Explicit teaching about private body parts, privacy, and appropriate behaviour in different spaces
- Managing feelings and friendships with concrete examples and role-play
- Understanding rules, responsibilities, and rights in school and community contexts
- Financial awareness and consumer rights at appropriate levels

### Key Stage 3

Secondary PSHE for pupils with SEND must address increasingly complex content about relationships, sexual health, substance use, and mental health, whilst remaining accessible. The PSHE Association's guidance on "Teaching about consent" (2015, updated 2020) emphasises that all pupils, including those with learning disabilities, must understand consent principles, though teaching approaches will vary.

Key adaptations include:

- Extended schemes of work allowing more time for processing and consolidation
- Use of clear, concrete scenarios and examples rather than abstract concepts
- Video modelling and role-play for social situations
- Adapted resources from organisations like the FPA, Brook, or Mencap's "Sexual Health: Making it Clear" resources
- Close collaboration with families regarding sensitive content, particularly RSE
- Use of subject-specific vocabulary alongside simplified explanations

### Key Stage 4 and Post-16

For pupils with SEND aged 14-19, PSHE education becomes increasingly focused on preparation for adulthood. The SEND Code of Practice (2015) requires schools to support pupils in preparing for adulthood outcomes including employment, independent living, community participation, and health. PSHE curriculum aligns directly with these outcomes.

In examination contexts, some pupils with SEND may study GCSE Citizenship (offered by AQA, Pearson Edexcel, and OCR). These boards provide access arrangements including extra time, readers, scribes, or use of word processors. However, many pupils with more significant learning needs will access non-examined PSHE learning.

Content priorities for this age group include:

- Sexual health, relationships, and consent with clear, explicit teaching
- Understanding of the law regarding relationships, sexual behaviour, and consent
- Online safety and digital relationships
- Preparation for employment: rights, responsibilities, workplace behaviour
- Managing money, banking, and avoiding exploitation
- Accessing health services independently
- Self-advocacy and understanding one's own needs and rights

## Specific Considerations by Type of Need

### Autism Spectrum Conditions (ASC)

The Autism Education Trust provides extensive guidance on adapting curriculum for pupils with autism. In PSHE, particular considerations include:

- Making implicit social rules and expectations explicit
- Providing clear, literal explanations avoiding metaphor or ambiguity
- Using visual supports including social stories, comic strip conversations, and video modelling
- Allowing processing time and avoiding sudden changes to routine
- Teaching about diversity of relationships and challenging rigid thinking
- Addressing sensory aspects of topics (e.g., menstrual products, contraception)
- Preparing for transitions with visual schedules and graduated exposure

### Moderate to Severe Learning Disabilities (MLD/SLD)

Pupils with MLD or SLD benefit from:

- Repeated, consistent teaching of key concepts over time
- Multi-sensory, practical learning approaches
- Symbol-supported resources (Widgit, Makaton)
- Simplified language with core vocabulary emphasis
- Use of real objects, photographs, and concrete examples
- Small group or individual teaching for sensitive topics
- Close liaison with families regarding reinforcement at home
- Emphasis on personal safety, including recognition of unsafe situations

### Social, Emotional and Mental Health Needs (SEMH)

For pupils with SEMH needs, PSHE offers crucial learning but may trigger anxiety or behavioural responses. Jenny Mosley's circle time approaches provide structured, safe spaces for discussion. Considerations include:

- Creating psychologically safe learning environments
- Offering choice and control where appropriate
- Avoiding unexpected discussion of traumatic topics
- Providing alternative means of participation (written responses, talking partners)
- Teaching emotion regulation and coping strategies
- Addressing mental health and wellbeing explicitly
- Understanding how trauma affects relationships and learning

### Physical Disabilities and Sensory Impairments

Pupils with physical disabilities or sensory impairments may require:

- Accessible formats: Braille, large print, audio materials
- British Sign Language interpretation or sign-supported English
- Additional content addressing disability identity, rights, and navigating inaccessible environments
- Specific teaching about intimate care, personal assistance, and maintaining dignity and privacy
- Addressing relationships and sexuality from disability perspectives, countering assumptions of asexuality
- Practical adaptations for activities (e.g., alternative recording methods)

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Co-Production and Person-Centred Planning

Effective PSHE education for pupils with SEND involves co-production with pupils themselves, families, and specialists. Person-centred planning approaches, as advocated in the SEND Code of Practice, ensure that content addresses pupils' actual needs, experiences, and future aspirations.

This means:

- Consulting pupils (using accessible methods) about what they need to learn
- Involving parents and carers, particularly for sensitive RSE content, whilst maintaining statutory obligations
- Working with speech and language therapists, educational psychologists, and other specialists to inform planning
- Respecting family cultural and religious perspectives whilst ensuring statutory content is covered

### Active and Experiential Learning

Lee Jerome's research emphasises that citizenship learning must be active and participatory rather than passive and didactic (Jerome and Kisby, 2015). This is equally true for pupils with SEND, who benefit particularly from:

- Role-play and drama approaches
- Community-based learning and visits
- Use of real-life scenarios and problem-solving
- Student voice activities adapted for accessibility (e.g., visual voting, symbol-based consultations)
- Partnerships with external organisations (e.g., local democracy projects, health professionals)

### Use of Specialist Resources

Quality resources are essential for accessible PSHE education. Recommended providers include:

- **PSHE Association**: "Adapted PSHE" guidance and resources
- **Mencap**: "Sexual Health: Making it Clear" resources
- **FPA**: "SRE for Children and Young People with Learning Disabilities" guidance
- **Brook**: "Traffic Light Tool" for understanding sexual behaviours
- **Council for Disabled Children**: "Sensory Stories" approach for PMLD pupils
- **Autism Education Trust**: Curriculum adaptations guidance
- **TACTYC**: Early years inclusive practice resources

### Communication Adaptations

Communication is central to PSHE education, yet many pupils with SEND face communication challenges. Adaptations include:

- Total communication approaches: speech, sign, symbols, gesture
- Alternative and Augmentative Communication (AAC) systems with PSHE-specific vocabulary
- Visual timetables and lesson structure prompts
- Talking mats and other visual consultation tools
- Peer support and communication partners
- Video and photographic supports
- Simplified, structured questions with processing time

## Assessment and Progress

### Assessment Principles

Assessing progress in PSHE for pupils with SEND requires moving beyond written assessment. The PSHE Association's guidance on "Assessment without Levels" (2015, revised 2018) emphasises formative assessment focused on demonstrating learning over time through multiple means.

For pupils with SEND, assessment might include:

- Observation of behaviour changes and skill application in real contexts
- Video evidence of role-play or social interaction
- Pupil voice captured through accessible means
- Parent/carer reports of changed behaviour or understanding at home
- Symbol or pictorial communication about learning
- Small-steps progression frameworks (e.g., B Squared, PIVATS)

### Measuring Impact

Evidence of effective PSHE education for pupils with SEND includes:

- Increased safety awareness and reduced vulnerability to exploitation
- Improved social relationships and reduced isolation
- Greater emotional regulation and wellbeing
- Enhanced understanding of bodily changes and health management
- Increased independence and self-advocacy
- Successful transitions to post-16 provision and adulthood outcomes

The Preparing for Adulthood framework provides useful outcome measures aligned with PSHE content for students aged 14 and above.

## Training and Professional Development

### Teacher Knowledge and Confidence

Research by the Sex Education Forum (2021) found that teachers often lack confidence in delivering RSE to pupils with SEND. Professional development is essential, covering:

- Understanding of different types of SEND and their implications for PSHE learning
- Legal requirements and safeguarding responsibilities
- Communication strategies and use of specialist resources
- Collaboration with specialists and families
- Managing sensitive conversations and disclosures
- Addressing own discomfort or assumptions about disability and sexuality

### Whole-School Approaches

The PSHE Association advocates whole-school approaches to PSHE education. For SEND provision, this includes:

- Senior leadership commitment to PSHE curriculum entitlement for all
- Strategic use of TA and specialist support
- Timetabling arrangements allowing sufficient, regular PSHE time
- Investment in adapted resources and training
- Policies addressing confidentiality, safeguarding, and responding to disclosures
- Integration of PSHE with broader inclusion and SEND strategies

## Controversial Issues and Ethical Considerations

### Parental Rights vs. Statutory Requirements

The right to withdraw from sex education (but not relationships or health education) exists for all pupils, but raises particular ethical questions for pupils with SEND. Nick Boddington notes the tension between parental rights and children's rights to education that keeps them safe (2017). Schools must:

- Engage proactively with parents about RSE content and rationale
- Explain the particular vulnerability of SEND pupils and importance of appropriate education
- Seek to find common ground and address concerns
- Make clear what is statutory and cannot be withdrawn from
- Balance parental perspectives with safeguarding duties

### Capacity, Consent, and Safeguarding

Teaching about consent to pupils with learning disabilities requires sensitivity to questions of mental capacity under the Mental Capacity Act 2005. The starting assumption must be that all pupils have capacity to learn about and understand consent, relationships, and sexuality at appropriate levels.

However, teachers must also be alert to safeguarding concerns, recognising that apparent "consensual" relationships may involve exploitation of vulnerability. Training on recognising concerning behaviours and appropriate reporting is essential.

### Inclusion vs. Segregation

Debates continue about whether PSHE for pupils with SEND is best delivered in mainstream classes with support, in small groups, or through alternative provision. Research by Lee Jerome (2018) suggests that inclusive mainstream delivery promotes full citizenship and social inclusion, whilst acknowledging that some content (particularly sensitive RSE topics) may require separate teaching for some pupils.

Best practice suggests:

- Default position of inclusive mainstream teaching wherever possible
- Small group or individual work for specific, identified needs rather than routine segregation
- Opportunities for mixed peer learning, promoting understanding and inclusion
- Specialist provision where necessary based on individual need, not blanket policy
