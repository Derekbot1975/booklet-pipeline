# Spiral Curriculum in PSHE

## Introduction and Conceptual Foundation

The spiral curriculum model, first articulated by Jerome Bruner in 'The Process of Education' (1960), has become the predominant curricular architecture for PSHE education in the UK. This approach involves revisiting core themes and concepts at increasing levels of complexity and sophistication across key stages, enabling pupils to build upon prior knowledge whilst developing age-appropriate understanding.

In PSHE contexts, the spiral curriculum model recognises that topics such as relationships, health, safety, and wellbeing cannot be adequately addressed through single teaching episodes. Instead, these themes require repeated engagement throughout a child's educational journey, with each encounter deepening understanding and developing more sophisticated skills and behaviours.

The PSHE Association's 'Programme of Study for PSHE Education (Key Stages 1–5)' (2020) explicitly endorses the spiral curriculum approach, stating that "learning in PSHE education is progressive, with concepts, knowledge and skills being revisited and built upon across key stages." This represents a fundamental shift from earlier models where PSHE topics were often delivered as discrete, one-off interventions.

## Theoretical Underpinnings

### Bruner's Original Framework

Jerome Bruner proposed that any subject could be taught effectively in some intellectually honest form to any child at any stage of development. For PSHE education, this means that complex concepts—such as consent, mental health, or financial capability—can be introduced in age-appropriate ways to very young children, then systematically revisited with increasing cognitive demands.

The spiral approach contrasts with linear curriculum models where topics are encountered once before progression to entirely new content. Bruner argued that returning to ideas allows learners to achieve mastery through reinforcement, whilst encountering familiar concepts in new contexts promotes transfer of learning and metacognitive development.

### Application to PSHE Education

Nick Boddington, former Director of Development at the PSHE Association, has written extensively on curriculum design in PSHE. He emphasises that the spiral curriculum model is particularly suited to PSHE because it acknowledges the developmental nature of children's social, emotional and moral growth. Concepts such as 'healthy relationships' mean fundamentally different things to a five-year-old, a twelve-year-old, and a seventeen-year-old, yet the underlying principles can be progressively developed.

Lee Jerome and Stephen Roffey-Barentsen, in their work on citizenship education ('Effective Citizenship Education: A Teacher's Handbook', 2008), argue that spiral curriculum models enable the integration of experiential learning with progressive knowledge acquisition. In PSHE and Citizenship, pupils need opportunities to practice skills (such as negotiation, critical evaluation of media, or democratic participation) repeatedly, with each iteration building capability.

## Structure Across Key Stages

### Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS)

At EYFS level, the spiral curriculum introduces foundational concepts through the Personal, Social and Emotional Development prime area. Children encounter ideas about:

- Recognising feelings in themselves and others
- Basic concepts of fairness and taking turns
- Personal hygiene and self-care
- Safe and unsafe situations
- Similarities and differences between people

These themes establish the conceptual groundwork that will be revisited throughout statutory education. The vocabulary introduced—'feelings', 'kind', 'safe', 'different'—becomes progressively refined in subsequent key stages.

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The spiral curriculum at KS1 returns to EYFS themes with increased complexity. Following the statutory Relationships Education requirements (DfE, 2020), pupils revisit:

**Relationships themes**: Moving from recognising feelings to naming a broader range of emotions; from simple friendship concepts to understanding that friendships have ups and downs; from basic family recognition to understanding that families are diverse.

**Health and wellbeing themes**: Expanding personal hygiene understanding to include why these practices matter; developing from recognising unsafe situations to knowing how to respond and seek help; progressing from awareness of physical differences to understanding that bodies change and grow.

The Department for Education's statutory guidance ('Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education', 2019) explicitly requires this progressive approach, stating that teaching must be "sensitive to the age and the religious background of pupils" whilst ensuring all pupils receive their statutory entitlement.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

At KS2, the spiral curriculum significantly deepens conceptual understanding whilst maintaining connections to earlier learning. Jenny Mosley's Circle Time model, widely implemented in primary schools, exemplifies spiral curriculum principles by revisiting themes such as respect, cooperation, and problem-solving through increasingly sophisticated scenarios.

**Relationships themes**: Pupils revisit concepts of friendship but now explore issues of peer pressure, conflict resolution strategies, and different types of relationships including those online. The concept of 'respect' introduced at KS1 now encompasses respecting differences in protected characteristics as required by the Equality Act 2010.

**Health and wellbeing themes**: Physical health teaching spirals back to KS1 content on hygiene but now includes understanding nutrition, exercise, and the impact of substances like tobacco and alcohol. Mental health and emotional wellbeing, introduced through simple feelings vocabulary earlier, now encompasses strategies for managing difficult emotions and recognising when to seek support.

**Living in the wider world**: Citizenship concepts emerge more formally, with pupils revisiting ideas about rules (from KS1) but now exploring why societies need laws, how democracy functions, and their role as citizens. Financial capability themes spiral from basic money recognition to understanding saving, spending choices, and the concepts of risk and value.

The PSHE Association's KS2 Programme of Study (2020) organises these spiralling themes into three core areas: Health and Wellbeing, Relationships, and Living in the Wider World, with each containing strands that deliberately revisit earlier content.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At KS3, the spiral curriculum model enables sophisticated re-examination of familiar themes through adolescent developmental lenses. Jonathan Charlesworth's research on secondary PSHE delivery highlights that effective programmes at this stage acknowledge prior learning whilst recognising that physical, emotional and social changes of adolescence necessitate fresh engagement with fundamental concepts.

**Relationships and sex education**: Pupils who have studied healthy relationships, family diversity, and basic body changes at primary level now revisit these themes with explicit focus on puberty, reproduction, sexual health, contraception, and consent. The statutory RSE guidance (2019) requires this progressive approach, stating that "schools should build on the knowledge acquired at primary school."

**Health and wellbeing**: Mental health teaching spirals from emotional literacy and simple coping strategies to understanding specific mental health conditions, stigma, and accessing professional support. Substance education revisits the harms of alcohol and tobacco but now includes illegal drugs, addiction, and harm reduction strategies.

**Citizenship education**: Building on KS2 foundations, students revisit concepts of democracy, rights and responsibilities with increased political literacy. Bernard Crick's influential report 'Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools' (1998) emphasised that citizenship education must be developmental, with pupils returning to concepts of power, justice, and civic participation throughout their education with increasing sophistication.

Lee Jerome's work on political literacy argues that KS3 represents a critical spiral point where pupils move from understanding citizenship as personal behaviour to engaging with systemic and structural analyses of inequality, justice, and civic agency.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At KS4, the spiral curriculum reaches its most sophisticated iteration within compulsory education. Pupils revisit themes encountered since EYFS but now explore them with critical depth, ethical complexity, and personal relevance to immediate life transitions.

**Relationships and sexual health**: The spiral returns to consent, previously introduced through age-appropriate concepts of permission and bodily autonomy, but now engages with legal definitions, sexual consent in practice, and the relationship between consent, respect, and equality. Intimate relationships, introduced through family diversity and friendship quality at earlier stages, now encompass teenage relationships, sexual orientation, gender identity, and managing relationship breakdown.

**Career and financial education**: The Gatsby Benchmarks for careers guidance inform the spiral approach to career and economic wellbeing, with KS4 students revisiting themes of employability, financial capability, and economic understanding encountered at KS2 and KS3 but now applying these to genuine qualification and career choices.

**Mental health and resilience**: The spiral curriculum at this stage acknowledges that adolescence presents particular mental health vulnerabilities. Pupils revisit emotional wellbeing content but now engage with stress management in high-stakes contexts (examinations, social pressures), recognising mental health conditions in themselves and others, and understanding help-seeking pathways.

**Citizenship and political engagement**: Students revisit democratic processes and civic participation but now with immediate relevance as they approach voting age. The spiral curriculum enables connection between abstract principles studied at KS3 and practical political literacy.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

Though non-statutory, the spiral curriculum continues at KS5 for students remaining in education. The PSHE Association (2020) recommends that sixth form PSHE revisits core themes from compulsory education but with focus on transition to adult independence.

Relationships education spirals back to consent, healthy relationships, and sexual health but now addresses university/workplace contexts, long-term relationships, and adult safeguarding. Mental health teaching revisits coping strategies and help-seeking but addresses management of mental health conditions in adult services and workplace stress.

Financial capability spirals to include student finance, housing, taxation, and financial products. Citizenship education revisits democratic participation but with focus on emerging adult civic identities and responsibilities.

## Pedagogical Considerations

### Ensuring Progression Not Repetition

The central challenge of spiral curriculum implementation in PSHE is ensuring genuine progression rather than mere repetition. Nick Boddington emphasises that each return to a theme must offer new learning, increased challenge, and deeper engagement. Teachers must explicitly reference prior learning whilst clearly articulating what additional understanding or skills are being developed.

Practical strategies include:

**Assessment for learning**: Using baseline assessment to establish what pupils already know from previous spiral points. This might involve 'think-pair-share' activities recalling prior learning, or diagnostic quizzes identifying knowledge gaps.

**Explicit progression frameworks**: The PSHE Association's learning outcomes for each key stage provide detailed progression descriptors. For example, the outcome "recognise that families are characterised by love and care" (KS1) progresses to "recognise different types of family structures" (KS2), then to "analyse stereotypes and assumptions about family life" (KS3).

**Differentiated vocabulary**: Ensuring age-appropriate terminology whilst building technical vocabulary across the spiral. For instance, 'private parts' (KS1) progresses to anatomical terminology (KS2/3) to medical/legal terminology around sexual health (KS4).

### Maintaining Engagement

Pupils may express frustration at revisiting familiar topics. Effective spiral curriculum delivery addresses this through:

**Varied pedagogical approaches**: If relationships education at KS2 employed circle time discussions, the KS3 return might use drama, debate, or digital media analysis. Varying teaching methods maintains engagement whilst accessing topics through different learning modalities.

**Acknowledging prior learning**: Beginning units by explicitly recognising what pupils have previously studied, then clearly framing new learning. This validates pupils' existing knowledge whilst establishing the rationale for further study.

**Real-world connections**: Each spiral point should connect to pupils' current developmental stage and lived experience. Mental health teaching at KS2 might focus on everyday worries relevant to primary pupils, whilst KS4 teaching addresses examination anxiety and social media pressures specific to adolescence.

### Managing Sensitive Content

The spiral curriculum model is particularly valuable for managing sensitive or potentially distressing content in PSHE. By introducing concepts early in developmentally appropriate ways, schools avoid overwhelming pupils with complex issues whilst ensuring foundational understanding is established.

The DfE's statutory RSE guidance (2019) endorses this approach, requiring that "puberty is covered in Health Education and should, as far as possible, be addressed before onset" whilst acknowledging that "the onset of puberty differs across individuals." The spiral model accommodates this variability by introducing puberty concepts at upper KS2, revisiting with greater detail at KS3, and addressing psychological/social dimensions at KS4.

For topics such as child sexual exploitation, domestic abuse, or self-harm, the spiral curriculum enables age-appropriate engagement. At KS1, teaching focuses on recognising uncomfortable situations and knowing trusted adults. At KS2, this spirals to understanding different types of secrets and the right to be safe. By KS3/4, pupils have the foundational knowledge to engage with specific safeguarding issues, warning signs, and support pathways.

## Assessment and the Spiral Curriculum

### Formative Assessment

The spiral curriculum relies heavily on formative assessment to establish starting points for each return to core themes. The PSHE Association guidance (2020) recommends that assessment in PSHE should focus on:

**Knowledge recall**: Can pupils demonstrate understanding of concepts from previous spiral points? For example, can KS3 pupils recall and apply KS2 learning about peer pressure when exploring substance use?

**Skill development**: Are pupils demonstrating increasing sophistication in PSHE-specific skills (empathy, self-reflection, critical evaluation, communication)? The spiral curriculum should show progressive capability.

**Attitude and values exploration**: Whilst PSHE does not assess or judge pupils' personal values, formative assessment can explore whether pupils are engaging with ethical questions and showing increased sophistication in moral reasoning.

### Summative Assessment

Though PSHE is non-examined at GCSE level in most schools, some examination boards offer qualifications that reflect spiral curriculum principles:

**OCR Level 1/2 Cambridge National in Health and Social Care**: While technically a vocational qualification, this includes PSHE-related content on human development, health promotion, and wellbeing, with assessment structures that reward progressive understanding.

**AQA Level 2 Certificate in Further Mathematics (citizenship education component)**: Some schools have used this to assess aspects of citizenship education, though this has been discontinued.

More commonly, schools use internal assessment frameworks aligned with the PSHE Association's assessment guidance, which recommends evaluating pupil progress across multiple spiral points rather than assessing discrete units in isolation.

### Tracking Progression

Effective spiral curriculum implementation requires systematic tracking of pupil progress across key stages. Practical approaches include:

**Learning outcome tracking**: Mapping pupil achievement against the PSHE Association's progressive learning outcomes across KS1-5.

**Portfolio evidence**: Maintaining pupil portfolios (physical or digital) that demonstrate engagement with core themes across multiple key stages, allowing pupils and teachers to review progression.

**Transition documentation**: Ensuring information about PSHE curriculum coverage passes between key stages, particularly at primary-secondary transition where curriculum overlap is greatest.

## Challenges and Solutions

### Inconsistent Provision Across Schools

A significant challenge for spiral curriculum implementation is inconsistency in PSHE provision between schools. When pupils arrive at secondary school with vastly different prior experiences of PSHE, establishing appropriate starting points for KS3 teaching is problematic.

Solutions include:

**Transition information**: Primary schools providing detailed PSHE curriculum maps to receiving secondaries, specifying which topics have been covered and to what depth.

**Flexible curriculum planning**: Secondary schools planning KS3 PSHE with "foundation" elements that ensure all pupils have baseline understanding before progressing to more sophisticated content.

**Partnership working**: Local authority or Multi-Academy Trust PSHE networks establishing shared spiral curriculum frameworks ensuring consistency across feeder patterns.

### Parental Engagement and Consultation

The statutory RSE guidance (2019) requires schools to consult parents and publish their RSE policy. The spiral curriculum model complicates this because parents need to understand not only what will be taught in a given year, but how this builds on previous learning and prepares for future content.

Effective communication strategies include:

**Spiral curriculum mapping for parents**: Producing accessible documents showing how key topics (e.g., relationships, puberty, mental health) develop across key stages.

**Year-group specific communications**: Providing parents with information about the specific spiral point their child is at, what prior learning is assumed, and what subsequent learning will build on current teaching.

**Progressive language guides**: Helping parents understand how terminology and concepts develop across the spiral, particularly for sensitive RSE content.

### Workforce Development

Teachers delivering PSHE often lack subject-specific training and may be unfamiliar with spiral curriculum principles. Professional development must address:

**Understanding progression**: Training on what genuine progression looks like in PSHE, using concrete examples across key stages.

**Assessment literacy**: Developing teachers' capacity to assess prior learning and identify next steps within spiral frameworks.

**Long-term curriculum planning**: Supporting curriculum leaders to map spiral curriculum pathways across multiple years, ensuring coherence and progression.

The PSHE Association offers specialist training on spiral curriculum design, whilst organisations like School Health Education Unit provide resources supporting progressive teaching approaches.

## Research Evidence

### Effectiveness of Spiral Approaches

Research evidence on spiral curriculum effectiveness in PSHE specifically is limited but growing. The Education Endowment Foundation's 'Social and Emotional Learning' guidance report (2019) notes that effective SEL programmes are "sequenced to develop skills progressively," implicitly endorsing spiral approaches.

Research on specific PSHE topics supports spiral curriculum models:

**RSE**: A systematic review by Pound et al. (2017) in BMJ Open found that effective sex education is "age-appropriate, developmentally appropriate and delivered throughout the school career," consistent with spiral curriculum principles.
