# Progression in PSHE

## Introduction

Progression in PSHE education represents one of the most complex curriculum design challenges in UK schools. Unlike subjects with clear hierarchical knowledge structures, PSHE deals with concepts that recur throughout a child's education but require increasingly sophisticated understanding and application. The PSHE Association defines progression as "the means through which pupils build on prior learning and develop their understanding in an age and stage appropriate way" (PSHE Association, 2020).

The statutory status of Relationships Education (primary), Relationships and Sex Education (secondary), and Health Education (all phases) from September 2020, alongside non-statutory Citizenship and the broader PSHE curriculum, necessitates rigorous attention to progression models. This knowledge file examines how progression operates across PSHE's core strands and provides practical frameworks for curriculum leaders.

## Theoretical Foundations of PSHE Progression

### The Non-Linear Nature of PSHE Learning

Bernard Crick's seminal work on citizenship education (Crick Report, 1998) established that civic learning cannot follow a simple linear progression from "easy" to "difficult" concepts. Rather, concepts like democracy, rights, and responsibilities must be revisited at increasing levels of complexity. This principle extends across all PSHE domains.

Nick Boddington (2013, 2020) has articulated that PSHE progression operates through a "spiral curriculum" model, where learners encounter core themes repeatedly but with increasing sophistication, application to more complex contexts, and growing personal relevance. This contrasts sharply with subjects like mathematics where 2 + 2 = 4 remains constant, whilst a child's understanding of "healthy relationship" must evolve dramatically from Year 1 to Year 11.

Lee Jerome and colleagues at the Association for Citizenship Teaching have emphasised that progression in citizenship requires movement from the personal to the political, from concrete examples to abstract principles, and from understanding to critical analysis and action competence (Jerome et al., 2015).

### Jenny Mosley's Circle Time and Developmental Appropriateness

Jenny Mosley's Quality Circle Time model (1996, 2005) provides foundational principles for age-appropriate PSHE delivery, particularly in primary settings. Her work emphasises that progression must be rooted in children's social and emotional developmental stages, building from self-awareness to peer relationships to community understanding. Mosley's "Golden Rules" approach demonstrates how core values can remain consistent whilst application and understanding deepen progressively.

## Models of Progression in PSHE

### The PSHE Association Progression Framework

The PSHE Association's "Planning Toolkit for a Spiral Programme of Study" (updated 2020) identifies five key elements of progression:

1. **Increasing breadth and depth of knowledge**: From simple facts to complex understanding
2. **Developing skills and attributes**: From basic competencies to sophisticated application
3. **Understanding complexity**: From black-and-white thinking to nuanced analysis
4. **Increasing independence**: From supported learning to autonomous application
5. **Context expansion**: From personal to local to national to global perspectives

This framework explicitly acknowledges that pupils may demonstrate different levels of progression across different PSHE topics based on personal experience, maturity, and prior learning.

### The Three-Dimensions Model

Jonathan Charlesworth's work with the PSHE Association has articulated three dimensions of progression operating simultaneously:

**Knowledge Progression**: Moving from concrete, personal knowledge to abstract, societal understanding. For example, understanding "bullying" progresses from "bullying is being mean repeatedly" (KS1) to "bullying involves power imbalance, intent, repetition, and can take multiple forms including cyber-bullying with distinct legal and psychological dimensions" (KS4).

**Skills Progression**: Developing from basic skills (e.g., identifying feelings) to complex skills (e.g., managing conflicting emotions in ambiguous situations, negotiating consent in relationships, evaluating bias in health information sources).

**Attitudes and Values Progression**: Moving from accepting taught values to critically examining personal values, understanding cultural variation in values, and developing autonomous ethical reasoning.

## Progression Across Key Stages

### Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS)

The EYFS framework (DfE, 2021) embeds PSHE-related learning within Personal, Social and Emotional Development (PSED) and Understanding the World. Progression at this stage focuses on:

- Self-regulation: from co-regulation with adults to some independent emotional management
- Managing self: from dependency to increasing independence in self-care
- Building relationships: from parallel play to cooperative play and friendship formation

**Practical implication**: PSHE in EYFS must be experiential, play-based, and focused on developing foundational emotional literacy rather than abstract concepts.

### Key Stage 1 (Years 1-2)

Statutory Relationships Education begins in Year 1. Progression at KS1 emphasises:

- Moving from egocentric to recognising others' perspectives
- Understanding family diversity in concrete terms (different families, same value)
- Basic identification of emotions to beginning to understand causes of emotions
- Concrete rules and safety information (e.g., NSPCC's PANTS rule for body safety)
- Introduction to democratic processes through classroom contexts (voting on story choices)

The PSHE Association's KS1 Programme of Study (2020) structures learning around immediate, observable experiences rather than abstract concepts.

### Key Stage 2 (Years 3-6)

Upper KS2 represents a critical progression threshold where pupils develop capacity for:

- Abstract thinking about concepts like fairness, justice, and rights
- Understanding puberty and human reproduction (statutory Health Education)
- Risk assessment and consequences thinking (online safety, peer pressure)
- Understanding laws, rules, and their purposes (Citizenship concepts)
- Beginning to engage with local community and current affairs

**Progression marker**: By Year 6, pupils should progress from understanding "people are different" to actively valuing diversity, understanding discrimination, and knowing how to challenge prejudice (PSHE Association, 2020; Equality Act 2010 school duties).

The transition to KS3 requires careful bridging, as identified by Nick Boddington (2020). Schools must ensure Year 6 PSHE is sufficiently sophisticated whilst recognising that Year 7 will revisit topics with greater maturity.

### Key Stage 3 (Years 7-9)

KS3 PSHE progression involves:

- Moving from concrete scenarios to principles that apply across contexts
- Developing critical media literacy (distinguishing between advertising, opinion, fact)
- Understanding consent in relationships with increasing nuance
- Engaging with local and national citizenship (rights, democracy, law)
- Financial literacy progression from pocket money decisions to understanding economic systems

Statutory RSE at this stage must address changing adolescent relationships, sexual consent, contraception, and LGBT+ identities inclusively (DfE, 2019).

Lee Jerome's research (2012) demonstrates that effective KS3 Citizenship requires progression from "charity work" to "understanding structural causes of social issues" and from "following rules" to "understanding how laws are made and can be changed."

### Key Stage 4 (Years 10-11)

PSHE at KS4 must prepare students for adult life beyond school. Progression includes:

- Sophisticated understanding of sexual health, contraception, pregnancy choices
- Financial decision-making including debt, gambling, taxation
- Employment rights, workplace discrimination, career adaptability
- Critical political literacy (evaluating manifestos, understanding voting systems)
- Autonomous ethical reasoning on complex issues (euthanasia, abortion, surveillance)

**Examination context**: While PSHE itself is non-examined, several qualifications support KS4 progression:

- **AQA Level 1/2 Certificate in Citizenship Studies** (9-1): Assessed understanding of citizenship concepts, skills of enquiry and advocacy
- **OCR Level 1/2 Cambridge National in Health and Social Care**: Applies PSHE knowledge in vocational context
- **WJEC Level 1/2 Award in Health and Well-being**: Structured progression through health-related PSHE topics

These qualifications provide external validation of progression but should complement, not replace, comprehensive PSHE programmes.

### Key Stage 5 (Years 12-13)

Post-16 PSHE progression involves:

- Application to authentic adult contexts (tenancy agreements, workplace rights, taxation)
- Critical engagement with contested social and political issues
- Mental health self-management and accessing adult services
- Understanding of diverse relationships, commitment types, and family planning
- Preparation for higher education and independent living

The PSHE Association's "Preparing for Adulthood" guidance (2020) emphasises that KS5 progression must move decisively from the hypothetical to the practical and immediate.

## Subject-Specific Progression Strands

### Relationships and Sex Education (RSE)

Progression in RSE follows developmental appropriateness outlined in the DfE statutory guidance (2019):

**Primary**: Families, friendships, safe relationships, respecting ourselves and others → **KS3**: Intimate relationships, consent, contraception, STIs, online relationships → **KS4**: Long-term relationships, pregnancy choices, sexual health services, sexual pleasure and mutuality → **KS5**: Mature relationships, diverse family forms, fertility awareness, comprehensive sexual health

**Critical progression point**: The transition from "relationships" to "sexual relationships" must be developmentally appropriate, typically occurring during Year 7-9 as per the statutory guidance, though some primary schools introduce basic conception education in Year 6.

### Health Education

The DfE statutory Health Education framework (2019) structures progression through:

**Mental wellbeing**: Identifying emotions → Understanding mental health spectrum → Recognising mental health problems → Accessing support and understanding treatment

**Internet safety and harms**: Safe use → Understanding risks → Critical evaluation → Digital citizenship and challenging harmful content

**Physical health and fitness**: Basic hygiene → Understanding puberty → Nutrition and exercise science → Long-term health management and screening

**Healthy eating**: Food groups → Balanced diet → Nutritional science → Understanding eating disorders and body image pressures

**Drugs, alcohol, and tobacco**: Basic safety rules → Understanding substances and effects → Risk assessment and refusal skills → Understanding addiction and harm reduction

### Citizenship Education

Though non-statutory beyond the KS3/4 National Curriculum requirements, Citizenship progression is integral to PSHE. The Crick Report (1998) and subsequent NC programmes (2014, continuing) structure progression through three strands:

**Social and moral responsibility**: Following class rules → Understanding fairness → Rights and responsibilities → Active citizenship and advocacy

**Community involvement**: Awareness of local area → Participating in school democracy → Understanding local governance → Engaging with national campaigns

**Political literacy**: Awareness of current events → Understanding UK democratic systems → Evaluating political arguments → Engaging in informed political participation

Bernard Crick emphasized that progression must avoid both "infantilising young people by avoiding controversial issues" and "overwhelming them with complexity before they have foundational knowledge."

### Economic Wellbeing and Financial Capability

Progression in financial education (often integrated within PSHE):

**KS1-2**: Value of money, saving, needs vs wants → **KS3**: Budgeting, understanding risk (gambling, online fraud), consumer rights → **KS4**: Employment rights, taxation, debt, financial products → **KS5**: Student finance, tenancy, credit ratings, pension awareness

The MoneySense and Young Enterprise frameworks provide structured progression pathways used by many UK schools.

## Assessing Progression in PSHE

### The Assessment Challenge

Unlike subjects with right/wrong answers, PSHE assessment must capture changing attitudes, developing skills, and knowledge application rather than mere recall. Nick Boddington (2020) argues that "assessment in PSHE is fundamentally different from assessment in most other subjects, focusing on what learners can do with their knowledge rather than what they can reproduce."

### Assessment Approaches Supporting Progression

**Baseline and end-point assessment**: Establishing starting points for topics (e.g., "What do you know about...?") and reviewing learning enables teachers to identify progression within units.

**Skills-based assessment**: Observing pupils applying skills in role-play, discussion, and decision-making scenarios reveals progression more effectively than knowledge tests.

**Reflective self-assessment**: Year-on-year comparison of pupils' reflections on topics like "What makes a good friend?" or "What does democracy mean?" can demonstrate progression in sophistication.

**Case study analysis**: Progression is evident when pupils move from simplistic responses ("They should just...") to nuanced analysis considering multiple perspectives, systemic factors, and ethical complexity.

The PSHE Association recommends against numerical grading but supports qualitative assessment feedback focusing on "development in knowledge, understanding and skills, and the ability to apply learning in different contexts" (PSHE Association, 2018).

## Practical Implications for Curriculum Leaders

### Audit and Mapping

Curriculum leaders must:

1. **Conduct progression audits**: Map where each concept/topic appears across year groups and identify whether genuine progression exists or whether content merely repeats.

2. **Use the PSHE Association's "Spiral Programme" templates**: These provide exemplar progression pathways for core concepts.

3. **Apply the "Does this deepen?" test**: When planning recurring topics, explicitly identify what is different, more complex, or more sophisticated than previous encounters.

### Addressing Common Progression Pitfalls

**Repetition without progression**: The most common weakness in PSHE programmes. "Bullying" should not be identical in Year 3 and Year 9.

**Developmental inappropriateness**: Either infantilising older pupils (cartoon-based resources in Year 10) or introducing concepts too early (detailed STI information in Year 5).

**Assessment that doesn't capture progression**: Multiple-choice quizzes on factual recall miss the skills and attitudes progression central to PSHE.

**Timetabling that interrupts progression**: One-off sessions or collapsed timetable days make systematic progression nearly impossible. Regular, sequential lessons support better progression.

### Ensuring Progression Through Curriculum Design

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on long-term curriculum planning recommends:

- **Consistent terminology with expanding definitions**: E.g., consent appears in KS1 (permission for photos), KS2 (asking before touching), KS3 (relationship boundaries), KS4 (sexual consent law), KS5 (ongoing consent in relationships)

- **Vertical tracking of learning objectives**: Ensure each year's objectives build on previous years and prepare for subsequent years

- **Cross-phase moderation**: Primary-secondary PSHE leads should meet to ensure Y6-Y7 progression is appropriate

## Progression and Diverse Learners

### SEND Considerations

The DfE RSE guidance (2019) requires schools to ensure progression is appropriate for pupils with SEND. This means:

- Extending primary-style concrete approaches into secondary where appropriate
- Focusing on functional skills progression (e.g., understanding private/public in context of personal care)
- Not assuming that chronological age determines readiness for all content
- Differentiating progression pathways while maintaining high expectations

### Trauma-Informed Progression

Increasingly, PSHE curriculum leaders must consider that progression cannot be assumed for pupils affected by trauma or adverse experiences. A Year 9 pupil may need foundational work on "safe adults" more appropriate to KS1 due to life circumstances. Quality PSHE allows for flexible progression responsive to individual need whilst maintaining core age-appropriate curriculum entitlement.

## Evaluating Progression: Evidence and Impact

### Research Evidence

The Education Endowment Foundation's review of Social and Emotional Learning interventions (EEF, 2021) found that structured, progressive programmes show significantly better outcomes (+4 months) than ad-hoc approaches. This supports investment in well-planned PSHE progression.

Research by Humphrey et al. (2010) on Social and Emotional Aspects of Learning (SEAL) demonstrated that progression was most evident when:
- Concepts were revisited with increasing complexity
- Skills were practised repeatedly in varied contexts
- Whole-school approaches reinforced classroom learning

### Monitoring Progression

Leaders should monitor progression through:

- **Work scrutiny**: Comparing pupil responses to similar questions/tasks across year groups
- **Pupil voice**: Asking pupils to articulate what they've learned that goes beyond previous years
- **Lesson observation**: Noting whether teaching pitches learning appropriately and extends understanding
- **Outcomes data**: Tracking safeguarding incidents, attendance, wellbeing surveys over time

## Conclusion

Progression in PSHE requires sophisticated curriculum design that respects the spiral nature of learning, developmental appropriateness, and the complex interplay of knowledge, skills, and attitudes. As Nick Boddington emphasizes, effective progression means pupils "encounter similar themes repeatedly but each time engage with them at a deeper level, apply learning in more complex contexts, and develop greater autonomy in using their learning" (Boddington, 2020).

For curriculum leaders, ensuring genuine progression—rather than repetition—demands rigorous planning, clear assessment frameworks, and ongoing evaluation. The statutory status of RSE and Health Education has intensified accountability for demonstrating progression, making this a critical priority for all schools. When progression is well-designed, PSHE equips young people with increasingly sophisticated capabilities to navigate relationships, health, citizenship, and adult life successfully.
