# Handling Sensitive and Controversial Topics

## Introduction and Context

Handling sensitive and controversial topics represents one of the most challenging yet essential aspects of effective PSHE & Citizenship education. The PSHE Association defines controversial issues as those that "arouse strong feelings and divide communities" and where "conflicting explanations, values and judgements may be offered." In the UK context, teachers must navigate topics including racism, sexual health, political tensions, extremism, gender identity, and socioeconomic inequality whilst maintaining legal compliance and supporting all pupils' wellbeing.

The statutory Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education guidance (DfE, 2019, updated 2021) requires schools to address potentially controversial topics whilst respecting the backgrounds and beliefs of pupils and parents. This guidance explicitly acknowledges that "some of the topics taught may be of a sensitive nature," requiring carefully considered pedagogical approaches.

## Theoretical Foundations

### The Crick Report Legacy

Professor Bernard Crick's seminal 1998 report "Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools" established that controversial issues must not be avoided but rather taught through balanced presentation of opposing views. Crick argued that democracy requires citizens capable of engaging with disagreement constructively. His framework emphasised "procedural neutrality" – teachers facilitating discussion without imposing personal views whilst ensuring pupils encounter multiple perspectives.

### The Controversial Issues Framework

Lee Jerome and Nick Boddington's extensive research on teaching controversial issues (particularly their 2014 book "Teaching Controversial Issues in the Classroom") identifies four key approaches:

1. **The neutral chair approach**: Teacher acts as impartial facilitator
2. **Balanced approach**: Teacher presents multiple perspectives without declaring their own position
3. **Stated commitment**: Teacher declares their view but presents other perspectives fairly
4. **Devil's advocate**: Teacher challenges all viewpoints to develop critical thinking

Jerome emphasises that the choice of approach must be "context-dependent," considering pupils' age, the specific issue, school community values, and legal requirements.

## Legal and Statutory Framework

### Statutory Requirements

The Education Act 1996 (Section 406) prohibits the "promotion of partisan political views" and requires that "where political or controversial issues are brought to the attention of pupils...they are offered a balanced presentation of opposing views." This applies to all maintained schools, academies, and free schools.

The Equality Act 2010 requires schools to actively promote equality and eliminate discrimination regarding protected characteristics (age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation). Teaching about controversial topics related to these characteristics demands particular sensitivity.

The Counter-Terrorism and Security Act 2015 places a Prevent Duty on schools to "have due regard to the need to prevent people from being drawn into terrorism." This creates tension between open discussion and safeguarding concerns, requiring teachers to balance free enquiry with protection against extremist influences.

### Departmental Guidance

The DfE's "Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education" statutory guidance (2019, revised 2021) states that schools should provide a "safe environment to discuss sensitive topics" including mental health, sexual violence, consent, domestic abuse, pornography, gender identity, and online safety. The guidance emphasises age-appropriateness and parental consultation whilst maintaining children's rights to education.

## Key Pedagogical Principles

### Establishing Ground Rules

Jenny Mosley's Circle Time approach provides valuable structures for discussing sensitive topics. Her "Golden Rules" framework emphasises mutual respect, active listening, and the right to pass. For controversial issues, adapted ground rules should include:

- **Respectful disagreement**: Ideas can be challenged; individuals cannot be attacked
- **Confidentiality with boundaries**: Personal experiences shared remain private, but safeguarding concerns will be reported
- **Use of third-person scenarios**: Discussing hypothetical cases rather than personal circumstances
- **Right to withdraw**: Pupils may observe without participating, though not disengage entirely
- **Challenge comfort zones, not safety**: Pushing thinking whilst maintaining emotional security

The PSHE Association recommends co-constructing ground rules with pupils at Key Stage 3 and above, promoting ownership and accountability.

### Creating Psychological Safety

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on trauma-informed PSHE highlights that controversial topics may trigger adverse responses in pupils with previous trauma exposure. His approach requires:

- **Pre-teaching notification**: Alerting pupils to upcoming sensitive content, allowing mental preparation or withdrawal if necessary
- **Distancing techniques**: Using case studies, role-play, or media scenarios rather than direct personal disclosure
- **Exit strategies**: Designated "safe spaces" or trusted staff pupils can access if overwhelmed
- **Debrief opportunities**: Follow-up discussions to process emotional responses

Research by Wolfe and colleagues (2019) demonstrates that psychological safety significantly increases pupil engagement with difficult content and reduces anxiety around controversial topics.

## Age-Appropriate Approaches Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Ages 5-11)

At primary level, controversial topics are introduced through concrete, relatable scenarios. The focus is on developing:

- **Respectful disagreement**: Recognising that people hold different views on issues like vegetarianism, religious practices, or family structures
- **Fairness and justice**: Age-appropriate discussions of inequality, discrimination, and rights
- **Democratic participation**: Class councils, voting, and fair decision-making processes

Nick Boddington's research suggests that primary pupils can engage with controversial issues when presented through stories, drama, and guided questioning rather than abstract debate. For example, exploring fairness through picture books about refugees (e.g., "The Island" by Armin Greder) without requiring pupils to articulate complex political positions.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Early secondary education introduces more explicit engagement with controversy. The PSHE Association's Programme of Study recommends addressing:

- **Identity and diversity**: Gender identity, sexual orientation, disability rights, and cultural/religious diversity
- **Rights and responsibilities**: Human rights, British values, and competing rights claims
- **Media literacy**: Recognising bias, propaganda, and misinformation
- **Economic controversy**: Wealth distribution, taxation, and welfare

Teaching strategies should include structured discussion formats (think-pair-share, debate diamonds, continuum lines) and critical media analysis. Jerome and Elwick's research (2019) demonstrates that KS3 pupils benefit from "staged disclosure" – beginning with less personally threatening topics before addressing more sensitive issues.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE Citizenship Studies specifications explicitly require engagement with controversial issues. AQA's specification (8100) includes assessment of "the ability to recognise and handle controversial issues and explain different viewpoints." Topics include:

- **Political controversy**: Electoral systems, devolution, nationalism, immigration policy
- **Legal and ethical debates**: Criminal justice, drug policy, euthanasia, abortion
- **Rights tensions**: Freedom of expression versus hate speech, religious freedom versus equality law
- **Environmental controversy**: Climate action, economic growth, and intergenerational justice

Edexcel's specification (1CS0) similarly requires analysis of "contrasting opinions" on issues including multiculturalism, censorship, and international conflict. OCR's specification (J270) emphasises evaluation of "different viewpoints including those of pressure groups and political parties."

At this level, formal debate structures, parliamentary-style discussions, and extended writing analysing multiple perspectives become appropriate. Research by Hess (2009) indicates that adolescents develop stronger political efficacy and critical thinking when regularly engaging with authentic controversy.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

Post-16 PSHE should engage with sophisticated controversy, including:

- **Sexual ethics**: Consent culture, pornography, sex work, relationships and power
- **Contemporary extremism**: Far-right ideology, Islamist extremism, conspiracy theories
- **Bioethics**: Genetic engineering, AI ethics, reproductive technologies
- **Systemic inequality**: Institutional racism, patriarchy, class structures

Teaching should encourage independent research, philosophical enquiry, and awareness of cognitive biases. Students should recognise their own positioning and how identity shapes perspective on controversial issues.

## Specific Controversial Topics: Guidance and Approaches

### Teaching About Racism and Racial Justice

Following the 2020 Black Lives Matter protests and the Sewell Report (Commission on Race and Ethnic Disparities, 2021), teaching about racism has become particularly contentious. Schools must navigate:

- **Historical legacies**: British Empire, colonialism, and the slave trade require honest examination without inducing guilt or defensiveness
- **Contemporary racism**: Institutional racism, microaggressions, and white privilege are contested concepts requiring careful presentation
- **Political polarisation**: "Anti-racism" versus "colour-blind" approaches represent fundamentally different frameworks

The PSHE Association's "Talking about race and racism" guidance (2020) recommends:
- Using first-person accounts and testimony alongside statistical evidence
- Acknowledging discomfort whilst maintaining engagement
- Examining racism as systemic phenomenon, not merely individual prejudice
- Ensuring diverse voices and perspectives, avoiding single narratives

### Sexual Health and Relationships Education

The RSE statutory guidance generates significant controversy, particularly regarding:

- **Age-appropriateness**: When and how to introduce topics like masturbation, pornography, and sexual pleasure
- **LGBTQ+ inclusion**: Teaching about same-sex relationships, gender identity, and transgender issues
- **Religious and cultural sensitivities**: Balancing inclusive education with respect for diverse beliefs
- **Parental rights**: The right to withdraw from sex education (but not relationships or health education) until age 15

Research by Emmerson and colleagues (2021) demonstrates that comprehensive RSE reduces sexual risk-taking and improves understanding of consent. However, implementation requires:

- **Transparent curriculum sharing**: Publishing RSE content before delivery, allowing parental consultation
- **Inclusive language**: Presenting LGBTQ+ relationships as normative without erasing heterosexual majority
- **Values clarification**: Helping pupils identify their own values whilst understanding legal and rights frameworks
- **External expertise**: Utilising organisations like the School Nursing Service, Brook, or Stonewall for specialist input

### Religion and Belief

Religious controversy intersects with multiple PSHE topics. Jerome's research identifies particular challenges:

- **Creationism versus evolution**: Balancing religious belief with scientific consensus
- **Religious law versus civil law**: Sharia, Jewish law, and secular legal systems
- **Proselytisation concerns**: Ensuring education about religion doesn't become indoctrination
- **Inter-faith tensions**: Historical and contemporary conflicts between religious groups

Effective approaches include:
- Clear distinction between learning *about* religion (legitimate) and *into* religion (inappropriate in PSHE)
- Phenomenological approach: Examining how religious adherents experience and understand their faith
- Focusing on shared values across traditions whilst acknowledging genuine differences
- Addressing prejudice, discrimination, and religious hate crime as citizenship issues

### Political Issues and Elections

Teaching about party politics, elections, and policy debates requires scrupulous balance. The Education Act 1996 requirements are particularly relevant here. Boddington's framework suggests:

1. **Issue-based approach**: Examining policy questions (e.g., "Should university tuition be free?") rather than party allegiances
2. **Representative perspectives**: Ensuring major viewpoints receive fair hearing without false equivalence
3. **Evidence evaluation**: Teaching pupils to assess claims, manifestos, and political arguments critically
4. **Procedural democracy**: Emphasising democratic processes, voting systems, and representation mechanisms

During election periods, schools should avoid:
- Hosting single-party speakers without balanced representation
- Teachers wearing party political symbols or campaigning
- Mock elections that advantage particular parties
- Presenting policy preferences as factual correctness

The Association for Citizenship Teaching's "Elections Guidance" provides detailed protocols for maintaining political neutrality whilst engaging with authentic political controversy.

### Extremism and Terrorism

The Prevent Duty creates particular complexity. Schools must educate about extremism whilst avoiding:

- **Stigmatisation**: Disproportionately associating particular communities (especially Muslims) with extremism
- **Chilling effects**: Pupils avoiding legitimate political discussion from fear of referral
- **Oversimplification**: Presenting extremism as simple radicalisation process rather than complex phenomenon

Effective approaches, supported by research from Busher and colleagues (2017), include:

- **Critical media literacy**: Analysing extremist propaganda techniques and recruitment strategies
- **Democratic empowerment**: Strengthening belief in democratic participation as alternative to extremism
- **Contested concepts**: Examining how "terrorism," "extremism," and "radicalisation" are defined and deployed
- **Safeguarding clarity**: Distinguishing between extremist grooming (safeguarding concern) and controversial political views (legitimate discussion)

The DfE's "Promoting fundamental British values" guidance (2014) requires teaching democracy, rule of law, individual liberty, and mutual respect/tolerance. However, critics including Jerome and Elwick argue this can become "indoctrination" if presented uncritically rather than as contestable political values.

## Assessment and Evaluation

Assessing learning about controversial issues presents unique challenges. Traditional testing may incentivise pupils to identify "correct" answers rather than engage genuinely with complexity.

### Assessment Approaches

**GCSE Citizenship Studies** employs extended response questions requiring candidates to "analyse and evaluate different viewpoints" (AQA mark scheme descriptor). High-quality responses demonstrate:
- Recognition of multiple legitimate perspectives
- Evidence-based reasoning for different positions
- Evaluation of strengths and limitations of competing arguments
- Balanced judgement supported by evidence

**PSHE assessment** should focus on:
- **Process skills**: Active listening, respectful disagreement, perspective-taking
- **Knowledge**: Understanding of key concepts, legal frameworks, and evidence
- **Values reflection**: Ability to articulate personal values whilst respecting others'
- **Application**: Transferring learning to new controversial scenarios

The PSHE Association's assessment guidance recommends self and peer assessment, reflective journals, and case study analysis rather than fact-recall testing.

## Teacher Professional Development and Support

Handling controversial topics requires significant professional skill and ongoing support.

### Essential Professional Learning

Teachers need:

1. **Subject knowledge**: Deep understanding of topics including current evidence, legal frameworks, and competing perspectives
2. **Facilitation skills**: Managing discussion, handling challenging questions, responding to disclosure
3. **Self-awareness**: Recognising personal biases and how these might influence teaching
4. **Safeguarding competence**: Identifying concerning views or disclosures requiring escalation
5. **Cultural competence**: Understanding diverse community perspectives and avoiding assumptions

The PSHE Association's subject specialist training and the Association for Citizenship Teaching's professional development programmes provide essential support. Many schools also utilise external specialists from organisations including Stonewall, Show Racism the Red Card, or the Holocaust Educational Trust for particularly sensitive topics.

### Institutional Support Structures

Schools should provide:

- **Clear policies**: Protocols for handling complaints, parental concerns, and safeguarding issues arising from controversial topics
- **Departmental planning**: Team-teaching opportunities and shared resource development
- **Senior leadership backing**: Explicit support for teachers navigating controversy
- **Parental communication**: Proactive engagement explaining curriculum rationale
- **Incident response procedures**: Clear processes when controversial discussions generate concern

Research by Weinberg and Flack (2019) demonstrates that teacher confidence in handling controversial issues correlates strongly with perceived institutional support.

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge: Pupils Expressing Extreme or Offensive Views

**Response**: 
- Distinguish between exploring controversial ideas (legitimate) and expressing hatred/promoting violence (unacceptable)
- Challenge views with evidence and questioning rather than censorship
- Use "call-in" rather than "call-out" approach when appropriate, privately discussing concerning views
- Follow safeguarding procedures if views indicate vulnerability to extremism
- Model respectful disagreement and democratic debate

### Challenge: Teacher's Personal Views Under Pressure

**Response**:
- Decide approach in advance (neutrality, balanced approach, or stated commitment)
- If declaring position, explicitly label it as personal view and present alternatives fairly
- Redirect focus to evidence, arguments, and reasoning rather than personal opinion
- Use "many people believe..." rather than "I believe..." where appropriate
- Recognise that complete neutrality may be impossible or undesirable on issues like racism or homophobia where legal and ethical consensus exists

### Challenge: Parental Complaints About Content

**Response**:
- Proactive communication about curriculum prevents many complaints
- Meet with parents, listen to concerns, and explain educational rationale
- Reference statutory requirements and school's legal obligations
- Offer to share resources and teaching approaches
- Distinguish between parental rights to withdraw (limited to sex education) and broader curriculum requirements
- Involve senior leadership and governors where necessary
- Document conversations and decisions

### Challenge: Limited Curriculum Time

**Response**:
- Prioritise depth over breadth – fewer topics explored properly rather than superficial coverage
- Integrate controversial issues across curriculum (citizenship, RE, English, History) rather than isolating in PSHE
- Use homework for pre-reading, video watching, or reflection to maximise contact time for discussion
- Employ flipped learning approaches where appropriate
