# Teaching Active Citizenship

## Introduction

Active citizenship represents one of the most distinctive and challenging elements of PSHE & Citizenship education in UK schools. Bernard Crick, whose 1998 Advisory Group report fundamentally shaped modern citizenship education, distinguished between civic knowledge, social and moral responsibility, and political literacy as the three strands of effective citizenship teaching. Active citizenship specifically emphasises the participatory dimension—moving beyond knowledge acquisition to genuine engagement with democratic processes and community action.

The Department for Education's statutory guidance for Citizenship at Key Stages 3 and 4 (2013) explicitly requires that pupils "develop their understanding of democracy, government and how laws are made and upheld" whilst also gaining "skills to think critically and debate political questions". Active citizenship pedagogy provides the vehicle through which these requirements translate into meaningful student experiences.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Bernard Crick's Participatory Model

Bernard Crick's seminal report "Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools" (1998) established active citizenship as central to democratic education. Crick argued that citizenship education must be "more than a statutory subject" and should permeate school culture. His model distinguished between:

- **Civic knowledge**: Understanding political and legal systems
- **Skills of enquiry and communication**: The capacity to analyse, debate and participate
- **Values and dispositions**: Willingness to engage in public life

Crick emphasised that knowledge without participation produces "spectator citizens" rather than active participants in democracy.

### Lee Jerome's Action Competence Framework

Lee Jerome (Professor of Education at Middlesex University) has extensively researched citizenship pedagogy, particularly the concept of "action competence". In his work with the Citizenship Foundation and subsequent academic publications, Jerome identifies four key pedagogical challenges:

1. Moving from simulated to authentic participation
2. Balancing teacher direction with student agency
3. Addressing the "implementation gap" between curriculum intentions and classroom reality
4. Managing controversial issues within participatory activities

Jerome's research in "Teaching Politics and Citizenship" (2012) demonstrates that effective active citizenship requires structured progression from teacher-led activities through guided participation to student-initiated action.

### Nick Boddington's Reflective Citizenship Model

Nick Boddington, former Senior Adviser at the Citizenship Foundation, developed frameworks emphasising reflection as integral to citizenship action. His "Take Part" resources (2008-2012) promoted a cycle of:

1. **Identify**: Recognising issues that matter
2. **Plan**: Developing strategies for action
3. **Act**: Implementing citizenship activities
4. **Reflect**: Evaluating impact and learning

This cyclical model ensures active citizenship develops critical thinking alongside practical engagement.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 2 (Primary)

Although PSHE and Citizenship are non-statutory at primary level, many schools incorporate active citizenship through:

- **School councils**: Jenny Mosley's "Circle Time" model provides structured approaches to democratic classroom participation, with her Quality Circle Time framework supporting children to raise issues and make collective decisions
- **Local community projects**: Litter picks, charity fundraising, intergenerational activities
- **Democratic processes**: Class voting on decisions, developing class rules collectively

The Association for Citizenship Teaching recommends introducing concepts of fairness, rules, and collective decision-making from Year 3 onwards.

### Key Stage 3

The National Curriculum for Citizenship (2013) states pupils should be taught to:

- "Use a range of research strategies... to investigate political, social and moral issues"
- "Evaluate different viewpoints, exploring connections and distinctions"
- "Present reasoned arguments and take informed action"

Practical active citizenship approaches include:

**Year 7**: School-based democracy (student councils, peer mediation schemes), local community mapping exercises, and participation in national initiatives like UK Parliament Week.

**Year 8**: Campaign development on school issues, engagement with local councillors, mock elections, and analysis of pressure group strategies. Jonathan Charlesworth's work on teaching about activism (Curriculum Journal, 2019) emphasises connecting classroom learning about historical and contemporary campaigns to students' own capacity for action.

**Year 9**: Social action projects linking to curriculum topics (e.g., environmental campaigns connecting to Geography), developing petitions, engaging with online democratic tools, and participating in youth forums.

### Key Stage 4

GCSE specifications provide explicit frameworks for active citizenship:

**AQA Citizenship Studies (8100)**: Requires students to "plan and undertake at least one citizenship action". The specification defines this as activity "intended to bring about change or demonstrate viewpoint in relation to a citizenship issue". Assessment occurs through controlled assessment worth 25% of the final grade.

**Edexcel Citizenship (1CS0)**: Component 3 ("Citizenship in Action") requires students to plan, carry out and evaluate citizenship action, assessed through a written paper examining a pre-released scenario. This represents 25% of the qualification.

**OCR Citizenship Studies (J270)**: The "Citizenship in Action" component requires students to develop a hypothesis about a citizenship issue, research it, and propose action-based solutions.

All specifications require documented evidence of planning, implementation, and evaluation—embedding reflective practice within participatory activity.

### Key Stage 5

A-Level Politics and Citizenship courses incorporate active citizenship through:

- Extended research projects on political participation
- Engagement with current political events and campaigns
- Collaboration with external organisations (Parliament, pressure groups, NGOs)
- Development of policy proposals addressing contemporary issues

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Social Action Projects

Social action provides authentic contexts for active citizenship. Effective practice involves:

**Identification phase**: Students identify genuine issues within their experience (school, local community, national/global concerns). This requires teaching critical literacy—reading the social environment to recognise injustice, inequality, or areas for improvement.

**Research and planning**: Students investigate issues using primary and secondary sources, analyse stakeholder perspectives, and develop theories of change. Lee Jerome's research emphasises the importance of realistic goal-setting and understanding of political feasibility.

**Implementation**: Action may include campaigning, fundraising, direct service, advocacy, or awareness-raising. The key criterion is authentic impact rather than simulation.

**Evaluation**: Students assess effectiveness, reflecting on what worked, what didn't, and what they learned about democratic participation. This metacognitive dimension transforms activity into learning.

### Participatory Decision-Making

Democratic school structures provide ongoing active citizenship opportunities:

**School councils**: Effective practice requires genuine authority over meaningful decisions (not merely peripheral issues like break-time snacks). Jenny Mosley's model emphasises training both student representatives and staff in democratic facilitation, regular communication between representatives and constituents, and transparent decision-making processes.

**Participatory budgeting**: Some schools allocate budgets for student-led projects, requiring students to develop proposals, present to peers, and vote on resource allocation—mirroring democratic processes whilst developing financial literacy.

**Restorative justice approaches**: Student involvement in conflict resolution and disciplinary processes develops understanding of justice, rights, and responsibilities in practical contexts.

### Campaign Development

Teaching campaign strategies provides transferable citizenship skills:

**Campaign analysis**: Studying successful historical and contemporary campaigns (Suffragettes, Civil Rights Movement, climate activism) to identify strategies, tactics, and theories of change.

**Message development**: Teaching persuasive communication, targeting specific audiences, and using appropriate media (social media, posters, speeches, petitions).

**Coalition building**: Understanding stakeholder analysis and building support across different groups.

**Evaluation**: Measuring campaign impact through qualitative and quantitative indicators.

Jonathan Charlesworth's research emphasises the importance of connecting young people's digital activism with traditional forms of political participation, helping them understand how online and offline action complement each other.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Creating Safe Spaces for Controversial Issues

Active citizenship inevitably engages controversial political and social issues. Effective practice requires:

**Establishing ground rules**: Co-created classroom agreements emphasising respect, evidence-based argument, and right to hold different views.

**Balanced presentation**: Teachers must present multiple perspectives without imposing their own views (Professional Code of Practice guidance from the Association for Citizenship Teaching).

**Structured discussion**: Using protocols like "Devil's Advocate" debates, Socratic seminars, or "Question Time" formats to channel disagreement productively.

**Emotional support**: Recognising that political issues may affect students personally, particularly regarding identity, immigration, or social justice.

The 2019 guidance on "Political Impartiality in Schools" from the Department for Education emphasises that schools should not promote partisan political views, whilst simultaneously ensuring pupils encounter a range of perspectives.

### Assessment of Active Citizenship

Assessment presents particular challenges:

**Process vs. outcome**: Effective assessment focuses on students' planning, critical thinking, and reflection rather than campaign success (which may depend on factors beyond student control).

**Evidence collection**: Students should document their citizenship journey through logs, photographs, meeting minutes, correspondence with decision-makers, and reflective journals.

**Self and peer assessment**: Democratic values suggest students should participate in evaluating their own and others' contributions.

**Avoiding tokenism**: Assessment criteria must value authentic engagement over performative displays of participation.

### Partnership Working

Effective active citizenship often requires external partnerships:

**Local government**: Many councils have youth participation officers who can facilitate engagement with democratic processes.

**Parliament**: The UK Parliament Education Service provides resources, visits, and outreach supporting active citizenship pedagogy.

**NGOs and charities**: Organisations like Amnesty International UK, Oxfam Education, and the British Red Cross provide structured programmes connecting classroom learning with authentic action.

**Youth organisations**: UK Youth Parliament, local youth councils, and organisations like the British Youth Council provide platforms for student voice and action.

Teachers must vet partnerships carefully, ensuring they align with educational purposes rather than simply providing voluntary labour or advancing particular political agendas.

## Challenges and Solutions

### The Implementation Gap

Research by Lee Jerome and others consistently identifies a gap between curriculum intentions and classroom reality. Teachers cite barriers including:

**Time constraints**: Authentic participation requires sustained commitment rather than one-off lessons. Solutions include integrating active citizenship across curriculum areas, using tutor time, and designating dedicated "enrichment" periods.

**Teacher confidence**: Many teachers, particularly non-specialists, feel uncertain facilitating political discussion. Professional development through organisations like the Association for Citizenship Teaching provides subject-specific pedagogical support.

**Risk aversion**: School leaders may discourage student activism fearing controversy or disruption. Establishing clear frameworks for acceptable action, maintaining political balance, and documenting educational outcomes can alleviate concerns.

### Ensuring Inclusion

Active citizenship risks privileging already-confident, articulate students. Inclusive practice requires:

**Differentiated roles**: Ensuring participation opportunities match diverse skills and confidence levels (research, design, communication, organisation).

**Addressing barriers**: Recognising that some students face additional obstacles to participation (caring responsibilities, language barriers, disability, immigration status).

**Culturally responsive practice**: Understanding that conceptions of citizenship and appropriate participation vary across cultures and communities.

### Avoiding Performative Participation

Bernard Crick warned against "mock citizenship"—simulations that bear little relation to authentic democratic action. Effective practice distinguishes between:

**Preparatory activities**: Simulations, debates, and mock elections that develop skills and knowledge as foundation for authentic participation.

**Authentic participation**: Engagement with genuine decision-making processes, real campaigns addressing actual issues, and activity producing tangible outcomes.

The progression from preparatory to authentic participation should be explicit and intentional within schemes of work.

## Research Evidence

### Impact Studies

Research on active citizenship pedagogy demonstrates:

**Political efficacy**: Students engaging in authentic citizenship action report increased belief in their capacity to influence political processes (Keating et al., 2010, "Embedding Citizenship in English Secondary Schools").

**Civic knowledge**: Participatory approaches produce deeper understanding of democratic processes than didactic teaching (Hahn, 2015, comparative research on citizenship education).

**Engagement beyond school**: Young people who experience meaningful participation in school are more likely to participate in civic and political life as adults (Longitudinal Study of Young People in England, DfE 2011).

### Concerns from Research

Critical research also identifies potential problems:

**Social reproduction**: Active citizenship may reinforce existing inequalities if middle-class students dominate participation (Pykett, 2007, research on citizenship and social class).

**Depoliticisation**: Emphasis on "social action" may direct students toward charitable volunteering rather than political activism challenging structural injustice (Kisby, 2009).

**Consultation fatigue**: Students may become cynical if their participation produces no tangible outcomes or if adults predetermine decisions (Tisdall, 2008, research on children's participation rights).

These concerns emphasise the importance of authentic participation addressing genuine issues, balanced representation of structural and individual approaches to social problems, and ensuring student voice genuinely influences decisions.

## Curriculum Integration

Active citizenship need not be confined to discrete Citizenship lessons:

**English**: Persuasive writing for campaigns, analysing political speeches, drama exploring social issues.

**Mathematics**: Statistical literacy for campaign evidence, analysing polling data, budgeting for projects.

**Science**: Evidence-based advocacy on environmental or health issues, understanding scientific literacy in public debate.

**History**: Learning from historical campaigns and social movements, connecting past and present activism.

**Geography**: Community geography, development education, environmental activism.

This integrated approach reflects the cross-curricular nature of statutory Citizenship education and provides authentic contexts for skill application.

## Conclusion

Teaching active citizenship represents both the most challenging and potentially transformative dimension of PSHE & Citizenship education. Effective practice requires balancing structured pedagogy with genuine student agency, preparing students through knowledge and skill development whilst providing authentic opportunities for meaningful participation. As Bernard Crick argued, the goal is not to produce citizens who simply know about democracy, but citizens who actively participate in democratic life.

For teachers, this means creating classroom cultures valuing student voice, establishing partnerships extending learning beyond school walls, and having the courage to facilitate engagement with real political and social issues. The evidence demonstrates that when schools embrace active citizenship authentically, students develop not only knowledge and skills but the disposition and efficacy to participate in shaping their communities and society—the ultimate goal of citizenship education.
