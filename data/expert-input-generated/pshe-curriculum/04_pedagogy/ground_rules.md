# Ground Rules and Safe Learning

## Introduction and Context

Ground rules and safe learning environments are fundamental to effective PSHE (Personal, Social, Health and Economic) education and Citizenship teaching in UK schools. Unlike other curriculum areas, PSHE education requires students to explore sensitive, personal and potentially controversial issues, making the establishment of clear boundaries and psychological safety essential pedagogical prerequisites. The PSHE Association's Programme of Study (2020) explicitly identifies the creation of a safe learning environment as a core principle underpinning quality PSHE delivery.

The statutory Relationships and Health Education guidance (DfE, 2019, updated 2020) emphasises that schools must create "a safe and supportive environment in which pupils can develop the knowledge, skills and attributes they need to keep themselves healthy and safe and to prepare them for life and work in modern Britain." This requirement places ground rules at the heart of statutory compliance.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Circle Time and Jenny Mosley's Contribution

Jenny Mosley's Quality Circle Time model has profoundly influenced ground rules practice in UK primary education since the 1990s. Mosley's work emphasises that effective circle time requires explicit "Golden Rules" co-created with pupils, typically including: "We listen to each other," "We don't use put-downs," "We have the right to pass," and "We respect confidentiality." Her research demonstrates that when children participate in creating ground rules, ownership increases and behaviour improves (Mosley, 1996; Mosley & Sonnet, 2005).

Mosley's framework aligns with the broader PSHE pedagogy by positioning the teacher as facilitator rather than instructor, creating what she terms a "democratic learning community" where emotional safety enables genuine personal development.

### Citizenship Education and Bernard Crick's Legacy

Bernard Crick's influential report, "Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools" (QCA, 1998), established participatory democracy as central to Citizenship education. Crick argued that young people cannot learn democratic values through didactic teaching alone; they must experience democratic processes. Ground rules represent this principle in action—students negotiating collective norms mirrors civic participation.

Lee Jerome and Jonathan Charlesworth, contemporary scholars building on Crick's legacy, emphasise that ground rules in Citizenship education must accommodate controversy and disagreement. Jerome's work on teaching controversial issues (Jerome, 2012) identifies "procedural neutrality" as essential, where teachers facilitate multiple perspectives without imposing personal views. Ground rules must therefore enable respectful disagreement whilst preventing harmful speech.

### Nick Boddington and Values Education

Nick Boddington's contributions to values-based education highlight that ground rules themselves embody values: respect, fairness, honesty, and compassion. His work with the Values-Based Education Trust demonstrates that explicitly connecting ground rules to shared values deepens students' moral understanding and commitment to classroom norms (Boddington, 2017). This approach is particularly relevant to the values and character development strand of PSHE education.

## Essential Components of Ground Rules

### The Right to Pass

The "right to pass" principle is universally recognised in PSHE pedagogy as non-negotiable. Given that PSHE topics may touch on students' personal experiences—family structures, bereavement, abuse, health conditions—no student should be compelled to share personal information or participate in activities that cause distress. The PSHE Association (2020) states: "Pupils must always have the right to pass on answering questions or contributing, particularly when a topic is sensitive or where they may have personal experience."

However, "passing" requires careful management. Teachers should clarify that whilst students can pass on sharing personal information, they remain responsible for engaging with curriculum content at an age-appropriate level. Silent engagement—listening, observing, reflecting—constitutes valid participation.

### Confidentiality and its Limits

Confidentiality ground rules must balance trust-building with safeguarding obligations. The standard formulation used across UK PSHE teaching is: "What we share stays here, but if someone discloses something that suggests they or another person is at risk of harm, teachers must pass this information to the designated safeguarding lead."

The NSPCC's guidance on safeguarding in PSHE contexts (2021) recommends explicitly teaching students the difference between confidentiality and secrecy. Effective practice involves explaining disclosure protocols before sensitive lessons, ensuring students understand that safeguarding breaches confidentiality for protective purposes. This transparency maintains trust whilst fulfilling statutory duties under the Children Act 1989 and Working Together to Safeguard Children (HM Government, 2018).

### Distancing Techniques

Ground rules should incorporate "distancing language" to protect privacy whilst enabling meaningful discussion. Teachers establish that discussions use third-person scenarios ("someone might feel..." rather than "I feel...") or focus on "people in general" rather than personal experience. The Sex Education Forum's guidance (2020) particularly emphasises this for Relationships and Sex Education (RSE), where students explore intimate topics without inappropriate personal disclosure.

### Respectful Language and Challenge

Ground rules must prohibit discriminatory language whilst permitting respectful disagreement. The Equality Act 2010 requires schools to prevent discrimination based on protected characteristics (age, disability, gender reassignment, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity). Ground rules operationalise these legal duties.

Contemporary best practice, informed by Jerome and Charlesworth's work on controversial issues, distinguishes between challenging ideas (encouraged) and attacking individuals (prohibited). Useful formulations include: "We criticise ideas, not people" and "We can disagree respectfully." This enables genuine debate on issues like environmental responsibility, political systems, or ethical dilemmas without creating hostile environments.

## Establishing Ground Rules: Pedagogical Approaches

### Co-construction with Students

Research consistently demonstrates that co-constructed ground rules generate greater ownership than teacher-imposed regulations (Mosley & Sonnet, 2005; PSHE Association, 2014). Effective co-construction involves:

1. **Initial exploration**: Students discuss why ground rules matter, exploring scenarios where absent rules cause harm or discomfort.
2. **Brainstorming**: Students suggest rules, with teachers facilitating rather than directing.
3. **Refinement**: The class discusses, combines similar suggestions, and ensures clarity.
4. **Formalisation**: Rules are recorded visually and displayed permanently.
5. **Review**: Regular revisiting ensures rules remain relevant and understood.

At Key Stage 1, this process requires simplification—perhaps three or four pictorial rules. At Key Stages 3 and 4, students can engage with more sophisticated concepts like procedural neutrality and proportionate speech.

### Age-Appropriate Approaches

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)**: Ground rules should be concrete, positive, and visual. Circle time approaches work effectively, with rules like "Kind hands," "Listening ears," "One person speaks." Physical tokens (e.g., a talking object) support turn-taking. Teachers largely guide rule formation, framing suggestions positively.

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)**: Students can engage more actively in co-construction, understanding abstract concepts like confidentiality (with clear explanations). Rules can address specific PSHE content, such as appropriate anatomical language for puberty education or respectful discussion of diverse families.

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)**: Ground rules should accommodate increasing sophistication in handling controversial issues. Students can debate what constitutes "respectful disagreement" and understand nuanced confidentiality limits. The DfE's Citizenship Programme of Study (2013) expects students to explore "diverse national, regional, religious and ethnic identities" and "mutual respect and understanding," requiring ground rules that enable robust but respectful debate.

**Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)**: Ground rules may incorporate elements from examination specifications. AQA GCSE Citizenship Studies (8100) and OCR GCSE Citizenship Studies (J270) assess students' ability to "evaluate different viewpoints" and "advocate and justify a personal stance." Ground rules must therefore protect psychological safety whilst encouraging intellectual challenge. Students at this stage can engage with procedural neutrality concepts explicitly.

**Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)**: While PSHE is non-statutory post-16, many schools provide relationship and careers guidance. Ground rules in sixth-form contexts should reflect adult learning principles—mutual respect between teacher and students, acknowledgment of diverse perspectives, and sophisticated handling of contentious issues like political ideologies or sexual ethics.

## Safe Learning Environments: Beyond Ground Rules

### Physical Environment

The spatial arrangement of PSHE lessons signals safety and participation. Circle formations, advocated by Mosley and widely adopted in PSHE practice, create visual equality and facilitate eye contact. The PSHE Association recommends consistent physical spaces for PSHE when possible, building associations between location and safety.

Displays should reflect diversity—family structures, cultural backgrounds, abilities—ensuring all students see themselves represented. The Equality and Human Rights Commission's guidance for schools (2014) emphasises that inclusive environments actively counteract stereotypes.

### Emotional Safety and Psychological Containment

Wilfred Bion's concept of "containment" from psychodynamic theory, whilst not specific to education, offers useful insights for PSHE practitioners. Teachers "contain" difficult emotions by acknowledging them calmly, validating feelings without being overwhelmed, and providing appropriate boundaries. When discussing bereavement, abuse, or mental health, teachers' calm, boundaried responses model emotional regulation.

The Anna Freud Centre's resources for schools (2021) emphasise that emotionally safe classrooms require "predictable routines, clear expectations, and consistent adult responses." Ground rules provide this predictability in PSHE contexts where content may be unpredictable.

### Managing Disclosures

Despite preventative measures, PSHE lessons may trigger disclosures. Safe learning environments require teachers to respond appropriately without derailing lessons for other students. Effective protocols include:

- **Acknowledging briefly**: "Thank you for sharing that. I need to speak with you after class."
- **Avoiding detailed questioning**: This is the safeguarding lead's role, not the classroom teacher's.
- **Following school procedures**: Immediately recording concerns and referring to the designated safeguarding lead.
- **Supporting the rest of the class**: Smoothly returning to planned content without discussing the disclosure.

The PSHE Association's guidance on managing disclosures (2018) emphasises that preparation—knowing school protocols, understanding confidentiality limits, and anticipating potential disclosures related to lesson content—enables calm, professional responses.

### Language and Terminology

Safe learning requires agreed, appropriate terminology. In RSE, this means correct anatomical terms (penis, vulva, vagina) from Key Stage 1, as recommended by the Sex Education Forum and Brook. Using proper terminology:

- Reduces shame and embarrassment
- Supports safeguarding (children can accurately report abuse)
- Normalises discussion of bodies and relationships
- Meets statutory RSE requirements

Ground rules should establish that whilst slang terms are acknowledged as existing, curriculum content uses proper terminology. This approach respects young people's lived experiences whilst maintaining educational standards.

## Maintaining and Reinforcing Ground Rules

### Consistent Application

Ground rules fail when inconsistently applied. If teachers permit put-downs one week but challenge them the next, rules lose credibility. Effective PSHE departments establish shared expectations across all staff delivering PSHE, as recommended in the PSHE Association's Quality Assurance framework (2019).

Regular reinforcement is essential. Practical approaches include:

- **Beginning each lesson**: Briefly reviewing key rules, perhaps via displayed posters
- **Positive reinforcement**: Acknowledging when students demonstrate rule-following
- **Addressing breaches immediately**: Calmly but firmly, linking back to agreed rules
- **Periodic review**: Termly or half-termly class discussions on whether rules remain effective

### Responding to Breaches

When students breach ground rules, responses should be educational rather than purely punitive. Approaches consistent with restorative justice principles (increasingly common in UK schools) include:

1. **Private conversation**: Discussing the breach away from the group, exploring impact on others
2. **Reconnection to values**: Asking how the behaviour aligned with shared values and rules
3. **Repairing harm**: Where appropriate, encouraging apology or other restorative actions
4. **Re-engagement**: Welcoming the student back to the learning community

Serious breaches—discriminatory language, bullying, or safeguarding concerns—require school behaviour policies and safeguarding procedures to supersede classroom-level responses.

### Adapting Rules Over Time

As students mature or class dynamics shift, ground rules may require revision. Participatory review processes reinforce that rules serve the community rather than existing as fixed impositions. Teachers might ask: "Are our rules still working? Is anything missing? Does anything need changing?"

This flexibility demonstrates democratic citizenship in action, aligning with National Curriculum Citizenship aims of understanding how "citizens work together to improve their communities" (DfE, 2013).

## Special Considerations

### SEND Contexts

Students with special educational needs and disabilities may require adapted ground rules approaches. For students with autism spectrum conditions, concrete, explicit rules with visual supports reduce anxiety. Social scripts can clarify expected behaviours. The PSHE Association's guidance on SEND (2020) recommends:

- Smaller group work where mainstream class sizes overwhelm
- Pre-teaching ground rules before main lessons
- Visual timetables showing PSHE lesson structure
- Clear, literal language avoiding idioms or ambiguity

For students with social, emotional and mental health needs, consistent boundaries within safe relationships are particularly therapeutic. Trauma-informed approaches, increasingly recognised in UK education through frameworks like Attachment Aware Schools, emphasise that ground rules provide containment for students whose early experiences lacked safe boundaries.

### Alternative Provision and Pupil Referral Units

Students in alternative provision often have complex backgrounds involving trauma, exclusion, or safeguarding concerns. Ground rules require careful negotiation. The assumption that all students will share openly may be inappropriate; privacy and emotional safety take precedence. Experienced practitioners in these contexts often emphasise ground rules about respect and non-judgment above participation expectations.

### Faith Schools and Values Contexts

Faith schools teaching RSE and PSHE within religious frameworks must balance faith teachings with legal requirements, including the Equality Act 2010. Ground rules can acknowledge that "we respect different beliefs, including religious teachings and legal rights" whilst ensuring no student feels their identity is unacceptable. The Church of England's guidance on RSE (2019) and the Catholic Education Service's Learning to Love (2017) both emphasise respect and inclusion within faith contexts.

## Assessment and Evidence of Effectiveness

While PSHE education remains non-statutory and largely ungraded at GCSE level (Citizenship being the exception), assessing the effectiveness of ground rules in creating safe learning is essential for quality assurance.

Evidence of effective ground rules includes:

- **Student voice**: Surveys, focus groups, or School Council feedback indicating students feel safe discussing sensitive topics
- **Participation rates**: High engagement, with students willing to contribute (while respecting right to pass)
- **Absence of discriminatory incidents**: Monitoring shows reduced prejudice-based language or bullying
- **Quality of discussion**: Students engaging with diverse viewpoints respectfully
- **Safeguarding data**: Appropriate disclosures occurring, suggesting students trust adults

Ofsted's revised Education Inspection Framework (2019) examines personal development, including PSHE provision. Inspectors consider whether schools provide "a safe environment in which pupils can develop and thrive" and whether pupils "learn how to keep themselves safe." Well-established and consistently applied ground rules provide evidence for these judgements.

## Practical Resources and Ongoing Professional Development

The PSHE Association (www.pshe-association.org.uk) provides extensive resources on ground rules, including downloadable templates and training modules. Their teacher guidance documents offer scenario-based advice for managing difficult situations within safe learning parameters.

Local authority PSHE advisers often deliver training on establishing safe learning environments. The Sex Education Forum and Brook provide specialist RSE training emphasising ground rules for sensitive content.

Initial Teacher Training programmes increasingly include PSHE elements, though coverage remains inconsistent. The Association for Citizenship Teaching advocates for enhanced Citizenship-specific ITT content, including handling controversial issues within bounded classroom environments.

Continuing Professional Development should include regular refreshers on safeguarding procedures, as these directly inform confidentiality ground rules, and updates on contemporary issues (e.g., online safety, gender identity debates) that may test existing ground rules.

## Conclusion

Ground rules and safe learning environments are not optional extras in PSHE and Citizenship education; they are pedagogical necessities enabling curriculum delivery. Without psychological safety, students cannot genuinely explore identity, relationships, health, and citizenship. Without clear boundaries, teachers cannot balance openness with safeguarding, or respect with challenge.

The most effective ground rules are co-constructed with students, consistently applied, regularly reviewed, and explicitly connected to the values underpinning both PSHE education and democratic citizenship. They reflect the theoretical contributions of Mosley's circle time principles, Crick's democratic participation, Jerome's procedural neutrality, and Boddington's values education.

For teachers, establishing ground rules requires time, skill, and ongoing attention. The investment is essential: safe learning spaces enable transformative PSHE education that genuinely prepares young people for the opportunities, responsibilities, and experiences of adult life in contemporary Britain.
