# Distancing Techniques in PSHE

## Introduction and Rationale

Distancing techniques are pedagogical strategies that create psychological and emotional safety when teaching sensitive or potentially distressing content in PSHE education. These approaches enable pupils to explore challenging issues such as abuse, bereavement, relationship breakdown, mental health crises, and substance misuse without requiring personal disclosure or causing unnecessary distress. The PSHE Association's guidance consistently emphasises that "PSHE education should never put pupils in a position where they feel pressured to disclose personal information" (PSHE Association, 2020), making distancing techniques fundamental to effective and ethical practice.

Within the context of the Department for Education's statutory Relationships, Sex and Health Education guidance (DfE, 2019, updated 2020), distancing techniques provide the pedagogical framework through which teachers can meet their legal obligations whilst maintaining a safe learning environment. These techniques align with the principle that PSHE education should be "sensitive and age-appropriate in approach and content" whilst addressing topics that may be challenging or emotionally laden.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Educational Psychology Perspective

Distancing techniques draw upon Vygotsky's concept of the 'zone of proximal development', allowing pupils to engage with difficult content within a supportive framework that scaffolds emotional as well as cognitive learning. The techniques create what Jerome and Kisby (2019) describe as a "safe critical distance" in their research on controversial issues teaching, enabling pupils to develop critical thinking about sensitive topics without feeling personally vulnerable or exposed.

Bernard Crick's seminal work on citizenship education emphasised the importance of creating "procedural neutrality" when discussing controversial issues (Crick, 1998, *Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools*). Whilst Crick focused primarily on political literacy, his principles extend naturally to PSHE contexts where personal and social issues require similar careful handling.

### Safeguarding Considerations

Nick Boddington, in his work with the PSHE Association, has consistently emphasised that distancing techniques serve a dual safeguarding purpose. Firstly, they protect pupils from unnecessary emotional distress whilst learning. Secondly, they reduce the risk of inadvertent disclosure in inappropriate settings, ensuring that when disclosures do occur, they happen within proper safeguarding channels rather than in whole-class discussions (Boddington, 2016).

Jonathan Charlesworth's research on relationships and sex education pedagogy highlights that distancing techniques enable teachers to fulfil their statutory duty to teach sensitive content whilst respecting the diverse experiences, backgrounds, and cultural contexts within their classrooms (Charlesworth, 2015).

## Key Distancing Techniques

### Third-Person Narratives and Case Studies

This technique involves using fictional characters, scenarios, or anonymised case studies rather than asking pupils to draw on personal experience. For example, when teaching about family conflict at Key Stage 3, teachers might present a scenario: "Sarah's parents have recently separated. She finds it difficult to concentrate at school and feels torn between both parents. What support might help Sarah?"

**Practical application:**
- Ensure fictional names reflect the diversity of your school community
- Make scenarios age-appropriate and realistic but not so specific that pupils identify real individuals
- Provide sufficient detail for meaningful discussion whilst maintaining emotional distance
- Use written scenarios, video clips, or illustrated stories as stimulus materials

**Key Stage considerations:**
- **KS1-2:** Simple, clear narratives with relatable but distinctly fictional characters (Jenny Mosley's 'circle time' approaches often employ puppet characters or story book figures)
- **KS3:** More complex scenarios with multiple perspectives, developing empathy through distanced analysis
- **KS4-5:** Sophisticated case studies that enable critical analysis of policy, support systems, and ethical dimensions

### Depersonalised Language

Teachers should model and encourage the use of general rather than personal language. Using phrases such as "some people find that...", "research shows that...", or "young people might experience..." creates distance whilst validating that these issues affect real people.

**Practical strategies:**
- Establish ground rules explicitly stating "we won't ask anyone to share personal experiences"
- If pupils begin to disclose, gently redirect: "That sounds like an important issue—let's think about what people in that situation might need..."
- Use "someone" or "a person" rather than "you" when posing questions
- Frame discussions around "people's rights" or "what support exists" rather than "what would you do"

### Anonymous Question Boxes and 'Ask-It Baskets'

This technique, strongly advocated by the PSHE Association, allows pupils to submit questions anonymously, creating significant emotional distance whilst enabling genuine concerns to be addressed. Teachers can then address questions collectively without requiring individual pupils to identify themselves.

**Implementation guidance:**
- Establish clear protocols about what happens to questions (answered in next lesson, filtered for appropriateness, etc.)
- Use questions as starting points for distanced discussions
- Have procedures for questions that indicate safeguarding concerns
- Make this a regular feature, normalising question-asking

### Role Play and Forum Theatre

Structured drama techniques enable pupils to explore scenarios whilst the role-play framework itself creates psychological distance. Forum theatre, where audiences can stop action and suggest alternatives, provides particularly effective distancing as developed by Augusto Boal's Theatre of the Oppressed methodology.

**Safeguarding considerations:**
- Never cast pupils in roles that mirror their known personal circumstances
- Provide clear 'de-roling' activities afterwards
- Allow pupils to opt out of participation whilst observing
- Use 'teacher-in-role' to model appropriate engagement

### Media and Literature Analysis

Examining how issues are portrayed in age-appropriate films, news articles, novels, or social media scenarios creates inherent distance. This approach aligns with media literacy objectives within both PSHE and Citizenship curricula.

**Cross-curricular links:**
- English curriculum connections through text analysis
- Citizenship links through media representation and rights
- Computing curriculum through digital literacy and online safety

**Examples by Key Stage:**
- **KS2:** Picture books addressing bereavement, family change, or bullying (e.g., Michael Rosen's *Sad Book*)
- **KS3:** Age-appropriate news articles, film clips, or documentary extracts
- **KS4:** Analysis of campaigns, policy documents, or public health materials

### Surveys and Statistical Data

Presenting anonymous survey data or national statistics creates maximum distance whilst validating that issues affect many people. This technique is particularly effective for topics where pupils may feel isolated in their experiences.

**Application examples:**
- NHS statistics on mental health prevalence among young people
- Office for National Statistics data on family structures
- Anonymous class surveys conducted beforehand (ensuring anonymity is genuine)
- Research findings from organisations like The Children's Society or YoungMinds

## Key Stage Applications

### Early Years Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1

At EYFS and KS1, distancing techniques primarily employ story-based approaches and puppet work. Jenny Mosley's 'Quality Circle Time' model (Mosley, 1996, 2005) extensively uses distancing through:
- A designated 'talking object' that creates ritual distance
- Stories where puppet characters face dilemmas
- 'Round' activities where children complete general sentences ("Someone might feel sad when...")

The PSHE Association's Planning Framework for Pupils with SEND (2018) emphasises that younger pupils or those with certain special educational needs may require more concrete distancing mechanisms such as physical puppets or visual story sequences.

### Key Stage 2

KS2 pupils can engage with more sophisticated narratives whilst still benefiting from clear fictional framing. The non-statutory PSHE curriculum guidance suggests addressing:
- Different family structures (using fictional family scenarios)
- Physical and emotional changes approaching puberty (using anatomical diagrams and general descriptions)
- Basic understanding of bereavement (through age-appropriate literature)

**Statutory Science curriculum connections:** When teaching reproduction and puberty (statutory at KS2), distancing through anatomical terminology and scientific framing provides appropriate distance whilst meeting curriculum requirements.

### Key Stage 3

At KS3, distancing techniques become more nuanced. Lee Jerome's work on discussion-based learning in citizenship education (Jerome, 2008) demonstrates that early secondary pupils can engage with controversial issues when:
- Multiple perspectives are explicitly presented
- Teachers model balanced facilitation
- Discussion protocols create structured distance (e.g., 'debate' formats, 'continuum' activities)

The PSHE Association's Key Stage 3 Programme of Study includes content on:
- Mental health and emotional wellbeing (addressed through case studies and statistics)
- Healthy and unhealthy relationships (through media analysis and scenarios)
- Substance use and misuse (via harm reduction information presented factually)

**Citizenship curriculum connections:** The statutory Citizenship curriculum at KS3 requires teaching about "the legal system in the UK, different sources of law and how the law helps society deal with complex problems." This naturally employs distancing through legal case analysis and rights-based frameworks.

### Key Stage 4 and 5

At KS4, PSHE becomes more explicitly connected to examined qualifications in some schools, though PSHE itself remains non-examined. Students may study:
- **GCSE Citizenship Studies** (AQA, Edexcel, OCR): Includes content on rights, laws, and social issues requiring distancing when discussing crime, discrimination, or political controversy
- **Religious Studies GCSE** (all exam boards): Ethical units on relationships, medical ethics, and social justice benefit from distancing techniques

Jonathan Charlesworth's guidance on teaching RSE at KS4 (Sex Education Forum, 2014) emphasises that older pupils still require distancing despite greater maturity, as sensitive topics remain potentially distressing or contentious. Techniques should evolve to include:
- Policy analysis and evaluation
- Ethical framework application
- Statistical analysis and research evaluation
- Case law examination (in citizenship contexts)

At Key Stage 5, A-Level qualifications may include:
- **A-Level Sociology** (AQA, OCR): Family, education, and crime units benefit from distancing through theoretical frameworks
- **A-Level Politics** (AQA, Edexcel, OCR): Rights-based content requiring balanced presentation
- **A-Level Religious Studies/Philosophy and Ethics** (all boards): Medical ethics, relationships ethics requiring case-based distance

## Creating Safe Learning Environments

### Establishing Ground Rules

Distancing techniques work most effectively within a framework of clearly established ground rules. The PSHE Association recommends co-creating class agreements that include:
- No personal questions directed at individuals
- Right to pass on activities without explanation
- Respect for different values and beliefs
- Clear distinction between facts, opinions, and values

Jenny Mosley's Golden Rules framework (Mosley, 1998) provides a whole-school approach that creates the conditions in which distancing techniques can operate effectively. Her emphasis on "we listen to people" and "we are kind and helpful" establishes the respectful climate necessary for sensitive discussions.

### Managing Inadvertent Disclosures

Despite distancing techniques, disclosures may still occur. Teachers require clear protocols:

1. **In-the-moment responses:** Acknowledge sensitively without probing, redirect to general discussion
2. **Post-lesson follow-up:** Speak privately with the pupil, follow safeguarding procedures as appropriate
3. **Documentation:** Record concerns following school safeguarding policy
4. **Staff support:** Ensure teachers have access to supervision or debrief opportunities

Nick Boddington's safeguarding guidance for PSHE teachers (PSHE Association, 2016) emphasises that distancing techniques significantly reduce inappropriate disclosures but teachers must remain vigilant and prepared.

### Differentiation and SEND Considerations

The PSHE Association's Planning Framework for Pupils with SEND (2018) recognises that distancing techniques require adaptation:

**For pupils with autism spectrum conditions:**
- Very concrete distancing mechanisms (clear fictional markers)
- Explicit explanation of the technique's purpose
- Visual supports distinguishing fiction from reality

**For pupils with social, emotional, and mental health needs:**
- Carefully calibrated distance—not so great that content seems irrelevant, not so close it triggers distress
- Opportunities for later one-to-one discussion if needed
- Clear signposting to support services

**For pupils with speech, language, and communication needs:**
- Visual distancing tools (picture cards, storyboards)
- Alternative response methods (writing, drawing, selecting from options)

## Addressing Controversial Issues

Lee Jerome's extensive research on teaching controversial issues (Jerome and Kisby, 2019; *Teaching Controversial Issues*, UCL IOE Press) demonstrates that distancing techniques are essential when PSHE overlaps with citizenship education on topics such as:

- Abortion and reproductive rights
- Same-sex relationships and gender identity
- Immigration and cultural diversity
- Political ideologies and activism
- Religious beliefs and practices

Jerome advocates for 'issues-based' approaches that:
1. Present multiple perspectives explicitly
2. Use structured protocols (e.g., 'Structured Controversy', 'Four Corners')
3. Distinguish between legal frameworks, rights, and personal beliefs
4. Examine real-world policy and evidence whilst maintaining critical distance

Bernard Crick's principle of "teaching about, not for" particular viewpoints (Crick Report, 1998) remains foundational—distancing enables this approach by preventing discussions from becoming personal testimonies or conversions.

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Lesson Planning

When planning lessons on sensitive topics:

1. **Identify potential sensitivities:** Consider diverse pupil experiences (family structures, trauma, cultural backgrounds)
2. **Select appropriate distancing technique(s):** Match technique to topic, age group, and learning objectives
3. **Prepare resources:** Create or source case studies, scenarios, data, or media materials
4. **Plan transitions:** Include clear introductions and conclusions that 'open' and 'close' sensitive discussions
5. **Prepare signposting:** Know what support services to reference (school counsellor, external organisations)

### Resource Development

High-quality distancing requires well-crafted resources:

**Case study criteria:**
- Realistic but clearly fictional
- Sufficient complexity for meaningful analysis
- Culturally sensitive and representative
- Age-appropriate language and scenarios
- Open-ended to enable discussion

**Recommended resource providers:**
- PSHE Association (comprehensive lesson planning frameworks)
- 1Decision, Jigsaw, Coram SCARF (commercial schemes with built-in distancing)
- NSPCC (safeguarding-focused scenarios)
- Childnet, UK Safer Internet Centre (online safety scenarios)
- Brook (relationships and sex education case studies)

### Assessment and Distancing

Assessment in PSHE should not require personal disclosure. Distancing techniques extend to assessment through:

- **Knowledge assessment:** Testing understanding of facts, rights, services (no personal application required)
- **Application tasks:** Applying understanding to hypothetical scenarios, not personal situations
- **Evaluation questions:** Analysing effectiveness of policies or support services generally
- **Case study responses:** Written analysis of provided scenarios

The PSHE Association's guidance *Assessment in PSHE Education* (2020) emphasises that "pupils should never be assessed on their personal views, attitudes, values or personal circumstances."

## Staff Training and Development

Effective use of distancing techniques requires professional development:

### Initial Training

- Understanding the rationale and ethics of distancing
- Practising specific techniques in safe training environments
- Recognising when distancing is insufficient and how to respond
- Understanding safeguarding intersections

### Ongoing Development

- Sharing effective practice across departments
- Reflecting on lessons where distancing was challenging
- Updating knowledge of current issues affecting young people
- Refreshing safeguarding knowledge

### Leadership Responsibilities

School leaders and PSHE coordinators should:
- Ensure all staff teaching PSHE understand distancing principles
- Provide appropriate resources that incorporate distancing
- Create protocols for managing disclosures
- Monitor that distancing techniques are consistently applied
- Ensure staff have access to supervision or debriefing

## Connections to Statutory Guidance

The DfE's *Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education* guidance (2019, updated 2020) states:

> "Pupils should be taught the facts and the law about sex, sexuality, sexual health and gender identity in an age-appropriate and inclusive way. All pupils should feel that the content is relevant to them and their developing sexuality. Sexual orientation and gender identity should be explored at a timely point and in a clear, sensitive and respectful manner."

Distancing techniques enable this inclusive, sensitive approach by:
- Presenting diverse relationships and identities through case studies and media analysis rather than assumptions about pupils' own identities
- Teaching "the law" through objective, distanced examination rather than moral directives
- Ensuring content is "relevant" without requiring personal identification

The guidance's emphasis on age-appropriateness aligns with developmentally appropriate distancing—greater concrete distance for younger pupils, more nuanced techniques for older students.

## Challenges and Limitations

### When Distancing May Be Insufficient

Distancing techniques, whilst essential, have limitations:

**Traumatised pupils:** For pupils with significant trauma histories, even distanced content may trigger distress. Schools should:
- Provide advance notice of sensitive topics (to parents and pupils)
- Offer alternative arrangements where necessary
- Ensure trauma-informed approaches throughout
- Have immediate access to pastoral support
