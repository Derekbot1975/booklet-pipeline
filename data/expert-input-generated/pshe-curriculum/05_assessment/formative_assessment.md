# Formative Assessment in PSHE

## Introduction

Formative assessment in Personal, Social, Health and Economic (PSHE) education presents unique challenges and opportunities that distinguish it from assessment in traditional academic subjects. Unlike subjects with clearly defined right or wrong answers, PSHE assessment must navigate the development of personal attributes, values, skills and knowledge whilst respecting pupils' privacy and personal circumstances. The PSHE Association emphasises that assessment in PSHE should focus on what pupils *know*, *understand* and *can do* rather than evaluating their personal choices, values or home circumstances.

## The Purpose and Nature of Formative Assessment in PSHE

### Distinctive Features of PSHE Assessment

Formative assessment in PSHE serves to support pupils' learning and progression without judging their personal beliefs, lifestimes or family backgrounds. As outlined in the PSHE Association's 'Assessment in PSHE Education' guidance (2019), effective formative assessment in PSHE should:

- Focus on knowledge acquisition and skill development
- Avoid making judgements about pupils' personal values or choices
- Respect confidentiality and safeguarding boundaries
- Recognise that progression may not be linear
- Acknowledge that pupils enter PSHE lessons with widely varying life experiences

Bernard Crick's influential 'Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools' (1998) established that citizenship education should develop knowledge, skills and values, whilst recognising that assessing values requires particular sensitivity. This principle extends throughout PSHE education.

### Assessment for Learning in PSHE

Black and Wiliam's seminal work on Assessment for Learning (AfL), further developed in 'Inside the Black Box' (1998), provides foundational principles applicable to PSHE. However, Lee Jerome and Jon Openshaw's research on citizenship education assessment (2019) highlights that AfL strategies must be adapted for PSHE contexts where:

- Success criteria may be individually variable
- Self-assessment requires particular emotional safety
- Peer assessment must be carefully scaffolded to avoid personal judgements
- Teacher feedback must balance challenge with pastoral sensitivity

## Key Principles of Formative Assessment in PSHE

### The Three Assessment Categories

The PSHE Association's framework identifies three assessable domains:

**1. Knowledge and Understanding**
- Factual information (e.g., understanding of the law, health information, economic concepts)
- Understanding of concepts (e.g., consent, democracy, mental health)
- Can be assessed through questioning, written work, quizzes and discussion

**2. Skills**
- Communication and interpersonal skills
- Critical thinking and decision-making
- Self-management and resilience
- Assessed through observation, role-play, problem-solving tasks and practical applications

**3. Personal Attributes**
- Empathy, respect, tolerance
- Self-awareness and reflection
- Can only be assessed through pupils' demonstrated understanding, not their personal characteristics

Nick Boddington's work on citizenship assessment emphasises that whilst we can assess pupils' *understanding* of values such as fairness and equality, we cannot assess whether they *are* fair or equal people.

## Formative Assessment Strategies for PSHE

### Baseline Assessment and Diagnostic Assessment

Establishing pupils' starting points in PSHE is essential but requires careful consideration:

**Anonymous baseline surveys**: Using tools such as:
- 'Think-Pair-Share' activities to gauge understanding
- Online anonymous polling (e.g., Mentimeter, Poll Everywhere)
- Question boxes allowing pupils to submit queries anonymously
- Pre-learning knowledge checks

Jonathan Charlesworth's research on PSHE curriculum design (2015) emphasises that baseline assessment must account for pupils' varying exposure to topics through lived experience, media and family discussion.

### Ongoing Formative Assessment Techniques

**1. Dialogue and Questioning**

Effective questioning in PSHE requires careful framing:
- Use hypothetical scenarios rather than personal questions ("What might someone do if..." rather than "What would you do...")
- Employ 'no hands up' strategies to ensure broad participation without forcing disclosure
- Use think-pair-share to allow processing time
- Implement 'pose, pause, pounce, bounce' questioning strategies

Jenny Mosley's Circle Time methodology provides structured approaches for inclusive dialogue where all voices can be heard safely.

**2. Self-Assessment and Reflection**

Self-assessment in PSHE should focus on learning objectives:
- Learning journals or reflection logs
- 'What I knew, what I now know, what I want to know' grids
- Skills self-audit checklists
- Goal-setting based on skill development

Lee Jerome's work on citizenship education (2012) highlights the importance of metacognitive reflection, where pupils consider *how* they learned rather than just *what* they learned.

**3. Peer Assessment**

Peer assessment requires careful scaffolding in PSHE:
- Establish clear ground rules about respectful feedback
- Focus feedback on demonstrated understanding, not personal opinions
- Use structured feedback frameworks (e.g., 'two stars and a wish')
- Ensure anonymity where appropriate (e.g., gallery critique activities)

### Low-Stakes Formative Tools

**Exit Tickets and Entrance Passes**
- Quick checks of understanding at lesson end
- Can be submitted anonymously
- Allow teachers to adjust subsequent lessons

**'Myths and Facts' Activities**
- Particularly valuable for Health Education topics
- Allow misconceptions to be identified and addressed
- Can be conducted anonymously to protect pupils

**Case Study Analysis**
- Pupils apply knowledge to hypothetical scenarios
- Demonstrates understanding without personal disclosure
- Allows for differentiated responses

**Skills Demonstration Tasks**
- Role-play scenarios for practicing skills
- Problem-solving activities
- Decision-making exercises using scenario cards

## Assessment Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 and 2

At primary level, formative assessment in PSHE often integrates with ongoing classroom practice:

- Observational assessment during structured activities
- Simple self-assessment (e.g., thumbs up/down, traffic light cards)
- 'Draw and write' activities to express understanding
- Circle time discussions following Jenny Mosley's framework
- Use of stories and puppets to discuss scenarios safely

The PSHE Association's Programme of Study for Key Stages 1-2 emphasises building foundational knowledge about relationships, health and the wider world, with assessment focusing on whether pupils can identify, describe and explain concepts.

### Key Stage 3

As outlined in the statutory Relationships and Health Education guidance (DfE, 2019), KS3 formative assessment should:

- Use more sophisticated self-assessment tools (skills audits, reflection frameworks)
- Employ debate and structured discussion for assessing understanding
- Incorporate project-based assessment (e.g., campaign planning, research presentations)
- Develop written responses to scenarios demonstrating knowledge and skills application

Lee Jerome and Liam Gearon's research (2019) on secondary citizenship education emphasises the importance of developing critical thinking skills that can be formatively assessed through analysis tasks.

### Key Stage 4

At KS4, formative assessment becomes more sophisticated:

- Extended written responses demonstrating depth of understanding
- Research projects requiring source evaluation
- Presentations and debates
- Portfolio development showing progression over time

For pupils studying PSHE-related qualifications (e.g., AQA's Level 1/2 Certificate in Personal and Social Development, OCR's Certificate in Understanding Personal Development, Health and Wellbeing), formative assessment must align with qualification specifications whilst maintaining PSHE's distinctive assessment principles.

### Key Stage 5

Post-16 PSHE often operates as a non-qualification programme but requires sophisticated assessment:

- Independent research and evaluation
- Critical analysis of contemporary issues
- Self-directed learning projects
- Skills application in realistic contexts

## Recording and Reporting Formative Assessment

### Recording Progress

Recording in PSHE must balance meaningful data with safeguarding and ethical considerations:

**What Should Be Recorded:**
- Evidence of knowledge acquisition
- Demonstration of skills development
- Engagement with learning objectives
- Progress against personal targets

**What Should NOT Be Recorded:**
- Personal disclosures
- Value judgements about pupils' lives
- Detailed personal information that could breach confidentiality

The PSHE Association recommends a 'best fit' approach, assessing whether pupils are working towards, meeting or exceeding age-related expectations for knowledge and skills.

### Reporting to Parents and Pupils

Nick Boddington's work on assessment emphasises that reporting should:
- Focus on curriculum coverage and skill development
- Avoid suggesting judgements about personal character
- Use phrases like "understands the importance of..." rather than "demonstrates..." when discussing values
- Highlight progress in knowledge and skills
- Identify next steps for development

## Challenges and Solutions in PSHE Formative Assessment

### Challenge 1: Balancing Assessment with Safeguarding

**The Issue**: PSHE lessons may prompt disclosures requiring safeguarding responses, but assessment should not pressure personal revelation.

**Solutions**:
- Clear ground rules distinguishing learning discussion from personal sharing
- Anonymous assessment tools where appropriate
- Alternative scenarios and hypothetical situations
- Designated alternative tasks for pupils who find topics sensitive

### Challenge 2: Measuring 'Soft Skills' and Personal Development

**The Issue**: Skills like empathy, resilience and communication are difficult to quantify.

**Solutions**:
- Use graduated descriptors (e.g., 'developing', 'secure', 'confident')
- Evidence through observable behaviours in structured activities
- Skills-based success criteria for specific tasks
- Pupil self-assessment against clear indicators

Jonathan Charlesworth's research (2020) emphasises using 'I can' statements that make abstract skills concrete and assessable.

### Challenge 3: Assessment Without Grades

**The Issue**: Many schools operate numerical or letter grading systems that don't translate well to PSHE.

**Solutions**:
- Criterion-referenced assessment against learning objectives
- Qualitative descriptors of progress
- Skills tracking across units
- Portfolio approaches showing development over time

### Challenge 4: Addressing Pupils' Diverse Starting Points

**The Issue**: Pupils enter PSHE with vastly different prior knowledge and life experiences.

**Solutions**:
- Differentiated success criteria
- Focus on individual progress rather than comparative achievement
- Recognition of different types of knowledge (academic and experiential)
- Flexible, responsive teaching informed by formative assessment

Lee Jerome's work on citizenship curriculum (2012) emphasises that diverse starting points should be seen as enriching rather than problematic, with formative assessment informing appropriate challenge for all.

## The Role of Formative Assessment in Curriculum Development

### Using Assessment to Inform Planning

Effective formative assessment creates a feedback loop:

1. **Pre-assessment** identifies gaps and misconceptions
2. **During-lesson assessment** allows responsive teaching
3. **Post-lesson assessment** evaluates effectiveness and informs next steps
4. **Unit-level assessment** shapes future curriculum delivery

The PSHE Association's Planning Framework emphasises that PSHE should be a spiral curriculum, with formative assessment identifying when to revisit concepts at greater depth.

### Whole-School Assessment Approaches

Nick Boddington and colleagues' work on citizenship education emphasises consistency across year groups:

- Shared understanding of progression in PSHE
- Common assessment language and frameworks
- Moderation activities to calibrate judgements
- Tracking systems that follow pupils across key stages

## Links to Examination Board Specifications

While PSHE itself is not typically examined at GCSE, related qualifications incorporate formative assessment principles:

**AQA Level 1/2 Certificate in Personal and Social Development**
- Internal assessment incorporating portfolio evidence
- Externally assessed examination
- Formative feedback informing final portfolio quality

**OCR Certificate in Understanding Personal Development, Health and Wellbeing**
- Centre-assessed tasks requiring evidence collection
- Formative opportunities through draft submissions
- Skills demonstration across real-life contexts

**WJEC Level 2 Health and Wellbeing Award**
- Controlled assessment tasks
- Skills application in practical contexts
- Formative feedback cycles built into specification

## Quality Assurance and Assessment Moderation

Jonathan Charlesworth's research on PSHE assessment quality emphasises the importance of:

- **Internal moderation**: PSHE teams reviewing assessment evidence together
- **Cross-school moderation**: Sharing practice with other schools
- **PSHE Association support**: Using national frameworks and guidance
- **Training**: Regular CPD on assessment approaches specific to PSHE

The PSHE Association offers moderation resources and networks supporting consistent, high-quality assessment practice.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Creating an Assessment-Capable Culture

1. **Share learning intentions clearly**: Pupils should understand what they're learning and why
2. **Develop success criteria collaboratively**: Involve pupils in defining what success looks like
3. **Normalise making mistakes**: PSHE requires exploring difficult questions without always having 'right' answers
4. **Build metacognitive skills**: Help pupils understand their own learning processes
5. **Use feedback effectively**: Ensure feedback is specific, actionable and focused on learning objectives

### Effective Feedback in PSHE

Feedback should:
- Be timely and specific to learning objectives
- Focus on knowledge demonstrated and skills applied
- Avoid personal judgements or assumptions
- Offer clear next steps for development
- Recognise effort and progress
- Use appropriate language that respects sensitivity of topics

Jenny Mosley's work emphasises the importance of positive reinforcement and recognition of all contributions within safe learning environments.

### Assessment and Differentiation

Formative assessment informs differentiation through:
- **Adjusted questioning** targeting different pupils' understanding levels
- **Tiered success criteria** allowing all pupils to achieve whilst being appropriately challenged
- **Varied recording methods** (written, verbal, visual, practical)
- **Flexible groupings** based on formative assessment insights
- **Additional support** for pupils needing it

## Conclusion

Formative assessment in PSHE requires sophisticated professional judgement, balancing the need to assess progress with respect for pupils' personal circumstances and safeguarding requirements. When implemented effectively, it enhances learning, informs teaching and enables pupils to develop the knowledge, skills and attributes they need to navigate their lives successfully.

The distinctive nature of PSHE means that assessment must focus on curriculum-based learning objectives rather than personal characteristics. As Lee Jerome argues, effective PSHE assessment makes visible the learning that occurs in this vital but often undervalued curriculum area, whilst the PSHE Association's frameworks provide practical tools enabling this approach.

By employing varied formative assessment strategies, maintaining ethical boundaries, and focusing on progression in knowledge and skills, teachers can ensure that assessment in PSHE genuinely serves learning whilst respecting the unique nature of this essential curriculum area.
