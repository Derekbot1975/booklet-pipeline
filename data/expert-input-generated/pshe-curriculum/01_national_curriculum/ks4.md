# PSHE at Key Stage 4

## Overview and Statutory Context

Personal, Social, Health and Economic (PSHE) education at Key Stage 4 encompasses students aged 14-16 (Years 10 and 11). As of September 2020, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education became statutory in secondary schools in England under the Children and Social Work Act 2017. Whilst the broader PSHE curriculum remains non-statutory, the RSE and Health Education components within it are now legally required.

The Department for Education's statutory guidance 'Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education' (DfE, 2019) provides the framework within which schools must operate. This guidance emphasises that PSHE education should be developmentally appropriate, building on what has been taught at Key Stage 3, and preparing students for adult life.

At Key Stage 4, students are approaching significant life transitions, including potential entry into further education, training, or employment. The curriculum must therefore address increasingly sophisticated themes whilst supporting students to develop critical thinking skills and personal agency.

## Core Curriculum Framework

### Health and Wellbeing

The statutory Health Education requirements at Key Stage 4 build substantially on earlier key stages. Schools must teach about:

**Mental Health**: Students should understand the full spectrum of mental health, recognising that mental health difficulties are common but can be prevented and overcome. The curriculum should address stress, anxiety, depression, self-harm, and suicide prevention. Research by Patalay and Fitzsimons (2018) in the 'Millennium Cohort Study' indicates that mental health difficulties affect approximately one in four 14-year-olds, making this content critical.

**Internet Safety and Harms**: Students must learn to evaluate online content critically, understand how algorithms shape their experiences, recognise extreme content and radicalisation risks, and manage screen time. The PSHE Association's 'Teacher Guidance: Preparing to teach about mental health and emotional wellbeing' (2015, updated 2021) provides evidence-based approaches.

**Physical Health and Fitness**: This includes detailed learning about diet, exercise, sleep, dental health, and the links between physical and mental health. Students should understand how lifestyle choices affect long-term health outcomes.

**Drugs, Alcohol and Tobacco**: At Key Stage 4, teaching must address the facts about legal and illegal substances, including their effects, risks of dependency, and the law. Nick Boddington's work emphasises the importance of moving beyond 'shock tactics' to develop genuine critical thinking about substance use decisions.

### Relationships and Sex Education (Statutory)

The statutory RSE guidance requires comprehensive teaching at Key Stage 4:

**Intimate Relationships**: Students must learn about different types of intimate relationships, including marriage, civil partnerships, and cohabitation. The guidance explicitly states that teaching should be "fully integrated into programmes of study" and that "all pupils should receive teaching on LGBTQ+ content" (DfE, 2019).

**Sexual Health**: This includes contraception in detail, STI prevention, accessing sexual health services, and understanding consent within the law. The guidance emphasises that students should know "the legal rights and responsibilities regarding equality with particular reference to the protected characteristics" defined in the Equality Act 2010.

**Consent**: Bernard Crick's emphasis on active citizenship connects strongly here – students must understand consent as an ongoing, freely given agreement, and recognise situations where consent cannot be given. The 2003 Sexual Offences Act provides the legal framework.

**Healthy and Unhealthy Relationships**: Teaching must address power dynamics, coercion, control, and domestic abuse, including online contexts. Jenny Mosley's Circle Time approaches can provide safe spaces for discussing these sensitive topics.

### Economic Wellbeing and Financial Capability

Financial education at Key Stage 4 prepares students for economic independence:

**Personal Finance**: Students should understand budgeting, banking products, savings and investments, credit and debt, insurance, and taxation. The MyBnk charity's research (2019) found that only 35% of teenagers felt confident managing money, indicating this need.

**Employment Rights and Responsibilities**: This includes understanding employment contracts, pay, workplace safety, discrimination law, and trade unions. Lee Jerome's work on 'Education for Employability' (Jerome and Kisby, 2021) argues for critical approaches that examine power relations in the workplace rather than merely preparing compliant workers.

**Financial Decision-Making**: Students should evaluate financial products, understand gambling risks, recognise financial scams, and plan for major expenditures. The Money and Pensions Service provides teaching resources aligned with their 'UK Strategy for Financial Wellbeing' (2020-2030).

### Living in the Wider World

**Careers Education**: The Gatsby Benchmarks (2014, revised 2018) provide the framework for careers guidance at Key Stage 4. Students should receive encounters with employers, experience workplaces, and access to personal guidance. Jonathan Charlesworth's work emphasises integration of careers across the curriculum rather than standalone provision.

**Digital Literacy**: Students must develop sophisticated understanding of online identity, digital footprints, data protection (GDPR), and how businesses use personal data. This connects to citizenship education about rights and responsibilities.

**Community Engagement**: Building on Bernard Crick's 'Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools' (1998), students should understand how to effect change through democratic processes, volunteering, and social action.

## Citizenship Education at Key Stage 4

Citizenship remains a statutory National Curriculum subject at Key Stage 4 in England. The Citizenship programmes of study (DfE, 2013) specify that students should be taught about:

**Democracy and Government**: The UK's parliamentary system, devolved administrations, local government, and the electoral system. Students should understand diverse forms of government and participation beyond voting.

**Law and Justice**: The development of law, the justice system, and the universal declaration of human rights. Lee Jerome and Liam Gearon's work 'What is citizenship education about?' (2021) emphasises critical examination of how laws affect different groups.

**Rights and Responsibilities**: Human rights, children's rights (UNCRC), consumer rights, and the Equality Act 2010. Students should understand both the freedoms these provide and the responsibilities that accompany them.

**Identity and Diversity**: Multiple identities, British values, migration, and community cohesion. The Ajegbo Report (2007) on diversity and citizenship remains influential in emphasising critical approach to these concepts.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Active and Participatory Learning

Research consistently demonstrates that didactic approaches are ineffective for PSHE and Citizenship. The PSHE Association's 'Programme of Study for PSHE Education (KS3-4)' (2020) recommends:

**Discussion and Debate**: Structured controversial issues discussions using frameworks like 'Philosophy for Children' or 'Structured Discussion' models allow students to examine multiple perspectives safely.

**Scenario-Based Learning**: Case studies and scenarios help students apply knowledge to realistic situations. This is particularly effective for relationships education and financial decision-making.

**Peer Education**: Older students can effectively deliver some PSHE content to younger peers, reinforcing their own learning whilst making content more relatable.

**Community Links**: Guest speakers, workplace visits, and community projects make learning relevant and develop employability skills.

Jenny Mosley's Quality Circle Time approaches, whilst originally designed for primary, can be adapted for Key Stage 4 through student-led circles and restorative practices that develop speaking and listening skills in safe environments.

## Assessment and Progression

PSHE education does not have National Curriculum levels or GCSE qualifications in most schools, though some exam boards offer qualifications:

### Available Qualifications

**AQA**: Offers Unit Award Scheme (UAS) for PSHE, allowing flexible certification of specific learning outcomes.

**WJEC**: Previously offered the Level 1/2 Award in Personal and Social Education (PSE) though this has been discontinued.

**OCR**: Provides the Cambridge National in Health and Social Care (Level 1/2) which overlaps substantially with PSHE content.

**ASDAN**: Offers the Certificate of Personal Effectiveness (CoPE) at Levels 1 and 2, widely used for assessing personal development.

### Assessment for Learning

The PSHE Association emphasises assessment of learning (evidencing what students know and can do) rather than summative grading. Effective approaches include:

**Baseline and End-of-Unit Assessments**: Anonymous self-assessments allow teachers to gauge knowledge and adjust teaching without causing student anxiety about 'wrong' answers on sensitive topics.

**Portfolio Approaches**: Students compile evidence of learning including reflections, action plans, and records of participation in activities.

**Self and Peer Assessment**: Students evaluate their own development against clear criteria, developing metacognitive skills.

**Skills Progression**: Assessing skills such as critical thinking, decision-making, empathy, and communication provides evidence of development beyond factual knowledge.

## Safeguarding and Sensitive Content

Teaching PSHE at Key Stage 4 requires careful attention to safeguarding:

### Ground Rules and Safe Spaces

The PSHE Association recommends establishing clear ground rules for discussions, including distancing techniques (discussing scenarios rather than personal experiences), confidentiality parameters, and options to not participate in particular activities.

### Distancing Techniques

Rather than asking students to share personal experiences, use case studies, scenarios, and third-person examples. This protects students whilst allowing meaningful engagement with content.

### Disclosure Management

Teachers must be prepared for disclosures that arise from PSHE teaching. All staff teaching PSHE should understand the school's safeguarding policy and procedures. The NSPCC guidance 'Managing allegations of abuse against teachers and other staff' provides relevant frameworks.

### Parental Rights and Consultation

Parents have the right to withdraw students from sex education components that fall outside the statutory science curriculum until three terms before the student turns 16. Schools must document their RSE policy and consult parents. The DfE guidance (2019) specifies that schools should work with parents with the aim of building understanding, but headteachers may refuse withdrawal requests if not in the student's best interests.

## Addressing Contemporary Issues

### Online Safety and Digital Wellbeing

The rapid evolution of technology presents ongoing challenges. At Key Stage 4, students need sophisticated understanding of:

- **Social Media Algorithms**: How content is curated and the psychological techniques used to increase engagement
- **Online Relationships**: Including meeting people online, 'catfishing', and online grooming
- **Image Sharing**: The legal implications of sharing intimate images (including 'sexting' under 18)
- **Online Gaming**: Including in-game purchases, gambling-like mechanics, and voice-chat risks

The UK Council for Internet Safety (UKCIS) provides the 'Education for a Connected World' framework (2020) which outlines progression in eight strands of digital literacy.

### Mental Health and Emotional Wellbeing

Research by the Children's Society ('The Good Childhood Report', 2021) indicates declining wellbeing among teenagers, particularly girls. Key Stage 4 PSHE must:

- Normalise discussion of mental health
- Teach practical strategies for managing stress and anxiety
- Ensure students know how to access support both within school and externally (including CAMHS, Young Minds, Childline)
- Address exam stress and academic pressure explicitly
- Challenge stigma and discrimination around mental health

The Anna Freud Centre's 'Mentally Healthy Schools' resources provide evidence-based approaches structured around whole-school frameworks.

### Radicalisation and Extremism

The Prevent Duty (2015) requires schools to protect students from radicalisation. PSHE and Citizenship education contribute by:

- Teaching critical evaluation of online content and sources
- Developing understanding of diverse perspectives and communities
- Building resilience to extremist narratives
- Creating space for discussing controversial issues safely

Nick Boddington's work emphasises that effective prevention comes through developing critical thinking rather than surveillance approaches.

## Curriculum Planning and Delivery Models

### Timetabling Approaches

Schools use various models for delivering PSHE at Key Stage 4:

**Discrete Lessons**: Weekly lessons allow systematic progression and build teacher-student relationships crucial for sensitive topics. The PSHE Association recommends minimum 45 minutes per week.

**Collapsed Timetable Days**: Intensive themed days can facilitate external speakers and deeper exploration but may lack continuity and can be difficult for students who find certain topics challenging.

**Tutor Time**: Brief regular sessions allow responsive teaching but may lack depth and suffer from inconsistent delivery quality across tutors.

**Integrated Curriculum**: Delivering PSHE through other subjects ensures curriculum time but risks fragmenting the coherent programme students need. Works best when mapped systematically with discrete PSHE complementing subject integration.

Research by Formby and Wolstenholme (2012) indicates discrete timetabled PSHE with specialist teachers produces better outcomes than other models, though resource constraints mean many schools use mixed approaches.

### Staffing and Professional Development

The PSHE Association's research (2020) found that only 48% of secondary teachers teaching PSHE had received training in the previous two years. Effective provision requires:

**Specialist Coordinators**: A lead teacher with allocated time for curriculum development, resource selection, and staff support.

**Whole-Staff Training**: All staff teaching PSHE need regular training in pedagogy, safeguarding, and content updates. This is particularly crucial for statutory RSE.

**Subject Knowledge Development**: Teachers need confidence with content knowledge, particularly for financial education, mental health, and sexual health where many feel under-prepared.

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on professional development emphasises the need for ongoing subject-specific pedagogy training rather than one-off awareness sessions.

## Inspections and Accountability

### Ofsted Framework

The Education Inspection Framework (2019, updated 2021) considers PSHE under the 'Personal Development' judgement. Inspectors examine:

- Quality of the PSHE curriculum and how it meets statutory requirements
- How the curriculum supports students' development and preparation for adult life
- How the school promotes equality and diversity
- The effectiveness of careers guidance (against Gatsby Benchmarks)
- Students' understanding of healthy relationships and their own wellbeing

Ofsted's 'Review of sexual abuse in schools and colleges' (2021) emphasised that RSE must address sexual harassment, online abuse, and harmful sexual behaviours explicitly.

### Quality Assurance

The PSHE Association provides a Quality Assurance framework with self-assessment tools helping schools evaluate and improve provision. Key indicators include:

- A planned, progressive curriculum with clear learning objectives
- Assessment processes that inform teaching and evidence learning
- Appropriate teacher training and subject leadership
- Safe learning environments with clear ground rules
- Effective partnerships with parents and external agencies

## Cross-Curricular Links

PSHE at Key Stage 4 connects significantly with other subjects:

**Science**: Biology curriculum includes puberty, reproduction, STIs, and drugs, which PSHE builds upon with decision-making and relationships contexts.

**PE**: Physical and mental health links, plus team-building and personal challenge connecting to wellbeing.

**English**: Discussion, debate, and textual analysis skills; literature often explores relationships, identity, and social issues.

**Mathematics**: Financial calculations, data interpretation for health statistics, probability for understanding risk.

**Computing**: Online safety, data protection, and critical evaluation of digital sources.

**History and Geography**: Citizenship topics including rights, justice, migration, and international development.

Lee Jerome's work on 'curriculum coherence' emphasises that planned cross-curricular approaches strengthen learning when subjects explicitly reference each other's contributions.

## Supporting Vulnerable Groups

PSHE must be accessible and relevant to all students:

### SEND Students

Adaptations should focus on:
- Concrete examples and visual supports
- Additional time for processing
- Modified language without oversimplifying content
- Individual risk assessments for topics like internet safety where vulnerability may be increased

The DfE guidance (2019) states that RSE "must be accessible for all pupils" including those with SEND, requiring adjustments to make content accessible whilst covering the same content areas.

### LGBTQ+ Students

The Stonewall 'School Report' (2017) found 45% of LGBTQ+ students experienced bullying. PSHE should:
- Integrate LGBTQ+ content throughout, not as an 'add-on'
- Use inclusive language and examples
- Address specific health concerns (e.g., sexual health resources)
- Challenge discrimination and promote understanding

### Students from Faith Backgrounds

Schools must balance statutory requirements with respecting religious perspectives. The DfE guidance (2019) states schools should teach "the facts" about different types of relationships and sexual health whilst making "clear that the school's teaching approach respects different religious viewpoints."

**Education and Training Foundation**: Offers professional development specifically for PSHE and RSE teaching.

**Young Citizens**: Practical citizenship resources including the 'Make a Difference Challenge' and 'Law Makers' programmes.
