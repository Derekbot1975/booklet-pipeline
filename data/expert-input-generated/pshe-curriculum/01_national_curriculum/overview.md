# PSHE and Citizenship Overview

## Introduction to PSHE and Citizenship Education

Personal, Social, Health and Economic (PSHE) education and Citizenship represent distinct but complementary areas of the UK curriculum designed to prepare young people for life in modern Britain. PSHE education encompasses learning about physical and mental health, relationships, and economic wellbeing, whilst Citizenship education focuses on political literacy, community engagement, and understanding of democratic institutions.

Since September 2020, Relationships Education (primary), Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) (secondary), and Health Education (all schools) became statutory in England under the Children and Social Work Act 2017. Citizenship has been a statutory National Curriculum subject at Key Stages 3 and 4 since 2002, following the landmark Crick Report (1998).

## Historical Context and Development

### The Crick Report (1998)

Professor Bernard Crick chaired the Advisory Group on Citizenship, producing the seminal report 'Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools' (QCA, 1998). Crick argued that citizenship education should encompass three interconnected strands:

- **Social and moral responsibility**: understanding self and others, leading to engagement in community life
- **Community involvement**: learning through active participation in school and community
- **Political literacy**: acquiring knowledge, skills and values relevant to democratic participation

Crick's vision emphasised 'active citizenship' rather than passive knowledge acquisition, fundamentally shaping the statutory Citizenship curriculum introduced in 2002.

### Evolution of PSHE Education

PSHE education has a less prescriptive statutory history. The subject developed from various curricula initiatives including health education, careers guidance, and personal development programmes. Jenny Mosley's 'Circle Time' methodology, introduced in the 1990s, significantly influenced primary PSHE practice, promoting emotional literacy and a whole-school approach to personal development (Mosley, 1996).

The PSHE Association, established in 1988 (originally as the Sex Education Forum), has provided professional leadership and guidance. The Association's Programme of Study (regularly updated) serves as the de facto framework for non-statutory PSHE content.

## Current Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Primary)

**Statutory Requirements:**
- **Relationships Education** (all primary schools from September 2020)
- **Health Education** (all primary schools from September 2020)
- **Citizenship** (non-statutory at primary, though elements appear in the National Curriculum for other subjects)

The DfE guidance 'Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education' (2019, updated 2021) specifies that primary pupils should learn about:

**Relationships Education:**
- Families and people who care for me
- Caring friendships
- Respectful relationships
- Online relationships
- Being safe

**Health Education:**
- Mental wellbeing
- Internet safety and harms
- Physical health and fitness
- Healthy eating
- Drugs, alcohol and tobacco
- Health and prevention
- Basic first aid
- Changing adolescent body

### Key Stage 3 and 4 (Secondary)

**Statutory Requirements:**
- **Relationships and Sex Education** (all secondary schools from September 2020)
- **Health Education** (all secondary schools from September 2020)
- **Citizenship** (National Curriculum statutory subject at KS3 and KS4)

#### Citizenship National Curriculum (2014)

The National Curriculum for Citizenship in England specifies that pupils should be taught about:

**Key Stage 3:**
- Parliamentary democracy and the key elements of the UK constitution
- The work of government, parliament, and the courts
- Local, regional and international governance
- The operation of diverse national, regional, religious and ethnic identities in the UK
- Human rights and international law
- Community action, volunteering and responsible participation

**Key Stage 4:**
- Parliamentary democracy, political parties and pressure groups
- The judiciary, law and justice systems
- Local and devolved government
- The UK's relations with the EU, Commonwealth and UN
- Income and expenditure, credit and debt, insurance, savings and pensions
- Rights and responsibilities of consumers, employers and employees

Nick Boddington, former head of Citizenship at QCA, emphasised that effective Citizenship education requires both knowledge acquisition and active participation, arguing against a purely academic approach (Boddington, 2011).

## The PSHE Association Programme of Study

Whilst PSHE remains largely non-statutory (except RSE and Health Education), the PSHE Association's Programme of Study provides comprehensive guidance organised around three core themes:

### Core Theme 1: Health and Wellbeing
- Mental health and emotional wellbeing
- Physical health, including diet, exercise, and personal hygiene
- Puberty and changing bodies
- Drugs, alcohol and tobacco education
- Managing risk and personal safety
- Keeping safe in different contexts

### Core Theme 2: Relationships
- Healthy relationships, including friendships and family
- Relationship values and consent
- Understanding and managing different feelings
- Recognising and responding to bullying
- Online and media literacy
- Intimate relationships (age-appropriate, including statutory RSE)

### Core Theme 3: Living in the Wider World
- Economic wellbeing and financial capability
- Careers education and enterprise
- British values and identity
- Critical thinking and media literacy
- Understanding democratic institutions
- Sustainable development and environmental responsibility

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Jenny Mosley's Circle Time

Jenny Mosley's 'Quality Circle Time' methodology remains influential in primary PSHE practice. This structured approach creates a safe space for discussion, emphasising:

- Speaking and listening skills
- Democratic participation through a 'talking object'
- Promoting self-esteem and positive behaviour
- Whole-school ethos development

Mosley's work connects explicitly with PSHE's aims of developing confident, articulate pupils capable of discussing sensitive issues respectfully (Mosley, 2005).

### Active Citizenship Pedagogy

Lee Jerome, Professor of Curriculum, Pedagogy and Society at Middlesex University, has extensively researched Citizenship pedagogy. Jerome and Clemitshaw (2012) argue that effective Citizenship teaching requires:

- **Issue-based enquiry**: exploring controversial issues through structured investigation
- **Pedagogical neutrality**: teachers facilitating balanced discussion rather than promoting particular viewpoints
- **Active participation**: genuine engagement with democratic processes in school and community
- **Critical thinking**: developing analytical skills to evaluate political arguments and media

Jerome's research emphasises that simply teaching about democracy is insufficient; pupils must experience democratic participation to develop genuine political literacy.

### Handling Controversial Issues

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on teaching controversial issues provides crucial guidance for PSHE and Citizenship educators. Charlesworth (2015) identifies several pedagogical approaches:

1. **Balanced approach**: presenting multiple perspectives without teacher advocacy
2. **Committed approach**: teacher shares their view after exploring alternatives
3. **Procedural neutrality**: teacher facilitates discussion without revealing personal position
4. **Devil's advocate**: teacher deliberately challenges consensus

Charlesworth recommends 'procedural neutrality' for most controversial topics, enabling pupils to develop independent judgement whilst ensuring the classroom remains a safe space for diverse opinions.

## Assessment in PSHE and Citizenship

### Non-Examination Assessment

Primary and Key Stage 3 PSHE/Citizenship typically employ formative assessment approaches:

- **Self-assessment**: pupil reflection on personal development
- **Peer assessment**: collaborative evaluation of group work and discussions
- **Portfolio evidence**: documented participation in community action
- **Teacher observation**: monitoring engagement and skill development

The PSHE Association recommends against grading PSHE, arguing that numerical marks may inhibit honest discussion of sensitive issues. Instead, schools should assess progress through descriptive feedback and evidence of skill development.

### GCSE Citizenship Studies

Three exam boards offer GCSE Citizenship Studies (for Key Stage 4):

#### AQA GCSE Citizenship Studies (8100)
**Content:**
- Theme A: Life in modern Britain (democracy, identity, rights)
- Theme B: Rights and responsibilities (law, justice, participation)
- Theme C: Politics and participation (government, elections, advocacy)
- Active Citizenship component (practical engagement project)

**Assessment:**
- Paper 1 (50%): Written examination covering Themes A and B
- Paper 2 (50%): Written examination covering Theme C plus active citizenship investigation

#### Edexcel GCSE Citizenship Studies (1CS0)
**Content:**
- Theme A: Living together in the UK
- Theme B: Democracy at work in the UK
- Theme C: Law and justice
- Theme D: Power and influence
- Citizenship Action component

**Assessment:**
- Paper 1 (50%): Citizenship in action
- Paper 2 (50%): Citizenship in context

#### OCR GCSE Citizenship Studies (J270)
**Content:**
- Citizenship in the UK
- Citizenship in context
- Rights and responsibilities

**Assessment:**
- Component 01 (50%): Citizenship in action
- Component 02 (50%): Our rights, our society

All specifications require pupils to undertake practical citizenship action, reflecting Crick's emphasis on active participation alongside knowledge acquisition.

## Curriculum Planning and Delivery Models

### Discrete Lessons vs Cross-Curricular Approaches

Schools typically adopt one of several delivery models:

**Discrete PSHE/Citizenship lessons:**
- Dedicated curriculum time (typically one lesson per week or fortnightly)
- Specialist teaching by trained staff
- Ensures systematic coverage of content
- Challenges: curriculum time pressure, perceived low status

**Cross-curricular integration:**
- PSHE/Citizenship themes embedded across subjects
- Natural connections (e.g., History teaching democratic development)
- Requires strong coordination and subject knowledge
- Risk of fragmented, incomplete coverage

**Collapsed timetable days:**
- Intensive focus through dedicated days or weeks
- Enables external speakers and extended activities
- Challenges: retention, lack of progression, planning demands

The PSHE Association advocates for regular, timetabled PSHE lessons complemented by cross-curricular reinforcement, arguing that 'drop-down days' alone cannot ensure systematic skill and knowledge development.

### Spiral Curriculum Design

PSHE and Citizenship curricula typically employ spiral design, revisiting themes with increasing depth and complexity. For example, online safety appears throughout:

- **KS1**: Recognising that people may be unkind online
- **KS2**: Understanding personal information risks and reporting concerns
- **KS3**: Analysing reliability of online information and understanding digital footprints
- **KS4**: Evaluating media influence, understanding data privacy and digital rights

This progression ensures age-appropriate content whilst building cumulative understanding.

## Safeguarding and Statutory Duties

PSHE and Citizenship education intersect significantly with schools' safeguarding responsibilities under the Children Act 1989, Education Act 2002, and 'Keeping Children Safe in Education' (DfE, annually updated).

**Key considerations:**

- **Disclosure protocols**: Staff must understand how to respond if pupils disclose abuse or harmful situations
- **Confidentiality boundaries**: Pupils should understand that teachers cannot guarantee absolute confidentiality when safety is at risk
- **External visitors**: All speakers must be vetted and supervised per safeguarding policies
- **Age-appropriate content**: Material must match pupils' developmental stage
- **Parental engagement**: Maintaining communication whilst respecting statutory requirements (note: parents cannot withdraw from Relationships Education, Health Education, or Citizenship, but may request withdrawal from non-statutory Sex Education components)

The PSHE Association's 'Creating a Safe Learning Environment' guidance (2020) provides detailed protocols for managing these responsibilities.

## The Right to Withdraw

Under the Education Act 1996 and updated by the 2020 regulations, parents retain limited withdrawal rights:

- **Cannot withdraw** from: Relationships Education, Health Education, Citizenship, or National Curriculum Science (which includes biological aspects of human reproduction)
- **Can withdraw** from: Sex Education beyond the statutory requirements, up until three terms before the child turns 16

Schools must clearly communicate what is statutory, provide consultation opportunities, and maintain dialogue with parents requesting withdrawal, emphasising the benefits of participation.

## Teacher Subject Knowledge and Training

### Professional Development Requirements

Effective PSHE and Citizenship teaching requires specific subject knowledge that may not feature in initial teacher training. The PSHE Association recommends:

- Understanding developmental stages and age-appropriate content
- Safeguarding awareness and disclosure procedures
- Facilitation skills for sensitive discussions
- Knowledge of relevant law, policy and guidance
- Ability to challenge stereotypes and prejudice
- Confidence in addressing diverse family structures and identities

### Subject Specialist Qualifications

Several organisations offer PSHE/Citizenship specialist qualifications:

- **PSHE Association Training Programmes**: Certificate and Diploma levels for subject leaders
- **UCL Institute of Education**: MA Citizenship and Human Rights Education
- **Canterbury Christ Church University**: MA Citizenship Education
- **Association for Citizenship Teaching**: Various CPD modules and conferences

Research by Formby (2011) demonstrates that specialist training significantly improves teacher confidence and the quality of PSHE/RSE delivery, particularly for sensitive topics.

## Current Debates and Challenges

### Status and Curriculum Time

Both PSHE and Citizenship face challenges regarding perceived status within schools. Despite statutory Citizenship and partially statutory PSHE, these subjects often receive limited curriculum time and may be delivered by non-specialists. The All-Party Parliamentary Group on PSHE Education has repeatedly called for fully statutory, Ofsted-inspected PSHE taught by trained specialists.

### Political Neutrality and British Values

Since the Counter-Terrorism and Security Act 2015 introduced the Prevent duty, schools must promote 'fundamental British values' (democracy, rule of law, individual liberty, mutual respect and tolerance). Jerome and Clemitshaw (2012) argue that this risks instrumentalising Citizenship education for government policy rather than developing genuine critical thinking. The tension between promoting specific values and maintaining pedagogical neutrality continues to generate debate.

### Inclusion and Diversity

The statutory inclusion of diverse families in Relationships Education has proven contentious in some communities. The DfE guidance (2019) explicitly requires teaching about LGBT+ relationships and families. Research by Formby (2015) demonstrates that inclusive education reduces bullying and supports LGBT+ pupils' wellbeing, though implementation requires careful planning and community engagement.

### Mental Health Education

With rising adolescent mental health concerns, Health Education's statutory inclusion of mental wellbeing represents a significant development. However, teachers report concerns about their preparation to handle disclosures and complex mental health issues (Spratt et al., 2006). The distinction between education (appropriate for teachers) and intervention (requiring specialist support) requires clear understanding.

## Quality Assurance and Inspection

### Ofsted Framework

Under the Education Inspection Framework (2019, updated 2021), Ofsted inspects PSHE as part of the 'Personal Development' judgement. Inspectors evaluate:

- Curriculum design and implementation
- How schools develop responsible, respectful and active citizens
- Promotion of equality and diversity
- Preparation for adult life in modern Britain

Citizenship, as a National Curriculum subject, is also evaluated under 'Quality of Education'. Inspectors assess curriculum intent, implementation and impact, examining both knowledge acquisition and skill development.

### PSHE Association Quality Mark

The PSHE Association offers a Subject Award recognising high-quality PSHE provision. Schools undergo external assessment against criteria including:

- Senior leadership commitment
- Comprehensive, progressive curriculum
- Specialist staff training
- Effective assessment approaches
- Safeguarding integration
- Pupil voice and evaluation

This voluntary accreditation provides external validation of PSHE quality and supports school improvement.

## International Perspectives and Comparison

The UK approach to citizenship and personal development education reflects specific historical and political contexts. Comparative research by Kerr et al. (2010) in the International Citizenship and Civic Education Study (ICCS) demonstrates varied international approaches:

- **Minimalist**: Basic civics knowledge about institutions
- **Communitarian**: Emphasis on shared values and social cohesion  
- **Maximalist**: Critical pedagogy, social justice, activism

The UK model, influenced by Crick's vision, sits toward the maximalist end, emphasising active participation and critical thinking alongside knowledge. This contrasts with more knowledge-focused approaches in some European countries.

## Practical Implications for School Leaders

### Strategic Implementation

Successful PSHE and Citizenship provision requires strategic planning:

1. **Leadership commitment**: Senior leader responsibility and governor oversight
2. **Curriculum mapping**: Systematic coverage across key stages with clear progression
3. **Staffing**: Designation of specialist coordinators with appropriate training
4. **Resources**: Investment in quality materials and external partnerships
5. **Assessment systems**: Meaningful progress tracking without inappropriate grading
6. **Parental engagement**: Proactive communication and consultation
7. **Evaluation**: Regular review using pupil feedback and outcome measures

### Whole-School Approaches

Both Jenny Mosley and Bernard Crick emphasised that effective PSHE and Citizenship extend beyond discrete lessons. Schools should consider:

- **Ethos and environment**: Visible commitment to values taught
- **Student voice**: Genuine opportunities for democratic participation (school councils, pupil leadership)
- **Community links**: Partnerships with local organisations and civic institutions
- **Extra-curricular activities**: Volunteering, campaigns, debates
- **Staff modelling**: Adults demonstrating respectful relationships and community
