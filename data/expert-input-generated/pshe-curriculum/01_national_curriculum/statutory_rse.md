# Statutory RSE and Health Education

## Overview and Legislative Framework

Since September 2020, Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE), and Health Education became statutory in all schools in England under the Children and Social Work Act 2017. This landmark legislation, implemented through the Department for Education's statutory guidance "Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education" (DfE, 2019, updated 2021), represents the most significant reform of PSHE provision in a generation.

### Legislative Background

The statutory status emerged from concerns highlighted in educational research and parliamentary reviews. Nick Boddington's work on PSHE curriculum development emphasised the critical need for consistency and quality assurance in relationship and health education. The Women and Equalities Select Committee report (2016) on sexual harassment and violence in schools provided compelling evidence that inadequate RSE contributed to normalisation of harmful behaviours.

The statutory guidance applies to all maintained schools, academies, and free schools, though independent schools must meet the Independent School Standards which include similar requirements. Bernard Crick's foundational work on citizenship education informed the interconnected nature of these subjects, emphasising that personal development and social responsibility are inseparable.

## Statutory Requirements by Phase

### Primary: Relationships Education and Health Education

All primary schools must deliver Relationships Education and Health Education. Sex education is not compulsory at primary level (except for content in the National Curriculum for Science), though the DfE guidance recommends that schools prepare pupils for puberty.

**Relationships Education must cover:**
- Families and people who care for me
- Caring friendships
- Respectful relationships
- Online relationships
- Being safe

**Health Education must cover:**
- Mental wellbeing
- Internet safety and harms
- Physical health and fitness
- Healthy eating
- Drugs, alcohol and tobacco
- Health and prevention
- Basic first aid
- Changing adolescent body

Jenny Mosley's Circle Time methodology has proven particularly effective for delivering primary Relationships Education, creating safe spaces for discussing feelings, friendships, and family relationships.

### Secondary: RSE and Health Education

Secondary schools must deliver RSE (not just Relationships Education) and Health Education to all pupils. This includes all secondary academies, including those previously exempt from teaching Sex Education under their funding agreements pre-2020.

**Additional RSE content beyond Relationships Education includes:**
- Intimate and sexual relationships, including sexual health
- Consent, including the legal framework
- Contraception and reproductive health
- How to access sexual health services
- Understanding of menstrual wellbeing

### Key Stage-Specific Considerations

**Key Stage 1 and 2:** Focus on building vocabulary, understanding family structures, recognising healthy relationships, and understanding bodily autonomy. The NSPCC's Pants rule and concepts of privacy align well with statutory requirements.

**Key Stage 3:** Introduction of content on consent, healthy sexual relationships (within age-appropriate contexts), contraception basics, and sexual health. Lee Jerome's research on controversial issues pedagogy is particularly relevant here, as teachers navigate sensitive discussions.

**Key Stage 4:** More detailed coverage of sexual health, contraception, STIs, consent law, sexual orientation, gender identity, and how to access services. Content must be age-appropriate while ensuring pupils leave school adequately prepared for adult life.

## Curriculum Content and Learning Outcomes

### The "By the End of" Statements

The DfE statutory guidance provides specific learning outcomes that pupils should achieve "by the end of primary school" and "by the end of secondary school." These outcomes form the minimum statutory requirement.

**Primary outcomes include:**
- Understanding that families are important for children growing up because they provide love, security and stability
- Recognising that there are different types of loving relationships
- Knowing the characteristics of healthy friendships
- Understanding personal boundaries and the right to privacy
- Recognising who to trust and how to get help

**Secondary outcomes include:**
- Understanding consent, including the legal age and the role of power imbalances
- Knowing how to access contraception and sexual health services
- Understanding the risks related to unprotected sex
- Recognising the influence of pornography on expectations and behaviours
- Understanding the law in relation to sexual consent, sexual exploitation, and grooming

### Integration with the Wider PSHE Curriculum

While RSE and Health Education are statutory, they exist within the broader PSHE framework. The PSHE Association's Programme of Study (2020) demonstrates how statutory content integrates with non-statutory but equally important economic wellbeing and careers education.

Jonathan Charlesworth, former Chair of the PSHE Association, has emphasised that statutory status must not lead to a narrowing of PSHE provision. Schools should deliver comprehensive PSHE education that includes statutory elements within a coherent, progressive programme.

## Parental Rights and School Responsibilities

### Right to Withdraw

Parents retain the right to withdraw their children from sex education (but not Relationships Education or Health Education) up to three terms before the child turns 16. After that point, pupils may opt themselves into sex education if they wish, regardless of parental preference.

This right does not extend to:
- National Curriculum Science content (including puberty, reproduction, and pregnancy)
- Any statutory Relationships Education or Health Education content
- Any sex education delivered incidentally within other subjects

Schools must document withdrawal requests and discuss them with parents to ensure they understand what their child will miss. The DfE guidance requires that alternative arrangements respect parental wishes while ensuring pupils receive their statutory entitlement.

### Consultation Requirements

Schools must consult with parents when developing or reviewing their RSE and Health Education policy. This consultation should be meaningful and allow parents to understand what will be taught and when. However, consultation does not give parents a veto over curriculum content – schools must deliver the statutory requirements.

## Policy Requirements and School Leadership

### The School Policy

Every school must have a written RSE policy (and Health Education policy, though these are often combined). The policy must be made available to parents and must include:
- A statement of what will be taught and when
- The school's approach to teaching potentially sensitive topics
- How the policy reflects the school's values and ethos
- Arrangements for consultation with parents
- The right to withdraw from sex education

The governing body or proprietor must approve the policy, demonstrating senior leadership accountability for statutory provision.

### Quality Assurance and Ofsted

Ofsted's Education Inspection Framework (2019, updated 2021) explicitly includes RSE and Health Education within the "Personal Development" judgement. Inspectors will review:
- Whether the school meets its statutory obligations
- The quality and coherence of the curriculum
- How well the curriculum is implemented and taught
- Pupils' knowledge and understanding of keeping healthy and safe

Schools rated "Inadequate" for Personal Development have often failed to deliver age-appropriate RSE or have not ensured curriculum coverage addresses safeguarding risks, particularly online safety and consent.

## Teaching Approaches and Pedagogy

### Creating Safe Learning Environments

Jenny Mosley's work on establishing ground rules and creating emotionally safe classrooms is fundamental to effective RSE delivery. The PSHE Association recommends:
- Establishing clear ground rules co-created with pupils
- Using distancing techniques (third-person scenarios, case studies)
- Employing anonymous question boxes
- Ensuring access to appropriate support

### Safeguarding Considerations

Teachers delivering RSE must balance openness with safeguarding responsibilities. Schools should:
- Train staff to recognise disclosures requiring safeguarding action
- Ensure pupils know how to report concerns
- Never promise absolute confidentiality
- Follow school safeguarding procedures

The Brook Sexual Behaviours Traffic Light Tool helps staff assess whether disclosed behaviours are age-appropriate or indicate concern.

### Inclusive Practice

The Equality Act 2010 requires that teaching is accessible to all pupils and that schools advance equality of opportunity. RSE must:
- Represent diverse family structures (including same-sex parents, single parents, kinship care)
- Address LGBTQ+ identities appropriately and affirmatively
- Be accessible to pupils with SEND through adapted resources and differentiation
- Challenge sexism, misogyny, homophobia, biphobia and transphobia

Lee Jerome's research on teaching controversial issues emphasises the importance of balanced inquiry-based approaches that allow pupils to develop critical thinking while ensuring teaching upholds fundamental British values and human rights principles.

## Evidence Base and Research

### Educational Research Supporting Statutory RSE

The Evidence for the Sex and Relationships Education (SRE) Guidance (Public Health England, 2015) reviewed international evidence demonstrating that comprehensive RSE:
- Does not encourage early sexual activity
- Reduces teenage pregnancy rates
- Improves knowledge of consent and healthy relationships
- Enhances help-seeking behaviours

Research by the University of Exeter (2019) found that young people who received comprehensive RSE were more likely to delay first intercourse, use contraception when they did become sexually active, and report positive sexual experiences characterised by mutual respect and consent.

### Teacher Confidence and Training

Studies consistently identify teacher confidence as a key factor in RSE quality. The Sex Education Forum's research (2020) found that 79% of teachers wanted more training on teaching RSE, particularly on:
- LGBTQ+ inclusion
- Teaching about consent
- Addressing pornography's influence
- Responding to disclosures

The PSHE Association's Quality Mark provides professional development pathways and accreditation for subject leadership in PSHE, including RSE.

## Resources and Curriculum Planning

### DfE-Endorsed Providers

The Department for Education maintains a list of external organisations and materials that have been quality-assured for teaching RSE and Health Education. These include:
- PSHE Association resources and training
- Medway Public Health Directorate's teaching resources
- 1Decision's PSHE curriculum materials
- Coram Life Education's SCARF programme

Schools should critically evaluate all resources against the statutory guidance and their own policy before use.

### Progression and Spiral Curriculum

Effective RSE follows a spiral curriculum model, revisiting topics with increased depth and complexity. Jonathan Charlesworth's work emphasises that progression should be evident in:
- Vocabulary complexity and precision
- Sophistication of concepts
- Application of knowledge to increasingly complex scenarios
- Development of critical thinking and decision-making skills

For example, teaching about families might progress from:
- **KS1:** Identifying different family members and their roles
- **KS2:** Understanding diverse family structures and sources of support
- **KS3:** Analysing societal changes in family patterns and their implications
- **KS4:** Evaluating the importance of commitment, parenting responsibilities, and family law

## Assessment and Monitoring Progress

### Assessing Statutory Content

While PSHE is not a formal examination subject, schools must assess pupils' knowledge and understanding of statutory RSE and Health Education. Assessment methods include:
- Knowledge recall activities
- Scenario-based application exercises
- Self-assessment against learning outcomes
- Discussion and questioning

AQA's Certificate in Personal and Social Education (Level 2) and similar qualifications from other exam boards provide optional accreditation routes, though these are not requirements of the statutory framework.

### Monitoring Curriculum Coverage

Schools should maintain clear records demonstrating:
- That all statutory content has been taught
- When topics were covered and with which year groups
- Which staff delivered the teaching
- How the curriculum is differentiated and adapted

This documentation supports accountability to governors, parents, and inspectors.

## Special Considerations and Adaptations

### Faith Schools and Institutional Ethos

Faith schools must teach the statutory RSE and Health Education curriculum while reflecting their religious character. However, teaching must be:
- Factually accurate and evidence-based
- Compliant with the Equality Act 2010
- Balanced and respectful of different viewpoints
- Supportive of fundamental British values

Schools may teach their faith's perspective on relationships and sexuality but must also ensure pupils receive the information they need to keep themselves safe and healthy.

### SEND Provision

The statutory guidance emphasises that RSE and Health Education must be accessible to all pupils. For pupils with SEND, schools should:
- Adapt content and teaching methods appropriately
- Consider specialist resources (e.g., Mencap's RSE resources for learning disabilities)
- Address specific vulnerabilities, including higher risks of abuse
- Involve parents/carers and SEND specialists in planning

The National Children's Bureau's guidance on RSE for children with learning disabilities provides research-based approaches.

## Contemporary Challenges and Emerging Issues

### Digital Literacy and Online Safety

The Health Education requirement to cover "internet safety and harms" reflects growing concerns about children's digital lives. Teaching must address:
- Online relationships and the differences from face-to-face interactions
- Sharing of sexual imagery (including legal implications under the Criminal Justice Act 1988)
- Pornography's influence on sexual expectations
- Online grooming and exploitation

The UK Council for Internet Safety provides educational frameworks aligned with statutory requirements.

### Contextual Safeguarding

Research by Carlene Firmin on contextual safeguarding emphasises that young people's relationships and experiences extend beyond family settings. RSE must prepare pupils to recognise and respond to risks in peer groups, neighbourhoods, and online spaces, including:
- Child criminal exploitation
- County lines
- Peer-on-peer abuse
- Sexual exploitation outside the home

## Professional Standards and Teacher Development

### Teacher Standards and Subject Knowledge

The Teachers' Standards (DfE, 2011, updated 2021) require that teachers demonstrate good subject knowledge and keep this knowledge up to date. For RSE and Health Education, this includes:
- Current law on consent, age of consent, and sexual offences
- Up-to-date information on contraception, STIs, and sexual health services
- Understanding of safeguarding procedures and thresholds
- Knowledge of diverse identities and relationships

### Continuing Professional Development

Nick Boddington's research on PSHE subject leadership highlights that effective RSE delivery requires ongoing CPD, including:
- Subject-specific training on content and pedagogy
- Safeguarding updates
- Awareness of emerging issues (e.g., online harms)
- Training on inclusive practice

The PSHE Association, Sex Education Forum, and Local Authority PSHE advisers provide specialist training.

## Conclusion and Future Directions

The introduction of statutory RSE and Health Education represents a significant advancement in children's entitlement to essential knowledge and skills. However, effective implementation requires sustained investment in teacher training, high-quality resources, and senior leadership commitment. As research by Lee Jerome and others demonstrates, when schools approach these subjects with the rigour and importance they deserve, they make measurable contributions to young people's health, wellbeing, and safeguarding.

The ongoing review of the statutory guidance (with government commitment to regular updates) ensures that content remains responsive to emerging evidence and societal changes, maintaining the curriculum's relevance for future generations.
