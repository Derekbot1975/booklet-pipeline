# PSHE at Key Stage 1

## Overview and Statutory Framework

Key Stage 1 (KS1) encompasses Years 1 and 2, typically serving pupils aged 5-7 years. Following the Children and Social Work Act 2017 and subsequent DfE guidance, Relationships Education became statutory in all primary schools from September 2020, with Health Education also mandatory. The broader PSHE curriculum, whilst non-statutory, is strongly recommended by the DfE and Ofsted as essential for pupils' personal development.

The PSHE Association provides the most widely-adopted framework for KS1, organising content into three core themes:
- Health and Wellbeing
- Relationships
- Living in the Wider World

At KS1, the curriculum must be developmentally appropriate, recognising that young children are beginning to understand themselves as individuals within families, friendships, and communities. The teaching approach should be concrete rather than abstract, using stories, role-play, and visual resources that connect to children's lived experiences.

## Core Content Areas

### Health and Wellbeing at KS1

The statutory Health Education guidance (DfE, 2020) requires KS1 pupils to learn about:

**Physical Health and Mental Wellbeing:**
- Understanding that mental wellbeing is a normal part of daily life, just like physical health
- Simple self-care techniques, including the importance of rest, time spent with friends and family, and activities they enjoy
- How to recognise and talk about their emotions, including using appropriate vocabulary
- That there is a normal range of emotions (e.g., happy, sad, angry, worried) and scale of emotions

**Healthy Eating:**
- What constitutes a healthy diet, including understanding the Eatwell Guide at an age-appropriate level
- The principles of planning and preparing simple meals
- Characteristics of a poor diet and risks associated with unhealthy eating, including obesity and tooth decay

**Physical Activity:**
- The benefits of physical exercise, time outdoors, and community participation on mental and physical health
- Simple activities they can do to maintain their own physical health

Jenny Mosley's Circle Time methodology is particularly effective at KS1 for exploring emotional literacy and wellbeing themes. Her Quality Circle Time model (Mosley, 1996, 2005) provides structured opportunities for young children to discuss feelings, develop empathy, and practise social skills in a safe, democratic forum. Circle Time activities help KS1 pupils develop the vocabulary and confidence to articulate their emotions—a fundamental building block for later mental health awareness.

### Relationships Education at KS1

The statutory Relationships Education guidance (DfE, 2020) outlines specific content for KS1:

**Families and People Who Care for Me:**
- That families are important for children growing up because they can give love, security and stability
- The characteristics of healthy family life, including commitment to each other, including in times of difficulty
- That stable, caring relationships, which may be of different types, are at the heart of happy families
- How to recognise if family relationships are making them feel unhappy or unsafe, and how to seek help

**Caring Friendships:**
- How important friendships are in making us feel happy and secure
- That healthy friendships are positive and welcoming towards others
- How people choose and make friends
- The characteristics of friendships, including mutual respect, truthfulness, trustworthiness, loyalty, kindness, generosity, sharing interests and experiences
- That most friendships have ups and downs, and that these can often be worked through
- How to recognise who to trust and who not to trust
- How to judge when a friendship is making them feel unhappy or uncomfortable
- How to seek help if feeling lonely or excluded

**Respectful Relationships:**
- The importance of respecting others, even when they are different
- Practical steps they can take in a range of different contexts to improve or support respectful relationships
- The conventions of courtesy and manners
- The importance of self-respect and how this links to happiness
- About different types of bullying (including cyberbullying), the impact of bullying, responsibilities of bystanders
- How to get help with bullying

**Online Relationships:**
- That people sometimes behave differently online, including pretending to be someone they are not
- That the same principles apply to online relationships as face-to-face relationships
- The rules and principles for keeping safe online
- How to recognise harmful content and contact online
- How to report concerns about content or contact online

**Being Safe:**
- What sorts of boundaries are appropriate in friendships with peers and others
- About the concept of privacy and the implications of it for both children and adults
- That each person's body belongs to them, and the differences between appropriate and inappropriate touch
- How to respond safely and appropriately to adults they may encounter whom they do not know
- How to recognise and report feelings of being unsafe or feeling bad about any adult
- How to ask for advice or help for themselves or others, and to keep trying until they are heard
- How to report concerns or abuse, and the vocabulary to do so

The NSPCC's Pants resources are widely used at KS1 to teach body autonomy and safety messages in an age-appropriate manner.

### Living in the Wider World at KS1

Whilst Citizenship is not statutory at primary level, the PSHE Association framework recommends KS1 pupils learn about:

**Belonging to a Community:**
- About what rules are, why they are needed, and why different rules are needed for different situations
- How people and other living things have different needs
- About things they have in common and differences between themselves and others
- How to listen to other people and play and work cooperatively
- How to talk about and share their opinions on things that matter to them

**Media Literacy and Digital Resilience:**
- How to use the internet and digital devices to communicate with people they know
- That not all information online is true

**Money and Work:**
- What money is and its different forms (coins, notes, and ways of paying digitally)
- How money is obtained (earned, borrowed, won)
- That people make different choices about how to save and spend money
- That jobs help people earn money to pay for things
- Different jobs that people they know or people who work in the community do

Bernard Crick's influential work on citizenship education, whilst primarily focused on secondary level (Crick Report, 1998), established principles of active citizenship that filter down to KS1. Even young children can begin to understand their role as members of communities—classroom, school, family, neighbourhood—and participate in age-appropriate democratic processes like class votes and decision-making.

## Pedagogical Approaches for KS1

### Developmental Considerations

Young children at KS1 are at critical developmental stages:
- **Cognitive Development:** Moving from pre-operational to early concrete operational thinking (Piaget). They benefit from concrete examples, visual aids, and experiential learning rather than abstract concepts.
- **Social Development:** Developing beyond egocentric thinking to understand others' perspectives. Parallel play transitions to cooperative play.
- **Emotional Development:** Building emotional vocabulary and beginning to regulate emotions with adult support.
- **Moral Development:** Understanding rules and fairness, though still requiring clear boundaries and consistent expectations.

### Effective Teaching Strategies

**Stories and Literature:**
Literature is exceptionally powerful at KS1 for exploring PSHE themes. Quality picture books provide safe distance for discussing sensitive topics whilst engaging young imaginations. Examples include:
- *The Colour Monster* by Anna Llenas (emotions)
- *The Invisible String* by Patrice Karst (attachment and relationships)
- *Ruby's Worry* by Tom Percival (anxiety and mental health)
- *Elmer* by David McKee (diversity and belonging)

**Role-Play and Drama:**
Simple role-play scenarios allow KS1 pupils to rehearse social situations, practise responses, and explore different perspectives. This might include:
- Practising how to ask to join a game
- Acting out what to do if someone is unkind
- Rehearsing how to tell a trusted adult about a worry

**Circle Time:**
Jenny Mosley's Circle Time model remains the gold standard for KS1 PSHE delivery. Weekly Circle Time sessions provide structured opportunities for:
- Speaking and listening in turn
- Sharing experiences and feelings
- Problem-solving together
- Building class cohesion and belonging

Mosley's approach emphasises the teacher's role as facilitator rather than instructor, creating democratic spaces where children's voices are genuinely heard.

**Visual and Concrete Resources:**
KS1 pupils benefit from:
- Emotion cards and faces charts
- Social stories (developed from Carol Gray's work with autistic children but beneficial for all)
- Puppets and soft toys as talking partners
- Visual timetables and routine charts
- Photographs and real-world examples

**Cross-Curricular Links:**
PSHE themes naturally integrate with other KS1 subjects:
- **Science:** Life cycles, growing, keeping healthy, hygiene
- **Computing:** Online safety, digital literacy
- **PE:** Cooperation, fair play, physical health
- **English:** Empathy through stories, vocabulary for emotions
- **RE:** Values, belonging, community, celebrations

## Assessment at KS1

PSHE is not formally assessed through tests or levels, but teachers must monitor pupils' progress and understanding. The PSHE Association (2020) recommends assessment that is:
- **Ongoing:** Through observation, discussion, and pupil responses
- **Age-appropriate:** Not requiring written evidence that exceeds literacy capabilities
- **Focused on knowledge, skills, and attributes:** Not judging pupils' personal circumstances or family situations

### Assessment Methods for KS1:

**Observation:**
Teachers note pupils' participation in discussions, ability to express feelings, cooperation in group activities, and application of learning in unstructured times.

**Verbal Responses:**
Through questioning and discussion, teachers assess understanding of key concepts like friendship qualities, healthy choices, and safety messages.

**Simple Recording:**
Age-appropriate activities might include:
- Drawing pictures to show understanding (e.g., drawing healthy foods)
- Circling emoji faces to indicate feelings
- Matching activities (e.g., matching scenarios to appropriate responses)

**Self-Assessment:**
Simple self-assessment tools help develop metacognition:
- Thumbs up/middle/down for confidence
- Traffic light cards (red/amber/green)
- "Before I learned this, I thought... Now I know..."

## Safeguarding Considerations

PSHE lessons at KS1, particularly those addressing relationships and being safe, operate within the context of safeguarding. Teachers must:

**Establish Safe Learning Environments:**
- Use ground rules co-created with pupils (age-appropriately)
- Employ distancing techniques (stories, scenarios about other children, not personal disclosure)
- Clarify that some topics require telling a trusted adult
- Never promise confidentiality

**Be Prepared for Disclosures:**
PSHE lessons, particularly those addressing body autonomy, appropriate/inappropriate touch, and safe relationships, may lead to disclosures. All KS1 teachers must:
- Know their school's Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL)
- Follow school safeguarding procedures immediately
- Record disclosures verbatim when possible
- Not investigate but pass concerns to appropriate personnel

**Work Within Professional Boundaries:**
When teaching about families, teachers must be inclusive of diverse family structures (single parents, same-sex parents, kinship care, adoptive families) whilst remaining sensitive to pupils' own circumstances. The DfE guidance (2020) explicitly requires teaching about different types of families.

## Partnership with Parents and Carers

The DfE statutory guidance requires schools to consult with parents when developing Relationships and Health Education policies. At KS1, effective parental engagement is crucial because:
- Young children's learning is reinforced through home-school consistency
- Parents are often the primary educators on sensitive topics
- Early intervention and partnership prevents later difficulties

**Effective Strategies:**

**Communication:**
- Share PSHE curriculum overviews at the start of each term
- Provide information about upcoming topics, particularly sensitive ones
- Use parent workshops to demonstrate teaching approaches
- Send home key vocabulary and suggested conversation starters

**Resources for Home:**
- Recommended book lists
- Simple activities families can do together
- Signposting to quality websites and resources

**Right to Withdraw:**
Parents cannot withdraw children from statutory Relationships Education or Health Education at primary level. However, they can request withdrawal from non-statutory sex education beyond the Science National Curriculum. At KS1, sex education beyond the statutory content is rarely taught. The PSHE Association recommends being clear with parents about what is and isn't taught at each key stage.

## Supporting Diverse Learners at KS1

### SEND Considerations

The Equality Act 2010 requires reasonable adjustments for pupils with special educational needs and disabilities. At KS1, this might include:

**For pupils with Communication Needs:**
- Makaton signs or PECS alongside spoken vocabulary
- Visual timetables showing PSHE lesson structure
- Now-and-next boards
- Extended processing time

**For pupils with Social, Emotional, and Mental Health Needs:**
- Consistent routines and predictability
- Pre-teaching key concepts
- Safe spaces and calming strategies
- Small-group or 1:1 work when needed
- Social stories personalised to individual needs

**For pupils with Cognition and Learning Needs:**
- Simplified language and instructions
- Concrete examples and repetition
- Multi-sensory approaches
- Over-learning opportunities

### Cultural Sensitivity and Inclusion

KS1 PSHE must respect the religious and cultural backgrounds of all pupils whilst delivering statutory content. This requires:
- Using diverse examples in teaching materials (ethnicities, family structures, religions, abilities)
- Being aware of cultural sensitivities around particular topics
- Teaching British values (democracy, rule of law, individual liberty, mutual respect, tolerance) in age-appropriate ways
- Ensuring no child feels their family or background is being judged

Lee Jerome's work on controversial issues in primary citizenship (Jerome & Kisby, 2019) emphasises that even young children encounter difference and controversy. Rather than avoiding difficult topics, teachers should address them honestly at an appropriate level, helping children develop respect for diversity from an early age.

## Resources and Support

### Key Organisations:

**PSHE Association:**
The leading national body, providing:
- Programme of Study for PSHE Education (2020 update)
- Key Stage-specific planning frameworks
- Quality assurance mark for resources
- Teacher training and CPD

**Coram Life Education:**
Delivers interactive PSHE programmes through trained educators and online resources, including their Scarf curriculum widely used in primary schools.

**1Decision:**
Web-based PSHE programme with age-appropriate videos and resources specifically designed for primary schools.

**Votes for Schools:**
Provides weekly topical debates and voting activities, suitable for developing early citizenship skills at KS1.

### Quality Assurance:

Not all PSHE resources are evidence-based or age-appropriate. The PSHE Association's quality mark indicates resources that:
- Are factually accurate
- Use appropriate pedagogies
- Are inclusive and non-discriminatory
- Align with statutory guidance
- Have been reviewed by experts

Teachers should critically evaluate resources, particularly free online materials, which may contain safeguarding concerns, cultural insensitivity, or commercial bias.

## Monitoring and Evaluation

School leaders responsible for PSHE at KS1 should:

**Ensure Curriculum Coverage:**
- Audit curriculum mapping against PSHE Association framework and statutory guidance
- Check progression from Year 1 to Year 2
- Verify cross-curricular links are identified

**Support Staff Development:**
- Provide CPD on sensitive topics, teaching methodologies, and safeguarding
- Share effective practice across year groups
- Ensure all staff understand their role in PSHE, not just class teachers

**Gather Evidence:**
- Pupil voice activities (suitable for KS1: drawing, talking, showing)
- Learning walk observations
- Parent feedback
- Analysis of behaviour logs and pastoral concerns

**Review and Adapt:**
- Annual policy and curriculum review
- Responsive adjustments for cohort needs
- Updates following new guidance or local issues

## Current Research and Evidence Base

Whilst research specifically on KS1 PSHE is limited, broader evidence supports early intervention:

**Social and Emotional Learning:**
The EEF Teaching and Learning Toolkit (2021) identifies social and emotional learning programmes as having moderate impact (four months' additional progress) at relatively low cost. Early years and primary interventions show the strongest effects.

**Emotional Literacy:**
Katherine Weare's longitudinal research (Weare & Gray, 2003; Weare, 2004) demonstrates that emotional literacy programmes in primary schools improve behaviour, attendance, and academic attainment, with effects sustained over time. Programmes starting at KS1 show better outcomes than later interventions.

**Relationships Education:**
Evidence from the Sex Education Forum and Brook (2018) suggests that early, age-appropriate relationships education builds foundations for healthy relationships, reduces harmful behaviours, and supports safeguarding. Children who learn about body autonomy, appropriate touch, and trusted adults at KS1 are better protected.
