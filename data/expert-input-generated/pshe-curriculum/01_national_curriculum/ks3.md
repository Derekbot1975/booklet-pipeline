# PSHE at Key Stage 3

## Overview and Statutory Framework

Personal, Social, Health and Economic (PSHE) education at Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14) occupies a unique position within the English education system. Following the Children and Social Work Act 2017, aspects of PSHE became statutory from September 2020, specifically Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE), and Health Education. The Department for Education's statutory guidance (2019, updated 2020) mandates that all secondary schools must deliver RSE and Health Education, whilst the broader PSHE curriculum, including economic wellbeing and careers education, remains non-statutory but strongly recommended.

The PSHE Association, the national body for PSHE education, provides the most comprehensive framework for Key Stage 3 delivery through their Programme of Study (2020 edition), which schools across England widely adopt as their planning framework. This builds upon Bernard Crick's seminal work on citizenship education, outlined in the Crick Report (1998), which established citizenship as a statutory subject and emphasised active participation, political literacy, and social and moral responsibility.

## Core Content Areas at Key Stage 3

### Health and Wellbeing

At Key Stage 3, the Health and Wellbeing strand addresses students' physical and mental health during a critical developmental period. The statutory guidance requires schools to cover mental wellbeing, internet safety and harms, physical health and fitness, healthy eating, drugs, alcohol and tobacco, health and prevention, basic first aid, and changing adolescent body.

Nick Boddington's work on mental health education emphasises that Key Stage 3 is the optimal time to develop resilience strategies, as this age group faces increasing academic pressure, social media influence, and identity formation challenges. Research by the Education Policy Institute (2018) demonstrates that 50% of lifetime mental health problems emerge by age 14, making preventative PSHE education crucial.

Practical content includes:
- Recognising warning signs of mental health issues, including anxiety, depression, self-harm, and eating disorders
- Understanding the mind-body connection and how physical health, sleep, and nutrition affect mental wellbeing
- Developing coping strategies and resilience skills, building on Jenny Mosley's circle time approaches
- Understanding that mental health exists on a continuum and everyone can experience difficulties
- Knowing how and when to seek support, including from school counsellors, GPs, and services like Childline

### Relationships and Sex Education (RSE)

The statutory RSE curriculum at Key Stage 3 builds upon primary Relationships Education, introducing age-appropriate content about intimate relationships, sexual health, and consent. This reflects significant curriculum development since the Education Act 2002 and represents what Jonathan Charlesworth describes as a "shift from biological reproduction to relationships-centred education."

Key Stage 3 RSE must address:
- Families and parenting, including different family structures and the characteristics of healthy family life
- Respectful relationships, including friendship dynamics during adolescence
- Online and media relationships, including consent in digital contexts
- Being safe, including sexual harassment, violence, and exploitation
- Intimate and sexual relationships, including consent, contraception, and the law
- LGBTQ+ relationships and identities, ensuring inclusive curricula

Lee Jerome's research on controversial issues in citizenship education (2012) emphasises that RSE at Key Stage 3 requires skilled facilitation, establishing ground rules, and creating safe spaces for discussion. The PSHE Association's "Creating a safe and supportive environment" guidance (2018) provides practical strategies including distancing techniques, anonymous question boxes, and clear referral pathways.

### Living in the Wider World: Economic Wellbeing and Careers

This strand encompasses financial capability, enterprise, employability, and careers education. Whilst not statutory, Gatsby Benchmarks (2014, revised 2018) establish quality standards for careers provision, with Benchmark 4 specifically addressing curriculum-linked careers education.

At Key Stage 3, students should develop:
- Understanding of the UK employment landscape and changing world of work
- Financial literacy including budgeting, saving, borrowing, and understanding risk
- Consumer rights and responsibilities, including online transactions
- Enterprise skills and entrepreneurial thinking
- Career exploration linked to subjects and personal strengths
- Understanding of routes post-16, including academic and vocational pathways

The Careers & Enterprise Company's research (2019) demonstrates that early careers education correlates with improved outcomes, particularly for disadvantaged students. Key Stage 3 should include workplace encounters, employer engagement, and exploration of stereotypes that might limit aspirations.

### Citizenship Education

Citizenship remains statutory at Key Stage 3, as established by the Education Act 2002. The National Curriculum for Citizenship (2014) specifies that students should develop knowledge about democracy, government, and the rights and responsibilities of citizens, skills of enquiry and communication, and aptitude for participation and responsible action.

Bernard Crick's three-strand model remains influential:
1. **Social and moral responsibility**: Understanding rights and responsibilities, recognising diversity, and developing community engagement
2. **Community involvement**: Active participation in school and community life
3. **Political literacy**: Understanding political systems, democratic processes, and how citizens can influence decisions

Lee Jerome and Jonathan Charlesworth's work on political literacy (2020) argues that Key Stage 3 students should encounter "real politics" through discussion of current affairs, controversial issues, and political concepts like power, justice, and equality. Their research challenges deficit models of young people's political understanding, demonstrating that 11-14 year-olds can engage with sophisticated political ideas when appropriately scaffolded.

## Pedagogical Approaches for Key Stage 3

### Active Learning and Participation

Jenny Mosley's Quality Circle Time model, whilst originally developed for primary schools, influences Key Stage 3 PSHE through its emphasis on speaking and listening skills, democratic participation, and problem-solving. At secondary level, this translates into discussion-based learning, debates, role-play, and student voice mechanisms.

Effective pedagogies at Key Stage 3 include:
- **Case studies and scenarios**: Allowing students to explore issues from safe distances whilst developing critical thinking
- **Debate and discussion**: Structured formats including balloon debates, fishbowl discussions, and Socratic seminars
- **Drama and role-play**: Particularly effective for exploring relationships, conflict resolution, and ethical dilemmas
- **Project-based learning**: Linking PSHE to action, such as social action projects or campaigns
- **Peer education**: Older students mentoring younger peers, developing leadership and communication skills

### Managing Sensitive and Controversial Issues

Key Stage 3 PSHE inevitably addresses sensitive topics including mental health, relationships, sexuality, substance use, and political issues. The PSHE Association's guidance on handling sensitive topics emphasises establishing clear ground rules, knowing students' backgrounds, providing advance notice of topics, and ensuring robust safeguarding procedures.

Nick Boddington's training materials on safeguarding in PSHE highlight that disclosures are more likely during PSHE lessons. Teachers require:
- Clear understanding of school safeguarding policies and designated safeguarding leads (DSLs)
- Strategies to manage disclosures appropriately without promising confidentiality
- Awareness that normalising help-seeking is protective
- Resources for signposting support services

Lee Jerome's research on teaching controversial issues identifies that teachers often avoid political topics due to concerns about impartiality, complaints, or insufficient knowledge. However, his work demonstrates that students benefit from exploring controversial issues when teachers facilitate rather than advocate, present multiple perspectives, and teach critical evaluation skills.

## Assessment in Key Stage 3 PSHE

Unlike most subjects, PSHE does not have traditional assessment frameworks or GCSE qualifications, though some schools adopt assessment systems. The PSHE Association's "Assessment in PSHE Education" guidance (2019) recommends formative assessment focusing on knowledge, understanding, skills, and attributes rather than personal information or outcomes.

Appropriate assessment approaches include:
- **Baseline and endpoint assessment**: Measuring changes in knowledge and understanding using age-appropriate tools
- **Self-assessment**: Students reflecting on learning and personal development
- **Skills demonstration**: Assessing communication, critical thinking, and decision-making through activities
- **Portfolio or journal work**: Documenting learning journeys whilst maintaining appropriate boundaries

Assessment must never require students to disclose personal information about themselves or their families. Ofsted's PSHE subject report (2021) criticised schools that confused assessment with surveying students about personal experiences or behaviours.

## Curriculum Planning and Progression

### Spiral Curriculum Design

PSHE at Key Stage 3 should follow spiral curriculum principles, revisiting topics with increasing depth and sophistication. The PSHE Association Programme of Study structures content across three key stages, ensuring progression from KS2 through KS3 to KS4.

Effective progression at Key Stage 3 means:
- **Year 7**: Transition focus, establishing new relationships, belonging, and developing independence
- **Year 8**: Deepening understanding of health risks, relationships complexity, and identity exploration
- **Year 9**: Preparing for GCSE options, considering future pathways, and developing more sophisticated critical analysis

### Cross-Curricular Links

PSHE connects with multiple curriculum areas. Science teaches biological aspects of health and reproduction; English develops communication skills and explores texts addressing social issues; Religious Studies examines ethics and worldviews; and History and Geography provide contexts for understanding rights, development, and global citizenship.

The National Curriculum (2014) emphasises that all teachers should promote fundamental British values, including democracy, rule of law, individual liberty, and mutual respect and tolerance. This connects directly with citizenship education and should be reinforced across subjects.

## Safeguarding and Safe Learning Environments

Statutory safeguarding guidance "Keeping Children Safe in Education" (DfE, 2023) applies to all PSHE teaching. Key Stage 3 PSHE must balance openness about sensitive topics with robust safeguarding, ensuring students can learn safely whilst knowing how to access help.

Safe learning requires:
- **Ground rules co-created with students**: Including respect, confidentiality boundaries, right to pass, and no personal disclosures
- **Distancing techniques**: Using third-person scenarios, case studies, or fictional characters
- **Anonymous questions**: Allowing students to ask sensitive questions via question boxes or digital platforms
- **Clear signposting**: Regular reminders about how to access support in school and externally

## Special Considerations for Key Stage 3

### Supporting Vulnerable Students

Key Stage 3 encompasses significant developmental changes and increasing independence, making some students particularly vulnerable. PSHE must be inclusive and responsive to:
- Students with SEND, requiring differentiated materials and approaches
- Looked-after children and those with social care involvement
- Students experiencing or at risk of abuse, including sexual or criminal exploitation
- LGBTQ+ students, ensuring inclusive content and challenging discrimination
- Students from particular religious or cultural backgrounds requiring sensitive handling of topics

The Equality Act 2010 requires schools to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of opportunity, and foster good relations. PSHE teaching must reflect diverse experiences whilst ensuring no group is stereotyped or marginalised.

### Parental Engagement and the Right to Withdraw

Parents have the right to withdraw students from sex education content outside the statutory science curriculum up to three terms before the student turns 16. They cannot withdraw students from Relationships Education or Health Education. Schools must communicate PSHE content to parents, address concerns, and maintain positive relationships whilst delivering statutory requirements.

The PSHE Association and Sex Education Forum provide template letters and policies supporting schools to work constructively with parents, emphasising that inclusive, age-appropriate RSE protects all children.

## Resources and Quality Assurance

### Recommended Resources

Quality-assured resources for Key Stage 3 PSHE include:
- **PSHE Association resources**: Complete schemes of work and lesson plans
- **Brook's RSE resources**: "Are You Getting It?" and Traffic Light Tool
- **Educate & Celebrate**: LGBTQ+ inclusive curricula
- **NSPCC resources**: Including PANTS and age-appropriate safeguarding content
- **Money and Pensions Service**: Financial education resources
- **UK Safer Internet Centre**: Digital literacy and online safety resources

Schools should evaluate resources using the PSHE Association's quality assurance criteria, checking for accuracy, age-appropriateness, inclusivity, and alignment with statutory guidance.

### External Visitors

Many schools invite external organisations to contribute to PSHE at Key Stage 3, including sexual health services, police, financial education providers, or equality organisations. The PSHE Association's "Using external contributors" guidance (2019) emphasises that external visitors supplement rather than replace curriculum delivery by trained teachers.

Schools must ensure external contributors:
- Align with school policies and values
- Deliver within the planned curriculum framework
- Are supervised by school staff
- Meet safeguarding requirements including DBS checks where appropriate
- Use age-appropriate, evidence-based content

## Professional Development for PSHE Teaching

Research by the Sex Education Forum (2020) found that only 30% of teachers delivering RSE felt they had received adequate training. Effective PSHE teaching at Key Stage 3 requires subject-specific professional development covering content knowledge, pedagogical approaches, safeguarding, and handling sensitive topics.

Professional development should include:
- Subject knowledge updates, particularly regarding legislation, services, and current issues
- Pedagogical training in active learning, managing discussions, and assessment
- Safeguarding training specific to PSHE contexts
- Support for managing personal triggers or values conflicts

The PSHE Association offers professional development programmes and quality marks recognising effective PSHE provision. Some schools appoint specialist PSHE coordinators or leads, whilst others use tutors or mixed delivery models.

## Impact and Evidence

Ofsted's PSHE review (2021) identified that students value PSHE most when it addresses real issues they face, is delivered by confident teachers who create safe environments, and connects learning to action. Students reported that high-quality PSHE at Key Stage 3 helped them develop healthier relationships, make informed choices, and feel better equipped for adult life.

Research evidence demonstrates that effective PSHE education:
- Delays onset of sexual activity and increases contraception use (Shepherd et al., 2010)
- Reduces substance use and associated harms (Faggiano et al., 2014)
- Improves mental health literacy and help-seeking (Kidger et al., 2016)
- Enhances financial capability and decision-making (Money Advice Service, 2018)
- Develops critical thinking and democratic participation (Jerome et al., 2020)

## Current Challenges and Future Directions

Key Stage 3 PSHE faces several challenges including curriculum time pressures, teacher confidence and training, assessment approaches, and rapidly changing contexts including social media, mental health concerns, and evolving relationships and family structures.

Jonathan Charlesworth and Nick Boddington's recent work emphasises that PSHE must remain responsive to emerging issues whilst maintaining core content. The post-2020 statutory framework provides stronger foundations for PSHE, but schools must ensure adequate resourcing, training, and leadership support.

Looking forward, developments include greater integration of digital literacy and critical media analysis, enhanced mental health education reflecting increasing adolescent mental health needs, more sophisticated careers and economic education preparing students for changing employment landscapes, and continued emphasis on equality, diversity, and inclusion reflecting contemporary British society.
