# PSHE at Key Stage 2

## Overview and Statutory Framework

PSHE (Personal, Social, Health and Economic education) at Key Stage 2 encompasses pupils aged 7-11 (Years 3-6) in England. Following the Children and Social Work Act 2017 and subsequent statutory guidance published by the Department for Education in 2019 (updated 2020), Relationships Education became statutory for all primary schools from September 2020, whilst Health Education also became compulsory. The broader PSHE curriculum, whilst non-statutory, remains strongly recommended by Ofsted and the PSHE Association as essential for pupils' personal development.

The statutory guidance 'Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education' (DfE, 2019) establishes clear expectations for what pupils should know by the end of primary school. This represents a significant shift from the previous non-statutory framework, giving schools legal obligations in these specific areas whilst maintaining professional autonomy in delivering the wider PSHE curriculum.

## Curriculum Structure and Content

### The Three Core Strands

The PSHE Association, the national body for PSHE education established in 2008 and building on earlier work by organisations including the National Healthy Schools Programme, organises Key Stage 2 PSHE into three core themes:

1. **Health and Wellbeing** (including statutory Health Education)
2. **Relationships** (including statutory Relationships Education)
3. **Living in the Wider World** (Economic wellbeing and being a responsible citizen)

This structure reflects Bernard Crick's influential conception of citizenship education, outlined in the landmark 'Crick Report' (Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools, 1998), which emphasised social and moral responsibility, community involvement, and political literacy as interconnected dimensions.

### Statutory Relationships Education at Key Stage 2

Under the statutory guidance, pupils must be taught about:

**Families and people who care for me:**
- Different types of families including single parents, same-sex parents, extended families, and blended families
- The characteristics of healthy family life, including commitment and love
- How to recognise if family relationships are making them feel unhappy or unsafe and how to seek help

**Caring friendships:**
- How important friendships are in making us feel happy and secure
- The characteristics of friendships including mutual respect, truthfulness, loyalty, and kindness
- How to recognise who to trust and who not to trust
- How to judge when a friendship is making them unhappy or uncomfortable
- Managing conflict and reconciliation in friendships

**Respectful relationships:**
- The importance of respecting others even when they are different
- Practical steps they can take to combat stereotypes
- The importance of permission-seeking and giving in relationships
- That in school and wider society they can expect to be treated with respect

**Online relationships:**
- That people sometimes behave differently online including pretending to be someone they are not
- Rules and principles for keeping safe online
- How to critically consider online friendships and sources
- The risks of excessive screen time
- How to report concerns about content or contact online

**Being safe:**
- What sorts of boundaries are appropriate in friendships and with others
- About the concept of privacy and the implications of it for both children and adults
- That each person's body belongs to them and the differences between appropriate and inappropriate physical contact
- How to respond safely and appropriately to adults they may encounter whom they do not know
- How to recognise and report feelings of being unsafe or feeling bad about any adult
- How to ask for advice or help and whom to ask

This statutory content represents a significant strengthening of safeguarding education, responding to increased awareness of child sexual exploitation, online grooming, and the need for age-appropriate understanding of consent and bodily autonomy.

### Statutory Health Education at Key Stage 2

The Health Education requirements include:

**Mental wellbeing:**
- That mental wellbeing is a normal part of daily life in the same way as physical health
- Simple self-care techniques, including the importance of rest, time spent with friends and family, and the benefits of hobbies and interests
- How to recognise and talk about their emotions using accurate vocabulary
- That there is a normal range of emotions (e.g., happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, nervousness)
- How to judge whether what they are feeling and how they are behaving is appropriate and proportionate
- The benefits of physical exercise, time outdoors, community participation, and voluntary service
- Where and how to seek support including whom in school they should speak to

**Internet safety and harms:**
- The rules and principles for keeping safe online
- How to recognise harmful content and contact
- How to report concerns

**Physical health and fitness:**
- The characteristics and mental and physical benefits of an active lifestyle
- The importance of building regular exercise into daily and weekly routines
- The risks associated with an inactive lifestyle including obesity

**Healthy eating:**
- What constitutes a healthy diet including understanding calories and other nutritional content
- The principles of planning and preparing a range of healthy meals
- The characteristics of a poor diet and risks associated with unhealthy eating including obesity and tooth decay

**Drugs, alcohol and tobacco:**
- The facts about legal and illegal harmful substances and associated risks including smoking, alcohol use and drug-taking

**Health and prevention:**
- How to recognise early signs of physical illness such as weight loss or unexplained changes to the body
- About safe and unsafe exposure to the sun and how to reduce the risk of sun damage including skin cancer
- The importance of sufficient good quality sleep for good health
- About dental health and the benefits of good oral hygiene and regular visits to the dentist
- About personal hygiene and germs including bacteria and viruses, how they are spread and treated, and the importance of handwashing

**Basic first aid:**
- How to make a clear and efficient call to emergency services
- Concepts of basic first aid including dealing with common injuries including head injuries

**Changing adolescent body:**
- Key facts about puberty and the changing adolescent body, particularly from age 9 through to age 11, including physical and emotional changes
- About menstrual wellbeing including the key facts about the menstrual cycle

Jenny Mosley's 'Circle Time' approach, widely adopted in UK primary schools since the 1990s, provides an effective pedagogical framework for discussing these sensitive topics. Her emphasis on creating a safe, inclusive environment where all children can express feelings and concerns aligns well with the statutory requirements' emphasis on building confidence in seeking support.

### Non-Statutory PSHE Content at Key Stage 2

Beyond the statutory requirements, the PSHE Association's Programme of Study (2020 edition) recommends additional content across Key Stage 2:

**Economic wellbeing and financial capability:**
- Understanding that people make different choices about spending and saving money
- Recognising that people have different attitudes towards saving and spending
- Understanding the role money plays in their lives including how to manage it
- About enterprise and the skills that make someone 'entrepreneurial'
- Understanding career stereotypes and challenging them
- Beginning to understand the range of jobs carried out by people they know

**Living in the wider world - rights and responsibilities:**
- Understanding that they belong to different groups and communities
- Understanding the value of rules and laws
- Recognising the nature and consequences of discrimination, teasing, bullying and aggressive behaviours
- Understanding democratic processes and how they can participate (building on Crick's political literacy strand)
- Learning about human rights including the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child

**Environmental responsibility:**
- Understanding that resources can be allocated in different ways and that these choices affect individuals, communities and the environment
- About the relationship between rights and responsibilities regarding the environment

## Pedagogical Approaches for Key Stage 2

### Age-Appropriate Teaching Methods

Research by the PSHE Association and academics including Lee Jerome (London Metropolitan University, now at Middlesex University) and Jonathan Charlesworth has emphasised the importance of active, participatory teaching methods in PSHE. Jerome's work on citizenship education pedagogies (2012) highlights that discussion, enquiry, and participation are more effective than didactic approaches for developing critical thinking and values formation.

Effective pedagogies for Key Stage 2 include:

**Circle Time:** Jenny Mosley's structured approach provides regular opportunities for discussion, active listening, and problem-solving. The 'golden rules' and 'circle time' structure create predictability and safety essential for discussing sensitive issues.

**Philosophy for Children (P4C):** Sara Stanley and Robert Fisher's work demonstrates how philosophical enquiry develops reasoning skills whilst exploring moral and social questions appropriate to PSHE themes.

**Role-play and drama:** Particularly effective for exploring relationships, emotions, and decision-making scenarios. Nick Boddington's work emphasises how drama techniques enable pupils to 'step into' different perspectives safely.

**Case studies and scenarios:** Age-appropriate dilemmas enable pupils to apply learning to realistic situations whilst maintaining appropriate distance from personal circumstances.

**Distancing techniques:** When addressing sensitive issues, including abuse or family difficulties, using stories, scenarios about other children, or third-person approaches prevents inappropriate disclosure whilst ensuring children learn recognition and reporting strategies.

### Spiral Curriculum Design

Key Stage 2 PSHE should follow a spiral curriculum model where themes are revisited with increasing depth and sophistication across the four years. For example:

**Year 3:** Understanding basic emotions and simple self-regulation strategies; recognising different family structures; understanding the concept of rules.

**Year 4:** Developing more sophisticated emotional vocabulary; understanding empathy; exploring wider community beyond family; understanding rights and responsibilities.

**Year 5:** Managing more complex emotions including dealing with change and loss; understanding peer pressure; exploring democracy and decision-making; developing critical thinking about online content.

**Year 6:** Consolidating emotional resilience strategies; preparing for transition to secondary school; understanding puberty comprehensively; developing financial literacy basics; understanding global citizenship concepts.

This progression ensures age-appropriate content whilst building cumulative understanding, as recommended in the PSHE Association's Key Stage planning guidance.

## Assessment in Key Stage 2 PSHE

### Non-Formal Assessment Approaches

Unlike statutory curriculum subjects, PSHE assessment focuses on evidencing pupil learning rather than measuring attainment levels. The PSHE Association's guidance (2018) recommends:

**Baseline and endpoint assessment:** Establishing what pupils know at the start of a unit and evidencing progression by the end.

**Self and peer assessment:** Developing pupils' capacity to reflect on their own learning and personal development.

**Teacher observation:** Noting contributions in discussions, participation in activities, and application of learning.

**Portfolio evidence:** Collecting samples of work that demonstrate understanding without requiring extensive written responses (important given the age range and varying literacy levels).

### Avoiding Common Pitfalls

Research by Ofsted ('Not Yet Good Enough', 2013) and subsequent PSHE subject reports identified common weaknesses in assessment:

- Attempting to grade pupils' personal qualities, values or attitudes (inappropriate and potentially harmful)
- Over-reliance on written work unsuitable for younger or less literate pupils
- Failure to recognise progression in knowledge and skills
- Insufficient checking of understanding of key concepts

Best practice involves assessing knowledge and understanding, not personal circumstances or values, and recognising that PSHE aims to equip pupils with skills for future application rather than demonstrate immediate behavioural change.

## Safeguarding Considerations

### Creating a Safe Learning Environment

PSHE at Key Stage 2 necessarily addresses sensitive topics including abuse, family difficulties, and personal safety. Teachers must establish clear ground rules, including:

- Using correct terminology for body parts to enable accurate reporting
- Establishing that no one will be pressured to share personal information
- Clarifying routes for one-to-one discussion after lessons
- Ensuring designated safeguarding leads are informed about PSHE content in advance

### Responding to Disclosures

The statutory guidance emphasises that teaching about keeping safe may lead to disclosures. All teachers delivering PSHE must understand safeguarding procedures as outlined in 'Keeping Children Safe in Education' (DfE, 2023). This includes:

- Recognising signs of abuse and neglect
- Understanding how to record and report concerns immediately
- Never promising confidentiality when safeguarding concerns arise
- Following local safeguarding partnership procedures

### Managing Sensitive Topics

When addressing potentially sensitive content such as different family structures, bereavement, or keeping safe, teachers should:

- Inform parents about upcoming units through newsletters or curriculum information
- Use distancing techniques (stories, scenarios) rather than asking about personal circumstances
- Provide opportunities for private questions through question boxes
- Ensure pastoral support is available
- Consider individual pupil circumstances when planning (recent bereavement, known safeguarding concerns, etc.)

## Cross-Curricular Links and Whole-School Approaches

### Integration with Other Subjects

Effective PSHE builds on and reinforces learning across the curriculum:

**English:** Exploring texts addressing PSHE themes; developing vocabulary for emotions and relationships; debate and discussion skills.

**Science:** The statutory Science curriculum at Key Stage 2 includes 'Animals including humans' addressing basic body parts, nutrition, and skeletons/muscles. Schools must ensure Science and Health Education complement each other, particularly regarding puberty education where Science addresses physical changes and PSHE addresses emotional and social aspects.

**Computing:** Digital literacy and online safety form part of both Computing and PSHE curricula. The DfE guidance 'Teaching Online Safety in School' (2019) emphasises integrated approaches.

**PE:** Physical wellbeing, team work, resilience, and managing competition connect directly to PSHE themes.

**RE:** Exploring values, beliefs, and worldviews provides context for understanding diversity and respect in relationships education.

### Whole-School Ethos

Research by the PSHE Association emphasises that effective PSHE requires consistency between taught curriculum and whole-school practice. This includes:

**Behaviour policies:** That promote positive relationships, restorative approaches, and clear responses to bullying and discrimination.

**Pupil voice mechanisms:** Including school councils, pupil surveys, and consultation on school decisions (connecting to democratic participation learning).

**Pastoral systems:** That enable pupils to seek support confidently, including worry boxes, peer mentoring schemes, and accessible trusted adults.

**Staff modelling:** Adults demonstrating respectful relationships, emotional literacy, and healthy behaviours.

Jenny Mosley's 'Quality Circle Time Model' extends beyond classroom circle time to encompass whole-school values and systems, creating consistency between what is taught and what is modelled.

## Working with Parents and Carers

### Communication and Consultation

The statutory guidance requires schools to consult with parents when developing their Relationships and Health Education policies. Effective practice includes:

- Clear information about what will be taught and when
- Opportunities to view resources
- Parent workshops exploring content and pedagogies
- Addressing concerns and misconceptions
- Regular curriculum information through newsletters, websites, and communications

### The Right to Withdraw

Importantly, there is **no right to withdraw** from Relationships Education or Health Education at primary level. This differs from secondary RSE where parents may withdraw pupils from Sex Education content beyond the statutory requirements. The DfE guidance clarifies that primary-level content about changing bodies and reproduction forms part of either statutory Health Education or Science, from which withdrawal is not permitted.

Schools should help parents understand:
- What is age-appropriate and why it is taught
- How sensitive topics are handled
- The safeguarding importance of this education
- That content is factual, not promoting particular lifestyles

Research by the National Children's Bureau and Sex Education Forum consistently demonstrates that age-appropriate relationships and health education does not accelerate sexual activity and actually contributes to safeguarding and wellbeing.

## Inclusion and Differentiation

### Meeting Diverse Needs

PSHE at Key Stage 2 must be accessible to all pupils, including those with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND). The Equality Act 2010 requires schools to ensure pupils with protected characteristics are not discriminated against, including in curriculum access.

**For pupils with SEND:**
- Visual supports, concrete resources, and multi-sensory approaches
- Additional processing time and simplified language where appropriate
- Small group or individual pre-teaching of key concepts
- Careful matching of scenarios and examples to comprehension levels
- Working with families and specialists to identify appropriate learning outcomes

**For pupils with different cultural or religious backgrounds:**
- Acknowledging diverse family structures, beliefs, and practices whilst delivering statutory content
- Explaining the distinction between learning about different relationships and choices, and promoting specific lifestyles
- Engaging sensitively with concerns whilst maintaining statutory requirements
- Using diverse, inclusive resources reflecting British society's diversity

### Challenging Stereotypes

The statutory guidance explicitly requires teaching that challenges stereotypes. This includes:

- Presenting diverse family structures, including same-sex parents, as equally valid
- Challenging gender stereotypes in relationships, careers, and behaviours
- Addressing racial, religious, and cultural stereotypes
- Exploring disability and difference positively
- Presenting diverse role models across all areas

Nick Boddington's work on inclusive PSHE emphasises that representation matters: resources, examples, and scenarios should reflect the diversity of British society to ensure all pupils see themselves represented whilst learning about others.

## Resources and Quality Assurance

### Selecting Appropriate Resources

The PSHE Association provides a Quality Assurance scheme for PSHE resources, endorsed by the Department for Education. Resources displaying the QA badge have been assessed against criteria including:

- Age-appropriateness
- Accuracy of information
- Teaching and learning approaches
- Inclusion and diversity
- Safeguarding considerations
