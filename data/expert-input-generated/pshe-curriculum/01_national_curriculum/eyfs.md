# PSED in EYFS

## Overview and Statutory Framework Context

Personal, Social and Emotional Development (PSED) is one of the three prime areas in the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) framework, alongside Communication and Language, and Physical Development. The statutory EYFS framework (Department for Education, 2021, revised 2024) recognises PSED as fundamental to children's development and future learning, establishing it as a crucial foundation for what later becomes PSHE & Citizenship education in Key Stages 1-4.

The prime areas are so called because they are the foundation for children's development in all other areas. PSED is particularly significant as it underpins children's ability to form relationships, develop self-awareness, manage emotions, and engage with learning. Jenny Mosley's pioneering work on circle time and whole-school approaches to emotional literacy has significantly influenced EYFS practice, emphasising the importance of creating nurturing environments where children feel valued and secure.

## The Three Aspects of PSED

The EYFS framework divides PSED into three interconnected aspects:

### Self-Regulation

Self-regulation involves children developing the ability to manage their feelings, behaviour, and attention. This aspect focuses on:

- Understanding and managing emotions appropriately
- Developing concentration and perseverance
- Following rules and understanding their importance
- Being able to wait and take turns
- Recovering from upsets and adapting behaviour to different situations

The Early Learning Goal states: "Children at the expected level of development will show an understanding of their own feelings and those of others, and begin to regulate their behaviour accordingly. Set and work towards simple goals, being able to wait for what they want and control their immediate impulses when appropriate. Give focused attention to what the teacher says, responding appropriately even when engaged in activity, and show an ability to follow instructions involving several ideas or actions."

Research by the Education Endowment Foundation (2018) demonstrates that self-regulation skills developed in EYFS are strong predictors of academic attainment and social-emotional wellbeing throughout schooling. Practitioners should provide explicit teaching of emotional vocabulary, model self-regulation strategies, and create predictable routines that help children feel secure.

### Managing Self

This aspect centres on children developing independence, resilience, and a positive sense of self. Key elements include:

- Building confidence in own abilities
- Developing independence in self-care (toileting, dressing, hygiene)
- Understanding the importance of healthy eating, exercise, and sleep
- Showing resilience and perseverance when facing challenges
- Being able to express needs and ask for help when needed

The Early Learning Goal states: "Children at the expected level of development will be confident to try new activities and show independence, resilience and perseverance in the face of challenge. Explain the reasons for rules, know right from wrong and try to behave accordingly. Manage their own basic hygiene and personal needs, including dressing, going to the toilet and understanding the importance of healthy food choices."

This aspect directly connects to later PSHE education themes around health and wellbeing. The framework's emphasis on healthy choices aligns with the Association for PSHE Education's programme of study, establishing early foundations for understanding physical and mental health.

### Building Relationships

Building relationships encompasses children's ability to form positive attachments with adults and peers, showing empathy and cooperation. This includes:

- Forming positive attachments to familiar adults
- Playing cooperatively with peers
- Showing sensitivity to others' feelings and needs
- Developing friendships
- Resolving conflicts with adult support
- Working collaboratively towards shared goals

The Early Learning Goal states: "Children at the expected level of development will work and play cooperatively and take turns with others. Form positive attachments to adults and friendships with peers. Show sensitivity to their own and to others' needs."

This aspect establishes foundations for Bernard Crick's conception of citizenship education, which emphasises social and moral responsibility alongside community involvement. Even in EYFS, children begin developing the relational skills essential for active citizenship.

## Links to Later PSHE & Citizenship Education

### Foundation for PSHE Themes

PSED directly underpins the three core themes identified by the Association for PSHE Education (2020 Programme of Study):

**Health and Wellbeing**: EYFS children learning about healthy eating, physical activity, and basic hygiene establish foundations for understanding physical health and mental wellbeing. The self-regulation aspect particularly connects to later learning about managing difficult emotions and seeking support.

**Relationships**: The building relationships aspect of PSED flows naturally into KS1 and KS2 Relationships Education (statutory from 2020 under the Children and Young People Act 2017, implemented 2020). Early learning about friendship, cooperation, and respecting differences establishes groundwork for understanding healthy relationships, consent concepts (age-appropriate), and diversity.

**Living in the Wider World**: Managing self and understanding rules connect to later citizenship education and economic wellbeing themes. Nick Boddington's work on primary citizenship emphasises that even young children can understand concepts of fairness, rules, and belonging to communities.

### Progression into Key Stage 1

The transition from EYFS PSED to KS1 PSHE should be seamless and progressive. Lee Jerome and colleagues (2015) in their research on citizenship education progression emphasise the importance of recognising children's prior learning. Reception teachers should share PSED assessments with Year 1 colleagues to ensure continuity.

In Year 1, children build upon EYFS foundations by:
- Extending emotional literacy vocabulary
- Understanding more complex social situations
- Developing greater independence in resolving conflicts
- Beginning to understand their role in wider communities beyond the immediate setting

## Developmental Approaches and Pedagogy

### Creating the Enabling Environment

The EYFS framework emphasises learning through play and enabling environments. For PSED, this means creating spaces and opportunities where children can:

**Practise social skills**: Role-play areas, small world play, and collaborative construction activities allow children to negotiate, share, and cooperate. Jenny Mosley's quality circle time approach, adapted for EYFS, provides structured opportunities for children to practise speaking and listening within supportive group contexts.

**Experience managed risk**: Forest School approaches and adventurous physical play opportunities help children develop resilience, assess risks, and recover from minor setbacks. Research by Gill (2007) and subsequent studies demonstrate that managed risk-taking in early years contributes to emotional resilience and self-regulation.

**Access emotional support**: Designated key persons, cosy spaces for emotional regulation, and visual emotion resources (such as zones of regulation or emotion faces) help children recognise and manage feelings.

### Adult-Child Interactions

High-quality adult-child interactions are fundamental to PSED development. The Effective Provision of Pre-School Education (EPPE) project (Sylva et al., 2004) and subsequent EPPSE longitudinal study identified 'sustained shared thinking' as crucial for cognitive and social-emotional development. For PSED specifically, practitioners should:

- **Model emotional literacy**: Name emotions explicitly and model appropriate responses
- **Provide scaffolded support**: Step back as children develop competence in managing situations independently
- **Offer specific praise**: Focus on effort and process rather than only outcomes to build growth mindset
- **Co-regulate before expecting self-regulation**: Recognise that young children need adult co-regulation support before developing independent self-regulation

### Observation and Assessment

The EYFS framework (2024) emphasises formative assessment through ongoing observation. For PSED, practitioners should:

- Observe children across contexts (child-initiated play, adult-led activities, routines, outdoor learning)
- Note patterns in behaviour rather than isolated incidents
- Consider cultural contexts when interpreting behaviour
- Use observations to plan next steps tailored to individual needs
- Complete the Reception Baseline Assessment (RBA), which includes elements of PSED
- Conduct the statutory EYFS Profile at end of Reception year

The EYFS Profile judgment for PSED requires teachers to assess whether children have met the Early Learning Goals across all three aspects. This provides Year 1 teachers with essential information for planning PSHE provision.

## Supporting Children with Additional Needs

### SEND Considerations

Children with special educational needs and disabilities may require differentiated support for PSED development. Common considerations include:

**Autism Spectrum Condition**: Children may need explicit teaching of social communication skills, visual supports for emotional regulation, and adjustments to sensory environments. The National Autistic Society's resources for early years settings emphasise predictable routines and clear visual structures.

**Speech, Language and Communication Needs**: PSED and communication development are deeply interconnected. Children with SLCN may struggle to express emotions verbally and require alternative communication methods (Makaton, visual aids, communication books).

**Social, Emotional and Mental Health (SEMH) needs**: Some children experience significant difficulties with emotional regulation and relationships. Trauma-informed approaches, as outlined by the Anna Freud Centre's Early Years resources, emphasise safety, consistency, and attunement to individual needs.

### Disadvantage and Vulnerable Groups

Research consistently shows that children experiencing disadvantage or adversity face greater challenges in PSED development. The Education Endowment Foundation's work on social and emotional learning identifies effective interventions:

- Small group targeted support (e.g., nurture groups)
- Explicit teaching of social-emotional skills
- Strong partnerships with families
- Attention to attachment and relationships

Pupil Premium funding can support enhanced PSED provision in EYFS settings attached to maintained schools and academies.

## Partnership with Parents and Carers

### Home-School Connections

The EYFS framework emphasises partnership with parents as fundamental. For PSED specifically:

- Share observations of children's emotional and social development sensitively
- Provide practical strategies parents can use at home (consistent approaches to behaviour, emotional coaching techniques)
- Recognise cultural variations in child-rearing approaches whilst maintaining safeguarding standards
- Invite parent/carer contributions to understanding their child's needs and strengths

Jenny Mosley's work emphasises the importance of whole-community approaches to emotional literacy, extending beyond the setting into families and communities.

### Two-Way Information Flow

Effective partnership requires information sharing in both directions:

- **From home to setting**: Understanding family contexts, cultural practices, significant events affecting the child
- **From setting to home**: Sharing what children are learning, how parents can support, celebrating progress

The Department for Education's statutory guidance 'Working Together to Safeguard Children' (2023) emphasises that strong relationships with parents support both PSED development and safeguarding.

## Safeguarding Dimensions

PSED has crucial safeguarding implications. Children who develop:

- **Strong self-awareness and emotional literacy**: Can better recognise when something feels wrong and express concerns
- **Understanding of appropriate/inappropriate behaviour**: Have foundations for understanding safe/unsafe situations
- **Confidence to speak to trusted adults**: Are more likely to disclose concerns
- **Healthy relationships skills**: Can recognise unhealthy relationship patterns

'Keeping Children Safe in Education' (DfE, 2024) emphasises that safeguarding is everyone's responsibility. EYFS practitioners should recognise that PSED teaching contributes to children's resilience and safety.

## Behaviour and British Values

### Behaviour Management Approaches

Effective behaviour management in EYFS supports PSED development rather than simply controlling children. Research-informed approaches include:

**Positive Behaviour Support**: Focus on teaching desired behaviours explicitly rather than only responding to unwanted behaviours. The Behaviour Hubs programme and evidence from Education Endowment Foundation emphasise:
- Clear, consistent expectations taught explicitly
- Specific positive reinforcement
- Addressing underlying needs driving behaviour
- Restorative approaches when harm occurs

**Emotion Coaching**: Based on John Gottman's research and adapted for education settings, emotion coaching involves recognising emotional moments as opportunities for connection and teaching. Steps include:
- Recognising the child's emotion
- Validating the feeling whilst setting limits on behaviour
- Problem-solving together

### Fundamental British Values in EYFS

The Prevent Duty (2015) requires all settings, including EYFS providers, to promote fundamental British values. In EYFS, this is embedded naturally within PSED:

- **Democracy**: Giving children choices, involving them in simple decisions about play and routines, taking turns to speak and listen
- **Rule of Law**: Understanding that rules help everyone feel safe and happy, developing understanding of right and wrong
- **Individual Liberty**: Making choices, expressing preferences, developing independence
- **Mutual Respect and Tolerance**: Respecting differences, learning about diverse families and communities, challenging stereotypes

Lee Jerome's work on political literacy and citizenship education emphasises that even young children can engage with these concepts through age-appropriate pedagogy focused on experiences rather than abstract concepts.

## Monitoring and Quality Assurance

### Ofsted Expectations

Under the Education Inspection Framework (2019, updated 2024), Ofsted inspects EYFS provision through the Quality of Education, Behaviour and Attitudes, Personal Development, and Leadership and Management judgements. PSED is particularly relevant to:

**Personal Development**: Inspectors evaluate how settings promote children's character development, resilience, confidence, and independence. Strong PSED provision contributes significantly to this judgement.

**Behaviour and Attitudes**: Self-regulation and relationship-building aspects of PSED directly relate to behaviour expectations and children's attitudes to learning.

The Early Years Inspection Handbook emphasises that inspectors observe the curriculum implementation across all areas of learning, expecting to see PSED integrated throughout the day rather than delivered in isolation.

### Quality Improvement

Settings should have clear processes for monitoring and improving PSED provision:

- Regular review of PSED data from observations and assessments
- Professional development for staff on emotional literacy, behaviour support, and relationship-building
- Seeking external support where patterns of concern emerge (Educational Psychology, Early Help services)
- Engaging with quality improvement programmes such as the National Day Nurseries Association's quality improvement schemes or Local Authority QA processes

## Resources and Training

### Evidence-Based Programmes

Several structured programmes support PSED development in EYFS:

**Incredible Years**: Evidence-based programme originally developed for parents, with versions adapted for early years settings. Focuses on emotional regulation, social skills, and problem-solving. Endorsed by Early Intervention Foundation as having strong evidence base.

**Zones of Regulation**: Framework for developing self-regulation and emotional control, using colour-coded zones. Widely used in EYFS settings, though originally designed for older children.

**PATHS (Promoting Alternative Thinking Strategies)**: Early years version available. Teaches emotional literacy, self-control, and social competence through explicit lessons and reinforcement throughout the day.

### Professional Development

Practitioners should access ongoing professional development in:

- Attachment theory and its implications for practice
- Trauma-informed approaches
- Child development theory (particularly emotional and social development)
- Observation and assessment techniques
- Supporting children with SEND in PSED development

Early Years Alliance, National Day Nurseries Association, and PACEY offer training programmes specifically focused on PSED and emotional wellbeing.

## Future Directions and Current Debates

### Screen Time and Technology

Emerging research on screen time and young children's social-emotional development presents challenges for practitioners. While technology is part of modern life, excessive screen use may impact:
- Face-to-face social interaction opportunities
- Attention span development
- Physical activity levels
- Sleep patterns

Balanced approaches ensure technology enhances rather than replaces human interaction and active play essential for PSED.

### Mental Health Awareness

Increasing recognition of children's mental health needs influences EYFS PSED approaches. The Anna Freud Centre and Place2Be have developed early years mental health resources emphasising:
- Recognition that mental health begins in early childhood
- Preventative approaches through strong PSED foundations
- Early identification and intervention when concerns arise
- Whole-setting approaches to wellbeing

### Cultural Responsiveness

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on citizenship education and diversity emphasises the importance of culturally responsive teaching. In EYFS PSED, this means:
- Recognising that cultural backgrounds influence emotional expression, relationship patterns, and child-rearing practices
- Valuing diverse family structures and cultural practices
- Challenging assumptions about 'normal' development based on Western, middle-class norms
- Addressing bias and stereotyping in resources and interactions

## Conclusion

PSED in EYFS establishes crucial foundations for children's lifelong personal, social and emotional wellbeing, and for their later learning in PSHE and Citizenship education. High-quality PSED provision requires:
- Understanding of child development and attachment
- Skilful adult-child interactions that support co-regulation and sustained shared thinking
- Enabling environments that provide opportunities for relationship-building, independence, and emotional expression
- Partnership with families respecting diverse cultural contexts
- Integration of PSED across all aspects of the EYFS curriculum rather than viewing it in isolation

As children transition from EYFS to Key Stage 1, the foundations established in PSED enable them to engage with increasingly sophisticated PSHE education content, developing the knowledge, skills and attributes needed to thrive as individuals and citizens. Practitioners, curriculum leaders and policymakers must continue to prioritise this prime area of learning, recognising its fundamental importance for children's present wellbeing and future flourishing.
