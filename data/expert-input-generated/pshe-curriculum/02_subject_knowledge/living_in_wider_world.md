# Living in the Wider World

## Overview and Statutory Context

'Living in the Wider World' forms one of three core strands within PSHE education in England, alongside Health and Wellbeing and Relationships. This strand encompasses economic wellbeing, careers education, and understanding of citizenship rights and responsibilities. The Department for Education's statutory guidance on Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education (2020) identifies this as a crucial component of preparing young people for adult life.

The PSHE Association programme of study (2020) defines Living in the Wider World as covering: "the knowledge, skills and attributes pupils need to succeed in education, training and employment; to understand and participate in the economy; and to live fulfilled lives, making a positive contribution to society."

Bernard Crick's influential *Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools* (1998) established foundational principles for citizenship education, arguing that active citizenship requires "social and moral responsibility, community involvement and political literacy." These remain central to contemporary curriculum design.

## Key Components and Learning Areas

### Economic Wellbeing and Financial Capability

Economic wellbeing education addresses pupils' understanding of money management, employment, and economic systems. The PSHE Association framework recommends age-appropriate progression:

**Key Stage 1 and 2:** Foundation concepts include understanding where money comes from, basic saving and spending decisions, and recognising different jobs in the community. The Money and Pensions Service (formerly Money Advice Service) provides the UK Strategy for Financial Wellbeing, which emphasises early intervention.

**Key Stage 3:** Learning advances to budgeting, understanding wage slips, banking systems, and consumer rights. Pupils explore income and expenditure, the purposes of taxation, and different payment methods. The Personal Finance Education Group (pfeg) research demonstrates that financial capability education is most effective when contextualised within real-world scenarios.

**Key Stage 4:** Content encompasses understanding payslips, income tax and National Insurance, pensions, credit and debt management, and different financial products. The Young Money Financial Education Framework provides detailed learning outcomes for ages 3-18.

### Careers Education, Information, Advice and Guidance (CEIAG)

The Gatsby Benchmarks (2014) establish eight standards for good careers guidance, providing the definitive framework for careers education. These benchmarks, developed by Sir John Holman, are embedded in statutory guidance through the Department for Education's *Careers guidance and access for education and training providers* (2018, updated 2023).

**Statutory Requirements:** All maintained schools and academies must provide independent careers guidance from Years 8-13. The Education Act 2011 (sections 29 and 42A) and the Technical and Further Education Act 2017 establish these legal obligations.

**Key Stage 2:** Career-related learning focuses on recognising different jobs, understanding skills and interests, and beginning to consider aspirations. Jenny Mosley's 'Circle Time' approach provides pedagogical methods for exploring occupations and ambitions through discussion-based learning.

**Key Stage 3-4:** Students explore labour market information, career pathways, qualifications, and progression routes. They develop employability skills including communication, teamwork, and problem-solving. Research by Professor Anthony Mann (Education and Employers Research) demonstrates that encounters with employers and employees significantly improve career readiness.

### Rights and Responsibilities as Citizens

Lee Jerome and Jonathan Charlesworth's work on citizenship education emphasises that effective teaching must balance knowledge of democratic systems with opportunities for participation and action. Their research, published in the *Citizenship Teaching and Learning* journal, demonstrates that students develop deeper civic understanding through experiential learning.

**Key Stage 1-2:** Foundation citizenship includes understanding rules, rights and responsibilities in different contexts, diversity in the community, and how people help one another. The United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) provides an accessible framework for teaching about rights.

**Key Stage 3:** Learning encompasses the British political system, parliamentary democracy, electoral systems, and the operation of local and national government. The UK Parliament's Education Service provides authoritative resources aligned with National Curriculum requirements.

Nick Boddington's *Citizenship, A Very Short Introduction* (2016) offers accessible explanations of citizenship concepts including democracy, justice, and rights. His work emphasises that citizenship is both a status and a practice, requiring both knowledge and active engagement.

**Key Stage 4:** Students develop sophisticated understanding of democratic systems, voting systems, the role of the media, and how citizens can effect change. This includes understanding diverse political perspectives and the operation of the UK constitution.

### Critical Thinking and Media Literacy

Contemporary citizenship education must address digital literacy and critical evaluation of information sources. The Department for Education's guidance on *Teaching Online Safety in Schools* (2019) emphasises this as integral to Living in the Wider World.

Students must learn to identify misinformation, understand how algorithms shape information consumption, and recognise bias in media reporting. Research by the Centre for Information and Digital Literacy at the University of Liverpool demonstrates that explicit teaching of critical thinking skills improves students' ability to evaluate sources.

## Pedagogical Approaches and Classroom Practice

### Active Citizenship Projects

Bernard Crick advocated for experiential learning through community participation. Effective practice includes:

- **Service learning projects:** Connecting curriculum learning with community action
- **Democratic participation:** School councils, student voice initiatives, and participatory decision-making
- **Issue-based enquiry:** Investigating local and global issues through research and discussion

The National Council for Voluntary Organisations (NCVO) provides frameworks for school-community partnerships that enable authentic citizenship experiences.

### Discussion-Based Learning

Lee Jerome's research demonstrates that structured discussion using controversial issues develops political literacy and critical thinking. The Controversial Issues Framework, developed by Lee Jerome and others at the Association for Citizenship Teaching (ACT), provides guidance for teachers navigating sensitive topics.

Effective discussion-based approaches include:
- **Philosophy for Children (P4C):** Structured enquiry developing reasoning skills
- **Debate and deliberation:** Formal exploration of different perspectives
- **Democratic ground rules:** Student-created frameworks for respectful dialogue

### Career Learning Activities

The Career Development Institute's framework for career development learning identifies six career learning areas: self-development, career exploration, career management, awareness of opportunity, transition learning, and resilience.

Practical activities include:
- **Employer encounters:** Workplace visits, speakers, and mentoring (Gatsby Benchmark 5)
- **Labour market information:** Analysis of career pathways and progression routes
- **Experiential learning:** Work experience, enterprise activities, and volunteering

## Assessment Approaches

Living in the Wider World presents particular assessment challenges as outcomes include values, attitudes, and participatory skills alongside knowledge. The PSHE Association's *Assessment in PSHE Education* guidance (2018) recommends:

**Baseline and Endpoint Assessment:** Identifying starting points and progress over time rather than attainment against fixed standards.

**Knowledge Assessment:** Testing understanding of concepts including rights, responsibilities, financial systems, and democratic processes.

**Skills Assessment:** Evaluating competencies in critical thinking, decision-making, communication, and enquiry.

**Reflective Assessment:** Student self-evaluation of their developing capabilities and understanding.

### Examination Specifications

Several examination boards offer qualifications incorporating Living in the Wider World content:

**AQA:** GCSE Citizenship Studies (8100) includes sections on 'Rights and responsibilities' and 'Politics and participation'. The specification requires understanding of UK governance, legal systems, and active citizenship.

**OCR:** GCSE Citizenship Studies (J270) covers 'Citizenship in action' and 'Our rights, our society', examining democratic systems and how citizens effect change.

**Edexcel:** GCSE Citizenship Studies (1CS0) includes 'Theme A: Living together in the UK' and 'Theme B: Democracy at work in the UK', addressing community cohesion, diversity, and democratic participation.

**WJEC Eduqas:** GCSE Citizenship Studies includes Welsh, UK and international contexts, addressing devolution and national identity alongside core citizenship concepts.

These qualifications typically assess through written examinations and coursework involving citizenship action projects.

## Cross-Curricular Connections

Living in the Wider World connects extensively with other curriculum areas:

**History:** Understanding democratic development, civil rights movements, and constitutional change provides essential context for contemporary citizenship.

**Geography:** Study of development, sustainability, and global interdependence connects with citizenship understanding of global issues and responsibilities.

**English:** Oracy, rhetoric, and persuasive writing develop communication skills essential for active citizenship.

**Mathematics:** Financial literacy requires numeracy skills including percentages, ratios, and data interpretation.

**Computing:** Digital literacy and online safety form crucial components of modern citizenship.

## Current Debates and Developments

### Political Literacy and Impartiality

The Education Act 1996 (Section 406-407) requires schools to ensure balanced treatment of political issues. However, Lee Jerome and Jonathan Charlesworth's research highlights tensions between political impartiality and addressing issues of social justice. Their work suggests effective citizenship education requires exploring controversial issues whilst ensuring students encounter multiple perspectives.

### Financial Education Priorities

Following the 2008 financial crisis and subsequent research demonstrating low financial capability amongst UK adults, financial education has gained prominence. The Money and Pensions Service's UK Strategy for Financial Wellbeing (2020-2030) emphasises school-based financial education as crucial for improving financial outcomes.

### Preparing for Changing Work Patterns

Research by the Institute for the Future of Work highlights that young people will navigate multiple career changes, requiring adaptive career management skills. Professor Tristram Hooley's work on career education emphasises developing 'career adaptability' rather than linear career planning.

### Digital Citizenship

The rapid evolution of digital technology necessitates ongoing curriculum development. The UK Council for Internet Safety provides guidance on online rights, responsibilities, and digital wellbeing as core citizenship competencies.

## Quality Assurance and Support Organisations

**PSHE Association:** Provides the authoritative programme of study, quality assurance mark for resources, and training for teachers. Their guidance represents sector consensus on effective practice.

**Association for Citizenship Teaching (ACT):** Offers specialist subject expertise, publications, and professional development for citizenship educators.

**The Careers & Enterprise Company:** Coordinates careers education support through Enterprise Advisers and Career Hubs, facilitating delivery of Gatsby Benchmarks.

**Young Enterprise:** Delivers enterprise education programmes developing financial capability and employability skills through experiential learning.

**UK Parliament Education Service:** Provides authoritative resources and outreach programmes supporting teaching about democracy and political systems.

## Research Evidence and Effectiveness

The Education Endowment Foundation's Teaching and Learning Toolkit suggests that social and emotional learning programmes, which include aspects of Living in the Wider World, show moderate impact (+4 months) on academic outcomes, with additional benefits for citizenship outcomes.

Professor Jan Germen Janmaat's research at the UCL Institute of Education demonstrates positive correlation between citizenship education and civic participation, though effectiveness depends significantly on pedagogical approaches emphasising discussion, participation, and action.

Research by the Jubilee Centre for Character and Virtues at the University of Birmingham demonstrates that character education programmes incorporating civic virtues develop both individual wellbeing and community participation.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

**Specialist Knowledge Requirements:** Teachers delivering Living in the Wider World require secure understanding of economic systems, democratic institutions, and career pathways. The PSHE Association recommends subject-specific CPD, particularly for non-specialist teachers.

**External Partnerships:** Effective delivery requires establishing relationships with employers, community organisations, and democratic institutions. The Gatsby Benchmarks emphasise this partnership approach for careers education.

**Safeguarding Considerations:** Discussion of controversial issues requires careful classroom management. Teachers should establish ground rules, ensure balanced perspectives, and create safe environments for disagreement.

**Resource Currency:** Content relating to employment, financial systems, and political processes requires regular updating to maintain relevance and accuracy.

**Progression Planning:** The PSHE Association's Key Stage frameworks provide detailed progression from foundation concepts through to sophisticated analysis, enabling teachers to plan coherent learning journeys.

## Inclusion and Diversity Considerations

Living in the Wider World content must reflect the diversity of British society and address potential barriers. This includes:

- **Representation:** Ensuring diverse role models in careers education, challenging stereotypes about particular occupations or sectors
- **Accessibility:** Addressing how systems may create barriers for disabled people, ethnic minorities, or other marginalised groups
- **Cultural sensitivity:** Recognising diverse family economic circumstances and avoiding assumptions about resources
- **SEND provision:** Adapting content and pedagogy to ensure students with special educational needs access appropriate careers guidance and citizenship understanding

The Equality Act 2010 and related PSHE Association guidance emphasise these inclusive practice requirements.
