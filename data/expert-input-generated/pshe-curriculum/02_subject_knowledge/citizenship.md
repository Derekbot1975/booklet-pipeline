# Citizenship: Democracy and Law

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Democracy and law form the foundational pillars of Citizenship education in England, as established in the landmark **Crick Report** (1998), formally known as *Education for Citizenship and the Teaching of Democracy in Schools*. Bernard Crick's influential committee established that understanding democratic processes, the rule of law, and legal systems is essential for active and responsible citizenship. This framework continues to underpin the statutory requirements set out in the **National Curriculum for Citizenship** (2014).

The study of democracy and law enables pupils to understand how British democratic institutions function, how laws are made and enforced, and their role as citizens within these systems. As Lee Jerome (2012) argues in *Teaching about Parliament*, effective citizenship education must move beyond descriptive knowledge of institutions towards critical engagement with democratic processes and their limitations.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 3

The **National Curriculum in England: Citizenship Programmes of Study** (2014) requires pupils to be taught about:

- The development of the political system of democratic government in the United Kingdom, including the roles of citizens, Parliament and the monarch
- The operation of Parliament, including voting and elections, and the role of political parties
- The precious liberties enjoyed by citizens of the United Kingdom
- The nature of rules and laws and the justice system, including the role of the police and the operation of courts and tribunals
- The roles played by public institutions and voluntary groups in society
- The different electoral systems used in and beyond the United Kingdom and actions citizens can take in democratic and electoral processes

### Key Stage 4

At Key Stage 4, students deepen their understanding through:

- The legal system in England and Wales, diverse national, regional, religious and ethnic identities in the United Kingdom
- How the United Kingdom government works
- The skills of researching, planning and taking social action in school and/or in the wider community

GCSE Citizenship Studies specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) mandate detailed knowledge of the UK's democratic system, legal framework, and comparative international systems.

## Understanding Democracy

### Theoretical Foundations

Nick Boddington's work on *Developing Citizenship in Schools* (2008) emphasises that democracy education should encompass both procedural understanding (how democracy works) and normative understanding (why democracy matters). Students must grasp:

- **Representative democracy**: The UK's parliamentary system where citizens elect MPs to represent their interests
- **Direct democracy**: Referenda such as the 2016 EU referendum and the 2011 Alternative Vote referendum
- **Liberal democracy**: Protection of individual rights alongside majority rule
- **Pluralism**: Multiple political parties, pressure groups, and diverse viewpoints

### UK Democratic Structures

Teachers should ensure pupils understand the **UK's constitutional framework**:

**Parliament**:
- The House of Commons (650 elected MPs)
- The House of Lords (appointed and hereditary peers, bishops)
- The role of the Speaker and parliamentary procedures
- Legislative process: Green Papers, White Papers, Bills, Royal Assent
- Prime Minister's Questions and select committees

**The Executive**:
- The Prime Minister and Cabinet
- Government departments and the civil service
- The distinction between government and state
- Collective ministerial responsibility

**The Monarchy**:
- Constitutional monarchy and royal prerogative
- The ceremonial and reserve powers of the Crown
- The relationship between monarch and Prime Minister

**Devolution**:
- The Scottish Parliament, Welsh Senedd (formerly Assembly), and Northern Ireland Assembly
- Reserved and devolved powers under the Scotland Act 1998, Government of Wales Act 2006, and Northern Ireland Act 1998
- The **West Lothian Question** and English votes for English laws

### Electoral Systems

Jonathan Charlesworth's research on *Young People's Politics* (2015) emphasises that understanding electoral systems is crucial for meaningful participation. Key systems include:

- **First Past the Post (FPTP)**: Used for Westminster elections, where the candidate with most votes wins
- **Additional Member System (AMS)**: Used for Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd
- **Single Transferable Vote (STV)**: Used in Northern Ireland Assembly elections
- **Supplementary Vote**: Historically used for London Mayor elections (changed to FPTP in 2022)

Students should evaluate advantages and disadvantages: proportionality versus strong government, constituency links versus wasted votes, and the impact on smaller parties.

## The Rule of Law and Legal System

### Foundational Principles

The **rule of law** is a fundamental principle examined by the House of Lords Select Committee on the Constitution (2007), which identified key components:

- Laws apply equally to all citizens, including government officials
- Laws are clear, accessible, and predictable
- Independent judiciary ensures fair application
- Fundamental human rights are protected
- Access to justice is available to all

Bernard Crick argued that understanding the rule of law distinguishes between arbitrary power and legitimate authority, essential for democratic citizenship.

### UK Legal System Structure

**Criminal Law**:
- Offences against the state and society
- Crown Prosecution Service (CPS) role
- Police powers under the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (PACE)
- Youth justice system and age of criminal responsibility (10 in England/Wales)

**Civil Law**:
- Disputes between individuals or organisations
- Contract law, family law, employment law, tort law
- County Courts and High Court
- Alternative dispute resolution (ADR) and mediation

**Court Hierarchy**:
- Magistrates' Courts (lay magistrates and District Judges)
- Crown Court (jury trials for serious criminal cases)
- Court of Appeal (Criminal and Civil Divisions)
- UK Supreme Court (established 2009, replacing Law Lords)
- Relationship with European Court of Human Rights post-Brexit

### Human Rights Framework

The **Human Rights Act 1998** incorporated the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law. Students should understand:

- Absolute rights (e.g., prohibition of torture)
- Limited rights (e.g., right to liberty with lawful exceptions)
- Qualified rights (e.g., freedom of expression balanced against other rights)
- Current debates about potential replacement with a British Bill of Rights

Lee Jerome and Nick Boddington's *Teaching about Rights* (2012) emphasises teaching rights alongside responsibilities, avoiding both entitlement culture and rights dismissal.

## Democratic Participation and Action

### Forms of Participation

Jenny Mosley's **Circle Time** approach (1996, 2005) provides structured opportunities for democratic participation at classroom level, modelling:

- Inclusive decision-making
- Active listening and respectful dialogue
- Collaborative problem-solving
- Democratic values in practice

Beyond school councils, participation includes:

**Electoral participation**:
- Registering to vote (automatic registration introduced 2015)
- Understanding manifestos and party positions
- Voting age debates (lowering to 16, achieved for Scottish Parliament elections)

**Non-electoral participation**:
- Petitions (e-petitions to Parliament requiring 100,000 signatures for debate)
- Campaigning and lobbying MPs
- Pressure groups and social movements
- Trade unions and professional associations
- Community organising
- Direct action and protest within legal frameworks

### Social Action

The **#iwill campaign** (launched 2013) promotes youth social action. GCSE Citizenship Studies requires students to undertake active citizenship projects. Jonathan Charlesworth's research (2015) demonstrates that meaningful social action develops:

- Political efficacy (belief that participation makes a difference)
- Civic skills (organisation, communication, collaboration)
- Democratic values and empathy
- Critical thinking about social issues

Teachers should facilitate projects addressing real community issues, ensuring students experience the citizenship process from research through planning to implementation and evaluation.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Active and Participatory Learning

Lee Jerome's work emphasises **participatory pedagogy** where students experience democracy rather than merely learning about it. Effective strategies include:

**Deliberative discussions**: Structured debates on controversial issues using the **Structured Controversy** approach, where students research different perspectives before forming judgements.

**Simulations and role-plays**: Mock elections, mock trials, Model United Nations, and parliamentary debates. The **UK Parliament Education Service** provides resources including the *Parliament Week* initiative.

**Enquiry-based learning**: Students investigate democratic questions through research, developing critical thinking about sources, bias, and evidence.

### Handling Controversial Issues

The **Crick Report** (1998) and subsequent guidance emphasise that controversial issues are central to Citizenship education, not peripheral. Teachers should:

- Establish clear ground rules for respectful discussion
- Present balanced perspectives without indoctrination
- Distinguish between facts, opinions, and values
- Use the **procedural neutrality** approach advocated by Bernard Crick
- Create safe spaces for genuine disagreement

The **Education Act 1996 Section 406** and **Section 407** require political balance and prohibit promotion of partisan political views. However, this does not mean avoiding controversial topics but rather ensuring balanced treatment.

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Democracy in Crisis?

Students should critically examine contemporary challenges to UK democracy:

**Participation decline**:
- Voter turnout (68.8% in 2019 General Election, down from 83.9% in 1950)
- Youth engagement (though 2017 and 2019 saw increased young voter turnout)
- Party membership decline

**Trust and legitimacy**:
- Parliamentary expenses scandal (2009)
- Impact of Brexit referendum on democratic norms
- Social media and misinformation
- Populism and polarisation

**Constitutional reform debates**:
- House of Lords reform proposals
- Electoral system reform
- Codified constitution debates
- English devolution and regional assemblies
- Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011 (repealed 2022)

### Technology and Democracy

Nick Boddington's recent work highlights digital citizenship challenges:

- E-democracy and online petitions
- Social media's role in political engagement and echo chambers
- Misinformation, "fake news", and media literacy
- Data protection and surveillance (Investigatory Powers Act 2016)
- Digital rights and freedom of expression online

## Assessment Approaches

### GCSE Citizenship Studies

**AQA Citizenship Studies (8100)**:
- Paper 1: Life in Modern Britain (focuses on rights, law, and politics)
- Paper 2: Citizenship in Action (includes social action project)

**Edexcel Citizenship Studies (1CS0)**:
- Component 1: Citizenship in Perspective
- Component 2: Participating in Democratic Processes and Systems

**OCR Citizenship Studies (J270)**:
- Component 1: Citizenship in the UK
- Component 2: Our Rights, Our Society

**WJEC Eduqas Citizenship Studies**:
- Component 1: Citizenship Concepts and Processes
- Component 2: Active Citizenship

All specifications assess knowledge of UK democratic systems and legal frameworks alongside skills of analysis, evaluation, and active citizenship.

### Assessment Without Examination

For non-GCSE citizenship at KS3, assessment might include:

- Research projects on democratic institutions
- Presentations analysing laws or legal cases
- Portfolios documenting social action projects
- Self and peer assessment of participation in democratic processes
- Reflective journals on citizenship learning

Lee Jerome advocates for **authentic assessment** that evaluates students' capacity for democratic engagement rather than merely recalling institutional knowledge.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning Effective Lessons

1. **Begin with real-world contexts**: Current legislation, recent elections, topical legal cases
2. **Use authentic resources**: Parliamentary broadcasts, actual laws, court judgments
3. **Ensure active participation**: Deliberation, debate, and democratic decision-making in every lesson
4. **Build progression**: From local (school council) through national (Parliament) to global (international institutions)
5. **Connect to students' lives**: Youth voting rights, employment law for young workers, consumer rights

### Common Misconceptions to Address

- The Queen/King governs the country (clarify constitutional monarchy)
- Laws and rules are interchangeable (distinguish moral rules, social conventions, and legal requirements)
- Democracy equals majority rule without limit (emphasise rights protections and rule of law)
- UK has a written constitution (explain uncodified constitution)
- All judges are appointed by government (clarify Judicial Appointments Commission independence)

### Ensuring Inclusivity

Democracy and law teaching must be accessible to all students:

- Use visual aids and diagrams for complex institutional structures
- Provide vocabulary support for legal and political terminology
- Ensure examples reflect diverse experiences and identities
- Address how different communities experience justice system differently
- Consider international students' perspectives on comparative systems

Bernard Crick's vision was citizenship education for all, emphasising that democratic competence is developed through practice, not inherited through background. This remains the guiding principle for effective teaching of democracy and law in UK schools.
