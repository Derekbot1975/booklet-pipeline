# Relationships Education

## Introduction and Statutory Framework

Relationships Education became a statutory requirement in all primary schools in England from September 2020, following the Children and Social Work Act 2017 and subsequent DfE guidance published in 2019. The statutory guidance 'Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education' (DfE, 2019) replaced previous non-statutory guidance from 2000, marking a significant shift in how relationships are taught across UK schools.

Relationships Education must be taught in all primary schools, whilst Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) is compulsory in all secondary schools. The guidance emphasises that this education should be delivered in an age-appropriate, developmentally appropriate, and sensitive manner, with particular attention to the protected characteristics defined in the Equality Act 2010.

The statutory framework emerged partly from concerns about online safety, mental health, and the need for children to navigate increasingly complex social relationships. Bernard Crick's earlier work on citizenship education (Crick Report, 1998) laid foundations for understanding relationships within a broader civic and social context, though his focus was primarily on democratic participation rather than personal relationships specifically.

## Core Principles and Pedagogical Approaches

Jenny Mosley's Circle Time methodology has been particularly influential in establishing safe spaces for discussing relationships in primary settings. Her approach emphasises creating classroom environments where children feel valued, heard, and safe to express emotions and concerns (Mosley, 1996). This child-centred, democratic approach aligns with the participatory ethos recommended in DfE guidance, where children's questions and concerns should inform curriculum content.

The statutory guidance emphasises that Relationships Education should be delivered through a spiral curriculum approach, where themes are revisited with increasing depth and complexity. This reflects constructivist learning theory and ensures concepts are reinforced and built upon throughout a child's educational journey.

Nick Boddington's work on values-based PSHE education highlights the importance of establishing clear pedagogical approaches that move beyond information-giving to developing skills, attributes and values. Effective Relationships Education requires active learning methodologies including discussion, role-play, collaborative activities, and critical reflection rather than didactic teaching (Boddington, 2021, PSHE Association resources).

## Key Stage 1: Foundation Relationships Education

At Key Stage 1 (ages 5-7), the statutory guidance requires teaching about:

### Families and People Who Care for Me

Children should understand that families come in different forms (single parents, same-sex parents, extended families, adoptive families) and that stable, caring relationships are characterised by love and care. Teaching should acknowledge diversity without explicitly addressing sexual relationships or sexual orientation in detail at this stage. Children learn that families provide stability, care and love, and that marriage represents a formal and legally recognised commitment (including same-sex marriage since the Marriage (Same Sex Couples) Act 2013).

### Caring Friendships

Pupils learn the characteristics of healthy friendships, including mutual respect, trust, truthfulness, loyalty, kindness and generosity. They should understand how friendships support wellbeing and the importance of seeking support if feeling lonely or excluded. Practical teaching includes identifying when friendships are making them unhappy and strategies for managing conflict appropriately.

### Respectful Relationships

Even at this early stage, children begin learning about respect for others, politeness, recognising when people are being unkind, and that bullying and deliberately hurtful behaviour are unacceptable. They learn about the distinction between playful teasing and hurtful behaviour, and practise strategies for responding assertively.

### Online Relationships

Given children's increasing digital exposure, even Key Stage 1 pupils need age-appropriate guidance about online interactions, understanding that people may not be who they claim online, and the importance of telling a trusted adult about worrying content or requests.

### Being Safe

This crucial area includes understanding boundaries (appropriate and inappropriate touch), the right to bodily autonomy, recognising when they feel unsafe, and knowing how to seek help from trusted adults. This foundation supports safeguarding and provides vocabulary and concepts necessary for disclosing concerns about abuse.

## Key Stage 2: Developing Understanding

At Key Stage 2 (ages 7-11), all Key Stage 1 themes are revisited with greater depth:

### Extended Understanding of Families

Children develop more sophisticated understanding of different family structures, the characteristics of healthy family life (including commitment, love, security, stability), and why marriage is an important relationship choice for many (civil partnership is also introduced). Teaching acknowledges that some families experience difficulties, separation or divorce, and that children are not to blame for these situations.

### Friendship Complexity

Upper Key Stage 2 particularly addresses the increasing complexity of peer relationships approaching adolescence. Children explore strategies for recognising and responding to peer pressure, the distinction between healthy and unhealthy friendships, and understanding that friendships change over time. Gender stereotypes about friendships should be actively challenged.

### Respectful Relationships and Equality

Building on earlier foundations, children examine respect in relationships more critically, including understanding equality between people, the protected characteristics under the Equality Act 2010, and recognising prejudice-based language and behaviour. They learn that discriminatory behaviour towards others is unacceptable, including racism, sexism, homophobia, and disability discrimination.

Jenny Mosley's quality circle time activities are particularly effective for exploring these themes, creating democratic spaces where all voices are valued and children practise respectful dialogue even when holding different views (Mosley & Sonnet, 2005).

### Digital Literacy and Online Relationships

The UKCCIS guidance 'Education for a Connected World' (2020) complements the statutory Relationships Education framework, providing detailed progression in online safety across age ranges. Key Stage 2 pupils learn about online reputation, the permanence of digital footprints, recognising and responding to cyberbullying, and critically evaluating online information and relationships.

### Physical and Emotional Boundaries

Children's understanding of personal boundaries becomes more sophisticated, including concepts of privacy, consent in everyday interactions (e.g., consent to physical games or sharing belongings), recognising uncomfortable situations, and understanding that they have rights over their own bodies. This prepares pupils for more explicit consent education in secondary RSE.

### Personal Safety and Safeguarding

Children learn specific skills for keeping safe, including recognising situations or people that feel unsafe, understanding secrets that should never be kept, knowledge of FGM and forced marriage as illegal practices, and multiple routes for seeking help (including Childline, trusted adults in school, and appropriate helplines). The NSPCC's 'Speak Out Stay Safe' programme is widely used to support this statutory requirement.

## Key Stage 3: Transition to RSE

At secondary level (Key Stage 3, ages 11-14), Relationships Education transitions into the statutory Relationships and Sex Education curriculum. The DfE guidance (2019) specifies that all secondary content should build upon primary Relationships Education foundations.

### Relationships and Sex Education Core Themes

The statutory secondary curriculum includes:

**Families**: Understanding different family structures, the nature of commitment, marriage and civil partnership, parenting and the consequences of separation/divorce. Content explicitly addresses forced marriage and honour-based violence as illegal practices.

**Respectful Relationships**: Detailed examination of healthy relationships (including romantic and sexual relationships from Key Stage 4), consent, recognising abuse and coercive control, understanding power dynamics in relationships, and practical skills for maintaining respectful relationships.

**Online and Media**: Critical media literacy regarding how relationships and bodies are portrayed, recognising manipulation and persuasive techniques, understanding pornography's impact on expectations, and developing digital resilience.

**Being Safe**: Comprehensive coverage of consent (including legal frameworks), recognising and reporting abuse, understanding the law regarding sexual consent (Sexual Offences Act 2003), exploitation, safeguarding, and accessing sexual health services.

### GCSE Citizenship Studies

Both AQA (8100) and Edexcel (1CS0) Citizenship Studies specifications include relationships content within broader citizenship themes. AQA's 'Life in Modern Britain' section addresses families, community cohesion, and rights and responsibilities in relationships. Edexcel examines relationships through the lens of identity, equality and human rights. OCR's J270 specification includes relationships within 'Citizenship in Action' themes.

Lee Jerome and Jonathan Charlesworth's research on citizenship education emphasises that relationships content should connect personal experiences to broader political and social structures, examining how laws, policies and social movements shape relationship norms and rights (Jerome, Liddle & Young-Smith, 2014).

## Differentiation and Inclusion

The statutory guidance emphasises that content must be accessible to all pupils, including those with SEND, those from diverse backgrounds, and those with particular vulnerabilities. The PSHE Association's 'Programme of Study for PSHE Education' (updated 2020) provides detailed guidance on differentiation strategies.

### SEND Considerations

For pupils with SEND, teaching should be differentiated according to developmental stage rather than chronological age. Pupils with learning disabilities may need concrete, repeated teaching about boundaries, relationships and safety using visual supports, social stories, and practical scenarios. The 'Equals' RSE curriculum provides specialist resources for SEND settings.

For pupils with autism spectrum conditions, explicit teaching about social rules, non-verbal communication in relationships, and practical strategies for navigating social situations is essential, as these skills may not develop intuitively (National Autistic Society, 2016).

### Cultural and Religious Sensitivity

The DfE guidance requires schools to consult with parents and communities when developing their RSE policies, whilst maintaining that all content is taught in an age-appropriate manner that respects the law. Schools must teach about same-sex relationships, diverse families, and equality whilst remaining sensitive to religious beliefs. The guidance explicitly states that schools should address issues sensitively but should not represent LGBT relationships or families in a negative light, as this would breach Equality Act 2010 requirements.

The 'No Outsiders' programme developed by Andrew Moffat has been influential but also controversial, demonstrating the challenges of balancing legal requirements regarding LGBT inclusion with community concerns. Effective practice involves ongoing dialogue with parents, clarity about curriculum content and age-appropriateness, and situating LGBT relationships within broader diversity education rather than as isolated, potentially contentious topics.

## Assessment and Progression

Unlike other curriculum areas, Relationships Education and RSE should not involve formal testing or grading. The PSHE Association guidance emphasises formative assessment approaches including:

- **Baseline and endpoint assessments**: Understanding pupils' starting points and measuring progression in knowledge, understanding, attitudes and skills
- **Self and peer assessment**: Pupils reflecting on their own learning and development
- **Teacher observation**: Noting participation, engagement, and demonstration of skills in discussions and activities
- **Exit cards and question boxes**: Anonymous methods for pupils to demonstrate understanding or raise concerns

The National Foundation for Educational Research (NFER) has developed assessment frameworks for PSHE education that can support schools in evidencing progression and impact (Sims & Stothard, 2020).

Assessment should focus on:
- **Knowledge**: Understanding concepts (e.g., characteristics of healthy relationships, legal frameworks)
- **Skills**: Demonstrating communication, negotiation, help-seeking, critical thinking
- **Attitudes**: Showing respect, empathy, equality, and positive relationship behaviours

## Safeguarding Considerations

Relationships Education and RSE have unique safeguarding dimensions. The statutory guidance emphasises that these subjects may lead to disclosures of abuse or concerning situations. Teachers require:

### Disclosure Management

Clear protocols for responding to disclosures, including understanding the limits of confidentiality, appropriate listening skills, and immediate referral to designated safeguarding leads. Training should address how to respond supportively whilst following statutory safeguarding procedures (Working Together to Safeguard Children, 2018).

### Creating Safe Environments

Establishing ground rules, using distancing techniques (discussing scenarios rather than personal experiences), offering anonymous question facilities, and being alert to signs of distress. Jenny Mosley's Circle Time golden rules provide useful frameworks for establishing these safe parameters.

### Identifying Vulnerable Pupils

Teachers should be alert to pupils who may be particularly vulnerable, including those experiencing domestic abuse, those with harmful sexual behaviour, those at risk of exploitation, or those questioning their sexual orientation or gender identity who may lack support.

### Referring to External Support

Knowledge of specialist services including NSPCC, Childline (0800 1111), Brook sexual health services, and local safeguarding partnerships. The PSHE Association maintains an updated list of appropriate support services.

## Parental Engagement and the Right to Withdraw

The statutory guidance affirms parents' right to request their child be withdrawn from Sex Education (but not Relationships Education or Health Education) up to three terms before their 16th birthday, after which point the young person can choose to receive SRE. Parents have no right to withdraw from Relationships Education in primary or secondary settings.

Effective practice involves:

### Transparent Communication

Schools should publish their RSE policy, clearly outlining what will be taught and when. The PSHE Association recommends sharing curriculum maps and resources with parents before delivery. Some schools hold parent information sessions or provide access to resources used in lessons.

### Consultation and Co-production

The statutory guidance requires consultation with parents when developing and reviewing RSE policies. This should be meaningful engagement, potentially involving parent working groups, surveys, and opportunities for dialogue. Research by the Sex Education Forum demonstrates that when parents are well-informed about content, concerns typically decrease significantly (Sex Education Forum, 2020).

### Supporting Home Learning

Providing resources that enable parents to reinforce learning at home, including conversation starters, recommended books, and guidance on discussing potentially sensitive topics. The NSPCC's 'PANTS' resources for protective behaviours and the Sex Education Forum's parent guides are valuable resources.

## Cross-Curricular Links and Whole-School Approaches

Relationships Education should not exist in isolation but connect with broader school ethos and curriculum areas:

### Whole-School Ethos

Bernard Crick's emphasis on citizenship education as fundamentally about creating democratic school communities remains relevant. Schools where pupils experience respectful relationships with adults and peers, where voices are heard through school councils, and where equality is actively promoted provide living examples of healthy relationship cultures (Jerome & Kisby, 2019).

### Curriculum Connections

- **English**: Literature exploring relationships, families, and identity; persuasive writing about relationship issues; dramatic explorations of conflict and resolution
- **Science**: Human development, puberty, reproduction (coordinated with RSE)
- **RE**: Beliefs about relationships, marriage, family structures across different faiths
- **History**: How relationships, rights and family structures have changed over time; civil rights movements including women's suffrage and LGBT rights
- **ICT/Computing**: Digital literacy, online safety, critically evaluating online relationships
- **PE**: Teamwork, respect, cooperation, body awareness

### Behaviour and Anti-Bullying Policies

Relationships Education should align with behaviour policies that promote respectful relationships and explicitly address bullying, including prejudice-based bullying. The Anti-Bullying Alliance's research demonstrates that effective approaches integrate PSHE learning with consistent responses to relationship difficulties and bullying incidents (Anti-Bullying Alliance, 2018).

## Contemporary Challenges and Emerging Issues

Relationships Education must address evolving challenges that young people navigate:

### Digital Relationships and Online Safety

The proliferation of social media, online communication, and dating apps creates new relationship dynamics. Teaching must address online reputation management, the difference between online and face-to-face relationships, recognising online manipulation or grooming, understanding how algorithms shape social interactions, and critically evaluating relationship portrayals online.

Internet Matters and the UK Safer Internet Centre provide regularly updated resources addressing emerging online risks.

### Pornography and Sexualisation

Research indicates many young people encounter pornography before secondary school (Children's Commissioner, 2020). Age-appropriate responses include helping children understand that online content may be inappropriate, that pornography presents unrealistic and often harmful depictions of relationships and sex, and developing critical media literacy. Secondary education should explicitly address pornography's impact on expectations and consent.

### Mental Health and Relationships

Understanding the bidirectional relationship between relationships and mental health is crucial. Healthy relationships support wellbeing, whilst poor mental health can impact relationship quality. Content should address emotional regulation, recognising when relationships negatively affect mental health, and accessing support. This connects with statutory Health Education requirements regarding mental wellbeing.

### Consent Culture

Developing consent understanding begins in primary school with age-appropriate content about bodily autonomy and permission in everyday interactions. Secondary education develops this into comprehensive consent education including legal frameworks, affirmative consent models, understanding that consent is ongoing and reversible, recognising situations where consent cannot be given (intoxication, power imbalances), and challenging rape myths. The 'Consent is Everything' campaign by Thames Valley Police provides educational resources.

### LGBT+ Inclusion

Teaching about LGBT+ relationships and identities is statutory and must be integrated throughout Relationships Education rather than addressed as a separate, potentially othering, topic. Content should include same-sex relationships when discussing families and relationships, challenge homophobic and transphobic language, and ensure LGBT+ young people see their identities reflected positively. Stonewall's education resources and the DfE guidance 'Plan your relationships, sex and health curriculum' (2020) provide specific guidance on LGBT-inclusive practice.

### Relationship Abuse and Coercive Control

The Serious Crime Act 2015 made coercive control a criminal offence, recognising that abuse involves patterns of behaviour beyond physical violence. Secondary RSE must include recognising warning signs of abusive relationships, understanding coercive control, emotional abuse, and economic abuse. Women's Aid 'Expect Respect' programme provides educational resources addressing teenage relationship abuse.
