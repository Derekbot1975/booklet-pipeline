# Mental Health Education

## Overview and Policy Context

Mental health education became a statutory requirement within Health Education in England from September 2020, following the Children and Young People's Mental Health Tranche reforms and the DfE's guidance on Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education (2019). This marked a significant shift in recognition that mental health literacy is as essential as physical health understanding.

The statutory guidance requires that by the end of secondary school, pupils should know "how to recognise the early signs of mental wellbeing concerns" and "that mental wellbeing is a normal part of daily life, in the same way as physical health" (DfE, 2019). This legislative change emerged from recommendations in the Five Year Forward View for Mental Health (NHS England, 2016) and the Government Green Paper *Transforming Children and Young People's Mental Health Provision* (2017).

The Public Health England document *Promoting Children and Young People's Emotional Health and Wellbeing* (2015, updated 2021) provides the evidence base, demonstrating that approximately one in six children aged 5-16 has a probable mental health condition (NHS Digital, 2020). Early intervention through education is recognised as critical in the stepped-care model advocated by Anna Freud National Centre for Children and Families.

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Whole School Approach

The concept of a "whole school approach" to mental health, championed by Jenny Mosley through her Circle Time and Quality Circle Time models, emphasises that mental health education cannot exist in isolation. Mosley's work on creating emotionally literate school communities provides foundational principles: emotional literacy, relationship building, and creating safe spaces for dialogue. Her Golden Rules and philosophy of "we all have hopes, dreams, rights and responsibilities" underpin much contemporary practice.

The PSHE Association's framework (2020) adopts this holistic perspective, arguing that mental health education must integrate across curriculum, culture, targeted support, and staff wellbeing. This aligns with the whole school approach advocated by Public Health England and supported by evidence from Weare and Nind's systematic review (2011) showing that integrated approaches are significantly more effective than discrete interventions.

### Mental Health Continuum Model

Contemporary mental health education employs the continuum model rather than binary healthy/unwell distinctions. Keyes' (2002) work on "flourishing" versus "languishing" has influenced UK frameworks, particularly the PSHE Association's progression model. This approach, reflected in resources from MindEd and the Anna Freud Centre, teaches pupils that mental health fluctuates and that everyone has mental health, just as everyone has physical health.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Primary)

At primary level, the statutory Health Education guidance focuses on building emotional vocabulary and understanding feelings. The framework specifies pupils should know:

- That mental wellbeing is a normal part of daily life
- That there is a normal range of emotions (e.g., happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, nervousness)
- How to recognise and talk about their emotions, including using accurate vocabulary
- That bullying (including cyberbullying) has a negative impact on mental wellbeing

**Practical implications**: Teachers should employ age-appropriate resources such as the Colour Monster (Anna Llenas), emotion cards, and mood meters. Jenny Mosley's Circle Time provides structured opportunities for emotional expression. The PATHS (Promoting Alternative Thinking Strategies) curriculum, evaluated positively in UK settings by Humphrey et al. (2016), offers systematic lessons on emotional literacy.

Key vocabulary progression moves from basic emotions (happy, sad, angry, scared) in Year 1-2, to more nuanced terms (frustrated, anxious, excited, disappointed) in Years 3-4, and understanding of mental health states (worried, stressed, overwhelmed, calm) in Years 5-6.

### Key Stage 3

The transition to secondary requires explicit teaching about mental health conditions, risk factors, and help-seeking. The statutory guidance specifies that pupils should know:

- How to recognise the early signs of mental wellbeing concerns
- Common types of mental ill health (e.g., anxiety and depression)
- How to critically evaluate when something they do or are involved in has a positive or negative effect on their own or others' mental health
- The benefits and importance of physical exercise, time outdoors, community participation and voluntary service for mental wellbeing
- Where and how to seek support including whom to speak to in school

**Practical implications**: Use case studies, scenarios, and guest speakers from organisations like Young Minds or Place2Be. The Mental Health Foundation's "Peer Education Project" provides age-appropriate peer-led materials. Teachers should establish clear safeguarding protocols before teaching, as disclosures often increase following mental health lessons.

Resources from Minded (previously MindEd) provide classroom materials aligned with the NHS's language around anxiety disorders, depression, eating disorders, and self-harm. Teaching should emphasise that mental health conditions are common, treatable, and not shameful.

### Key Stage 4

GCSE specifications include mental health within broader PSHE frameworks, though it is not separately examined. AQA's Health and Social Care qualification includes explicit mental health content within Unit 1: Human Lifespan Development. OCR's Cambridge National in Health and Social Care (J835) includes mental health awareness within R032: Principles of care in health and social care settings.

At KS4, content deepens to include:

- Understanding of specific mental health conditions (depression, anxiety disorders, eating disorders, psychosis, bipolar disorder)
- The relationship between mental and physical health
- Impact of substances on mental health
- Strategies for maintaining positive mental health during exam stress
- How to support peers experiencing mental health difficulties
- Understanding of professional services (CAMHS, counselling, GP services)

**Practical implications**: Use Time to Change resources to challenge stigma. Teach the Mental Health First Aid for Young People curriculum or similar programmes. Address exam anxiety explicitly, providing cognitive-behavioural techniques for stress management.

### Key Stage 5

Post-16 mental health education should cover:

- Mental health in the transition to adulthood and independence
- University/workplace mental health support systems
- Understanding of the Mental Health Act and mental capacity
- Relationship between mental health, academic pressure, and identity formation
- Specific contemporary challenges: social media, body image, loneliness

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Active Learning Strategies

Bernard Crick's emphasis on experiential learning in citizenship education applies equally to mental health teaching. Purely didactic approaches are insufficient; students need opportunities to practice emotional regulation, communication skills, and help-seeking behaviours.

Effective strategies include:

- **Role play and scenario work**: Students practice conversations about mental health concerns
- **Reflective journaling**: Personal mood tracking and reflection (with appropriate safeguarding considerations)
- **Discussion-based learning**: Philosophy for Children (P4C) techniques applied to mental health dilemmas
- **Solution-focused approaches**: Rather than dwelling on problems, teaching coping strategies and resilience

Nick Boddington's work on controversial issues in citizenship education provides useful frameworks for discussing mental health, which remains stigmatised despite increasing awareness. His approach emphasises creating safe spaces for disagreement whilst maintaining respect.

### Language and Framing

Language matters profoundly in mental health education. The Time to Change campaign (2007-2021) demonstrated that anti-stigma education reduces discrimination when language is carefully chosen. Teachers should:

- Use person-first language ("a person with depression" rather than "a depressive")
- Avoid casual use of diagnostic terms ("I'm so OCD about organisation")
- Distinguish between clinical conditions and everyday experiences
- Teach students the difference between "mental health" and "mental ill health"

Lee Jerome's research on language in citizenship education highlights how terminology shapes understanding. In mental health contexts, this means explicitly teaching that "mental health" refers to everyone's emotional and psychological wellbeing, not just to illness.

## Evidence-Based Interventions

### Universal Approaches

The Education Endowment Foundation (EEF) Teaching and Learning Toolkit identifies social and emotional learning (SEL) approaches as offering approximately four months' additional progress when implemented well. However, their 2020 report emphasises that quality varies significantly and that teacher training is critical.

Programmes with strong evidence bases in UK contexts include:

**FRIENDS for Life Programme**: A cognitive-behavioural programme for anxiety prevention, evaluated positively by Barrett and Turner (2001) and in UK settings by Stallard et al. (2014), showing reduced anxiety symptoms in universal prevention trials.

**Penn Resiliency Programme**: Based on cognitive-behavioural principles and positive psychology, adapted for UK schools as the UK Resilience Programme. Challen et al.'s (2014) evaluation showed modest positive effects on depression symptoms.

**Mindfulness in Schools Project (MiSP)**: The .b and Paws b curricula for secondary and primary respectively have shown promise in UK trials, though Kuyken et al.'s (2013) RCT showed mixed results, with benefits for high-risk students but requiring careful implementation.

### Targeted Approaches

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on vulnerable groups in PSHE highlights that universal mental health education must be supplemented with targeted support for at-risk populations, including:

- Young people with experience of trauma
- LGBTQ+ youth (experiencing higher rates of mental health difficulties)
- Young carers
- Those in care or with care experience
- Pupils with SEND

The PSHE Association's guidance on Vulnerable and Disadvantaged Groups (2018) recommends differentiated approaches whilst maintaining inclusion in mainstream PSHE lessons.

## Assessment and Evaluation

Mental health education presents unique assessment challenges, as teachers must evaluate learning without assessing personal mental health status. The PSHE Association's Assessment Framework (2018) recommends:

**Knowledge assessment**: Can students define key terms, identify symptoms, know where to access help?

**Skills assessment**: Can students demonstrate help-seeking conversations, use calming strategies, support peers appropriately?

**Attributes assessment**: Do students show reduced stigmatising attitudes, increased empathy, self-awareness?

Assessment methods include:

- Case study analysis
- Role-play observation
- Written reflection with clear boundaries about disclosure
- Quiz-based knowledge checks
- Project work on mental health awareness campaigns

Critically, assessment should never require students to disclose personal mental health information or be used to identify students with concerns (which is a safeguarding function, not an assessment purpose).

## Safeguarding Considerations

Mental health education often triggers disclosures. Teachers must:

- Clearly establish ground rules before lessons, explaining confidentiality boundaries
- Provide alternative activities for students who find content triggering
- Follow school safeguarding procedures immediately for disclosures indicating risk
- Avoid teaching content beyond their competence (e.g., detailed discussion of suicide methods)
- Have support resources available (school counsellor drop-in times, helpline numbers)

The PSHE Association's *Teacher Guidance: Preparing to teach about mental health and emotional wellbeing* (2015, updated 2021) provides detailed protocols. The guidance emphasises that PSHE teachers are not therapists and should not attempt to provide mental health support beyond signposting and basic empathy.

## Staff Training and Wellbeing

Effective mental health education requires adequately trained staff. The DfE's Senior Mental Health Lead training (funded from 2022) aims to build school capacity. However, research by Spratt et al. (2006) found that teachers often feel underprepared for mental health education, particularly around managing disclosures and understanding clinical boundaries.

Professional learning should include:

- Mental Health First Aid training or equivalent
- Subject-specific PSHE training from organisations like the PSHE Association
- Understanding of local CAMHS pathways and school counselling services
- Regular supervision or peer support networks

Critically, schools must address staff mental health alongside pupil wellbeing. Education Support's 2020 survey found that 78% of education professionals experienced mental health difficulties due to work in the previous academic year. Modelling healthy boundaries, stress management, and help-seeking is essential if mental health education is to be authentic.

## Current Challenges and Developments

### Social Media and Digital Wellbeing

The relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health remains contested, with Orben and Przybylski's (2019) large-scale study suggesting small negative effects comparable to wearing glasses or eating potatoes. However, specific aspects—cyberbullying, appearance comparison, sleep disruption—require explicit teaching.

The UK Safer Internet Centre provides updated resources annually. Teaching should focus on critical evaluation of online content, managing screen time, and recognising when digital use negatively impacts wellbeing rather than demonising technology.

### Post-Pandemic Mental Health

The COVID-19 pandemic significantly impacted young people's mental health, with NHS Digital (2021) finding increased prevalence of probable mental disorders. Schools are addressing increased anxiety, grief, trauma, and social skills difficulties. Mental health education must acknowledge collective trauma whilst building hope and agency.

### Exam Pressure and Academic Stress

The More Than A Score campaign (2018) highlighted concerns about examination pressure. Mental health education at KS4 and KS5 should explicitly address academic stress, perfectionism, and fear of failure, teaching that wellbeing should not be sacrificed for grades whilst acknowledging real pressures students face.

## Links to Citizenship Education

Mental health education intersects significantly with citizenship education, particularly Bernard Crick's three strands: social and moral responsibility, community involvement, and political literacy.

**Social and moral responsibility**: Understanding our responsibility to support others experiencing mental health difficulties, challenging stigma, and creating inclusive communities.

**Community involvement**: Youth social action can improve mental health (Bandura, 1997; Wiggins et al., 2004), whilst mental health advocacy projects develop citizenship skills.

**Political literacy**: Understanding mental health policy, NHS funding debates, and inequalities in access to services connects personal wellbeing to political and economic structures.

Lee Jerome's work on controversial issues pedagogy is particularly relevant when discussing debates around diagnostic practices, medication, or mental health service provision.

## Resources and Professional Organisations

**The PSHE Association**: Provides detailed planning frameworks, assessment guidance, and CPD. Their Core Theme 1 (Health and Wellbeing) specifically addresses mental health across all key stages.

**Anna Freud National Centre for Children and Families**: Offers evidence-based resources including the "Mentally Healthy Schools" website with lesson plans, staff training materials, and whole-school approaches.

**MindEd**: Free e-learning platform for professionals working with children and young people, with modules on specific conditions and developmental stages.

**Place2Be**: Schools-based mental health charity providing counselling services and "Mental Health Champions" training for pupils.

**Young Minds**: Youth mental health charity with resources for young people, parents, and schools, including the Schools Hub with lesson plans.

**Mental Health Foundation**: Provides Mental Health Awareness Week resources annually, with themes applicable to school communities.

## Conclusion

Mental health education represents one of the most significant developments in PSHE over recent decades, moving from peripheral concern to statutory requirement. However, statutory status alone does not ensure quality. Effective mental health education requires well-trained teachers, whole-school approaches, age-appropriate progression, and integration with broader citizenship and wellbeing initiatives.

The field continues to develop, with ongoing debates about curriculum content, pedagogical approaches, and the boundary between education and intervention. Teachers must stay informed through professional organisations like the PSHE Association whilst maintaining the core principle that mental health education aims to promote understanding, reduce stigma, and equip young people with knowledge and skills for lifelong wellbeing—not to diagnose or treat mental health conditions, which remains the role of health professionals.

As Jenny Mosley observed, emotional literacy is foundational to all learning. Mental health education, properly implemented, not only supports individual wellbeing but creates the conditions for thriving school communities where all young people can develop their potential.
