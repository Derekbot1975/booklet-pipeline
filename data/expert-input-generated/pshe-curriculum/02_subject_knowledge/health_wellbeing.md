# Health and Wellbeing

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Health and Wellbeing represents one of three core strands within statutory PSHE education in England, alongside Relationships and Living in the Wider World. Since September 2020, Health Education became statutory for all pupils in primary schools and Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) became statutory for all pupils in secondary schools, following the Children and Social Work Act 2017 and subsequent DfE guidance published in 2019.

The PSHE Association, led by researchers including Jonathan Charlesworth, defines Health and Wellbeing as encompassing physical health, mental wellbeing, and emotional literacy. This strand addresses fundamental life skills including understanding mental health, physical health behaviours, drug and alcohol education, and developing resilience and emotional intelligence. The statutory guidance emphasises that schools must have regard for the guidance while maintaining flexibility to develop their own programmes appropriate to the needs of their pupils.

## Statutory Requirements and Frameworks

### DfE Statutory Guidance (2019, updated 2021)

The Department for Education's statutory guidance "Relationships Education, Relationships and Sex Education (RSE) and Health Education" establishes clear expectations for Health Education. The guidance mandates teaching about:

- Mental wellbeing
- Internet safety and harms
- Physical health and fitness
- Healthy eating
- Drugs, alcohol and tobacco
- Health and prevention
- Basic first aid
- Changing adolescent body

Schools must publish their RSE and Health Education policy online and consult parents when developing this policy. The guidance explicitly states that parents cannot withdraw children from Health Education as it is statutory, unlike elements of Sex Education beyond the Science National Curriculum.

### PSHE Association Programme of Study

The PSHE Association's Programme of Study (2020 edition) provides comprehensive non-statutory guidance that articulates learning outcomes across key stages. This framework, developed with contributions from practitioners and researchers including Jonathan Charlesworth, organises Health and Wellbeing into progressive learning objectives that build upon prior learning.

## Key Theoretical Frameworks

### Jenny Mosley's Circle Time and Emotional Wellbeing

Jenny Mosley's Quality Circle Time model has significantly influenced Health and Wellbeing education in UK primary schools since the 1990s. Her approach emphasises creating emotionally safe classroom environments where pupils can discuss feelings, develop emotional vocabulary, and build self-esteem through structured activities. Mosley's "Golden Rules" and whole-school approach to emotional wellbeing provide a foundation for preventative mental health education.

The Circle Time methodology involves establishing clear ground rules, using speaking objects, and creating democratic spaces where children's voices are valued. Research published in Pastoral Care in Education (Mosley, 1996) demonstrates that Quality Circle Time contributes to improved emotional literacy, reduced behavioural incidents, and enhanced peer relationships.

### Mental Health Foundation Frameworks

The Mental Health Foundation's work on children and young people's mental health provides evidence-based approaches for classroom practice. Their "Mentally Healthy Schools" resources, developed in partnership with the PSHE Association, offer assessment tools and teaching resources aligned with statutory requirements. The Foundation emphasises the "Five Ways to Wellbeing" (Connect, Be Active, Take Notice, Keep Learning, Give) as an accessible framework for promoting positive mental health.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At KS1, Health and Wellbeing education focuses on establishing foundational understanding of physical and emotional health. Pupils should learn to:

- Recognise and name basic emotions
- Understand that mental wellbeing is a normal part of daily life
- Identify who can help them when they feel worried or sad
- Recognise what keeps them healthy (sleep, exercise, nutrition)
- Understand simple hygiene practices and their importance
- Know about safe and unsafe situations, including online safety basics

The PSHE Association framework emphasises using stories, circle time, and play-based activities. Teachers should establish emotional literacy vocabulary through daily routines, using resources such as emotion cards and mood check-ins.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

KS2 builds complexity, developing pupils' ability to articulate feelings and understand factors affecting wellbeing. Learning objectives include:

- Understanding that feelings change over time and can be managed
- Recognising mental health problems and that they can be treated
- Understanding the benefits of physical activity, good nutrition, and adequate sleep
- Recognising the risks associated with online activity
- Learning about the harmful effects of tobacco, alcohol, and other drugs
- Understanding basic first aid including calling emergency services
- Learning about puberty and physical changes (statutory Health Education)

The transition to upper KS2 requires sensitive handling of topics such as mental health conditions, bereavement, and substance misuse. The DfE guidance specifies that teaching about puberty must occur before onset, typically Year 5, requiring schools to provide information for parents.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At KS3, Health and Wellbeing education addresses increasingly complex physical and mental health topics. The PSHE Association framework identifies learning outcomes including:

- Understanding the spectrum of mental health conditions including anxiety, depression, self-harm, and eating disorders
- Recognising when to seek help and available support services
- Understanding the risks and consequences of substance use including legal highs
- Developing strategies for managing stress and exam pressure
- Understanding healthy relationships with food and exercise
- Learning comprehensive first aid skills
- Understanding sexual health within statutory RSE requirements

Lee Jerome's research on citizenship and PSHE pedagogy (Education, Citizenship and Social Justice, 2012) emphasises the importance of participatory approaches at KS3. Young people benefit from enquiry-based learning that acknowledges their developing autonomy whilst providing accurate health information.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

KS4 Health and Wellbeing education prepares students for adult life and decision-making. Content includes:

- Managing mental health during high-pressure periods including examinations
- Understanding the relationship between physical and mental health
- Accessing health services independently including sexual health services
- Understanding the law relating to substance use and the impact of drugs on mental health
- Critically evaluating health messages and media representations
- Managing financial wellbeing and understanding links between poverty and health
- Developing strategies for maintaining wellbeing during transition to post-16 education or employment

## Core Content Areas

### Mental Wellbeing

Mental wellbeing education must address the statutory requirement to teach pupils how to recognise and talk about their emotions, including using accurate vocabulary. The DfE guidance emphasises teaching about the normal range of emotions, the benefits of physical activity and time spent outdoors for mental wellbeing, and strategies for self-regulation.

Teachers should explicitly address common mental health conditions including anxiety disorders, depression, and self-harm. Anna Freud Centre resources provide age-appropriate materials explaining these conditions. The statutory guidance requires schools to teach that mental health problems can be treated and that seeking help is a sign of strength.

Schools must establish clear referral pathways when pupils disclose mental health concerns. The "Mental Health and Behaviour in Schools" guidance (DfE, 2018) outlines how PSHE education should complement whole-school approaches to mental health support, including designated senior mental health leads (now recommended for all schools).

### Physical Health and Fitness

Physical health education encompasses understanding the benefits of regular physical activity, balanced nutrition, sufficient sleep, and dental health. The statutory guidance requires teaching about the characteristics of a balanced diet, the risks of poor nutrition, and the physical and mental health benefits of an active lifestyle.

Schools should address the risks of sedentary lifestyles, particularly screen-based sedentary behaviour. Public Health England's "What Works in Schools and Colleges to Increase Physical Activity" briefing (PHE, 2020) provides evidence that PSHE education combined with environmental changes increases activity levels.

Teaching must include understanding of the changing adolescent body, including physical, emotional, and hormonal changes during puberty. For girls, this includes menstruation and menstrual wellbeing; for boys, this includes wet dreams and voice changes. Schools must ensure teaching is inclusive of all pupils' experiences.

### Drugs, Alcohol, and Tobacco Education

The statutory guidance mandates teaching about the facts about legal and illegal harmful substances, including alcohol, tobacco, nicotine products (vaping), and volatile substances. Education must include understanding the law relating to supply and possession of substances, the risks of substance misuse, and where to access support.

The PSHE Association's "Drug and Alcohol Education Guidance" (2019) emphasises evidence-based approaches that avoid scare tactics. Frank Furedi's research on risk education cautions against creating anxiety whilst Nick Boddington's work on critical thinking in PSHE (Young Citizens, 2014) advocates for analytical approaches where pupils evaluate evidence about substance effects.

Schools should address the prevalence of vaping among young people, emerging research on health impacts, and commercial targeting of young people through flavoured products. The spiral curriculum approach revisits substances education with increasing sophistication across key stages.

### Health and Prevention

The statutory guidance requires teaching about personal hygiene, germs including bacteria and viruses, infection prevention, and the importance of immunisation. Following the COVID-19 pandemic, understanding of disease transmission, hand hygiene, and respiratory hygiene has gained prominence.

Schools must teach basic first aid, including concepts of head injuries, choking, bleeding, and when to call emergency services. St John Ambulance and British Red Cross provide accredited programmes suitable for different age groups. By the end of primary school, pupils should understand the recovery position and cardiopulmonary resuscitation concepts.

Sun safety, dental health, and vision care represent additional statutory content. Schools should address health inequalities, acknowledging that access to preventative health services varies by socioeconomic status and geographic location.

### Online Safety and Digital Wellbeing

Health and Wellbeing overlaps with Relationships Education in addressing online safety. The DfE guidance requires teaching about internet use risks including exposure to harmful content, contact with harmful individuals, and conduct that damages wellbeing or reputations.

Digital wellbeing education must address screen time impacts on sleep, physical activity, and mental health. The "Digital Resilience Framework" developed by UK Safer Internet Centre provides age-appropriate learning objectives covering critical evaluation, self-regulation, and seeking help.

Specific content includes understanding that social media presents curated representations, not reality; recognising signs of problematic internet use; and managing online peer pressure. Schools must teach about image sharing risks, online grooming, and radicalisation whilst maintaining age-appropriateness.

## Assessment and Monitoring Progress

### Assessment Principles in PSHE

Unlike most subjects, PSHE and Health and Wellbeing education do not involve summative assessment or grading of pupils' personal views, attributes, or private lives. The PSHE Association's "Assessment in PSHE Education" guidance (2018) clarifies that assessment focuses on knowledge and understanding, not personal qualities or circumstances.

Teachers should assess pupils' understanding of health concepts, ability to apply strategies learned, and development of skills such as decision-making and risk assessment. Assessment methods include:

- Low-stakes quizzes on factual knowledge (e.g., emergency services numbers, substance effects)
- Case study responses demonstrating application of learning
- Self-assessment against learning objectives
- Contribution to discussions and activities
- Project work demonstrating research and critical thinking

Schools must ensure assessment creates safe environments where pupils can explore sensitive topics without fear of judgment. Jonathan Charlesworth's work with the PSHE Association emphasises that assessment should support learning rather than creating barriers to participation.

### Monitoring Whole-School Impact

Beyond individual pupil assessment, schools should monitor the impact of Health and Wellbeing education on school culture and pupil outcomes. Indicators include:

- Pupil voice surveys on wellbeing and knowledge
- Attendance rates and patterns
- Referrals to pastoral support services
- Engagement with school health services (e.g., school nurse)
- Bullying and behaviour incident data

Ofsted's "PSHE education in schools" research review (2022) examined effective practice and found that successful schools integrate PSHE across the curriculum, train staff comprehensively, and use assessment to refine provision.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Active and Participatory Learning

Lee Jerome's research on citizenship and PSHE pedagogy emphasises that Health and Wellbeing education requires active, participatory methods rather than didactic transmission of facts. Effective approaches include:

- Discussion and debate activities that develop critical thinking
- Role play and drama exploring decision-making scenarios
- Collaborative problem-solving tasks
- Use of case studies and dilemma activities
- Peer education approaches (with appropriate training and safeguarding)

Teachers should establish ground rules collaboratively, using the PSHE Association's recommended approach of distancing techniques (using third-person scenarios rather than requiring personal disclosure), anonymous question boxes, and clear signposting of support services.

### Creating Safe Learning Environments

Jenny Mosley's Quality Circle Time principles remain relevant across key stages for creating emotionally safe spaces. Essential elements include:

- Consistent routines and predictable structures
- Clear boundaries around confidentiality (explaining when information must be shared)
- Non-judgmental teacher responses to questions and comments
- Inclusive language and materials representing diverse experiences
- Options for pupils to participate without personal disclosure

The PSHE Association's "Creating a Safe Learning Environment" guidance (2020) emphasises that teachers should never put pupils on the spot or require them to share personal information. Using anonymous methods (sticky notes, online polling) enables participation without personal risk.

### Responding to Sensitive Disclosures

Health and Wellbeing lessons may prompt safeguarding disclosures. Teachers must understand their school's safeguarding procedures and their professional responsibilities. Best practice includes:

- Briefing pupils at the start of topics about support available and confidentiality limits
- Responding calmly and non-judgmentally to disclosures
- Following school procedures immediately (speaking to the Designated Safeguarding Lead)
- Not promising absolute confidentiality
- Recording disclosures accurately using the child's words

The "Keeping Children Safe in Education" statutory guidance (DfE, updated annually) outlines all school staff's responsibilities. PSHE teachers require additional training in handling disclosures related to mental health, abuse, substance use, and self-harm.

## External Contributions and Visitors

### Quality Assurance of Visitors

The PSHE Association's "Using Visitors to Support PSHE Education" guidance (2019) establishes quality standards for external contributors. Health professionals, youth workers, and charity representatives can enhance learning but require clear briefing about:

- Learning objectives aligned with curriculum plans
- Age-appropriateness of content and language
- Pedagogical approaches consistent with PSHE education principles
- Safeguarding responsibilities

Teachers must remain present during all sessions delivered by visitors, retaining professional responsibility for learning. Schools should vet visitors using DBS checks where appropriate and reviewing materials in advance.

### Health Service Partnerships

School nurses provide valuable expertise for Health and Wellbeing education. The Healthy Child Programme (PHE, 2009, updated 2019) outlines school nurses' roles in delivering health education and supporting pupils with health needs. Effective partnerships involve:

- Collaborative planning of health education programmes
- School nurse-led sessions on specific topics (e.g., puberty, sexual health)
- Clear referral pathways for pupils requiring health support
- Training for teachers delivered by health professionals

Schools should also establish relationships with local authority public health teams, Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS), and sexual health services to ensure pupils know how to access support.

## Inclusion and Equality

### SEND Considerations

The Equality Act 2010 requires schools to make reasonable adjustments for pupils with special educational needs and disabilities. Health and Wellbeing education must be accessible to all pupils, requiring differentiated approaches:

- Visual supports and concrete resources for pupils with learning disabilities
- Simplified language and additional processing time
- Individual or small group teaching for topics that may be particularly sensitive
- Collaboration with specialist staff (e.g., speech and language therapists)
- Modified personal care education for pupils with physical disabilities

The PSHE Association's "PSHE Education and SEND" guidance emphasises that pupils with SEND have equal rights to RSE and Health Education and may require additional teaching to keep them safe.

### Cultural Sensitivity and Religious Perspectives

Schools must teach Health Education whilst respecting diverse cultural and religious backgrounds. The DfE guidance acknowledges that some topics may generate parental concern, particularly where religious teachings differ from health education content.

Effective practice includes:

- Consulting diverse communities when developing policies
- Presenting factual health information whilst acknowledging different values
- Distinguishing between legal requirements, health recommendations, and personal/religious choices
- Addressing homophobia, biphobia, and transphobia in mental health contexts
- Ensuring resources represent diverse family structures, body types, and abilities

Schools should emphasise that teaching about health topics does not constitute promotion of particular behaviours but equips young people with knowledge to make informed decisions and stay safe.

### Addressing Health Inequalities

Health and Wellbeing education must acknowledge and address health inequalities affecting pupils. Public Health England data demonstrates clear correlations between socioeconomic status and health outcomes. Effective teaching:

- Avoids assumptions about pupils' home circumstances
- Provides information about free health services (NHS, school nurse)
- Addresses food poverty sensitively when teaching about nutrition
- Recognises that some pupils have caring responsibilities affecting their wellbeing
- Challenges stigma related to poverty, mental health conditions, and disability

Schools should consider providing resources (e.g., period products, toothbrushes) alongside education, recognising that knowledge without resources creates frustration rather than empowerment.
