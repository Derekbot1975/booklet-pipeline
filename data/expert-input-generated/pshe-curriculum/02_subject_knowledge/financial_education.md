# Financial Education

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Financial education within PSHE & Citizenship equips young people with the knowledge, skills and confidence to manage money effectively throughout their lives. Since September 2014, financial education has been a statutory component of the National Curriculum for England within Citizenship at Key Stages 3 and 4, and within Mathematics at Key Stages 1-4. However, comprehensive financial education is most effectively delivered through PSHE education, where it can be contextualised within broader well-being and life skills frameworks.

The PSHE Association's Programme of Study (updated 2020) provides the most authoritative non-statutory framework for financial education delivery. This framework recognises that financial capability encompasses knowledge, skills, attitudes and behaviours - not merely economic literacy. As Bernard Crick's seminal report (1998) established for citizenship education more broadly, effective learning requires not just knowledge transmission but the development of practical competencies and critical thinking.

The Money and Pensions Service (MaPS), established in 2019, has consolidated responsibility for financial capability strategy across the UK, building on the work of the previous Money Advice Service. Their UK Strategy for Financial Wellbeing identifies children and young people as a priority audience, emphasising early intervention.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At this foundational stage, children begin developing basic concepts of money and its role in everyday life. The National Curriculum for Mathematics requires pupils to "recognise and use symbols for pounds (£) and pence (p)" and "combine amounts to make a particular value."

**Core learning objectives include:**
- Understanding that money comes in different denominations (coins and notes)
- Recognising that people receive money through work
- Distinguishing between needs and wants
- Understanding that money can be saved for future use
- Basic concepts of ownership and borrowing

**Practical implications for teachers:** Use role-play activities such as classroom shops, real coins for mathematical calculations, and picture books that normalise conversations about money. Research by Whitebread and Bingham (2013) at Cambridge University demonstrates that money habits are formed by age 7, making this early intervention critical.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Building on foundational understanding, Key Stage 2 develops more sophisticated financial concepts aligned with increasing mathematical capability and abstract reasoning.

**Core learning objectives include:**
- Understanding different ways to pay for items (cash, card, contactless, online)
- Recognising that people make spending choices based on priorities and circumstances
- Understanding the concept of budgeting and planning expenditure
- Appreciating the role of banks and building societies
- Understanding basic principles of interest on savings and loans
- Recognising financial risks, including online scams targeting children
- Understanding that advertising and peer influence affect purchasing decisions

The PSHE Association emphasises connecting financial education to pupils' lived experiences, including pocket money management and understanding family financial decisions at an age-appropriate level. Teachers should introduce the concept of enterprise and entrepreneurship, recognising that financial capability includes income generation alongside money management.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Financial education becomes statutory within the Citizenship curriculum at Key Stage 3. The National Curriculum for Citizenship (2014) requires students to understand "income and expenditure, credit and debt, insurance, savings and pensions, financial products and services, and how public money is raised and spent."

**Core learning objectives include:**
- Understanding different income sources (employment, self-employment, benefits, pensions)
- Distinguishing between gross and net income, including taxation and National Insurance
- Understanding credit and debit cards, including risks of debt
- Evaluating financial products including savings accounts, comparing interest rates
- Understanding student loans and their implications (increasingly relevant as pupils approach post-16 choices)
- Recognising gambling risks, including online betting and loot boxes in gaming
- Understanding consumer rights and responsibilities
- Appreciating how financial decisions connect to personal wellbeing and future aspirations

**Practical implications for teachers:** Lee Jerome's research (2012) on citizenship education emphasises the importance of "pedagogies of deliberation" - creating opportunities for students to debate real financial dilemmas rather than simply receiving information. Case studies should reflect diverse socioeconomic contexts, avoiding assumptions about family financial circumstances. The Young Money/PSHE Association Teacher Guidance on financial education (2019) recommends inviting speakers from financial services, credit unions or debt advice organisations, whilst maintaining critical distance from commercial interests.

Nick Boddington's work on moral education in PSHE contexts reminds us that financial education must address ethical dimensions: fair trade, charitable giving, environmental costs of consumption, and social responsibility of businesses.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At Key Stage 4, financial education continues within statutory Citizenship and should be comprehensively delivered through PSHE education, supporting students approaching financial independence.

**Core learning objectives include:**
- Understanding wages, salaries, and employment deductions in detail
- Comparing financial products (current accounts, savings accounts, ISAs)
- Understanding credit comprehensively: personal loans, overdrafts, credit cards, hire purchase
- Calculating APR and understanding compound interest consequences
- Understanding insurance principles (health, travel, motor, property)
- Planning for further/higher education costs, including maintenance loans and bursaries
- Understanding pensions and long-term financial planning concepts
- Recognising predatory lending and scam awareness
- Understanding relationship between financial decisions and mental health
- Appreciating housing costs: rent, deposits, mortgages, household bills

**Examination specifications:** Whilst PSHE itself is non-examined, Citizenship GCSEs include substantial financial education content. AQA Citizenship Studies GCSE (8100) requires understanding of "the functions of money, personal finance and financial capability." OCR Citizenship Studies GCSE (J270) includes "personal finance and economic understanding" within Theme C. Edexcel Citizenship GCSE (1CS0) addresses "the role of money, tax and public spending" within Theme 2.

Jonathan Charlesworth's research on assessment in PSHE education (2015) argues against high-stakes testing of personal financial capability, suggesting portfolio-based approaches that document decision-making processes rather than examining factual recall.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

Though non-statutory, PSHE education for sixth form students addresses sophisticated financial planning as young people approach legal and financial adulthood.

**Core learning objectives include:**
- Detailed understanding of student finance for higher education
- Comparing employment options and understanding contracts
- Tax systems including self-assessment for self-employment
- Advanced budgeting for independent living
- Understanding credit scores and their long-term implications
- Pension enrollment and compound growth over working life
- Investment principles and risks (stocks, shares, ISAs)
- Understanding housing market: renting, shared ownership, mortgages
- Financial abuse within relationships and financial coercion recognition
- Understanding socioeconomic inequality and systemic financial barriers

## Pedagogical Approaches and Best Practice

### Creating a Safe Learning Environment

Financial capability varies enormously among students and correlates with socioeconomic background, though not deterministically. Research by the Money Advice Service (2016) found significant financial capability gaps related to family income, ethnicity and parental education. Teachers must therefore establish ground rules ensuring that:

- Students are never required to disclose family financial circumstances
- Diverse economic experiences are normalised and respected
- Language avoids stigmatising poverty or welfare receipt
- Examples reflect varied family structures and income levels

Jenny Mosley's "Circle Time" methodology, whilst developed for PSHE more broadly, provides valuable frameworks for sensitive discussion. Financial scenarios should be presented through case studies or fictional personas rather than assuming shared experience.

### Active Learning Strategies

Evidence from the Education Endowment Foundation's review of financial education programmes (2020) demonstrates that interactive, experiential learning produces better outcomes than didactic teaching. Effective strategies include:

**Simulations and role-play:** Budget planning exercises using realistic scenarios (student accommodation costs, first car purchase, mobile phone contracts). Online tools like "My Money" from Young Enterprise provide scaffolded budgeting activities.

**Real-world mathematics:** Moving beyond abstract calculations to contextualised problems: comparing loan APRs, calculating savings growth, understanding payslip deductions. This reinforces mathematical skills whilst demonstrating relevance.

**Deliberative discussions:** Lee Jerome's research emphasises structured debate about controversial issues: "Should student loans be interest-bearing?", "Are buy-now-pay-later schemes ethical?", "Should financial education be examined?" These develop critical thinking and recognise that financial decisions involve values, not just calculations.

**Enterprise activities:** Young Enterprise programmes enable students to experience entrepreneurship, understanding profit, loss, investment and risk firsthand. These experiential approaches align with Bernard Crick's emphasis on active citizenship through participation.

### Addressing Digital and Emerging Issues

The Young Money Financial Education Group's research (2021) highlights the rapidly changing financial landscape that education must address:

**Cryptocurrency and digital assets:** Age-appropriate discussion of Bitcoin, NFTs and digital investment risks, avoiding both uncritical enthusiasm and dismissive rejection.

**Open banking and fintech:** Understanding how technology is transforming banking, including benefits (accessibility, budgeting apps) and risks (data privacy, algorithmic decision-making).

**Social media and financial influence:** Critical evaluation of "finfluencers", recognising that social media financial advice may be unregulated, commercially motivated or contextually inappropriate.

**Gambling convergence:** Understanding how gaming, social media and gambling increasingly overlap through loot boxes, in-game purchases and cryptocurrency betting.

## Assessment and Evaluation

Jonathan Charlesworth's work on PSHE assessment emphasises that financial education assessment should focus on capability development rather than knowledge testing. Appropriate assessment strategies include:

**Self-assessment tools:** Students evaluate their own financial confidence and capability using tools like the MaPS Financial Capability Questionnaire (adapted for age).

**Portfolio evidence:** Students compile evidence of financial decision-making, budget planning or enterprise activities, with reflective commentary demonstrating learning progression.

**Scenario-based assessment:** Students respond to realistic financial situations, demonstrating decision-making processes, evaluation of options and justification of choices.

**Peer teaching:** Students research and present specific financial topics, demonstrating understanding through explanation to others.

## Partnership and Resources

Effective financial education requires high-quality, impartial resources. The PSHE Association endorses several key providers:

**The Money and Pensions Service (MaPS):** Provides the Talk Learn Do framework and free resources for all key stages.

**Young Money:** Offers comprehensive teacher resources, including the Young Money Baseline Survey for evaluating pupil financial capability.

**Young Enterprise:** Delivers enterprise education programmes including Company Programme and Tenner Challenge.

**Personal Finance Education Group (pfeg), now Young Money:** Their Quality Mark recognises schools demonstrating excellent financial education provision.

**MyBnk:** Provides expert-led financial education workshops focusing on money management, enterprise and employability.

Teachers should exercise critical judgement regarding commercial organisations offering "free" financial education, ensuring content is genuinely educational rather than promotional. The Financial Conduct Authority provides guidance on appropriate financial sector engagement with schools.

## Links to Broader PSHE Themes

Financial education cannot be isolated from other PSHE domains. Effective provision connects financial capability to:

**Health and wellbeing:** Understanding the bidirectional relationship between financial stress and mental health; recognising emotional spending and financial self-care.

**Relationships:** Discussing financial aspects of relationships appropriately (wedding costs, shared finances, financial abuse warning signs); understanding economic dimensions of exploitation.

**Careers and employability:** Connecting financial planning to career choices; understanding lifetime earnings variations; evaluating employment options holistically, not solely by starting salary.

**Living in the wider world:** Understanding taxation and public services; appreciating economic inequality; recognising corporate responsibility and ethical consumption.

Nick Boddington's framework for moral education in PSHE emphasises that financial decisions are ethical decisions, involving considerations of fairness, sustainability and social impact alongside personal advantage.

## Addressing Inequality and Social Justice

Critical financial education recognises that individual financial capability, whilst important, operates within structural constraints. As Lee Jerome's research on citizenship education emphasises, young people should understand systemic issues including:

- Wealth inequality and its intergenerational transmission
- Differential access to affordable credit
- Geographic variations in employment and housing costs
- Discrimination in financial services provision
- The poverty premium (higher costs faced by low-income households)

This critical dimension prevents financial education from implicitly blaming individuals for structural problems, whilst still developing practical capability within existing systems. Bernard Crick's original conception of citizenship education included "social and moral responsibility" alongside community involvement and political literacy - financial education should similarly balance individual agency with systemic awareness.

## Conclusion

Financial education within PSHE & Citizenship represents a crucial dimension of preparation for adult life, directly impacting young people's future wellbeing, opportunities and citizenship participation. Effective provision requires age-appropriate progression, active pedagogies, sensitive facilitation, and critical engagement with both personal capability and systemic issues. When delivered comprehensively, financial education empowers young people to make informed decisions, protects them from exploitation, and enables fuller participation in economic life.
