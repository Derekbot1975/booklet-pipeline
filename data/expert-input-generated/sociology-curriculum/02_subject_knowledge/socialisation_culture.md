# Socialisation, Culture and Identity

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Socialisation, Culture and Identity forms a foundational topic within GCSE and A-Level Sociology curricula across all UK exam boards. This topic examines how individuals develop their sense of self through social processes, cultural transmission and interaction with social institutions. The topic is explicitly covered in:

- **AQA GCSE Sociology (8192)**: Paper 1, 'The Sociology of Families' and 'The Sociology of Education'
- **AQA A-Level Sociology (7192)**: Families and Households; Education
- **OCR GCSE Sociology (J197)**: Socialising the Individual
- **WJEC Eduqas GCSE Sociology**: Acquiring Culture

Ken Browne's *Sociology for AQA* (2019, 5th edition) and Haralambos and Holborn's *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* (9th edition, 2017) provide comprehensive coverage that aligns with National Curriculum expectations for critical thinking, evidence evaluation and understanding of social diversity.

## Core Concepts and Definitions

### Culture

Culture refers to the entire way of life of a particular society, encompassing both material and non-material elements. Haralambos and Holborn (2017) distinguish between:

**Material culture**: Physical objects created by a society (technology, buildings, clothing, food)

**Non-material culture**: Intangible aspects including values, beliefs, norms, customs and language

Ken Browne (2019) emphasises the importance of distinguishing between:

- **High culture**: Cultural products and activities associated with elite social groups (opera, classical music, fine art)
- **Popular culture**: Mass-produced cultural forms consumed by majority populations (television, popular music, social media)
- **Consumer culture**: The contemporary emphasis on purchasing goods and services as expressions of identity and status
- **Global culture**: The spread of cultural products and practices across national boundaries

### Norms and Values

**Values** are widely accepted beliefs that define what is considered important, worthwhile or desirable in a society. Examples include achievement, individualism, democracy and religious faith.

**Norms** are specific rules or socially accepted standards that govern behaviour in particular situations. They translate abstract values into concrete expectations. Steve Chapman's work highlights how norms vary by context and can be formal (laws) or informal (customs, etiquette).

### Cultural Diversity

The UK's multicultural character makes understanding cultural diversity essential. Teachers should reference:

- **Subcultures**: Groups within society sharing distinctive norms, values and lifestyle patterns whilst participating in mainstream culture (youth subcultures, occupational groups, ethnic communities)
- **Countercultures**: Groups consciously rejecting dominant cultural values (punk movements, environmental activists)

Heidi Safia Mirza's research on race, gender and educational achievement (2009) demonstrates how British Asian and Black Caribbean communities maintain distinctive cultural practices whilst negotiating British mainstream culture.

## Socialisation Processes

### Primary Socialisation

Primary socialisation occurs during early childhood, principally within the family unit. Browne (2019) identifies key mechanisms:

**Imitation**: Children copy behaviours modelled by parents and siblings
**Role modelling**: Parents consciously demonstrate appropriate behaviours
**Manipulation**: Use of rewards (praise, treats) and sanctions (withdrawal of privileges, disapproval)
**Canalisation**: Channelling children toward gender-appropriate toys, activities and interests
**Verbal appellations**: Using gendered language ("good girl", "brave boy")

Ann Oakley's classic research (1974) on gender socialisation remains relevant for understanding how families transmit cultural expectations about masculinity and femininity.

### Secondary Socialisation

Secondary socialisation extends beyond the family through various agencies:

**Education**: Schools transmit formal knowledge, hidden curriculum values (punctuality, respect for authority, competitive achievement) and peer group influences. Diane Reay's ethnographic research (2017) demonstrates how British schools reproduce class-based cultural capital and differential identity formation among working-class and middle-class pupils.

**Peer groups**: Increasingly influential during adolescence, peer groups may reinforce or challenge family values. Chapman emphasises their role in youth identity formation and subcultural participation.

**Mass media**: Television, social media, advertising and digital platforms shape attitudes, aspirations and lifestyle choices. Contemporary relevance includes influencer culture and online identity construction.

**Religion**: For many UK families, religious institutions provide moral frameworks, community belonging and cultural traditions, particularly within Muslim, Hindu, Sikh and Christian communities.

**Workplace**: Adult socialisation into occupational roles, organisational cultures and professional identities.

### Resocialisation

Resocialisation involves learning new norms and values that replace previously learned patterns. Examples include:

- Adaptation to university life
- Military training
- Immigration and cultural adjustment
- Religious conversion
- Recovery from addiction

## Identity Formation

### Nature of Identity

Identity encompasses an individual's sense of self, shaped by social categories, personal characteristics and group memberships. Contemporary sociology distinguishes between:

**Personal identity**: Unique characteristics, biography and self-perception
**Social identity**: Derived from group memberships and social categories

### Multiple Identities

Modern British individuals typically hold multiple, intersecting identities based on:

- **Gender identity**: Masculine, feminine, non-binary expressions shaped by biological sex, cultural expectations and personal identification
- **Ethnic identity**: Connections to cultural heritage, traditions, language and ethnic communities
- **Class identity**: Socioeconomic position influencing lifestyle, opportunities and cultural consumption
- **National identity**: Belonging to British, English, Scottish, Welsh or Northern Irish communities
- **Age identity**: Generational experiences and life-stage positioning
- **Sexual identity**: Heterosexual, lesbian, gay, bisexual orientations and LGBTQ+ communities
- **Religious identity**: Faith traditions and spiritual practices
- **Disability identity**: Experiences of physical or mental impairment

Heidi Safia Mirza's concept of "intersectionality" (drawn from Kimberlé Crenshaw's framework) proves particularly valuable for understanding how British Muslim women, for example, experience overlapping dimensions of gender, ethnicity and religion simultaneously.

### Hybrid Identities

In multicultural Britain, many individuals develop hybrid identities combining elements from multiple cultural traditions. Second and third-generation British Asians, for instance, may blend Pakistani or Indian cultural heritage with British contemporary culture, creating distinctive fusion identities. This challenges simplistic assimilation models and recognises cultural fluidity.

### Postmodern Perspectives on Identity

Contemporary theorists suggest identity has become increasingly fluid, fragmented and voluntaristic. Individuals "pick and mix" from available cultural resources rather than inheriting fixed identities from families or communities. However, Diane Reay's research cautions that identity choices remain constrained by class position, economic resources and educational opportunities.

## Theoretical Perspectives

### Functionalism

Functionalist sociologists emphasise socialisation's role in maintaining social stability and consensus. Talcott Parsons argued that primary socialisation internalises core values, creating shared culture and social solidarity. Schools provide secondary socialisation preparing young people for adult roles through meritocratic selection.

**Strengths**: Recognises socialisation's importance in cultural transmission and social order
**Limitations**: Overlooks conflict, diversity and unequal power relations in socialisation processes

### Marxism

Marxist approaches view socialisation as ideological conditioning that reproduces class inequality. The dominant class transmits values justifying capitalism (individualism, competition, materialism) through families, education and media. Working-class children learn subordinate identities accepting their structural disadvantage.

**Strengths**: Highlights power dimensions and class reproduction
**Limitations**: Potentially deterministic, underestimating resistance and agency

### Feminism

Feminist perspectives examine how socialisation creates and maintains gender inequality. Families, schools and media transmit patriarchal values, channelling girls toward subordinate, domestic roles whilst encouraging male assertiveness and ambition. Heidi Safia Mirza's work extends this analysis by examining how British educational institutions particularly disadvantage Black Caribbean boys whilst creating specific barriers for Muslim girls.

**Strengths**: Reveals gendered nature of socialisation and identity formation
**Limitations**: Earlier feminism sometimes neglected differences among women based on ethnicity and class

### Interactionism

Symbolic interactionists focus on how identity emerges through social interaction and interpretation. Charles Cooley's "looking-glass self" suggests we develop self-concepts by imagining how others perceive us. Erving Goffman's dramaturgical approach treats social life as performance where individuals manage impressions strategically.

**Strengths**: Recognises active identity construction and negotiation
**Limitations**: May underplay structural constraints on identity formation

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Case study method**: Use contemporary examples of identity negotiation among British youth, including social media identity construction, hybrid cultural identities and youth subcultures. Ken Browne's textbooks provide excellent UK-focused case studies.

**Ethnographic extracts**: Share research findings from Diane Reay's work on class and education or Heidi Safia Mirza's studies of ethnic minority achievement to demonstrate authentic sociological investigation.

**Personal reflection activities**: Carefully structured exercises exploring students' own socialisation experiences can enhance engagement, but require sensitivity regarding family diversity, cultural backgrounds and personal circumstances.

**Media analysis**: Examine gender representations in advertising, ethnic diversity in television programming, or class stereotypes in reality television to develop critical media literacy.

### Assessment Considerations

**AQA GCSE**: 10-mark questions requiring explanation of socialisation processes with appropriate examples. Students must demonstrate understanding of agencies of socialisation and their differential effects.

**AQA A-Level**: Extended essay questions (20-30 marks) requiring application of theoretical perspectives to identity formation, evaluation of competing explanations and synthesis of research evidence.

**OCR GCSE**: Source-based questions requiring students to apply sociological concepts to novel scenarios, demanding flexible understanding rather than rote learning.

### Common Misconceptions

**Socialisation as passive absorption**: Students often view socialisation as one-directional transmission rather than active negotiation. Emphasise agency, resistance and interpretation.

**Culture as static**: Challenge essentialist views of cultures as fixed or homogeneous. Highlight cultural change, diversity within cultures and hybrid formations.

**Identity as singular**: Address tendency to view individuals as having single identities rather than multiple, intersecting identity dimensions.

**Nature versus nurture dichotomy**: Students may oversimplify debates about biological versus social influences. Introduce nuanced positions recognising interaction between genetic predispositions and social shaping.

### Inclusive Practice

Given Britain's multicultural character, teachers must:

- Represent diverse family forms (single-parent, same-sex parents, extended families, reconstituted families)
- Include non-Western cultural examples alongside British mainstream culture
- Address contemporary identity categories including non-binary gender identities
- Recognise working-class cultural practices and identities positively rather than through deficit models
- Challenge stereotypes about ethnic minority cultures whilst respecting genuine cultural differences

Diane Reay's research (2017) reminds educators that classroom discussions of culture and identity inevitably intersect with students' lived experiences of class inequality and cultural marginalisation.

## Links to Other Curriculum Areas

Socialisation, Culture and Identity connects with:

- **PSHE education**: Identity development, relationships and diversity
- **Citizenship**: British values, cultural diversity and community cohesion
- **English Literature**: Character development and social contexts in texts
- **History**: Cultural change and identity formation across historical periods
- **Religious Studies**: Faith identities and religious socialisation

This interdisciplinary potential enables powerful cross-curricular projects examining identity in contemporary Britain.
