# The Media

## Overview and Curriculum Context

The Media is a core topic within GCSE and A-Level Sociology specifications across all major UK exam boards. It examines how media institutions, representations, and consumption patterns shape and reflect social relations, inequalities, and power structures. The topic connects fundamentally to questions of ideology, identity, social control, and cultural production.

**Exam Board Coverage:**
- **AQA A-Level Sociology (7192)**: Optional topic under Paper 2 (Topics in Sociology)
- **AQA GCSE Sociology (8192)**: Covered within 'Socialisation, culture and identity'
- **OCR A-Level Sociology (H580)**: Part of 'Globalisation and the digital social world'
- **WJEC/Eduqas**: Integrated across 'Culture and Identity' units

The topic requires students to engage critically with media content whilst applying sociological theories and research methodologies to understand media's role in contemporary British society.

## Key Theoretical Perspectives

### Marxist Approaches

Marxist theorists view the media as part of the ideological state apparatus that maintains ruling class hegemony. The media, largely owned by capitalist corporations, promotes dominant ideology that legitimises inequality and discourages working-class consciousness.

**Key concepts:**
- **Hegemony** (Gramsci): The media secures consent for capitalism through cultural dominance rather than force
- **False consciousness**: Media representations prevent the working class from recognising exploitation
- **Ownership and control**: Concentration of media ownership in few hands (News Corp, Daily Mail Group)

**Contemporary relevance**: Analysis of billionaire-owned newspapers (Murdoch, Rothermere, Barclay brothers) and their political influence during elections and Brexit referendum. The **Glasgow University Media Group** research (Greg Philo and colleagues) has provided extensive evidence of media bias in reporting industrial disputes, presenting strikers negatively whilst rarely questioning management perspectives.

### Pluralist Perspectives

Pluralism argues that the media reflects diverse viewpoints in democratic societies, with audiences exercising choice and power through consumption decisions. Competition between media outlets ensures variety.

**Key arguments:**
- Market forces compel media to respond to audience demands
- Press freedom enables dissenting voices
- New media has democratised content creation
- Professional journalistic standards ensure balance

**Criticisms**: Steve Chapman's textbook *Sociology for AQA* (2015, 5th edition) highlights how pluralist perspectives underestimate structural inequalities in media access and the persistent under-representation of working-class and minority ethnic perspectives in mainstream media.

### Feminist Perspectives

Feminist media analysis examines gendered representations and the media's role in reproducing patriarchal ideology. Research demonstrates persistent objectification, trivialisation, and stereotyping of women across media forms.

**Key research findings:**
- Women remain significantly under-represented in news media as experts and decision-makers
- **Laura Mulvey's** concept of the 'male gaze' (1975) explains how cinema positions women as objects for masculine pleasure
- **Heidi Safia Mirza** has examined the intersectional dimensions of media representation, highlighting how Black British women face compounded marginalisation
- **Symbolic annihilation** (Gaye Tuchman, 1978): women's absence, trivialisation, or condemnation in media

**Contemporary developments**: The #MeToo movement and increased scrutiny of media industries (BBC pay gap revelations, 2017) demonstrate ongoing feminist concerns whilst showing media's potential for social change.

### Postmodernist Perspectives

Postmodernist theorists emphasise media saturation in contemporary life, the blurring of reality and representation (hyperreality), and the fragmentation of grand narratives.

**Key concepts:**
- **Baudrillard's simulacra**: Media images replace reality; we experience 'copies without originals'
- **Identity as media construction**: Individuals construct fluid identities through media consumption
- **Globalisation and media flows**: Traditional boundaries dissolve

## Ownership, Control and Regulation

### Patterns of Media Ownership

Ken Browne's *Sociology for AQA* (2017, Volume 2) provides detailed analysis of UK media ownership concentration. Key patterns include:

**Horizontal integration**: Companies own multiple outlets in same sector (News UK owns The Sun, The Times, Sunday Times)

**Vertical integration**: Control across production, distribution, exhibition (streaming services like Netflix)

**Cross-media ownership**: Corporations spanning newspapers, television, film, digital platforms

**Transnational conglomerates**: Murdoch's News Corporation, Disney, Comcast operate across national boundaries

**Statistical context**: In 2020, three companies (News UK, Daily Mail Group, Reach PLC) controlled approximately 90% of national newspaper circulation in the UK.

### Regulation and Public Service Broadcasting

The UK maintains distinctive regulatory frameworks:

**Ofcom** (established 2003): Independent regulator for broadcasting, telecommunications, and postal services. Enforces broadcasting code covering harm, offence, impartiality, and fairness.

**IPSO** (Independent Press Standards Organisation): Self-regulatory body for print media, criticised for lacking independence and enforcement powers following Leveson Inquiry recommendations.

**BBC Charter**: Royal Charter renewed 2017 defines public service obligations including impartiality, universality, and cultural value. Funded through licence fee (£159 annually, 2023), creating independence from commercial pressures whilst facing political scrutiny over governance.

**Teaching implications**: Students should analyse specific regulatory cases (complaints, sanctions) and evaluate effectiveness of different regulatory models, connecting to democratic accountability debates.

## Media Representations

### Social Class

Media representations of class remain significantly skewed towards middle-class perspectives and experiences. Mike Haralambos and Martin Holborn's *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* (9th edition, 2017) documents persistent patterns:

**Working-class representations**:
- Over-representation in crime, benefit fraud, and anti-social behaviour stories
- 'Chavs' and 'poverty porn' programming (Benefits Street, 2014) providing voyeuristic spectacle
- **Owen Jones' *Chavs*** (2011) analyses demonisation of working class in media and politics
- Absence from positions of authority, expertise, and respectability

**Middle-class invisibility**: The middle class appears as 'normal' or universal, their class position unmarked and naturalised.

**Diane Reay's research** on educational inequalities includes analysis of how media representations of 'aspiration' individualise class disadvantage, presenting working-class educational underachievement as personal failure rather than structural inequality.

### Ethnicity and Race

Media representations of minority ethnic groups show historical patterns of stereotyping, marginalisation, and problematisation, though contemporary landscapes show increasing complexity.

**Stereotypical representations**:
- **Stuart Hall et al.'s *Policing the Crisis*** (1978) remains foundational, demonstrating how media constructed 'moral panics' around Black youth and mugging
- Muslims consistently associated with terrorism, extremism, fundamentalism (**Alison Moore, 2013**)
- Asian British populations stereotyped as insular, tradition-bound, or academically successful 'model minorities'

**Heidi Safia Mirza's work** emphasises intersectionality in media analysis. Her research demonstrates how Black British women experience specific forms of media marginalisation distinct from both white women and Black men. Her edited collection *Black British Feminism* (1997) includes media representation analysis.

**Contemporary developments**:
- Increased diversity in broadcasting (BBC diversity targets)
- Social media enabling counter-narratives and minority ethnic voice
- Ongoing concerns: lack of minority ethnic journalists (7% of UK journalism workforce vs 13% population, 2018 data)

### Gender

Gender representations demonstrate both continuity and change, requiring nuanced analysis beyond simple 'improved' or 'worsened' narratives.

**Traditional stereotypes persist**:
- Women as domestic, decorative, dependent, or sexual objects
- Men as authoritative, active, breadwinners, problem-solvers
- Under-representation of women in news media (globally, only 24% news subjects are women – **GMMP 2015**)

**Developments and contradictions**:
- Increased visibility of successful women professionals in media
- 'Post-feminist' media suggesting equality achieved whilst maintaining sexist content
- 'Lad culture' resurgence in men's magazines and advertising
- Scrutiny of body image pressures and mental health impacts

**Age intersections**: Older women face particular marginalisation, whilst older men retain authority and visibility (news presenters, experts).

### Disability

Disability representation remains significantly problematic:
- Under-representation across all media forms
- **Paul Hunt's stereotypes** (1991): pitiable, object of ridicule, sinister/evil, super-cripple, burden, sexually abnormal
- **Tom Shakespeare** (disability studies scholar) critiques 'medical model' focus presenting disability as individual tragedy
- Campaigning for authentic representation (disabled actors in disabled roles)

## New Media and Digital Technologies

### Transformations in Media Production and Consumption

Digital technologies have fundamentally restructured media landscapes since the late 1990s, creating what some theorists term 'Web 2.0' characterised by user-generated content, interactivity, and convergence.

**Key developments**:
- **Participatory culture**: Audiences become producers (citizen journalism, YouTube content, blogging)
- **Convergence**: Technologies, industries, content, and audiences merge (smartphones as multi-media devices)
- **Social media platforms**: Facebook (2004), Twitter (2006), Instagram (2010), TikTok (2016) reshape communication
- **On-demand consumption**: Netflix, iPlayer, Spotify enable time-shifted, personalised media use

### Optimistic Perspectives on New Media

**Democratisation arguments**:
- Barrier reduction to media production and distribution
- Alternative voices challenge mainstream narratives (Black Lives Matter, #MeToo movements organised via social media)
- Wikileaks and investigative journalism platforms enable scrutiny of powerful institutions
- Global connectivity and transnational social movements

Steve Chapman's examination materials emphasise evaluating whether new media genuinely transfers power or maintains existing hierarchies through different mechanisms.

### Critical Perspectives on New Media

**Digital divides**:
- Access inequalities persist by class, age, ethnicity, geography (rural broadband access)
- **Skills gaps**: critical digital literacy unevenly distributed
- Global North/South technological disparities

**Surveillance and data exploitation**:
- **Shoshana Zuboff's 'surveillance capitalism'** (2019): tech companies monetise personal data
- State surveillance capabilities expanded (Investigatory Powers Act 2016)
- Privacy erosion normalised

**Echo chambers and filter bubbles**:
- Algorithms personalise content, potentially limiting exposure to diverse viewpoints
- **Fake news and misinformation**: 2016 US election and Brexit referendum concerns
- Polarisation of political discourse

**Mental health concerns**:
- Cyberbullying and online harassment
- Social comparison and body image issues
- Screen time and wellbeing debates

### Implications for Democracy

Research examines whether new media strengthens or undermines democratic participation:

**Positive aspects**:
- Political engagement opportunities (e-petitions, online activism)
- Accountability mechanisms (politicians scrutinised via social media)
- Grassroots organisation and mobilisation

**Concerns**:
- **Misinformation spread**: Difficulty distinguishing reliable sources
- **Political manipulation**: Cambridge Analytica scandal (2018) revealed data harvesting for targeted political advertising
- **Slacktivism**: Online engagement may substitute for substantive political action

## Media Effects and Audience Theories

### Hypodermic Syringe Model

This early effects model suggests media messages are directly 'injected' into passive audiences who accept them uncritically. Associated with 1930s-1940s research and mass society theory.

**Limitations**: Oversimplifies audience agency, ignores selective interpretation, lacks empirical support. However, moral panics around violent video games or 'copycat' crimes often implicitly reference this model.

### Uses and Gratifications Theory

Reverses effects focus by examining why audiences actively choose particular media to satisfy needs:
- Information and education
- Personal identity and self-comparison
- Social interaction and relationships
- Entertainment and escapism

**Teaching applications**: Students can conduct audience research examining their own media use patterns and motivations, connecting to methodological skills.

### Encoding/Decoding Model

**Stuart Hall's model** (1973) represents sophisticated understanding of media communication:

- **Encoding**: Producers create messages with intended meanings (dominant ideology)
- **Decoding**: Audiences interpret messages through their social positioning

**Three reading positions**:
1. **Dominant-hegemonic**: Accept intended meaning
2. **Negotiated**: Accept general framework but resist specific elements
3. **Oppositional**: Reject intended meaning, read against grain

This model enables analysis of how different social groups (by class, ethnicity, gender) may interpret identical media content differently.

### Reception Analysis and Ethnography

Building on Hall's work, researchers conduct empirical studies of actual audiences:

- **David Morley's *Nationwide* study** (1980): Different occupational groups decoded television programme variously
- Emphasises interpretive communities sharing cultural resources
- Connects media consumption to everyday life contexts

## Violence and Media Effects Debate

This remains contentious, with significant implications for regulation and censorship debates.

**Concerns about media violence**:
- Desensitisation to violence
- Imitation ('copycat' effects)
- Aggressive behaviour normalisation
- Psychological harm to children

**Research evidence complexity**:
- **Laboratory experiments** (Bandura's Bobo doll studies, 1961): Show imitation under experimental conditions but questionable ecological validity
- **Correlation studies**: Association between violent media consumption and aggression doesn't establish causation
- **Longitudinal research**: Mixed findings; effects appear small and mediated by numerous factors

**Alternative explanations**:
- Violence has deeper social roots (poverty, inequality, family dysfunction)
- Catharsis hypothesis: Media violence may reduce aggression
- Contextual factors crucial (parental mediation, existing attitudes)

**Policy implications**: BBFC age classifications, watershed (21:00) for adult television content, video game ratings (PEGI system). Teachers should encourage critical evaluation of censorship debates.

## Moral Panics

**Stanley Cohen's *Folk Devils and Moral Panics*** (1972) provides essential framework for analysing media amplification of social problems.

**Key features**:
1. Something/someone defined as threat to social values
2. Media presents threat in stereotypical, simplified ways
3. Moral entrepreneurs, experts, politicians respond
4. Social control measures escalated
5. Condition disappears or deeper structural issues ignored

**British examples for classroom analysis**:
- 1960s Mods and Rockers (Cohen's original study)
- 1970s Mugging (Hall et al.)
- 1980s Video nasties
- 1990s Acid house and rave culture
- 2000s Hoodies and knife crime
- 2010s Social media dangers, county lines drug dealing

**Analytical framework**: Students should identify stakeholders, examine language and imagery, consider whose interests are served, evaluate proportionality of responses.

## Global Media and Cultural Imperialism

### Cultural Imperialism Thesis

Argues that Western (particularly American) media corporations dominate global media flows, imposing Western values and undermining local cultures.

**Evidence**:
- Hollywood dominance of global film markets
- American television format exports
- English language hegemony online
- Coca-colonisation and homogenisation of culture

**Counter-arguments**:
- **Cultural hybridity**: Local cultures adapt and blend global influences (Bollywood)
- **Reverse flows**: Korean Wave (K-pop), telenovelas, British television exports
- Active audiences reinterpret global media through local frameworks
- Orientalism in cultural imperialism thesis (assumes non-Western passivity)

### Globalisation and Media

Mike Haralambos' texts examine how media both reflects and drives globalisation processes:

- Time-space compression: instant global communication
- Transnational corporations and media conglomerates
- Diaspora communities maintain connections via media
- Global social movements coordinate online
- Cosmopolitan identities alongside reasserted nationalism

## Practical Teaching Strategies

### Media Analysis Skills

Develop students' ability to 'read' media texts sociologically:

**Content analysis methods**:
1. Select sample (newspapers, television news, advertisements)
2. Develop coding categories (representation patterns, language choices, visual composition)
3. Systematic recording
4. Quantitative and qualitative interpretation
5. Connect findings to sociological theories

**Semiotic analysis**: Examine signs, symbols, connotations, ideological meanings (particularly effective with advertising)

**Representation audits**: Count and categorise whose voices appear, in what roles, with what authority across media sample

### Contemporary Case Studies

Use current examples to demonstrate concepts' relevance:

- **Brexit coverage analysis**: Compare newspaper representations across political spectrum
- **COVID-19 pandemic reporting**: Government messaging, 'fake news' concerns, international comparisons
- **Black Lives Matter**: Social media organisation, mainstream media framing, representation debates
