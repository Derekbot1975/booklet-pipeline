# Families and Households

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Families and Households is a foundational topic within UK Sociology curricula, featuring prominently in A-level specifications across all major exam boards. AQA dedicates an entire paper (Paper 2, Section A) to this topic, whilst OCR includes it within their 'Socialisation, Culture and Identity' component, and Eduqas (WJEC) addresses it in Component 2. The topic examines the diverse forms, functions and changes in family structures within contemporary British society, with particular emphasis on social policy, demographic trends and theoretical perspectives.

This topic typically accounts for 20-25% of A-level Sociology assessment and is considered essential foundational knowledge for understanding wider sociological concepts such as socialisation, social structure, inequality and social change.

## Core Theoretical Perspectives

### Functionalist Perspectives

**George Peter Murdock** (1949) identified four universal functions of the nuclear family: sexual, reproductive, economic and educational. His cross-cultural study of 250 societies concluded that the nuclear family was a universal institution, though this has been extensively critiqued for ethnocentrism and heteronormative assumptions.

**Talcott Parsons** (1955) developed the 'functional fit theory', arguing that family structure adapts to meet societal needs. He distinguished between the pre-industrial extended family and the modern nuclear family, which he viewed as ideally suited to industrial society through geographical and social mobility. Parsons identified two 'irreducible functions': primary socialisation of children and stabilisation of adult personalities. Haralambos and Holborn (2013) in *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* provide extensive critical analysis of Parsons, noting his perspective has been challenged for ignoring family diversity and dysfunctional aspects such as domestic violence.

**Teaching implications**: Teachers should present functionalist perspectives as historically significant but encourage critical evaluation. Ken Browne's *Sociology for AQA* (2019) provides accessible student-facing material that balances explanation with contemporary critique.

### Marxist and Feminist Critiques

**Friedrich Engels** (1884) argued that the monogamous nuclear family emerged with private property to ensure legitimate heirs for inheritance. Contemporary Marxists like **Eli Zaretsky** (1976) describe the family as serving capitalism through reproducing labour power and providing an 'illusory haven' that cushions workers from exploitation whilst ultimately maintaining false consciousness.

**Feminist perspectives** challenge the functionalist view of families as harmonious units:

- **Marxist feminists** such as **Margaret Benston** (1972) highlight women's unpaid domestic labour subsidising capitalism
- **Radical feminists** including **Delphy and Leonard** (1992) conceptualise the family as a patriarchal institution where men exploit women's domestic and emotional labour
- **Difference feminists** such as **Heidi Safia Mirza** emphasise that Black and minority ethnic women's family experiences differ significantly from white, middle-class norms. Mirza's research (1997) challenges deficit models of Black Caribbean families, demonstrating the strength and resilience of female-headed households

**Diane Reay's** research (2005) on educational involvement highlights the 'emotional capitalism' of contemporary motherhood, where middle-class mothers perform intensive emotion work to secure their children's educational advantage, revealing class-based inequalities in family practices.

### Postmodern and Interpretivist Approaches

**Anthony Giddens** (1992) argues that late modernity has transformed intimate relationships through the 'pure relationship' based on emotional satisfaction rather than economic necessity. **Ulrich Beck** and **Elisabeth Beck-Gernsheim** (1995) discuss the 'negotiated family' where roles and relationships are increasingly individualised and democratised.

**Jeffrey Weeks, Catherine Donovan and Brian Heaphy** (2001) researched 'families of choice' amongst LGBTQ+ communities, challenging heteronormative family definitions. This research is particularly relevant for contemporary UK curricula given legal changes including civil partnerships (2004) and equal marriage (2013).

## Family Diversity in Contemporary Britain

### Types of Family Diversity

The **Rapoport and Rapoport** (1982) framework identifies five types of diversity relevant to contemporary Britain:

1. **Organisational diversity**: variations in family structure, roles and relationships
2. **Cultural diversity**: differences based on ethnicity, religion and nationality
3. **Class diversity**: variations in family life across socio-economic groups
4. **Life course diversity**: changes throughout individual lifetimes
5. **Cohort diversity**: generational differences in family experiences

**Teaching implications**: Steve Chapman's materials emphasise using contemporary statistics from the Office for National Statistics (ONS) to illustrate demographic change. The 2021 Census data shows significant increases in cohabitation, single-person households and reconstituted families.

### Specific Family Forms

**Nuclear families** now constitute approximately 38% of UK families (ONS, 2021), representing a significant decline from the 1960s. However, **Peter Willmott and Michael Young** (1973) demonstrated that even when the classic extended family declined, modified extended families maintained strong kinship networks through telephone contact and mutual support.

**Single-parent families** represent approximately 15% of families with dependent children, with approximately 90% headed by mothers. Research challenges pathological representations: **Joan Chandler** (1993) argues that poverty, not family structure, creates disadvantage. Haralambos emphasises the importance of distinguishing between correlation and causation when examining outcomes for children in different family forms.

**Reconstituted (stepfamilies)** account for approximately 11% of families with dependent children. **Allan and Crow** (2001) discuss the complexity of relationships and potential role conflicts in these families.

**Same-sex families** have gained legal recognition through civil partnerships and marriage equality. Research by **Susan Golombok** (2015) demonstrates that children's wellbeing depends on relationship quality rather than parental gender or sexual orientation.

**Beanpole families**: increasing life expectancy has created vertically extended but horizontally narrow family structures with multiple generations but fewer siblings or cousins. **Julia Brannen** (2003) researched four-generation families and transmission of resources across generations.

### Ethnic Diversity in Family Structures

Research challenges stereotypical representations of minority ethnic families. **Roger Ballard** (1982, 1990) demonstrated the diversity within South Asian families and the adaptation of family forms in British contexts. Heidi Safia Mirza's work (1997) challenges deficit models of African-Caribbean families, highlighting how **matrifocal families** represent adaptation and resilience rather than pathology.

**Geoffrey Driver** (1982) identified strengths in African-Caribbean family structures, whilst critics like **Errol Lawrence** (1982) warned against cultural explanations that ignore structural racism and economic disadvantage.

## Marriage, Cohabitation and Divorce

### Demographic Trends

UK marriage rates have declined significantly: in 1972, there were 426,000 marriages compared to 219,000 in 2020 (ONS). Average age at first marriage has increased to 38 for men and 35.7 for women (ONS, 2020).

**Cohabitation** has increased dramatically: approximately 3.6 million cohabiting couples in the UK (2021 Census). **Barlow, Duncan, Edwards and James** (2005) researched the 'cohabitation myth', revealing many couples incorrectly believe they have 'common law marriage' rights.

**Divorce rates** peaked in 1993 at 165,000 divorces but declined to approximately 108,000 in 2019 (ONS). **Robert Chester** (1985) coined the term 'neo-conventional family' to describe serial monogamy. The Divorce Reform Act 1969 and subsequent legislation made divorce more accessible, particularly following the 'irretrievable breakdown' principle.

### Explanatory Factors

Ken Browne (2019) identifies multiple factors explaining these trends:

- **Secularisation**: declining religious influence reduces stigma around divorce and cohabitation
- **Legal changes**: successive legislation from the Matrimonial Causes Act 1857 through to the Divorce, Dissolution and Separation Act 2020
- **Women's economic independence**: increased female employment and earning power
- **Rising expectations**: Functionalists like **Ronald Fletcher** (1966) argue higher expectations of marriage lead to divorce when relationships fail to meet these standards
- **Individualisation thesis**: Beck and Beck-Gernsheim emphasise personal fulfilment over institutional obligation

## Social Policy and the Family

### Historical Perspectives

**Jacques Donzelot** (1977) identified the 'policing of families' whereby state agencies increasingly surveilled and regulated family life. **Diane Reay's** research demonstrates how contemporary educational policies intensify parental responsibilities, particularly for mothers.

### Contemporary Policy Debates

The **New Right perspective** (particularly influential during the 1980s-90s) emphasised traditional family values. **Patricia Morgan** (1999) and **Dennis and Erdos** (1992) argued that lone parenthood, particularly amongst working-class communities, created social problems including educational underachievement and criminality.

Contrastingly, **feminist sociologists** critique policies that reinforce traditional gender roles. The **Child Support Agency** (1993) and subsequent iterations have been analysed as reinforcing maternal care responsibilities whilst emphasising fathers' financial obligations.

**Sure Start** programmes (introduced 1998) represented New Labour's approach to supporting families in disadvantaged areas, though **Norman Glass** (2005), the programme's architect, later critiqued its dilution. Research by **Jay Belsky** and others demonstrated modest positive impacts on parenting and child development.

Recent Conservative policies including **Universal Credit** have been critiqued for penalising larger families through the 'two-child limit' (introduced 2017). The **Marriage Allowance** (2015) has been analysed as promoting particular family forms through fiscal incentives.

### Family Policy Cross-culturally

**Allan and Crow** (2001) distinguish between:
- **Familistic policies**: supporting traditional families (e.g., France's pronatalist policies)
- **Individualistic policies**: supporting individuals regardless of family form (e.g., Scandinavian welfare states)

Chapman's curriculum materials usefully employ comparative analysis to evaluate UK approaches against international alternatives.

## Childhood and Generational Relations

### Social Construction of Childhood

**Philippe Ariès** (1960) argued childhood is a social construction emerging in early modern Europe. His methodology has been critiqued, but the fundamental insight—that childhood varies historically and culturally—remains influential.

**Neil Postman** (1982) controversially argued that childhood was 'disappearing' due to television and media access. Contemporary debates focus on digital technology and social media impacts on childhood experiences.

**Sue Palmer** (2006) coined 'toxic childhood', arguing contemporary lifestyles harm children's development, though critics highlight middle-class moral panic elements and lack of robust evidence.

### The 'March of Progress' Debate

**Edward Shorter** (1975) represents the optimistic view that childhood has improved through:
- Reduced infant mortality
- Compulsory education
- Child protection legislation (Children Act 1989, 2004)
- Enhanced children's rights

**Conflict perspectives** challenge this narrative:

- **Child poverty**: approximately 4.3 million children lived in relative poverty in the UK (2019/20, before housing costs)
- **Inequality between childhoods**: Diane Reay's research demonstrates vastly different childhood experiences across social classes
- **Child abuse**: approximately 50,000 children subject to child protection plans in England (2020)
- **'Hurried childhood'**: **David Elkind** (1981) argued children face excessive pressure

### Age Patriarchy

**Shulamith Firestone** (1979) argued children represent an oppressed class. Contemporary research examines children's agency whilst acknowledging vulnerability and structural constraints.

**Berry Mayall's** research (1994, 2002) applies sociological analysis to children's perspectives and experiences, challenging adult-centric sociology.

## Domestic Labour and Power Relations

### The Gender Division of Labour

Research consistently demonstrates women perform disproportionate domestic labour despite increased female employment. **Ann Oakley's** pioneering research (1974) *The Sociology of Housework* identified housework as monotonous, fragmented and undervalued, establishing domestic labour as legitimate sociological inquiry.

**Mary Boulton** (1983) challenged symmetrical family claims by distinguishing between 'helping' with childcare and taking responsibility for it—typically remaining women's domain.

Contemporary time-use studies (ONS, 2016) show women perform approximately 60% more unpaid work than men, though gaps narrow amongst younger cohorts.

### The Symmetrical Family Thesis

**Peter Willmott and Michael Young** (1973) argued the family was becoming more equal and home-centred, identifying three stages of family development culminating in the 'Stage 3' symmetrical family characterised by joint conjugal roles.

**Ann Oakley** (1974) critiqued this thesis, arguing their evidence was weak (men performing domestic tasks infrequently doesn't constitute equality) and that fundamental inequalities persisted.

**Jonathan Gershuny** (1994) provided longitudinal evidence showing gradual movement towards greater equality, through 'lagged adaptation'—men's contributions increasing as women's employment becomes normalised.

### Resources and Decision-Making

**Jan Pahl's** research (1989, 1993) on money management identified different systems:
- Female whole wage system (typically in low-income families)
- Male whole wage system
- Pooling systems (appearing most equitable but often concealing inequalities)
- Independent management systems

**Vogler and Pahl** (1994) demonstrated that apparent equality in pooling systems often masked men's greater influence over major financial decisions.

**Edgell** (1980) distinguished between 'very important' decisions (typically male-dominated), 'important' decisions (joint) and 'less important' decisions (female-dominated, particularly concerning children and household management).

### Domestic Violence

Domestic violence represents the 'dark side' of family life ignored by functionalist perspectives. **Dobash and Dobash** (1979, 1992) researched violence against women, identifying patriarchal power structures as fundamental causes.

**Ansley** (1976) described wives as 'takers of shit' absorbing male frustrations generated by capitalism. The Crime Survey for England and Wales (2020) estimated 1.6 million people aged 16-74 experienced domestic abuse in the year ending March 2020, with women disproportionately affected.

The Domestic Violence Act 2004 and subsequent legislation reflects growing recognition, though feminist researchers argue enforcement remains inadequate.

## Assessment and Pedagogical Approaches

### Exam Board Specific Requirements

**AQA A-level Sociology (7192)**: Paper 2, Section A (Families and Households) requires students to:
- Demonstrate knowledge of changing family patterns
- Apply theoretical perspectives
- Evaluate the impact of social policy
- Analyse demographic trends using statistical data

Assessment includes 4-mark, 6-mark, 10-mark (application) and 20-mark extended response questions.

**OCR A-level Sociology (H580)**: Component 1 includes family within 'Socialisation, Culture and Identity', emphasising family's role in identity formation and social reproduction.

**Eduqas (WJEC) A-level Sociology**: Component 2, Section A addresses 'Families and Households', with similar theoretical and empirical requirements.

### Effective Teaching Strategies

**Using contemporary data**: Teachers should regularly incorporate ONS statistics, census data and contemporary research. Haralambos and Holborn (2013) provide extensive statistical appendices, whilst Ken Browne's textbooks include regular updates.

**Challenging assumptions**: Diane Reay's work emphasises reflexivity and examining students' own family assumptions. Activities comparing students' family experiences with sociological concepts promote engagement whilst requiring sensitivity.

**Evaluative frameworks**: Steve Chapman's materials emphasise systematic evaluation using:
- Theoretical critiques
- Methodological limitations
- Contemporary relevance
- Alternative perspectives

**Representation and inclusion**: Given increasing family diversity, teachers must ensure curriculum content reflects varied family forms. Heidi Safia Mirza's emphasis on intersectionality reminds us that gender, ethnicity, class and sexuality intersect in shaping family experiences.

### Common Student Misconceptions

1. **Assuming nuclear families are 'normal'**: Teachers must challenge normative assumptions whilst acknowledging statistical patterns
2. **Conflating correlation with causation**: Particularly regarding single parenthood and child outcomes
3. **Oversimplifying theoretical perspectives**: Functionalism isn't inherently 'wrong'; it provides partial insights requiring critical evaluation
4. **Ignoring class and ethnicity**: Family experiences vary significantly by social location

### Synoptic Connections

Families and Households connects with:
- **Education**: Cultural capital transmission, parental involvement, educational inequality (Reay's research bridges both topics)
- **Crime and Deviance**: Family socialisation, domestic violence, moral panic about 'broken families'
- **Beliefs in Society**: Secularisation's impact on marriage and divorce
- **Social Stratification**: Class reproduction through families, gendered inequalities
