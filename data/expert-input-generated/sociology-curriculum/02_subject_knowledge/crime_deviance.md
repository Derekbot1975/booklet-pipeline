# Crime and Deviance

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Crime and Deviance is a core topic within A-level Sociology specifications across all major UK exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas). This topic typically accounts for 20-25% of A-level assessment and requires students to understand theoretical perspectives, contemporary debates, and empirical research on criminal behaviour and social reactions to rule-breaking.

**Key Curriculum Positioning:**
- **AQA**: Paper 3, Topics in Sociology (one option alongside Beliefs, Media, Theory & Methods)
- **Edexcel**: Paper 3, option alongside Globalisation and Social Inequality
- **OCR**: Component 3, Debates option
- **WJEC Eduqas**: Component 2, Understanding Social Inequalities

As Haralambos and Holborn (2013) emphasise in their seminal textbook *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives*, Crime and Deviance bridges theoretical sociology with empirical policy debates, making it particularly engaging for students whilst demanding rigorous analytical skills.

## Fundamental Concepts and Definitions

### Distinguishing Crime and Deviance

**Crime** refers to acts that break formal written laws and are subject to official sanctions by the state. **Deviance** is broader, encompassing behaviour that violates social norms and expectations, which may or may not be criminal. Ken Browne (2019) in *Sociology for AQA Volume 2* emphasises that deviance is culturally and historically relative—what constitutes deviance varies between societies and across time periods.

**Teaching Implication:** Students must grasp that all crimes are deviant (in most contexts), but not all deviance is criminal. Use contemporary examples: visible tattoos may be considered deviant in certain professional contexts but are not criminal; cannabis possession is criminal but increasingly normalised in some social groups.

### Social Construction of Crime

Crime statistics, particularly the Official Crime Survey (OCS) published by the Office for National Statistics and the Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW), reveal that crime is socially constructed through:
- Legislative processes (law-making)
- Detection and reporting practices
- Media representation
- Public perception and moral panics

Chapman and Haralambos (2013) argue that understanding crime requires examining not just the act itself but the social processes through which behaviours become labelled as criminal.

## Theoretical Perspectives on Crime and Deviance

### Functionalist Approaches

**Durkheim (1893)** argued that crime is inevitable, universal, and functional for society. His key contributions include:
- **Anomie theory**: Crime increases during periods of rapid social change when normative regulation breaks down
- **Boundary maintenance**: Crime reinforces collective conscience by clarifying moral boundaries
- **Social change function**: Deviance can challenge outdated norms and promote social evolution

**Merton's Strain Theory (1938)** adapts Durkheim for American society, identifying five adaptations to the strain between cultural goals (wealth/success) and institutional means:
1. Conformity (accept both)
2. Innovation (accept goals, reject means—typical criminal response)
3. Ritualism (reject goals, accept means)
4. Retreatism (reject both)
5. Rebellion (replace both with alternatives)

**Teaching Application:** Use contemporary UK examples—gang crime as 'innovation', benefit fraud, corporate crime. Critique: Why do middle-class individuals with access to legitimate means still commit crime?

### Subcultural Theories

**A.K. Cohen (1955)** developed subcultural theory to explain working-class delinquency as a collective solution to status frustration. Working-class boys unable to achieve middle-class status through education create alternative subcultural values that invert mainstream norms.

**Cloward and Ohlin (1960)** identified three delinquent subcultures:
- **Criminal subcultures**: organised, utilitarian crime in stable working-class areas
- **Conflict subcultures**: gang violence in disorganised areas
- **Retreatist subcultures**: drug use by 'double failures'

**Critical Perspective:** Ken Browne (2019) notes these theories are historically specific to post-war America and assume value consensus. They struggle to explain middle-class crime, white-collar crime, and female criminality.

### Marxist and Neo-Marxist Perspectives

**Traditional Marxism** views crime as inevitable under capitalism:
- Criminogenic capitalism creates conditions for crime through poverty, inequality, and alienation
- Selective law enforcement targets working-class 'street crime' whilst ignoring corporate crime
- Law serves ruling-class interests (Chambliss, 1975)

**The New Criminology (Taylor, Walton & Young, 1973)** synthesised Marxism with labelling theory, arguing criminology must consider:
- The wider origins of the deviant act (capitalism)
- The immediate origins (individual circumstances)
- The act itself (often deliberate choice)
- Societal reaction
- Impact on the deviant's subsequent behaviour

**Critical Realist Criminology (Lea & Young, 1984)** developed Left Realism in response to rising crime in working-class communities. Unlike radical criminology that romanticised criminals as rebels, Left Realism takes crime seriously as a real problem requiring practical solutions. The **Square of Crime** identifies four interacting elements:
- The state and its agencies
- The public
- Offenders
- Victims

**Teaching Context:** Use contemporary UK debates about police funding, knife crime, and county lines drug operations. Discuss why working-class communities bear the greatest burden of both crime victimisation and criminal justice intervention.

### Interactionist and Labelling Perspectives

**Becker (1963)**: "Deviance is not a quality of the act the person commits, but rather a consequence of the application by others of rules and sanctions." Labelling theory examines how social reactions create deviance through:

**Primary deviance**: Initial rule-breaking (often trivial, widespread)
**Secondary deviance**: Deviance resulting from societal reaction and labelling

**Lemert (1951)** argued that public labelling, stigmatisation, and exclusion can create a self-fulfilling prophecy, whereby individuals accept their deviant label and organise their identity around it.

**Cicourel (1976)** demonstrated how police use typifications (stereotypical assumptions) about 'typical criminals' that lead to differential enforcement. His study of juvenile justice in California showed middle-class youth were more likely to be counselled whilst working-class youth were arrested for similar offences.

**Braithwaite's Reintegrative Shaming (1989)** distinguishes:
- **Disintegrative shaming**: stigmatises and excludes offenders (increases crime)
- **Reintegrative shaming**: disapproves of acts whilst maintaining offender's community bonds (reduces crime)

**Practical Implication:** Examine restorative justice programmes in UK schools and youth offending services. Discuss how labelling theory supports diversion from criminal justice systems.

### Feminist Perspectives

Feminist criminology addresses the **gender gap in crime statistics** (approximately 4:1 male:female ratio for recorded crime) and the historical neglect of female offenders and victims. Heidi Safia Mirza's broader work on intersectionality informs contemporary feminist criminology by highlighting how gender interacts with race and class.

**Heidensohn (1985)** identified patriarchal controls limiting female criminality:
- Domestic sphere: expectations of caregiving limit opportunity
- Public sphere: fear of male violence and reputation damage
- Workplace controls: supervision, dress codes, sexual harassment

**Smart (1976)** critiqued mainstream criminology as operating a "double deviance" standard—women who offend are judged both as criminals and as failing feminine roles.

**Contemporary Issues:**
- Rising female imprisonment (UK female prison population increased 33% 1993-2018)
- Gender-specific pathways to crime (abuse, exploitation, poverty)
- Criminalisation of survival strategies (sex work, shoplifting)

**Carlen (1988)** studied women in Scottish prisons, identifying:
- **Class deal**: material rewards for conformity
- **Gender deal**: emotional rewards of family life
Both deals must fail before women turn to crime.

**Teaching Application:** Use case studies like the criminalisation of victims of trafficking, corporate masculinity and fraud (examine gender dimensions of banking scandals), and female gang involvement.

### Right Realism

**Wilson & Kelling's Broken Windows Theory (1982)** argues visible disorder signals absence of social control, inviting further crime. Minor offences (vandalism, graffiti) should be strictly controlled to prevent escalation.

**Charles Murray (1990)** controversially argued Britain was developing an 'underclass' with deviant values, particularly concerning single mothers and benefit dependency.

**Rational Choice Theory** (Clarke, 1980) assumes offenders make cost-benefit calculations, therefore deterrence requires:
- Increased surveillance (CCTV)
- Target hardening (security measures)
- Swift, certain punishment

**Critique:** Ken Browne (2019) notes Right Realism ignores structural causes of crime (poverty, inequality), potentially blames victims ('cultures of poverty'), and provides theoretical justification for punitive policies that disproportionately affect marginalised groups.

**Policy Influence:** Zero-tolerance policing, Anti-Social Behaviour Orders (ASBOs, 1998-2014), Criminal Behaviour Orders (CBOs, 2014-present).

## Patterns and Trends in Crime Statistics

### Sources of Crime Data

**1. Official Crime Statistics (OCS)**
Police-recorded crime published quarterly by ONS. Limitations include:
- Under-reporting (British Crime Survey estimates only 42% of crimes reported)
- Under-recording by police (facing target pressures)
- Changes in recording practices create artificial trends
- 'Dark figure' of unreported crime

**2. Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW)**
Self-report victimisation survey of 35,000 households. Advantages:
- Captures unreported crime
- Consistent methodology allows trend analysis
Limitations:
- Excludes under-16s, homeless, residents of institutions
- Relies on memory and honesty
- Cannot measure 'victimless crimes' or corporate crime

**3. Self-Report Studies**
Offenders anonymously report criminal behaviour, revealing:
- Crime is widely distributed across social classes
- Gender gap narrows for minor offences
- Vast majority of crime goes undetected

### Class and Crime

Official statistics consistently show working-class overrepresentation in criminal justice system. However, interpretation is contested:

**Realist position**: Working-class people commit more street crime due to:
- Relative deprivation (Lea & Young, 1984)
- Marginalisation and exclusion
- Formation of criminal subcultures

**Social constructionist position**: Statistics reflect:
- Selective law enforcement targeting working-class communities
- Differential criminalisation (street crime vs white-collar crime)
- Middle-class ability to avoid criminalisation

**White-Collar and Corporate Crime**
Sutherland (1949) defined white-collar crime as offences committed by respectable persons in the course of occupation. Examples include:
- Fraud (UK estimated £130bn annually)
- Health and safety violations
- Environmental crimes
- Tax evasion

Haralambos emphasises white-collar crime causes more financial harm and death than conventional crime but receives less attention and lighter sanctions due to:
- Complexity reducing visibility
- Ideological sympathy with business
- Power to influence legislation
- Enforcement agencies lacking resources

**Teaching Context:** Use Grenfell Tower (2017) as case study examining corporate manslaughter, regulatory failure, and class dimensions of victimisation. Compare sentencing for corporate crimes versus benefit fraud.

### Ethnicity and Crime

Highly sensitive area requiring careful, evidence-based teaching. **Key statistics:**
- Black people are 9.7 times more likely to be stopped and searched (Home Office, 2020)
- Black people constitute 13% of prison population but 3.5% of general population
- Overrepresentation varies by offence type

**Explanatory frameworks:**

**1. Realist interpretations** (Lea & Young, 1984):
- Marginalisation of minority ethnic groups
- Relative depriviation
- Media scapegoating creating frustrated identities

**2. Neo-Marxist perspectives** (Gilroy, 1982):
- Crime as political resistance to racism
- 'Myth of black criminality' serving ideological functions
- Colonial forms of policing perpetuated

**3. Institutional racism** (Macpherson Report, 1999):
- Differential enforcement
- Stereotyping influencing stop and search
- Discrimination throughout criminal justice system

**4. Left Realist critique** (Bowling & Phillips, 2002):
- Some ethnic differences in offending are real
- Disproportionality results from complex interaction of discrimination and social factors
- Must acknowledge intra-ethnic variations and intersectionality with class

**Critical Teaching Point:** Diane Reay's work on educational inequalities and working-class experiences provides valuable intersectional context. Racism, poverty, and social exclusion interact to create conditions for both offending and disproportionate criminalisation. Avoid essentialising ethnic groups or suggesting inherent criminality.

### Gender and Crime

Males commit approximately 80% of recorded crime. Explanations include:

**Biological/psychological**: testosterone, socialisation, risk-taking
**Social control**: Heidensohn's triple controls
**Opportunity structures**: Cloward and Ohlin extended to gender
**Masculinity theories**: Messerschmidt (1993) argues crime as resource for 'accomplishing masculinity' when conventional resources unavailable

**Female crime trends:**
- Increasing female arrests for violence (debate whether real increase or changes in police practice)
- Persistent gender gap remains across all categories
- Different pathways to crime (victimisation history, relationship coercion)

**Teaching Application:** Examine moral panics about 'ladette culture' and girl gangs versus empirical evidence. Use Young and Ballard (2017) research on young women in gangs showing exploitation rather than equality.

### Age and Crime

Peak offending age: males 15-19, females 14-15. Age-crime curve shows:
- Rapid increase through adolescence
- Peak in late teens
- Sharp decline through twenties
- Low rates 30+

**Explanations:**
- Status frustration (A.K. Cohen)
- Peer group pressure
- Biological maturation
- Reduced social bonds (marriage, employment)
- Rational calculation (more to lose)

**Contemporary issues:**
- Childhood criminalisation debates (age of criminal responsibility: 10 in England/Wales, compared to 12-16 in most Europe)
- Youth justice reform movements
- School exclusions and criminal justice pipeline

## Contemporary Debates and Applications

### Media and Crime

**Stan Cohen's Folk Devils and Moral Panics (1972)** remains foundational, examining 1960s Mods and Rockers. Identified stages:
1. Initial deviance
2. Media amplification
3. Public concern
4. Moral entrepreneurs demand action
5. Legislative response
6. Deviancy amplification spiral

**Contemporary examples:**
- Knife crime moral panics
- Drill music controversies
- Social media and cybercrime

**Teaching Resource:** Use recent examples like county lines drug dealing, examining how media representation shapes policy responses. Chapman's work on media effects provides accessible frameworks.

### Surveillance and Digital Crime

**Foucault's Panopticism** (1975): surveillance as disciplinary power creating self-regulation. Contemporary applications:
- CCTV (UK has highest per capita coverage globally)
- Digital surveillance
- Algorithmic policing
- Social media monitoring

**Cybercrime categories:**
- Computer-assisted (fraud, harassment)
- Computer-focused (hacking, viruses)
- Digital piracy

**Teaching Context:** Examine tension between security and privacy, particularly post-terrorism legislation (2000 Terrorism Act, 2016 Investigatory Powers Act).

### Globalisation and Crime

**Castells (1998)** identifies global criminal networks exploiting:
- Transnational trafficking (drugs, people, weapons)
- Money laundering
- Cybercrime transcending borders
- State-corporate crime (environmental destruction)

**Green criminology** (Lynch & Stretesky, 2014) examines environmental harms, particularly relevant given climate emergency.

### Criminal Justice Responses

**Punishment paradigms:**

**1. Retribution**: punishment as moral desert
**2. Deterrence**: preventing future crime through sanctions
**3. Rehabilitation**: addressing causes of offending
**4. Incapacitation**: removing offenders from society
**5. Restoration**: repairing harm

**Current UK context:**
- Prison population 83,000+ (2023), significant increase from 45,000 (1993)
- Reoffending rates approximately 48% within one year
- Rising use of community sentences
- Restorative justice expansion in youth justice
