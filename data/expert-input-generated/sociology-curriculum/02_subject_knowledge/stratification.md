# Social Stratification and Inequality

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Social stratification and inequality form a foundational component of A-level Sociology across all major UK examination boards. This topic examines how societies are hierarchically organised and how resources, opportunities and life chances are unequally distributed. The subject appears explicitly in:

- **AQA A-level Sociology** (7192): Paper 1, Section A – Education (examining class and achievement); Paper 2, Section C – Social Inequality (covering social class, gender, ethnicity and age)
- **Edexcel (Pearson) A-level Sociology** (9SO0): Paper 1 includes Education with Theory and Methods; Paper 2 covers Social Inequality
- **OCR A-level Sociology** (H580): Topic areas include Socialisation, Culture and Identity, alongside specific units on inequality
- **WJEC/Eduqas A-level Sociology**: Component 1 addresses Acquiring Culture, whilst Component 2 explicitly covers Understanding Social Inequality

The topic typically receives 15-25% weighting across examination specifications and requires students to apply theoretical perspectives, analyse data and evaluate competing explanations of inequality.

## Defining Social Stratification

### Core Concepts

Social stratification refers to the hierarchical arrangement of individuals and groups within society based on unequal access to scarce resources. As Haralambos and Holborn (2013) define it in their seminal textbook *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives*, stratification involves "structured inequalities between different groupings of people" that are systematic rather than random.

Key characteristics include:

- **Institutionalisation**: Inequality becomes embedded in social structures and institutions
- **Intergenerational transmission**: Social positions are often inherited across generations
- **Legitimation**: Systems of stratification develop ideologies that justify inequality
- **Multi-dimensional nature**: Contemporary stratification operates across multiple axes (class, gender, ethnicity, age, disability)

### Systems of Stratification

Teachers should distinguish between different historical and contemporary systems:

**Slavery**: Ownership of people as property, historically prevalent in ancient societies and early modern colonialism. Whilst formally abolished in the British Empire (1833), its legacy profoundly shapes contemporary racial inequalities, as explored by scholars including Paul Gilroy and Stuart Hall.

**Caste**: Rigid, religiously-sanctioned hierarchies most associated with traditional Hindu societies. Relevant for understanding South Asian diasporic communities in Britain.

**Estates (Feudalism)**: Medieval European system with legally defined orders (clergy, nobility, commoners). Understanding this helps contextualise the evolution of British class structures.

**Social Class**: The dominant form of stratification in contemporary Britain, theoretically more fluid than previous systems but empirically demonstrating significant rigidity.

## Theoretical Perspectives on Stratification

### Classical Sociological Foundations

**Karl Marx (1818-1883)**

Marx's conflict theory remains central to A-level specifications. His economic determinism positions class as the fundamental social division, arising from relationships to the means of production:

- **Bourgeoisie**: Own the means of production (capital, factories, land)
- **Proletariat**: Sell their labour power, alienated from production processes
- **Reserve army of labour**: Unemployed workers who depress wages
- **Lumpenproletariat**: Underclass outside class consciousness

Key Marxist concepts include:

- **False consciousness**: Workers' failure to recognise their exploitation
- **Class consciousness**: Recognition of shared class interests
- **Polarisation**: Inevitable intensification of class divisions under capitalism
- **Revolution**: Predicted overthrow of capitalism by organised workers

**Practical Teaching Note**: Students often struggle with the abstract nature of Marxist terminology. Use contemporary examples such as zero-hours contracts, gig economy workers (Deliveroo riders, Uber drivers) and platform capitalism to illustrate exploitation and alienation.

**Max Weber (1864-1920)**

Weber provided a more nuanced, multidimensional model comprising three interrelated but distinct dimensions:

- **Class (economic)**: Market position and life chances based on economic resources
- **Status (social)**: Social honour, prestige and lifestyle, independent of wealth
- **Party (political)**: Access to power through political organisations

Weber's approach acknowledges that a wealthy entrepreneur (high class) might lack aristocratic status, whilst an impoverished aristocrat retains social prestige. This framework proves particularly useful for understanding contemporary Britain's complex stratification patterns.

### Contemporary Theoretical Developments

**Neo-Marxism**

Writers including Antonio Gramsci introduced **cultural hegemony** – the idea that ruling classes maintain dominance through cultural and ideological means rather than solely through economic control. This concept proves invaluable when examining education, media and cultural institutions.

**Pierre Bourdieu (1930-2002)**

French sociologist Bourdieu's work profoundly influences contemporary British sociology education. His conceptual framework includes:

- **Economic capital**: Financial resources and assets
- **Cultural capital**: Knowledge, skills, education, cultural tastes and dispositions that confer social advantages. Diane Reay's extensive research applies this to British education, demonstrating how middle-class families accumulate advantages
- **Social capital**: Networks, connections and group memberships
- **Symbolic capital**: Recognition and prestige

Bourdieu's concept of **habitus** – deeply ingrained habits, skills and dispositions acquired through socialisation – explains how class reproduction occurs subtly through embodied practices rather than conscious transmission.

**Teaching Application**: Diane Reay's research (2017, *Miseducation: Inequality, Education and the Working Classes*) provides accessible British case studies. She demonstrates how middle-class mothers' intensive educational involvement ('concerted cultivation') contrasts with working-class parenting styles, reproducing inequality across generations.

## Social Class in Contemporary Britain

### Measuring and Defining Class

Unlike historical systems, class boundaries in Britain lack legal definition, creating methodological challenges. Various classification schemes exist:

**The Registrar General's Social Class (RGSC)** (used until 2000)
- Based on occupational skill levels
- Six categories from professional to unskilled manual
- Criticised for gender bias (classifying married women by husband's occupation)

**National Statistics Socio-Economic Classification (NS-SEC)** (2001-present)
- Current official UK classification
- Eight categories based on employment relations and conditions
- Used by Office for National Statistics (ONS), schools data and educational research

**The Great British Class Survey (Savage et al., 2013)**

Published in *Sociology* journal, this influential study challenged traditional class models. Using Bourdieusian concepts and surveying 161,000 participants, researchers identified seven classes:

1. Elite (6% of population)
2. Established middle class (25%)
3. Technical middle class (6%)
4. New affluent workers (15%)
5. Traditional working class (14%)
6. Emergent service workers (19%)
7. Precariat (15%)

This model incorporates economic, cultural and social capital, revealing contemporary class complexity. The identification of the 'precariat' (precarious proletariat) – characterised by economic insecurity, minimal social networks and limited cultural engagement – proves particularly relevant for understanding post-2008 austerity Britain.

**Teaching Consideration**: Mike Savage's work provides excellent contemporary material for evaluating traditional class theories. Students can critically assess whether two-class (Marxist) or three-class (Weberian) models adequately capture 21st-century stratification.

### Evidence of Class Inequality

**Income and Wealth**

- The ONS *Wealth and Assets Survey* (2018-2020) reveals the wealthiest 10% of households hold 43% of total wealth
- Income inequality, measured by the Gini coefficient, increased significantly during the 1980s and remains high
- Regional disparities persist, with London and the South East substantially wealthier

**Health Inequalities**

The Marmot Review (*Fair Society, Healthy Lives*, 2010, updated 2020) documented systematic health inequalities across the social gradient:

- Life expectancy differences of 9.5 years for men and 7.7 years for women between most and least deprived areas
- 'Social determinants of health' including education, employment, housing and income shape health outcomes
- Health inequalities widened between 2010-2020, reversing previous improvements

**Educational Attainment**

Despite widening participation, class remains the strongest predictor of educational achievement:

- Department for Education statistics consistently show Free School Meals (FSM) eligible students – a proxy for disadvantage – achieve significantly lower GCSE and A-level results
- Only 13% of FSM students progress to higher education compared to 43% of non-FSM students
- 'Sutton Trust' research demonstrates elite universities remain dominated by privately-educated and middle-class students

**Teaching Resource**: Ken Browne's *Sociology for AQA* textbooks systematically present statistical evidence of class inequalities, appropriately pitched for A-level students. The books include activities helping students interpret official statistics and sociological research.

### Explaining Class Inequality: Theoretical Debates

**Functionalist Perspectives**

Parsons (1902-1979) and Davis & Moore (1945) argued stratification serves positive functions:

- **Meritocracy**: Rewards distributed according to talent and effort
- **Role allocation**: Stratification ensures the most talented individuals occupy the most important positions
- **Incentivisation**: Unequal rewards motivate people to train for demanding roles

Critiques include:
- Assumes genuine equality of opportunity exists
- Doesn't explain inherited wealth and advantage
- Questions whether highly rewarded roles (e.g., investment bankers) genuinely contribute most to society

**Marxist and Neo-Marxist Explanations**

Contemporary Marxists emphasise:

- **Capitalism's inherent contradictions**: Wealth concentration inevitably produces inequality
- **Ideological state apparatuses** (Althusser): Education and media systems legitimate class divisions
- **The 1%**: Occupy movement's framing highlights extreme wealth concentration

Danny Dorling's research (*Injustice: Why Social Inequality Still Persists*, 2015) updates Marxist analysis for contemporary Britain, arguing inequality results from deliberate political choices rather than economic inevitability.

**Weberian and Neo-Weberian Approaches**

John Goldthorpe's social mobility research (Oxford Mobility Studies) applies Weberian principles. His work demonstrates:

- Absolute mobility increased (more professional jobs created) but relative mobility remained static
- Social fluidity – the comparative chances of children from different backgrounds reaching particular destinations – shows limited change
- 'Glass floor' protects advantaged children from downward mobility whilst 'glass ceiling' limits working-class advancement

## Gender and Inequality

### Feminist Perspectives

Feminist sociology challenges class-centric analyses by foregrounding gender as a fundamental stratification axis. UK A-level specifications require understanding of multiple feminist perspectives:

**Liberal Feminism**
- Emphasises legal and political reform
- Documents progress towards gender equality
- Critiqued for neglecting structural inequalities and intersectionality

**Marxist Feminism**
- Views women's oppression as integral to capitalism
- Women's domestic labour reproduces labour power whilst remaining unpaid
- Reserve army of labour concept applies particularly to women's employment

**Radical Feminism**
- Patriarchy as universal, primary oppression
- Sylvia Walby's theorisation of patriarchal structures (employment, household, state, violence, sexuality, culture)
- Emphasis on male violence and reproductive control

**Black and Postcolonial Feminism**
- Challenges white, middle-class feminist assumptions
- **Heidi Safia Mirza's** groundbreaking work (*Black British Feminism*, 1997; *Young, Female and Black*, 1992) demonstrates how race and gender intersect in British education
- Highlights specific barriers facing Black and minoritised women

**Intersectionality**

Kimberlé Crenshaw's concept (1989) – now central to contemporary sociology – examines how multiple identities intersect to create unique experiences of discrimination and privilege. UK sociologist **Heidi Safia Mirza** extensively applies intersectional analysis to British contexts, showing how young Black women navigate overlapping systems of oppression within education.

**Teaching Strategy**: Intersectionality can initially confuse students. Use concrete examples such as employment discrimination data showing Black women face different (often worse) outcomes than either white women or Black men separately, demonstrating multiplicative rather than additive effects.

### Contemporary Gender Inequalities

**Employment and Pay**

- The gender pay gap (median hourly earnings) was 15.4% in 2020 (ONS), though narrowing
- Occupational segregation persists: women concentrated in 'five Cs' (caring, cashiering, catering, cleaning, clerical)
- 'Glass ceiling' limits women's advancement to senior positions
- 'Sticky floor' traps women in low-paid work
- Part-time work penalties disproportionately affect women

**Domestic Labour**

ONS Time Use Surveys consistently show women perform more unpaid domestic work and childcare, even when employed full-time. This 'dual burden' or 'second shift' (Hochschild, 1989) contributes to women's labour market disadvantage.

**Violence and Safety**

Feminist researchers emphasise how violence and harassment constrain women's freedom and life chances, though this receives less attention in examination specifications than economic inequalities.

### Theoretical Debates

**'End of Gender' Arguments**

Some commentators claim gender equality has been achieved:
- Legal equality established
- Educational achievement reversed (girls outperform boys)
- Generational change in attitudes

Counter-arguments emphasise:
- Persistent pay gaps and occupational segregation
- Domestic labour inequality
- Under-representation in elite positions
- Violence against women

**Exam Board Focus**: AQA and Eduqas specifications explicitly require evaluation of 'to what extent' gender inequalities persist, making this debate examination-relevant.

## Ethnicity, Racism and Inequality

### Theoretical Frameworks

**Racial Formation Theory**

Michael Omi and Howard Winant's concept emphasises race as socially constructed through historical processes rather than biological reality. In Britain, racial categories emerged through colonial expansion and continue shaping contemporary stratification.

**Institutional Racism**

The Macpherson Report (1999), investigating Stephen Lawrence's murder, defined institutional racism as "the collective failure of an organisation to provide appropriate service to people because of their colour, culture or ethnic origin." This concept extends beyond individual prejudice to recognise how organisational structures and cultures perpetuate racial disadvantage.

### Contemporary Ethnic Inequalities in Britain

**Educational Attainment**

Contrary to simplistic assumptions, educational outcomes vary significantly by ethnic group:

- Chinese and Indian students achieve highest GCSE results
- Black Caribbean and Pakistani/Bangladeshi students historically underachieved but gaps narrowing
- White working-class boys identified as lowest-achieving group

**Teaching Note**: Steve Chapman's *Sociology for AQA* discusses how intersectionality of class, gender and ethnicity shapes educational outcomes more than ethnicity alone. Teachers should avoid essentialising ethnic groups whilst recognising racism's impacts.

**Employment and Income**

- Unemployment rates consistently higher for most minoritised ethnic groups
- 'Ethnic penalty' in labour market: equivalent qualifications yield lower returns for minoritised workers
- Occupational segregation concentrates minoritised workers in particular sectors
- Glass ceiling effects particularly pronounced for Black and South Asian employees

The 2020 Commission on Race and Ethnic Disparities (controversial in findings) and earlier reports document persistent disparities across employment, criminal justice, health and housing.

**Criminal Justice**

David Lammy's review (*The Lammy Review*, 2017) documented disproportionality throughout criminal justice:

- Black people constitute 3% of population but 12% of prisoners
- Stop-and-search powers used disproportionately against Black people
- Discrimination at multiple stages from policing to sentencing

### Explaining Ethnic Inequalities

**Cultural Explanations**

These attribute inequalities to cultural differences in values, family structures or attitudes. Critiques include:
- Risk of 'victim-blaming'
- Ignore structural constraints and discrimination
- Often rely on stereotypical assumptions

**Structural and Institutional Explanations**

Emphasise:
- Labour market discrimination
- Institutional racism in employment, education, policing
- Legacy of colonialism and migration policies
- Concentration in deprived areas due to housing discrimination

**Postcolonial and Critical Race Perspectives**

Writers including Paul Gilroy (*There Ain't No Black in the Union Jack*, 1987) and Stuart Hall examine how:
- Colonial legacies structure contemporary racism
- 'Race' as a floating signifier adapts to new contexts
- New racism operates through cultural rather than biological claims
- Media representations construct racialised identities
