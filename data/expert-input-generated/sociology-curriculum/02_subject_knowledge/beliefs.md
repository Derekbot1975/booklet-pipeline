# Beliefs in Society

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Beliefs in Society is a substantial topic area within A-level Sociology specifications, primarily taught at Key Stage 5 (ages 16-19). This topic examines the role of religious and secular belief systems in contemporary society, exploring theories of religion, secularisation, religious organisations, and the relationship between belief systems and social change.

**Examination Board Coverage:**
- **AQA**: Paper 2, Section B (Beliefs in Society)
- **OCR**: Component 2 (Researching and Understanding Social Inequalities)
- **WJEC Eduqas**: Component 2 (Understanding Social Inequality)

The topic typically accounts for 20-25% of the full A-level qualification and is assessed through extended essay questions requiring theoretical application and evaluative skills.

## Theoretical Perspectives on Religion

### Functionalist Approaches

Functionalist sociologists view religion as serving positive functions for society and individuals. **Émile Durkheim** (1912) argued in *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* that religion creates social solidarity through collective worship and shared beliefs. His study of Australian Aboriginal totemism demonstrated how the **sacred and profane** distinction reinforces collective conscience.

**Key concepts:**
- **Collective conscience**: shared values and beliefs that bind society together
- **Sacred symbols**: representations of society itself (the totem represents the clan)
- **Social integration**: religion unites individuals into a moral community

**Bronisław Malinowski** (1954) identified religion's role in providing emotional support during life crises and uncertainty. His anthropological research in the Trobriand Islands showed how religious rituals manage anxiety around unpredictable events (fishing in open sea versus lagoon fishing).

**Talcott Parsons** (1967) argued religion provides:
1. **Primary socialisation** through religious values
2. **Meaning and purpose** to answer ultimate questions
3. **Social cohesion** during periods of crisis

**Practical teaching implications:**
Teachers should encourage students to evaluate functionalism's inability to account for religious conflict, persecution, and diversity. Students often struggle to understand why functionalists ignore dysfunctions; using contemporary examples of religious violence (sectarian conflict in Northern Ireland, historical context) helps demonstrate theoretical limitations.

### Marxist Perspectives

**Karl Marx** famously described religion as "the opium of the people" (1843), arguing it serves ruling-class interests by legitimising inequality and preventing revolutionary consciousness.

**Key Marxist concepts:**
- **Ideological apparatus**: religion distorts reality, making exploitation appear natural
- **False consciousness**: religion prevents the proletariat recognising their exploitation
- **Alienation**: religion provides illusory compensation for alienated labour

**Louis Althusser** (1971) developed the concept of **Ideological State Apparatuses** (ISAs), identifying religion as an institution transmitting ruling-class ideology alongside education and media.

**Neo-Marxist perspectives:**
**Antonio Gramsci** (1971) recognised religion's dual character - whilst typically supporting hegemony, it can also inspire counter-hegemonic movements. This explains **Liberation Theology** in Latin America, where Catholic priests (including Archbishop Óscar Romero) supported revolutionary movements against oppressive regimes.

**Otto Maduro** (1982), a Venezuelan liberation theologian, argued religion can be "relatively autonomous" from economic base, occasionally acting as a revolutionary force when church interests align with oppressed groups.

**Teaching approach:**
Students benefit from comparing traditional Marxist determinism with neo-Marxist recognition of religion's complexity. Case studies of Liberation Theology, the American Civil Rights Movement (Martin Luther King Jr.'s religious inspiration), or Islamic Revolution in Iran (1979) demonstrate religion's revolutionary potential.

### Feminist Analyses

Feminist sociologists critique religion as a patriarchal institution legitimising female subordination.

**Evidence of patriarchy in religion:**
1. **Sacred texts**: women portrayed as subordinate (Eve's creation from Adam's rib; Qur'anic inheritance laws)
2. **Religious organisations**: male dominance in leadership positions (Catholic priesthood, Orthodox Judaism's male rabbis)
3. **Religious laws**: restrictions on women's dress, behaviour, and autonomy
4. **Places of worship**: segregation and exclusion (Orthodox synagogues' mechitza)

**Karen Armstrong** (1993) in *A History of God* demonstrated women's exclusion from monotheistic religions' development, arguing early goddess worship was systematically suppressed.

**Simone de Beauvoir** (1949) argued religion reinforces women's "otherness", presenting male experience as universal.

**Jean Holm** (1994) identified religion as promoting traditional gender roles across cultures, though noting variations in women's religious experiences.

**Linda Woodhead** (2002) offered more nuanced analysis, recognising religion provides some women with:
- Community support networks
- Spaces for female authority (women's prayer groups)
- Identity and meaning (particularly for marginalised women)

**Contemporary debates:**
The ordination of women bishops in the Church of England (first appointments 2015) and continuing debates about female imams illustrate ongoing patriarchal contestation. Students should examine why some women embrace traditional religious roles - **Heidi Safia Mirza's** work on young British Muslim women (2013) demonstrates how religious identity can empower minority ethnic women against racism, even whilst reinforcing patriarchal structures.

### Interpretivist and Phenomenological Approaches

**Max Weber** (1905) in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* pioneered interpretivist sociology of religion, arguing Calvinist beliefs about predestination created psychological conditions for capitalist development. This **verstehen** (understanding) approach examines meaning for social actors.

**Peter Berger** (1967) described religion as a **sacred canopy** - a socially constructed shield against chaos providing meaning systems. He later (1999) revised views on secularisation, recognising religion's continuing vitality.

**Clifford Geertz** (1966) defined religion as a cultural system of symbols creating powerful motivations and moods. This anthropological approach emphasises religion's meaning-making functions.

## Religious Organisations: Typologies

### Church-Sect Typology

**Ernst Troeltsch** (1931) distinguished:

**Churches:**
- Large, bureaucratic organisations
- Claim monopoly of religious truth
- Integrated with wider society
- Universal membership (born into)
- Professional clergy hierarchy
- Example: Church of England (established church)

**Sects:**
- Small, exclusive groups
- Claim monopoly of truth
- Hostile to wider society
- Voluntary membership (must prove commitment)
- Charismatic leadership
- Example: Exclusive Brethren, Jehovah's Witnesses

**H. Richard Niebuhr** (1929) argued sects are inherently unstable - second generation born into membership lacks founders' fervour. Sects either:
1. Denominationalise (become more church-like)
2. Disappear
3. Become established sects (Niebuhr failed to predict this)

### Extended Typologies

**Howard Becker** (1950) added:
- **Denominations**: midway between church and sect (Methodist Church, Baptist Union)
- **Cults**: individualistic, least organised, tolerant of other beliefs

**Roy Wallis** (1984) distinguished new religious movements by relationship to wider society:
1. **World-rejecting**: sects rejecting society's values (Moonies, People's Temple)
2. **World-accommodating**: focus on religious rather than worldly goals (Neo-Pentecostalism)
3. **World-affirming**: accepting society, offering techniques for success (Scientology, Transcendental Meditation)

**Steve Bruce** (2011) critiques typologies as increasingly inadequate for understanding contemporary religious diversity and "pick and mix" spirituality.

**Practical application:**
Students should classify contemporary religious organisations using these typologies whilst evaluating their limitations. The rise of megachurches, online religious communities, and hybrid movements challenges neat categorisation.

## New Age Movements and Spirituality

**Paul Heelas** and **Linda Woodhead** (2005) conducted the **Kendal Project**, researching religion and spirituality in a typical English town. Their findings:
- Traditional congregational domain declining
- Holistic milieu (alternative spirituality) growing but not compensating for religious decline
- **Subjectivisation thesis**: modern culture values individual subjective experience over external authority

**Characteristics of New Age spirituality:**
- Individualistic rather than collective
- Personal development and self-discovery
- Eclectic beliefs (combining elements from different traditions)
- Therapeutic focus (healing, wellbeing)
- Rejection of traditional religious authority

**Steve Bruce** (2002) argues New Age appeals particularly to:
- Middle-class, educated women (67% female participation in Kendal Project)
- Those in "person-centred" occupations (teaching, counselling, healthcare)
- Baby boomer generation experiencing identity transitions

**Explaining New Age growth:**
1. **Postmodernisation**: breakdown of grand narratives, preference for multiple truths
2. **Consumerism**: pick-and-mix approach to beliefs
3. **Disenchantment** with organised religion
4. **Social change**: women's changing roles create demand for female-centred spirituality

## Secularisation Debate

### Defining Secularisation

Secularisation is contested both as concept and empirical reality. **Bryan Wilson** (1966) defined it as "the process whereby religious thinking, practice and institutions lose social significance."

**José Casanova** (1994) distinguished three secularisation processes:
1. **Differentiation**: separation of religion from other social spheres
2. **Decline**: reduction in religious belief and practice
3. **Privatisation**: religion confined to private sphere

### Evidence For Secularisation (UK Context)

**Steve Bruce** (2002, 2011) is the principal secularisation theorist, citing:

**Statistical evidence:**
- Church attendance: 10% weekly in 1960s → 5% by 2015 (British Social Attitudes Survey)
- Church membership: significant decline across mainstream denominations
- Religious ceremonies: 70% marriages in church (1971) → 25% (2016) (ONS data)
- Clergy numbers: Methodist ministers declined from 5,000 (1960) to 1,500 (2015)

**Institutional evidence:**
- Church closure: C of E closed 1,600 churches (1970-2010)
- Reduced political influence: bishops' declining House of Lords impact
- Desacralisation: former churches converted to residential/commercial use

**Belief evidence:**
- Census data: "No religion" 15% (2001) → 25% (2011) → 37% (2021)
- Declining belief in God, afterlife, religious teachings
- Younger generations significantly less religious

**Bruce's causal explanations:**
1. **Structural differentiation**: religion displaced from education, welfare, healthcare
2. **Societalisation**: decline of small-scale communities where religion was socially enforced
3. **Rationalisation**: scientific worldview displaces religious explanations
4. **Cultural diversity**: religious pluralism undermines certainty in any single tradition

**David Voas** (2009) demonstrated "generational replacement" - each generation approximately half as religious as previous, predicting continued decline.

### Evidence Against Secularisation

**Grace Davie** (1994, 2007) coined **"believing without belonging"** - many retain religious beliefs whilst not formally practising. She argues:
- Religious belief more persistent than institutional participation
- **Vicarious religion**: non-attenders want churches available for life events
- Britain exhibits "unchurched spirituality"

**Danièle Hervieu-Léger** (2000) argues modern religion is **"chain of memory"** - religion persists through transmission across generations, though taking different forms.

**Critics of secularisation thesis:**

**José Casanova** (1994) demonstrated religion's continuing public role through:
- Liberation theology movements
- Islamic Revolution in Iran
- American Religious Right
- Polish Catholicism's role against communism

**Global perspective:**
**Peter Berger** (1999) recanted earlier secularisation support, arguing world is "furiously religious" outside Western Europe. Global Christian growth (especially Pentecostalism in Global South) and Islamic revival demonstrate religion's vitality.

**Rodney Stark** and **Roger Finke** (2000) proposed **religious economy theory**: religious vitality depends on competitive religious marketplace. American religious pluralism explains higher religiosity versus European state monopolies.

### Teaching the Secularisation Debate

**Mike Haralambos** and **Martin Holborn** (2013) in *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* present balanced evaluation - UK evidence supports institutional secularisation whilst belief remains more complex. Teachers should:

1. **Distinguish definitions**: institutional decline versus belief decline versus privatisation
2. **Evaluate measurement**: attendance statistics versus subjective belief measurement
3. **Consider alternative explanations**: believing without belonging, religious transformation rather than decline
4. **Apply evaluation skills**: AQA Assessment Objectives require evaluating competing perspectives

**Practical activities:**
- Analyse census data trends (2001, 2011, 2021)
- Local research: survey student religiosity, visit local churches to interview clergy about attendance trends
- Evaluate whether specific evidence supports secularisation (attendance) or challenges it (continued belief)

## Religion and Social Change

### Religion as Conservative Force

**Conservative functions:**
1. **Legitimising existing structures**: divine right of kings, caste system
2. **Social control**: moral regulation, promises of afterlife rewards
3. **Preventing social change**: discouraging questioning of inequality

**Examples:**
- Hindu caste system legitimised by karma and dharma concepts
- Catholic Church historically opposing contraception, abortion, LGBTQ+ rights
- Christian teaching "render unto Caesar" supporting political obedience

### Religion as Force for Change

**Max Weber's Protestant Ethic thesis** argued religious ideas can drive economic transformation. Calvinist beliefs about predestination created:
- Ascetic lifestyle (hard work, thrift)
- Profit-seeking as proof of salvation
- **Unintended consequence**: capitalist spirit

**Critiques:**
- **R.H. Tawney** (1926) reversed causation: capitalism came first
- **Kautsky** argued economic factors, not ideas, drove change
- Contradictory evidence: Catholic Venice, Buddhist Japan developed capitalism

**Contemporary examples of religion driving change:**

1. **American Civil Rights Movement** (1950s-60s): Black churches provided organisational base; Martin Luther King Jr.'s theology of equality challenged segregation

2. **Liberation Theology**: Latin American Catholic priests (Camilo Torres, Helder Camara) combined Christianity with Marxism, supporting revolutionary movements against inequality

3. **Islamic Revolution in Iran** (1979): Religious leaders (Ayatollah Khomeini) overthrew Shah's secular modernisation

4. **Polish Solidarity Movement** (1980s): Catholic Church supported trade union opposition to communist government

**Explaining variations:**
**Otto Maduro** (1982) argued religion supports change when:
- Church interests align with oppressed groups
- Religious leaders experience oppression
- Religion provides only available organisational framework

**Steve Bruce** (1992) identified conditions for religion supporting change:
1. **Mobilisation**: religion provides leadership, organisation, legitimacy
2. **Channelling dissent**: religious language expresses opposition in repressive contexts
3. **Defending community**: religion symbolises group identity against threats

## Religion, Gender, and Ethnicity

### Gender and Religious Participation

**Statistical patterns (UK):**
- Women comprise 60-65% of church congregations
- Women more likely to express religious belief (British Social Attitudes Survey)
- Women participate more in New Age spirituality (67% in Kendal Project)

**Explanations for women's greater religiosity:**

**Structural explanations:**
- **Differential socialisation**: girls socialised to be passive, obedient (Miller and Hoffman, 1995)
- **Life experience**: women more involved in birth, death, child-rearing (Greeley, 1992)
- **Work patterns**: part-time work gives more time for religious participation

**Compensatory explanations:**
- Religion compensates for deprivation (Glock and Stark, 1965)
- Women's subordinate position creates religious need
- Religion provides community, status, identity

**Critical perspectives:**
**Linda Woodhead** (2013) challenges simple deprivation theory, arguing religion offers women:
- Moral authority in families
- Community beyond domestic sphere
- Alternative source of status
- Framework for understanding life transitions

**Heidi Safia Mirza** (2013) examined young British Muslim women, finding religious identity provides:
- Protection against racism
- Framework for negotiating family expectations
- Community solidarity
- Empowerment through religious knowledge
