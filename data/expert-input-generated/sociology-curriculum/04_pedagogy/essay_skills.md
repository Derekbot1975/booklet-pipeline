# Extended Writing and Essay Skills

## Introduction

Extended writing represents one of the most challenging yet essential components of A-Level Sociology assessment in the UK. The ability to construct coherent, analytical essays that synthesise theoretical perspectives, empirical evidence, and evaluative commentary distinguishes high-achieving sociology students. This knowledge file examines the pedagogical approaches, assessment frameworks, and specific strategies that enable teachers to develop students' extended writing capabilities within the UK Sociology curriculum.

## Examination Board Requirements

### AQA Sociology

AQA's A-Level Sociology specification requires students to demonstrate extended writing skills across all examination papers. The **10-mark and 20-mark essay questions** demand structured responses that apply sociological theories and concepts to specific contexts. The **30-mark "assess" questions** (Papers 1, 2, and 3) represent the pinnacle of extended writing, requiring students to evaluate competing perspectives systematically whilst demonstrating synoptic understanding.

Assessment Objectives specifically relevant to extended writing include:
- **AO1** (Knowledge and Understanding): 35-40% of marks
- **AO2** (Application): 20-25% of marks
- **AO3** (Analysis and Evaluation): 35-40% of marks

### Edexcel (Pearson) Sociology

Edexcel's specification emphasises **12-mark and 20-mark essay questions**, with particular focus on command words such as "evaluate," "assess," and "examine." The examination papers require students to integrate contemporary examples with classical sociological theory, a skill that demands sophisticated extended writing capabilities.

### OCR Sociology

OCR's H580 specification includes **26-mark extended response questions** that specifically assess synoptic understanding. These questions require students to draw connections across different topic areas, making extended writing skills particularly crucial for achieving higher grade boundaries.

### WJEC Sociology

WJEC Eduqas specification includes similar extended writing requirements, with emphasis on Welsh-specific examples where appropriate, demonstrating the importance of contextualising sociological arguments within relevant geographical and cultural frameworks.

## Theoretical Frameworks for Teaching Extended Writing

### The Haralambos and Holborn Model

Mike Haralambos's influential textbook **"Sociology: Themes and Perspectives"** (9th edition, 2013) has shaped how UK Sociology teachers approach extended writing instruction. The text's structure—presenting competing theoretical perspectives systematically before evaluating their relative strengths—provides a template for essay organisation that many teachers adopt explicitly in their pedagogy.

Haralambos's approach emphasises:
- Clear delineation between different sociological perspectives (Functionalism, Marxism, Feminism, etc.)
- Systematic presentation of supporting evidence for each perspective
- Explicit evaluation using contemporary research and theoretical critiques

### Ken Browne's Accessible Approach

Ken Browne's **"Sociology for AQA"** series (various editions, 2011-2019) provides scaffolded approaches to extended writing that have proven particularly effective for students requiring additional support. Browne emphasises:

- **The "PEE" chain**: Point, Evidence, Explanation (expanded to PEEL—Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link—in more sophisticated variants)
- **Key terms boxes** that encourage students to demonstrate sociological vocabulary
- **"How to write good sociology essays"** sections that make explicit the assessment criteria

### Chapman and Moore's Critical Pedagogy

Steve Chapman's work, including **"OCR Sociology for A Level Book 1"** (2015), emphasises developing students' critical consciousness through extended writing. Chapman advocates for:

- Teaching students to identify **epistemological assumptions** underlying different theoretical perspectives
- Encouraging **reflexivity** in sociological writing—students should recognise their own positionality
- Developing **sociological imagination** (Mills, 1959) through extended writing tasks that connect personal biography to wider social structures

## Pedagogical Strategies for Developing Extended Writing Skills

### Progressive Skill Development Across Key Stage 5

Extended writing skills should develop progressively across Year 12 and Year 13:

#### Year 12 (First Year of A-Level)

**Term 1 (September-December):**
- Introduction to basic essay structure (introduction, main body paragraphs, conclusion)
- Practice with **10-mark application questions** focusing on AO2 skills
- Explicit teaching of sociological vocabulary and terminology
- Modelling effective introductions that define key terms and identify relevant perspectives

**Term 2 (January-March):**
- Development of **analytical paragraphs** using theoretical perspectives
- Introduction to **20-mark questions** requiring synthesis of multiple perspectives
- Practice in using **contemporary examples and statistics** effectively
- Peer assessment activities using simplified mark schemes

**Term 3 (April-July):**
- Introduction to **30-mark evaluative essays** (AQA format)
- Teaching **counterargument construction** and theoretical debate
- Practice in writing **synoptic conclusions** that draw connections across topic areas
- Self-assessment using full mark schemes

#### Year 13 (Second Year of A-Level)

**Term 1 (September-December):**
- Refinement of evaluative writing through analysis of **competing methodological approaches**
- Development of **synoptic understanding** across all topics studied
- Practice in **timed essay writing** under examination conditions
- Introduction to **contemporary sociological research** for evaluation purposes

**Term 2 (January-March):**
- Intensive examination practice with focus on **command word interpretation**
- Development of **individualised improvement strategies** based on mock examination performance
- Practice in **sophisticated theoretical integration** (e.g., combining feminist and Marxist perspectives)

**Term 3 (April-May):**
- Final examination preparation with emphasis on **examination technique**
- Review of **contemporary social issues** for application purposes
- Practice in **timing management** for extended writing under pressure

### Explicit Teaching of Essay Structure

Research by Graham et al. (2012) in their systematic review of writing instruction demonstrates that **explicit teaching of text structure** significantly improves student writing outcomes. In Sociology, this translates to teaching specific essay frameworks:

#### The "Three Perspectives" Model (for 30-mark AQA questions)

**Introduction (3-4 minutes writing time):**
- Define key terms from the question
- Identify the main sociological debate or issue
- Briefly outline which perspectives will be discussed (typically three main theoretical positions)

**Main Body Paragraph 1 (8-10 minutes):**
- Present Perspective A (e.g., Functionalist view on education)
- Provide supporting evidence (studies, concepts, contemporary examples)
- Brief internal evaluation (limitations of this perspective)

**Main Body Paragraph 2 (8-10 minutes):**
- Present Perspective B (e.g., Marxist view on education)
- Provide supporting evidence
- Brief internal evaluation
- **Comparative analysis**: how does this perspective challenge or complement Perspective A?

**Main Body Paragraph 3 (8-10 minutes):**
- Present Perspective C (e.g., Feminist view on education)
- Provide supporting evidence
- Brief internal evaluation
- **Comparative analysis**: how does this perspective add to the debate?

**Evaluation Section (8-10 minutes):**
- Assess the **relative strengths and limitations** of the perspectives presented
- Introduce **contemporary research** that supports or challenges the perspectives
- Consider **methodological issues** in the evidence base
- Address **alternative explanations** or contemporary developments

**Conclusion (3-4 minutes):**
- Provide a **balanced judgement** responding directly to the question
- Demonstrate **synoptic understanding** by connecting to wider sociological debates
- Avoid introducing entirely new material

### The Role of Sociological Vocabulary

Diane Reay's work on **symbolic violence in education** (Reay, 2017, "Miseducation: Inequality, Education and the Working Classes") highlights how academic discourse can exclude students from working-class backgrounds. Teachers must therefore balance:

- **Explicitly teaching sociological terminology** to ensure all students can access academic discourse
- **Explaining why sociological vocabulary matters**—it demonstrates precise understanding and engages with disciplinary conventions
- **Avoiding unnecessary jargon** that obscures rather than clarifies meaning

Practical strategies include:
- Maintaining **keyword glossaries** specific to each topic area
- Requiring students to use **minimum numbers of sociological terms** in practice essays (e.g., "use at least 8 relevant concepts")
- **Modelling effective use** of terminology in teacher-written exemplar paragraphs
- **Etymology activities** exploring the roots of sociological terms (e.g., "bourgeoisie," "patriarchy," "anomie")

### Integration of Contemporary Examples and Evidence

Heidi Safia Mirza's research on **race, gender, and educational achievement** (Mirza, 2009, "Race, Gender and Educational Desire") demonstrates the importance of using contemporary, diverse examples in sociological writing. Teachers should:

- Maintain **regularly updated example banks** organised by topic area
- Encourage students to engage with **contemporary media sources** (The Guardian, BBC News, Office for National Statistics)
- Teach students to **contextualise historical studies** (e.g., explaining that Paul Willis's "Learning to Labour" was conducted in 1970s Hammersmith)
- Promote **critical engagement with statistics** rather than uncritical citation

Practical activities include:
- **"Example of the Week"** discussions in each lesson
- **Statistical literacy workshops** teaching students to interpret ONS data, British Social Attitudes Survey findings, etc.
- **Contemporary case study research tasks** where students investigate current social issues
- **Comparative analysis activities** examining how social phenomena have changed over time

## Assessment and Feedback Strategies

### Formative Assessment Approaches

#### Live Modelling

The most effective pedagogical strategy for developing extended writing involves **live modelling**—teachers writing essay paragraphs in real-time whilst explaining their thought processes. This technique, advocated by Lemov (2015) in "Teach Like a Champion 2.0," makes expert writing processes visible to novice writers.

Effective live modelling includes:
- **Think-aloud protocols**: verbalising decision-making processes ("I'm choosing to use Durkheim here because...")
- **Deliberate errors**: occasionally making mistakes then correcting them to demonstrate editing processes
- **Incremental building**: constructing paragraphs sentence-by-sentence with student input

#### Structured Peer Assessment

Research by Black and Wiliam (1998) on **formative assessment** demonstrates that peer assessment significantly improves student outcomes when properly structured. In Sociology extended writing:

- Provide students with **simplified mark schemes** focusing on specific Assessment Objectives
- Train students in **constructive feedback language** ("This evaluation would be strengthened by...")
- Use **anonymous peer assessment** to reduce bias and anxiety
- Implement **paired marking** where students mark the same essay then discuss differences

#### Dialogic Feedback

Rather than providing extensive written feedback, which research suggests has limited impact (Elliott et al., 2016), teachers should prioritise:

- **Whole-class feedback sheets** identifying common strengths and areas for development
- **Targeted individual feedback** focusing on 2-3 specific improvements
- **Feedback dialogues** where students discuss their work with teachers
- **Feed-forward tasks** requiring students to redraft specific paragraphs addressing feedback

### Summative Assessment and Grade Boundaries

Understanding grade descriptors is essential for teaching effective extended writing:

#### AQA A-Level Sociology Grade Boundaries (typical ranges)

- **Grade A*** (approximately 80%+): Demonstrates comprehensive knowledge, sophisticated analysis, sustained evaluation using diverse evidence
- **Grade A** (approximately 70-79%): Detailed knowledge, clear analysis, effective evaluation with good use of evidence
- **Grade C** (approximately 50-59%): Reasonable knowledge, some analysis, limited evaluation with basic evidence
- **Grade E** (approximately 40-49%): Basic knowledge, descriptive rather than analytical, minimal evaluation

Teachers should provide students with **annotated exemplar essays** at different grade boundaries, making explicit what distinguishes higher-achieving responses.

## Addressing Common Challenges

### Challenge 1: Descriptive Rather Than Analytical Writing

Many students struggle to move beyond description to genuine analysis. Specific strategies include:

- Teaching **analytical sentence stems**: "This suggests that...," "The significance of this is...," "This perspective assumes that..."
- Requiring students to answer **"So what?"** after each piece of evidence
- Using **colour-coding exercises** where students highlight descriptive sentences (yellow) and analytical sentences (green) in their own work
- Providing **analytical question prompts**: "How does this evidence support the theoretical perspective?" "What are the implications of this finding?"

### Challenge 2: Inadequate Evaluation

Evaluation represents the highest-order skill in Sociology extended writing. Students often struggle to:

- Distinguish between **internal evaluation** (criticisms of a perspective's logic or assumptions) and **external evaluation** (competing perspectives or empirical challenges)
- Provide **balanced evaluation** rather than one-sided criticism
- Use **contemporary research** effectively for evaluative purposes

Teaching strategies include:
- Explicitly teaching **evaluation sentence stems**: "However, critics argue...," "This perspective has been challenged by...," "A limitation of this view is..."
- Creating **evaluation grids** where students list strengths and limitations of each perspective
- Teaching the **"Yes, but..." technique**: presenting a perspective, then systematically addressing counterarguments
- Providing **contemporary research briefings** that students can use for evaluation

### Challenge 3: Synoptic Understanding

Synoptic assessment requires students to make connections across different topic areas. This proves challenging because:

- The modular structure of A-Level teaching often creates **artificial boundaries** between topics
- Students struggle to **recall earlier topic content** when studying later units
- The **cognitive load** of integrating multiple topics whilst writing under pressure is significant

Practical solutions include:
- **Synoptic mapping activities** where students create visual representations of connections between topics
- **Regular retrieval practice** using low-stakes quizzes covering earlier topics
- **Cross-topic essay questions** that explicitly require synoptic understanding (e.g., "Assess the view that social class remains the most significant factor affecting both educational achievement and health outcomes")
- **"Synoptic Fridays"** or similar regular sessions dedicated to making cross-topic connections

### Challenge 4: Writing Under Timed Conditions

The pressure of examination conditions significantly affects writing quality. Students require:

- **Regular timed practice** building from 20-minute focused paragraphs to full 45-minute essays
- **Explicit time management strategies**: time allocations for planning (5 minutes), writing each section (specific minutes per paragraph), and checking (5 minutes)
- **Planning techniques** such as spider diagrams, linear plans, or quick bullet-point lists
- **Examination stamina building** through progressive exposure to longer timed writing sessions

## Differentiation and Inclusive Pedagogy

### Supporting Students with SEND

Students with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities require tailored support for extended writing:

- **Writing frames** providing paragraph structures without limiting higher-order thinking
- **Speech-to-text software** for students with dysgraphia
- **Extended time allocations** during practice sessions (as per examination access arrangements)
- **Chunked planning templates** breaking essays into manageable components
- **Pre-prepared vocabulary banks** reducing cognitive load whilst maintaining academic standards

### Stretch and Challenge for High-Attaining Students

High-attaining students benefit from:

- **Extended reading programmes** engaging with original sociological texts (Bourdieu, Foucault, Giddens)
- **Independent research projects** requiring extended writing in different formats (literature reviews, research reports)
- **Critical engagement with methodology**: evaluating the research methods underlying key sociological studies
- **Theoretical synthesis tasks**: combining multiple theoretical perspectives into coherent analytical frameworks
- **Academic journal access**: reading contemporary sociological research (British Journal of Sociology, Sociology journals)

### Culturally Responsive Pedagogy

Following Mirza's work on race and education, teachers should ensure:

- **Diverse examples** representing different ethnicities, social classes, genders, and sexualities
- **Critical examination of sociological theory itself**: whose voices are centred? Whose experiences are marginalised?
- **Contemporary research** by diverse sociologists (Mirza, Rollock, Bhopal, Shukla, etc.)
- **Opportunities for students** to bring their own cultural knowledge and experiences to sociological analysis
