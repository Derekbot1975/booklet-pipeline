# A Level Sociology Specifications

## Overview of A Level Sociology in the UK

A Level Sociology is a two-year post-16 qualification offered by the four main examination boards in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland: AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. The subject does not form part of the National Curriculum but follows specifications regulated by Ofqual. Sociology is not typically offered at Key Stage 3 or 4, making A Level the primary entry point for students into sociological study in the UK education system.

The current specifications were introduced in September 2015 (first teaching) and September 2016 (first assessment for full A Level) following reforms that removed modular AS qualifications from contributing to full A Level grades. These reforms emphasised linear assessment, with all examinations taken at the end of Year 13.

## AQA Sociology (7192)

AQA is the most popular examination board for A Level Sociology, with approximately 70% of entries nationally. The specification is structured around seven substantive topic areas plus two theory and methods components.

### Core Components

**Paper 1: Education with Theory and Methods (2 hours, 80 marks, 33.3% of A Level)**

The Education component requires detailed knowledge of educational achievement patterns by social class, ethnicity, and gender. Key thinkers include Diane Reay, whose work on social class and educational choice is essential reading, particularly her research on habitus and 'circuits of schooling'. Students must engage with material on educational policy from 1988 onwards, including marketisation, academisation, and the role of neoliberalism.

The Theory and Methods component integrated into Paper 1 examines consensus and conflict theories, social action theories, and postmodern/late modern perspectives. Mike Haralambos and Martin Holborn's *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* (currently in its 9th edition, published by HarperCollins) remains the definitive textbook, providing comprehensive coverage of theoretical perspectives.

**Paper 2: Topics in Sociology (2 hours, 80 marks, 33.3% of A Level)**

Students select two topics from: Families and Households; Beliefs in Society; Global Development; or The Media. 

The Families and Households option requires analysis of demographic changes, family diversity, and feminist perspectives. Key debates include Ann Oakley's work on the conventional family, Judith Stacey on family diversity, and contemporary discussions of same-sex families and Living Apart Together (LAT) relationships.

**Paper 3: Crime and Deviance with Theory and Methods (2 hours, 80 marks, 33.3% of A Level)**

Crime and Deviance is compulsory for all AQA students. This component requires engagement with ethnicity and the criminal justice system, where Heidi Safia Mirza's intersectional approach to understanding inequality is particularly relevant. Her edited collection *Black British Feminism* (Routledge, 1997) provides crucial insights into how race, gender, and class intersect in patterns of criminalisation and educational experience.

The second Theory and Methods component in Paper 3 focuses specifically on sociological research methods, including quantitative and qualitative techniques, ethical considerations, and the relationship between theory and methods.

### Assessment Objectives

AQA specifies three Assessment Objectives weighted as follows:
- **AO1** (Knowledge and Understanding): 39-41%
- **AO2** (Application): 28-32%
- **AO3** (Analysis and Evaluation): 30-32%

This distribution requires teachers to move beyond knowledge transmission towards developing critical analytical skills. The 10-mark 'Outline and Explain' questions assess AO1, whilst 10-mark 'Applying Material from Item' questions assess AO2, and 20-mark 'Evaluate' questions assess AO3.

## Edexcel Sociology (9SO0)

Pearson Edexcel structures its specification around four compulsory papers, each worth 25% of the total A Level.

### Paper Structure

**Paper 1: Socialisation, Culture and Identity (1 hour 45 minutes, 90 marks)**

This paper uniquely focuses on socialisation processes, youth subcultures, and identity formation. Steve Chapman's work, particularly *Sociology for AQA* published by HarperCollins (co-authored with Martin Stevenson), provides comprehensive coverage aligned with this specification.

**Paper 2: Researching and Understanding Social Inequalities (1 hour 45 minutes, 90 marks)**

This paper combines social stratification with research methods. Students examine social class, gender, ethnicity, and age as dimensions of inequality, alongside detailed methodological study.

**Paper 3: Globalisation and the Digital Social World (1 hour 45 minutes, 90 marks)**

With sections on Global Development and The Media, this paper reflects contemporary sociological concerns about digital culture, transnational migration, and globalised capitalism.

**Paper 4: Crime and Deviance (1 hour 45 minutes, 90 marks)**

Crime is examined separately rather than combined with Theory and Methods, allowing deeper exploration of contemporary issues including green criminology, state crime, and terrorism.

### Distinctive Features

Edexcel incorporates more contemporary sociological issues than other boards, including identity politics, digital sociology, and environmental sociology. Teachers using this specification should reference current research from organisations like the British Sociological Association (BSA) and engage with journals such as *Sociology* and the *British Journal of Sociology*.

## OCR Sociology (H580)

OCR offers a distinctive approach with three papers examining different sociological debates.

### Component Structure

**Component 1: Socialisation, Culture and Identity (2 hours 15 minutes, 105 marks, 37% of A Level)**

This combines elements similar to Edexcel's first paper with additional focus on sociology as a social science and debates around culture, identity, and socialisation across the life course.

**Component 2: Researching and Understanding Social Inequalities (2 hours 15 minutes, 105 marks, 37% of A Level)**

OCR uniquely requires study of all four dimensions of inequality (class, gender, ethnicity, age) rather than allowing optional topics. This comprehensive approach means students develop broader understanding of intersectionality - a concept central to Heidi Safia Mirza's work.

**Component 3: Debates in Contemporary Society (1 hour 30 minutes, 90 marks, 26% of A Level)**

Students answer questions on debates drawn from contemporary British society, requiring application of sociological concepts to current issues. This synoptic component assesses the ability to draw upon knowledge from Components 1 and 2.

### Research Methods Integration

OCR integrates research methods throughout all components rather than treating it as a discrete section. This requires teachers to contextualise methodological discussion within substantive topics, aligning with how sociological research actually operates.

## WJEC Eduqas Sociology (A680QS)

WJEC Eduqas serves primarily Welsh schools but is available in England. Its specification was designed with input from Welsh HEIs and reflects particular attention to the Welsh context.

### Component Structure

**Component 1: Studying Society (2 hours 30 minutes, 40% of A Level)**

This includes an overview of theory and methods plus one substantive topic chosen from Education or Families and Households. The extended time allocation allows for one 50-mark extended essay question requiring sustained evaluation.

**Component 2: Understanding Social Inequalities (2 hours 30 minutes, 30% of A Level)**

This compulsory component examines social differentiation and stratification, with particular focus on class, gender, and ethnicity.

**Component 3: Understanding Social Change (2 hours, 30% of A Level)**

Students select Crime and Deviance or Media, examining how contemporary social change affects these areas.

### Welsh Context

WJEC specification guidance explicitly encourages use of Welsh examples where appropriate, including research from Cardiff University's School of Social Sciences and Wales-specific policy contexts. However, the specification remains appropriate for schools outside Wales.

## Practical Implications for Teaching

### Textbook Selection

Ken Browne's *Sociology for AQA* (Polity Press, 5th edition 2019) provides accessible introduction for students. Browne's approach emphasises clear explanations with contemporary examples, making complex theory accessible. For AQA specifically, his coverage aligns precisely with specification requirements.

Haralambos and Holborn's *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* remains essential for detailed theoretical coverage and evaluative material. Teachers should use this as a reference text for planning rather than expecting students to read it cover-to-cover. The depth of coverage supports preparation for high-tariff evaluation questions.

Steve Chapman's *OCR Sociology for A Level* series (written with Jeanette Lawrence and Kathryn Musson, published by Illuminate Publishing) provides specification-specific coverage for OCR centres, whilst his AQA book (with Martin Stevenson) serves AQA centres similarly.

### Addressing Inequality in Content Selection

Diane Reay's research requires particular attention in Education units. Her work on 'white middle-class identities' and educational choice-making challenges deficit models of working-class educational engagement. Teachers should reference her 2017 book *Miseducation: Inequality, Education and the Working Classes* (Policy Press), which synthesises decades of ethnographic research.

Heidi Safia Mirza's concept of 'educational desire' challenges deficit narratives around minoritised ethnic groups. Her 1992 study *Young, Female and Black* (Routledge) remains relevant for understanding educational achievement patterns that persist today. When teaching intersectionality, Mirza's work demonstrates how race and gender compound to create specific experiences not reducible to either category alone.

### Assessment Preparation

Teachers must familiarise themselves with Assessment Objectives weightings for their specific board. AQA's emphasis on AO2 (application) requires practice with Item-based questions where students must explicitly reference provided source material. Edexcel's more balanced distribution allows slightly different pedagogical approaches.

Mark schemes reveal that AO3 (evaluation) rewards sustained critical analysis rather than simple point-counterpoint structures. High-achieving students develop arguments using concepts like 'methodological pluralism' or 'theoretical complementarity' rather than treating perspectives as mutually exclusive.

### Contemporary Examples and Currency

Sociology specifications require engagement with contemporary society. Teachers should regularly update examples from:
- Office for National Statistics (ONS) data on inequality, family structures, and crime
- Ministry of Justice statistics on criminal justice outcomes
- Department for Education data on achievement gaps
- British Social Attitudes Survey findings
- Research published in *Sociology Review* magazine (Philip Allan/Hodder, published five times yearly)

The BSA's *Sociology* journal publishes research applicable to A Level teaching. Many universities provide public access to working papers that can inform teaching.

### Developing Synoptic Skills

All specifications require synoptic assessment - the ability to make connections across different topic areas. Teachers should explicitly teach these connections:
- Family structure relating to educational achievement (Education and Families)
- Media representations relating to crime and deviance (Media and Crime)
- Research methods applied to studying any substantive area
- Theoretical perspectives explaining patterns across all topics

Regular low-stakes assessments requiring students to apply one topic's concepts to another build synoptic capability before high-stakes examinations.

### Supporting Diverse Learners

Sociology attracts diverse student populations, including mature students, those from backgrounds under-represented in higher education, and students with particular interest in social justice. The subject content directly addresses inequalities students may experience.

Teachers should create classroom environments where discussion of sensitive topics (poverty, racism, family breakdown) proceeds with appropriate sensitivity. Using sociological concepts provides analytical distance whilst maintaining engagement with real experiences.

### Transition to Higher Education

Approximately 40% of A Level Sociology students progress to sociology or related social sciences at university. Teachers should familiarise themselves with undergraduate sociology curriculum to support UCAS applications and interview preparation. The British Sociological Association website (britsoc.co.uk) provides resources on studying sociology at HE level.

Russell Group universities typically require A Level Sociology accompanied by other humanities or social sciences. Understanding these patterns helps provide appropriate guidance for students with HE aspirations.

## Resource Recommendations

### Essential Reading for Teachers

- *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* (Haralambos & Holborn) - comprehensive theoretical coverage
- *Education, Equality and Human Rights* (Mike Cole, ed., Routledge) - critical perspectives on education
- *Miseducation* (Diane Reay) - essential for teaching social class and education
- Specification-specific textbooks by Ken Browne, Steve Chapman, or Rob Webb depending on examination board
- *Sociology Review* magazine - termly publication with accessible articles on specification topics

### Academic Journals for Teacher Development

- *Sociology* (BSA official journal)
- *British Journal of Sociology*
- *The Sociological Review*
- *British Journal of Sociology of Education* - for Education topic specialist knowledge
- *British Journal of Criminology* - for Crime and Deviance specialist knowledge

### Professional Development

The British Sociological Association Schools Network provides CPD opportunities, including the annual Teaching Sociology conference. Regional subject associations offer specification-specific training, particularly valuable when specifications next undergo revision (anticipated 2024-2025 for implementation from 2026).

Examination boards provide training events, exemplar materials, and examiner reports. These board-specific resources are invaluable for understanding mark scheme application and common student errors.
