# Sociology Curriculum Overview (A Level)

## Introduction to A Level Sociology in the UK

A Level Sociology is offered as a two-year qualification in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, typically studied by students aged 16-18. Unlike many other subjects, Sociology does not form part of the compulsory National Curriculum, which concludes at Key Stage 4 (GCSE level). Instead, it represents a social science option within post-16 education, providing students with critical analytical tools to understand society, social structures, and human behaviour.

The subject has experienced significant growth since its introduction to A Level curricula in the 1960s, with current specifications reflecting contemporary sociological debates while maintaining classical theoretical foundations. According to the Joint Council for Qualifications (JCQ), Sociology consistently attracts approximately 30,000-35,000 entries annually across UK examination boards.

## Examination Board Specifications

### AQA Sociology (7192)

AQA dominates the UK Sociology market with approximately 85-90% of entries. The specification, revised in 2015 and first examined in 2017, comprises:

**Year 1 (AS Level content):**
- Education with Theory and Methods
- Research Methods
- Families and Households

**Year 2 (A Level continuation):**
- Beliefs in Society
- Crime and Deviance with Theory and Methods
- Theory and Methods (synoptic component)

The AQA specification is structured around three two-hour written examinations, each worth 33.3% of the total A Level. Paper 1 focuses on Education with Theory and Methods; Paper 2 covers Topics in Sociology (typically Families and Households plus one optional topic from Media, Health, Work and Leisure, or Sociological Theory); Paper 3 addresses Crime and Deviance with Theory and Methods.

### Edexcel/Pearson Sociology (9SO0)

Edexcel's specification offers alternative topic combinations and assessment structures:

**Paper 1:** Socialisation, Culture and Identity with Research Methods
**Paper 2:** Two options from Families and Households, Education, Beliefs in Society, Global Development
**Paper 3:** Crime and Deviance, Theory and Methods

Edexcel provides greater flexibility in topic selection, though this exam board commands significantly smaller market share than AQA.

### OCR Sociology (H580)

OCR's specification emphasises methodological understanding and includes:

- Socialisation, Culture and Identity
- Researching and Understanding Social Inequalities
- Debates in Contemporary Society
- Power and Control

OCR's approach integrates sociological debates throughout, with particular emphasis on intersectionality and contemporary social issues.

### WJEC (Wales) Sociology

WJEC offers specifications tailored to Welsh educational contexts, incorporating Welsh social policy examples while maintaining comparable academic rigour to English boards.

## Core Theoretical Perspectives

All UK A Level specifications require students to understand and apply major sociological perspectives, a framework comprehensively outlined in Michael Haralambos and Martin Holborn's seminal textbook *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* (now in its 9th edition, 2017). This 1,200-page volume remains the definitive reference text for UK Sociology teachers.

### Functionalism

Drawing on the work of Émile Durkheim, Talcott Parsons, and Robert Merton, functionalism views society as a system of interconnected institutions serving specific functions. Students examine consensus theory, value consensus, and functional prerequisites. Ken Browne's *Sociology for AQA* (5th edition, 2019) provides accessible explanations of functionalist applications across topics, particularly in education and family structures.

### Marxism

Classical Marxist theory (Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels) and neo-Marxist perspectives (Louis Althusser, Antonio Gramsci, Stuart Hall) feature prominently. Students analyse economic determinism, ideology, hegemony, and the role of the superstructure. The concept of cultural capital, developed by Pierre Bourdieu and applied to UK education by Diane Reay, represents crucial contemporary Marxist-influenced scholarship.

### Feminism

UK specifications require engagement with multiple feminist perspectives:

- **Liberal feminism** (Betty Friedan, Ann Oakley)
- **Radical feminism** (Kate Millett, Shulamith Firestone)
- **Marxist feminism** (Michele Barrett, Sylvia Walby)
- **Black feminism** (bell hooks, Patricia Hill Collins)
- **Intersectional feminism** (Kimberlé Crenshaw)

Heidi Safia Mirza's work on intersectionality in British education, particularly *Young, Female and Black* (1992) and her edited collection *Black British Feminism* (1997), provides essential UK-specific feminist perspectives. Diane Reay's research on social class and gender in education, including *Misbehavior, Truancy and Boredom* (2017), offers contemporary empirical evidence for feminist educational analysis.

### Interactionism and Social Action Theory

Symbolic interactionism (George Herbert Mead, Herbert Blumer), labelling theory (Howard Becker, Edwin Lemert), and dramaturgical approaches (Erving Goffman) enable students to analyse micro-level social interactions. These perspectives prove particularly valuable in education (self-fulfilling prophecy, classroom interaction) and crime/deviance (labelling, deviance amplification) topics.

### Postmodernism

Jean-François Lyotard, Jean Baudrillard, and Zygmunt Bauman's work informs discussions of meta-narratives, hyperreality, and liquid modernity. UK curricula apply postmodern perspectives to family diversity, religious pluralism, and media representations.

### New Right/Neo-liberalism

Charles Murray's underclass theory, alongside neoliberal educational policies, appears across specifications. Students critically evaluate individualistic explanations of social inequality.

## Research Methods and Methodology

Methodological understanding represents a synoptic requirement across all specifications. Steve Chapman and Martin Holborn's *A Level Sociology: Research Methods* (2019) provides comprehensive coverage tailored to current specifications.

### Quantitative Methods

- Structured interviews and questionnaires
- Social surveys (British Social Attitudes Survey, Census data)
- Longitudinal studies (Millennium Cohort Study)
- Official statistics (ONS data, crime statistics)
- Comparative methods

### Qualitative Methods

- Unstructured and semi-structured interviews
- Participant and non-participant observation
- Content analysis
- Personal documents and historical sources

### Methodological Debates

Students evaluate positivism versus interpretivism, verstehen, value freedom, objectivity, validity, reliability, representativeness, and triangulation. Key studies requiring detailed knowledge include:

- Paul Willis, *Learning to Labour* (1977)
- Tony Sewell, *Black Masculinities and Schooling* (1997)
- Ann Oakley, *The Sociology of Housework* (1974)
- Beverley Skeggs, *Formations of Class and Gender* (1997)

## Topic-Specific Content

### Education

Education remains compulsory across AQA specifications. Key areas include:

**Differential achievement:** Social class (Diane Reay's concept of emotional capital, Basil Bernstein's language codes, J.W.B. Douglas's longitudinal research), ethnicity (Heidi Safia Mirza's research on Black girls' educational resilience, Tony Sewell on African-Caribbean boys), gender (Mitsos and Browne's work on boys' underachievement, Becky Francis on classroom interaction).

**Education policy:** Tripartite system, comprehensivisation, marketisation, academisation, Free Schools, Educational Maintenance Allowance (EMA), Pupil Premium, tuition fees. Ken Browne provides systematic policy analysis across historical periods.

**Perspectives on education:** Functionalist views (Davis and Moore, Parsons), Marxist critiques (Bowles and Gintis's correspondence principle, Paul Willis), feminist perspectives (Michelle Stanworth, Becky Francis), and New Right approaches (Chubb and Moe).

### Families and Households

This topic examines:

**Family diversity:** Rapoport and Rapoport's five types, Robert Chester's neo-conventional family, postmodern family diversity (Judith Stacey).

**Demographic trends:** Marriage, divorce, cohabitation, civil partnerships, childbearing patterns, ageing population. Students analyse ONS data and demographic transition theory.

**Conjugal roles:** Symmetrical family (Young and Willmott), dual burden and triple shift (feminist critiques), emotion work (Duncombe and Marsden), domestic violence.

**Childhood:** Social construction of childhood (Philippe Ariès), toxic childhood (Sue Palmer), child-centredness, children's rights.

### Crime and Deviance

A Level-only content typically includes:

**Crime statistics:** Official statistics versus victim surveys (Crime Survey for England and Wales) and self-report studies.

**Explanations of crime:** Functionalist perspectives (Durkheim, Merton's strain theory, subcultural theory), Marxist approaches (Chambliss, Box), labelling theory (Becker, Lemert), right realism (Wilson and Kelling's broken windows), left realism (Lea and Young).

**Social groups and crime:** Class (white-collar and corporate crime), ethnicity (over-representation in statistics and prisons), gender (chivalry thesis, liberation thesis, feminist explanations).

**Social control and punishment:** Formal versus informal social control, surveillance society (Michel Foucault's panopticism), penology.

### Beliefs in Society

Content addresses:

**Theories of religion:** Functionalism (Durkheim's collective conscience, Malinowski, Parsons), Marxism (opium of the people, Neo-Marxist approaches), feminism (religious patriarchy, Armstrong, El Saadawi), interpretivism (Weber's Protestant Ethic).

**Secularisation debate:** Defining secularisation, evidence for (Wilson, Bruce) and against (Davie, Stark and Bainbridge), believing without belonging, vicarious religion.

**Religious organisations:** Church, sect, denomination, cult (Troeltsch, Wallis), New Age movements, fundamentalism.

**Religion and social position:** Class, ethnicity, age, gender variations in religiosity.

## Assessment Objectives and Skills

All specifications assess three objectives:

**AO1: Knowledge and Understanding (33-34%)** – Demonstrating knowledge of sociological theories, concepts, evidence, and research methods.

**AO2: Application (33-34%)** – Applying sociological theories, concepts, evidence, and research methods to sociological debates and contemporary society.

**AO3: Analysis and Evaluation (33-34%)** – Analysing and evaluating sociological theories, concepts, evidence, and research methods.

### Extended Writing Requirements

Students must produce extended essays ranging from 10-30 marks. The AQA 30-mark essay questions require sustained evaluation, theoretical application, contemporary examples, and synoptic links across topics. Success requires explicit use of sociological vocabulary, comparative evaluation, and evidential support.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Curriculum Planning

Teachers should:

1. **Establish theoretical foundations early:** Introduce core perspectives systematically before applying them to substantive topics. Haralambos and Holborn's introductory chapters provide comprehensive theoretical frameworks.

2. **Integrate contemporary evidence:** Use ONS data, British Social Attitudes Survey findings, and recent sociological research. Ken Browne's textbooks include updated statistical tables and contemporary case studies relevant to UK contexts.

3. **Develop synoptic thinking:** Explicitly connect topics (e.g., educational inequality links to stratification, family diversity connects to postmodernism, crime statistics relate to methodological debates).

4. **Emphasise sociological literacy:** Build subject-specific vocabulary systematically. Create glossaries, use terminology walls, and require precise conceptual language.

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Sociological imagination (C. Wright Mills):** Encourage students to connect personal experiences to broader social structures. Use examples relevant to student backgrounds while maintaining sociological distance.

**Critical evaluation:** Model comparative evaluation explicitly. Use sentence starters: "However, Marxists would criticise...", "From a feminist perspective...", "In contrast, postmodernists argue...".

**Contemporary application:** Reference current events through sociological lenses. Brexit, COVID-19 pandemic impacts, Black Lives Matter, #MeToo movement provide rich application opportunities when analysed using sociological concepts rather than personal opinion.

**Active learning strategies:** Debates between perspectives, role-play (e.g., mock panel discussions with functionalist, Marxist, feminist viewpoints), research proposal design, statistical analysis tasks.

### Resource Recommendations

- **Haralambos and Holborn** (2017): *Sociology: Themes and Perspectives* – comprehensive reference
- **Ken Browne** (2019): *Sociology for AQA*, Volumes 1 and 2 – specification-aligned, accessible
- **Steve Chapman** (multiple titles) – research methods and topic-specific texts
- **Napier Press** – examination-focused revision guides
- **Sociology Review** magazine (Philip Allan Updates) – accessible articles on contemporary issues

## Challenges and Opportunities

### Widening Participation

Sociology remains underrepresented in elite Russell Group universities compared to traditional humanities subjects, despite its analytical rigour. Heidi Safia Mirza's work highlights how Sociology attracts diverse student populations, including ethnic minority and working-class students who see their experiences reflected in curriculum content. Teachers should emphasise Sociology's academic credibility and progression routes into social research, policy analysis, social work, teaching, and numerous graduate careers.

### Addressing Confirmation Bias

Students may approach Sociology with preconceptions based on media representations or personal politics. Teachers must emphasise that Sociology requires evidence-based analysis rather than opinion. Diane Reay's ethnographic work exemplifies rigorous qualitative methodology producing findings that challenge both left and right political assumptions.

### Decolonising the Curriculum

Recent debates encourage broadening perspectives beyond predominantly white, Western, male sociologists. Heidi Safia Mirza advocates for centring rather than marginalising Black British and post-colonial perspectives. Teachers can incorporate:

- Stuart Hall on race and identity
- Paul Gilroy on Black Atlantic cultures
- Tariq Modood on multiculturalism
- Heidi Safia Mirza on intersectionality in education
- Avtar Brah on diaspora communities

### Digital Literacy and Research Methods

Contemporary Sociology must address big data, social media research, digital ethnography, and online survey methods. The British Sociological Association (BSA) provides updated ethical guidelines for digital research.

## Assessment Preparation

### Examination Technique

Students require explicit instruction in:

1. **Command word interpretation:** "Outline" (AO1, description), "Apply" (AO2, contextualised examples), "Analyse" (AO3, break down arguments), "Evaluate" (AO3, weigh strengths/limitations).

2. **Point-Evidence-Explain structure:** Sociological claims require evidential support (studies, statistics, theories) and analytical explanation connecting evidence to the question.

3. **Evaluation criteria:** Students should assess theoretical perspectives against criteria including explanatory power, empirical support, contemporary relevance, internal logic, and comparative strengths/weaknesses.

4. **Time management:** AQA examinations require approximately 1.5 minutes per mark, leaving planning and checking time.

### Common Misconceptions

- Confusing correlation with causation
- Treating perspectives as monolithic (e.g., "feminists believe..." without specifying which feminist perspective)
- Descriptive rather than analytical responses
- Insufficient contemporary application
- Anecdotal rather than sociological evidence

## Progression and Impact

A Level Sociology develops transferable skills valued by universities and employers: critical thinking, analytical writing, research literacy, statistical interpretation, and evidence-based argumentation. The British Sociological Association (BSA) and learned societies like the Social Policy Association provide resources supporting transition from A Level to undergraduate study.

Diane Reay's research demonstrates how Sociology education can develop critical consciousness about structural inequality, potentially inspiring civic engagement and social justice commitments. However, teachers must balance critical analysis with academic objectivity, presenting multiple perspectives fairly while encouraging students to evaluate evidence independently.

## Conclusion

A Level Sociology provides students with powerful conceptual tools for understanding contemporary UK society and their position within it. Effective curriculum delivery requires comprehensive theoretical knowledge, engagement with contemporary empirical research, and explicit skill development across AOs. By connecting classical sociological thinking with contemporary British evidence—drawing on scholars like Haralambos, Browne, Mirza, and Reay—teachers can deliver rigorous, engaging courses preparing students for higher education and informed citizenship.
