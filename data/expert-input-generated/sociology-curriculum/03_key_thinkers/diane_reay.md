# Diane Reay

**Full Name:** Diane Reay

**Current Role:** Emerita Professor of Education, University of Cambridge (formerly Professor in the Faculty of Education)

**Known For:** Groundbreaking research on social class and education, theorisation of emotional capital, feminist applications of Bourdieu's work, critiques of educational inequality and neoliberalism in UK schooling, and sustained examination of working-class experiences in education.

---

## Biography and Career History

Diane Reay is one of the UK's most influential sociologists of education, whose work has fundamentally shaped contemporary understanding of social class reproduction through schooling. Her academic trajectory is distinctive for combining lived experience as a primary school teacher in inner London with theoretical sophistication, creating research that bridges practice and critical sociology.

### Early Career and Academic Formation

Reay began her career as a primary school teacher in inner-city London schools during the 1970s and 1980s, an experience that profoundly informed her later academic work. This decade of teaching working-class children provided firsthand insight into educational disadvantage that became central to her scholarly agenda. She subsequently pursued academic qualifications whilst teaching, eventually completing her PhD at South Bank University in the 1990s.

### Academic Positions

After her doctorate, Reay joined King's College London before moving to London Metropolitan University. She then held a professorship at the Institute of Education, University of London (now UCL Institute of Education), where she conducted much of her seminal research on social class and education. In 2012, Reay was appointed Professor of Education at the University of Cambridge, a significant achievement particularly given her working-class background and her critical stance toward elite institutions. She remained at Cambridge until her retirement, after which she became Emerita Professor.

### Recognition and Standing

Reay has received numerous accolades including Fellowship of the Academy of Social Sciences and the British Educational Research Association (BERA) Distinguished Award. Her work is internationally recognised, though her focus remains firmly on UK educational contexts and inequalities. She has supervised numerous doctoral students who have themselves become influential in sociology of education.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Emotional Capital

One of Reay's most significant theoretical contributions is the concept of **emotional capital**, developed through her empirical work on mothers' involvement in children's education. Building on Bourdieu's capitals framework (economic, cultural, social), Reay argued that emotional resources—including emotional knowledge, time, and labour—constitute a distinct form of capital that is crucial in educational reproduction.

In her research, Reay demonstrated that middle-class mothers perform substantial emotional labour in supporting children's schooling: managing anxieties, maintaining motivation, liaising with teachers, and navigating educational systems. This emotional work is classed and gendered, with middle-class mothers typically possessing greater emotional resources and confidence to deploy them effectively. Working-class mothers often invest equivalent emotional energy but with less institutional recognition and effectiveness, experiencing what Reay terms 'costs without the benefits'. This concept has been particularly influential in understanding gendered dimensions of educational inequality and the hidden work of social reproduction.

### 2. Bourdieusian Feminism and Habitus

Reay is recognised for pioneering feminist applications of Pierre Bourdieu's sociological concepts to education. She has consistently argued that Bourdieu's framework, whilst powerful, requires feminist augmentation to adequately theorise gendered inequalities. Her work demonstrates how **habitus**—the embodied dispositions shaped by social position—is simultaneously classed and gendered.

Through ethnographic research, Reay has shown how working-class students, particularly girls, develop a 'habitus clivé' (split habitus) when accessing elite educational spaces. This divided habitus creates psychological costs as students negotiate contradictions between their backgrounds and institutional cultures. Her analysis reveals education as a site of both symbolic violence and resistance, where working-class students experience belonging as conditional and uncomfortable.

### 3. Social Class and Educational Choice

Reay's extensive research on school choice, conducted with colleagues Stephen Ball and Miriam David, has been foundational to understanding how educational markets reproduce inequality. Their study, documented in *'Degrees of Choice'* (2005) and related publications, demonstrated that school choice is profoundly classed.

Middle-class families approach choice as 'skilled choosers', possessing cultural capital to decode educational distinctions, social capital to access privileged information networks, and economic capital to exercise spatial mobility. Working-class families, characterised as 'semi-skilled' or 'disconnected choosers', face constrained choices shaped by locality, material resources, and relationships with educational institutions. Reay's work revealed that marketisation, far from empowering all parents equally, amplifies existing inequalities through differential capacities to 'play the game'.

### 4. The Miseducation Thesis

Reay's 2017 book *'Miseducation: Inequality, Education and the Working Classes'* represents a powerful synthesis of her career-long research. The concept of **miseducation** refers to how the English education system systematically fails working-class children not merely through underachievement but through pedagogical approaches, curricular content, and institutional cultures that devalue working-class identities and knowledges.

Reay argues that contemporary education inflicts symbolic violence through several mechanisms: the narrowing of curricula under neoliberal reform; obsessive testing regimes that label working-class children as failures; the erosion of comprehensive education; and elite institutions' monopolisation of educational success. Miseducation captures both the inadequate education working-class children receive and the psychological harm of being positioned as educationally inferior. This thesis challenges deficit framings that locate educational failure in working-class families rather than in systemic structures.

### 5. White Working-Class Educational Disadvantage

Reay has contributed significantly to debates about white working-class underachievement, offering nuanced analysis that avoids both pathologisation and romanticisation. Her research reveals how white working-class students, particularly boys, experience education as alienating and hostile to their identities. However, she consistently contextualises this within broader class inequalities rather than treating it as an isolated 'white disadvantage' issue.

In *'Miseducation'* and related articles, Reay demonstrates that white working-class educational marginalisation results from deindustrialisation, spatial segregation, and neoliberal abandonment rather than multicultural policies, as some commentators claim. She critiques how white working-class disadvantage is sometimes mobilised in reactionary political discourse whilst substantive educational inequalities remain unaddressed.

### 6. The Psychosocial Costs of Educational Mobility

Reay's work has illuminated the **psychic costs** of working-class students accessing elite education. Drawing on psychoanalytic concepts alongside Bourdieu, she has shown how educational mobility is rarely straightforward upward trajectory but involves loss, ambivalence, and ongoing negotiation of class identity.

Working-class students at elite universities experience what Reay terms 'fish out of water' sensations—a persistent sense of not belonging that affects wellbeing and academic confidence. Her Cambridge-based research revealed how working-class students engage in extensive identity work, managing impressions and hiding backgrounds to mitigate stigma. This analysis challenges simple meritocratic narratives, revealing how class inequalities persist even for those who 'succeed' within the system.

### 7. Critique of Neoliberalism in Education

Throughout her work, Reay provides sustained critique of **neoliberal education reform** in England. She analyses how marketisation, performativity, and accountability regimes have intensified inequalities whilst claiming to address them. Her research documents how policies including league tables, academisation, and free schools have fragmented comprehensive provision and legitimised new forms of segregation.

Reay is particularly critical of how neoliberalism reconfigures educational discourse around parental choice and aspiration, individualising structural inequalities. She argues that contemporary policy borrows social justice rhetoric whilst implementing mechanisms that concentrate advantage. Her critique connects educational change to broader political economy, situating school reform within austerity, privatisation, and erosion of public services.

### 8. Higher Education Access and Elitism

Reay's recent work examines social class inequalities in higher education, particularly regarding elite universities including Cambridge. Her research reveals persistent barriers facing working-class students: financial constraints, cultural alienation, insufficient information, and institutional practices that favour privileged applicants.

Significantly, Reay critiques widening participation initiatives that place responsibility on individuals to 'aspire' rather than addressing institutional exclusivity. She argues that elite universities engage in symbolic inclusion whilst maintaining substantive barriers, admitting small numbers of working-class students who face ongoing marginalisation. This work challenges universities to fundamentally transform cultures rather than simply diversifying intake.

## Key Publications

### Books

**'Degrees of Choice: Social Class, Race and Gender in Higher Education'** (2005, with Stephen Ball and Miriam David, Trentham Books)

This influential study examined how social class, ethnicity, and gender shape higher education choices. Based on extensive qualitative research with prospective students and families, it revealed systematic differences in how students navigate higher education applications. The concept of 'contingent choosers' versus 'embedded choosers' demonstrated how working-class students' choices are constrained by practical considerations and institutional knowledge gaps. This book established Reay as a leading scholar of educational choice and remains essential reading for understanding classed dimensions of university access.

**'White Middle Class Identities and Urban Schooling'** (2008, with Gill Crozier and David James, Palgrave Macmillan)

This ethnographic study explored how white middle-class families navigate urban, ethnically diverse comprehensive schools. Contrary to assumptions that middle-class families inevitably flee state schools, Reay and colleagues identified 'middle-class pioneers' committed to comprehensive ideals. However, the research revealed contradictions as these families simultaneously deployed advantage to secure benefits for their children. The book provides sophisticated analysis of how middle-class progressivism coexists with practices that reproduce privilege, contributing to understanding of classed educational strategies.

**'Miseducation: Inequality, Education and the Working Classes'** (2017, Policy Press)

Reay's magnum opus synthesises decades of research into a powerful indictment of English education's treatment of working-class children. Drawing on historical analysis, policy critique, and ethnographic data, the book argues that working-class miseducation is structural rather than incidental. Reay traces how neoliberal reforms have intensified class inequalities, eroded comprehensive principles, and psychologically damaged working-class young people. The book proposes a 'really good education for all' based on principles of equality, comprehensive provision, and valuing diverse knowledges. *'Miseducation'* has been widely acclaimed as essential reading for understanding educational inequality in contemporary Britain.

**'The new sociology of social class'** (2022, co-edited with Mike Savage and Helene Snee, Palgrave Macmillan)

This edited collection, to which Reay contributed, addresses contemporary debates in class analysis, including education's role in class reproduction. It demonstrates Reay's ongoing engagement with sociological theory and her influence on wider class scholarship beyond education specifically.

### Key Journal Articles and Chapters

**'Classroom Encounters: Power, Pedagogy and the Ghetto'** (1991, in *European Journal of Intercultural Studies*)

An early article drawing on Reay's teaching experience, analysing pedagogical relationships in inner-city classrooms and introducing themes that would characterise her career.

**'Cultural Reproduction: Mothers' Involvement in Their Children's Primary Schooling'** (1998, in *British Journal of Sociology of Education*)

Seminal article introducing the concept of emotional capital and analysing gendered class dynamics in parental involvement. This paper established Reay's reputation for innovative theoretical development grounded in empirical research.

**'Staying Local, Embedding Global: The Micro-politics of Choosing Schools'** (2004, with Gill Crozier and David James, in *Journal of Education Policy*)

Important contribution to school choice literature, examining how working-class families' localised social networks shape educational decisions, contrasting with middle-class spatial mobility.

**'Gendering Bourdieu's Concept of Capitals? Emotional Capital, Women and Social Class'** (2004, in *The Sociological Review*)

Theoretical paper developing emotional capital concept and arguing for feminist augmentation of Bourdieu's capitals framework. Widely cited in subsequent scholarship on capitals and gender.

**'Troubling the Polarisation of Urban Educational Landscapes'** (2007, in *British Journal of Sociology of Education*)

Analysis challenging simplistic polarisations in urban education discourse, examining complexity of middle-class engagement with comprehensive schools.

**'A Dangerous Place to Be? Identity Work and Working-Class Scholarship Boys and Girls at Elite Universities'** (2010, in Ball, S. (ed.) *Routledge International Handbook of Sociology of Education*)

Chapter examining psychosocial experiences of working-class students at elite universities, introducing concepts of 'belonging' and identity management that influenced subsequent research.

**'Social Class in Higher Education: Still Aā Issues for Feminist Research?'** (2012, in *Gender and Education*)

Argues for continued attention to class in feminist education research, demonstrating intersections of class and gender in higher education access and experience.

**'The Working Classes and Higher Education: Meritocratic Fallacies'** (2013, in *Sociological Research Online*)

Critique of meritocracy discourse, demonstrating persistent class barriers in higher education despite widening participation rhetoric.

**'Social Class, Authenticity and Adult Learning'** (2015, with Gill Crozier, in *The Sociological Review*)

Examines working-class adult learners' negotiations of authenticity and belonging in educational contexts.

**'The Perverse Incentives of Academic Publishing: Challenging the Tyranny of Theory Over Social Justice'** (2016, in *International Journal of Inclusive Education*)

Methodological and political reflection on academic publishing's prioritisation of theory over social justice impact, arguing for research accountability to marginalised communities.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on Sociology Curriculum

Reay's work has become foundational in A-Level and undergraduate Sociology curricula addressing education. Her research on cultural capital, educational inequality, and social reproduction appears extensively in examination board specifications and textbooks.

For **AQA A-Level Sociology**, Reay's concepts feature prominently in the Education topic, particularly regarding differential educational achievement by social class. Students engage with her research on parental choice, cultural and emotional capital, and critiques of marketisation. Textbooks routinely cite Reay alongside Ball, Bourdieu, and Willis as essential theorists of educational inequality.

At undergraduate level, Reay's work is core reading for sociology of education modules across UK universities. *'Miseducation'* has become standard on reading lists addressing educational inequality, whilst her empirical studies of school choice and higher education access provide detailed case material for teaching qualitative methodology.

### Influence on Educational Policy Debates

Whilst Reay adopts an explicitly critical stance toward government policy, her research has significantly influenced policy debates, particularly regarding:

**Widening Participation in Higher Education:** Reay's research has informed critiques of university access programmes, highlighting limitations of aspiration-raising interventions that ignore structural barriers. Her work is cited by organisations including the Sutton Trust and universities developing contextual admissions policies.

**School Admissions and Choice:** Reay's research with Ball and David provided evidence for understanding how school choice mechanisms reproduce inequality, influencing debates about admissions policies, catchment areas, and social segregation. The Fair Admissions Campaign and Comprehensive Future draw on her work.

**Academies and Free Schools:** Reay has been a prominent critic of academisation, providing evidence of increased segregation. Her research is cited by organisations opposing fragmentation of comprehensive provision.

**Social Mobility Agenda:** Reay challenges dominant social mobility discourse that emphasises individual aspiration over structural change, offering alternative frameworks prioritising educational equality. Her critique has influenced left educational thinkers and practitioners seeking alternatives to neoliberal reform.

### Public Engagement

Reay regularly contributes to public debate through media engagement, writing for outlets including *The Guardian*, *Times Higher Education*, and appearing on broadcast media. She has given evidence to parliamentary committees and participates in educational campaigns. This public sociology work extends her academic influence into wider social and political spheres.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Commitment to Critical Pedagogy

Reay's teaching philosophy reflects her commitment to challenging educational inequalities rather than simply studying them. Former students and colleagues describe her pedagogy as:

**Democratically Oriented:** Reay works to flatten hierarchies in educational relationships, valuing students' experiential knowledge alongside academic theory. This reflects her critique of elitism and belief that educational institutions should be transformative rather than reproductive spaces.

**Politically Engaged:** Her teaching explicitly addresses power, inequality, and social justice, encouraging students to connect sociological analysis to political action. She rejects notions of value-neutral sociology, modelling politically committed scholarship.

**Methodologically Rigorous:** Despite critical orientation, Reay maintains exacting standards in research methodology. She teaches qualitative research techniques with emphasis on ethical practice, reflexivity, and representing research participants' voices authentically.

**Supportive of Working-Class Students:** Reay is known for particular commitment to supporting working-class and non-traditional students, providing mentorship addressing not only academic development but also navigation of elite institutional cultures. This reflects her research insights into belonging and identity work.
