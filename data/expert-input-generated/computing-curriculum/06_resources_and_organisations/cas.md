# Computing At School (CAS)

## Overview and Historical Context

Computing At School (CAS) is the subject association for computing education in the UK, operating as a grassroots, member-led organisation within BCS, The Chartered Institute for IT. Established in 2008, CAS played a pivotal role in transforming computing education across the United Kingdom, leading the campaign that culminated in the replacement of ICT with Computing in the National Curriculum in 2014.

The organisation emerged from growing concerns among computing professionals and educators that the existing ICT curriculum focused predominantly on digital literacy and application use, whilst neglecting fundamental computer science principles. **Simon Peyton Jones**, Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research Cambridge and Professor at Cambridge University, was instrumental in CAS's formation and advocacy work, co-authoring the influential report "The Case for Computer Science in Schools" which articulated the intellectual rigour and economic importance of computing education.

**Mark Dorling**, a founding member and former CAS National Coordinator, led much of the grassroots mobilisation that built CAS into a network of thousands of teachers. His work on the CAS Computing Progression Pathways provided one of the first comprehensive frameworks for assessing computational thinking and programming skills across Key Stages 1-3.

## Organisational Structure and Community

### Regional Networks

CAS operates through a distributed model of regional CAS Communities (formerly CAS Hubs), each led by volunteer Master Teachers. These communities typically meet termly in schools, universities, or technology companies to share practice, access continuing professional development (CPD), and build local networks. As of 2024, there are over 400 active communities across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

The hub model recognises the geographical and contextual diversity of UK schools. **Miles Berry**, Principal Lecturer at the University of Roehampton and prominent CAS contributor, has written extensively about how these communities embody professional learning communities, enabling teachers to develop pedagogical content knowledge through collaborative inquiry rather than top-down training.

### Online Community and Resources

The CAS Community platform (community.computingatschool.org.uk) hosts discussion forums, resource repositories, and working groups focused on specific aspects of computing education. Key resources include:

- **Barefoot Computing**: Primary computing resources emphasising computational thinking and unplugged activities
- **QuickStart Computing**: Cross-curricular projects linking computing to other National Curriculum subjects
- **Project Quantum**: Secondary computing resources aligned to GCSE specifications
- **CAS TV**: Video resources featuring expert teachers demonstrating pedagogy

## Key Contributions to UK Computing Education

### The 2014 National Curriculum Reform

CAS's most significant achievement was shaping the 2014 National Curriculum for Computing. Through evidence submissions, parliamentary briefings, and extensive consultation, CAS advocated for a curriculum encompassing computer science, information technology, and digital literacy. The resulting Programme of Study introduced computational thinking, algorithms, programming, and data representation as statutory requirements from Key Stage 1.

**Simon Peyton Jones** chaired the Computing at School Working Group that drafted the initial curriculum proposals, bringing together academics, industry representatives, and practising teachers. The curriculum's emphasis on foundational concepts rather than specific technologies reflected CAS's philosophy that computing education should develop transferable understanding rather than transient skills.

### Computing Progression Pathways

The CAS Computing Progression Pathways, developed under **Mark Dorling**'s leadership in 2013-2014, provided detailed assessment guidance when the new curriculum offered only broad statements of attainment. These pathways articulated progression from Year 1 to Year 9 across five strands: algorithms, programming and development, data and information, computers and hardware, and communication and networks.

Whilst statutory assessment frameworks have since evolved, the Progression Pathways influenced subsequent development of computing assessment, including examination board specifications and the National Centre for Computing Education's progression frameworks.

### Computational Thinking Frameworks

CAS popularised computational thinking as a unifying concept in computing education. Drawing on Jeannette Wing's academic work, CAS materials emphasised four key concepts (algorithms, decomposition, abstraction, pattern recognition) and practical approaches such as tinkering, creating, debugging, and persevering.

**Phil Bagge**, a primary computing specialist and CAS member, developed influential unplugged activities and visual programming resources that made computational thinking accessible to younger learners. His work demonstrated that sophisticated algorithmic thinking could be developed without requiring keyboard proficiency or complex syntax knowledge.

## Curriculum Support Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

CAS resources for Key Stage 1 focus on introducing algorithmic thinking through physical activities, programmable toys (such as Bee-Bots), and visual programming environments. The Barefoot Computing resources provide structured lesson sequences addressing:

- Creating and debugging simple algorithms using precise instructions
- Understanding that programs execute by following precise sequences
- Predicting behaviour of simple programs
- Using logical reasoning to explain how simple algorithms work

CAS advocates embedding computing within familiar contexts—storytelling, geography, art—rather than treating it as isolated technical content, particularly at this age.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

For Key Stage 2, CAS promotes progression from floor robots to screen-based programming using environments like Scratch, while developing understanding of computer systems, networks, and data. **Miles Berry**'s contributions to the QuickStart Computing project exemplify CAS's cross-curricular approach, with units linking programming to mathematics (coordinates, angles), science (simulations), and literacy (interactive stories).

Key Stage 2 CAS guidance emphasises:

- Block-based programming with sequence, selection, and repetition
- Variables for storing and manipulating data
- Understanding inputs, processes, and outputs
- Network architecture and internet safety in context
- Text-based programming introduction (typically Python in Year 6)

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

CAS resources for Key Stage 3 transition students toward text-based programming and deeper computer science concepts. The Progression Pathways identify development from "basic" to "intermediate" to "mastery" levels across topics including:

- **Algorithms**: From designing simple algorithms to analysing efficiency and using standard searching/sorting algorithms
- **Programming**: From sequence, selection, and iteration to procedures, functions, and data structures (lists, dictionaries)
- **Data Representation**: From understanding binary to representing text, images, and sound digitally, and compression principles
- **Hardware**: From identifying components to understanding fetch-execute cycles and Boolean logic
- **Networks**: From local networks to internet protocols, encryption, and cyber security

**Sue Sentance**, Chief Learning Officer at the Raspberry Pi Foundation and formerly King's College London, has researched extensively how teachers develop pedagogical content knowledge for these more complex topics. Her research with CAS teachers identified that many lacked prior computer science education themselves, highlighting the importance of subject-specific CPD.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16) - GCSE Support

Whilst CAS doesn't write examination specifications, it provides extensive support for GCSE Computer Science teaching. The Project Quantum resources align with content from AQA, OCR, Edexcel, and WJEC specifications, covering:

- Programming project development methodologies
- Computational thinking problem-solving strategies
- Binary arithmetic, hexadecimal, and character encoding
- Computational logic, truth tables, and logic gates
- Network topology, protocols, and layers
- Algorithm efficiency and Big-O notation
- Data structures (arrays, stacks, queues, linked lists)
- Programming paradigms and language translators

CAS working groups monitor examination outcomes and provide feedback to Ofqual and examination boards regarding question accessibility, programming language choices, and appropriate cognitive demand.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18) - A Level Support

For A Level Computer Science, CAS provides fewer ready-made resources but maintains active communities of practice where teachers share approaches to challenging topics such as:

- Object-oriented programming paradigms
- Functional programming concepts
- Regular expressions and finite state machines
- Database normalisation and SQL
- Big Data and emerging computational paradigms
- Computing-related legislation and ethical frameworks

CAS has contributed to discussions about the non-exam assessment component, particularly regarding appropriate scope for student projects and academic integrity issues.

## Professional Development and Teacher Support

### Master Teacher Programme

The CAS Master Teacher programme recognises expert computing teachers who provide peer mentoring and lead local communities. Master Teachers receive enhanced CPD, attend national conferences, and often contribute to resource development. This cascading model attempts to address the severe shortage of specialist computing teachers—research by **Sue Sentance** and colleagues identified that in 2015, only 35% of schools had a teacher with post-A Level computing qualifications.

### Network of Teaching Excellence in Computer Science (NoE)

From 2018, CAS partnered with the National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE) to deliver the Network of Teaching Excellence in Computer Science. This initiative provided structured CPD pathways through face-to-face courses, online modules, and certificate programmes. The partnership recognised that whilst CAS excelled at building community, systematic national improvement required coordinated professional development infrastructure.

### Certificated Professional Development

CAS offers several formal qualifications for teachers:

- **Certificate in Computer Science Teaching (Primary)**: Covering computational thinking, programming pedagogy, physical computing, and curriculum design
- **Certificate in Computer Science Teaching (Secondary)**: Including programming concepts, algorithm design, data representation, and assessment strategies
- **BCS Certificate in Computer Science Teaching**: Industry-recognised qualification validating subject knowledge and pedagogical understanding

These qualifications address the reality that many computing teachers, particularly at primary level, teach outside their initial training specialism.

## Research and Evidence Base

### CAS/TACK Project

The CAS/TACK (Technology, Algorithms, Computational thinking, and Knowledge) project, conducted with King's College London, provided empirical evidence about implementing the new computing curriculum. Research led by **Sue Sentance** examined teacher confidence, resource needs, and pedagogical approaches across early adopter schools.

Key findings included:

- Teachers required substantial subject knowledge development, not just pedagogical training
- Programming pedagogy differed significantly from other subjects, with debugging and problem-solving requiring specific instructional strategies
- Primary teachers particularly valued unplugged approaches before transitioning to digital tools
- Assessment remained problematic, with few schools developing reliable formative or summative approaches

### Longitudinal Studies

CAS has contributed to longitudinal research tracking student progression and teacher development. Studies by **Miles Berry** analysed Scratch project repositories to identify developmental trajectories, finding wide variation in computational sophistication even within age cohorts. This research informed discussion about appropriate expectations and differentiation strategies.

**Phil Bagge**'s classroom-based research demonstrated that primary-age children could engage with sophisticated algorithmic concepts (including recursion) when appropriately scaffolded through physical activities before abstract representation.

## Curriculum Debates and Positions

### Balance of Curriculum Strands

CAS advocated strongly for a three-strand curriculum model encompassing computer science, information technology (creating digital solutions), and digital literacy (using technology safely and effectively). This contrasted with some reform proposals that emphasised computer science almost exclusively.

**Simon Peyton Jones** articulated this position, arguing that whilst computer science provided intellectual foundations, practical creative computing motivated learners and demonstrated relevance. The final National Curriculum reflected this balance, requiring programming skills be applied to "create programs, systems, and content."

### Primary Programming Languages

CAS discussions frequently address appropriate programming environments for primary pupils. Whilst Scratch dominates Key Stage 2, CAS materials increasingly introduce text-based languages (particularly Python) in Years 5-6. **Phil Bagge** advocates this transition, arguing that familiarity with syntax reduces the cognitive leap required at Key Stage 3.

However, CAS recognises diversity in school contexts; Barefoot Computing also presents alternatives like ScratchJr, Code.org, and Blockly-based tools, emphasising concepts over specific platforms.

### Physical Computing

CAS championed physical computing (Raspberry Pi, micro:bit, Arduino) as motivating and tangible. The BBC micro:bit, distributed to all Year 7 pupils in 2016, emerged from collaboration between BBC, technology companies, and educational organisations including CAS. Physical computing provides immediate feedback, supports inclusive pedagogy, and connects computing to design technology and engineering contexts.

### Inclusion and Diversity

CAS has increasingly focused on gender balance and broadening participation. Research shows persistent gender gaps in computing uptake, particularly at GCSE and A Level. CAS resources highlight diverse role models, emphasise collaborative problem-solving over competitive coding, and provide guidance on inclusive classroom culture.

**Miles Berry** has written about ensuring computing curriculum connects with diverse interests, using contexts from creative arts, social sciences, and humanities rather than assuming all pupils are motivated by games or technical systems.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Accessing CAS Support

Teachers benefit from CAS membership (free via BCS registration) by:

1. Joining local CAS Communities for face-to-face professional learning
2. Accessing the online community for resources and peer support
3. Participating in special interest groups (primary, A Level, girls in computing, etc.)
4. Attending CAS conferences and professional development events
5. Engaging with curriculum development consultations

### Implementing CAS Approaches

CAS pedagogy emphasises:

- **Constructionist learning**: Students learn programming by creating personally meaningful projects, not merely completing exercises
- **Pair programming**: Collaborative coding with driver/navigator roles, promoting articulation of thinking
- **PRIMM pedagogy**: Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make—a structured approach to introducing new programming concepts
- **Semantic wave theory**: Moving between concrete examples and abstract principles (work by **Sue Sentance** and colleagues)
- **Unplugged activities**: Teaching computational thinking concepts without computers, addressing resource constraints and deepening conceptual understanding

### Assessment Strategies

CAS guidance recommends:

- Formative assessment through debugging challenges and code comprehension tasks
- Structured portfolios documenting project development processes
- Self and peer assessment using concept-specific success criteria
- Low-stakes testing of factual knowledge (binary, terminology, etc.) separate from programming assessment
- Recognising that debugging capability indicates deeper understanding than initial code production

## Contemporary Developments and Future Directions

### National Centre for Computing Education Partnership

Since 2018, CAS operates in close partnership with the NCCE, which assumed responsibility for systematic teacher professional development whilst CAS continues grassroots community building. This partnership attempts to combine national coordination with local community strengths.

### Post-Pandemic Adjustments

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated consideration of remote computing education. CAS communities shifted online during lockdowns, identifying pedagogies for teaching programming remotely. This experience influenced ongoing discussions about blended learning and appropriate use of online programming environments versus installed software.

### Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

Recent CAS resources address emerging topics including artificial intelligence, machine learning, and ethical implications of algorithmic decision-making. These topics feature increasingly in GCSE and A Level specifications, requiring teacher professional development beyond traditional computing topics.

**Miles Berry** and others advocate introducing AI concepts through accessible platforms like Machine Learning for Kids and Teachable Machine, enabling primary and secondary pupils to train simple models and critique algorithmic bias.

### Computational Science Integration

CAS increasingly promotes integrating computing with STEM subjects—using programming for mathematical modelling, scientific simulation, and data analysis. This addresses concerns that computing might become isolated rather than recognised as foundational for modern scientific and mathematical practice.

## Critical Perspectives and Limitations

Whilst CAS transformed UK computing education, several challenges remain:

- **Teacher supply**: Severe shortages of specialist computing teachers persist, particularly in disadvantaged areas
- **Curriculum overload**: Computing's expansion created time allocation challenges, particularly at primary level where subjects compete for timetable space
- **Resource inequality**: Schools vary dramatically in access to hardware, software, and specialist expertise
- **Assessment validity**: Concerns persist about whether GCSE programming tasks appropriately assess computational thinking versus syntax memorisation
- **Pedagogical research**: Robust empirical evidence about effective computing pedagogy remains limited compared to established subjects

**Sue Sentance**'s research identifies that despite curriculum change, teaching quality depends heavily on individual teacher expertise and confidence. The variation in computing education experience between schools remains substantial.

## Conclusion

Computing At School represents the UK's most significant subject-specific teacher organisation for computing education. Through grassroots mobilisation, evidence-based advocacy, and sustained professional community building, CAS fundamentally reshaped computing education across the United Kingdom. The organisation's emphasis on computer science foundations, computational thinking, and practical programming skills now influences computing education internationally.

For teachers, CAS provides invaluable professional support through local communities, online resources, and structured CPD. The organisation's materials, frameworks, and pedagogical approaches offer evidence-informed starting points for curriculum planning across all key stages.

As computing education continues evolving with technological change and emerging pedagogical research, CAS remains the primary forum for practising teachers to shape the subject's future direction, ensuring computing education serves learners' needs whilst maintaining disciplinary integrity.
