# National Centre for Computing Education

## Overview and Historical Context

The National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE) was established in 2018 as part of the UK government's response to the Computing At School (CAS) movement and the transformative 2014 National Curriculum reforms that replaced ICT with Computing. Funded by the Department for Education with an initial £84 million investment, the NCCE represents the UK government's largest single investment in subject-specific continuing professional development (CPD).

The NCCE emerged from recommendations made by the Royal Society's influential 2012 report "Shut down or restart? The way forward for computing in UK schools," which advocated for a comprehensive national infrastructure to support Computing education. Key figures including Simon Peyton Jones (Microsoft Research and University of Cambridge) and Mark Dorling (CAS) were instrumental in establishing the evidence base that led to the NCCE's creation.

The centre operates through a consortium model led by STEM Learning, in partnership with the Raspberry Pi Foundation and BCS, The Chartered Institute for IT. This partnership model draws on the pedagogical expertise of researchers including Sue Sentance (Raspberry Pi Foundation and King's College London) and Miles Berry (University of Roehampton).

## Core Mission and Objectives

The NCCE's primary mission is to ensure that every child in every school in England has access to high-quality Computing education. This encompasses three interconnected objectives:

### Supporting Teachers Through CPD

The NCCE provides extensive professional development opportunities, recognising that many teachers delivering Computing lack formal qualifications in the subject. Research by Sue Sentance and colleagues (2012) identified that only 35% of Computing teachers in England held a relevant degree, highlighting the urgent need for subject-specific training.

The centre offers:
- Face-to-face courses through a network of Computing Hubs
- Online courses accessible nationally
- Remote teaching provision
- A-level subject knowledge courses aligned with AQA, OCR, and Edexcel specifications
- Primary Computing certificate programmes
- Key Stage 3 pedagogy courses

### Providing High-Quality Teaching Resources

The NCCE has developed the "Teach Computing Curriculum," a comprehensive, evidence-informed scheme of work covering Key Stages 1-4. This curriculum embodies principles advocated by Phil Bagge (author and Computing educator) regarding age-appropriate progression in computational thinking and programming concepts.

The curriculum incorporates pedagogical approaches including:
- PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) methodology developed by Sue Sentance and colleagues
- Unplugged activities inspired by CS Unplugged and CAS Barefoot Computing
- Use of block-based programming environments (Scratch) transitioning to text-based languages (Python)
- Clear learning graphs showing progression pathways

### Building a Community of Practice

Following the community-building model established by Computing At School, the NCCE maintains a network of Computing Hubs across England and facilitates peer support through online platforms and regional events.

## Structure and Delivery Model

### Computing Hubs Network

The NCCE operates through approximately 40 Computing Hubs distributed across England, each typically based in a school, Multi-Academy Trust, or university. These hubs employ Computing Hub Leads who coordinate local CPD delivery and provide ongoing support to schools in their region.

This distributed model ensures geographical accessibility and allows for context-specific support, particularly important given the varied nature of Computing provision across different school types and demographics.

### Certificate Programmes

The NCCE offers structured certificate pathways that provide recognition for teachers' professional development:

**Primary Certificate in Computing Education**: Comprising 8 hours of CPD, this programme covers foundational Computing concepts including algorithms, programming (typically using Scratch or ScratchJr), data handling, and digital safety aligned with Key Stage 1 and 2 requirements.

**Secondary Certificate in Computing Education**: Requiring 12 hours of CPD, this pathway addresses Key Stage 3 content including more advanced programming concepts, networks, cybersecurity, and computational thinking pedagogy.

**A Level Subject Knowledge Certificate**: This intensive programme supports teachers delivering A Level Computer Science, covering content from AQA (8525), OCR (H446), Edexcel (9CP0), and WJEC specifications, including advanced programming paradigms, data structures, algorithms, and systems architecture.

## Alignment with National Curriculum

The NCCE's resources and training directly support delivery of the National Curriculum programmes of study for Computing at Key Stages 1-4.

### Key Stage 1 and 2 Provision

The NCCE's primary resources address statutory requirements including:
- Understanding algorithms and creating simple programs (KS1)
- Designing, writing and debugging programs using sequence, selection and repetition (KS2)
- Using logical reasoning to detect errors (KS1) and explain algorithms (KS2)
- Understanding computer networks and internet basics (KS2)
- Safe, respectful and responsible digital behaviour (KS1 and 2)

Miles Berry's work on progression in primary Computing informs the NCCE's scaffolded approach, emphasising concrete-to-abstract transitions in algorithmic thinking.

### Key Stage 3 Resources

The Teach Computing Curriculum for KS3 provides comprehensive coverage of statutory requirements:
- Programming in two or more languages (typically Python and HTML/CSS/JavaScript)
- Data representation including binary, hexadecimal, and character encoding
- Boolean logic and truth tables
- Computer systems architecture
- Networks, including the internet and protocols
- Cybersecurity principles
- Computational thinking and problem decomposition

Mark Dorling's framework for progression pathways in Computing heavily influences the NCCE's KS3 curriculum structure, particularly the logical sequencing of concepts building towards GCSE.

### Key Stage 4 Support

Whilst GCSE specifications are determined by exam boards, the NCCE provides:
- Transition materials from KS3 to GCSE
- Subject knowledge enhancement for teachers
- Teaching approaches for challenging topics (e.g., assembly language, computational complexity)
- Resources supporting coursework elements (programming projects for AQA, OCR)

## Pedagogical Approaches and Research Base

The NCCE's methodology is grounded in Computing education research, particularly work conducted at King's College London, the Raspberry Pi Foundation's Computing Education Research Centre, and international research communities.

### PRIMM Framework

Developed by Sue Sentance, Jane Waite, and colleagues, PRIMM provides a structured approach to teaching programming:

**Predict**: Learners read code and predict outcomes, developing code comprehension
**Run**: Execute code to test predictions, developing debugging skills
**Investigate**: Examine code structure and functionality, understanding patterns
**Modify**: Make targeted changes to code, applying understanding
**Make**: Create original programs, demonstrating mastery

This approach addresses common difficulties in novice programming identified by research, including the "plan-composition effect" and challenges with variable comprehension.

### Use-Modify-Create Progression

Phil Bagge's advocacy for gradual release of responsibility is reflected in NCCE resources that scaffold learning from:
1. Using existing programs to understand concepts
2. Modifying code with increasing independence
3. Creating original solutions to novel problems

This aligns with cognitive load theory and helps prevent overwhelming novice programmers whilst building confidence and competence.

### Semantic Waves and Concept Development

Drawing on work in cumulative knowledge building, NCCE materials demonstrate how concepts "wave" between concrete examples and abstract principles. For instance, teaching sequence, selection, and iteration through tangible robot programming (e.g., Bee-Bots) before abstract Scratch implementations, then formalising as control structures in Python.

## Subject Knowledge Enhancement

A distinctive feature of NCCE provision is comprehensive subject knowledge development, addressing the reality that many teachers transitioned from ICT or were recruited from other subjects.

### Computer Science Accelerator Programme

This intensive programme, supported by Isaac Computer Science (developed at the University of Cambridge with involvement from Simon Peyton Jones), provides structured subject knowledge development for secondary teachers covering:

- Programming fundamentals and advanced concepts (object-oriented programming, recursion, event-driven programming)
- Theoretical computer science (Big O notation, searching and sorting algorithms, graph theory)
- Computer architecture (Von Neumann model, fetch-execute cycle, processor components)
- Data representation (floating-point arithmetic, compression algorithms)
- Networks and security (TCP/IP stack, encryption methods, cybersecurity principles)
- Databases and SQL
- Ethical, legal and environmental impacts

This programme particularly supports teachers preparing for A Level delivery and addresses subject knowledge gaps identified in Ofsted's 2022 report "Finding the optimum: the Computer Science subject report."

## Impact and Evidence Base

Research evaluating the NCCE's impact demonstrates significant improvements in teaching quality and teacher confidence:

### Teacher Participation

By 2023, over 90,000 teachers had engaged with NCCE CPD, representing approximately 70% of state-funded secondary schools and 60% of primary schools in England. This extensive reach addresses the scale of need identified when the programme launched.

### Improved Subject Knowledge

Studies by King's College London (Sue Sentance et al., 2020) found statistically significant improvements in teachers' Computing subject knowledge following NCCE courses, with particularly strong effects in programming confidence and understanding of pedagogical content knowledge—knowing how to teach Computing concepts effectively.

### Teaching Practice Changes

Observational research indicates teachers who complete NCCE training demonstrate:
- More frequent use of live coding and worked examples
- Better questioning techniques around computational thinking
- Increased use of formative assessment in practical programming lessons
- More effective differentiation strategies

### Persistent Challenges

Despite progress, Ofsted's 2022 Computing subject report identified ongoing concerns:
- Variability in Computing provision between schools
- Insufficient curriculum time at KS3 (often single lessons per week)
- Gaps in teacher subject knowledge at primary level
- Assessment practices focusing on programming rather than broader Computing concepts

The NCCE continues to address these through enhanced primary support and assessment guidance.

## Resources and Platforms

### Teach Computing Curriculum

The flagship resource, freely available to all educators, provides:
- Detailed lesson plans with timing, differentiation, and common misconceptions
- Slide decks and worksheets
- Assessment materials including formative checks and summative assessments
- Video demonstrations of key concepts
- Homework and extension activities

The curriculum follows a spiral approach where concepts are revisited with increasing depth, reflecting Jerome Bruner's theories on curriculum design adapted for Computing by Miles Berry and others.

### Isaac Computer Science

Developed specifically for A Level and focusing on theoretical content, Isaac provides:
- Interactive questions with instant feedback
- Topic-by-topic coverage aligned with exam specifications
- Practice examination questions
- Progress tracking for teachers

This platform particularly supports independent study and flipped classroom approaches advocated by Sue Sentance for A Level Computing.

### Resource Collections by Topic

The NCCE curates resources for specific topics presenting common difficulties:
- Teaching algorithms and algorithmic thinking
- Networks and internet fundamentals
- Binary and hexadecimal conversion
- Boolean logic
- Cybersecurity principles
- Physical computing (using micro:bit, Raspberry Pi)

These collections often incorporate resources from partner organisations including BCS, Raspberry Pi Foundation projects (Code Club, CoderDojo), and CAS community contributions.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Getting Started with NCCE Support

Teachers new to Computing should:

1. **Register on the NCCE website** to access free resources and identify your local Computing Hub
2. **Complete the relevant certificate programme** (Primary or Secondary) to build foundational pedagogical content knowledge
3. **Adopt the Teach Computing Curriculum** or use it to audit and enhance existing schemes of work
4. **Join the online community** to connect with peers and access additional support
5. **Attend local network events** organised by Computing Hubs for subject-specific professional dialogue

### Integrating NCCE Resources into Planning

The NCCE resources are designed for flexible implementation:

**Direct adoption**: Schools can use the Teach Computing Curriculum as their complete scheme of work, particularly valuable where subject expertise is limited.

**Enhancement model**: More confident departments can use NCCE resources to supplement existing planning, addressing specific knowledge gaps or introducing new pedagogical approaches like PRIMM.

**Transition support**: The resources are particularly valuable when transitioning from ICT-focused schemes to Computing, providing structured progression in computational thinking.

### Subject Leadership Development

Computing subject leaders can use NCCE provision strategically:
- Audit staff subject knowledge against NCCE course content
- Plan coordinated CPD across the department
- Use the curriculum progression pathways to evaluate and develop departmental schemes of work
- Access subject leadership courses addressing Computing-specific issues (assessment, curriculum design, pedagogy)

### Progression Planning

Mark Dorling's progression pathways work, integrated into NCCE planning, emphasises:
- Clear prerequisite knowledge for each concept
- Age-appropriate abstraction levels
- Coherent progression from concrete to abstract thinking
- Links between practical skills and theoretical understanding

Teachers should consider how NCCE resources build toward GCSE and A Level, ensuring KS3 provision creates strong foundations rather than simply being "taster" lessons.

## Supporting Different Key Stages

### Primary (Key Stages 1 and 2)

Primary teachers face particular challenges given many lack Computing backgrounds. The NCCE primary provision emphasises:

- **Building teacher confidence** through subject knowledge development before pedagogical training
- **Unplugged activities** that teach computational concepts without computers, reducing technical barriers
- **Progressive use of tools**: ScratchJr (KS1) → Scratch (KS2) → text-based introductions (upper KS2)
- **Cross-curricular links** showing Computing's relevance to mathematics, science, and literacy

Phil Bagge's work on primary programming pedagogy particularly influences NCCE primary resources, emphasising playful exploration building to structured program creation.

### Key Stage 3

This crucial transition phase establishes Computing as a rigorous discipline:

- **Two programming languages**: Block-based (Scratch/Python hybrid) and text-based (Python)
- **Introduction of formal concepts**: Binary representation, Boolean logic, algorithms
- **Computational thinking explicitly taught** as transferable problem-solving skills
- **Physical computing projects** using micro:bit or Raspberry Pi to engage diverse learners

Resources address the challenge identified by Sue Sentance of maintaining engagement whilst increasing cognitive demand.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

NCCE support for GCSE includes:
- Specification-specific guidance for AQA (8525), OCR (J277), Edexcel (1CP2), and WJEC
- Programming project support addressing common pitfalls in coursework elements
- Difficult concept deep-dives (e.g., binary arithmetic, assembly language, data structures)
- Examination technique workshops

### Key Stage 5 (A Level)

The Computer Science Accelerator and Isaac Computer Science particularly support A Level delivery, addressing:
- Advanced programming (recursion, object-oriented design, event-driven programming)
- Theory of computation (finite state machines, regular expressions, Big O notation)
- Complex algorithms (searching, sorting, graph traversal, tree traversal)
- Systems architecture (pipelining, cache memory, virtual memory)
- Functional programming (examined by OCR and AQA)

Simon Peyton Jones' work on functional programming education influences the NCCE's Haskell resources for OCR's specification.

The NCCE maintains quality through:

All resources undergo pedagogical review informed by:
- Computing education research literature
- Classroom trialling with feedback loops

- Alignment with National Curriculum statutory requirements

### Continuous Improvement Cycle

Resources are regularly updated based on:
- Teacher feedback collected through evaluations
- Emerging research in Computing pedagogy

- Technological developments requiring curriculum updates

Delivery partners (Computing Hubs, course providers) must meet quality standards including:
- Qualified facilitators with relevant expertise

- Participation in facilitator CPD
