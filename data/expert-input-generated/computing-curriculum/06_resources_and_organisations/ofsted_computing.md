# Ofsted and Computing

## Overview of Ofsted's Role in Computing Education

The Office for Standards in Education, Children's Services and Skills (Ofsted) plays a crucial role in monitoring and evaluating the quality of Computing education in English schools. Since the introduction of the Computing curriculum in 2014, replacing the previous ICT curriculum, Ofsted has been instrumental in assessing how effectively schools implement the subject's three core strands: computer science, information technology, and digital literacy.

Ofsted's approach to inspecting Computing has evolved significantly since 2014, with inspectors increasingly recognising the subject's importance in preparing pupils for the digital economy and its contribution to broader educational outcomes.

## Ofsted's Computing Inspection Framework

### The Education Inspection Framework (EIF) and Computing

Under the Education Inspection Framework (introduced in September 2019), Ofsted evaluates Computing through four key judgement areas:

**Quality of Education**: This examines whether the Computing curriculum is ambitious, coherent, and properly sequenced. Inspectors assess whether schools have developed a progression model that builds pupils' knowledge from EYFS through to Key Stage 4 and beyond. Miles Berry has emphasised the importance of progression in computational thinking, noting that schools must demonstrate how concepts like abstraction, algorithms, and decomposition develop in complexity across key stages.

**Behaviour and Attitudes**: Inspectors consider how well pupils engage with Computing lessons, particularly in practical programming tasks and collaborative problem-solving activities.

**Personal Development**: Computing contributes to pupils' broader development through digital citizenship, online safety, and understanding the ethical implications of technology.

**Leadership and Management**: This includes evaluation of subject leadership, staff expertise, and how schools have addressed the significant challenge of teacher recruitment and retention in Computing, an issue highlighted by Sue Sentance's research at King's College London.

### Deep Dives in Computing

Since the EIF's introduction, Computing has frequently been selected for "deep dive" scrutiny. During a deep dive, inspectors:

- Review the curriculum's ambition and coherence across year groups
- Examine progression in key concepts such as algorithms, programming, data representation, and networks
- Conduct work scrutiny of pupils' programming projects, computational thinking exercises, and digital artefacts
- Observe lessons at different key stages
- Interview subject leaders about curriculum intent, implementation, and impact
- Speak with pupils about their learning and retention of knowledge
- Evaluate assessment approaches and how teachers identify and address misconceptions

Phil Bagge, a prominent Computing educator and author, has noted that schools often struggle during deep dives when they cannot articulate clear progression in programming concepts or when Computing is taught in isolated blocks rather than throughout the academic year.

## Key Ofsted Publications on Computing

### Research Review Series: Computing (2022)

Ofsted published a comprehensive Research Review for Computing in May 2022, representing the most significant analysis of Computing education evidence to date. This document draws on international research and expert consultation to establish what high-quality Computing education looks like.

**Key findings from the Research Review include**:

1. **Knowledge Organisation**: The review emphasises that Computing knowledge should be organised into substantive knowledge (the 'what') and disciplinary knowledge (the 'how'). Substantive knowledge includes concepts like variables, selection, iteration, and Boolean logic, whilst disciplinary knowledge encompasses practices like debugging, testing, and algorithmic thinking.

2. **Progression Models**: The review endorses clear progression models, citing the work of researchers including Sue Sentance and the Raspberry Pi Foundation. It notes that effective progression involves both horizontal development (breadth of contexts) and vertical development (depth and complexity of concepts).

3. **Programming Pedagogy**: Drawing on research by Sentence, Waite, and Kallia (2019), the review highlights effective approaches including PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make), Use-Modify-Create frameworks, and block-based languages as stepping stones to text-based programming.

4. **Common Misconceptions**: The review identifies prevalent misconceptions in Computing, such as pupils confusing assignment with equality, misunderstanding variable scope, and struggling with the concept of abstraction. Mark Dorling's work on progression pathways is referenced in addressing these challenges.

5. **Assessment Approaches**: The review recommends frequent low-stakes testing, live programming assessments, code comprehension tasks, and project-based assessments that evaluate both process and product.

### Subject Reports and Annual Reports

Ofsted's annual reports have increasingly featured Computing since 2014. The 2018 Annual Report noted concerns about:

- Insufficient curriculum time for Computing, particularly at Key Stage 3
- The quality of teaching, especially where non-specialist staff delivered the curriculum
- Assessment practices that focused more on completed products than underlying computational concepts
- Weak progression from Key Stage 2 to Key Stage 3

## Ofsted Findings: Key Stage Analysis

### Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS)

Ofsted recognises that whilst Computing is not a statutory subject in EYFS, early computational thinking develops through:

- Logical reasoning in play activities
- Creating and following instructions
- Understanding cause and effect through programmable toys
- Beginning to understand algorithms through everyday routines

The Research Review notes that early programming experiences with floor robots like Bee-Bots lay foundations for later abstract thinking, aligning with research by Berry and Donaldson on primary Computing.

### Key Stage 1

Ofsted expects to see pupils developing foundational understanding of:

- Algorithms as precise instructions
- Creating and debugging simple programs
- Logical reasoning to predict program behaviour
- Purposeful use of technology to create, organise, and store digital content
- Safe and respectful technology use

Common weaknesses identified include superficial engagement with programming concepts and insufficient emphasis on debugging as a learning opportunity.

### Key Stage 2

At Key Stage 2, Ofsted's Research Review emphasises the importance of:

- Systematic progression in programming complexity, moving from sequence to selection and iteration
- Understanding computer networks and the internet's structure
- Introduction to variables and different data types
- Developing more sophisticated debugging and testing strategies
- Beginning to understand Boolean logic

Simon Peyton Jones, who chaired the 2014 curriculum working group, has emphasised that Key Stage 2 is critical for developing computational thinking before the abstract challenges of Key Stage 3. Ofsted inspections have noted that schools performing well ensure regular, sustained Computing teaching rather than blocked ICT-style units.

### Key Stage 3

Ofsted has consistently identified Key Stage 3 as the weakest phase for Computing. The 2022 Research Review and subsequent inspection findings highlight:

**Curriculum Time**: Many schools allocate insufficient time to Computing, sometimes rotating it with other subjects or providing only one hour per fortnight. This fragmentation prevents deep learning and skill development.

**Textual Programming**: The transition from block-based languages (Scratch, Blockly) to text-based languages (Python, Java) represents a significant cognitive leap. Ofsted notes that successful schools scaffold this transition carefully, often using hybrid environments or PRIMM pedagogy.

**Conceptual Depth**: The curriculum requires understanding of:
- Two programming languages (at least one textual)
- Boolean logic and truth tables
- Binary representation and hexadecimal
- Data representation (text, images, sound)
- Network protocols and cybersecurity fundamentals
- Computational thinking applied to real-world problems

**Common Weaknesses**: Ofsted reports frequently cite:
- Overemphasis on software applications rather than computer science concepts
- Programming projects without systematic teaching of underlying concepts
- Weak assessment that cannot distinguish between pupils who copy code and those who understand it
- Insufficient attention to fundamental concepts like abstraction and decomposition

### Key Stage 4

At GCSE level, Ofsted's focus shifts to examining:

- Curriculum choices and uptake rates (Computing GCSE numbers declined initially after 2014 but have since recovered)
- Teaching quality, particularly in programming and algorithmic thinking
- Assessment preparation across both examination and practical programming components
- The gender gap in Computing qualifications (typically 80:20 male to female)

The Research Review notes that schools with strong Key Stage 3 provision see better GCSE outcomes. It references research showing that project-based assessments (like OCR's programming project or AQA's practical endorsement) develop deeper understanding when scaffolded appropriately.

### Key Stage 5

For A-level Computing/Computer Science, Ofsted considers:

- Prior attainment requirements and whether schools set appropriate entry criteria
- Teaching of complex topics including advanced programming, systems architecture, and theory of computation
- The substantial programming project component (AQA NEA, OCR non-exam assessment, etc.)
- Progression to university computer science courses or technology apprenticeships

## Implications for Subject Leaders

### Curriculum Intent

Subject leaders must articulate a clear curriculum intent that demonstrates:

1. **Ambitious Scope**: Coverage of all National Curriculum requirements across computer science, information technology, and digital literacy strands
2. **Coherent Progression**: Clear learning sequences showing how concepts develop from EYFS to Key Stage 4
3. **Cultural Capital**: How Computing contributes to pupils' understanding of the digital world and prepares them for future participation in technology-driven society

Miles Berry's work on curriculum design emphasises that intent should reference both the National Curriculum's statutory requirements and broader objectives around computational thinking, creativity, and digital citizenship.

### Curriculum Implementation

Ofsted scrutinises implementation through:

**Pedagogical Approaches**: Evidence of effective teaching strategies including:
- PRIMM methodology for programming instruction
- Unplugged activities for introducing abstract concepts
- Pair programming and collaborative problem-solving
- Live coding demonstrations and worked examples
- Semantic waves (moving between concrete and abstract)

Sue Sentance's research at King's College London, particularly the TIPS project (Teacher Involvement in Programming Studies), informs Ofsted's understanding of effective Computing pedagogy.

**Assessment for Learning**: Ongoing formative assessment that:
- Identifies and addresses misconceptions promptly
- Uses diagnostic questions to probe conceptual understanding
- Incorporates code comprehension and tracing exercises
- Employs debugging tasks as assessment opportunities
- Provides meaningful feedback on programming practice and computational thinking

**Resources and Tools**: Appropriate selection of:
- Programming environments (Scratch, Python, JavaScript, etc.)
- Hardware (including considerations for physical computing with Micro:bit, Raspberry Pi, Arduino)
- Online platforms and learning resources
- Equity of access to technology

### Curriculum Impact

Demonstrating impact requires evidence of:

1. **Knowledge Retention**: Pupils remembering key concepts and being able to apply them in new contexts
2. **Skill Development**: Progressive improvement in programming capability, computational thinking, and digital creation
3. **Engagement and Aspiration**: Particularly addressing the gender gap and ensuring diverse pupils see Computing as relevant to their futures
4. **Progression Routes**: Numbers taking GCSE Computing, post-16 courses, and computing-related career paths

Mark Dorling's work on Computing progression frameworks provides models that many subject leaders use to evidence impact against age-appropriate expectations.

## Common Inspection Findings and Issues

### Strengths in High-Performing Schools

Ofsted's reports identify several characteristics of schools with strong Computing provision:

1. **Specialist Leadership**: Subject leaders with computer science expertise or sustained professional development
2. **Curriculum Time**: Protected curriculum time, particularly at Key Stage 3 (typically 1 hour per week minimum)
3. **Teacher Expertise**: Sustained investment in CPD through organisations like CAS (Computing at School) and the National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE)
4. **Sequenced Learning**: Careful progression planning with concepts revisited and deepened over time
5. **Assessment Integration**: Regular low-stakes assessment informing teaching
6. **Cultural Embedding**: Computing concepts reinforced across the curriculum, particularly in mathematics and science

### Areas for Development

Common weaknesses identified include:

1. **Curriculum Narrowing**: Overemphasis on programming at the expense of networks, data representation, or computational thinking
2. **Gender Disparity**: Insufficient strategies to address the significant gender gap in Computing uptake and attainment
3. **Key Stage 2-3 Transition**: Repetition of Key Stage 2 content rather than building on prior learning
4. **Assessment Validity**: Over-reliance on finished products rather than assessing understanding of underlying concepts
5. **Online Safety Integration**: Treating online safety as separate from Computing rather than integral to digital literacy
6. **Differentiation**: Insufficient challenge for more able pupils or support for those struggling with abstract concepts

## Supporting Evidence and Research

### Key Research Informing Ofsted's Approach

**The Royal Society (2012) - "Shut down or restart?"**: This influential report, involving Simon Peyton Jones, preceded the curriculum change and established the case for rigorous computer science education. Ofsted's approach reflects the report's recommendations.

**After the Reboot: Computing Education in UK Schools (2017)**: The Royal Society's follow-up study assessed early implementation of the 2014 curriculum, identifying concerns about teacher confidence and curriculum time that continue to inform Ofsted's inspection focus.

**Raspberry Pi Foundation Research**: Research by Sue Sentance, Jane Waite, and colleagues on pedagogy, including studies on PRIMM, teacher professional development, and common misconceptions, directly informs Ofsted's Research Review recommendations.

**CAS/Barefoot Computing Progression Framework**: This framework, developed with input from Miles Berry and others, provides a model for progression that Ofsted inspectors recognise as evidence of coherent curriculum planning.

**International Comparisons**: Ofsted's Research Review references international evidence from countries including Estonia, Israel, and New Zealand, acknowledging that Computing education remains relatively new globally and evidence continues to develop.

## Practical Recommendations for Teachers

### Preparing for Inspection

**Documentation**: Maintain clear curriculum documentation showing:
- Long-term plans demonstrating progression
- Medium-term plans showing lesson sequences
- Assessment records evidencing pupil progress
- Examples of pupil work across ability ranges

**Subject Leader Readiness**: Be prepared to discuss:
- Curriculum rationale and design decisions
- How misconceptions are identified and addressed
- Staff expertise and CPD engagement
- Assessment approaches and their validity
- Strategies to address diversity and inclusion challenges

**Pupil Voice**: Ensure pupils can articulate:
- What they have learned (not just what they have done)
- How current learning builds on prior knowledge
- Real-world applications of Computing concepts
- Their enjoyment and aspirations in Computing

### Continuous Improvement

Effective Computing departments demonstrate:

1. **Engagement with CAS and NCCE**: Regular participation in professional development, local CAS hubs, and NCCE training
2. **Action Research**: Small-scale investigations into effective pedagogy, informed by current research
3. **Cross-Phase Collaboration**: Communication with feeder primary schools and receiving secondary schools
4. **Industry Links**: Partnerships with technology employers and universities
5. **Curriculum Review**: Regular evaluation of curriculum coverage, progression, and impact

### Quality Assurance

Implement robust quality assurance including:
- Regular work scrutiny focusing on progression in concepts
- Learning walks observing pedagogy across key stages
- Pupil voice activities to understand their experience
- Analysis of internal and external assessment outcomes
- Comparison with national data on Computing uptake and outcomes

## Future Directions

Ofsted's evolving approach to Computing inspection reflects the subject's maturation since 2014. The 2022 Research Review represents a significant milestone in establishing evidence-based expectations. Subject leaders should anticipate:

1. **Increased Rigour**: Higher expectations for curriculum coherence and pedagogical sophistication
2. **Research-Informed Practice**: Greater emphasis on evidence-based approaches like PRIMM and Use-Modify-Create
3. **Equity Focus**: Increased scrutiny of strategies to address gender and diversity gaps
4. **Assessment Validity**: Closer examination of how assessment truly measures computational thinking and conceptual understanding
5. **Interdisciplinary Connections**: Recognition of Computing's role in STEM education and broader curriculum

The Computing education community, led by figures including Mark Dorling, Miles Berry, Sue Sentance, Phil Bagge, and organisations like CAS and the Raspberry Pi Foundation, continues to develop the evidence base and pedagogical approaches that inform Ofsted's evolving understanding of high-quality Computing education.

Subject leaders should engage actively with this community, contributing to and drawing from the collective expertise that shapes the future of Computing education in England.
