# Raspberry Pi Foundation

## Overview and Mission

The Raspberry Pi Foundation is a UK-based educational charity founded in 2008 with the mission to enable young people to realise their full potential through the power of computing and digital technologies. Headquartered in Cambridge, the Foundation has become one of the most influential organisations in UK Computing education, directly supporting the implementation of the 2014 Computing curriculum and providing resources, professional development, and research that underpin Computing teaching across all key stages.

The Foundation's work extends beyond the Raspberry Pi computer itself to encompass teacher training, educational research, online learning platforms, and curriculum development. As Mark Dorling has noted, the Foundation's contribution to the Computing at School (CAS) movement and its emphasis on computational thinking has been transformative for UK Computing education.

## The Raspberry Pi Computer

### Educational Hardware Design

The Raspberry Pi is a low-cost, credit-card-sized computer designed specifically to make computing accessible and to support the teaching of computer science. First released in 2012, the device was conceived by Eben Upton and colleagues at the University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory who were concerned about declining numbers and skills of students applying to study Computer Science.

The device typically costs between £5-£50 depending on the model, making it accessible for schools with limited budgets. It runs a variant of Linux (Raspberry Pi OS, formerly Raspbian) and supports multiple programming languages including Python, Scratch, and others. The inclusion of General Purpose Input/Output (GPIO) pins enables physical computing projects, directly supporting Key Stage 3 requirements to "design, use and evaluate computational abstractions that model the state and behaviour of real-world problems and physical systems" (National Curriculum for Computing, 2014).

### Curriculum Applications Across Key Stages

**Key Stage 1 and 2**: Whilst direct use of Raspberry Pi hardware may be challenging for younger learners, the Foundation's resources support the statutory requirement to "understand what algorithms are" and to "use logical reasoning to predict the behaviour of simple programs" through Scratch projects that can run on the device.

**Key Stage 3**: The device directly supports requirements to "use two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual" with Python being particularly well-supported. Phil Bagge has extensively documented classroom approaches using Raspberry Pi for teaching physical computing, noting its effectiveness in making abstract computational concepts concrete and engaging.

**Key Stage 4 and 5**: Raspberry Pi projects feature prominently in GCSE and A-Level coursework. OCR's GCSE Computer Science (J277) explicitly references physical computing systems, whilst AQA's A-Level Computer Science (7517) includes content on embedded systems that can be effectively taught using Raspberry Pi. The device supports the teaching of systems architecture, operating systems, and programming concepts required across all major UK exam boards.

## Educational Resources and Platforms

### The Raspberry Pi Foundation's Online Learning

The Foundation operates several major educational platforms:

**Computing Education Research Hub**: Provides evidence-based resources and research findings to support teachers. Sue Sentance, who has been closely associated with the Foundation's research work, has emphasised the importance of this research-practitioner dialogue in improving Computing pedagogy.

**Raspberry Pi Projects Website**: Offers over 200 free project guides suitable for learners aged 4-18, structured by difficulty level and curriculum area. Projects are explicitly mapped to curriculum requirements and include teacher guides, assessment opportunities, and differentiation suggestions.

**Code Club and CoderDojo Networks**: The Foundation supports and provides resources for these international networks of volunteer-led coding clubs. As of 2024, there are over 2,000 Code Clubs in UK schools and community venues, providing extra-curricular Computing opportunities that complement statutory provision.

### Professional Development and Training

The Foundation has become one of the UK's most significant providers of Computing teacher professional development:

**Teach Computing Curriculum**: Developed in partnership with the National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE), this comprehensive curriculum framework provides lesson plans, resources, and assessment materials for Key Stages 1-4. Miles Berry has highlighted this curriculum's careful pedagogical sequencing and its grounding in research-informed practice.

**Online Teacher Training**: The Foundation offers free Continuing Professional Development (CPD) through platforms including FutureLearn and its own learning platform. Courses cover programming pedagogy, physical computing, and computational thinking, with many offering CPR-accredited certificates.

**Isaac Computer Science**: A free online platform providing resources specifically designed for A-Level Computer Science teaching, featuring theory content, programming challenges, and examination practice aligned to all major UK exam boards (AQA, OCR, Edexcel, WJEC).

## Research and Evidence Base

### Computing Education Research

The Raspberry Pi Foundation maintains one of the UK's most active Computing education research teams. Their work has significantly influenced teaching practice and policy:

**Pedagogical Research**: Studies on block-based versus text-based programming transitions, the effectiveness of pair programming, and approaches to teaching debugging have informed the NCCE's recommended teaching strategies. This research directly addresses challenges identified by Sue Sentance and colleagues regarding effective progression in programming education.

**PRIMM Approach**: The Foundation has promoted the PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) framework for teaching programming, originally developed by Sue Sentance and colleagues. This structured approach to introducing new programming concepts is now widely adopted in UK secondary schools and features in resources from all major exam boards.

**Semantic Waves**: Working with researchers including Sue Sentance, the Foundation has explored how Legitimation Code Theory and semantic waves can help teachers move learners between abstract concepts and concrete examples—particularly important given Simon Peyton Jones's observation that Computing education must balance theoretical understanding with practical application.

### Impact Studies

Research commissioned by the Foundation has demonstrated:
- Increased engagement in Computing subjects, particularly among girls when physical computing projects are emphasised
- Improved problem-solving skills and computational thinking capabilities
- Enhanced teacher confidence when appropriate CPD and resources are provided

## Supporting Diverse and Inclusive Computing Education

### Addressing Participation Gaps

The Foundation has prioritised addressing gender and socioeconomic participation gaps in Computing:

**Gender Balance in Computing**: A major research and practical initiative documenting what works in encouraging girls' participation. Research findings emphasise the importance of relevance, collaboration, and avoiding stereotypes—principles now embedded in many schools' Computing curriculum design.

**Astro Pi**: The European Space Station-based educational programme engages learners with authentic, meaningful Computing applications. This initiative exemplifies Miles Berry's emphasis on showing learners the real-world significance of computational thinking.

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND)

Foundation resources include specific adaptations and guidance for supporting learners with SEND, including:
- Alternative input devices and accessibility features in project designs
- Differentiated resources and scaffolded approaches
- Visual programming options for learners who struggle with syntax

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Integrating Raspberry Pi in Computing Schemes of Work

**Resource Planning**: Schools typically require 1-2 Raspberry Pi devices per pair of students for effective hands-on learning. Many schools maintain a dedicated set for Computing lessons, with additional devices for physical computing projects. The Foundation's "Raspberry Pi for Education" scheme offers educational discounts.

**Curriculum Mapping**: The Foundation's resources explicitly indicate National Curriculum coverage, making integration into existing schemes of work straightforward. Teachers should note that whilst powerful for programming and physical computing, Raspberry Pi resources should complement rather than constitute the entire Computing curriculum—particularly regarding digital literacy and information technology skills.

**Assessment Opportunities**: Raspberry Pi projects provide authentic assessment opportunities for both KS3 teacher assessment and KS4/5 examination coursework. OCR's Computer Science GCSE Programming Project is particularly well-suited to Raspberry Pi implementation, as is AQA's A-Level Non-Examined Assessment.

### Professional Development Recommendations

Teachers new to Computing or physical computing should consider:

1. **Foundation Courses**: Begin with the Foundation's "Teaching Programming to 11-16 Year-Olds" or "Programming 101" courses before attempting physical computing projects
2. **CAS Community**: Engage with local CAS community meetings where Raspberry Pi projects and troubleshooting are regularly discussed
3. **Progression Pathway**: Follow the recommended progression from Scratch (visual) to Python (text-based) programming, which aligns with Phil Bagge's documented approach to programming pedagogy

### Technical Considerations

**Network Requirements**: Schools must consider network policies regarding Linux devices. Many schools create separate VLANs or use standalone networks for Raspberry Pi devices to satisfy security requirements whilst enabling full functionality.

**Software Management**: The Foundation provides an approved applications list and SD card images suitable for educational use. Regular updates are essential—many schools designate a Computing technician time specifically for maintaining Raspberry Pi devices.

**Physical Computing Safety**: When using GPIO pins and electronic components, standard DT workshop safety protocols apply. The Foundation's projects include risk assessments and safety guidance.

## Synergies with Other Organisations

### Partnership with CAS and BCS

The Raspberry Pi Foundation works closely with Computing at School (CAS) and BCS, The Chartered Institute for IT. Mark Dorling's work in establishing CAS created the grassroots community that the Foundation's resources serve, whilst BCS provides the professional body recognition for Computing teachers engaging with Foundation CPD.

### National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE)

Since 2018, the Foundation has been the lead delivery partner for the NCCE, a £84 million government-funded programme. This partnership has systematised access to high-quality Computing education resources and professional development across England, with the Teach Computing Curriculum now used by over 45,000 teachers.

### Exam Board Alignment

All major UK exam boards reference or recommend Raspberry Pi resources:

- **OCR**: Explicit endorsement for programming projects and physical computing coursework
- **AQA**: Recommended for teaching systems architecture and embedded systems
- **Edexcel**: Featured in teacher support materials for programming units
- **WJEC**: Included in Welsh-language adapted resources through partnership working

## International Influence and UK Leadership

Whilst the Foundation operates internationally (over 30 million Raspberry Pi computers sold worldwide), its UK focus remains paramount. The organisation exemplifies Simon Peyton Jones's vision that the UK could become a world leader in Computing education through the right combination of curriculum ambition, teacher development, and resource provision.

The Foundation's work has influenced Computing curricula internationally but maintains particular attention to UK requirements, including England's National Curriculum, Scotland's Curriculum for Excellence (Technologies), Northern Ireland's statutory requirements for ICT/Computing, and Wales's Digital Competence Framework.

## Future Developments and Ongoing Influence

The Raspberry Pi Foundation continues to evolve its educational provision:

**AI and Machine Learning**: Recent resources address curriculum developments in artificial intelligence, particularly relevant as exam boards incorporate this content into revised specifications (e.g., AQA's 2024 GCSE specification updates).

**Data Science**: New resources support the teaching of data science concepts, reflecting both labour market demand and curriculum evolution, particularly at Key Stage 5.

**Expanded Access**: The Foundation's commitment to ensuring every young person can access Computing education continues through partnerships with libraries, community organisations, and disadvantaged schools.

## Conclusion

The Raspberry Pi Foundation represents an exemplar of how a well-resourced, research-informed organisation can support systemic curriculum change. For UK Computing teachers, the Foundation provides an invaluable ecosystem of hardware, software, resources, professional development, and community support that makes the ambitious 2014 Computing curriculum achievable in practice. As Sue Sentance has observed, the Foundation's distinctive contribution lies in its integration of rigorous research, practical teacher support, and learner-facing resources—a combination that continues to define excellence in UK Computing education.
