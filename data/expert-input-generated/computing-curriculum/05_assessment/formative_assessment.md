# Formative Assessment in Computing

## Introduction

Formative assessment in Computing represents one of the most significant pedagogical challenges and opportunities in the subject. Unlike summative assessment, which measures learning outcomes at fixed points, formative assessment is the ongoing process of gathering evidence about student learning to inform immediate teaching decisions. The Computing curriculum's emphasis on computational thinking, practical programming skills, and theoretical understanding requires particularly nuanced approaches to formative assessment.

The Black and Wiliam (1998) seminal work "Inside the Black Box" established that formative assessment can raise standards significantly when implemented effectively. In Computing specifically, Sue Sentance and colleagues at King's College London have explored how formative assessment practices must adapt to the unique characteristics of the subject, including the immediate feedback provided by compilers and interpreters, the visibility of student thinking through code, and the iterative nature of programming tasks.

## Theoretical Foundations for Computing

### Assessment for Learning in Computing Contexts

Dylan Wiliam's five key formative assessment strategies apply distinctively in Computing:

1. **Clarifying learning intentions and success criteria**: In Computing, this means making explicit not just what programs should do (functional requirements) but how code should be structured (computational thinking concepts, coding conventions, efficiency considerations). Phil Bagge emphasises that success criteria in primary Computing must distinguish between "working code" and "well-constructed code".

2. **Engineering effective discussions and tasks**: Computing lessons provide unique opportunities through pair programming, code reviews, and debugging exercises. Miles Berry advocates for classroom discourse that makes computational thinking visible, encouraging students to articulate their algorithmic reasoning.

3. **Providing feedback that moves learning forward**: Sue Sentance's research (2017) at King's College London highlights that feedback in Computing must address multiple dimensions—algorithmic logic, syntax, program structure, efficiency, and style—whilst avoiding overwhelming learners.

4. **Activating students as learning resources**: Peer assessment in Computing is particularly powerful through code review practices and pair programming, where students must explain their reasoning to others.

5. **Activating students as owners of their learning**: Self-assessment in Computing develops metacognitive awareness about problem-solving strategies and debugging approaches.

## Key Stage-Specific Applications

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to "understand what algorithms are; how they are implemented as programs on digital devices; and that programs execute by following precise and unambiguous instructions" and to "create and debug simple programs".

**Formative Assessment Strategies:**

- **Observational assessment during unplugged activities**: Teachers observe students creating algorithms with physical resources (e.g., giving instructions to programmable toys like Bee-Bots). Mark Dorling emphasises the importance of assessing whether children can create, follow, and debug instructions systematically.

- **Questioning techniques**: Using Socratic questioning to probe understanding: "What will happen next?", "How could we fix this?", "What instruction did we miss?"

- **Success criteria for algorithms**: Phil Bagge recommends simple visual success criteria showing whether algorithms are: precise (no ambiguous steps), sequenced correctly, and achieve the intended outcome.

- **Talk partners**: Pairing students to explain their thinking makes computational thinking audible and assessable.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to "design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals" and "use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs; work with variables and various forms of input and output".

**Formative Assessment Strategies:**

- **Live coding with strategic errors**: Teachers deliberately introduce bugs for students to identify, making debugging strategies explicit. This "productive failure" approach helps assess students' understanding of program flow.

- **Concept mapping**: Students create visual representations of how programming concepts (variables, loops, conditionals) relate to each other, revealing misconceptions.

- **Diagnostic questions**: CAS (Computing At School) provides diagnostic questions for concepts like variables, selection, and iteration. These multiple-choice questions with targeted distractors reveal specific misconceptions.

- **Code tracing exercises**: Students predict program output before execution, revealing understanding of sequential execution, variable state, and control flow. Miles Berry advocates for prediction-explanation-observation cycles.

- **Programming journals**: Students maintain reflective logs documenting their problem-solving process, debugging strategies, and learning insights.

- **Worked examples with self-explanation**: Following Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory, students study partially completed programs and explain each line's purpose, reducing cognitive load whilst revealing understanding.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to "use 2 or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual" and to "understand simple Boolean logic and some of its uses in circuits and programming".

**Formative Assessment Strategies:**

- **Code comprehension assessments**: Students read unfamiliar code and explain its purpose, identifying algorithms and evaluating efficiency. This assesses reading skills separately from writing skills.

- **Parson's Problems**: Students arrange shuffled lines of code into correct order, reducing cognitive load whilst assessing understanding of program structure and logic. Research by Parsons and Haden (2006) and subsequent studies by Ericson et al. show these effectively diagnose understanding.

- **PRIMM approach**: Sue Sentance and colleagues developed Predict-Run-Investigate-Modify-Make as a pedagogical framework that embeds formative assessment throughout. Each stage provides assessment opportunities:
  - **Predict**: Students predict code behaviour (assessing reading comprehension)
  - **Run**: Students test predictions (revealing misconceptions)
  - **Investigate**: Students answer targeted questions about code (assessing conceptual understanding)
  - **Modify**: Students make specified changes (assessing application)
  - **Make**: Students create original programs (assessing synthesis)

- **Hinge-point questions**: Strategic multiple-choice questions at lesson transition points, with response options designed to reveal specific misconceptions about concepts like Boolean logic, variable scope, or iteration.

- **Debugging challenges**: Presenting programs with deliberate errors across syntax, logic, and runtime categories. Students must identify, classify, and correct errors, revealing their debugging sophistication.

- **Peer code review**: Structured protocols where students evaluate each other's code against clear criteria (functionality, readability, efficiency, documentation). This develops evaluative skills whilst providing formative feedback.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE specifications from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas require assessment of programming skills, computational thinking, and theoretical understanding.

**Formative Assessment Strategies:**

- **Specification-aligned success criteria**: Break down programming project assessment objectives into granular, assessable components. For example, OCR's J277 programming project assesses decomposition, design, implementation, and evaluation separately.

- **Mock practical programming tasks**: Regular practice of exam-style programming challenges (20-hour programming projects for OCR; controlled assessment elements for other boards) with formative feedback before summative submission.

- **Trace tables for algorithm analysis**: Students complete trace tables for algorithms involving arrays, searching, and sorting. This reveals understanding of step-by-step execution and data structure manipulation.

- **Theoretical knowledge checks**: Regular low-stakes quizzes on topics like network topologies, storage types, or ethical considerations. Research by Roediger and Karpicke (2006) on retrieval practice shows regular testing enhances long-term retention.

- **Explain-the-code interviews**: One-to-one or small group discussions where students explain their code decisions, revealing depth of understanding beyond what written code alone demonstrates.

- **Progressive problem sets**: Sequences of related problems increasing in complexity (e.g., simple calculation → validation → file handling → error handling). Performance patterns reveal exactly where understanding breaks down.

## Assessment Dimensions Unique to Computing

### Assessing Computational Thinking

Simon Peyton Jones, in his influential work with CAS and the Royal Society's "Shut Down or Restart?" report (2012), emphasised that Computing is fundamentally about computational thinking, not just coding syntax. Formative assessment must therefore address:

**Abstraction**: Can students identify relevant details and ignore irrelevant ones? Can they create appropriate models? Assessment through: evaluating student-designed data structures; reviewing their choice of what to represent in simulations; examining their function/procedure design showing appropriate parameterisation.

**Decomposition**: Can students break problems into manageable parts? Assessment through: reviewing their program structure (use of functions/procedures); examining their approach to planning complex tasks; observing their problem-solving process when facing novel challenges.

**Pattern recognition**: Can students identify similarities between problems? Assessment through: asking students to apply known solutions to new contexts; examining their use of code reuse and abstraction; discussing their approach to unfamiliar problems.

**Algorithm design**: Can students create step-by-step solutions? Assessment through: pseudocode or flowchart creation before coding; comparing different algorithmic approaches to the same problem; analysing efficiency considerations.

**Evaluation**: Can students judge solution quality? Assessment through: code review activities; analysis of trade-offs (e.g., readability vs. efficiency); testing strategy comprehensiveness.

### Assessing Programming Skills

The immediate feedback provided by interpreters and compilers creates distinctive assessment opportunities and challenges:

**The Syntax-Semantics Distinction**: Students may write syntactically correct code that doesn't solve the problem, or have correct algorithmic logic expressed incorrectly. Formative assessment must diagnose which type of difficulty students face. Tools like syntax-free block-based languages (Scratch, Blockly) can isolate semantic understanding from syntactic knowledge.

**Testing and Debugging Competence**: Sue Sentance's research emphasises that debugging is a higher-order skill requiring hypothesis formation and systematic testing. Assessment opportunities include: observing debugging strategies (random changes vs. systematic hypothesis testing); examining test case comprehensiveness; evaluating error message interpretation skills.

**Code Quality Dimensions**: Beyond functionality, assess: readability (naming conventions, comments, structure); efficiency (algorithm choice, resource usage); maintainability (modularity, documentation); robustness (error handling, input validation).

### Common Misconceptions and Diagnostic Assessment

Research by the Cambridge RCUK projects and Sue Sentance's team has identified recurring misconceptions:

**Variable misconceptions**: Students may believe variables must be declared in execution order, that variable names carry meaning to the computer, or that assignment works bidirectionally. Diagnostic assessment: code prediction tasks involving variable swaps; questions about variable scope; tracing programs with multiple assignments to the same variable.

**Loop misconceptions**: Confusion about when loop conditions are checked (before/after body; each iteration vs. once). Diagnostic assessment: predicting iteration counts; identifying infinite loops; explaining loop termination conditions.

**Boolean logic misconceptions**: Difficulties with operator precedence, De Morgan's laws, and complex conditions. Diagnostic assessment: truth table completion; simplifying Boolean expressions; predicting conditional outcomes.

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Live Coding and Modeling

Phil Bagge advocates for teachers writing code live with students, deliberately making mistakes and thinking aloud about debugging. This makes expert thinking visible and creates natural formative assessment opportunities through:

- Pausing to ask "What should happen next?"
- Deliberately introducing errors and asking students to spot them
- Asking students to predict output before running code
- Discussing alternative approaches

### Differentiated Questioning Frameworks

Bloom's Taxonomy applied to Computing provides a questioning hierarchy:

- **Remembering**: "What does the print function do?"
- **Understanding**: "Explain what this loop will do"
- **Applying**: "Use a loop to solve this problem"
- **Analysing**: "Compare these two sorting algorithms"
- **Evaluating**: "Which approach is more efficient and why?"
- **Creating**: "Design a solution to this novel problem"

Teachers should plan questions across levels, using lower-level questions diagnostically and higher-level questions to extend thinking.

### Technology-Enhanced Formative Assessment

**Automated code analysis tools**: Tools like Replit, Pythonanywhere, or IDE built-in analysis provide immediate feedback on syntax errors, style issues, and potential bugs. However, Mark Dorling cautions that over-reliance on automated feedback can prevent students developing independent debugging skills.

**Digital portfolios**: Platforms like Google Classroom or Microsoft Teams enable students to maintain programming portfolios with teacher feedback over time, showing progression.

**Learning management systems**: Platforms like Kahoot, Quizizz, or Plickers enable quick formative checks of theoretical understanding with immediate results informing lesson pace.

**Pair programming analytics**: When managed through platforms like Replit's multiplayer features, teachers can observe collaboration patterns and intervene formatively.

## Feedback Practices in Computing

### Effective Feedback Characteristics

Drawing on Hattie and Timperley's (2007) feedback model, Computing feedback should address:

**Task level** ("Is this correct?"): Does the program work? Are test cases passed? This is the level at which automated testing operates.

**Process level** ("How to improve?"): Code structure, algorithm choice, debugging strategy. This requires teacher or peer expertise and is where most learning occurs.

**Self-regulation level** ("Self-monitoring skills"): Developing independent debugging, testing strategies, and problem-solving approaches.

**Self level** ("Personal praise"): Used sparingly and focused on effort and strategy rather than ability.

### Practical Feedback Strategies

**Two stars and a wish**: Students receive two specific strengths and one specific improvement target. In Computing: "Your function decomposition is excellent, and your variable names are clear. Next, consider adding error handling for invalid inputs."

**Code comment feedback**: Teachers or peers insert comments directly into student code at the point of improvement, making feedback contextual and specific.

**Feedback codes**: Developing a shared vocabulary (e.g., "Logic error - line 23", "Consider modularity", "Test edge cases") enables efficient, consistent feedback.

**Whole-class feedback**: After reviewing all submissions, identify common issues and provide collective feedback, reducing marking time whilst addressing misconceptions systematically.

**Video feedback**: Recording screen captures explaining code issues can be more efficient than written feedback for complex programming problems.

## Addressing Challenges

### The Copying Problem

Computing's practical nature creates opportunities for copying code. Formative assessment strategies to address this:

- Regular code explanation requirements (students must explain their code's operation)
- Varied problem sets (students solve different problems, preventing direct copying)
- Process portfolios (documenting development stages, not just final products)
- Paired programming with rotating partners (collaboration without individual dependency)

### Assessment Without De-motivating

Programming's immediate failure feedback (error messages, incorrect output) can discourage learners. Miles Berry emphasises celebrating debugging as learning:

- Reframe errors as "learning opportunities" not "failures"
- Teach explicit debugging strategies rather than expecting trial and error
- Recognise progress in problem-solving process, not just correct solutions
- Use "productive failure" deliberately - challenging problems where struggle is expected and valued

### Time Constraints

Computing teachers face significant pressure balancing practical work with assessment demands. Efficiency strategies:

- Focus feedback on key learning points, not every error
- Use peer assessment with clear success criteria for routine tasks
- Leverage automated testing for functional requirements
- Use code reviews as both learning activities and assessment opportunities
- Plan formative assessment into lesson activities rather than as additions

## Links to Summative Assessment

Effective formative assessment directly supports GCSE and A-Level success:

**GCSE programming projects** (OCR J277, AQA 8525, etc.) benefit from ongoing formative assessment addressing decomposition, design quality, and robust implementation before final submission.

**On-screen programming exams** (increasingly common across boards) require fluency developed through regular formative practice with code prediction, completion, and debugging tasks.

**Theoretical examinations** require conceptual understanding built through regular low-stakes knowledge checks and misconception-addressing feedback.

## Professional Development Implications

Teachers developing formative assessment expertise in Computing should:

- Study common misconceptions in Computing education research (resources from CAS, King's College London research team)
- Practice live coding with deliberate mistakes
- Develop banks of diagnostic questions and hinge-point questions
- Collaborate with departmental colleagues to share effective strategies
- Engage with Computing education research through CAS conferences and publications
- Trial and refine feedback approaches, evaluating efficiency and impact

## Conclusion

Formative assessment in Computing requires distinctive approaches reflecting the subject's unique characteristics: the visibility of thinking through code, immediate feedback from machines, the importance of process alongside product, and the multi-dimensional nature of programming competence. When implemented effectively, drawing on research from Sue Sentance, Miles Berry, Phil Bagge, and the broader assessment for learning literature, formative assessment transforms Computing education from teaching syntax to developing computational thinking. The practical strategies outlined—from PRIMM to Parson's Problems, from diagnostic questions to code reviews—provide teachers with evidence-based approaches to make student learning visible and to adjust teaching responsively, ultimately supporting both engagement and achievement in this foundational subject.
