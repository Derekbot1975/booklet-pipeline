# Computational Thinking

## Introduction and Definition

Computational thinking (CT) represents a fundamental approach to problem-solving that underpins the entire Computing curriculum in the UK. The term gained prominence in education following Jeannette Wing's seminal 2006 paper, though its educational application in the UK context has been significantly shaped by Mark Dorling, Miles Berry, and Simon Peyton Jones through their work with Computing at School (CAS) and the Royal Society.

The Royal Society's 2012 report "Shut down or restart?" defined computational thinking as "the process of recognising aspects of computation in the world that surrounds us, and applying tools and techniques from Computer Science to understand and reason about both natural and artificial systems and processes." This definition emphasises CT as both a cognitive skill and a practical methodology.

Jeannette Wing's original conceptualisation described computational thinking as a fundamental skill for everyone, not just computer scientists, stating it "involves solving problems, designing systems, and understanding human behaviour, by drawing on the concepts fundamental to computer science." In the UK education context, this has been refined to focus on four core components: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, and algorithmic thinking.

## The Four Pillars of Computational Thinking

### Decomposition

Decomposition involves breaking down complex problems or systems into smaller, more manageable parts. Mark Dorling, through his work with CAS, emphasises that decomposition is often the first step in computational thinking, enabling learners to approach seemingly insurmountable problems systematically.

In practical classroom terms, Sue Sentance's research at King's College London has demonstrated that effective decomposition requires explicit teaching. Teachers should model the process of identifying sub-problems and demonstrate how solving these components contributes to the overall solution. For example, when creating a game in Scratch, pupils might decompose the task into sprite creation, movement mechanics, scoring system, and collision detection.

The National Curriculum for Computing states that pupils should be taught to "use logical reasoning to explain how some simple algorithms work and to detect and correct errors in algorithms and programs" (KS2). Decomposition is implicit in this requirement, as debugging necessarily involves breaking down code into constituent parts.

### Pattern Recognition

Pattern recognition involves identifying similarities, trends, or regularities within problems or across different problems. Phil Bagge, a prominent CAS Master Teacher and author, argues that pattern recognition is crucial for efficiency in programming and problem-solving, as it allows learners to apply known solutions to new contexts.

Miles Berry emphasises that pattern recognition connects directly to mathematical thinking and is particularly evident in understanding loops and functions in programming. When pupils recognise that certain operations need repeating, they are applying pattern recognition to identify where iteration is appropriate.

At KS3, the National Curriculum requires pupils to "use two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual, to solve a variety of computational problems." This inherently requires pattern recognition as pupils identify similarities between language structures and problem types.

### Abstraction

Abstraction is the process of removing unnecessary detail to focus on relevant information or features. Simon Peyton Jones, in his work with the Royal Society and Computing at School, identifies abstraction as perhaps the most sophisticated element of computational thinking, requiring learners to determine what information is essential and what can be ignored.

Sue Sentance's research highlights that abstraction is particularly challenging for novice programmers. She recommends using unplugged activities (computing concepts taught without computers) to develop abstraction skills before applying them in programming contexts. For example, creating a simplified model of a traffic light system requires pupils to abstract the essential features while ignoring colour specifications, bulb types, or electrical circuits.

Phil Bagge's classroom resources demonstrate abstraction through activities like creating simplified representations of familiar objects or processes, helping pupils understand how models retain essential features whilst omitting unnecessary complexity.

### Algorithmic Thinking

Algorithmic thinking involves creating step-by-step solutions to problems—essentially designing algorithms. Mark Dorling's computational thinking framework emphasises that algorithms must be precise, unambiguous, and executable, whether by humans or computers.

The National Curriculum explicitly addresses algorithms from KS1 onwards: pupils should "understand what algorithms are; how they are implemented as programs on digital devices; and that programs execute by following precise and unambiguous instructions." This progression continues through KS2 and KS3, with increasing complexity and rigour.

Miles Berry points out that algorithmic thinking encompasses both algorithm design and algorithm analysis—not just creating solutions but evaluating their efficiency, correctness, and elegance. This connects to the GCSE requirement across all exam boards (AQA, OCR, Edexcel, WJEC) to understand, analyse, and evaluate algorithms.

## Computational Thinking in the UK National Curriculum

### Key Stage 1

At KS1, computational thinking is introduced through practical, concrete activities. The Programme of Study states pupils should "understand what algorithms are; how they are implemented as programs on digital devices; and that programs execute by following precise and unambiguous instructions."

Decomposition might involve breaking down the steps needed to make a sandwich or tidy a classroom. Pattern recognition emerges through identifying repeated actions in stories or daily routines. Abstraction appears when pupils create simple pictorial representations. Algorithmic thinking develops through creating and following simple instructions.

### Key Stage 2

KS2 sees computational thinking become more explicit and sophisticated. Pupils should "design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals" and "use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs." These requirements demand all four CT components.

Sue Sentance's ScratchMaths project demonstrates how computational thinking can be explicitly taught through mathematical contexts at KS2, with pupils using Scratch to explore mathematical concepts whilst simultaneously developing CT skills. Decomposition becomes more complex, perhaps involving breaking down a multi-level game. Pattern recognition extends to identifying when to use variables or procedures. Abstraction involves creating reusable code blocks. Algorithmic thinking includes understanding sequence, selection, and iteration.

### Key Stage 3

At KS3, the National Curriculum requires greater sophistication: pupils should "understand several key algorithms that reflect computational thinking" and "use two or more programming languages." The curriculum explicitly mentions "sorting and searching" algorithms, requiring pupils to analyse and compare algorithmic approaches.

Mark Dorling's work emphasises that KS3 is crucial for developing metacognitive awareness of computational thinking—pupils should recognise when and why they're using each component. Teachers should explicitly label CT concepts during lessons, helping pupils develop a vocabulary for their problem-solving approaches.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

All major exam boards incorporate computational thinking throughout their GCSE specifications, though terminology varies slightly.

**AQA Computer Science (8525)** includes "Computational thinking, algorithms and programming" as a major component, requiring pupils to "understand and apply the fundamental principles and concepts of Computer Science, including abstraction, decomposition, logic, algorithms, and data representation."

**OCR Computer Science (J277)** explicitly assesses "Computational thinking, algorithms and programming," requiring candidates to "apply computational thinking" to various problem contexts.

**Edexcel GCSE Computer Science (1CP2)** integrates computational thinking throughout its "Computational thinking and problem solving" component.

**WJEC GCSE Computer Science** similarly embeds computational thinking across units, particularly in algorithm design and problem-solving contexts.

All specifications require pupils to apply computational thinking to solve problems, design algorithms, and trace/analyse code—demonstrating that CT has moved from an abstract concept to an assessable skill.

## Research Evidence and Pedagogical Approaches

### Explicit vs. Implicit Teaching

Sue Sentance and Andrew Csizmadia's research at King's College London suggests that computational thinking requires explicit teaching rather than expecting pupils to develop it implicitly through programming. Their "Teaching Computational Thinking" research found that teachers need professional development to recognise CT opportunities and make them explicit in lessons.

Phil Bagge advocates for a balanced approach: initially teaching CT concepts explicitly through unplugged activities, then reinforcing them through programming tasks, and finally encouraging pupils to apply CT independently to novel problems. His "Code-IT" series demonstrates this progression through carefully scaffolded activities.

### Unplugged Computing

Miles Berry, amongst others, champions unplugged computing activities as effective vehicles for developing computational thinking without the cognitive load of syntax and debugging. Activities like CS Unplugged (adapted for UK contexts) allow pupils to focus purely on problem-solving approaches.

Mark Dorling's "Developing Computational Thinking" resource includes numerous unplugged activities: sorting algorithms with playing cards, binary numbers with hands, or network routing with string and pupils. These activities make abstract concepts tangible and accessible.

### Assessment of Computational Thinking

Assessing computational thinking remains challenging. Sue Sentance's work on assessment suggests that CT should be evaluated through problem-solving tasks rather than knowledge recall. However, GCSE examinations necessarily include both—pupils must demonstrate knowledge of algorithms and data structures (assessable through written exams) and apply CT to solve problems (assessable through programming projects and exam questions).

The Bebras Computational Thinking Challenge, promoted by Miles Berry and CAS, offers an alternative assessment approach. These short, puzzle-like problems assess CT without requiring programming knowledge, providing formative assessment opportunities for teachers.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning and Progression

Teachers should ensure computational thinking is explicitly planned into schemes of work rather than assumed to develop naturally. Mark Dorling's progression frameworks suggest starting with concrete, physical examples at KS1, moving to visual programming contexts at KS2, and progressing to abstract problem-solving at KS3/4.

Each lesson should identify which CT components are being developed. For example, a lesson on creating a quiz program might explicitly address:
- **Decomposition**: Breaking the quiz into questions, answers, scoring, and feedback
- **Pattern recognition**: Identifying repeated question structures
- **Abstraction**: Creating a general question format
- **Algorithmic thinking**: Designing the question-answer-check-feedback sequence

### Language and Vocabulary

Phil Bagge emphasises the importance of consistent vocabulary. Teachers should explicitly use terms like "decomposition," "abstraction," "pattern recognition," and "algorithm," building pupils' metacognitive awareness. Wall displays showing the CT framework help pupils recognise when they're applying these skills.

Sue Sentance's research indicates that pupils benefit from sentence starters and thinking frames: "I can decompose this problem by...", "I notice a pattern in...", "The essential features are..." These scaffolds help pupils articulate their computational thinking processes.

### Cross-Curricular Applications

Simon Peyton Jones argues that computational thinking has value beyond Computing lessons. Teachers can identify CT opportunities across the curriculum:
- **Mathematics**: Using algorithms for calculations, recognising patterns in sequences
- **Science**: Creating models (abstraction), designing experimental procedures (algorithms)
- **Design & Technology**: Decomposing design problems, recognising design patterns
- **Geography**: Abstracting map features, recognising geographical patterns

Miles Berry advocates for collaboration between Computing and other subject teachers to reinforce CT as a transferable skill rather than domain-specific knowledge.

### Differentiation and Challenge

Computational thinking allows natural differentiation. Less confident pupils might decompose problems with greater scaffolding or work with more concrete examples. More able pupils can tackle problems requiring sophisticated abstraction or optimise algorithms for efficiency—a concept Miles Berry calls "algorithmic elegance."

Phil Bagge suggests "low floor, high ceiling" tasks: problems accessible to all but with extension opportunities. For example, all pupils might create a sorting algorithm, but extension work could involve comparing bubble sort, insertion sort, and merge sort efficiency.

### Common Misconceptions

Research by Sue Sentance and colleagues identifies common misconceptions:

1. **Computational thinking equals programming**: CT is broader—it's problem-solving applicable beyond computing
2. **Algorithms must be code**: Algorithms can be expressed in pseudocode, flowcharts, or natural language
3. **There's one correct decomposition**: Problems can be decomposed in multiple valid ways
4. **Abstraction means simplification**: Abstraction is selective representation, retaining essential features

Teachers must address these explicitly, perhaps by demonstrating multiple valid approaches to the same problem or showing how CT applies in non-programming contexts.

## Computational Thinking and Inclusion

Mark Dorling emphasises that computational thinking can make Computing more inclusive. Unlike programming, which can have barriers (typing speed, syntax recall), CT can be demonstrated through discussion, diagrams, or physical activities. This allows pupils with various needs to access the curriculum.

Sue Sentance's research on gender and computing suggests that framing Computing around problem-solving and real-world applications (core to CT) may increase engagement amongst groups traditionally underrepresented in computing. Rather than focusing on technical programming syntax, emphasising how CT solves meaningful problems can broaden appeal.

## Future Directions and Ongoing Debates

The Computing education community continues debating CT's role. Simon Peyton Jones argues for CT as foundational knowledge, comparable to literacy or numeracy. Others, including some practitioners, question whether CT's components are sufficiently distinct from general problem-solving skills.

Sue Sentance's ongoing research at the Raspberry Pi Foundation explores how CT develops across age ranges and how teachers can assess it reliably. Her work suggests that whilst CT provides a useful framework, teachers need substantial professional development to implement it effectively.

Miles Berry and CAS continue developing resources that embed CT explicitly, recognising that whilst the concept is now established in the National Curriculum, consistent, high-quality implementation across schools remains variable. The challenge lies not in defining computational thinking but in ensuring every pupil develops these essential skills through well-designed, expertly taught Computing education.

## Conclusion

Computational thinking represents the intellectual foundation of Computing as a curriculum subject. Through the advocacy of Mark Dorling, Simon Peyton Jones, Phil Bagge, Miles Berry, Sue Sentance, and others, it has moved from academic concept to practical classroom reality. The explicit inclusion of CT in the National Curriculum and GCSE specifications reflects recognition that these problem-solving approaches have value beyond computing, equipping pupils with transferable skills for an increasingly computational world.

For teachers, the challenge is making CT explicit, assessable, and progressive whilst maintaining the creative, engaging aspects of Computing that inspire pupils. When taught effectively, computational thinking transforms Computing from a technical skill into a fundamental way of thinking about and engaging with the world.
