# Computing at Key Stage 2

## Overview of Key Stage 2 Computing

Key Stage 2 (KS2) comprises Years 3–6, typically for pupils aged 7–11. The National Curriculum for Computing, established in 2014, transformed the previous ICT curriculum into a more rigorous programme focusing on computational thinking, programming, and digital literacy. As Simon Peyton Jones (Microsoft Research and co-founder of Computing At School) argued in his influential advocacy work, this shift was essential to ensure pupils become creators of technology rather than merely consumers.

The KS2 Computing curriculum builds substantially upon Key Stage 1 foundations, requiring pupils to develop more sophisticated algorithmic thinking, debugging skills, and understanding of computer systems. Miles Berry, former principal lecturer at University of Roehampton, emphasises in his writings that KS2 is where pupils transition from concrete, block-based programming to beginning to understand more abstract computational concepts.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Statutory Programme of Study

The National Curriculum for Computing at KS2 (DfE, 2013) specifies that pupils should be taught to:

**Design, write and debug programs:**
- Design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals, including controlling or simulating physical systems; solve problems by decomposing them into smaller parts
- Use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs; work with variables and various forms of input and output
- Use logical reasoning to explain how some simple algorithms work and to detect and correct errors in algorithms and programs

**Understand computer networks:**
- Understand computer networks including the internet; how they can provide multiple services, such as the world wide web; and the opportunities they offer for communication and collaboration

**Use search technologies:**
- Use search technologies effectively, appreciate how results are selected and ranked, and be discerning in evaluating digital content

**Select, use and combine software:**
- Select, use and combine a variety of software (including internet services) on a range of digital devices to design and create a range of programs, systems and content that accomplish given goals, including collecting, analysing, evaluating and presenting data and information

**Use technology safely and respectfully:**
- Use technology safely, respectfully and responsibly; recognise acceptable/unacceptable behaviour; identify a range of ways to report concerns about content and contact

### Three Strands of the Curriculum

Mark Dorling (CAS National Coordinator) and others have characterised the Computing curriculum as comprising three distinct but interconnected strands:

1. **Computer Science** – the foundational principles of information and computation
2. **Information Technology** – applying computational thinking to create digital artefacts
3. **Digital Literacy** – being able to use, express oneself and develop ideas through technology, and being safe, respectful and responsible online

At KS2, all three strands should be given appropriate curriculum time, with progression carefully planned across year groups.

## Computer Science at Key Stage 2

### Algorithmic Thinking and Programming

Phil Bagge, computing consultant and author, has developed extensive resources demonstrating how algorithmic thinking develops at KS2. His work emphasises that pupils should progress from simple sequences to incorporating:

- **Selection (conditional statements):** Using if/then/else structures to make programs respond to different conditions
- **Repetition (loops):** Understanding both count-controlled loops (repeat n times) and condition-controlled loops (repeat until)
- **Variables:** Understanding how data can be stored, retrieved and manipulated within programs

**Practical implications for teachers:**

Teachers should plan a progression that allows pupils to encounter these concepts multiple times across KS2 with increasing sophistication. For example:
- Year 3: Simple sequences with basic repetition (e.g., repeat 4 times to draw a square)
- Year 4: Introduction to selection and simple variables (e.g., score in a game)
- Year 5: Combining selection and repetition; multiple variables
- Year 6: Nested structures, more complex conditions, lists/arrays

### Programming Environments

Common programming environments for KS2 include:

**Scratch** (developed by MIT): The most widely adopted platform in UK primary schools. Scratch uses block-based programming and is appropriate for the full KS2 age range. Scratch 3.0, released in 2019, improved browser compatibility and tablet support.

**Logo and variants:** Turtle graphics environments remain valuable for teaching geometric programming and introducing variables and procedures.

**Physical computing platforms:** BBC micro:bit (distributed to all Year 7 pupils from 2016) is increasingly used in upper KS2. The Microsoft MakeCode block-based editor makes it accessible whilst the physical outputs enhance engagement and understanding.

**Python:** Some schools introduce text-based Python in Year 6 as a transition to KS3. While not essential, this can support pupils' understanding that blocks represent actual code.

Sue Sentance (King's College London and Raspberry Pi Foundation) has conducted extensive research on block-based versus text-based programming. Her research suggests that block-based programming reduces syntax barriers and allows pupils to focus on algorithmic thinking, making it highly appropriate for most KS2 pupils.

### Decomposition and Abstraction

Decomposition – breaking problems into smaller parts – is explicitly mentioned in the KS2 programme of study. Teachers should model this process regularly:

- Planning a project by identifying required features
- Creating separate sprites or procedures for different aspects of a program
- Testing components independently before integration

Abstraction, whilst not explicitly named at KS2, underpins effective computing education. Phil Bagge suggests that pupils develop abstraction skills through:
- Creating custom blocks/procedures that can be reused
- Understanding how similar patterns can be represented generically
- Recognising unnecessary detail when modelling problems

### Debugging and Logical Reasoning

The curriculum requires pupils to "detect and correct errors in algorithms and programs." Research by Sue Sentance and others identifies different categories of errors:

- **Syntax errors:** Reduced in block-based environments but still occur (e.g., missing inputs)
- **Logic errors:** The program runs but doesn't do what was intended
- **Runtime errors:** The program crashes or stops unexpectedly

Teachers should explicitly teach debugging strategies:
- Reading code carefully to predict what it should do
- Testing systematically with different inputs
- Using step-through or slow-motion execution
- Adding temporary outputs to check variable values
- Breaking problems into smaller parts to isolate issues

## Information Technology at Key Stage 2

### Creating Digital Content

KS2 pupils should progress from basic content creation to purposeful design that considers audience and purpose. This includes:

**Multimedia projects:** Combining text, images, sound and video. Teachers should emphasise planning, storyboarding, and iterative improvement rather than merely using software tools.

**Data handling:** Using spreadsheets or databases to collect, organise and analyse information. Pupils should understand basic functions, sorting, filtering and creating appropriate charts or graphs.

**Digital art and music:** Using software to create and manipulate graphics or compose music. This provides contexts for mathematical concepts (coordinates, transformation) and algorithmic thinking (procedural generation).

### Combining Applications and Services

The requirement to "select, use and combine" software encourages pupils to:
- Choose appropriate tools for specific purposes
- Transfer content between applications
- Integrate multiple services (e.g., embedding videos, linking documents)
- Understand file formats and compatibility

Miles Berry emphasises that this strand helps pupils develop technical fluency and understand how different technologies interoperate.

## Digital Literacy at Key Stage 2

### Online Safety

Online safety at KS2 builds upon KS1 foundations but must address pupils' increasing online independence. Key concepts include:

**SMART rules or similar frameworks:**
- Safe: Protecting personal information
- Meeting: Understanding dangers of meeting online contacts
- Accepting: Not opening files or clicking links from unknown sources
- Reliable: Evaluating whether information is trustworthy
- Tell: Reporting concerns to trusted adults

**Age-appropriate social media awareness:** Whilst most social platforms have 13+ age restrictions, teachers should acknowledge that pupils may encounter these services and discuss:
- Why age restrictions exist
- Privacy settings and personal information
- Permanence of online content
- Digital footprints

**Cyberbullying:** Understanding what constitutes bullying behaviour online and how to respond or report it.

**Practical implications:** Online safety should be integrated throughout computing lessons rather than taught as isolated topics. When pupils publish work online, this provides authentic contexts for discussing privacy, appropriate content, and copyright.

### Evaluating Digital Content

The curriculum specifies that pupils should "appreciate how results are selected and ranked" in search engines. This critical digital literacy includes:

- Understanding that search engines use algorithms that can be influenced
- Recognising that not all online information is accurate or unbiased
- Identifying sponsored content or advertisements
- Cross-referencing information across multiple sources
- Recognising persuasive techniques in digital media

Teachers might use activities such as:
- Comparing search results from different engines
- Evaluating websites for reliability indicators (authorship, date, domain, citations)
- Identifying fake news or misleading images
- Understanding how personalisation affects what content people see

### Responsible Use of Technology

Beyond safety, pupils should understand:
- **Copyright and intellectual property:** Respecting others' creative work, understanding Creative Commons and attribution
- **Environmental impact:** Understanding that digital technologies consume resources and energy
- **Accessibility:** How technology can support people with disabilities and the importance of inclusive design
- **Digital wellbeing:** Balancing screen time with other activities

## Understanding Computer Networks and the Internet

### Networks and the Internet

KS2 pupils should develop an age-appropriate understanding of how computer networks function:

**Local networks:** Understanding that computers, printers and other devices in school connect via a local area network (LAN), often using both wired and wireless connections.

**The Internet:** Understanding that the internet is a network of networks connecting computers globally. Miles Berry suggests using analogies such as the postal system to explain packet switching and routing.

**Services vs infrastructure:** Distinguishing between the internet (infrastructure) and services that run on it (World Wide Web, email, video streaming, online gaming).

**Physical vs virtual:** Understanding that the internet relies on physical infrastructure (cables, satellites, servers, routers) despite appearing intangible.

### The World Wide Web

Pupils should understand:
- The web is one service that runs on the internet
- Web pages are documents written in HTML
- Web browsers interpret HTML to display pages
- Hyperlinks connect pages together
- Web addresses (URLs) identify specific pages

Some teachers introduce basic HTML at upper KS2, allowing pupils to see the code underlying web pages. This reinforces understanding of how instructions (code) produce visible outputs and connects to the programming strand.

## Assessment at Key Stage 2

### Assessment Approaches

Unlike at KS3 and beyond, there are no formal external examinations at KS2 Computing. Schools must assess against the National Curriculum objectives using teacher assessment.

**Formative assessment strategies:**

- **Observation during practical work:** Observing pupils' problem-solving approaches, collaboration, and debugging strategies
- **Code review:** Examining pupils' programs for evidence of understanding (appropriate structures, meaningful variable names, annotations)
- **Questioning:** Using targeted questions to probe understanding of concepts
- **Peer assessment:** Pupils evaluating each other's work against success criteria develops metacognition

**Summative assessment:**

Many schools use commercially available assessment frameworks or develop their own. Computing At School (CAS) and the Raspberry Pi Foundation have published progression frameworks that map progression from KS1 through KS4.

Mark Dorling's work on progression pathways identifies that pupils typically move through stages:
1. **Use:** Operating technology with support
2. **Understand:** Explaining how and why technology works
3. **Apply:** Selecting and combining technologies purposefully
4. **Create:** Designing novel solutions independently

### Common Misconceptions and Difficulties

Research by Sue Sentance and others identifies typical misconceptions at KS2:

**Programming misconceptions:**
- Believing computers understand imprecise instructions
- Not understanding that programs execute sequentially (unless explicitly branched)
- Confusion between the program (code) and what it produces (output)
- Difficulty distinguishing variable names from their contents

**Network/Internet misconceptions:**
- Believing "the cloud" is somewhere abstract rather than physical servers
- Not understanding that all internet content comes from computers somewhere
- Confusion between browsers, search engines, and websites

Teachers should use diagnostic questions and activities to identify and address these misconceptions explicitly.

## Progression and Transition

### Progression Across Key Stage 2

Well-planned progression ensures pupils revisit concepts with increasing depth. Phil Bagge's "12 steps to success" framework and similar approaches suggest pupils should experience programming concepts multiple times:

- First encounter: Concrete, guided exploration
- Consolidation: Applying the concept in different contexts
- Mastery: Using the concept flexibly and combining it with others

### Transition to Key Stage 3

Upper KS2 should prepare pupils for KS3 computing by:
- Ensuring confident use of core programming constructs
- Developing independence in problem-solving and debugging
- Building technical vocabulary
- Experiencing collaborative project work

Some secondary schools report that KS2 provision varies significantly, creating challenges for KS3 planning. Primary–secondary liaison should include sharing information about:
- Programming languages/environments used
- Concepts covered and depth achieved
- Project examples demonstrating capability
- Assessment information

## Practical Considerations for Schools

### Resourcing

**Hardware requirements:**
- Sufficient computers/tablets for pupils to work individually or in pairs
- Interactive display for demonstrations
- Physical computing devices (micro:bit, robotics) for at least occasional use
- Reliable internet connectivity

**Software:**
Most suitable KS2 software is free or low-cost:
- Scratch (free, browser-based)
- Purple Mash, J2e, or similar (subscription services offering integrated tools)
- Office/productivity software
- Photo/video editing tools appropriate for age range

### Timetabling and Curriculum Time

The National Curriculum does not specify minimum time allocations, but CAS and others recommend at least one hour per week for discrete Computing teaching, plus integration across other subjects where appropriate.

Discrete lessons allow for:
- Systematic coverage of progression in programming concepts
- Time for extended project work
- Development of subject-specific vocabulary and ways of thinking

### Cross-Curricular Links

Computing naturally connects to other subjects:

**Mathematics:** Programming reinforces understanding of coordinates, angles, variables, logical reasoning, and data handling.

**Science:** Programming simulations or controlling physical computing devices links to scientific enquiry. Understanding algorithms relates to scientific methods.

**Design and Technology:** Computational thinking and programming are increasingly part of D&T, particularly in control systems.

**Literacy:** Creating multimedia presentations, digital stories, or programmed narratives develops communication skills.

Miles Berry advocates for these connections whilst cautioning that computing's distinct knowledge and skills must not be diluted through excessive integration at the expense of subject-specific teaching.

### Professional Development

The shift from ICT to Computing required substantial teacher professional development. Ongoing support remains essential as the subject evolves. Resources include:

**Computing At School (CAS):** The grassroots organisation (now part of BCS, The Chartered Institute for IT) provides regional hubs, online community, and resources.

**Barefoot Computing:** Funded by BT, providing free training and resources specifically for primary teachers.

**Raspberry Pi Foundation:** Offers free online courses through their educational platform, including "Teaching Programming to Primary Pupils" and "Teaching Physical Computing."

**NCCE (National Centre for Computing Education):** Established in 2018, providing funded training, resources, and support for schools across England.

Simon Peyton Jones and others involved in the curriculum reform emphasised that effective implementation required substantial investment in teacher professional development, as many primary teachers had limited computing background.

## Conclusion

Computing at Key Stage 2 provides essential foundations for computational thinking, digital creativity, and safe, responsible technology use. Successful implementation requires:

- Clear understanding of progression in computational concepts
- Regular curriculum time for discrete computing teaching
- Access to appropriate hardware and software
- Teachers confident in subject knowledge and pedagogy
- Connections to other curriculum areas without losing subject integrity
- Ongoing assessment and adaptation to pupils' developing understanding

When implemented effectively, KS2 Computing enables pupils to become thoughtful, capable, and creative users and creators of technology, prepared for increasingly digital futures in education, employment, and citizenship.
