# Computing at Key Stage 1

## Overview and National Curriculum Requirements

Computing at Key Stage 1 (ages 5-7, Years 1 and 2) represents pupils' first formal encounter with the Computing curriculum introduced in England in September 2014. The National Curriculum for Computing at this key stage establishes foundational concepts that underpin all subsequent learning in Computer Science, Information Technology, and Digital Literacy.

The statutory requirements, as set out in the Department for Education's *National Curriculum in England: Computing programmes of study* (2013), state that pupils should be taught to:

- Understand what algorithms are; how they are implemented as programs on digital devices; and that programs execute by following precise and unambiguous instructions
- Create and debug simple programs
- Use logical reasoning to predict the behaviour of simple programs
- Use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content
- Recognise common uses of information technology beyond school
- Use technology safely and respectfully, keeping personal information private; identify where to go for help and support when they have concerns about content or contact on the internet or other online technologies

## The Three Strands at Key Stage 1

### Computer Science

**Algorithms and Programming**

Mark Dorling's work with Computing At School (CAS) emphasises that Key Stage 1 is where pupils develop their 'computational thinking' foundation. At this stage, algorithms should be understood as precise sequences of instructions, initially introduced through unplugged activities before transitioning to on-screen programming.

Phil Bagge, author of *Primary Computing and ICT: Knowledge, Understanding and Practice* (2019), advocates for beginning with tangible, physical representations of algorithms. Effective approaches include:

- **Unplugged activities**: Following recipes, treasure map directions, or programmable toy instructions (Bee-Bots, Roamer)
- **Decomposition tasks**: Breaking down everyday activities into step-by-step instructions (making a sandwich, brushing teeth)
- **Pattern recognition**: Identifying repeated sequences in stories, songs, or daily routines

Programming at Key Stage 1 typically involves block-based or icon-based environments. Miles Berry, in his contributions to the CAS Primary Curriculum (2014), identifies appropriate tools including:

- **ScratchJr**: Simplified block programming for ages 5-7
- **Bee-Bots and Blue-Bots**: Floor robots providing immediate physical feedback
- **Scratch**: Introduced towards Year 2 for more capable pupils
- **Code.org**: Structured progression through visual programming concepts
- **Lightbot**: Puzzle-based introduction to procedures and loops

**Debugging and Logical Reasoning**

Sue Sentance's research at King's College London emphasises that debugging is not merely error-correction but a fundamental computational thinking skill. At Key Stage 1, debugging involves:

- Identifying when instructions don't produce expected outcomes
- Systematically testing and correcting sequences
- Developing resilience and problem-solving dispositions
- Predicting outcomes before running programs (logical reasoning)

Phil Bagge's 'Quick Start Computing' resources demonstrate how teachers can embed debugging naturally through 'deliberate mistakes' in algorithms, encouraging pupils to spot and correct errors.

### Information Technology

**Creating and Manipulating Digital Content**

The IT strand at Key Stage 1 focuses on purposeful use of technology. Pupils should experience a range of creative tools:

- **Word processing**: Basic text entry, formatting, and editing (introducing keyboard skills)
- **Digital art**: Paint programs, drawing apps, photography
- **Digital media**: Simple animation, audio recording, basic video capture
- **Data handling**: Pictograms, simple databases or branching databases

Miles Berry emphasises that IT at this stage should connect to other curriculum areas. Computing should not be taught in isolation but integrated meaningfully—for example, creating digital books in English, data visualisations in mathematics, or multimedia presentations in topic work.

**Organisation and Retrieval**

Key Stage 1 pupils should understand basic file management concepts:

- Saving and naming files appropriately
- Locating saved work
- Understanding folder structures (at a basic level)
- Recognising different file types through icons

### Digital Literacy

**Safe and Responsible Use**

The digital literacy strand at Key Stage 1 is crucial for establishing safe online behaviours early. The UK Council for Child Internet Safety (UKCCIS) framework *Education for a Connected World* (2020) provides detailed progression in online safety that aligns with Key Stage 1 expectations.

Core concepts include:

- **Privacy**: Understanding that some information should remain private (full names, addresses, passwords)
- **Kind and respectful behaviour**: Treating others well online
- **Recognising concerns**: Knowing when to seek adult help
- **Critical thinking**: Beginning to question whether online content is real or trustworthy

Simon Peyton Jones, who chaired the Royal Society's 2012 report *Shut down or restart?* that influenced the Computing curriculum change, emphasised that digital literacy must not be reduced to 'acceptable use policies' but should develop genuine critical engagement with technology.

Common Sense Media and Childnet resources provide age-appropriate materials, whilst the NSPCC's 'Net Aware' guidance supports teachers in understanding age ratings and risks for various platforms.

**Understanding Technology in Society**

Pupils should recognise technology beyond computers:

- Traffic lights, automatic doors, barcode scanners
- Technology at home (washing machines, microwaves, televisions)
- Technology in the community (libraries, shops, healthcare)

This develops awareness that computing principles are embedded throughout modern life, not confined to desktop computers.

## Pedagogical Approaches for Key Stage 1

### Unplugged Computing

Mark Dorling and others at CAS have championed 'unplugged' computing activities that teach computational concepts without devices. At Key Stage 1, this approach is particularly valuable because:

- It removes technical barriers for young learners still developing fine motor skills
- It makes abstract concepts concrete through physical manipulation
- It allows teachers less confident with technology to teach core concepts
- It develops computational thinking transferable across the curriculum

Effective unplugged activities include:

- **Binary bracelets**: Representing numbers in binary using two colours
- **Sorting algorithms**: Physical card sorting to understand algorithmic efficiency
- **Human robots**: Pupils giving each other precise instructions to complete tasks
- **Pixelation**: Creating pictures on grid paper to understand digital images

### Progression and Differentiation

Phil Bagge's work identifies clear progression within Key Stage 1:

**Year 1 focus areas:**
- Simple directional instructions (forward, backward, turn)
- Sequences of 3-5 instructions
- Recognising obvious errors
- Single-step digital content creation
- Basic online safety rules

**Year 2 focus areas:**
- More complex sequences with repetition (loops)
- Introducing prediction before execution
- Systematic debugging approaches
- Combining multiple digital tools
- Understanding why we keep information private

Differentiation should address:

- **Physical development**: Some pupils may struggle with mouse control or tablet precision
- **Vocabulary**: Computing introduces significant new terminology
- **Prior experience**: Digital divide means vastly different home technology exposure
- **Metacognition**: Making thinking visible through talk and demonstration

### Cross-Curricular Connections

The Computing curriculum at Key Stage 1 should not exist in isolation. Effective integration includes:

**Mathematics:**
- Directional language and coordinates (Bee-Bot activities)
- Data handling and pictograms (simple databases)
- Patterns and sequences (algorithmic thinking)
- Shape and position (programming sprites)

**English:**
- Digital storytelling and e-books
- Collaborative writing using word processors
- Podcasts and audio recording
- Sequencing narratives (algorithmic thinking)

**Science:**
- Recording observations using digital cameras
- Grouping and classifying (database thinking)
- Understanding technology in scientific instruments

**Design and Technology:**
- Designing and making digital content
- Understanding how everyday products incorporate computing

## Assessment at Key Stage 1

As Key Stage 1 is not subject to formal SATs in Computing, assessment is entirely teacher-led. The principle of assessment should be formative, supporting next steps rather than summative judgement.

**Observable Evidence of Computational Thinking:**

Miles Berry's work on assessment identifies key indicators:

- **Algorithms**: Can pupils create step-by-step instructions? Do they include necessary detail?
- **Debugging**: Do pupils test their work? Can they identify where errors occur?
- **Logical reasoning**: Can pupils predict outcomes? Do they explain their thinking?
- **Abstraction**: Can pupils identify the important features of a problem?
- **Generalisation**: Do pupils recognise similar patterns across contexts?

**Practical Assessment Strategies:**

- **Observation**: Teacher notes during programming activities
- **Pupil voice**: Explaining what they did and why
- **Digital portfolios**: Collections of created content showing progression
- **Peer assessment**: Pupils testing each other's algorithms
- **Self-assessment**: Simple success criteria and traffic light systems

Phil Bagge's assessment resources include 'I can' statements aligned to the National Curriculum, such as:
- "I can give instructions to a programmable toy"
- "I can find and correct mistakes in my program"
- "I can predict what my program will do"

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Technical Infrastructure

Many primary schools face challenges with:

- **Ageing hardware**: Tablets often more reliable than desktop computers for KS1
- **Network restrictions**: Overly restrictive filters blocking educational content
- **Ratio concerns**: Insufficient devices for class sizes

**Solutions:**
- Unplugged activities reduce device dependency
- Rotation stations allow sharing of limited resources
- Parent volunteers or older pupils as technical support
- Free online tools accessible on any device (Code.org, ScratchJr web version)

### Teacher Confidence

Sue Sentance's research identifies primary teacher confidence as a significant barrier. Many Key Stage 1 teachers qualified before the 2014 Computing curriculum.

**Professional Development Strategies:**
- CAS regional hubs providing free CPD
- Online courses (NCCE, Barefoot Computing, Code Club)
- Peer coaching within schools
- Subject-specific networks and Twitter communities (#CompEd, #PrimaryComputing)

The National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE), established in 2018, provides quality-assured training and resources specifically addressing primary teacher needs.

### Inclusion and SEND

Computing at Key Stage 1 can be particularly inclusive when appropriately differentiated:

- **Visual programming**: Supports pupils with literacy difficulties
- **Concrete resources**: Floor robots support kinaesthetic learners
- **Voice recording**: Alternatives to written communication
- **Assistive technologies**: Introduction at this stage can transform access

However, considerations include:
- Screen time limits for all pupils, especially those with attention difficulties
- Sensory sensitivities to screens or sounds
- Physical access to input devices
- Cognitive load of multi-step instructions

## Looking Forward: Progression Beyond Key Stage 1

The foundations established at Key Stage 1 directly enable Key Stage 2 learning. Pupils leaving Year 2 should have:

- Confidence with basic programming concepts (sequence, debugging, prediction)
- Experience creating various forms of digital content
- Understanding of basic online safety principles
- Positive attitudes towards problem-solving and resilience with technology
- Vocabulary to discuss computational concepts

Mark Dorling's progression frameworks show how Key Stage 1 concepts (simple sequences with Bee-Bots) evolve into Key Stage 2 work (procedures, variables, and selection in Scratch), which later support Key Stage 3 text-based programming.

## Conclusion

Computing at Key Stage 1 is not about producing proficient programmers but about developing computational thinking dispositions, digital creativity, and safe, responsible technology use. As Simon Peyton Jones argues, we are teaching "a new foundational subject, as important as mathematics or science," that equips young pupils with ways of thinking applicable throughout their education and lives.

Effective Key Stage 1 Computing balances playful exploration with structured skill development, embraces both plugged and unplugged approaches, integrates meaningfully across the curriculum, and establishes the conceptual foundations upon which all subsequent computing education builds. When taught well, it empowers even our youngest pupils as creators, not merely consumers, of technology.
