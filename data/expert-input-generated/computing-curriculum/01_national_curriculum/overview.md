# Computing National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction

The Computing National Curriculum in England represents a fundamental shift from the previous ICT curriculum, introduced in September 2014 following the Livingstone-Hope Next Gen Review and sustained advocacy from the computing education community. As Simon Peyton Jones of Microsoft Research and one of the key architects of the change famously stated, the reform moved from teaching children *about* computers to teaching them *to program* computers. The statutory programmes of study apply to maintained schools in England; academies must teach a 'broad and balanced curriculum' but are not legally bound to follow the National Curriculum, though most use it as a foundation.

## Statutory Framework and Legal Basis

The Computing programmes of study form part of the statutory National Curriculum for England, established under the Education Act 2002. Computing is a foundation subject at Key Stages 1-4, meaning all maintained schools must teach it. The Department for Education published the current programmes of study in September 2013 for implementation from September 2014.

The curriculum replaced the previous ICT National Curriculum and was influenced significantly by the Royal Society's 2012 report 'Shut down or restart? The way forward for computing in UK schools', chaired by Professor Steve Furber. This report, alongside advocacy from Computing At School (CAS) – a grassroots community founded by Simon Peyton Jones and Mark Dorling – provided the evidence base for change.

## Three Pillars of Computing Education

The National Curriculum is structured around three interconnected strands, a framework articulated clearly by Miles Berry, Principal Lecturer at the University of Roehampton:

### Computer Science

This strand encompasses the foundational principles of computation, including algorithms, programming, data structures, and computer architecture. As Mark Dorling and Sue Sentance argued in their seminal 2014 paper 'Computational Thinking and the National Curriculum', computer science provides the theoretical framework and problem-solving approaches that underpin all computing work.

The focus is on:
- Algorithmic thinking and logical reasoning
- Programming and software development
- Understanding how computers work at hardware and system levels
- Boolean logic and computational abstractions

### Information Technology

This strand addresses the application of computational tools to create, manipulate, and process digital content. It builds on computer science foundations but emphasises practical creation and problem-solving with technology.

Key elements include:
- Creating digital content across media types
- Using applications effectively and purposefully
- Understanding file management and data organisation
- Evaluating and improving digital solutions

### Digital Literacy

This encompasses the safe, responsible, and competent use of technology. Phil Bagge, author of 'A Primary Teacher's Guide to Computing', emphasises that digital literacy is not merely about safety but about critical evaluation and informed citizenship in a digital world.

Core components include:
- E-safety and online responsibility
- Evaluating digital content critically
- Understanding technology's role in society
- Responsible digital communication and collaboration

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The statutory requirements state that pupils should be taught to:

- Understand what algorithms are; how they are implemented as programs on digital devices; and that programs execute by following precise and unambiguous instructions
- Create and debug simple programs
- Use logical reasoning to predict the behaviour of simple programs
- Use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content
- Recognise common uses of information technology beyond school
- Use technology safely and respectfully, keeping personal information private; identify where to go for help and support when they have concerns about content or contact on the internet or other online technologies

**Practical implications**: Teachers typically introduce programming through tools like Bee-Bots, Scratch Jr, and block-based environments. Phil Bagge recommends concrete, unplugged activities to develop algorithmic thinking before moving to screen-based work. The emphasis is on play-based learning and exploration.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The programmes of study require that pupils should be taught to:

- Design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals, including controlling or simulating physical systems; solve problems by decomposing them into smaller parts
- Use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs; work with variables and various forms of input and output
- Use logical reasoning to explain how some simple algorithms work and to detect and correct errors in algorithms and programs
- Understand computer networks including the internet; how they can provide multiple services, such as the world wide web; and the opportunities they offer for communication and collaboration
- Use search technologies effectively, appreciate how results are selected and ranked, and be discerning in evaluating digital content
- Select, use and combine a variety of software (including internet services) on a range of digital devices to design and create a range of programs, systems and content that accomplish given goals
- Use technology safely, respectfully and responsibly; recognise acceptable/unacceptable behaviour; identify a range of ways to report concerns about content and contact

**Practical implications**: Miles Berry advocates for a 'little and often' approach, with regular, focused computing lessons rather than occasional extended projects. Scratch becomes the predominant programming environment, though Python is increasingly introduced in upper Key Stage 2. Sue Sentance's research through King's College London emphasises the importance of structured programming progression, moving from concrete to abstract thinking.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The statutory requirements specify that pupils should be taught to:

- Design, use and evaluate computational abstractions that model the state and behaviour of real-world problems and physical systems
- Understand several key algorithms that reflect computational thinking; use logical reasoning to compare the utility of alternative algorithms for the same problem
- Use two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual, to solve a variety of computational problems
- Understand simple Boolean logic and some of its uses in circuits and programming; understand how numbers can be represented in binary, and be able to carry out simple operations on binary numbers
- Understand the hardware and software components that make up computer systems, and how they communicate with one another and with other systems
- Understand how instructions are stored and executed within a computer system; understand how data of various types can be represented and manipulated digitally, in the form of binary digits
- Undertake creative projects that involve selecting, using, and combining multiple applications, preferably across a range of devices
- Create, re-use, revise and re-purpose digital artefacts for a given audience, with attention to trustworthiness, design and usability
- Understand a range of ways to use technology safely, respectfully, responsibly and securely, including protecting their online identity and privacy; recognise inappropriate content, contact and conduct and know how to report concerns

**Practical implications**: Mark Dorling emphasises that Key Stage 3 should develop deeper computational thinking skills. Most schools use Python or similar text-based languages, though JavaScript and Java are also common. The curriculum explicitly requires teaching of at least two languages, encouraging computational thinking transfer. Network infrastructure, binary representation, and Boolean logic typically receive dedicated teaching units.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At Key Stage 4, the National Curriculum states: 'All pupils must have the opportunity to study aspects of information technology and computer science at sufficient depth to allow them to progress to higher levels of study or to a professional career.'

Whilst detailed programme of study requirements end at Key Stage 3, schools must provide access to qualifications. Most students follow GCSE Computer Science, which is offered by:

**AQA (8525)**: Focuses on computational thinking, programming (Python preferred), and theoretical computer science including algorithms, data representation, networks, and systems architecture.

**OCR (J277)**: Emphasises practical programming skills alongside computer science theory, with a focus on problem-solving and computational thinking.

**Edexcel/Pearson (1CP2)**: Balances theory and programming, with mandatory on-screen programming examination.

**WJEC/Eduqas**: Used primarily in Wales, with similar content but adapted Welsh context where applicable.

Sue Sentance's research at King's College London, particularly through the Teaching London Computing project, has shown that many teachers find the transition to GCSE challenging due to the depth of computer science knowledge required, particularly in areas like computational thinking and abstract algorithm analysis.

## Subject Content Deep Dive

### Computational Thinking

Computational thinking is central to the National Curriculum, though the term appears only once explicitly in the Key Stage 3 programme of study. Mark Dorling and Sue Sentance's 2014 work identified computational thinking as comprising:

- **Algorithmic thinking**: Expressing solutions as step-by-step procedures
- **Decomposition**: Breaking problems into manageable parts
- **Abstraction**: Identifying and extracting relevant information
- **Pattern recognition**: Identifying similarities and patterns
- **Evaluation**: Assessing efficiency and fitness for purpose

These concepts thread through all key stages, becoming more sophisticated as pupils progress.

### Programming and Algorithms

The progression in programming is central to the curriculum's architecture:

- **KS1**: Simple sequences, debugging, predicting outcomes
- **KS2**: Sequence, selection, repetition, variables, input/output
- **KS3**: Multiple languages, algorithms, efficiency, abstraction
- **KS4**: Complex problem-solving, data structures, sophisticated algorithms

Phil Bagge recommends introducing programming through purposeful projects that solve genuine problems, rather than isolated skill exercises. The 'Use-Modify-Create' framework, popularised by the Scratch community, provides effective scaffolding.

### Data Representation

Binary representation and data encoding receive explicit attention from Key Stage 3 onwards. Teachers must address:

- Binary number systems and conversions
- Hexadecimal representation
- Character encoding (ASCII, Unicode)
- Image representation (pixels, resolution, colour depth)
- Sound representation (sampling, bit depth)
- Compression techniques

### Networks and the Internet

Network understanding progresses from recognising basic internet services at Key Stage 2 to understanding protocols, packet switching, and network topologies at Key Stage 3 and GCSE level. The curriculum requires understanding of the internet as a network of networks, though the programmes of study remain deliberately non-specific about technical depth.

## Assessment Approaches

The National Curriculum does not prescribe assessment methods for Computing. Schools develop their own assessment frameworks, though many reference the CAS/Barefoot Computing progression framework or commercial schemes like Teach Computing (formerly Barefoot and Project Quantum).

Miles Berry advocates for assessment that recognises both product (what students create) and process (how they think computationally). Sue Sentance's research suggests that assessment should focus on:

- Algorithmic design and logical reasoning
- Practical programming skills
- Understanding of concepts
- Problem-solving approaches
- Debugging and error correction

At GCSE, assessment is standardised by examination boards with typically 40% practical programming assessment and 60% written examination.

## Implementation Challenges and Support

### Teacher Knowledge and Confidence

The Royal Society's 2017 report 'After the Reboot' identified that only 35% of current computing teachers hold a relevant post-A level qualification. The National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE), established in 2018 and delivered by a consortium including the Raspberry Pi Foundation, provides extensive CPD including:

- Certificate in Teaching Computer Science (Primary)
- Certificate in Teaching Computer Science (Secondary)
- A level Computer Science subject knowledge course
- Specialist courses in networks, programming, data representation

### Curriculum Resources

The NCCE's Teach Computing Curriculum provides free, comprehensive schemes of work for all key stages, developed by educational experts including contributors from CAS and academic researchers. These materials follow the 'Primm' approach (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) developed by Sue Sentance and colleagues.

### Progression and Continuity

Mark Dorling's work on progression pathways emphasises that computing education must build systematically. The CAS progression pathways document (2018) provides detailed progression across all three strands from ages 5-16, though it is non-statutory guidance.

## Subject Leadership and Curriculum Planning

Effective computing curriculum leaders must ensure:

- **Progression mapping**: Clear progression of concepts across year groups
- **Assessment frameworks**: Robust assessment of computational thinking alongside practical skills
- **Resource allocation**: Adequate hardware, software, and online resources
- **Staff development**: Ongoing CPD to maintain and develop teacher subject knowledge
- **Cross-curricular links**: Integration with mathematics, science, design technology where appropriate
- **Industry engagement**: Links with computing professionals and current practice

Phil Bagge emphasises that computing should be taught as a discrete subject with dedicated curriculum time, not merely integrated into other subjects, to ensure systematic skill and knowledge development.

## Future Directions and Ongoing Debates

The computing education community continues to debate several key issues:

**Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning**: Should these be incorporated earlier in the curriculum? Current programmes of study do not explicitly mention AI, though GCSE specifications increasingly include basic machine learning concepts.

**Equity and Inclusion**: Sue Sentance's research highlights persistent gender gaps in computing uptake at GCSE and A level. The curriculum itself is gender-neutral, but implementation, pedagogy, and classroom culture significantly affect participation.

**Physical Computing**: The integration of hardware (e.g., micro:bit, Raspberry Pi, Arduino) provides tangible connections between computer science theory and real-world applications. While not mandated, these tools are widely adopted.

**Curriculum Breadth**: Tension exists between depth in computer science and breadth across IT applications and digital literacy. Schools must balance these within limited curriculum time.

## Conclusion

The Computing National Curriculum represents a significant achievement in educational reform, establishing computer science as a foundational discipline alongside traditional sciences and humanities. Its implementation requires sustained investment in teacher development, resources, and curriculum design. As Simon Peyton Jones argues, computing education is not primarily about vocational preparation but about providing all young people with a foundational understanding of the digital world they inhabit and will shape.

Success depends on viewing the curriculum as an integrated whole, where computer science, information technology, and digital literacy combine to develop genuinely computational thinkers capable of creating with, not merely consuming, technology.
