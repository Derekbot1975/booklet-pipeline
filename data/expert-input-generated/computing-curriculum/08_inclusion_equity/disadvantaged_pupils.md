# Disadvantaged Pupils and Digital Access

## Introduction

Digital access represents one of the most significant barriers to equity in Computing education. The Department for Education's research consistently demonstrates that disadvantaged pupils face substantial challenges in accessing the technology, connectivity, and home support necessary to develop computational thinking and digital literacy skills. As Sue Sentance (King's College London) notes in her research on equity in Computing education, "access to technology is not merely about having a device—it encompasses connectivity, space to work, family support, and the cultural capital to navigate digital learning" (Sentance, 2020).

The COVID-19 pandemic starkly illuminated the digital divide, with an estimated 1.8 million children in the UK lacking adequate digital access for home learning (Ofcom, 2020). For Computing teachers, this reality demands explicit pedagogical responses that recognise and address structural inequalities rather than compounding them.

## Defining Digital Access and the Digital Divide

### Dimensions of Digital Access

Digital access operates across multiple dimensions, as identified by Miles Berry (University of Roehampton) in his work on inclusion in Computing education:

**Hardware Access**: Physical availability of suitable devices. The Education Endowment Foundation (EEF) reports that 9% of secondary pupils and 15% of primary pupils lack access to a suitable device at home, with rates significantly higher (25%+) among pupils eligible for Free School Meals.

**Connectivity**: Reliable broadband or mobile data. Ofcom's 2021 Connected Nations report indicates approximately 600,000 UK homes cannot access decent broadband speeds (10Mbps download), disproportionately affecting rural and economically disadvantaged areas.

**Digital Literacy**: The knowledge and skills to use technology effectively. This includes parental/carer digital literacy, which Phil Bagge emphasises as crucial for supporting children's Computing learning beyond school.

**Software and Platform Access**: Availability of appropriate software, platforms, and online services. Many schools assume home access to specific tools or cloud services.

**Physical Environment**: Adequate space, privacy, and conditions for digital learning at home—a dimension often overlooked in policy discussions.

### The Poverty Premium in Computing

Mark Dorling (CAS) has written extensively about the 'poverty premium' in Computing education—the additional barriers faced by disadvantaged pupils that compound over time. These include:

- Limited exposure to diverse technology uses beyond social media and gaming
- Reduced access to enrichment activities (coding clubs, robotics competitions, tech visits)
- Lower likelihood of having family members in computing-related professions
- Restricted ability to purchase revision guides, attend paid courses, or access premium online resources
- Device sharing among siblings, limiting individual practice time
- Older, slower devices that cannot run current software effectively

## National Curriculum and Statutory Framework Context

### National Curriculum for Computing Requirements

The National Curriculum for Computing (2014) states that "a high-quality computing education equips pupils to use computational thinking and creativity to understand and change the world." However, it contains no specific guidance on ensuring equitable access, placing the onus on schools to address this.

**Key Stage 1 and 2**: Pupils must learn to "use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content" and "recognise common uses of information technology beyond school." For disadvantaged pupils with limited home access, the phrase "beyond school" may encompass primarily passive consumption rather than creative production.

**Key Stage 3**: The requirement to use "two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual" assumes access to appropriate software and hardware both in school and ideally at home for practice and homework.

**Key Stage 4**: GCSE Computing specifications across all exam boards (AQA, OCR, Edexcel, WJEC Eduqas) require substantial practical programming work. OCR's specification states that 20 hours of programming practice is expected, much of which typically occurs outside timetabled lessons.

### Pupil Premium and Computing

The Pupil Premium grant (£1,455 per eligible primary pupil, £1,035 per secondary pupil in 2023-24) provides dedicated funding for disadvantaged pupils. However, EEF guidance rarely explicitly addresses Computing, with most research focused on core subjects. Computing leads must advocate for appropriate use of Pupil Premium to address digital access gaps.

## Evidence Base and Research

### Key Research Findings

**Royal Society (2017) - "After the Reboot"**: This landmark report highlighted that socio-economic background significantly impacts computing attainment and participation. Pupils from lower socio-economic backgrounds are less likely to take Computing GCSE and A-Level, partly due to concerns about the need for home access to suitable technology.

**Livingstone and Helsper (2007) - Gradations in Digital Inclusion**: Although pre-dating the current curriculum, this foundational work identified that digital inequality is not binary but exists on a spectrum, with disadvantaged pupils experiencing not just lack of access but lower-quality access—older devices, slower connections, more restrictions on usage.

**Kemp, Wong and Berry (2021)**: Research examining computing participation post-2014 curriculum reform found persistent gaps, with schools in areas of higher deprivation less likely to offer GCSE Computer Science, and pupils less likely to select it even when available.

**Sentance and Humphreys (2020)**: Their examination of teacher perspectives on equity in Computing revealed that many teachers recognise access issues but feel ill-equipped to address them, often resorting to reducing homework demands rather than implementing systematic solutions.

**Simon Peyton Jones** (Microsoft Research) has consistently argued that Computing education must be considered an equity issue, stating "Computing is not a luxury subject for the privileged few—it is foundational literacy for the 21st century." His work with CAS on broadening participation emphasises that digital access barriers represent a form of structural exclusion from economic opportunity.

### Impact on Attainment and Progress

Research by the Department for Education (FFT Education Datalab, 2020) examining GCSE Computer Science outcomes found:

- Disadvantaged pupils achieve, on average, 1.2 grades lower than their peers
- The attainment gap is larger in Computer Science than many other GCSE subjects
- Schools with higher proportions of disadvantaged pupils show lower entry rates for Computer Science
- Gender disparities are more pronounced among disadvantaged cohorts

These gaps cannot be attributed solely to prior attainment or general academic factors, suggesting subject-specific barriers, of which digital access is a primary candidate.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Assessment and Homework Strategies

**Rethinking Homework Assumptions**: Phil Bagge advocates for "homework that doesn't rely on home access." Strategies include:

- Providing optional extension activities rather than essential homework
- Offering lunchtime and after-school access sessions specifically for homework completion
- Using paper-based alternatives for algorithm design, computational thinking exercises, and planning
- Creating 'flipped learning' resources accessible on low-bandwidth mobile devices
- Allowing in-class time for activities traditionally assigned as homework

**Assessment Design**: Ensure assessment can be completed within school hours with school resources. For GCSE NEA (Non-Examined Assessment):

- **AQA**: The 20-hour programming project should be completable within lesson time, with appropriate planning
- **OCR**: Both J277 and J276 specifications require centres to ensure "access to appropriate resources," which should mean school-based provision
- **Edexcel**: Clear guidance that projects must be conducted under controlled conditions helps ensure equity
- **WJEC Eduqas**: The 30-hour NEA explicitly states this is guided learning hours, which should occur in school

### Curriculum Planning for Equity

**Deliberately Teach What Others Learn at Home**: Miles Berry emphasises that advantaged pupils often arrive with substantial informal computing experience gained at home. Teachers must explicitly teach:

- File management and organisation skills
- Keyboard shortcuts and efficient device navigation
- Troubleshooting approaches and debugging strategies
- How to search effectively and evaluate digital sources
- Software installation and configuration processes
- Backup and version control practices

**Low-Tech Computational Thinking**: Sue Sentance's work highlights the value of unplugged activities that develop computational thinking without requiring technology:

- Algorithm design using flowcharts on paper
- Sorting and searching algorithms with physical cards
- Binary and hexadecimal conversion exercises
- Network topologies modelled with string and paper
- Boolean logic with physical gates

These approaches ensure all pupils can access core concepts regardless of home digital access.

### School-Based Provision

**Extended Access Provision**: Evidence-informed strategies include:

- Breakfast, lunchtime, and after-school 'open lab' sessions
- Designated homework support sessions with teacher or teaching assistant support
- Priority booking systems for disadvantaged pupils
- Device loan schemes with appropriate acceptable use policies
- Printed resource packs for pupils without home connectivity
- USB drives with portable software environments

**Device Loan Schemes**: When implementing device loans, consider:

- Data protection and safeguarding requirements (safer internet filters, monitoring)
- Insurance and damage policies that don't penalise disadvantaged families
- Technical support for home issues
- Clear agreements with families about appropriate use
- Backup devices for emergencies
- Partnership with DfE programmes like Get Help with Technology

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Pair Programming and Collaborative Work**: Strategic pairing can address access issues:

- Rotate pairs regularly to ensure disadvantaged pupils work with different partners
- Use driver-navigator approach to ensure all pupils get 'hands-on' time
- Explicitly teach collaborative programming practices as professional skills
- Create group projects that can be decomposed into tasks suitable for different environments

**Flipped Learning Considerations**: If using flipped learning approaches:

- Provide multiple access methods (video, audio, PDF, paper)
- Keep video content short and optimised for mobile viewing
- Make all resources available on low-bandwidth platforms
- Never assume home viewing—provide in-school viewing time
- Assess content accessibility using mobile-first design principles

## Exam Board Specific Considerations

### GCSE Computer Science Requirements

**AQA 8525**:
- Programming project requires development of a working solution
- Specification states "access to a computer system" is necessary
- Schools must ensure in-school access for the recommended 20 hours
- Practical programming skills examined through on-screen assessment
- No assumption of home programming practice should underpin teaching

**OCR J277**:
- Two programming projects within NEA component
- 20 hours of programming project time must be provided
- On-screen examination of practical programming (Paper 2)
- Schools must provide "appropriate facilities and resources"
- Particular attention needed for learners without home practice opportunities

**Edexcel 1CP2**:
- Programming project worth 20% of total GCSE
- Must be completed under controlled conditions
- Paper 2 includes on-screen programming tasks
- Clear requirement for school-based computer access
- Specification acknowledges need for centre-provided resources

**WJEC Eduqas C670QS**:
- 30-hour programming project
- Two-tier specification may advantage disadvantaged learners who need more structured support
- On-screen examination component
- Welsh centres may face additional rural connectivity challenges
- Bilingual provision requires appropriate software localisation

### A-Level Computer Science

At A-Level (AQA 7517, OCR H446, Edexcel 9CP0, WJEC Eduqas A670QS), the assumption of substantial independent programming practice is more explicit. Teachers must:

- Recognise that disadvantaged pupils may need significantly more in-school time
- Provide structured after-school programming sessions
- Consider implications for university preparation and career readiness
- Advocate for appropriate centre resources to support all learners
- Address the significant under-representation of disadvantaged pupils at this level

## Addressing Systemic Barriers

### Whole-School Leadership Strategies

**Senior Leadership Team (SLT) Considerations**: Computing leads should work with SLT to:

- Audit digital access among disadvantaged pupils specifically
- Allocate Pupil Premium funding for device loans and connectivity support
- Timetable Computing in rooms with adequate device ratios
- Prioritise Computing for extended access provision
- Include Computing in school digital strategy documents
- Report on digital equity in Pupil Premium strategy statements

**Governor Engagement**: As Mark Dorling notes, governors must understand that Computing equity is both an educational and economic justice issue. Governing bodies should:

- Question digital access provision in Computing specifically
- Ensure Pupil Premium strategy addresses Computing access
- Support funding applications for hardware and connectivity
- Monitor participation and attainment gaps in Computing qualifications
- Link Computing access to school's equality duties under the Equality Act 2010

### Partnership and Funding Approaches

**DfE and External Programmes**:

- **Get Help with Technology**: DfE programme providing devices and connectivity for disadvantaged pupils, initially for COVID response but with ongoing iterations
- **EdTech Demonstrator Programme**: Schools can access support from demonstrator schools on effective technology use
- **Oak National Academy**: Free, curriculum-aligned resources designed for low-bandwidth access
- **NCCE (National Centre for Computing Education)**: Funded by DfE, provides free CPD and resources; Computing Hubs can advise on access strategies

**Charitable and Industry Support**:

- **Raspberry Pi Foundation**: Low-cost computing devices and free resources specifically designed for educational access
- **Google CS First, Microsoft MakeCode**: Browser-based platforms reducing software barriers
- **Code Club, CoderDojo**: Free extracurricular computing opportunities, though access requires transportation and awareness
- **Nominet**: Grants programme specifically supporting digital inclusion projects

### Policy Advocacy

Computing teachers and leaders should advocate at local and national levels for:

- Recognition of digital access as essential infrastructure, like textbooks
- Increased Pupil Premium flexibility for technology provision
- Exam board recognition of access constraints in coursework deadlines and requirements
- Ofsted framework explicitly addressing digital equity in Computing provision
- Government investment in universal basic digital access for school-age children

## Intersectional Considerations

### Multiple Disadvantage

Digital access issues compound with other characteristics:

**Gender**: Girls from disadvantaged backgrounds face dual barriers—both access issues and persistent gender stereotypes about computing aptitude. Research by Kemp and Berry shows they are least likely to continue Computing beyond KS3.

**SEND (Special Educational Needs and Disabilities)**: Pupils with SEND may require assistive technologies that are expensive and not available at home. Screen readers, adaptive keyboards, and specialist software create additional access barriers.

**EAL (English as an Additional Language)**: Disadvantaged pupils with EAL may lack both digital resources and language support at home. Visual programming environments (Scratch, Blockly) can reduce language barriers in school.

**Looked After Children**: Frequent placement changes disrupt both learning and accumulation of digital resources. Virtual School Heads should be engaged regarding Computing provision.

**Rural Disadvantage**: Combines economic disadvantage with connectivity challenges. Rural schools may have poor broadband and pupils may live beyond reasonable after-school access distance.

### Cultural Capital and Aspiration

Sue Sentance's research emphasises that digital access extends beyond devices to cultural capital—knowledge of computing careers, exposure to diverse technology applications, and contact with computing professionals. Disadvantaged pupils are less likely to:

- Have family members in computing careers
- Attend computing enrichment events (often in city centres)
- Access paid-for coding camps or courses
- Visit tech companies or universities
- Encounter diverse role models in computing

Schools must explicitly provide these experiences through:

- Industry partnership programmes (speakers, mentoring, workplace visits)
- Promotion of computing careers and pathways
- Showcasing diverse role models across materials and examples
- Free enrichment opportunities scheduled and promoted equitably
- UCAS and careers support specifically addressing computing pathways

## Monitoring and Evaluation

### Data Collection and Analysis

Schools should monitor:

- Entry rates for Computing qualifications by disadvantage (FSM, Pupil Premium, IDACI)
- Attainment gaps between disadvantaged pupils and peers in Computing
- Participation in computing enrichment activities by disadvantage
- Device loan take-up and usage patterns
- Homework completion rates in Computing compared to other subjects
- Destination data for Computing qualifications by disadvantage
- Teacher referral patterns for Computing interventions

### Quality Assurance

Use pupil voice to evaluate provision:

- Regular surveys on digital access and barriers
- Focus groups with disadvantaged pupils about Computing experiences
- Student panel involvement in curriculum planning
- Feedback on homework accessibility
- Exit surveys for pupils not continuing Computing

### Impact Evaluation

Drawing on EEF evaluation frameworks, assess:

- Has the attainment gap narrowed in Computing specifically?
- Have entry rates increased among disadvantaged pupils?
- Do disadvantaged pupils report confidence and enjoyment in Computing?
- Are homework completion rates equitable?
- Do disadvantaged pupils access enrichment opportunities at similar rates?
- Have teacher perceptions of disadvantaged pupils' computing potential changed?

## Conclusion and Key Recommendations

Digital access represents a critical equity challenge in Computing education. As Simon Peyton Jones argues, without deliberate action, Computing education risks becoming "a driver of inequality rather than a force for social mobility."

**Essential Actions for Computing Teachers**:

1. **Never assume home digital access** when planning homework, assessment, or extended learning
2. **Provide explicit, structured in-school time** for all aspects of Computing qualifications
3. **Teach foundational digital skills explicitly** rather than assuming prior knowledge
4. **Use unplugged and low-tech approaches** to develop computational thinking universally
5. **Advocate with school leadership
