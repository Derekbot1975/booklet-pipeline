# SEND in Computing

## Introduction

Computing education in the UK must be accessible to all learners, including those with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND). The Equality Act 2010 establishes a legal duty for schools to make reasonable adjustments for disabled pupils, whilst the SEND Code of Practice (DfE & DoH, 2015) provides statutory guidance on supporting pupils with additional needs. Computing, when taught inclusively, offers unique opportunities for SEND learners to develop problem-solving skills, creativity, and independence. As Sue Sentance has noted in her work with the Raspberry Pi Foundation, Computing can be particularly empowering for SEND learners when barriers to access are systematically addressed.

## Legal and Statutory Framework

### UK National Curriculum Requirements

The Computing Programmes of Study (DfE, 2013) contain the statutory inclusion statement which requires teachers to "set suitable learning challenges, respond to pupils' diverse learning needs, and overcome potential barriers to learning and assessment for individuals and groups of pupils." This applies across all three strands of Computing:
- Computer Science
- Information Technology
- Digital Literacy

Schools must ensure that SEND pupils can access the full Computing curriculum, making reasonable adjustments as required under the Equality Act 2010. This includes providing auxiliary aids, adapting teaching methods, and modifying assessment approaches.

### SEND Code of Practice Implications

The graduated approach of Assess, Plan, Do, Review must be applied to Computing lessons. Computing coordinators should work closely with SENCOs to ensure appropriate provision mapping includes Computing-specific interventions and support strategies.

## Categories of SEND and Computing-Specific Considerations

### Cognition and Learning Needs

#### Moderate Learning Difficulties (MLD)

Pupils with MLD may struggle with abstract computational thinking concepts. Phil Bagge's work on concrete-pictorial-abstract approaches in Computing is particularly relevant here. Practical implications include:

- **Breaking down algorithms**: Use physical computing resources (such as Bee-Bots or Crumble controllers) to make abstract concepts tangible
- **Scaffolded programming**: Provide partially completed code with clear comments and visual structure
- **Reduced cognitive load**: Limit the number of new commands or concepts introduced in a single lesson
- **Multi-sensory approaches**: Combine verbal instructions with visual prompts and kinaesthetic activities

Research by the National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE) demonstrates that concrete resources significantly improve understanding of sequence, selection, and iteration for pupils with MLD.

#### Severe Learning Difficulties (SLD) and Profound and Multiple Learning Difficulties (PMLD)

For pupils with SLD/PMLD, Computing objectives should focus on cause-and-effect relationships and switch access technology:

- **Switch-accessible software**: Applications like Sensory Software's suite or Inclusive Technology's resources
- **Assistive technology**: Eye-gaze systems, touch-sensitive screens, and adaptive controllers
- **Simplified programming**: Tools such as Blue-Bot or switch-operated toys to demonstrate basic sequencing
- **Digital sensory experiences**: Creating multimedia sensory stories using sound, light, and vibration

### Specific Learning Difficulties (SpLD)

#### Dyslexia

Miles Berry has written extensively about making text-based programming accessible. Dyslexia presents specific challenges in Computing, particularly with syntax-heavy programming languages:

**Practical adjustments:**
- **Visual programming languages**: Scratch, Blockly, and MakeCode reduce syntax burden and spelling errors
- **Code colour-coding**: Syntax highlighting with high-contrast themes
- **Screen readers for code**: Tools like CodeTalk or accessible IDEs
- **Dyslexia-friendly fonts**: Use OpenDyslexic or Comic Sans MS in coding environments
- **Spell-checkers in IDEs**: Enable autocomplete and syntax correction features
- **Reduced copying**: Provide code templates or starter files to minimise transcription

**Assessment considerations:**
- Allow extra time for reading exam questions (OCR, AQA, and Edexcel all permit this)
- Provide questions on coloured paper
- Accept verbal explanations of code understanding
- Use programming languages with forgiving syntax where appropriate

#### Dyscalculia

Computing involves logical and mathematical thinking, which can challenge pupils with dyscalculia:

- **Visual representations**: Use number lines, hundred squares, and physical manipulatives for binary and hexadecimal work
- **Concrete data handling**: Use real, meaningful datasets rather than abstract numbers
- **Calculator use**: Permit calculators for binary/hexadecimal conversions at GCSE level (check specific exam board regulations)
- **Pattern recognition**: Focus on computational patterns rather than numerical accuracy
- **Variables with meaning**: Use descriptive variable names that clearly indicate purpose

#### Dyspraxia

Motor coordination difficulties affect practical Computing tasks:

- **Keyboard alternatives**: Voice recognition software, on-screen keyboards, or alternative input devices
- **Modified peripherals**: Ergonomic keyboards, trackballs, or larger mice
- **Scaffolded physical computing**: Pre-connected circuits for projects focusing on programming rather than assembly
- **Extended time**: For practical programming tasks and physical computing projects
- **Flexible seating**: Allow standing or alternative seating positions

### Communication and Interaction Needs

#### Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC)

Computing can be particularly appealing to autistic pupils, as noted in research by the National Autistic Society's education team. Mark Dorling has advocated for recognising the strengths many autistic pupils bring to computational thinking:

**Strengths-based approach:**
- Attention to detail and pattern recognition
- Logical, systematic thinking
- Focus and persistence with interesting problems
- Visual thinking skills

**Reasonable adjustments:**
- **Predictable structure**: Clear lesson routines and visible timers for activities
- **Explicit success criteria**: Detailed rubrics and exemplar work
- **Reduced ambiguity**: Precise language in instructions; avoid idioms
- **Alternative social activities**: Option for individual rather than paired programming
- **Sensory considerations**: Dimmed screens, noise-cancelling headphones, and quiet workspaces
- **Special interests**: Incorporate pupil interests into programming projects where possible
- **Processing time**: Allow additional thinking time before responses expected

#### Speech, Language and Communication Needs (SLCN)

- **Visual instructions**: Use flowcharts, diagrams, and pictorial guides
- **Keyword vocabulary**: Explicitly teach and display Computing-specific terminology
- **Alternative communication**: Accept written or typed responses instead of oral explanations
- **Widgit symbols**: Use symbol-supported resources for key concepts
- **Collaborative tools**: Communication apps or shared documents for group work

### Social, Emotional and Mental Health Difficulties (SEMH)

- **Safe environments**: Use offline coding environments or sandboxed online spaces
- **Achievable challenges**: Quick wins and progressive difficulty levels
- **Choice and autonomy**: Allow selection of project themes within curriculum requirements
- **Positive relationships**: Build trust through Computing clubs or one-to-one support sessions
- **Clear boundaries**: Explicit acceptable use policies with consistent application

### Sensory and Physical Needs

#### Visual Impairment (VI)

Mark Dorling's work with inclusive Computing highlights the importance of accessible programming for VI pupils:

- **Screen readers**: JAWS, NVDA, or VoiceOver compatible programming environments
- **High contrast**: White text on black backgrounds or user-preferred colour schemes
- **Magnification**: Screen magnification software alongside standard tools
- **Tactile programming**: Physical coding blocks with braille labels
- **Auditory programming**: Tools like QuorumLanguage which is designed for blind users
- **Alternative outputs**: Sound or speech rather than visual-only feedback
- **Descriptive text**: Alt-text for all images and diagrams in resources

At GCSE level, exam boards provide modified papers. OCR's J277 and J278, AQA's 8525, Edexcel's 1CP2, and WJEC's C00100 all offer enlarged print and braille versions with appropriate accommodations.

#### Hearing Impairment (HI)

- **Visual presentations**: Ensure all auditory information has visual equivalents
- **Captions and transcripts**: For video resources and tutorials
- **BSL interpretation**: For specialist Computing vocabulary if required
- **Written instructions**: Supplement verbal explanations with clear written steps
- **Preferential seating**: Clear sightlines to teacher and screen
- **Loop systems**: If available, for pupils with hearing aids

#### Physical Disability (PD)

- **Assistive technology**: Switch access, eye-gaze, or voice control for computers
- **Adapted peripherals**: Large keys, one-handed keyboards, or joystick mice
- **Accessible furniture**: Adjustable-height desks for wheelchair users
- **Extended time**: For practical tasks requiring physical manipulation
- **Flexible project formats**: Alternative assessment through software rather than hardware projects
- **Paired support**: Scribing partnerships where appropriate

## Teaching Strategies and Pedagogical Approaches

### Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

Simon Peyton Jones, in his work on Computing education principles, emphasises designing lessons that are accessible from the outset rather than retrofitting support. UDL principles in Computing include:

**Multiple means of representation:**
- Present computational concepts through text, diagrams, video, and physical models
- Use both block-based and text-based programming languages
- Provide worked examples alongside practice problems

**Multiple means of action and expression:**
- Offer choice in programming languages (within curriculum constraints)
- Allow video demonstrations, written explanations, or live coding to show understanding
- Accept annotated screenshots, pseudocode, or flowcharts as alternatives to written code

**Multiple means of engagement:**
- Connect Computing to diverse interests and careers
- Vary between individual, paired, and group work
- Include game-based learning, creative projects, and problem-solving challenges

### Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract (CPA) Approach

Phil Bagge has adapted this mathematical pedagogy for Computing education:

**Concrete stage:**
- Physical algorithms (classroom robots, unplugged activities)
- Hardware components handled and explored
- Tangible debugging (sorting physical objects)

**Pictorial stage:**
- Flowcharts and algorithm diagrams
- Block-based programming (Scratch, Blockly)
- Visual representations of data structures

**Abstract stage:**
- Text-based programming
- Written pseudocode
- Theoretical computer science concepts

SEND pupils may need extended time at concrete and pictorial stages, and some may not progress to fully abstract representations—this is acceptable as long as conceptual understanding is demonstrated.

### Worked Examples and Scaffolding

Research by Sentance and Waite (2017) with King's College London and the Raspberry Pi Foundation demonstrates that worked examples significantly reduce cognitive load:

- **PRIMM approach**: Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make—allows graduated independence
- **Parsons problems**: Rearranging pre-written code lines reduces syntax burden
- **Code completion**: Fill-in-the-blank programming tasks
- **Annotated code**: Teacher-provided comments explain function and logic
- **Live coding**: Teacher demonstrates thinking process aloud

### Metacognitive Strategies

Explicitly teaching problem-solving approaches benefits SEND pupils:

- **Think-alouds**: Model debugging process verbally
- **Strategy cards**: Laminated prompt cards for "I'm stuck" moments
- **Error analysis**: Structured approach to interpreting error messages
- **Reflection protocols**: Sentence starters for evaluating own code

## Assessment Approaches

### Formative Assessment Adaptations

- **Low-stakes quizzes**: Multiple-choice or matching activities using platforms like Kahoot or Quizizz
- **Exit tickets**: Simple checks for understanding with minimal writing
- **Peer assessment**: Structured rubrics guide feedback on code
- **Observational assessment**: Teacher notes during practical work
- **Verbal questioning**: One-to-one discussions about code functionality

### Summative Assessment and Qualifications

#### Key Stage 3

Teacher assessment should consider:
- Alternative evidence formats (video, audio, presentations)
- Extended timescales for projects
- Adjusted success criteria maintaining curriculum ambition
- Assessment of computational thinking separate from programming syntax accuracy

#### GCSE (Key Stage 4)

All UK exam boards must provide access arrangements under JCQ regulations:

**OCR Computer Science (J277):**
- Paper 1 (Computer Systems): 25% extra time permitted for SEND candidates
- Paper 2 (Computational Thinking): Readers, scribes, or word processors allowed
- Practical Programming Project: Flexibility in format; can submit annotated Scratch rather than text-based language with prior arrangement

**AQA Computer Science (8525):**
- Extra time, rest breaks, supervised rest breaks available
- Coloured overlays and modified papers for VI/dyslexia
- Prompts and readers permitted for questions (but not for code reading)

**Edexcel Computer Science (1CP2):**
- Similar access arrangements to other boards
- On-screen examination option available for practical components

**WJEC Computer Science:**
- Welsh-language provision includes SEND accommodations
- Modified language versions for pupils with language processing difficulties

#### Vocational Qualifications

- **OCR Cambridge Nationals in Creative iMedia**: Coursework-focused; allows differentiated evidence collection
- **BTEC Digital Information Technology**: Flexible assessment through portfolio; suitable for pupils requiring extended time or alternative assessment formats

Access arrangements must be evidenced through Form 8 applications following normal access history in class. Computing departments should maintain evidence portfolios throughout Key Stage 3 to support applications.

## Technology Tools and Resources for SEND

### Assistive Technology

- **Text-to-speech**: Natural Reader, Read&Write Gold for reading code and documentation
- **Speech-to-text**: Dragon Naturally Speaking for coding through dictation
- **Word prediction**: Co:Writer or Penfriend for reducing typing burden
- **Mind mapping**: Inspiration or MindView for algorithm planning

### Inclusive Programming Environments

- **Microsoft MakeCode**: Accessibility features include screen reader support and high contrast
- **Scratch 3.0**: Improved accessibility features including keyboard navigation
- **Python with IDLE**: Configurable colours and fonts; screen reader compatible
- **Replit**: Cloud-based with accessibility features and collaborative tools

### Physical Computing for SEND

- **BBC micro:bit**: Large buttons, accessible inputs, compatible with assistive technology
- **Crumble controller**: Simpler than Arduino; works with switch access
- **Sphero robots**: Visual programming app suitable for dyslexia
- **Makey Makey**: Highly tactile; excellent for pupils with sensory processing differences

## Professional Development and Whole-School Approaches

### Staff Training

Computing teachers require CPD on:
- Specific SEND conditions and their impact on Computing learning
- Access arrangements and exam accommodations
- Assistive technology training
- Adaptive pedagogy in Computing
- Collaboration with SENCO and support staff

The National Centre for Computing Education offers specific courses including "Supporting SEND Learners in Computing" as part of their certificated professional development pathway.

### Collaboration with SENCOs

Computing coordinators should:
- Share Computing-specific SEND strategies at SENCO meetings
- Contribute to EHCPs with Computing targets and provision
- Jointly observe lessons to identify barriers
- Share resources and successful adaptations
- Involve SENCO in curriculum planning

### Deployment of Teaching Assistants (TAs)

Research by the Education Endowment Foundation highlights the importance of effective TA deployment:

- **Pre-teaching**: TAs introduce vocabulary or concepts before main lesson
- **Skilled support**: TAs trained in Computing concepts, not just generic support
- **Scaffolding independence**: TAs use prompt hierarchies and gradually withdraw support
- **Avoid dependency**: Ensure SEND pupils work directly with teacher regularly

### Inclusive Classroom Culture

Miles Berry emphasises the importance of growth mindset in Computing. Creating an inclusive culture involves:

- Celebrating diverse approaches to problem-solving
- Normalising error and debugging as learning
- Showcasing work from SEND pupils
- Avoiding ability grouping that segregates SEND learners
- Using inclusive language (avoiding "low ability" labels)

## Case Studies and Research Evidence

### Research from King's College London

Sue Sentance's research with the NCCE has demonstrated that:
- Block-based languages reduce syntax barriers but must transition carefully to text-based
- Pair programming benefits SEND learners when roles are structured and rotated
- Semantic waves (moving between concrete and abstract) improve retention for all learners including SEND

### Raspberry Pi Foundation Research

Studies on inclusive Computing (Sentance et al., 2019) found:
- Physical computing projects increased engagement for SEND pupils
- Projects connected to personal interests significantly improved outcomes
- Teacher confidence in SEND provision varied widely and requires focused CPD

## Monitoring and Evaluation

### Tracking Progress

- Use of assessment without levels frameworks adapted for Computing
- Progress measured against personalised starting points, not just age expectations
- Value-added measures considering SEND barriers
- Qualitative evidence alongside quantitative data

### Evaluating Provision

Regular evaluation should address:
- Are SEND pupils accessing the full Computing curriculum?
- What percentage of SEND pupils achieve nationally expected standards?
- What barriers remain and what further adjustments are needed?
- How effective are current interventions and adaptations?
- Are SEND pupils progressing to GCSE Computing and related pathways?
