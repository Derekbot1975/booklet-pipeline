# Addressing the Gender Gap in Computing

## Introduction and Context

The gender gap in Computing represents one of the most significant equity challenges facing UK education. Despite Computing becoming a mandatory National Curriculum subject in 2014, persistent disparities exist in participation, achievement, and progression, particularly at GCSE and A-level. According to research by Sue Sentance and colleagues at King's College London, female students comprise fewer than 20% of A-level Computer Science entries, and this disparity begins manifesting as early as Key Stage 2.

The Computing at School (CAS) organisation, co-founded by Simon Peyton Jones, has identified addressing the gender gap as a strategic priority, recognising that diversity in Computing is essential for both educational equity and the future UK digital economy. Mark Dorling's work with CAS has emphasised that this is not merely a participation issue but fundamentally concerns curriculum design, pedagogical approach, and classroom culture.

## The Scale and Nature of the Problem

### Statistical Evidence

Data from UK exam boards demonstrates stark gender disparities:

- **GCSE Computer Science**: Female entries represent approximately 20% of total candidates (AQA, OCR, Edexcel data 2019-2023)
- **A-level Computer Science**: Female entries consistently below 15% across all boards (AQA, OCR, WJEC)
- **AS-level uptake**: Shows similar patterns, with girls representing under 13% in recent years

The Royal Society's 2017 report "After the Reboot" documented these trends comprehensively, noting that whilst the new Computing curriculum initially saw increased female participation at Key Stage 3, this did not translate into sustained engagement at examination level.

### Where the Gap Emerges

Research by Sue Sentance and Jane Waite indicates the gender gap develops progressively:

- **Key Stage 1-2**: Relatively equitable participation in computational thinking activities
- **Key Stage 3**: Divergence begins, with girls reporting lower confidence despite comparable achievement
- **Key Stage 4**: Sharp decline in female uptake of GCSE Computer Science (often 4:1 male-to-female ratio)
- **Key Stage 5**: Further attrition, with some centres reporting no female A-level candidates

Miles Berry's curriculum research suggests the gap intensifies when programming becomes the dominant focus, particularly when disconnected from broader contexts and applications.

## Root Causes and Research Evidence

### Stereotyping and Cultural Factors

Mark Dorling's work identifies several contributing factors:

1. **Subject perception**: Computing persistently viewed as a "masculine" domain, associated with gaming, isolation, and technical obsession
2. **Lack of role models**: Limited visibility of female computing professionals and teachers
3. **Media representation**: Reinforcement of "geek" stereotypes in popular culture
4. **Prior experience gaps**: Boys more likely to have informal coding exposure before formal education

### Pedagogical Approaches

Phil Bagge's classroom research highlights how traditional Computing pedagogy can inadvertently disadvantage girls:

- **Competition-focused activities**: Emphasis on speed coding and gaming challenges may alienate students who prefer collaborative approaches
- **Decontextualised programming**: Teaching syntax and algorithms without meaningful real-world application
- **Technical language barriers**: Unnecessary jargon creating exclusionary environments
- **Single solution emphasis**: Privileging efficiency over creativity and multiple valid approaches

### Curriculum Design Issues

The National Curriculum for Computing (2014) states pupils should be taught to "design, use and evaluate computational abstractions" and "understand several key algorithms." However, Sue Sentance notes that interpretation and implementation significantly affect gender equity:

- **Assessment weighting**: GCSE specifications with heavy programming components (particularly OCR J277 and AQA 8525) correlate with lower female participation
- **Project flexibility**: Specifications allowing creative, contextualised projects (e.g., AQA NEA options) show marginally better gender balance
- **Theory-practice balance**: Overemphasis on either extreme can disengage different learners

## Effective Interventions and Strategies

### Primary Phase (Key Stages 1 and 2)

Phil Bagge's work with primary Computing emphasises prevention rather than cure:

**Curriculum Approaches:**
- Integrate Computing across the curriculum, demonstrating relevance to art, literacy, science, and humanities
- Use diverse contexts for computational thinking, including fashion design, environmental monitoring, and storytelling
- Introduce programming through creative platforms (Scratch, Purple Mash, 2Code) emphasising expression rather than technical mastery
- Ensure National Curriculum objectives like "use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs" are taught through varied, inclusive contexts

**Practical Classroom Strategies:**
- Collaborative pair programming with strategic partnering (avoiding fixed gender pairing which can reinforce stereotypes)
- Showcase female computing pioneers: Ada Lovelace, Grace Hopper, Katherine Johnson (linking to National Curriculum history)
- Use unplugged activities that don't privilege prior technical experience
- Celebrate multiple forms of success, not just fastest completion

### Secondary Phase (Key Stage 3)

Mark Dorling emphasises Key Stage 3 as the critical intervention point:

**Curriculum Design:**
- Explicitly teach the breadth of Computing careers, including UX design, data science, cybersecurity, and digital ethics
- Connect programming to social issues: environmental data analysis, accessibility tools, health applications
- Include significant creative computing projects where aesthetic and functional success are equally valued
- Ensure National Curriculum coverage of "computational thinking" emphasises problem decomposition and pattern recognition alongside programming

**Pedagogical Approaches:**
- Use PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) methodology developed by Sue Sentance and colleagues, which scaffolds programming learning
- Implement block-based and text-based programming bridges (Scratch to Python transitions)
- Develop classroom cultures that normalise error and iteration
- Provide choice in project themes while maintaining technical rigour

### Key Stage 4 and 5 (GCSE and A-level)

**Addressing Uptake Issues:**

Miles Berry and Simon Peyton Jones have advocated for structural changes:

- **Whole-cohort teaching**: Making GCSE Computer Science compulsory removes self-selection bias (implemented successfully in some schools)
- **Early decision support**: Provide KS3 girls with explicit information about Computing pathways, challenging misconceptions
- **Female-only taster sessions**: Allow exploration of GCSE content without stereotype threat
- **Highlight exam board differences**: OCR J277, AQA 8525, Edexcel 1CP2, and WJEC A601U have different balances of programming, theory, and coursework

**Specification-Specific Strategies:**

For **AQA 8525 GCSE**: The NEA (non-exam assessment) provides opportunities for student choice. Encourage girls to select personally meaningful problems, connecting the programming and computational thinking requirements to their interests.

For **OCR J277 GCSE**: This specification's computational thinking paper (J277/01) and algorithms paper (J277/02) provide opportunities to demonstrate understanding without pure coding pressure. Emphasise these components early.

For **A-level specifications** (AQA 7517, OCR H446, WJEC/Eduqas): The substantial programming project offers creative scope. Showcase previous successful projects addressing social issues, creative applications, or accessibility challenges.

### Whole-School Approaches

**Structural Interventions:**

1. **Staffing**: Actively recruit female Computing teachers; where not possible, invite female computing professionals as speakers
2. **Option structures**: Avoid timetable clashes between Computer Science and subjects with high female uptake (e.g., certain language combinations)
3. **Careers education**: Integrate Computing career pathways throughout CEIAG provision, not just in Computing lessons
4. **Extra-curricular activities**: Establish inclusive coding clubs, avoiding competitive gaming focus

**Cultural Change:**

Mark Dorling's CAS work emphasises:
- Challenge "geek" culture explicitly through assemblies, displays, and discourse
- Celebrate diverse success (not just competition winners)
- Use gender-neutral language in all Computing communications
- Audit classroom displays for representation and role models

## Assessment and Tracking

### Monitoring Gender Equity

Schools should implement:

1. **Participation audits**: Track gender ratios from KS3 to KS5, identifying dropout points
2. **Attainment analysis**: Compare achievement by gender within Computing cohorts
3. **Confidence surveys**: Use Sue Sentance's Computing Attitude Survey to identify confidence gaps before they become participation gaps
4. **Option process analysis**: Review how GCSE choices are presented and supported

### Addressing Assessment Bias

Research by Kemp, Wong, and Berry suggests:

- **Programming tasks**: Ensure contexts don't privilege stereotypically masculine interests (gaming, sports statistics)
- **Problem scenarios**: Use diverse contexts across exam papers and controlled assessments
- **Exemplar materials**: Exam boards' specimen papers should demonstrate varied approaches

Teachers can influence this through:
- Advance preparation using diverse practice scenarios
- Teaching multiple problem-solving approaches
- Emphasising that specification requirements can be met through varied contexts

## Teacher Development and Professional Learning

### Essential Knowledge

Computing teachers addressing the gender gap require understanding of:

1. **Stereotype threat**: How awareness of negative stereotypes affects performance (extensive educational psychology research applicable to Computing)
2. **Growth mindset**: Carol Dweck's work applied to Computing contexts
3. **Inclusive pedagogy**: Florian and Black-Hawkins' framework applied to Computing education
4. **Subject-specific research**: Sue Sentance's extensive work on Computing education

### CPD Opportunities

**CAS Regional Centres**: Provide face-to-face and online CPD specifically on inclusion and diversity

**National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE)**: Offers:
- "Teaching GCSE Computer Science" with inclusion modules
- "Gender Balance in Computing" specific courses
- Online resources and research summaries

**Raspberry Pi Foundation**: Research seminars featuring international and UK-based evidence on gender and Computing education

## Policy and Leadership Considerations

### Senior Leadership Actions

Simon Peyton Jones's advocacy work emphasises whole-school responsibility:

1. **Option guidance**: Ensure careers advisors understand Computing career breadth
2. **Resource allocation**: Prioritise CPD funding for inclusive pedagogy training
3. **Accountability**: Include gender participation metrics in subject review processes
4. **Strategic planning**: Make gender equity an explicit Computing department target

### Department-Level Leadership

Computing departments should:

- Audit curriculum for inclusive contexts and examples
- Review assessment task choices for bias
- Establish partnerships with local tech companies (particularly those with diversity programmes)
- Create progression pathways visible from Year 7
- Document and share successful interventions

### Multi-Academy Trust and Local Authority Level

Larger organisations can:
- Share female Computing teachers across schools for role model visits
- Coordinate industry partnership programmes
- Commission subject-specific equality training
- Facilitate cross-school student networking (female Computing student groups)

## Looking Forward: Emerging Research and Practice

### Current Research Priorities

Sue Sentance and the King's College London computing education research group continue investigating:
- Long-term impact of curriculum interventions
- Intersectionality: how gender intersects with ethnicity, SEND, and socio-economic factors
- Teacher beliefs and their impact on student outcomes
- International comparative studies

### Promising Developments

1. **AI and machine learning contexts**: Increasing relevance of AI provides new, socially-connected curriculum contexts
2. **Data science emphasis**: Growing recognition that Computing encompasses data analysis, not just programming
3. **Digital ethics**: Stronger curriculum focus provides engaging philosophical and social content
4. **Physical computing**: Continued development of resources like micro:bit and Raspberry Pi with creative project potential

### Remaining Challenges

Mark Dorling and colleagues acknowledge persistent issues:
- Teacher supply limitations (particularly female specialists)
- Entrenched subject perceptions requiring long-term cultural change
- Tension between academic rigour and accessibility in qualification design
- Regional variation in resources and expertise

## Practical Action Planning Template

Schools addressing the gender gap should develop action plans including:

**Immediate actions (Term 1):**
- Audit current participation data
- Review curriculum contexts and examples
- Survey student attitudes and confidence
- Establish baseline metrics

**Short-term actions (Academic year):**
- Implement inclusive pedagogy training
- Diversify project options and contexts
- Establish female role model programme
- Review option guidance processes

**Medium-term actions (2-3 years):**
- Track cohorts through transitions
- Evaluate intervention effectiveness
- Build sustainable partnerships
- Share successful practice regionally

**Long-term goals (3-5 years):**
- Achieve equitable participation across key stages
- Eliminate confidence gaps
- Establish Computing as inclusive subject within school culture
- Contribute to national good practice

## Conclusion

Addressing the gender gap in Computing requires sustained, evidence-informed action at classroom, department, and whole-school levels. The work of UK researchers like Sue Sentance, practitioner-researchers like Phil Bagge and Mark Dorling, and advocates like Simon Peyton Jones provides robust foundations for effective intervention. Success requires recognising that this is fundamentally a curriculum, pedagogy, and culture challenge rather than a problem with girls themselves. The National Curriculum's breadth—emphasising computational thinking, creativity, and purposeful use of technology alongside technical programming skills—provides the framework for inclusive Computing education when implemented thoughtfully. With appropriate support, resources, and commitment, schools can develop Computing provision that engages, challenges, and inspires all students, regardless of gender.
