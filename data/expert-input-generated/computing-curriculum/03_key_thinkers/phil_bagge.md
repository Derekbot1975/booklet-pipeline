# Phil Bagge

**Full Name:** Philip Bagge

**Current Roles:** 
- Independent Computing education consultant and trainer
- Primary Computing specialist
- Author and curriculum developer

**Known For:** 
- Pioneering work in primary Computing pedagogy
- Development of the Code-It series of resources
- Innovative approaches to teaching computational thinking
- Advocacy for playful, creative Computing education
- Contributions to teacher professional development in Computing

## Biography

Phil Bagge established himself as one of the most influential figures in UK primary Computing education during the period surrounding the introduction of the National Curriculum for Computing in 2014. His career trajectory represents a significant shift from the ICT curriculum to the more rigorous Computing curriculum that emphasised computer science concepts.

### Early Career and Transition

Bagge worked as a primary school teacher and ICT coordinator before transitioning into a specialist role focused on Computing education. His practical classroom experience proved invaluable in understanding the challenges primary teachers faced when the curriculum shifted from ICT (which focused on using technology) to Computing (which included programming, algorithms, and computational thinking). Unlike many advisors who approached Computing from a secondary or computer science background, Bagge's primary education roots enabled him to develop pedagogically appropriate approaches for younger learners.

### Development of Code-It

Between 2013 and 2015, Bagge developed the Code-It series, initially published by Rising Stars (later Hodder Education). This series became one of the most widely adopted Computing schemes of work in UK primary schools. The resources were specifically designed to address the gap in quality primary Computing materials following the curriculum change. Code-It distinguished itself through its emphasis on unplugged activities, creative programming projects, and clear progression in computational thinking concepts.

### Training and Consultancy Work

Bagge has delivered training to thousands of primary teachers across the UK through local authorities, multi-academy trusts, and computing education organisations including the Computing At School (CAS) network. His training sessions are characterised by practical, classroom-ready activities that teachers can implement immediately, combined with deeper pedagogical understanding of Computing concepts.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Unplugged-First Approach to Computational Thinking

Bagge has been a strong advocate for beginning Computing lessons with unplugged activities—teaching computational concepts without computers. This approach addresses several pedagogical challenges simultaneously: it removes the barrier of unfamiliar technology, allows teachers less confident with programming to focus on core concepts, ensures all children can participate regardless of device availability, and develops genuine understanding rather than procedural button-pushing.

His unplugged activities typically use physical manipulation, role-play, and kinaesthetic learning. For example, children might act as robots following precise algorithms written by peers, or physically sort themselves according to different algorithms to understand comparison and efficiency. These activities make abstract concepts concrete and memorable for primary-aged children.

### 2. Creative Computing Through Purposeful Projects

Bagge emphasises that primary Computing should be creative and purposeful rather than purely technical. His Code-It resources consistently frame programming activities within meaningful contexts—creating interactive stories, designing games with specific audiences in mind, or solving problems that matter to children. This approach aligns with primary pedagogy more broadly, where cross-curricular connections and purposeful outcomes enhance engagement and learning.

He argues that teaching programming syntax in isolation is inappropriate for primary children. Instead, projects should have creative goals (such as animating a poem or creating a quiz) with programming as the means to achieve them. This positions Computing as a creative, expressive subject rather than a purely technical one.

### 3. Computational Thinking as a Broader Literacy

Drawing on Jeanette Wing's influential work on computational thinking, Bagge has developed practical classroom approaches to teaching concepts such as decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, and algorithmic thinking. However, he adapts these concepts specifically for primary contexts, creating accessible language and activities suitable for Key Stage 1 and 2.

For instance, his approach to decomposition involves breaking down familiar activities (like making a sandwich) before applying the same thinking to programming problems. Pattern recognition exercises might involve identifying repeating elements in stories or art before recognising loops in code. This scaffolding from familiar to technical contexts makes computational thinking accessible to younger learners and generalist primary teachers.

### 4. Progression in Programming Concepts

Bagge has articulated clear progression pathways for programming in primary schools, beginning with simple sequenced instructions, progressing through selection (if/then statements) and repetition (loops), to variables and more complex logical structures. Crucially, he emphasises revisiting concepts multiple times with increasing sophistication—spiral curriculum design adapted for Computing.

His progression model recognises that children need multiple encounters with programming concepts across different platforms and contexts. A child might first encounter variables through physical sorting activities, then through simple coding toys, before using them in block-based programming environments like Scratch. This varied repetition builds robust understanding.

### 5. Pedagogy for Non-Specialist Teachers

Perhaps Bagge's most significant contribution has been developing pedagogical approaches that enable generalist primary teachers to teach Computing effectively. He recognises that most primary teachers lack computer science backgrounds and may feel anxious about teaching programming. His resources and training therefore emphasise:

- Clear explanations of concepts in accessible language
- Worked examples and model programs teachers can explore
- Anticipated misconceptions and how to address them
- Low-threshold activities that build teacher confidence
- The principle that teachers don't need to know everything before they start teaching Computing

This pedagogical scaffolding for teachers themselves represents sophisticated understanding of professional development and curriculum implementation challenges.

### 6. Playful Exploration and Debugging Culture

Bagge advocates for Computing classrooms where experimentation, play, and "productive failure" are normalised. He emphasises that debugging—finding and fixing errors—should not be seen as failure but as an essential part of Computing. His resources build in time for tinkering, testing, and improving code.

This approach counters anxiety that both teachers and children might feel about "getting it wrong" in Computing. By framing debugging as detective work or problem-solving challenges, Bagge helps create classroom cultures where errors are learning opportunities. He often uses the phrase "bugs are good" to reframe mistakes positively.

### 7. Physical Computing and Tangible Programming

Bagge has championed the use of physical computing devices (such as programmable floor robots, Bee-Bots, and later Crumble controllers and micro:bits) particularly for younger primary children. These tangible interfaces provide immediate, visible feedback and connect abstract code to concrete outcomes.

His pedagogy around physical computing emphasises prediction (what will happen?), observation (what actually happened?), and explanation (why?). This scientific approach to programming helps children develop systematic thinking and debugging skills whilst working with engaging, tactile resources.

### 8. Assessment for Learning in Computing

Bagge has developed practical assessment approaches for primary Computing that move beyond simplistic "can do" checklists. His assessment frameworks focus on:

- Depth of computational thinking rather than just completed programs
- Quality of debugging and problem-solving processes
- Ability to explain code and concepts
- Creativity and purposefulness of projects
- Progression in independence and complexity

He advocates for assessment that captures children's thinking processes, not just final products, recognising that Computing education aims to develop transferable thinking skills.

## Key Publications

### Code-It Series (Rising Stars/Hodder Education)

The **Code-It Primary Programming** series comprises pupil workbooks and teacher handbooks for Years 1-6. Each year group resource includes:

- Detailed lesson plans with timing and organisation guidance
- Unplugged activities introducing concepts
- Scaffolded programming activities across multiple platforms (including Scratch, Logo, and various physical computing devices)
- Differentiation strategies
- Assessment frameworks
- Cross-curricular links

The series was updated to **Code-It Plus** to incorporate newer technologies like micro:bit whilst maintaining the core pedagogical approach. The resources explicitly teach computational thinking vocabulary and concepts whilst building programming skills progressively.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Implementation of the 2014 Computing Curriculum

Bagge's work emerged at a critical juncture when schools were transitioning from the ICT National Curriculum to the new Computing curriculum. The 2014 curriculum, which made programming compulsory from Key Stage 1, created unprecedented demand for resources and training. Bagge's Code-It series became one of the most widely adopted commercial schemes of work, directly shaping how thousands of schools interpreted and implemented the statutory requirements.

His influence extended beyond his own resources through training that reached tens of thousands of teachers. Many local authorities and teaching school alliances commissioned Bagge to deliver Computing professional development, making his pedagogical approaches part of the mainstream implementation strategy across England.

### Computing At School (CAS) Network

Bagge has been an active contributor to the Computing At School network, the grassroots organisation that campaigned for the curriculum change and continues to support Computing teachers. Through CAS community events, online forums, and resource sharing, Bagge's approaches have influenced the broader Computing education community.

His emphasis on primary-appropriate pedagogy helped counter early tendencies to simply scale down secondary Computer Science content for younger children. By demonstrating that rigorous Computing education could be creative, playful, and developmentally appropriate, Bagge influenced the conversation about what primary Computing should look like.

### Professional Development Standards

Bagge's work contributed to broader understanding of what effective Computing professional development requires. His training model—combining conceptual understanding, hands-on practice, classroom-ready resources, and ongoing support—influenced how schools and organisations approached Computing CPD. The recognition that teachers need both subject knowledge and pedagogical content knowledge (how to teach Computing specifically) became increasingly central to professional development provision.

### Assessment Without Levels

Following the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014, schools needed new assessment frameworks for Computing. Bagge's progression frameworks and assessment guidance in Code-It provided practical alternatives that many schools adopted. His focus on assessing computational thinking processes rather than just programming outputs influenced broader assessment practice in Computing.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructionist Learning Theory

Bagge's pedagogy aligns with constructionist learning theory, particularly Seymour Papert's vision of children learning through creating personally meaningful artefacts. His emphasis on creative projects where children make games, stories, and animations reflects constructionist principles. The learning happens through designing, building, debugging, and refining these creations.

However, Bagge adapts pure constructionism for the realities of primary classrooms. His resources provide more structure and scaffolding than open-ended constructionist environments, recognising that young children and non-specialist teachers benefit from guided progression whilst still maintaining creative freedom within projects.

### Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract Progression

Drawing from mathematics pedagogy, Bagge employs concrete-pictorial-abstract progression in Computing teaching. Concepts begin with physical, manipulable activities (concrete), progress to pictorial representations like flowcharts or visual programming languages (pictorial), and eventually reach abstract text-based code or concepts (abstract). This mirrors effective practice from other curriculum areas, making Computing feel more familiar to primary teachers.

For example, teaching algorithms might begin with children physically following or creating instruction sequences (concrete), move to drawing flowcharts or using programmable toys (pictorial), and progress to writing code (abstract). This progression respects cognitive development and builds secure understanding.

### Dialogic Teaching

Bagge emphasises classroom dialogue and collaborative learning in Computing. His lessons typically include paired programming, group debugging challenges, and whole-class discussions about computational thinking strategies. This approach recognises that articulating ideas, explaining code, and critiquing solutions develops deeper understanding than silent individual work.

He encourages teachers to use targeted questioning: "What do you predict will happen?", "Why did the program behave that way?", "How could you make this more efficient?" These questions develop metacognition and systematic thinking.

### Differentiation Through Open-Ended Projects

Rather than creating different tasks for different ability groups, Bagge typically uses open-ended projects with consistent learning objectives but variable outcomes. All children might create a quiz program, but the complexity naturally differentiates based on individual capability. Some children create simple three-question quizzes with basic feedback, whilst others incorporate scoring systems, difficulty levels, and sophisticated graphics.

This approach maintains high expectations whilst acknowledging different starting points. It also mirrors real-world Computing where programmers tackle similar problems with varying sophistication.

### Formative Assessment and Responsive Teaching

Bagge advocates for continuous formative assessment in Computing. Teachers should circulate during practical sessions, observing children's approaches, asking probing questions, and identifying misconceptions to address. His lesson plans include common misconceptions and suggested interventions.

This responsive approach recognises that Computing learning isn't linear—children need different support at different moments. Mini-plenaries where the class pauses to address common challenges or share solutions become key teaching moments.

### Cross-Curricular Integration

Whilst maintaining Computing's integrity as a distinct subject, Bagge demonstrates extensive cross-curricular opportunities. Programming projects create animations for science concepts, data handling activities support geography fieldwork, and logical thinking connects with mathematics. This integration helps primary teachers who typically think holistically about curriculum and makes Computing feel purposeful to children.

However, he cautions against Computing becoming merely instrumental (just a tool for other subjects). The cross-curricular work still teaches genuine Computing concepts—it simply does so within meaningful contexts.

## Impact on Practice

### Influence on Teaching Resources

Bagge's approach influenced subsequent commercial and freely available Computing resources. The pattern of beginning with unplugged activities, using multiple programming platforms, and framing activities as creative projects became standard in primary Computing resources developed after Code-It. Publishers recognised that Bagge's pedagogical model worked effectively in diverse school contexts.

### Teacher Confidence and Subject Leadership

Evaluations of Bagge's training consistently report increased teacher confidence in Computing teaching. His accessible explanations, practical activities, and ongoing resource support help teachers who initially felt anxious about Computing become competent and even enthusiastic practitioners. Many Computing subject leaders cite Bagge's resources as foundational to establishing their school's Computing provision.

### Normalising Programming in Primary Education

Through his work training teachers and developing resources, Bagge contributed to normalising programming as a primary school subject. Before 2014, programming was rare in primary schools and often seen as too difficult. Bagge demonstrated that young children could engage with genuine programming concepts when taught appropriately. This shifted expectations about what primary Computing should encompass.

### Bridging Primary and Secondary Computing

Bagge's clear progression frameworks help secondary schools understand what children should have learned in primary Computing. This supports curriculum continuity, though transition remains challenging. His emphasis on computational thinking concepts (rather than specific programming languages) provides common ground between key stages.

## Critiques and Limitations

### Commercial Context

Whilst Code-It has been widely praised, some educators note that commercial schemes of work can constrain teacher autonomy and creativity. Schools heavily reliant on Code-It resources may struggle to adapt to new technologies or pedagogies emerging after publication. Bagge himself encourages adaptation and creative use of his materials, but purchasing structured schemes can sometimes reduce teacher agency.

### Platform-Specific Content

Some of Bagge's earlier resources focused on specific technologies (particular robots, software versions) that date rapidly in the fast-changing technology landscape. Whilst the underlying pedagogy remains sound, specific lessons may require updating. The Code-It Plus revisions addressed this partially, but technological change continues.

### Assessment Challenges

Despite Bagge's thoughtful assessment frameworks, Computing assessment remains contested. Some critics argue that assessing computational thinking processes is inherently subjective and difficult to standardise. The profession continues debating what rigorous Computing assessment looks like, and no consensus has emerged around Bagge's or any alternative approach.

### Depth vs Breadth in Primary Computing

Some secondary Computing teachers and computer scientists argue that primary Computing (as exemplified by Code-It) spreads too thinly across many concepts and platforms rather than developing depth in fundamental programming. Bagge's approach prioritises engagement, creativity, and broad exposure, which has trade-offs against deep mastery of specific skills.

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

Phil Bagge's influence on UK primary Computing education remains substantial more than a decade after the curriculum change. His pedagogical approaches have become embedded in how many schools teach Computing, even when not using his resources directly. The principles he championed—unplugged activities, creative projects, progression in computational thinking, and pedagogy designed for non-specialist teachers—now characterise mainstream primary Computing teaching.

### Ongoing Development

Bagge continues to develop his practice, incorporating newer technologies like artificial intelligence and machine learning into primary-appropriate activities. His online presence means teachers can access evolving ideas beyond published resources
