# Simon Peyton Jones

**Full Name:** Simon Peyton Jones, FRS, FACM, MAE

**Current Roles:** 
- Epic Games (since 2022)
- Honorary Professor of Computer Science, University of Cambridge
- Past: Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research Cambridge (1998-2022)

**Known For:**
- Co-founding Computing At School (CAS) working group
- Leading advocacy for the 2014 National Curriculum for Computing reform
- Principal designer of the Haskell functional programming language
- Transforming Computing education from ICT to Computer Science in England

---

## Biography and Career History

Simon Peyton Jones read Mathematics and Computer Science at Trinity College, Cambridge, completing his PhD in 1987 under the supervision of David Turner at University College London. His doctoral research focused on implementing functional programming languages, establishing his lifelong commitment to this programming paradigm.

From 1987 to 1998, Peyton Jones worked at the University of Glasgow, where he led the development of the Glasgow Haskell Compiler (GHC), which remains the definitive implementation of Haskell. In 1998, he joined Microsoft Research Cambridge, where he remained for 24 years until 2022, when he moved to Epic Games whilst retaining his academic affiliations.

However, his most significant contribution to UK education began in 2008 when he co-founded Computing At School (CAS) alongside Professor Steve Furber FRS and others. This marked a decisive pivot in his career towards educational advocacy. Frustrated by the declining state of ICT teaching in schools, which he described as teaching how to use Microsoft Word rather than computational thinking, Peyton Jones became the public face of the campaign to reform Computing education.

Between 2010 and 2014, Peyton Jones worked tirelessly with the Department for Education, BCS (The Chartered Institute for IT), Royal Society, and numerous other stakeholders to transform ICT into Computing as a foundational discipline. His influence was instrumental in Michael Gove's January 2012 announcement to "disapply" the ICT curriculum and begin a fundamental review.

He was appointed CBE in 2019 for services to Computer Science and education. He was elected Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 2016, Fellow of the ACM, and Member of the Academia Europaea, recognising both his technical contributions and educational impact.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Computing as a Foundational Discipline, Not ICT Skills

Peyton Jones consistently argued that Computing should be recognised as a rigorous academic discipline alongside Mathematics and Science, not merely vocational training in office applications. In his 2011 paper "Computing at School: The State of the Nation", he articulated that Computing encompasses:

- **Computer Science**: the foundational principles of information and computation
- **Information Technology**: creating and deploying digital solutions
- **Digital Literacy**: using technology confidently and safely

He emphasised that the old ICT curriculum focused almost entirely on digital literacy whilst neglecting the creative, intellectual challenge of programming and computational thinking. His vision, articulated through CAS, was that every child should understand that computers are machines they can control and program, not merely consume content from.

This conceptual framework directly informed the structure of the 2014 National Curriculum for Computing, which explicitly organises content around these three strands.

### 2. Programming as Literacy for the 21st Century

Peyton Jones championed the view that programming should be considered a form of literacy comparable to reading and writing. In numerous keynote addresses and papers, including "Teaching Creative Computer Science" (2012), he argued that:

- Programming develops problem-solving skills applicable across disciplines
- Computational thinking enables students to break down complex problems systematically
- Creating with code is deeply empowering and creative, akin to composition in music or writing
- The UK's economic competitiveness depends on a digitally skilled workforce

He rejected the notion that programming was only for future software engineers, instead positioning it as essential general education. His advocacy emphasised starting early—from Key Stage 1—when children are naturally curious and unafraid of experimentation.

### 3. Functional Programming as a Teaching Paradigm

Drawing on his expertise in Haskell and functional programming, Peyton Jones promoted functional programming as pedagogically superior for introducing computational concepts. His arguments, developed in papers like "Composing Contracts: An Adventure in Financial Engineering" (2000) and educational talks, included:

- **Referential transparency**: functions always produce the same output for the same input, making reasoning easier
- **Immutability**: avoiding mutable state reduces cognitive load for beginners
- **Composition**: building complex programs from simple, testable components
- **Mathematical foundations**: connecting computing to mathematical thinking

Whilst acknowledging that imperative languages dominate industry, he argued that functional concepts (pure functions, higher-order functions, pattern matching) provide clearer mental models for learners. This influenced his support for languages like Scratch (which embeds functional concepts) and Python (which supports functional programming styles) in schools.

### 4. Teacher Professional Development as Critical Infrastructure

Peyton Jones recognised early that curriculum reform would fail without addressing the teacher shortage in Computing. He was instrumental in establishing the Network of Teaching Excellence in Computer Science (NoE), funded by government and operational from 2014 onwards.

His philosophy emphasised:

- **Subject knowledge**: teachers need deep understanding of computational concepts, not just pedagogy
- **Communities of practice**: CAS's grassroots model of Master Teachers supporting peers
- **Continuous learning**: Computing evolves rapidly, requiring ongoing professional development
- **Academic credibility**: working with universities to establish Computing education research

CAS grew to over 35,000 members by 2016, with regional hubs providing face-to-face professional development. Peyton Jones frequently delivered keynotes at CAS conferences, maintaining direct connection with classroom teachers.

### 5. Evidence-Based Policy Through the Royal Society

Peyton Jones chaired the Royal Society's Education Committee's report "Shut Down or Restart?" (2012), which provided the evidence base for curriculum reform. The report:

- Documented the crisis in ICT teaching through teacher and student surveys
- Demonstrated declining participation in Computing qualifications
- Highlighted employer concerns about digital skills shortages
- Compared England unfavourably with international Computing education

This rigorous, evidence-based approach gave credibility to advocacy efforts and influenced policymakers. Peyton Jones understood that emotional appeals alone would not drive systemic change—data and academic authority were essential.

### 6. Accessibility and Inclusion in Computing

Throughout his advocacy, Peyton Jones emphasised that Computing should be for all students, not an elite minority. He specifically addressed:

- **Gender disparities**: supporting initiatives to encourage girls into Computing
- **Socioeconomic barriers**: ensuring state schools had resources for practical Computing
- **Special educational needs**: advocating for inclusive pedagogies
- **Cultural perceptions**: challenging stereotypes of Computing as antisocial or exclusively for "geeks"

His vision for the National Curriculum explicitly positioned Computing as a core subject for every student, with entitlement to high-quality teaching regardless of school context.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### The 2014 National Curriculum for Computing

Peyton Jones's influence on the National Curriculum for Computing (introduced September 2014 in England) was profound and direct. Working with the expert panel assembled by the Department for Education, he helped establish:

**Key Stage 1 (ages 5-7):**
- Understanding algorithms and programming simple sequences
- Creating and debugging simple programs
- Logical reasoning to predict program behaviour

**Key Stage 2 (ages 7-11):**
- Designing and writing programs to accomplish specific goals
- Using variables and sequence, selection, and repetition
- Detecting and correcting errors in algorithms and programs

**Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14):**
- Using two or more programming languages, one textual
- Understanding Boolean logic and application in programming
- Developing computational thinking skills

**Key Stage 4 (ages 14-16):**
- GCSE Computer Science specifications reformed to emphasise programming and computational thinking

This represented a fundamental shift from the previous ICT curriculum's focus on using software to creating software and understanding foundational principles.

### Computing At School (CAS) Working Group

Founded in 2008, CAS became the grassroots engine of curriculum reform under Peyton Jones's leadership. Key achievements included:

- **Membership growth**: from a handful of enthusiasts to over 35,000 members
- **Master Teacher Programme**: training expert teachers to support peers
- **Barefoot Computing**: primary resources developed with BT support
- **Regional hubs**: face-to-face communities across England
- **Academic partnerships**: collaborations with universities for research and teacher development

CAS operated as a formal working group under BCS oversight, giving it institutional credibility whilst maintaining grassroots energy. Peyton Jones served as chair during critical years (2009-2013), stepping back as the organisation matured.

### Network of Teaching Excellence in Computer Science

Following curriculum implementation, Peyton Jones advocated for sustained investment in teacher development. The Department for Education established the Network of Teaching Excellence in Computer Science (NoE) in 2014, initially funded for three years and subsequently extended.

The NoE model, influenced by Peyton Jones's vision, included:

- University-based regional centres providing subject-specific CPD
- Online resources and face-to-face training
- Research into effective Computing pedagogy
- Links between schools and higher education

By 2020, the NoE (subsequently renamed National Centre for Computing Education) had trained over 17,000 teachers, directly addressing the workforce challenge Peyton Jones identified.

### GCSE and A-Level Reform

Peyton Jones worked with examination boards and Ofqual to ensure qualifications aligned with the reformed curriculum. GCSE Computer Science specifications (introduced 2016) included:

- Mandatory programming assessment (50% practical programming)
- Computational thinking and problem-solving emphasis
- Removal of vocational ICT content
- Rigorous academic content comparable to Science GCSEs

A-Level Computer Science specifications were similarly reformed, with Peyton Jones advocating for functional programming concepts alongside imperative paradigms.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Principles for Teaching Computing

Peyton Jones articulated clear pedagogical principles through his writing and advocacy:

**1. Start with Concrete, Move to Abstract**  
Programming should begin with tangible experiences (e.g., Scratch, physical computing with micro:bit) before progressing to abstract concepts. Young children learn through making visible things happen.

**2. Emphasise Problem-Solving Over Syntax**  
The goal is computational thinking—decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithms—not memorising language syntax. Languages are tools for expressing solutions.

**3. Encourage Experimentation and Failure**  
Computing is learned through trial and error. Classroom culture should celebrate debugging and learning from mistakes, not penalise incorrect code.

**4. Make It Creative and Personal**  
Students should create programs that matter to them—games, animations, tools—not merely complete exercises. Ownership drives engagement and deeper learning.

**5. Connect to Other Disciplines**  
Computing amplifies learning in Mathematics, Science, Art, Music, and Humanities. Cross-curricular projects demonstrate Computing's broad applicability.

### Recommended Progression

Peyton Jones supported a developmental progression:

**Early Years and KS1**: Unplugged activities, programmable toys (Bee-Bot), visual programming (ScratchJr)

**KS2**: Block-based languages (Scratch), physical computing (micro:bit), introduction to text-based syntax (Python)

**KS3**: Text-based programming (Python), web technologies (HTML/CSS/JavaScript), data structures and algorithms

**KS4-5**: Advanced programming concepts, software engineering practices, theoretical computer science

This progression balances accessibility with intellectual challenge, ensuring all students develop foundational understanding whilst enabling specialisation.

### Assessment Philosophy

Peyton Jones advocated for formative assessment focused on understanding, not rote learning:

- **Live coding**: teachers modelling problem-solving processes
- **Pair programming**: collaborative learning and peer explanation
- **Code review**: students critiquing and improving each other's work
- **Project-based assessment**: demonstrating capability through creating substantial programs

He was critical of assessment that emphasised recall over application, arguing that Computing uniquely enables creative demonstration of understanding.

## Legacy and Ongoing Influence

### Impact on UK Education System

Peyton Jones's advocacy fundamentally transformed English education. By 2020:

- Computing was established as a foundational subject in the National Curriculum
- GCSE Computer Science entries exceeded 83,000 annually
- Primary schools taught programming from Year 1
- Computing was recognised by universities and employers as academically rigorous
- Government invested over £80 million in teacher training and resources (2014-2020)

The 2014 curriculum reform is widely regarded as the most significant change in Computing education in a generation, with Peyton Jones acknowledged as the key individual driving change.

### International Recognition

Peyton Jones's work influenced Computing education internationally:

- **Scotland**: adopted Computing Science as core curriculum
- **Wales**: developed Digital Competence Framework informed by English reforms
- **Australia**: Digital Technologies curriculum drew on CAS resources and research
- **United States**: advocacy groups cited UK reforms as exemplar

His presentations at international conferences (SIGCSE, ITiCSE, WCCE) disseminated lessons from the UK experience globally.

### Critiques and Debates

Not all aspects of the curriculum reform were uncontroversial. Critics noted:

- **Implementation challenges**: teacher confidence and resources varied significantly
- **Assessment burden**: programming assessment in GCSE proved challenging to standardise
- **Gender gap**: participation in GCSE Computer Science remained male-dominated
- **Curriculum breadth**: some argued insufficient attention to data, networks, and human-computer interaction

Peyton Jones acknowledged these challenges, emphasising that curriculum reform was a multi-decade project requiring sustained commitment and iteration.

### Continued Advocacy

Even after moving to Epic Games in 2022, Peyton Jones remains active in Computing education through:

- Continued involvement with CAS and professional development initiatives
- Research collaborations on Computing education pedagogy
- Public speaking and advocacy for sustained investment in education
- Mentoring the next generation of education researchers and advocates
