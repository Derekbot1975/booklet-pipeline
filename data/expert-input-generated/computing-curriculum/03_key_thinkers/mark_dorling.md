# Mark Dorling

**Full Name:** Mark Dorling

**Current Role:** Independent Computing education consultant, former Head of Innovation and Learning at Computing At School (CAS)

**Known For:** Co-founding Barefoot Computing, developing computational thinking progression frameworks for primary education, pioneering work in making Computing accessible to primary school teachers, and establishing pedagogical approaches for teaching Computing across Key Stages 1 and 2.

## Biography and Career History

Mark Dorling has been one of the most influential figures in UK Computing education, particularly at primary level, during the transition from ICT to Computing following the 2014 curriculum change. His career trajectory positioned him uniquely to bridge the gap between computer science academia and primary classroom practice.

Dorling began his career as a primary school teacher before moving into Computing education leadership roles. He served as Head of Innovation and Learning at Computing At School (CAS), the grassroots organisation established by the British Computer Society (BCS) and supported by Microsoft, Google, and other technology companies. In this capacity, he worked alongside Professor Simon Peyton Jones and other leading figures in the Computing education reform movement.

His most significant contribution came through co-founding Barefoot Computing in 2014, a professional development programme specifically designed to support primary teachers in delivering the new Computing curriculum. Barefoot was developed in partnership between CAS and BT, recognising that primary teachers—many of whom had no computer science background—needed accessible, pedagogically sound resources and training.

Dorling's work has been characterised by a deep understanding of both Computing as a discipline and primary pedagogy. He recognised early that simply translating secondary Computing approaches would not work for younger learners, and instead developed age-appropriate frameworks that maintained academic rigour whilst being accessible to generalist primary teachers.

He has worked extensively with universities, including collaborative research with King's College London and other institutions examining effective pedagogies for Computing education. His influence extends through CPD delivery, resource development, and policy consultation work with the Department for Education.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Computational Thinking as a Progression Framework

Dorling, working closely with Pete Kemp and colleagues, developed one of the UK's most widely used computational thinking progression frameworks. This framework, published through Barefoot Computing, identifies six core concepts of computational thinking: logic, algorithms, decomposition, patterns, abstraction, and evaluation. Crucially, Dorling's framework adds five approaches: tinkering, creating, debugging, persevering, and collaborating.

This concepts-and-approaches model proved revolutionary for primary teachers because it provided concrete learning objectives that could be observed and assessed. Unlike earlier, more abstract definitions of computational thinking (such as Jeannette Wing's influential 2006 paper), Dorling's framework translated theoretical constructs into classroom-observable behaviours. For example, rather than simply stating that children should understand 'decomposition', the framework specifies progressive stages: recognising that problems can be broken down, beginning to break problems down into smaller parts, and independently decomposing problems effectively.

The framework's genius lies in its applicability across both plugged (using technology) and unplugged (without computers) activities, making it accessible to teachers regardless of their school's technological resources.

### 2. Unplugged Computing for Primary Learners

Dorling became a leading advocate for unplugged Computing activities in UK primary education, building on international work by Tim Bell, Mike Fellows, and Ian Witten from Computer Science Unplugged. However, Dorling's contribution was to systematically embed unplugged approaches within a coherent curriculum progression rather than treating them as optional enrichment activities.

He argued that unplugged activities serve multiple purposes: they remove the cognitive load of learning interface conventions simultaneously with Computing concepts; they make Computing accessible in schools with limited device access; and they help teachers recognise that Computing is a discipline of thinking, not merely technology use.

Dorling developed numerous unplugged resources through Barefoot, including activities for teaching algorithms through treasure maps and dance sequences, binary numbers through card sorting, and networks through physical role-play. His pedagogical approach emphasised that unplugged activities should be followed by 'plugged' application to ensure transfer of understanding.

### 3. Primary Computing Pedagogical Patterns

Dorling identified and articulated several pedagogical patterns specific to effective primary Computing teaching. These include:

**PRIMM adaptation for primary:** Whilst PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) was developed for secondary programming education by Sue Sentence and colleagues, Dorling adapted similar principles for younger learners, emphasising the importance of working with existing code before creating from scratch.

**Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract in Computing:** Borrowing from mathematics education (particularly Singapore maths approaches), Dorling advocated for physical manipulation (unplugged activities), visual representation (block-based programming), and then abstract understanding (concepts applied across contexts).

**Use-Modify-Create progression:** Dorling's framework, developed through Barefoot, suggests learners should first use existing programs or algorithms, then modify them with teacher support, before independently creating their own. This challenged the prevalent 'blank screen' approach where children were expected to create from nothing.

### 4. Subject Knowledge Development for Non-Specialist Teachers

Recognising that most primary teachers in England are generalists teaching across the curriculum, Dorling pioneered approaches to subject knowledge development that acknowledged teachers' starting points whilst maintaining subject integrity. His Barefoot resources explicitly addressed teachers' potential anxieties about Computing, providing subject knowledge videos, glossaries, and explanatory content alongside pupil-facing activities.

Dorling's approach rejected the notion that teachers needed degree-level computer science knowledge to teach primary Computing effectively. Instead, he identified core knowledge boundaries appropriate for primary teaching and developed resources that built teacher confidence incrementally. For example, Barefoot's approach to teaching algorithms begins with everyday procedures familiar to teachers (recipes, morning routines) before progressing to more formal algorithmic representations.

This pedagogical content knowledge development model recognised that effective primary teachers needed to understand Computing concepts sufficiently deeply to answer children's questions and identify misconceptions, but not necessarily to the depth required of secondary specialist teachers.

### 5. Cross-Curricular Computing Without Compromising Discipline Integrity

Dorling navigated a careful path regarding cross-curricular Computing. Whilst recognising primary schools' integrated curriculum approaches, he consistently argued that Computing's disciplinary concepts must remain visible and explicit rather than being subsumed into other subjects.

His Barefoot resources modelled cross-curricular connections (creating animations in literacy, using data handling in science, programming floor robots in geography) whilst maintaining clear Computing learning objectives. This represented a middle ground between those advocating for discrete Computing lessons and those suggesting Computing should be taught entirely through other subjects.

Dorling emphasised that computational thinking, whilst transferable, must first be learned through Computing contexts before transfer can occur. This evidence-informed position drew on research by Lister and colleagues questioning assumptions about computational thinking's automatic transferability.

### 6. Assessment Beyond Programming

Dorling contributed significantly to discourse around assessing primary Computing holistically rather than focusing narrowly on programming capability. Through Barefoot and CAS resources, he developed assessment guidance emphasising observation of pupils applying computational thinking concepts across different contexts.

His assessment approach included pupil self-assessment tools, teacher observation frameworks, and portfolio approaches documenting progression across Computing's three strands (computer science, information technology, and digital literacy). This represented a more sophisticated model than simple programming skill checklists that dominated some early Computing assessment materials.

## Key Publications and Resources

### Barefoot Computing Resources (2014-present)

Dorling served as lead author and pedagogical architect for the comprehensive Barefoot Computing resource collection, available at barefootcomputing.org (later integrated into various platforms). These resources include:

- Complete lesson plans for Years 1-6 aligned to the English Computing curriculum
- Computational thinking progression framework documentation
- Subject knowledge videos for teachers explaining algorithms, programming, data, networks, and other core concepts
- Unplugged activity guides with detailed facilitation notes
- Assessment guidance and progression documentation

The Barefoot resources became among the most widely used primary Computing materials in England, with tens of thousands of teachers accessing them annually.

### Computational Thinking Progression Framework

**Dorling, M. and Kemp, P. (with contributions from others)** "Barefoot Computing Computational Thinking Progression Framework" (2014). This framework document identified six concepts and five approaches, each with detailed progression statements. The framework has been adapted and adopted by numerous schools, academy trusts, and local authorities as their Computing curriculum foundation.

### Computing At School Primary Guidance

As CAS Head of Innovation and Learning, Dorling contributed to numerous CAS publications including:

- **"Computing in the National Curriculum: A Guide for Primary Teachers"** (CAS/Naace, 2014) - Dorling contributed sections on progression, pedagogy, and assessment for primary phases.

- **CAS QuickStart Computing Guides** - Dorling authored primary-focused quick-start guides helping teachers understand curriculum requirements and teaching approaches.

### Academic and Professional Publications

**Dorling, M. and Walker, D.** "Computing Progression Pathways" - Work developing progression criteria later incorporated into CAS/Computing At School assessment frameworks.

**Contributions to "A Curriculum Framework for Computing: Key Stage 3"** (Computing At School Working Group, though primarily secondary-focused, Dorling contributed transition guidance from Key Stage 2).

Dorling has presented at numerous conferences including BETT, the CAS Conference, and Raspberry Pi Foundation events, often co-presenting with Pete Kemp, Clare Sutcliffe (founder of Code Club), and other Computing education leaders.

### Online Professional Learning Content

Dorling created extensive video content for teacher professional development, including the Barefoot video series explaining Computing concepts through primary-appropriate contexts. These videos modelled effective explanation techniques that teachers could adapt for their own classrooms.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Shaping the 2014 Computing Curriculum Implementation

Whilst Dorling was not directly involved in writing the 2014 Computing Programme of Study, his work through CAS and Barefoot proved crucial in translating the statutory curriculum into classroom practice. The official Programme of Study is notably brief and high-level; Dorling's progression frameworks and resources provided the interpretative layer schools needed.

His computational thinking framework influenced how many schools structured their curriculum, with the six concepts becoming organisational principles for schemes of learning across numerous academy trusts and local authorities. Schools using Barefoot resources effectively received Dorling's curriculum interpretation, making his influence on enacted curriculum substantial even if not formally policy-mandated.

### Primary Teacher Training and Professional Development

Dorling's frameworks and resources became embedded in primary teacher training. Many Initial Teacher Education programmes incorporated Barefoot materials into their Computing education modules. His work provided ITT tutors—often themselves not Computing specialists—with structured content for preparing new primary teachers.

Through CAS Master Teacher and Network of Excellence programmes, Dorling's pedagogical approaches spread via cascade training models. Lead teachers trained by Dorling and colleagues subsequently trained thousands of other primary teachers, multiplying his influence across the system.

### Ofsted Inspection Framework Interpretation

Whilst not directly influencing Ofsted policy, Dorling's progression frameworks provided schools with evidential basis for demonstrating Computing curriculum quality during inspections. Schools could point to clear progression in computational thinking concepts aligned to Dorling's frameworks when explaining their Computing provision to inspectors.

### International Influence

Dorling's work, particularly the computational thinking framework and unplugged approaches, has influenced Computing education internationally. The Barefoot resources have been accessed globally, and Dorling has presented in various countries about primary Computing pedagogy. His work contributed to international discourse about making computer science accessible in primary/elementary education during the global "computing for all" movement of the 2010s.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructionist Foundations with Structured Guidance

Dorling's pedagogy reflects constructionist principles (building on Seymour Papert's work) where learners construct understanding through making. However, unlike pure discovery learning approaches, Dorling advocated for carefully structured activities with clear learning intentions.

His resources model explicit teaching of concepts followed by guided practice and then independent application—a sequence familiar to primary teachers from literacy and mathematics teaching. This made Computing pedagogy feel less alien to generalist primary teachers than approaches advocating pure exploration.

### Teacher as Lead Learner

Recognising many primary teachers' limited Computing background, Dorling promoted a "teacher as lead learner" model where educators learn alongside pupils. His resources explicitly support this approach through clear subject knowledge explanations and suggested teacher preparation activities.

However, Dorling maintained that teachers must remain the pedagogical expert—understanding how to structure learning, identify misconceptions, and facilitate progression—even when pupils might have greater technical familiarity with certain tools.

### Language and Vocabulary Development

Dorling emphasised explicit vocabulary instruction in Computing, providing child-friendly definitions and consistent terminology throughout Barefoot resources. He recognised that Computing's technical vocabulary could be a barrier but argued that avoiding correct terminology was counterproductive.

His approach involved introducing technical terms with clear explanations and visual representations, then providing repeated opportunities to use vocabulary in context. This mirrored effective literacy teaching practices, making the approach accessible to primary teachers.

### Inclusive Computing Pedagogy

Dorling's resources reflect commitment to inclusive Computing education. Unplugged activities support learners with various accessibility needs; varied activity types (individual, paired, collaborative) accommodate different learning preferences; and progression frameworks allow teachers to identify appropriate challenge levels regardless of year group.

His work consistently presented Computing as for all children, not only those perceived as "good with computers." This democratic vision aligned with the policy intention behind the 2014 curriculum reform.

### Assessment for Learning Integration

Dorling advocated for integrating assessment naturally within Computing activities rather than through separate testing. His resources include questioning prompts, observation focuses, and peer/self-assessment opportunities embedded within lessons.

This formative assessment approach helps teachers identify conceptual understanding beyond surface-level completion of activities—for example, distinguishing between a child who has followed instructions to create a program and one who understands the algorithmic concepts applied.

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

Mark Dorling's influence on UK primary Computing education remains substantial despite his reduced public profile in recent years. The Barefoot resources continue to be widely used, and his computational thinking framework has been incorporated into numerous commercial schemes of learning and school curricula.

His work addressed a critical need during Computing education reform—providing accessible, pedagogically sound resources for generalist primary teachers. Without this infrastructure, the 2014 curriculum implementation might have been significantly less successful in primary schools.

Dorling's emphasis on unplugged activities, progression frameworks, and computational thinking concepts has become mainstream in UK primary Computing education. These approaches, innovative when introduced, are now standard practice in many schools.

His career exemplifies successful translation between academic computer science, policy requirements, and classroom practice. Dorling combined sufficient technical understanding with primary pedagogical expertise and practical resource development skills—a rare combination that enabled his significant impact.

Current challenges in Computing education—including teacher confidence, assessment validity, and curriculum sustainability—remain areas where Dorling's frameworks and approaches continue to offer valuable foundations. His work provided not just short-term implementation support but enduring pedagogical principles for primary Computing education.
