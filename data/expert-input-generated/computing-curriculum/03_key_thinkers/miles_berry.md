# Miles Berry

**Full Name:** Miles Berry

**Current Role:** Principal Lecturer in Computing Education at the University of Roehampton, London

**Known For:** Influential Computing education researcher, curriculum developer, and advocate for primary Computing education in England; expert in Scratch pedagogy; significant contributor to the development of the 2014 National Curriculum for Computing; pioneer of the #CASChat initiative; leading voice in computational thinking and creative computing pedagogy

---

## Biography and Career History

Miles Berry is one of the most influential figures in Computing education in the United Kingdom, particularly recognised for his work in primary Computing and teacher professional development. Currently serving as Principal Lecturer in Computing Education at the University of Roehampton, Berry has been instrumental in shaping how Computing is taught in English schools since the subject replaced ICT in 2014.

Berry's career in education spans multiple decades. Prior to his university role, he worked as a primary school headteacher, bringing valuable classroom experience to his academic work. This combination of practical teaching experience and academic research positions him uniquely to bridge theory and practice in Computing education. His time as a headteacher gave him first-hand understanding of the challenges schools face in implementing new curricula, particularly in subjects requiring specialist knowledge.

At the University of Roehampton, Berry leads initial teacher training programmes in Computing and directs continuing professional development initiatives for serving teachers. His work focuses on developing pedagogical approaches that make Computing accessible and engaging, particularly for primary-aged children and for teachers without specialist Computing backgrounds. He has been instrumental in Roehampton's development as a centre of excellence for Computing education.

Berry has been deeply involved with Computing At School (CAS), the grassroots organisation that campaigned for the introduction of Computing as a subject. He was a member of the CAS Curriculum and Assessment working group and contributed significantly to policy discussions that led to the 2014 curriculum reform. His blog (milesberry.net) has become an essential resource for Computing teachers, offering regular insights into pedagogy, curriculum design, and educational technology.

His international influence extends through consultancy work, conference presentations, and collaborations with organisations including the Raspberry Pi Foundation, where he has contributed to educational initiatives and research projects. Berry has also worked with examination boards and textbook publishers, helping to shape assessment frameworks and learning resources aligned with the Computing curriculum.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. Computational Thinking in Primary Education

Berry has been a leading advocate for introducing computational thinking concepts to young children, arguing that these skills are foundational literacies for the 21st century. He emphasises that computational thinking—including concepts such as algorithms, decomposition, pattern recognition, and abstraction—should not be taught in isolation but integrated meaningfully into creative, engaging activities.

His approach rejects the notion that young children must wait until secondary school to encounter 'proper' Computing concepts. Instead, Berry demonstrates through his research and teaching materials how concepts like sequence, selection, and repetition can be introduced through play-based learning, storytelling, and cross-curricular projects. He advocates for 'unplugged' activities (computing concepts taught without computers) alongside programming, ensuring that understanding precedes syntax.

Berry's framework for computational thinking in primary schools emphasises making abstract concepts concrete through physical manipulation, visual representations, and collaborative problem-solving. He argues that primary-aged children naturally engage in algorithmic thinking when they follow recipes, create dance sequences, or design games—teachers simply need to make these connections explicit.

### 2. Scratch as a Pedagogical Tool

Berry is recognised as one of the UK's foremost experts on using Scratch in education. His work goes beyond simply teaching the mechanics of Scratch programming to examining how this visual programming environment supports computational thinking and creative expression. He has extensively researched how Scratch's blocks-based interface reduces cognitive load, allowing children to focus on logic and creativity rather than syntax errors.

His pedagogical approach to Scratch emphasises the importance of creative projects that allow personal expression whilst teaching Computing concepts. Berry advocates for 'tinkering' and exploration, encouraging teachers to allow children time to play with code, make mistakes, and discover solutions independently. He has developed numerous schemes of work showing how Scratch can be used to create animations, interactive stories, simulations, and games that connect to other curriculum areas.

Berry has written extensively about progression in Scratch, outlining how teachers can scaffold learning from simple sequences to complex programs involving custom blocks, variables, and data structures. His work addresses common misconceptions children develop when learning programming and provides strategies for formative assessment in practical Computing lessons.

He has also championed ScratchJr for early years and Key Stage 1, demonstrating how even the youngest children can engage with programming concepts through age-appropriate interfaces. His research includes case studies of primary schools successfully implementing whole-school Scratch curricula, providing evidence for the effectiveness of early programming education.

### 3. Curriculum Design Principles for Computing

Berry's contribution to Computing curriculum design extends from national policy to individual lesson plans. He was involved in discussions that shaped the 2014 National Curriculum for Computing in England, advocating for a balance between computer science, information technology, and digital literacy—the three strands that structure the curriculum.

His curriculum design philosophy emphasises:

- **Progression and continuity**: Clear developmental pathways from EYFS through to Key Stage 3, with concepts introduced at age-appropriate levels and revisited with increasing depth
- **Practical, project-based learning**: Learning Computing through making things, not passive consumption of knowledge
- **Cross-curricular connections**: Computing as a lens through which to explore other subjects, from mathematical algorithms to digital art
- **Inclusivity**: Ensuring all children, regardless of gender, background, or prior experience, can succeed in Computing

Berry has published detailed guidance on assessment in Computing, arguing for assessment approaches that recognise both technical skills and computational thinking. He advocates for portfolio-based assessment that captures the iterative, creative process of programming, not just finished products. His work addresses the challenge of assessing Computing in primary schools where teachers may lack specialist expertise.

### 4. Teacher Professional Development

Recognising that the success of the Computing curriculum depends on teacher confidence and subject knowledge, Berry has dedicated significant effort to teacher professional development. He understands that many primary teachers approach Computing with anxiety, having trained in the ICT era when the focus was on using technology rather than understanding its underlying principles.

Berry's CPD programmes at Roehampton and through CAS focus on building both subject knowledge and pedagogical content knowledge. He emphasises hands-on learning where teachers themselves program, debug, and create, experiencing the learning process from the student's perspective. His CPD materials explicitly address common teacher concerns, providing practical strategies and resources that can be immediately implemented in classrooms.

He has championed the #CASChat initiative on Twitter (now X), which became a vital professional learning community for Computing teachers. These regular online discussions created a supportive space where teachers could share resources, discuss pedagogical challenges, and learn from peers. Berry has facilitated numerous #CASChat sessions on topics ranging from assessment to inclusion.

His research into teacher professional development examines what makes CPD effective in Computing. He has studied the impact of subject knowledge enhancement, the role of collaboration in teacher learning, and barriers to implementing Computing in primary schools. His findings emphasise the importance of ongoing, subject-specific professional development rather than one-off training sessions.

### 5. Open Education and Open Source

Berry is a strong advocate for open educational resources and open-source software in education. He argues that the culture of sharing and collaboration in the open-source community aligns perfectly with educational values. His blog and online repositories make his teaching materials, schemes of work, and research freely available to educators worldwide.

He has promoted the use of open-source tools like Scratch, Scratch Jr, Python, and Sonic Pi in schools, partly for economic reasons but primarily because these tools embody principles of transparency and modifiability. Berry argues that children should understand and be able to modify the software they use, not be passive consumers of closed commercial products.

His commitment to open education extends to his contributions to Creative Commons-licensed resources, including materials for the Raspberry Pi Foundation and CAS. He has written about the pedagogical benefits of children sharing their code with peers and the global Scratch community, developing collaborative skills and learning through examining others' work.

### 6. Digital Literacy and Online Safety

Whilst Berry's primary focus is computational thinking and programming, he recognises the importance of digital literacy and online safety as essential components of Computing education. He advocates for an empowering approach to online safety that equips children with critical thinking skills rather than simply restricting access or creating fear.

Berry has written about teaching children to evaluate online information critically, understand how algorithms shape their online experiences, and make informed decisions about privacy and data sharing. His approach connects online safety to computer science concepts, helping children understand how search engines rank results, how recommendation algorithms work, and how their data is collected and used.

He emphasises that digital literacy should not be taught separately from Computing but integrated throughout the curriculum. When children create their own websites, apps, or games, they naturally encounter questions about user experience, accessibility, and ethical design—opportunities for meaningful discussions about digital citizenship.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### The 2014 National Curriculum for Computing

Berry's influence on the development of England's 2014 National Curriculum for Computing was substantial, though he worked as part of a broader community including CAS, BCS (British Computer Society), and other expert groups. His particular contribution focused on ensuring the curriculum was accessible and appropriate for primary schools.

He advocated for:
- **Clear progression**: Age-appropriate expectations from Reception through Year 6, ensuring concepts were introduced when developmentally appropriate
- **Balance**: Maintaining the three strands (computer science, information technology, digital literacy) with equal emphasis
- **Flexibility**: A curriculum framework that allowed teacher creativity rather than prescribing specific software or approaches
- **Ambitious but achievable expectations**: Raising the bar from the old ICT curriculum whilst recognising the starting point of most primary teachers

Berry's interpretations of the curriculum, published through his blog and CAS, helped clarify expectations for schools. His analysis of each programme of study statement, with practical examples of how these might be addressed, became unofficial guidance for many schools developing their Computing schemes of work.

### CAS Primary

Berry was instrumental in developing CAS Primary, the strand of Computing At School focused on primary education. He helped establish networks of primary Computing specialists, developed resources specifically for primary teachers, and advocated for primary Computing in policy discussions.

His work with CAS Primary included:
- Leading the development of subject knowledge materials for primary teachers
- Facilitating CAS Primary conferences and regional meetings
- Contributing to the CAS Master Teacher programme, developing expertise among lead teachers
- Advocating for appropriate assessment frameworks that recognise primary children's achievements

### Examination Board Consultancy

Berry has worked with examination boards including OCR to develop specifications and assessment materials for Computing GCSEs and A-levels. His primary expertise has informed discussions about prerequisite knowledge and skills from Key Stage 2, ensuring continuity and progression into secondary Computing.

### International Influence

Berry's work has influenced Computing curricula internationally. He has consulted for:
- **Ireland**: Contributing to discussions about Ireland's Junior Cycle computer science specification
- **International schools**: Advising on Computing curriculum development in British curriculum schools worldwide
- **Research collaborations**: Working with international researchers on comparative studies of Computing education

His blog and publications reach a global audience, and his research is cited in international Computing education literature. He has presented at conferences across Europe, North America, and Asia, sharing England's experience of curriculum reform and learning from international approaches.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructionist Learning

Berry's pedagogical approach is deeply influenced by constructionist learning theory, derived from Seymour Papert's work. He emphasises learning through making—children construct knowledge by creating personally meaningful artefacts (programs, animations, games) that they can share with others and reflect upon.

This approach manifests in his advocacy for:
- **Project-based learning**: Extended projects where children apply Computing concepts to create something of their own design
- **Creative briefs rather than tutorials**: Giving children challenges with constraints but allowing creative freedom in solutions
- **Iteration and debugging**: Embracing mistakes as learning opportunities, explicitly teaching debugging as a problem-solving skill
- **Sharing and reflection**: Opportunities for children to present their work, explain their thinking, and learn from peers

### Scaffolded Progression

Berry advocates for careful scaffolding that gradually increases complexity whilst building on prior knowledge. His schemes of work demonstrate this principle, showing how:
- **Concepts are introduced progressively**: Beginning with concrete, physical representations before moving to abstract code
- **Skills are developed systematically**: Each project introduces one or two new concepts whilst consolidating previous learning
- **Support is gradually withdrawn**: Moving from teacher-directed examples to guided exploration to independent problem-solving

### Active, Collaborative Learning

Berry's pedagogical approach emphasises active participation and collaboration. He recommends:
- **Pair programming**: Two children working at one computer, taking turns as 'driver' and 'navigator'
- **Peer debugging**: Children helping each other solve problems, explaining their thinking
- **Show and tell**: Regular opportunities to share work-in-progress, give and receive feedback
- **Class discussions**: Making computational thinking visible through whole-class conversations about algorithms and problem-solving strategies

### Cross-Curricular Integration

Berry frequently demonstrates how Computing can be integrated meaningfully with other curriculum areas, rather than taught in isolation. Examples from his work include:
- **Literacy**: Creating interactive stories, animations of poems, choose-your-own-adventure games
- **Mathematics**: Programming simulations to explore probability, creating geometric art with code, using spreadsheets to analyse data
- **Science**: Programming models of scientific phenomena, collecting and analysing data with sensors
- **Art and music**: Creating digital art, programming music with Sonic Pi, designing animations

This approach ensures Computing is not seen as a separate, technical subject but as a tool for creativity and learning across the curriculum.

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Berry has written extensively about ensuring Computing is accessible to all learners. His strategies include:
- **Open-ended projects**: Tasks with low floors (easy entry points) but high ceilings (opportunities for extension)
- **Multiple modalities**: Using unplugged activities, physical computing, and screen-based programming to suit different learning preferences
- **Attention to gender equity**: Consciously designing projects that appeal to all children, challenging stereotypes about who can succeed in Computing
- **Support for SEN**: Adaptations for children with specific learning needs, using visual programming languages and collaborative approaches

### Formative Assessment

Berry advocates for assessment approaches in Computing that focus on learning processes, not just products. His recommendations include:
- **Observation during lessons**: Noting how children approach problems, collaborate, and debug
- **Questioning**: Using targeted questions to probe computational thinking
- **Self and peer assessment**: Children reflecting on their own learning and evaluating peers' work against success criteria
