# Sue Sentance

**Full Name:** Professor Susan "Sue" Sentance

**Current Roles:** 
- Professor of Computer Science Education at King's College London (since 2018)
- Chief Learning Officer at the Raspberry Pi Foundation (2018-2024, prior to full-time academic role)
- Emeritus Fellow at the Raspberry Pi Foundation

**Known For:** Leading researcher in computing pedagogy, co-developer of the PRIMM framework, pioneer in computing education research in the UK, advocate for evidence-based teaching approaches in computing

---

## Biography and Career History

### Early Career and Teaching Experience

Sue Sentance began her career as a secondary school teacher, spending over 20 years teaching Computer Science and ICT in schools before transitioning to academic research. This extensive classroom experience has profoundly shaped her research agenda, keeping her work grounded in the practical realities of teaching computing. She taught at Haberdashers' Aske's Knights Academy in Bromley, where she developed a deep understanding of the pedagogical challenges inherent in computing education.

### Academic Transition and King's College London

In 2012, Sentance joined King's College London as a Research Associate, working with Professor Peter Kemp on the "Computing At School" research project. This marked her formal entry into computing education research. She completed her PhD at King's College London, examining how teachers learn to teach programming and the transition from ICT to Computer Science in English schools during the period following the 2014 curriculum reform.

By 2018, she had been appointed Professor of Computer Science Education at King's College London, where she leads the Computing Education Research Group. Her research group focuses on understanding how students learn computing concepts, how teachers develop their pedagogical content knowledge, and how to improve computing education at scale.

### Raspberry Pi Foundation

Sentance served as Chief Learning Officer at the Raspberry Pi Foundation from 2018 to 2024, where she led the Foundation's educational research agenda and the development of professional development programmes for computing teachers. In this role, she oversaw the creation of evidence-based resources and professional development courses that have reached tens of thousands of teachers worldwide. The Foundation's professional development offerings, including their online courses on teaching programming, semantic and syntactic understanding, and computational thinking, all bear her influence.

## Key Ideas

### 1. PRIMM Framework (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make)

The PRIMM framework is arguably Sentance's most influential contribution to computing pedagogy. Developed in collaboration with colleagues at King's College London, PRIMM provides a structured approach to teaching programming that emphasises reading and comprehension before code writing.

**Structure of PRIMM:**
- **Predict:** Students examine code and predict what it will do before running it, encouraging careful reading and hypothesis formation
- **Run:** Students execute the code and compare actual outcomes with their predictions, developing debugging skills
- **Investigate:** Students analyse the code structure and trace execution, often with targeted questions from the teacher
- **Modify:** Students make small, purposeful changes to the code to meet new requirements
- **Make:** Students create their own programs, building on the concepts and structures they've studied

The framework emerged from research conducted between 2015 and 2017, with formal publications appearing from 2018 onwards. PRIMM draws on literacy pedagogy principles, particularly the idea that students must learn to "read" code before they can effectively "write" it. This approach challenges the traditional "show and do" method prevalent in many computing classrooms, where students are expected to write code from the outset.

Research evidence supports PRIMM's effectiveness. In studies conducted by Sentance and colleagues, students taught using PRIMM demonstrated better understanding of program structure, improved debugging skills, and reduced cognitive load compared to traditional approaches. The framework has been particularly effective in reducing the achievement gap between students of different prior attainment levels.

### 2. Use-Modify-Create Progression

Sentance has been a strong advocate for the Use-Modify-Create (UMC) progression model in computing education. This pedagogical approach, which aligns closely with PRIMM, suggests that learners should:
- **Use** existing programs and systems to understand their purpose and functionality
- **Modify** these programs to meet new specifications or fix errors
- **Create** original programs from scratch

This progression reflects the professional practice of software developers, who regularly read and modify existing codebases. Sentance's research shows that many novice programming courses skip directly to "Create," leaving students without the scaffolding necessary for success. Her work demonstrates that students benefit significantly from spending time reading, tracing, and modifying code before attempting to write programs independently.

### 3. Semantic Wave Theory in Computing Education

Sentance has applied the concept of "semantic waves" (derived from the sociologist Basil Bernstein and developed by educational theorist Karl Maton) to computing education. Semantic wave theory describes how effective teaching moves between concrete, context-dependent examples (unpacking concepts) and abstract, generalised principles (repacking concepts).

In computing, this might involve:
- Starting with a specific program that solves a particular problem (concrete)
- Identifying the general programming construct or algorithm used (abstract)
- Applying that construct to new contexts (concrete again)
- Building towards general principles of computational thinking (abstract)

This oscillation between concrete and abstract helps students build both procedural skills and conceptual understanding, avoiding the trap of learning programming as mere syntax memorisation.

### 4. Block-Based to Text-Based Transition

Sentance has conducted extensive research on the transition from block-based programming environments (such as Scratch) to text-based languages (such as Python). Her work identifies this transition as a critical juncture where many students struggle, particularly in secondary schools.

Key findings from her research include:
- The transition involves more than syntax; students must develop new working practices including debugging strategies and text editing skills
- Block-based environments can create misleading mental models if not used carefully
- Teachers need explicit pedagogical strategies to support this transition
- The transition should be gradual, potentially using hybrid environments like Blockly or text-based environments with scaffolding

Her research has influenced how schools approach the Key Stage 3 to Key Stage 4 transition in England, with many schools now implementing more structured bridge-building between Scratch and text-based programming.

### 5. Teacher Professional Development and Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK)

A significant strand of Sentance's research focuses on how computing teachers develop their pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) – the intersection of subject knowledge and pedagogical skill specific to teaching computing.

Her research in this area has shown:
- Many computing teachers lack formal computer science qualifications, having transitioned from teaching ICT
- Effective professional development must address both subject knowledge and pedagogy simultaneously
- Teachers need ongoing support, not just one-off training events
- Community-based learning, such as that provided by Computing At School (CAS) hubs, plays a crucial role in teacher development
- Teachers benefit from seeing worked examples of teaching approaches, not just content explanations

This research directly informed the design of the Raspberry Pi Foundation's professional development programmes, including their "Teaching Programming to 11-16 Year Olds" course and their face-to-face Picademy training events.

### 6. Assessment in Computing Education

Sentance has contributed to understanding effective assessment practices in computing, particularly formative assessment. Her work emphasises:
- The importance of assessing understanding of concepts, not just ability to produce working code
- The use of Parsons Problems (where students arrange scrambled code blocks) as an assessment tool
- The role of predictive questions in assessing program comprehension
- The limitations of purely summative assessment through project work

Her research has informed assessment practice in schools and examination boards, though she has also been critical of aspects of GCSE Computer Science assessment that she views as insufficiently aligned with modern understanding of computing pedagogy.

### 7. Computational Thinking Operationalisation

Sentance has worked to operationalise computational thinking for classroom practice, moving beyond Jeannette Wing's influential but abstract definition. Her work identifies specific computational thinking concepts and practices that can be taught and assessed:

**Concepts:** abstraction, decomposition, algorithmic thinking, pattern recognition, evaluation
**Practices:** tinkering, creating, debugging, persevering, collaborating

Importantly, Sentance's work emphasises that computational thinking should be taught through meaningful computing activities, not as a separate abstract subject. She has been critical of approaches that teach computational thinking without programming or other authentic computing contexts.

### 8. Equity and Inclusion in Computing Education

Throughout her career, Sentance has been concerned with equity and inclusion in computing, particularly addressing gender imbalances and ensuring computing is accessible to students of all backgrounds. Her research has examined:
- Factors that influence girls' participation in computing at GCSE and A-level
- The role of teacher language and classroom culture in inclusion
- How curriculum design and pedagogical choices can widen or narrow participation gaps
- The importance of diverse representations in teaching materials and examples

She has been particularly vocal about the need for computing education to move beyond a narrow focus on game creation and other stereotypically masculine applications, advocating for a broader curriculum that includes data science, creative applications, and socially meaningful projects.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

While Sentance was not directly involved in writing the 2014 National Curriculum for Computing in England, her subsequent research has profoundly influenced how that curriculum is interpreted and implemented. Her work with Computing At School (CAS) and the Raspberry Pi Foundation has helped translate the statutory requirements into practical classroom approaches.

### Computing At School (CAS)

Sentance was an early and active member of Computing At School, the grassroots organisation that successfully campaigned for computer science to replace ICT in the English curriculum. She served on various CAS committees and working groups, contributing to the development of guidance documents, resource recommendations, and professional development frameworks. The CAS Master Teacher programme, which she helped design, created a network of expert computing teachers who provide subject-specific CPD to colleagues.

### National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE)

Sentance's research directly informed the design of the National Centre for Computing Education, established in 2018 to improve computing education across England. The NCCE's pedagogical principles, particularly its emphasis on PRIMM, Use-Modify-Create, and semantic waves, reflect her research findings. As Chief Learning Officer of the Raspberry Pi Foundation (one of the NCCE's delivery partners), she oversaw the creation of much of the Centre's curriculum and CPD materials.

The NCCE's "Teach Computing Curriculum," a comprehensive scheme of work from Key Stage 1 to Key Stage 4, embeds Sentance's pedagogical approaches throughout. This curriculum is now used by thousands of schools across England.

### International Influence

Sentance has been active in international computing education networks, including:
- The International Federation for Information Processing (IFIP) Working Group 3.4 on Professional and Vocational Education in ICT
- The Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) Special Interest Group on Computer Science Education (SIGCSE)
- Advisory roles with computing education initiatives in various countries

Her work on PRIMM has been adopted in several countries beyond the UK, including adaptations for use in Australia, the Netherlands, and parts of the United States.

### Examination Board Influence

Sentance has provided consultation to examination boards offering GCSE and A-level Computer Science, though she has also been a constructive critic of assessment approaches that emphasise project work over conceptual understanding. Her research on assessment has influenced thinking about how to better assess computational thinking and programming skills.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Evidence-Based Practice

Sentance's pedagogical philosophy centres on evidence-based practice – the idea that teaching approaches should be informed by rigorous research rather than tradition or intuition. She has advocated for:
- Randomised controlled trials in computing education research where feasible
- Careful documentation of "what works" in different contexts
- Critical evaluation of popular approaches that lack evidence
- Building a research-practitioner partnership model where teachers contribute to research and research informs practice

### Literacy-Informed Pedagogy

Drawing on research in literacy education, Sentance emphasises that programming is fundamentally about reading and comprehending code before writing it. This perspective challenges traditional computing pedagogy and has led to practical innovations like PRIMM. She advocates for:
- Explicit teaching of code reading strategies
- Using code examples as "texts" to study before attempting independent creation
- Vocabulary development and precise use of technical terminology
- Structured progression from comprehension to production

### Cognitive Load Theory Application

Sentance's work is informed by cognitive load theory, particularly the recognition that novices have limited working memory capacity. Her pedagogical approaches therefore emphasise:
- Breaking complex concepts into manageable chunks
- Using worked examples to reduce extraneous cognitive load
- Building from concrete examples to abstract principles
- Avoiding "split attention" effects by integrating code and explanations

### Socio-Cultural Perspective

Drawing on Vygotskian theory, Sentance emphasises the social nature of learning to program. This perspective informs:
- The role of classroom dialogue in developing understanding
- Pair programming and collaborative debugging
- The importance of teacher explanation and modelling
- Scaffolding that gradually transfers responsibility to students (the Zone of Proximal Development)
