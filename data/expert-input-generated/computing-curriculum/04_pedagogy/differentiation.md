# Differentiation in Computing

## Introduction

Differentiation in Computing education refers to the pedagogical approach of tailoring teaching methods, resources, and assessment strategies to meet the diverse needs, abilities, and learning preferences of all pupils. In Computing, this is particularly crucial given the subject's wide-ranging demands—from abstract computational thinking to practical programming skills—and the varied prior experiences pupils bring to the classroom.

Sue Sentance, Director of the Raspberry Pi Foundation's Research Seminars, has emphasised that Computing teachers face unique differentiation challenges because pupils often arrive with vastly different levels of prior exposure to technology and programming concepts. Unlike traditional subjects where pupils progress through similar foundational experiences, some Computing pupils may have extensive coding experience whilst others have never written a line of code.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Differentiation Models in Computing

Phil Bagge, a primary Computing specialist and author of 'Computing for Key Stage 1', advocates for differentiation by task, outcome, and resource in Computing lessons. He argues that Computing's practical nature makes it particularly suited to open-ended tasks where pupils can demonstrate understanding at different levels of sophistication.

Mark Dorling, former Chair of Computing at School (CAS), has written extensively about the importance of **'low floor, high ceiling'** tasks in Computing—activities that are accessible to all pupils but allow for significant extension and depth. This concept, originally developed by Seymour Papert in the context of Logo programming, has become central to Computing pedagogy in the UK.

Miles Berry, Principal Lecturer at the University of Roehampton, distinguishes between:
- **Differentiation by content**: varying what pupils learn
- **Differentiation by process**: varying how pupils learn
- **Differentiation by product**: varying how pupils demonstrate their learning

In Computing, Berry suggests that differentiation by process and product are often more effective than differentiation by content, as core computational concepts should be accessible to all pupils regardless of ability.

## Key Stage Specific Considerations

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At KS1, the National Curriculum requires pupils to "understand what algorithms are" and "create and debug simple programs". Differentiation at this stage must account for:

- **Pre-literacy considerations**: Not all pupils can read fluently, affecting their ability to use text-based programming environments
- **Fine motor skills**: Physical manipulation of devices varies significantly
- **Prior exposure**: Some pupils may have extensive experience with tablets and digital devices at home, whilst others have minimal access

**Practical approaches:**
- Use unplugged activities (as advocated by Tim Bell's Computer Science Unplugged) to make computational thinking accessible without requiring keyboard skills
- Employ visual programming environments like ScratchJr or Bee-Bot, which reduce literacy barriers
- Provide scaffolded algorithm design using physical resources (e.g., arrow cards, sequencing mats)

Phil Bagge recommends using **'same task, different support'** at KS1, where all pupils work towards the same learning objective but receive different levels of scaffolding, such as pre-completed code blocks or worked examples.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The KS2 National Curriculum expects pupils to "design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals" and "use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs". The gap between high and low attainers often widens during this phase.

**Differentiation strategies:**

Sue Sentance's research through King's College London's Computing Education Research Group identifies that **worked examples** are particularly effective for novice programmers. Providing partially completed programs with clear annotations helps pupils understand programming structures without overwhelming cognitive load.

**Practical approaches:**
- **PRIMM approach** (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make): Developed by Sue Sentance and colleagues, this framework naturally differentiates as pupils progress from predicting code behaviour to creating original programs
- **Tiered challenges**: Using platforms like Scratch, create three versions of the same project brief (bronze, silver, gold) with increasing complexity
- **Use of decomposition**: Break complex problems into smaller sub-problems, allowing pupils to tackle components at their own pace
- **Peer programming**: Pair pupils strategically, considering complementary skills rather than similar abilities

Miles Berry notes that at KS2, Computing offers excellent opportunities for **cross-curricular differentiation**, where Computing skills can be applied to topics in other subjects at varying levels of sophistication.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At KS3, the National Curriculum requires pupils to use "two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual". This transition from block-based to text-based languages presents significant differentiation challenges.

**Key considerations:**

Mark Dorling's work with Computing at School has highlighted that the transition to text-based programming represents a critical juncture where many pupils, particularly girls and those from disadvantaged backgrounds, may disengage if differentiation is inadequate.

**Practical approaches:**

- **Block-to-text scaffolding**: Use dual-mode environments like Edublocks or Scratch's transition to Python, allowing pupils to move between representations
- **Syntax support materials**: Provide reference sheets, code templates, and auto-complete tools to reduce syntax barriers
- **Conceptual understanding over syntax memorisation**: Focus on computational thinking concepts rather than language-specific syntax, as advocated by Simon Peyton Jones
- **Choice of programming language**: Consider offering Python and another language (e.g., JavaScript, C#) to suit different pupil interests and prior experiences

**Computational thinking differentiation:**

Sue Sentance's research emphasises differentiating computational thinking skills separately from programming skills:
- **Abstraction**: Provide varying levels of complexity in problem representation
- **Decomposition**: Scaffold the breakdown of problems with visual aids for some pupils whilst expecting independent decomposition from others
- **Pattern recognition**: Use explicit teaching of pattern-spotting strategies for pupils who struggle with this abstract skill
- **Algorithmic thinking**: Differentiate between flowcharts, pseudocode, and written descriptions based on pupil preference and understanding

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At KS4, pupils typically follow GCSE Computing courses (Computer Science or Creative iMedia). Differentiation becomes more formal and structured around examination requirements.

**Examination board specific considerations:**

**OCR J277 Computer Science:**
- Clearly defined programming language options (Python, Java, C#, C++, VB.NET)
- Tiered entry (though single tier assessment is standard for Computer Science)
- Non-examined assessment (NEA) allows for natural differentiation through problem choice

**AQA 8525 Computer Science:**
- Python or another high-level language
- Non-examined assessment worth 20%
- Clear marking criteria allowing differentiation by depth of analysis and technical sophistication

**Differentiation strategies for GCSE:**

1. **Targeted questioning**: Use Bloom's Taxonomy to create questions at different cognitive levels. Sue Sentance's research shows that novice programmers benefit from 'knowledge' and 'comprehension' level questions before progressing to 'application' and 'analysis'

2. **Problem decomposition scaffolding**: For programming projects, provide varying levels of problem breakdown:
   - **High attainers**: Brief specification only, requiring full decomposition
   - **Middle attainers**: Main components identified, requiring detailed breakdown
   - **Lower attainers**: Step-by-step breakdown provided, focusing on implementation

3. **Trace table scaffolding**: Provide partially completed trace tables for pupils who struggle with abstract variable tracking

4. **Programming project differentiation**: 
   - Allow choice of project complexity within assessment criteria
   - Provide code libraries for lower attainers whilst expecting high attainers to develop all components independently
   - Differentiate success criteria clearly (e.g., "All pupils will..., Most pupils will..., Some pupils will...")

**Theory content differentiation:**

Phil Bagge and other Computing educators recommend:
- **Dual coding**: Combine text explanations with visual representations (diagrams, animations) to support pupils with reading difficulties
- **Knowledge organisers**: Provide structured summaries of key concepts at different levels of detail
- **Spaced practice**: Regular low-stakes testing on theoretical concepts, with questions differentiated by complexity
- **Analogies and metaphors**: Use age-appropriate analogies to explain abstract concepts (e.g., RAM as a workbench, CPU as a brain)

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-level Computing (Computer Science) requires significant differentiation despite pupils being self-selected and generally high-attaining.

**Examination board specifications:**

**OCR H446/H446:**
- Programming project (NEA) worth 20%
- Significant breadth and depth of content requiring sophisticated differentiation

**AQA 7517:**
- On-screen programming examination (skeleton program)
- 20% NEA focused on investigative project

**Differentiation approaches for A-level:**

1. **Extension through depth not breadth**: Simon Peyton Jones, former Chair of the UK Computing at School Working Group and Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research, argues that A-level differentiation should focus on deeper understanding of core concepts rather than teaching additional content

2. **Research-led learning**: High-attaining pupils benefit from engaging with actual Computer Science research papers and contemporary developments in the field

3. **Algorithmic complexity**: Differentiate tasks by Big O notation complexity, allowing pupils to tackle algorithms appropriate to their mathematical confidence

4. **Programming paradigms**: Whilst functional programming is not required by specifications, it offers excellent extension for high attainers. Miles Berry has advocated for introducing Haskell or F# concepts to stretch pupils

5. **NEA differentiation**:
   - **Problem selection**: Guide pupils towards projects matching their technical capability
   - **Milestone scaffolding**: Provide different levels of intermediate checkpoint support
   - **Technical mentoring**: Allocate teacher support time strategically, providing intensive support where needed

## Differentiation by Special Educational Needs

### Visual Impairment

- Use high-contrast IDE themes and screen readers compatible with programming environments
- Provide audio-described programming tutorials
- Consider block-based programming environments with strong accessibility features

### Hearing Impairment

- Provide written transcripts of all video tutorials
- Use visual alerts in programming environments rather than audio-only feedback
- Ensure all spoken instructions are accompanied by written/visual equivalents

### Dyslexia

Research by Sue Sentance and colleagues at King's College London has identified specific challenges for dyslexic pupils in Computing:
- Syntax errors in programming are particularly frustrating for pupils with dyslexia
- Variable naming conventions can be problematic

**Strategies:**
- Use IDEs with strong autocomplete and syntax checking
- Allow use of assistive technology such as speech-to-text for commenting code
- Provide pre-written code libraries to reduce typing demands
- Accept more descriptive variable names rather than enforcing brevity
- Use pastel-coloured paper or IDE backgrounds to reduce visual stress

### Autism Spectrum Conditions (ASC)

Many pupils with ASC excel in Computing due to its logical, rule-based nature. However, differentiation may include:
- Clear, explicit success criteria for all tasks
- Structured routines and predictable lesson formats
- Direct instruction in collaborative programming practices, as pair programming social dynamics may not be intuitive
- Advance warning of changes to programming environments or software versions

### Pupils with Working Memory Difficulties

Mark Dorling's work emphasises that programming is cognitively demanding and places significant load on working memory. Strategies include:
- **Chunking information**: Break programming tasks into smaller, manageable components
- **Use of Visual aids**: Flowcharts, structure diagrams, and comment templates
- **Reduced cognitive load**: Provide pre-written functions for complex operations
- **Frequent practice**: Regular, short programming tasks rather than extended sessions

## Differentiation by Prior Attainment and Experience

### High Prior Attainers and Experienced Programmers

This group requires careful differentiation to maintain engagement. Strategies include:

1. **Extension not acceleration**: Rather than moving these pupils through content faster, deepen their understanding. Simon Peyton Jones argues that even experienced teenage programmers often have gaps in foundational understanding of computational thinking

2. **Code review and evaluation**: Ask experienced programmers to review and improve others' code, developing critical evaluation skills

3. **Algorithm optimisation**: Challenge pupils to create multiple solutions and analyse efficiency

4. **Software engineering practices**: Introduce version control, testing frameworks, and documentation standards beyond curriculum requirements

5. **Contribution to community**: Encourage pupils to contribute to open-source projects or create resources for less experienced peers

### Low Prior Attainment or Digital Poverty

Phil Bagge has highlighted that pupils from digitally impoverished backgrounds face significant barriers in Computing. Differentiation must address:

1. **Assumed digital literacy**: Don't assume familiarity with basic computer operations (saving files, navigating file systems)

2. **Cultural capital**: Pupils may lack exposure to Computing careers, applications, and cultural references used in examples

3. **Hardware access**: Limited access to devices outside school necessitates:
   - Maximising school-based practical time
   - Providing paper-based alternatives for homework (pseudocode, algorithm design)
   - Using web-based platforms accessible on any device

4. **Confidence building**: Research by Sue Sentance shows that pupils with low prior attainment in Computing often suffer from reduced self-efficacy. Strategies include:
   - Regular success experiences through appropriately pitched tasks
   - Explicit acknowledgment of progress
   - Use of 'debugging' as a teaching tool—normalising errors as learning opportunities
   - Showcasing diverse role models in Computing

## Gender and Computing Differentiation

Sue Sentance's extensive research on gender in Computing education identifies that whilst differentiation by gender is not pedagogically sound as a primary strategy, teachers must be aware of gendered participation patterns:

### Evidence-Based Approaches:

1. **Context of programming tasks**: Research shows girls respond more positively to programming tasks with clear social purpose or creative outlet. Mark Dorling recommends varied contexts rather than games-focused examples

2. **Stereotype threat**: Avoid reinforcing stereotypes through language, examples, or classroom organisation

3. **Collaborative work**: Research indicates girls often prefer collaborative programming tasks, though care must be taken to ensure equitable participation in mixed-gender pairs

4. **Teacher language**: Avoid gendered language ("guys", "lads") and ensure female contributions are equally acknowledged

5. **Role models**: Miles Berry emphasises the importance of showcasing diverse Computing professionals and historical figures (Ada Lovelace, Grace Hopper, etc.)

## Practical Differentiation Strategies Across Key Stages

### Task Differentiation

1. **PRIMM Framework**: Developed by Sue Sentance and Andrew Csizmadia, PRIMM provides inherent differentiation:
   - **Predict**: Lower attainers predict simple code outputs; higher attainers predict complex interactions
   - **Run**: All pupils run code and observe
   - **Investigate**: Differentiate by complexity of investigation questions
   - **Modify**: Scaffold modifications for some pupils; expect independent modifications from others
   - **Make**: Open-ended task allowing differentiation by sophistication of final product

2. **Abstraction ladders**: Provide the same computational problem at multiple levels of abstraction. For example, a sorting task might be presented as:
   - Physical cards for pupils needing concrete manipulation
   - Visual animation for pupils ready for semi-abstract representation
   - Pseudocode for pupils comfortable with abstract algorithmic thinking
   - Implementation code for pupils ready for full abstraction

3. **Challenge menus**: Phil Bagge advocates for providing 'must do, should do, could do' extension tasks that pupils select based on their progress and confidence

### Resource Differentiation

1. **Programming environments**:
   - **Scratch**: Block-based, visual, suitable for novices and planning
   - **Edublocks/Blocks to Text**: Transitional environments
   - **Python with IDLE/Thonny**: Simple text-based for developing programmers
   - **Professional IDEs** (PyCharm, Visual Studio Code): For experienced pupils

2. **Reference materials**:
   - Quick reference cards with syntax and examples
   - Worked examples at varying complexity levels
   - Video tutorials for pupils who benefit from multimedia presentation
   - Interactive tutorials (Code.org, CodeCademy) for independent practice

3. **Assessment frameworks**: Use rubrics that define success at multiple levels, allowing pupils to self-assess and set appropriate targets

### Outcome Differentiation

Computing is particularly suited to differentiation by outcome because:

1. **Open-ended projects**: Programming projects naturally allow pupils to demonstrate understanding at their level of sophistication

2. **Working solutions at different levels**: A sorting algorithm might be implemented using:
   - Simple bubble sort (accessible to most)
   - Optimised bubble sort with flag (deeper understanding)
   - Merge sort or quick sort (algorithmic sophistication)

3. **Documentation and evaluation**: All pupils can be required to document their work, with differentiation occurring in the depth of technical language, analysis of efficiency, and evaluation of alternatives
