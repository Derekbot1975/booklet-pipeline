# Progression in Programming

## Introduction

Progression in programming is a fundamental pillar of the Computing curriculum in England, representing the structured development of learners' abilities to create, debug, and understand computational solutions. The National Curriculum for Computing (2014) establishes programming as a core component from Key Stage 1 through Key Stage 4, requiring careful pedagogical consideration to ensure students develop both conceptual understanding and practical skills in a coherent sequence.

Mark Dorling and Sue Sentance have been particularly influential in defining progression pathways in Computing education. Their work with Computing At School (CAS) has established frameworks that move beyond simple age-related expectations to focus on conceptual development. As Sentance notes in her research on programming pedagogy, effective progression requires attention to both the "notional machine" (how programs execute) and the development of computational thinking practices.

## Theoretical Frameworks for Programming Progression

### The PRIMM Approach

Sue Sentance, along with researchers at King's College London, developed the PRIMM approach (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) as a pedagogical framework specifically designed to support progression in programming. This structure recognises that learners need scaffolded exposure to code before creating programs from scratch. PRIMM explicitly addresses the cognitive load challenges identified in programming education research, particularly for novice programmers who can become overwhelmed when asked to write code without adequate exposure to working examples.

### Computational Thinking and Programming

Simon Peyton Jones, in his work with the Royal Society and Computing At School, has emphasised that programming progression must be grounded in computational thinking concepts. His influential position is that programming is not merely "coding" but rather the expression of algorithmic thinking through formal notation. This perspective informs the National Curriculum's requirement that students understand concepts such as sequence, selection, and iteration before implementing them in specific programming languages.

Phil Bagge's work on teaching primary computing has similarly emphasised that progression begins with understanding computational concepts through unplugged activities and visual programming before transitioning to text-based languages. His "Computer Science for Fun" approach demonstrates how teachers can build conceptual understanding that transfers across different programming contexts.

## National Curriculum Expectations

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum states that pupils should be taught to "understand what algorithms are; how they are implemented as programs on digital devices; and that programs execute by following precise and unambiguous instructions."

**Progression indicators at KS1:**
- Understanding that algorithms are sequences of instructions
- Creating simple programs using floor robots (e.g., Bee-Bot, Roamer)
- Beginning to debug when instructions don't produce intended outcomes
- Using directional language (forward, backward, turn)
- Introduction to sequence through visual programming (e.g., ScratchJr)

Miles Berry's guidance for primary computing emphasises that at this stage, the focus should be on developing algorithmic thinking through concrete, physical experiences before screen-based programming.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to "design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals, including controlling or simulating physical systems; solve problems by decomposing them into smaller parts" and "use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs; work with variables and various forms of input and output."

**Progression indicators at KS2:**

**Lower KS2 (Years 3-4):**
- Using visual programming environments (Scratch, Scratch Jr, Code.org)
- Understanding and implementing sequence in multi-step programs
- Beginning to use repetition (loops) to make programs more efficient
- Understanding events and event-driven programming
- Creating simple animations and interactive stories
- Introduction to debugging as a systematic process

**Upper KS2 (Years 5-6):**
- Implementing selection (conditional statements/if-then-else)
- Using variables to store and manipulate data
- Understanding how sequence, selection, and repetition work together
- Decomposing problems into smaller, manageable parts
- Creating programs with defined inputs and outputs
- Introduction to procedures and simple functions
- Beginning abstraction through creating custom blocks/procedures
- More sophisticated debugging including prediction and tracing

Phil Bagge's progression framework, widely adopted in primary schools, emphasises that Year 5 represents a critical transition point where students should consolidate their understanding of the three fundamental programming constructs (sequence, selection, iteration) before advancing to more complex concepts.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum states that pupils should "use two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual, to solve a variety of computational problems" and "understand simple Boolean logic and some of its uses in circuits and programming."

**Progression indicators at KS3:**

**Year 7:**
- Transitioning from visual to text-based programming (typically Python)
- Understanding syntax and the importance of precision
- Consolidating sequence, selection (including elif), and iteration (for and while loops)
- Working with different data types (integers, strings, floats, Booleans)
- Using variables with proper naming conventions
- Understanding Boolean logic (AND, OR, NOT)
- Basic list/array manipulation
- Creating programs that use standard inputs and outputs

**Year 8:**
- Writing more complex programs involving multiple constructs
- Understanding procedures and functions with parameters
- String manipulation and more advanced data structure use
- Introduction to 2D lists/arrays
- File handling (reading from and writing to files)
- Understanding scope of variables
- More sophisticated problem decomposition
- Introduction to algorithm efficiency concepts

**Year 9:**
- Object-oriented programming concepts (classes, objects, methods)
- Advanced data structures (dictionaries/associative arrays)
- Understanding encapsulation and abstraction
- Writing programs that validate inputs
- Exception handling and robust error management
- Introduction to searching and sorting algorithms
- Understanding time complexity at a basic level

Mark Dorling's work on assessment in Computing emphasises that progression at KS3 should be evidenced not just through increased complexity of programs but through students' ability to explain their design decisions, predict program behaviour, and systematically debug increasingly complex code.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE Computer Science specifications from all major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC) include substantial programming components, typically requiring:

**Common requirements across exam boards:**
- Proficiency in at least one high-level programming language
- Understanding of structured programming techniques
- Ability to write, test, and refine programs
- Understanding of standard algorithms (searching, sorting, traversal)
- Use of sub-programs (procedures and functions)
- Understanding of scope and parameter passing
- File handling operations
- String manipulation and validation
- Understanding of data structures (arrays, 2D arrays, records/structures)
- SQL programming for database manipulation (in some specifications)

**OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277):**
OCR's specification explicitly requires understanding of the following programming constructs: variables and constants, operators (arithmetic, Boolean, comparison), sequence, selection (if, elif, else, switch/case), iteration (for, while, do-until), string manipulation, file handling, records/structures, arrays (including 2D), and sub-programs.

**AQA GCSE Computer Science (8525):**
AQA requires competency in Python, C#, Java, or VB.NET, with emphasis on writing programs of increasing complexity. Their specification includes database programming (SQL) as a distinct programming paradigm.

**Progression to advanced programming:**
By the end of KS4, students should demonstrate:
- Ability to solve substantial programming problems independently
- Understanding of algorithm efficiency and Big O notation (basic level)
- Competency in testing, including test plans and test data selection
- Ability to trace code and predict outcomes
- Understanding of defensive design principles
- Recognition of when to use different programming constructs appropriately

## Key Concepts in Programming Progression

### The Notional Machine

Sue Sentance and others have highlighted the importance of students developing a "notional machine" – an understanding of how programs actually execute. This is not language-specific but represents understanding of:
- How variables are stored and accessed in memory
- The flow of execution through a program
- How function calls work (call stack basics)
- How data structures are represented in memory

Progression in understanding the notional machine moves from concrete (watching a robot execute commands) to increasingly abstract (mentally tracing variable values through complex code).

### Debugging as a Skill Progression

Mark Dorling emphasises that debugging represents a distinct progression pathway. Students develop from:
1. **Trial and error** (randomly changing code hoping it will work)
2. **Systematic testing** (running code to see what happens)
3. **Hypothesis-driven debugging** (predicting what's wrong and testing that hypothesis)
4. **Tracing and prediction** (mentally executing code before running it)
5. **Using debugging tools** (breakpoints, watch variables, step execution)

Effective teachers explicitly teach debugging strategies rather than assuming students will develop them naturally.

### Abstraction and Decomposition

Phil Bagge and Miles Berry have both written extensively about how abstraction develops progressively:
- **KS1:** Recognising patterns in sequences
- **Lower KS2:** Identifying repeated actions that could become loops
- **Upper KS2:** Creating custom blocks/procedures to represent complex actions
- **KS3:** Understanding functions as reusable abstractions
- **KS4:** Object-oriented abstraction and understanding interfaces

Decomposition similarly progresses from teacher-guided breaking down of problems to independent analysis of complex challenges.

### Threshold Concepts in Programming

Research by Simon Peyton Jones and others identifies several "threshold concepts" in programming – ideas that, once understood, transform student capability but which are particularly challenging:

1. **Variable assignment** (understanding that `x = x + 1` doesn't imply algebraic equality)
2. **The distinction between definition and invocation** (defining a function vs calling it)
3. **Parameter passing** (understanding how data moves into and out of functions)
4. **Scope** (where variables exist and can be accessed)
5. **Iteration with aggregation** (accumulating values across multiple loop cycles)

Teachers should recognise these as points where students commonly struggle and require additional support and varied representations.

## Practical Progression Pathways

### Language Progression

While the National Curriculum is language-agnostic, typical progression in English schools follows:

**KS1:** Physical programming (Bee-Bot), ScratchJr
**Lower KS2:** Scratch, Code.org, possibly Logo
**Upper KS2:** Scratch (advanced features), possibly introduction to Python or HTML/CSS
**KS3:** Python (primary language), with exposure to web languages (HTML, CSS, JavaScript)
**KS4:** Python, Java, C#, or VB.NET (exam board dependent), SQL

Mark Dorling cautions against overemphasising language choice, noting that conceptual understanding should transfer across languages. However, he recognises that text-based syntax presents additional cognitive load that must be carefully managed during transition periods.

### Project Complexity Progression

Sue Sentance's research demonstrates that progression is evidenced through increasing project complexity:

**KS1-2 projects:**
- Single-purpose programs (e.g., animate a character, create a simple game)
- Clear, concrete goals
- Limited user interaction

**KS3 projects:**
- Multi-component systems (e.g., quiz game with score tracking and levels)
- Programs requiring planning and design documentation
- User input validation and error handling

**KS4 projects:**
- Substantial problems requiring multiple programming techniques
- Programs with clear real-world applications
- Full development lifecycle including testing documentation

## Assessment of Programming Progression

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Phil Bagge and others advocate for assessment approaches including:

**Code tracing exercises:** Students predict outputs without running code
**Parsons Problems:** Students arrange shuffled code blocks in correct order
**PRIMM-based activities:** Particularly the "Predict" phase as assessment
**Debugging challenges:** Providing buggy code for students to fix
**Code comprehension questions:** Explaining what code does in plain English
**Design challenges:** Pseudocode or flowchart creation before coding

### Summative Assessment at GCSE

All exam boards include:
- **Written examination** testing understanding of programming concepts
- **Practical programming project** (NEA/coursework)
- **On-screen examination** requiring program writing and problem-solving

Teachers must ensure progression pathways prepare students for both theory-based questions about programming concepts and practical programming under examination conditions.

## Common Misconceptions and Progression Barriers

Sue Sentance's research identifies common misconceptions that impede progression:

1. **Confusion between parameters and arguments**
2. **Misunderstanding variable scope** (believing all variables are global)
3. **Treating programs as static texts** rather than dynamic executions
4. **Expecting computers to "understand" imprecise instructions**
5. **Confusion between `=` as assignment vs equality**

Effective progression requires teachers to actively identify and address these misconceptions through targeted intervention rather than simply re-explaining correct concepts.

## Gender and Inclusion Considerations

Miles Berry and others have highlighted that programming progression must address equity issues. Research shows girls and other underrepresented groups often disengage from programming at the KS2/KS3 transition. Recommendations include:

- Using diverse contexts for programming problems
- Emphasising collaborative programming approaches
- Connecting programming to creative and social purposes
- Avoiding competitive environments that may discourage some learners
- Providing strong role models and highlighting diverse programmers

## Supporting Teachers in Delivering Progression

Computing At School, influenced by Sue Sentance and Mark Dorling's work, provides resources including:

- **Progression pathways documents** mapping conceptual development
- **Quick Read booklets** on specific programming topics
- **Barefoot Computing materials** for primary programming
- **CAS Community** for sharing effective progression approaches

The National Centre for Computing Education (NCCE), established following recommendations from the Royal Society report that Simon Peyton Jones contributed to, offers professional development specifically focused on effective programming pedagogy and progression.

## Conclusion

Effective progression in programming requires understanding that students develop through predictable conceptual stages, from concrete manipulation of physical devices to abstract reasoning about complex code. Teachers must recognise programming as encompassing far more than syntax knowledge, including debugging skills, problem decomposition, abstraction, and understanding of how programs execute. The frameworks developed by Dorling, Sentance, Bagge, Berry, and others provide essential guidance for ensuring all students can progress successfully through increasingly sophisticated programming challenges, ultimately developing the computational thinking capabilities that the National Curriculum envisions.
