# Digital Literacy Across the Curriculum

## Introduction and Definition

Digital literacy represents the capability to use information and communication technologies to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information, requiring both cognitive and technical skills. In the UK Computing context, digital literacy forms one of three core pillars alongside computer science and information technology, as articulated in the National Curriculum for Computing (2014).

Miles Berry has emphasised that digital literacy extends beyond simple functional skills, arguing that it encompasses critical thinking about technology, understanding of digital contexts, and the ability to participate meaningfully in digital society. This positions digital literacy as a fundamental competency that must permeate all curriculum areas, not merely remain confined to discrete Computing lessons.

## Policy Context and National Curriculum Framework

### Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Computing in England (2014) explicitly states that pupils should become "digitally literate" and able to use, express themselves, and develop their ideas through information and communication technology "at a level suitable for the future workplace and as active participants in a digital world."

The programme of study identifies digital literacy expectations across key stages:

**Key Stage 1**: Pupils should be taught to "use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content" and "recognise common uses of information technology beyond school."

**Key Stage 2**: Pupils should be taught to "use search technologies effectively," "select, use and combine a variety of software," "use technology safely, respectfully and responsibly," and "recognise acceptable/unacceptable behaviour."

**Key Stage 3**: Pupils should be taught to "undertake creative projects that involve selecting, using, and combining multiple applications, preferably across a range of devices" and "understand a range of ways to use technology safely, respectfully, responsibly and securely."

**Key Stage 4**: While not explicitly prescribed, digital literacy underpins all Computing qualifications and is embedded within assessment objectives across GCSE specifications.

## Cross-Curricular Integration: Subject-Specific Applications

### English

Digital literacy in English encompasses critical evaluation of online texts, understanding of multimodal communication, and creation of digital narratives. Sue Sentance has highlighted how computational thinking and digital literacy intersect when pupils analyse narrative structures using digital mind-mapping tools or create interactive fiction using platforms like Twine.

Teachers should integrate:
- Critical analysis of online sources and fake news identification
- Digital storytelling through video editing and podcasting
- Collaborative writing using cloud-based platforms (Google Docs, Microsoft 365)
- Understanding of digital publishing conventions and audience awareness

### Mathematics

Phil Bagge has extensively documented how digital tools can enhance mathematical understanding whilst developing digital literacy. Spreadsheet modelling, dynamic geometry software, and data visualisation tools require pupils to think critically about digital representation of mathematical concepts.

Practical applications include:
- Using spreadsheets for data analysis and formula manipulation
- Employing dynamic geometry software (GeoGebra) to explore mathematical relationships
- Creating data visualisations using appropriate graphing tools
- Understanding how algorithms underpin mathematical applications

### Science

Digital literacy in Science extends to data logging, simulation software, and critical evaluation of scientific information online. The Royal Society's 2017 report "After the Reboot" emphasised the complementary relationship between scientific and computational methods.

Key integration points:
- Data collection using digital sensors and data-logging equipment
- Analysis and graphing of experimental data using appropriate software
- Use of simulations to explore complex scientific phenomena
- Critical evaluation of online scientific sources and understanding of peer review

### Humanities (History and Geography)

Mark Dorling has emphasised how digital literacy enables pupils to engage with historical and geographical sources whilst developing critical evaluation skills. Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and digital archives provide rich contexts for developing digital literacy.

Teachers should incorporate:
- Critical analysis of historical sources in digital archives
- Use of GIS software for geographical data analysis
- Creation of digital presentations exploring historical or geographical themes
- Understanding of how digitisation affects preservation and access to historical materials

### Modern Foreign Languages

Digital literacy enhances language learning through authentic communication opportunities and access to native speaker resources. The integration of technology supports the National Curriculum's emphasis on practical communication.

Integration strategies include:
- Use of language learning applications with critical awareness of their pedagogical approach
- Digital communication with partner schools (email, video conferencing)
- Accessing and evaluating authentic target-language digital resources
- Creating digital portfolios of language work

### Design & Technology

Digital literacy in D&T encompasses Computer-Aided Design (CAD), computer-aided manufacture (CAM), and understanding of embedded systems. Simon Peyton Jones has argued that this subject area provides crucial contexts for applying computational thinking.

Practical applications:
- CAD software for product design (TinkerCAD, Fusion 360)
- Understanding of how microcontrollers and embedded systems function in products
- Digital presentation of design processes and iterations
- Use of digital fabrication tools (laser cutters, 3D printers)

### Art and Music

Creative digital literacy involves understanding digital tools as both media and mediators of artistic expression. The National Curriculum for Art and Design specifically references creating work using "a range of materials, including digital technologies."

Teachers should facilitate:
- Digital image manipulation and understanding of pixel-based versus vector graphics
- Music composition using Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs)
- Animation and video editing as artistic media
- Critical appreciation of how digital tools affect artistic practice

### Physical Education

Whilst perhaps less obvious, PE provides contexts for digital literacy through performance analysis tools, fitness tracking, and understanding of data in sports contexts.

Integration opportunities:
- Video analysis of performance using tablet technology
- Collection and interpretation of fitness data
- Critical evaluation of fitness applications and wearable technology
- Understanding of how technology mediates sporting performance

## Online Safety and Responsible Digital Citizenship

### Statutory Safeguarding Requirements

The "Teaching Online Safety in Schools" guidance (DfE, 2019) clarifies that all teachers share responsibility for online safety education. The Education Act 2011 removed the requirement for online safety to be taught solely within Computing, emphasising its cross-curricular nature.

UKCIS (UK Council for Internet Safety) framework identifies four key areas:
1. **Self-image and identity**: Understanding digital footprints and online reputation
2. **Online relationships**: Recognising healthy and unhealthy online interactions
3. **Online reputation**: Managing personal information and privacy
4. **Online bullying**: Recognising, responding to, and preventing cyberbullying

### Practical Whole-School Approaches

Miles Berry advocates for embedded, contextualised online safety teaching rather than standalone lessons. This requires subject teachers to address online safety within their discipline-specific contexts.

Effective practice includes:
- English teachers addressing cyberbullying through study of dramatic texts
- Science teachers discussing responsible sharing of research data
- History teachers exploring reliability of online historical sources
- PE teachers examining privacy implications of fitness tracking applications

## Assessment of Digital Literacy

### Challenges and Approaches

Unlike computer science with its clear knowledge domains and problem-solving assessment, digital literacy presents assessment challenges. Sue Sentance has researched how digital literacy is often assessed implicitly within subject-specific contexts rather than as a discrete skill set.

Effective assessment strategies:
- **Portfolio approaches**: Collections of digital work demonstrating capability across contexts
- **Observational assessment**: Teacher observation of pupils' digital working practices
- **Peer and self-assessment**: Reflection on digital literacy development
- **Task-embedded assessment**: Evaluation of digital literacy within subject-specific projects

### GCSE and Examination Context

Examination boards embed digital literacy within their specifications:

**AQA GCSE Computer Science (8525)**: Assessment Objective 3 requires candidates to "analyse and evaluate" digital content and systems.

**OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277)**: Includes specific content on "ethical, legal, cultural and environmental concerns" requiring critical digital literacy.

**Edexcel GCSE Computer Science (1CP2)**: Embeds digital literacy within "Impacts of Digital Technology" content.

**WJEC GCSE Computer Science**: Addresses digital literacy through "Implications of digital technology" strand.

## Teacher Development and Professional Learning

### Subject Knowledge Requirements

The Computing at School (CAS) framework, developed with substantial input from Mark Dorling and others, identifies digital literacy competencies for teachers across all subjects, not merely Computing specialists.

Core teacher competencies include:
- Understanding of how to evaluate digital tools for pedagogical effectiveness
- Capability to model effective digital practices to pupils
- Knowledge of current online safety issues and appropriate responses
- Awareness of accessibility considerations in digital content creation

### Professional Development Approaches

Simon Peyton Jones, in his work with the Royal Society, has emphasised that digital literacy professional development must be sustained and subject-contextualised rather than generic one-off training.

Effective CPD models:
- **Subject-specific digital pedagogy**: Training focused on how digital literacy applies within specific curriculum areas
- **Collaborative practice**: Subject teachers working alongside Computing specialists
- **Action research approaches**: Teachers investigating digital literacy development within their contexts
- **Peer observation and coaching**: Sharing effective practice across departments

## Equity and Access Considerations

### Digital Divide

Research by the Sutton Trust and others has documented persistent digital divides affecting pupils' ability to develop digital literacy. The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically highlighted these inequalities when schools moved to remote learning.

Schools must address:
- **Device access**: Ensuring all pupils have appropriate devices for schoolwork
- **Connectivity**: Supporting families without reliable internet access
- **Digital skills gaps**: Recognising that not all pupils arrive with equivalent digital experiences
- **Cultural capital**: Understanding that digital literacy develops through opportunity and exposure

### Inclusive Practice

Phil Bagge has written extensively about ensuring digital literacy education is accessible to all learners, including those with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND).

Inclusive approaches include:
- Use of accessibility features (text-to-speech, speech-to-text, magnification)
- Understanding of assistive technologies and how they support learning
- Consideration of cognitive load when introducing digital tools
- Recognition that some pupils may require alternative means of demonstrating digital literacy

## Progression Models and Frameworks

### CAS Digital Literacy Progression Framework

Computing at School, with contribution from Miles Berry and others, has developed progression frameworks that articulate development from early years through secondary education.

The framework identifies progression across strands:
- **Finding and evaluating information**: From simple searches to sophisticated evaluation of sources
- **Creating and communicating**: From basic content creation to sophisticated multimodal communication
- **Using technology safely and responsibly**: From following rules to making informed, principled decisions
- **Understanding technology's role in society**: From awareness of uses to critical analysis of impacts

### Practical Application for Teachers

Teachers should use progression frameworks to:
1. Identify current pupil capability within their subject context
2. Plan for incremental development of digital literacy skills
3. Ensure consistent expectations and language across departments
4. Provide clear success criteria for digital work

## Future Directions and Emerging Considerations

### Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

The increasing prevalence of AI tools (including large language models, image generators, and recommendation systems) requires evolution in digital literacy education. Pupils must understand how these systems work, their limitations, and their societal implications.

Sue Sentance and others have begun researching how to integrate AI literacy within the broader Computing curriculum, arguing that pupils need to understand:
- How AI systems are trained and their potential biases
- The appropriate and inappropriate uses of AI tools
- The implications of AI for employment and society
- Critical evaluation of AI-generated content

### Data Literacy

Understanding of how personal and societal data is collected, used, and potentially misused represents an increasingly critical component of digital literacy. This extends beyond simple privacy settings to encompass understanding of algorithms, profiling, and data-driven decision-making.

### Computational Thinking Integration

Mark Dorling has articulated how computational thinking and digital literacy intersect, with effective digital literacy requiring understanding of underlying computational concepts. This suggests that strong digital literacy education cannot be completely separated from foundational computer science education.

## Practical Implementation Recommendations

### Whole-School Digital Literacy Strategy

Effective digital literacy development requires coordinated whole-school approaches:

1. **Audit current practice**: Map where digital literacy is currently taught and assessed across subjects
2. **Establish common expectations**: Develop shared understanding of what digital literacy means in practice
3. **Create collaborative planning opportunities**: Enable Computing specialists and subject teachers to work together
4. **Develop consistent policies**: Ensure online safety, acceptable use, and responsible technology use policies align
5. **Invest in appropriate infrastructure**: Provide adequate devices, software, and connectivity
6. **Monitor and evaluate**: Track pupils' digital literacy development across their school career

### Subject Leadership Actions

Subject leaders should:
- Identify opportunities within their schemes of work for authentic digital literacy development
- Plan specific lessons addressing subject-contextualised online safety
- Ensure assessment practices capture digital literacy alongside subject knowledge
- Collaborate with the Computing department to align approaches
- Model effective digital practice in their own teaching

### Computing Department Leadership

Computing departments should position themselves as supporting digital literacy across the curriculum through:
- Offering consultancy to other departments on effective digital pedagogy
- Teaching foundational digital literacy skills that other subjects can build upon
- Coordinating whole-school online safety provision
- Leading evaluation and adoption of educational technology
- Providing professional development opportunities for colleagues

## Conclusion

Digital literacy represents a fundamental competency for 21st-century citizens, requiring systematic development across the curriculum. As articulated by the key thinkers in UK Computing education—Dorling, Peyton Jones, Bagge, Berry, and Sentance—effective digital literacy education requires collaboration between Computing specialists and subject teachers, embedded contextualised teaching rather than standalone skills lessons, and recognition that digital literacy encompasses critical thinking and responsible digital citizenship alongside technical capability.

The statutory requirements of the National Curriculum establish digital literacy as every teacher's responsibility, whilst the practical outworking requires sustained professional development, adequate resources, and coordinated whole-school approaches. As digital technologies continue to evolve, so too must our conception of digital literacy, ensuring pupils develop the adaptive capabilities needed to thrive in an increasingly digital world.
