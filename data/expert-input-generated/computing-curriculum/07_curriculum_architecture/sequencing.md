# Sequencing Computing Across Key Stages

## Introduction

Sequencing Computing across Key Stages represents one of the most significant challenges in curriculum design since the subject's introduction as a statutory National Curriculum subject in 2014. Unlike more established disciplines, Computing encompasses three interconnected strands—Computer Science, Information Technology, and Digital Literacy—each requiring careful progression planning from EYFS through to Key Stage 5.

Mark Dorling's seminal work on progression pathways, developed through his role with CAS (Computing at School) and documented in the "Progression Pathways" framework, established that effective sequencing in Computing requires attention to both conceptual development and practical skill acquisition. As Dorling emphasises, "progression in Computing is not simply about moving from simple to complex tasks, but about deepening understanding of fundamental concepts whilst expanding the contexts in which they are applied."

## Theoretical Foundations for Sequencing

### Spiral Curriculum Model

The spiral curriculum approach, advocated by Miles Berry and embedded within the National Curriculum for Computing, suggests that core concepts should be revisited repeatedly across Key Stages with increasing depth and complexity. Berry's 2013 paper "Computing in the National Curriculum: A Guide for Primary Teachers" articulates how abstraction, algorithms, decomposition, pattern recognition, and evaluation form recurring threads throughout a child's Computing education.

Simon Peyton Jones, in his influential 2011 Royal Society report "Shut down or restart? The way forward for computing in UK schools," argued that Computing should follow mathematical principles of sequencing: establishing foundational concepts early, then building systematically upon them. His advocacy for computational thinking as a foundational literacy directly influenced the structure of the 2014 National Curriculum.

### Threshold Concepts

Sue Sentance's research at King's College London identifies specific threshold concepts in Computing—transformative ideas that, once understood, fundamentally change a learner's perception of the discipline. These include:

- **Abstraction**: The ability to remove unnecessary detail whilst retaining essential features
- **State and its manipulation**: Understanding how data persists and changes through program execution
- **Algorithmic thinking**: Recognising that problems can be solved through precise sequences of instructions
- **Decomposition**: Breaking complex problems into manageable components

Sentance argues that curriculum sequencing must explicitly plan for encounters with these threshold concepts, recognising that learners may struggle initially before achieving breakthrough understanding.

## National Curriculum Sequencing Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum establishes foundational concepts that all subsequent learning builds upon:

**Algorithms and Programming:**
- Understand what algorithms are and how they are implemented as programs on digital devices
- Create and debug simple programs
- Use logical reasoning to predict the behaviour of simple programs

**Data and Information:**
- Use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content

The sequencing here moves from unplugged activities and concrete manipulatives (as advocated by Phil Bagge's "Computing Unplugged" approaches) towards screen-based programming using environments like Bee-Bots, ScratchJr, or Kodable.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The curriculum deliberately sequences increasing complexity:

**Computational Thinking:**
- Design, write and debug programs that accomplish specific goals
- Use sequence, selection, and repetition in programs
- Work with variables and various forms of input and output
- Use logical reasoning to explain how simple algorithms work and to detect and correct errors

**Networking and Digital Systems:**
- Understand computer networks including the internet
- Appreciate how search results are selected and ranked

Phil Bagge's progression model, detailed in his "Primary Computing" series, demonstrates how KS2 should sequence from simple sequential programs (Year 3) through selection and repetition (Year 4) to variables and more complex control structures (Years 5-6). This sequencing ensures prerequisite knowledge is secure before advancement.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum demands significant conceptual development:

**Programming and Development:**
- Use two or more programming languages, at least one of which is textual
- Understand several key algorithms including sorting and searching
- Use logical reasoning to compare the utility of alternative algorithms
- Understand simple Boolean logic and its application in programming

**Data Representation:**
- Understand how numbers, text, images and sound are represented in binary
- Understand how data of various types can be represented and manipulated digitally

Mark Dorling's Progression Pathways framework identifies that KS3 sequencing must transition learners from block-based programming to text-based languages (typically Python), whilst simultaneously deepening understanding of core computer science concepts like data structures, algorithms, and computational complexity.

The CAS/Barefoot Computing "Computational Thinking in Practice" guidance (2015) emphasises that KS3 sequencing should deliberately revisit KS2 concepts (sequence, selection, repetition) in more sophisticated contexts, introducing formal notation, efficiency considerations, and multiple solution approaches.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE Computing specifications from all major exam boards follow similar sequencing principles, building upon KS3 foundations:

**AQA Computer Science (8525):**
Sequences content across two years, beginning with fundamentals of algorithms and programming (Paper 1) before advancing to data representation, systems architecture, and networks (Paper 2). The programming project requires synthesis of prior learning.

**OCR Computer Science (J277):**
Structures learning around computational thinking, with explicit progression from understanding algorithms to designing, implementing, and testing solutions. Paper 2's sequencing moves from systems architecture through to ethical considerations.

**Edexcel Computer Science (1CP2):**
Sequences core content (programming, data, hardware) in Year 10, with Year 11 focusing on application, problem-solving, and the controlled assessment.

**WJEC Computer Science:**
Similar spiral approach, revisiting core concepts with Welsh contextualization where appropriate.

Sue Sentance's research on GCSE Computing pedagogy (2019) found that effective KS4 sequencing requires regular retrieval practice of foundational concepts whilst progressively introducing more complex applications.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-Level specifications sequence content with greater theoretical depth and mathematical rigour:

**AQA A-Level Computer Science (7517):**
Year 12 typically covers programming fundamentals, data structures, algorithms and complexity, and theory of computation. Year 13 advances to advanced algorithms, system architecture, databases, and the major project. This sequencing ensures foundational understanding before tackling complex applications.

**OCR A-Level Computer Science (H446):**
Sequences across four teaching units, deliberately returning to core concepts with increasing sophistication. The programming project (coursework) synthesizes learning across both years.

Miles Berry's guidance on KS5 Computing emphasizes that sequencing must prepare students for undergraduate Computer Science, requiring exposure to functional programming, formal algorithm analysis, and theoretical foundations.

## Vertical Progression Across Key Stages

### Programming Concepts

Effective sequencing of programming follows a clear developmental path:

**KS1:** Physical embodiment of algorithms (unplugged activities), then visual programming with immediate feedback (Bee-Bots, ScratchJr)

**KS2:** Block-based programming (Scratch), introducing sequence, selection, repetition, variables, and events systematically

**KS3:** Transition to text-based languages (Python typically), introducing functions, parameters, return values, and more complex data structures

**KS4:** Object-oriented concepts, file handling, SQL, algorithm efficiency, and substantial problem-solving

**KS5:** Advanced data structures (trees, graphs, stacks, queues), recursion, functional programming paradigms, formal algorithm analysis

Simon Peyton Jones emphasises that this progression must maintain "computational fluency"—regular programming practice at each stage, not isolated theoretical study.

### Data and Information

Phil Bagge's sequencing model for data concepts progresses from:

**KS1:** Creating and organizing simple digital content

**KS2:** Understanding data types, simple databases, spreadsheet manipulation, search techniques

**KS3:** Binary representation, data storage, compression, encryption fundamentals

**KS4:** Hexadecimal, character encoding, image/sound representation, database normalization

**KS5:** Floating-point representation, data structures, big data concepts, advanced database theory

### Hardware and Processing

Mark Dorling's progression framework sequences hardware understanding:

**KS1:** Recognizing common digital devices and their purposes

**KS2:** Understanding internal components, input/output devices, basic networking

**KS3:** CPU architecture, fetch-execute cycle, memory hierarchy, networking protocols

**KS4:** Von Neumann architecture, assembly language basics, network topologies, protocols in depth

**KS5:** Pipelining, cache memory, parallel processing, advanced networking, operating systems theory

## Horizontal Coherence Within Key Stages

### Integration of Strands

Sue Sentance's research on curriculum coherence (2016) demonstrates that effective sequencing integrates the three National Curriculum strands—Computer Science, IT, and Digital Literacy—rather than teaching them separately.

For example, a KS3 unit on digital graphics should sequence:
1. Understanding of binary image representation (Computer Science)
2. Practical image manipulation skills (Information Technology)
3. Consideration of copyright, attribution, and appropriate use (Digital Literacy)

This integrated approach, advocated by CAS and documented in their curriculum resources, ensures learners understand both the theoretical foundations and practical applications simultaneously.

### Cross-Curricular Sequencing

Miles Berry's work on cross-curricular Computing emphasizes that sequencing should consider how Computing skills support learning in other subjects:

- **Mathematics:** Logical reasoning, algorithms, data handling
- **Science:** Computational modeling, data analysis, simulations
- **Design Technology:** CAD/CAM, control systems, iterative design
- **English:** Digital communication, research skills, presentation

Effective sequencing ensures Computing concepts are introduced before (or concurrently with) their application in other curriculum areas.

## Practical Sequencing Strategies

### Medium-Term Planning

Phil Bagge's "Primary Computing" scheme demonstrates effective medium-term sequencing within units:

1. **Introduction:** Unplugged or exploratory activities establishing core concepts
2. **Direct Instruction:** Explicit teaching of new skills or knowledge
3. **Guided Practice:** Scaffolded application with teacher support
4. **Independent Application:** Learners apply concepts to new contexts
5. **Review and Consolidation:** Retrieval practice and assessment

Each lesson within a unit builds upon prior knowledge, with regular opportunities for consolidation.

### Long-Term Planning

Mark Dorling's Progression Pathways tool enables schools to map long-term sequencing, ensuring:

- **No gaps:** All National Curriculum requirements covered
- **No unnecessary repetition:** Each year group advances learning
- **Smooth transitions:** KS2/3 and KS4/5 transitions carefully managed
- **Depth over breadth:** Sufficient time for deep understanding before progression

### Assessment-Informed Sequencing

Sue Sentance's research emphasises that sequencing must respond to formative assessment. Key principles include:

- **Pre-assessment:** Understanding prior knowledge before introducing new concepts
- **Diagnostic assessment:** Identifying misconceptions requiring re-teaching
- **Summative assessment:** Confirming readiness for progression to next stage
- **Retrieval practice:** Regular revisiting of prior learning to combat forgetting

CAS resources advocate for "low-stakes quizzing" at each Key Stage, informing sequencing decisions and identifying where additional consolidation is required.

## Common Sequencing Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: Transition from KS2 to KS3

Research by Sue Sentance (2014) identified that KS2 primary specialists often teach Computing well, but KS3 teachers assume less prior knowledge than pupils possess. Effective sequencing requires:

- **Transition documentation:** Clear communication of KS2 coverage
- **Bridging units:** KS3 units that acknowledge and build upon KS2 learning
- **Assessment of prior learning:** Establishing actual rather than assumed starting points

### Challenge 2: Mixed Prior Attainment

Miles Berry acknowledges that Computing classes often have wide attainment ranges due to variable prior experience. Sequencing solutions include:

- **Flexible starting points:** Tasks with low floors and high ceilings
- **Differentiated pathways:** Core content plus extension challenges
- **Peer teaching:** More experienced learners supporting others consolidates their own understanding

### Challenge 3: Rapid Technological Change

Simon Peyton Jones argues that sequencing must focus on enduring concepts rather than current technologies. Effective sequencing prioritizes:

- **Fundamental concepts:** Algorithms, data structures, computational thinking
- **Transferable skills:** Problem-solving approaches applicable across contexts
- **Principles over tools:** Understanding why before which tool

### Challenge 4: Adequate Computing Time

Phil Bagge's surveys of primary schools reveal many allocate insufficient curriculum time for Computing. Where time is limited, sequencing must:

- **Prioritize core concepts:** Focus on essential National Curriculum requirements
- **Integrate efficiently:** Link Computing with other subjects where appropriate
- **Maximize active learning time:** Reduce setup time through preparation and systems

## Sequencing for Different Learner Groups

### SEND Considerations

Effective sequencing for learners with SEND, as documented in the CAS "Computing for All" guidance, requires:

- **Smaller steps:** Breaking progression into more granular stages
- **Additional consolidation time:** More practice before advancing
- **Multi-sensory approaches:** Physical computing, unplugged activities
- **Assistive technologies:** Screen readers, alternative input devices

### More Able Learners

Sequencing for high-attaining learners should provide:

- **Accelerated progression:** Moving through foundational concepts more quickly
- **Greater depth:** Exploring concepts in more sophisticated contexts (e.g., competitive programming, cryptography)
- **Independent projects:** Opportunities for self-directed learning beyond prescribed curriculum

Miles Berry's guidance suggests enrichment through competitions (Bebras, Olympiad), not simply acceleration through Key Stages.

## Institutional Sequencing Strategies

### Whole-School Computing Curriculum Maps

Mark Dorling recommends schools create curriculum maps showing:

- **Concepts taught:** Progression of key concepts across year groups
- **Skills developed:** Programming, data handling, digital literacy skills
- **Assessment points:** Where understanding is formally assessed
- **Resources required:** Software, hardware, online platforms needed

### Subject Leader Responsibilities

The Computing subject leader's role in sequencing includes:

- **Mapping existing practice:** Auditing current curriculum coverage
- **Identifying gaps:** Ensuring National Curriculum requirements met
- **Professional development:** Supporting staff to teach sequenced curriculum
- **Monitoring impact:** Evaluating whether sequencing achieves intended progression

### Collaborative Planning

Sue Sentance's research on effective Computing departments found that collaborative planning significantly improves sequencing:

- **Year group teams:** Planning units together ensures consistency
- **Key Stage teams:** Ensuring smooth transitions between year groups
- **Cross-phase liaison:** Primary-secondary and secondary-post-16 collaboration

## Evidence-Based Sequencing Decisions

### Research on Learning Progressions

The TPACK framework (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge), while not UK-specific, informs sequencing decisions by emphasizing that effective Computing teaching requires:

- **Content knowledge:** Understanding of Computer Science concepts
- **Pedagogical knowledge:** How learners develop understanding
- **Technological knowledge:** Appropriate tools for learning

Sequencing must develop all three dimensions.

### Cognitive Load Theory

Work by educational psychologists, applied to Computing by researchers including Sue Sentance, suggests effective sequencing should:

- **Reduce extraneous load:** Simplify tasks initially, removing unnecessary complexity
- **Manage intrinsic load:** Ensure prerequisite knowledge secure before advancing
- **Optimize germane load:** Focus attention on concepts being learned

For example, when introducing loops in programming, effective sequencing uses simple, familiar contexts (e.g., drawing squares) before complex applications (e.g., data processing), reducing cognitive load and focusing attention on the loop concept itself.

### Worked Examples and Problem-Solving

Research by Sue Sentance and colleagues at King's College London demonstrates that sequencing should move from worked examples through partially-completed problems to independent problem-solving—the "fading" approach. This applies particularly to programming, where learners benefit from studying annotated code before writing their own.

## Looking Forward: Evolving Sequencing Practice

As Computing education matures, sequencing practices continue to evolve. Current developments include:

- **Physical computing:** Earlier introduction of hardware programming (micro:bit, Raspberry Pi) from KS2 onwards
- **Data science:** Increasing emphasis on data analysis and visualization, particularly at KS4-5
- **AI and machine learning:** Consideration of where these concepts fit within progression pathways
- **Cybersecurity:** More explicit sequencing of security concepts across Key Stages

Simon Peyton Jones's recent work with the Raspberry Pi Foundation emphasizes that sequencing must remain flexible, responding to societal changes whilst maintaining focus on fundamental computational concepts.

## Conclusion

Effective sequencing in Computing requires deep understanding of both the discipline's conceptual structure and how learners develop computational thinking. The work of Mark Dorling, Sue Sentance, Miles Berry, Phil Bagge, and Simon Peyton Jones provides robust frameworks for designing progression pathways that build systematically from EYFS through Key Stage 5.
