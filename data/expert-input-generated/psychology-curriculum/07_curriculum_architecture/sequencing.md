# Sequencing Psychology Topics

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in Psychology

Sequencing psychology topics represents one of the most critical decisions in curriculum architecture, determining not only what students learn but when and in what order they encounter psychological concepts. Unlike subjects with inherently linear structures, psychology presents unique challenges: concepts are deeply interconnected, theoretical perspectives compete and complement simultaneously, and empirical evidence continuously evolves.

Cara Flanagan, whose work on psychology curriculum design has significantly influenced UK practice, emphasises that effective sequencing must balance conceptual progression with maintaining student engagement and ensuring that foundational knowledge supports increasingly sophisticated understanding. The sequencing decisions made at departmental level fundamentally shape whether students develop genuine psychological literacy or merely accumulate disconnected facts.

## Principles of Effective Sequencing in Psychology

### Building Conceptual Frameworks

Psychology education benefits from establishing clear conceptual frameworks before introducing specific studies or applications. Philip Banyard's approach to teaching psychology emphasises the importance of students understanding the "big ideas" – such as the scientific method, individual differences, and the interaction between nature and nurture – before exploring detailed content.

Effective sequencing should ensure students encounter:

1. **Foundational concepts first**: Basic psychological terminology, research methods, and the scientific approach to studying behaviour
2. **Progressive complexity**: Moving from observable behaviour to internal mental processes, from simple explanations to multi-factorial models
3. **Spiral curriculum design**: Revisiting core concepts with increasing sophistication across key stages

Research by Bjork and Bjork (2011) on desirable difficulties supports spaced repetition of key concepts rather than massed practice, suggesting that returning to core ideas (such as the nature-nurture debate) across different topics strengthens long-term retention more effectively than teaching them once comprehensively.

### Cognitive Load Considerations

When sequencing psychology content, teachers must consider cognitive load theory. Mike Cardwell's work on psychology teaching emphasises that students cannot simultaneously grapple with new psychological concepts, unfamiliar research methodology, and complex terminology. Effective sequencing introduces these elements progressively.

For instance, when teaching approaches in psychology, begin with behaviourism because its focus on observable behaviour and relatively straightforward principles (classical and operant conditioning) creates lower cognitive load than immediately introducing cognitive psychology with its emphasis on unobservable mental processes and information-processing models.

## Sequencing by Key Stage

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Though psychology is not a National Curriculum subject at KS3, many schools introduce psychological concepts through PSHE, science, or dedicated enrichment programmes.

**Recommended sequence for KS3 introduction:**

1. **What is psychology?** – Establishing psychology as a science of behaviour and mental processes
2. **How psychologists study behaviour** – Simple observational methods and basic experimental concepts
3. **Memory and learning** – Concrete concepts students can relate to their own experience
4. **Social influence** – Conformity and obedience studies that engage adolescent interests
5. **Development** – Attachment and child development linking to students' own experiences

This sequence moves from concrete to abstract, from personally relevant to more theoretical, building confidence before introducing complex concepts.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE, Ages 14-16)

GCSE specifications from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC each suggest different sequencing, but common principles emerge.

**AQA GCSE Psychology (8182) sequencing considerations:**

The specification divides content into Cognition and Behaviour; Social Context and Behaviour; and Development. Mark Billingham, senior examiner for AQA, suggests schools should not necessarily teach these in specification order. Instead, consider:

1. **Research methods throughout** – Integrated across all topics rather than as standalone unit
2. **Memory first** – Accessible entry point with clear practical applications
3. **Perception or Social Influence second** – Both offer engaging studies (visual illusions or Asch/Milgram) that consolidate research methods understanding
4. **Development mid-course** – Once students grasp research evaluation and can handle Piaget's more abstract concepts
5. **Psychological problems later** – Requires understanding of approaches and ability to synthesise across topics

**Edexcel GCSE Psychology (1PS0) sequencing:**

Edexcel's thematic approach (Development; Psychological Problems; Brain and Neuropsychology; Social Influence; Criminal Psychology) lends itself to different sequencing:

1. **Social Influence** – High engagement value with Milgram and Asch studies
2. **Development** – Building on social influence with attachment and Piaget
3. **Brain and Neuropsychology** – Introduces biological basis after establishing behavioural understanding
4. **Criminal Psychology** – Applies learning from previous topics
5. **Psychological Problems** – Synthesises biological, cognitive, and learning approaches

### Key Stage 5 (A-Level, Ages 16-18)

A-Level psychology presents the most significant sequencing challenges due to content volume and conceptual complexity.

**AQA A-Level Psychology (7182) sequencing recommendations:**

Cara Flanagan's guidance for AQA A-Level emphasises that the specification order (Social Influence, Memory, Attachment, Psychopathology, Approaches, Biopsychology, Research Methods, Issues and Debates) serves administrative purposes but is not pedagogically optimal.

**Research-informed sequence for Year 12:**

1. **Research Methods (introduction)** – Essential foundation, though phased throughout the year
2. **Memory** – Concrete concepts, accessible studies, clear methodology
3. **Attachment** – Builds on research methods, introduces animal studies debate
4. **Social Influence** – More complex evaluation, requires confident research methods knowledge
5. **Psychopathology** – Synthesises approaches, requires maturity in evaluation
6. **Approaches in Psychology** – Provides theoretical framework retrospectively applied to previous topics

**Rationale**: This sequence develops research methods skills progressively whilst building from concrete (memory models) to more abstract (psychopathology). Teaching approaches at Year 12 end allows students to revisit earlier topics through different theoretical lenses.

**Year 13 sequencing:**

1. **Biopsychology** – Consolidates biological approach from Year 12
2. **Research Methods (advanced)** – Statistical testing and advanced research design
3. **Optional topics** – Selected based on teacher expertise and student interest
4. **Issues and Debates** – Requires comprehensive understanding of all Year 12 content

Philip Banyard advocates for integrating Issues and Debates throughout both years rather than as discrete end-point, arguing that psychology's cross-cutting themes (such as socially sensitive research, free will versus determinism, reductionism) gain meaning when encountered within specific content contexts.

## Vertical Sequencing: Within-Topic Architecture

### Memory Sequencing Example

Within individual topics, sequencing decisions significantly impact comprehension. Using memory as exemplar:

**Less effective sequence:**
- Working memory model → Multi-store model → Types of long-term memory → Forgetting (interference) → Forgetting (retrieval failure) → Eyewitness testimony

**More effective sequence:**
1. **Multi-store model** – Foundational, introduces key terminology (STM, LTM, encoding, retrieval)
2. **Working memory model** – Develops understanding of STM specifically, introduces alternative models
3. **Types of long-term memory** – Expands LTM understanding
4. **Explanations for forgetting** – Interference and retrieval failure together, showing competing explanations
5. **Eyewitness testimony** – Applies theoretical understanding to real-world context

This sequence progresses from breadth (MSM overview) to depth (specific memory stores) to application, with each stage building prerequisite knowledge for the next.

### Psychopathology Sequencing

Psychopathology presents particular sequencing challenges because understanding psychological disorders requires integration of multiple approaches.

**Mike Cardwell's recommended sequence:**

1. **Definitions of abnormality** – Establishes fundamental question of what constitutes mental disorder
2. **Depression** – Clear symptoms, accessible to teenage understanding
3. **OCD** – Introduces anxiety disorders, different treatment approaches
4. **Phobias** – Demonstrates learning theory applications clearly
5. **Approaches to treatment** – Biological and psychological interventions across disorders

Alternative sequences might begin with phobias (simpler behavioural explanations) before depression (more complex, multi-factorial). The optimal choice depends on class confidence and prior knowledge.

## Horizontal Sequencing: Interleaving and Retrieval Practice

Recent cognitive science research emphasises that sequencing decisions extend beyond topic order to include strategic interleaving and retrieval practice opportunities.

### Interleaving Psychology Content

Rather than blocking topics (completing memory entirely before starting attachment), interleaving involves alternating between topics. Research by Rohrer and Taylor (2007) demonstrates that interleaved practice enhances discrimination between concepts and improves long-term retention.

**Practical application in psychology:**

Whilst completing memory's multi-store model, introduce attachment's Strange Situation study, then return to working memory model, then cover Ainsworth's attachment types. This approach:

- Reduces cognitive overload by varying cognitive demands
- Strengthens retrieval pathways through spaced practice
- Helps students recognise methodological similarities across different content areas
- Maintains engagement through variety

Cara Flanagan acknowledges that interleaving requires careful planning to ensure students maintain coherent understanding and aren't confused by rapid topic changes. Successful interleaving depends on clear signposting and explicit connections.

### Retrieval Practice Architecture

Building retrieval practice into sequence planning ensures students regularly access previously learned material. This might involve:

- **Low-stakes quizzing** on memory content whilst teaching attachment
- **Comparative tasks** requiring students to contrast research methods across different studies
- **Cumulative assessments** that require integration of multiple topics

## Approaches-Based Versus Topics-Based Sequencing

A fundamental sequencing decision involves whether to structure courses around psychological approaches (biological, behavioural, cognitive, psychodynamic, humanistic) or around topics (memory, attachment, social influence).

### Topics-Based Sequencing (Current UK Mainstream)

All major UK exam boards structure specifications around topics. This approach:

**Advantages:**
- Allows depth of study within specific areas
- Facilitates systematic coverage of research evidence
- Aligns with university psychology course structures
- Supports clear assessment objectives

**Disadvantages:**
- Students may struggle to recognise approaches operating across different topics
- Requires explicit teaching of theoretical perspectives retrospectively
- Can fragment understanding of psychology as unified discipline

### Approaches-Based Sequencing (Alternative Model)

Some educators, including Philip Banyard in earlier work, have advocated for approaches-first sequencing:

1. **Biological approach** – Brain structure, neurotransmitters, genetic influences
2. **Behavioural approach** – Classical and operant conditioning, systematic desensitisation
3. **Cognitive approach** – Information processing, schema theory, cognitive therapies
4. **Psychodynamic approach** – Freud, defence mechanisms, psychoanalysis
5. **Humanistic approach** – Self-actualisation, person-centred therapy

Topics like memory, attachment, or psychopathology are explored through each approach's lens.

**Advantages:**
- Provides clear theoretical frameworks before detailed content
- Emphasises psychology's multi-perspective nature
- Facilitates Issues and Debates understanding
- Reduces fragmentation

**Disadvantages:**
- Less alignment with exam specifications
- May lack engaging "hook" studies that draw students into psychology
- Can feel abstract without concrete content examples
- Challenges assessment design

**Hybrid approach**: Mark Billingham suggests teaching key approaches concepts early (perhaps first half-term) then exploring topics whilst consistently referencing relevant approaches. This maintains specification alignment whilst building theoretical literacy.

## Sequencing Considerations by Student Cohort

### Prior Attainment and Sequencing

Student prior attainment in science influences optimal sequencing. Classes with strong science backgrounds can handle biopsychology earlier because they possess foundational knowledge of neurons, synapses, and experimental design. Classes with weaker science backgrounds benefit from delaying biopsychology until Year 13, first building confidence with more accessible topics.

Similarly, research methods sequencing should reflect mathematical confidence. Classes requiring extensive scaffolding in statistical testing benefit from introducing descriptive statistics (measures of central tendency and dispersion) in Year 12, reserving inferential statistics for Year 13.

### Cultural Capital and Accessibility

Cara Flanagan has written extensively about ensuring psychology education is accessible to students from diverse backgrounds. Sequencing decisions impact accessibility:

- Beginning with culturally-embedded content (attachment theory's Western nuclear family assumptions) may alienate some students
- Starting with universal cognitive processes (memory) provides more equitable entry point
- Social influence studies (conformity, obedience) engage students from diverse backgrounds but require sensitive handling of ethical issues

## Practical Implementation for Teachers

### Departmental Planning Considerations

When departments determine teaching sequences, consider:

1. **Teacher expertise distribution** – Assign topics to teachers based on specialist knowledge whilst ensuring all students experience consistent quality
2. **Resource availability** – If practical equipment for memory experiments is limited, stagger memory teaching across different classes
3. **Assessment scheduling** – Sequence topics so mock examinations can assess meaningful content combinations
4. **Student engagement patterns** – Place potentially less engaging content (research methods theory) strategically when motivation typically dips

### Scheme of Work Architecture

Effective schemes of work should specify:

- **Lesson-level sequencing** within each topic
- **Explicit prerequisite knowledge** required before introducing new concepts
- **Retrieval practice opportunities** for previous content
- **Formative assessment points** checking understanding before progression
- **Interleaving strategy** if employed

### Communicating Sequence to Students

Research on metacognition (Flavell, 1979) demonstrates that students benefit from understanding why content is sequenced as it is. Explicitly explain:

- How today's lesson builds on previous learning
- What foundational knowledge is required for upcoming content
- Why topics are taught in specific orders
- How different topics connect and reinforce each other

This metacognitive awareness helps students construct coherent mental schema rather than viewing psychology as disconnected facts.

## Sequencing Research Methods

Research methods presents unique sequencing challenges because it functions simultaneously as discrete content (examination questions assess methods knowledge) and as essential toolkit for evaluating substantive psychological research.

### Integrated Versus Discrete Approaches

**Discrete approach** teaches research methods as standalone block, typically:
- Early in course (first half-term)
- Or immediately before examinations

**Integrated approach** introduces methods concepts when first encountered within substantive topics, then consolidates understanding later.

Mike Cardwell advocates integrated approach because:
- Students understand methods' purpose when applied to real research
- Reduces cognitive load by distributing methods learning across course
- Prevents methods becoming "boring" standalone block
- Mirrors how psychologists actually use research methods

**Recommended sequence:**
1. **Basic experimental concepts** (IV, DV, experimental/control conditions) introduced with first study encountered
2. **Research design types** (laboratory, field, natural experiments) distributed across Social Influence, Memory, Attachment topics
3. **Sampling methods** when discussing study generalisability
4. **Ethical considerations** when encountering ethically controversial research (Milgram, Harlow)
5. **Statistical testing** in Year 13 once students have encountered sufficient data sets
6. **Consolidation unit** before examinations, synthesising all methods content

## Sequencing for Examination Success

### AQA A-Level Assessment Architecture

AQA's three-paper structure influences sequencing decisions:

- **Paper 1** (Introductory Topics): Social Influence, Memory, Attachment, Psychopathology
- **Paper 2** (Psychology in Context): Approaches, Biopsychology, Research Methods
- **Paper 3** (Issues and Debates): plus optional topics

Some schools teach all Paper 1 content in Year 12, then Paper 2 content in Year 13. However, this creates problems:

- Approaches taught too late to inform Year 12 topic evaluation
- Research methods taught too late for Year 12 mock examinations
- Students lack Issues and Debates framework when learning substantive content

**More effective sequence** intersperses Paper 1 and Paper 2 content, ensuring approaches and research methods inform topic teaching throughout.

### Building Examination Skills Progressively

Sequencing should consider progression in examination skills alongside content:

**Year 12 focus:**
- AO1 (knowledge and understanding) – description skills
- AO2 (application) – applying knowledge to novel scenarios
- AO3 (evaluation) – basic evaluation using research evidence

**Year 13 development:**
- Extended AO1 – integrating knowledge from multiple topics
- Complex AO2 – multi-step application to unfamiliar contexts
- Sophisticated AO3 – using Issues and Debates for evaluation, comparing approaches

Topic sequencing should provide graduated opportunities to develop these skills, not expecting Year 12 students to immediately produce Year 13 level evaluation.
