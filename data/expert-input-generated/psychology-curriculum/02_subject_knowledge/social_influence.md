# Social Influence

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Social influence is a foundational topic within UK Psychology education, particularly at A-Level and forms a core component of the AQA Psychology A-Level specification (7181/7182). This topic examines how individuals' thoughts, feelings and behaviours are affected by the actual, imagined or implied presence of others. The study of social influence emerged from crucial 20th-century research examining conformity, obedience and group behaviour, with significant implications for understanding historical events and contemporary social phenomena.

According to Flanagan and Cardwell (2015) in their comprehensive textbook *Psychology AS/A Level*, social influence represents one of the most empirically robust areas within psychology, with clear applications to education, criminal justice, and public health policy. The topic typically occupies 15-20% of the first year A-Level Psychology curriculum across UK exam boards.

## Key Theoretical Frameworks

### Types of Conformity

Conformity refers to yielding to group pressure, and Kelman (1958) identified three distinct types that remain central to UK Psychology curricula:

**Compliance** occurs when individuals publicly conform to group norms whilst privately maintaining their own views. This represents the most superficial level of conformity, driven by normative social influence (the desire to be liked or accepted).

**Identification** involves conformity to the demands of a given social role, adopting the group's attitudes and behaviours because there is something about the group we value. This type is temporary and context-dependent.

**Internalisation** represents the deepest level of conformity, where individuals genuinely accept group norms as their own beliefs. This occurs through informational social influence (the desire to be right) and results in permanent change.

### Explanations for Conformity

Deutsch and Gerard (1955) proposed two fundamental explanations for conformity behaviour:

**Normative Social Influence (NSI)** occurs when people conform to be liked, accepted or to avoid social rejection. This explanation accounts for compliance and is particularly powerful in situations involving strangers or stressful circumstances. AQA specifications explicitly require students to evaluate NSI using research evidence, including Asch's (1951) variations demonstrating increased conformity with larger group sizes.

**Informational Social Influence (ISI)** operates when people conform because they genuinely believe others possess superior knowledge or are more likely to be correct. This particularly occurs in ambiguous situations or where individuals feel they lack expertise. Jenness's (1932) bean-counting experiment exemplifies ISI, though UK specifications typically emphasise Sherif's (1935) autokinetic effect studies.

## Core Research Studies

### Asch's Line Study (1951, 1955)

Asch's conformity experiments remain compulsory content across all UK A-Level Psychology specifications. In the baseline study, 123 American male undergraduate participants were asked to match line lengths in groups containing confederates who deliberately gave incorrect answers. Findings revealed that 36.8% of responses conformed to the incorrect majority, whilst 75% of participants conformed at least once.

**Key variations examined in UK curricula include:**

- **Group size**: Conformity increased with group size up to approximately 3-4 confederates, then plateaued
- **Unanimity**: When one confederate gave correct answers, conformity dropped to 5.5%
- **Task difficulty**: Ambiguous tasks increased conformity through ISI
- **Answer privacy**: Written responses reduced conformity significantly

Flanagan and Davies (2019) emphasise that teachers should encourage critical evaluation of Asch's work, particularly regarding temporal validity (1950s American culture), gender bias (male participants), and ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting).

### Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (1971)

Though ethically controversial, Zimbardo's research remains crucial for understanding conformity to social roles (identification). The study assigned 21 emotionally stable male university students to prisoner or guard roles in a simulated prison environment. The experiment, planned for two weeks, terminated after six days due to participants' extreme behavioural changes.

Guards became increasingly brutal and authoritarian, whilst prisoners showed signs of severe psychological distress. Zimbardo attributed these changes to conformity to social role expectations rather than individual disposition.

**UK curriculum requirements** (particularly AQA and Edexcel) mandate discussion of:

- Dispositional versus situational explanations for behaviour
- Ethical issues (harm to participants, lack of informed consent, researcher bias)
- Alternative interpretations (Reicher and Haslam's 2006 BBC Prison Study)
- Control and validity issues

Banyard and Grayson (2017) note that recent reanalyses question Zimbardo's conclusions, with evidence suggesting guards were coached to behave harshly, making this an important case study for developing students' critical thinking skills.

### Milgram's Obedience Studies (1963)

Milgram's research on obedience to authority represents perhaps the most significant social psychology research in UK specifications. The baseline study involved 40 male participants who believed they were administering increasingly severe electric shocks (15-450 volts) to a learner (confederate) for incorrect answers in a supposed memory study.

**Baseline findings:**
- 65% of participants continued to 450 volts (maximum)
- 100% continued to 300 volts
- All participants showed signs of extreme stress and conflict

**Variations mandated by UK exam boards:**

1. **Location**: Obedience dropped to 47.5% in a run-down office building (reduced legitimacy)
2. **Proximity**: Obedience decreased when teacher and learner were in the same room (65% to 40%)
3. **Uniform**: Obedience dropped to 20% when experimenter wore ordinary clothes
4. **Social support**: With disobedient peers present, obedience fell to 10%

Cardwell and Flanagan (2016) stress the importance of contextualising Milgram's work within post-Holocaust psychology's attempt to understand evil actions by ordinary people.

## Situational Explanations for Obedience

### Agentic State Theory

Milgram (1974) proposed that obedience occurs when individuals transition from an autonomous state (acting according to personal values) to an agentic state (seeing oneself as an agent executing another's wishes). In this state, individuals experience moral strain but feel no personal responsibility.

**Agentic shift** occurs due to:
- Perception of legitimate authority
- Gradual commitment (escalation through small requests)
- Buffers that reduce awareness of consequences

UK specifications require evaluation of this theory, including supporting evidence from Milgram's variations and criticism regarding its inability to explain why some individuals resist authority.

### Legitimacy of Authority

This explanation suggests obedience occurs when we perceive authority figures as having rightful power over us. Legitimate authority is established through:
- Social hierarchies learned through socialisation
- Visual symbols (uniforms, institutional settings)
- Cultural norms and expectations

Flanagan and Cardwell (2015) note that this explanation accounts for cross-cultural differences in obedience rates and integrates well with Hofstede's (1980) cultural dimensions theory, particularly power distance.

## Dispositional Explanations for Obedience

### The Authoritarian Personality

Adorno et al. (1950) proposed that individuals with specific personality characteristics are more likely to obey authority. Using the F-scale (Fascism scale), they identified traits including:
- Rigid adherence to conventional values
- Submissiveness to authority figures
- Hostility toward outgroups
- Black-and-white thinking

Adorno attributed this personality type to harsh, conditional parenting creating psychodynamic conflicts. However, UK specifications emphasise critical evaluation, including:
- Methodological flaws (acquiescence bias in F-scale)
- Political bias in question construction
- Limited explanation (cannot account for entire societies showing obedience)
- Alternative explanations (social identity theory)

## Resistance to Social Influence

UK curricula (particularly AQA and OCR) require understanding of how individuals resist conformity and obedience pressures.

### Social Support

Research demonstrates that the presence of dissenting others dramatically reduces conformity and obedience. Asch found unanimity breaking reduced conformity to 5.5%, whilst Milgram observed obedience dropping to 10% with disobedient peers present.

**Psychological mechanisms:**
- Breaks unanimous group position
- Provides model for disobedience
- Reduces normative social influence
- Increases confidence in independent judgment

### Locus of Control

Rotter (1966) proposed that individuals vary in perceived control over life events. Those with internal locus of control (internals) believe they control outcomes through their own efforts, whilst those with external locus of control (externals) attribute outcomes to external forces like luck or powerful others.

Research by Holland (1967) found that internals were more resistant to obedience in Milgram-type situations. However, Flanagan and Davies (2019) caution that the relationship is complex and moderated by situational factors.

## Minority Influence

Whilst majority influence (conformity) receives extensive coverage, UK specifications increasingly emphasise minority influence—how small, consistent groups create social change.

### Moscovici's Research (1969)

Moscovici and colleagues demonstrated that consistent minorities could influence majority judgments. In a colour perception study, a consistent minority calling blue slides "green" influenced 8.42% of majority responses, compared to only 1.25% for inconsistent minorities.

**Key factors in minority influence:**

1. **Consistency** (synchronic and diachronic)—maintaining position over time and between group members
2. **Commitment**—demonstrating personal sacrifice or risk (augmentation principle)
3. **Flexibility**—appearing reasonable rather than rigid
4. **Relevance**—connecting to majority values
5. **Snowball effect**—gradual conversion leading to social cryptoamnesia

### The Process of Social Change

UK curricula require students to understand how minority influence creates social change through:

1. **Drawing attention** to the issue
2. **Cognitive conflict** in majority members
3. **Consistency** of position over time
4. **Augmentation principle** through personal sacrifice
5. **Snowball effect** as more convert
6. **Social cryptoamnesia**—majority forgets minority origins of now-accepted position

**Historical examples** commonly used in UK teaching include suffragette movements, civil rights campaigns, and environmental activism.

## Cultural and Individual Differences

### Cross-Cultural Research

Smith and Bond's (1998) meta-analysis of Asch-type studies across cultures found significant variation, with collectivist cultures showing higher conformity rates than individualist cultures. This challenges universality claims in early social influence research.

Banyard (2019) emphasises that UK teachers should contextualise classic studies within their specific cultural and historical contexts, particularly given contemporary multicultural classroom environments.

### Gender Differences

Early research predominantly used male participants, raising questions about generalisability. Subsequent research (e.g., Eagly & Carli, 1981) suggests gender differences in conformity are small and context-dependent, with women showing slightly higher conformity in group pressure situations but not in private judgment tasks.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Pedagogical Approaches

**1. Critical Evaluation Skills**: Billingham (2018) emphasises that social influence provides excellent opportunities for developing evaluation skills through examining research methods, ethics, and alternative explanations.

**2. Ethical Discussions**: Classic studies raise significant ethical issues, providing natural entry points for discussing BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct, informed consent, protection from harm, and cost-benefit analysis.

**3. Contemporary Applications**: Connect historical research to contemporary phenomena including social media influence, advertising psychology, bystander intervention, and political propaganda.

**4. Practical Activities**: 

- Conformity demonstrations (ensuring ethical compliance)
- Analysis of historical social change movements
- Evaluation of advertising techniques
- Discussion of jury decision-making
- Examination of organisational hierarchies

### Assessment Considerations

UK exam boards require specific assessment objectives:

**AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding)**: Detailed description of research procedures, findings, and theories

**AO2 (Application)**: Applying concepts to novel scenarios, often involving hypothetical situations or real-world examples

**AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation)**: Critical evaluation using research evidence, considering methodological issues, alternative explanations, and real-world applications

Flanagan and Cardwell's (2015) assessment guidance emphasises that social influence questions frequently require integrated answers combining multiple studies and explanations.

## Specification-Specific Content

### AQA Psychology (7181/7182)

Social influence comprises approximately 12-15% of Paper 1 (Introductory Topics in Psychology). Specific requirements include:

- Types and explanations of conformity (NSI/ISI)
- Asch's research and variations
- Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment
- Milgram's baseline and variations
- Situational and dispositional explanations for obedience
- Resistance to social influence
- Minority influence and social change

### Edexcel Psychology (9PSO)

Includes social influence within Topic 1, emphasising applications to understanding crowd behaviour, jury decision-making, and social movements.

### OCR Psychology (H567)

Integrates social influence with individual differences, requiring comparison of social and dispositional explanations across topics.

## Evaluative Themes

### Methodological Issues

UK curricula emphasise critical analysis of research methods in social influence studies:

**Laboratory experiments** (Asch, Milgram): High control but questionable ecological validity and demand characteristics

**Field experiments** (Hofling et al., 1966 hospital study): Greater realism but ethical concerns and less control

**Meta-analyses**: Provide overview but subject to publication bias and cultural/temporal limitations

### Ethical Considerations

Social influence research raises profound ethical questions that UK specifications explicitly require discussion of:

- **Deception**: Necessary for valid results but violates informed consent
- **Protection from harm**: Psychological distress in Milgram and Zimbardo studies
- **Right to withdraw**: Often compromised by experimental pressure
- **Debriefing**: Essential but may not fully reverse harm

Cardwell and Flanagan (2016) argue these studies catalysed modern ethical guidelines, making them valuable teaching tools for developing ethical awareness.

### Real-World Applications

Billingham (2018) emphasises connecting theory to practice:

**Criminal Justice**: Understanding false confessions, eyewitness conformity, jury influence

**Health**: Improving treatment compliance, public health campaigns, peer influence on health behaviours

**Education**: Classroom dynamics, peer pressure, anti-bullying interventions

**Business**: Organisational behaviour, leadership styles, whistleblowing decisions

## Recent Developments and Contemporary Relevance

### Social Media and Digital Influence

Contemporary UK teaching increasingly addresses online conformity, including:
- Social media echo chambers and filter bubbles
- Online disinhibition and cyberbullying
- Viral challenges and online trends
- Digital minority influence and activism

### Replication Crisis

Recent failures to replicate classic findings (e.g., Burger's 2009 partial Milgram replication showing similar results but others showing reduced effects) provide opportunities to discuss research reliability and the self-correcting nature of science.
