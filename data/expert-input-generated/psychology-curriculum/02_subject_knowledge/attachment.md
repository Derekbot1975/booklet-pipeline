# Attachment

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Attachment theory represents one of the foundational topics in A-Level Psychology across all major UK exam boards. The topic appears prominently in AQA Psychology (7182), Edexcel Psychology (9PSO), OCR Psychology (H567), and WJEC Psychology specifications. Attachment is typically taught as part of developmental psychology or social-developmental approaches, usually in Year 12.

According to Flanagan and colleagues (2015), attachment theory provides students with their first systematic exposure to how early relationships shape psychological development, making it pedagogically crucial for establishing understanding of the nature-nurture debate and longitudinal research methods.

## Theoretical Foundations

### John Bowlby's Attachment Theory (1958-1988)

Bowlby's monotropic theory forms the cornerstone of attachment teaching in UK Psychology. His key propositions include:

**Monotropy**: Bowlby proposed that infants form one primary attachment relationship, usually with the biological mother, which is qualitatively different from other attachments. This concept is assessed across all UK exam boards, with AQA specifically requiring evaluation of the monotropic assumption.

**Critical Period**: Bowlby identified a critical period (birth to approximately 2.5 years, with a sensitive period extending to 5 years) during which attachment must develop. As Cardwell and Flanagan (2016) emphasise in their AQA specification teaching materials, this concept links directly to later discussions of privation and deprivation.

**Internal Working Model**: This cognitive framework, developed through early attachment experiences, shapes expectations about relationships throughout life. The internal working model demonstrates clear links to cognitive psychology, allowing teachers to make cross-topic connections, as recommended by Banyard and Grayson (2000).

**Social Releasers**: Innate behaviours (crying, smiling, cooing) that elicit caregiving responses. These demonstrate the biological basis of attachment, satisfying curriculum requirements for biopsychological understanding.

**The Maternal Deprivation Hypothesis**: Bowlby (1951) proposed that separation from the primary caregiver during the critical period leads to long-term cognitive, social, and emotional difficulties. His work for the World Health Organisation has particular historical significance in UK child welfare policy.

### Mary Ainsworth's Strange Situation (1970, 1978)

Ainsworth's Strange Situation represents the most significant methodological contribution to attachment research and features prominently in all UK specifications.

**Procedure**: The Strange Situation comprises eight episodes, each lasting approximately three minutes, involving systematic separations and reunions between infant and caregiver in an unfamiliar environment. As Billingham and colleagues (2019) note in their OCR teaching materials, students must understand both the procedural details and the theoretical rationale.

**Attachment Classifications**:

1. **Secure Attachment (Type B)**: Approximately 60-75% of British infants. The infant explores confidently with the caregiver present, shows distress at separation, and seeks comfort upon reunion. Associated with sensitive, responsive caregiving.

2. **Insecure-Avoidant (Type A)**: Approximately 20-25% of British samples. The infant shows limited separation distress and actively avoids contact upon reunion. Associated with rejecting caregiving.

3. **Insecure-Resistant/Ambivalent (Type C)**: Approximately 3-5% of British samples. The infant shows intense distress at separation but cannot be easily comforted upon reunion, displaying anger and resistance. Associated with inconsistent caregiving.

Main and Solomon (1986) later identified:

4. **Disorganised Attachment (Type D)**: Characterised by contradictory behaviours and lack of coherent attachment strategy. Associated with frightening or frightened caregiving and early trauma.

## Research Evidence and Evaluation

### Supporting Evidence

**Hazan and Shaver (1987)**: The 'Love Quiz' study demonstrated continuity between infant attachment types and adult romantic relationships, supporting the internal working model concept. This study appears frequently in AQA and Edexcel mark schemes as sophisticated evaluation material.

**Bailey et al. (2007)**: This longitudinal study found correlations between mothers' attachment types and their infants' attachment classifications, supporting intergenerational transmission. As Flanagan, Berry and Jarvis (2016) highlight, this provides evidence for the lasting influence of early attachment.

**Sroufe et al. (2005)**: The Minnesota Longitudinal Study demonstrated that secure attachment at 12 months predicted social competence in adolescence, supporting Bowlby's claims about long-term consequences.

### Critical Evaluation

**Cultural Variations**: Van IJzendoorn and Kroonenberg (1988) conducted a meta-analysis of Strange Situation studies across eight countries, finding variations in attachment type distributions. This challenges the universality claims of attachment theory. In UK teaching, this study is essential for demonstrating cultural psychology and evaluation skills (AQA Assessment Objective 3).

**Temperament Hypothesis**: Kagan (1984) argued that attachment classifications reflect innate temperamental differences rather than caregiving quality. This represents a crucial nature-nurture debate within the topic, explicitly required by all UK specifications.

**Multiple Attachments**: Schaffer and Emerson (1964), conducted in Glasgow, found that most infants form multiple attachments by 18 months, challenging Bowlby's monotropy principle. Rutter (1981) similarly argued for the importance of multiple attachment figures rather than monotropic relationships.

**Methodological Concerns**: The Strange Situation has been criticised for:
- Artificial laboratory setting reducing ecological validity
- Observer bias in classification (though inter-rater reliability is typically >90%)
- Measuring temperament rather than attachment quality
- Cultural bias towards Western childrearing practices

As Cardwell and Flanagan (2015) emphasise, students must evaluate methodology rigorously when discussing attachment research.

## Cultural and Cross-Cultural Considerations

### Cultural Variations in Attachment

**Grossmann and Grossmann (1991)**: German infants showed higher rates of insecure-avoidant attachment (35-40%), reflecting cultural values of early independence. This demonstrates how cultural childrearing practices influence attachment patterns.

**Takahashi (1990)**: Japanese infants showed higher rates of insecure-resistant attachment and no avoidant attachments, reflecting cultural practices where infants are rarely separated from mothers. However, the study also revealed methodological issues, as the Strange Situation proved too stressful for Japanese infants.

**Simonelli et al. (2014)**: Italian research demonstrated increasing secure attachment rates following parenting interventions, suggesting attachment classifications are malleable and culture-specific.

**Implications for Teaching**: Banyard and Flanagan (2005) emphasise that teachers must present attachment theory as culturally embedded rather than universal, developing students' critical thinking about imposed etic approaches in psychology.

## Attachment Research Methods

### Observational Techniques

The Strange Situation exemplifies controlled observation, teaching students about:
- Structured observation protocols
- Behavioural categories and coding schemes
- Inter-rater reliability
- Ethical considerations in developmental research

**Practical Implications**: Teachers should use video footage of Strange Situation episodes (available through educational suppliers) to develop observational skills, as recommended by OCR guidance documents (2015).

### Longitudinal Studies

Attachment research demonstrates the value and challenges of longitudinal designs:
- Tracking developmental continuity
- Understanding causal relationships
- Managing participant attrition
- Cohort effects

## Practical Applications and Policy Implications

### Childcare and Day Care

Attachment theory has significantly influenced UK childcare policy. The NICHD Study of Early Child Care (1997-2006), though American, is frequently cited in UK policy documents regarding early years provision.

**Robertson and Robertson (1967-1973)**: Their filmed observations of young children in brief separations documented protest-despair-detachment responses, directly influencing UK hospital visiting policies and fostering arrangements.

**Impact on UK Policy**:
- Children Act 1989 incorporated attachment principles
- Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) framework emphasises key person approach
- Adoption and fostering guidelines based on attachment research

### Educational Applications

**Key Person Approach**: The EYFS statutory framework requires designated key persons in early years settings, directly derived from attachment theory. This provides excellent real-world application for student understanding.

**Behaviour Management**: Understanding attachment patterns helps explain challenging behaviours in educational settings. Geddes (2006), in "Attachment in the Classroom", applies attachment theory to UK school contexts.

## Critical Period, Sensitive Period and Later Attachments

### Romanian Orphan Studies

**Rutter et al. (1998, 2007, 2011)**: The English and Romanian Adoptees (ERA) study followed 165 Romanian orphans adopted into UK families. This longitudinal research provided crucial evidence regarding:
- Effects of severe early privation
- Existence of a sensitive rather than critical period
- Possibility of recovery with appropriate intervention
- Individual differences in vulnerability

As Billingham (2020) notes, the ERA study is essential for teaching about privation versus deprivation and demonstrates how natural experiments can address ethical research questions.

### Disinhibited Attachment

Rutter's research identified disinhibited attachment syndrome in severely deprived children, characterised by attention-seeking behaviour with unfamiliar adults. This pattern persisted in many children despite adequate adoptive care, suggesting some enduring effects of early privation.

## Caregiver-Infant Interactions

### Reciprocity and Interactional Synchrony

**Reciprocity**: Turn-taking interactions where infant and caregiver respond to each other's signals. Brazelton et al. (1975) described this as a "dance" between caregiver and infant.

**Interactional Synchrony**: The temporal coordination of micro-level behaviours between infant and caregiver. Meltzoff and Moore (1977) demonstrated imitation in infants as young as 2-3 weeks, suggesting innate capacity for interaction.

**Isabella et al. (1989)**: Found that high levels of interactional synchrony predicted secure attachment, establishing the importance of sensitive responsiveness.

**Teaching Implications**: Flanagan and Berry (2019) recommend using video demonstrations of reciprocity and synchrony to make these abstract concepts concrete for students.

## Stages of Attachment Development

### Schaffer and Emerson (1964)

This Glasgow-based longitudinal study of 60 infants identified four stages:

1. **Asocial Stage (0-6 weeks)**: Similar responses to objects and people
2. **Indiscriminate Attachment (6 weeks - 6 months)**: Preference for human company
3. **Specific Attachment (7-9 months)**: Primary attachment formed; separation anxiety emerges
4. **Multiple Attachments (10+ months)**: Secondary attachments develop

This research, conducted in working-class Glasgow families, provides important UK-specific data and demonstrates how social class and cultural context influence developmental patterns.

## The Role of the Father

### Research Evidence

**Schaffer and Emerson (1964)**: Found that fathers were rarely the primary attachment figure initially, but by 18 months, 75% of infants had formed attachments to fathers.

**Grossmann (2002)**: Longitudinal German study suggested fathers' role differs from mothers', with fathers more important for play and stimulation rather than emotional security.

**Field (1978)**: Found that primary caregiver fathers showed similar responses to primary caregiver mothers, suggesting role rather than gender determines attachment behaviours.

**Lamb (1997)**: Argued that fathers can be sensitive caregivers when given opportunity, challenging biological determinism.

**Teaching Applications**: As Cardwell et al. (2017) emphasise, discussion of paternal attachment allows exploration of gender roles, changing family structures, and socially sensitive research – all essential for UK specification requirements.

## Teaching Strategies and Pedagogical Approaches

### Recommended Approaches

**Sequential Building**: Flanagan (2018) recommends teaching attachment in this sequence:
1. Caregiver-infant interactions (observable foundations)
2. Stages of attachment development (descriptive framework)
3. Types of attachment (Strange Situation classifications)
4. Theoretical explanations (Bowlby, learning theory)
5. Cultural variations and evaluation
6. Effects and applications

**Active Learning**: Banyard (2019) suggests:
- Video analysis of Strange Situation episodes
- Role-play of caregiver-infant interactions
- Analysis of case studies (Romanian orphans, Robertson's films)
- Debate activities examining monotropy versus multiple attachments

**Assessment for Learning**: Cardwell and Flanagan (2016) emphasise regular formative assessment using:
- AO1 (knowledge) recall tasks
- AO2 (application) scenario analysis
- AO3 (evaluation) structured essay planning

### Common Misconceptions

1. **Attachment equals love**: Attachment is a biological drive for proximity, not necessarily emotional warmth
2. **Only mothers can be attachment figures**: Research demonstrates multiple attachment figures are normative
3. **Insecure attachment equals poor outcome**: Many insecurely attached individuals develop positive relationships
4. **Critical period is absolute**: Evidence supports sensitive rather than critical periods
5. **Attachment types are fixed**: Research demonstrates continuity and change depending on circumstances

**Addressing Misconceptions**: Billingham et al. (2019) recommend explicitly addressing these misconceptions through direct instruction and evaluative discussion.

## Assessment Considerations

### Mark Scheme Analysis

**AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding)**: Students must accurately describe:
- Theoretical concepts (monotropy, internal working model, social releasers)
- Research procedures (Strange Situation episodes)
- Key findings (attachment type proportions, cultural variations)

**AO2 (Application)**: Students should apply attachment concepts to:
- Novel scenarios (childcare situations, family case studies)
- Explaining behaviour patterns
- Predicting developmental outcomes

**AO3 (Evaluation)**: Students must critically analyse:
- Research methodology (ecological validity, observer bias)
- Alternative explanations (temperament hypothesis)
- Cultural and individual differences
- Real-world applications and limitations

### Common Assessment Pitfalls

As identified by Flanagan and Berry (2020):
- Describing rather than evaluating research
- Confusing privation and deprivation
- Oversimplifying cultural research
- Neglecting methodological evaluation
- Insufficient use of research evidence
