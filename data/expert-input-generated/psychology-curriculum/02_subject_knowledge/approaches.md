# Approaches in Psychology

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Approaches in Psychology represents a foundational component of UK A-level Psychology specifications, particularly within AQA Psychology (7182), where it forms a mandatory unit typically taught in Year 12. This topic provides students with a comprehensive understanding of how psychology has evolved as a discipline and the different theoretical perspectives that inform psychological research and practice. As Flanagan and colleagues note in their widely-used textbook *AQA Psychology for A Level* (2016), understanding approaches is essential because it enables students to "appreciate the assumptions, methods and contributions of each perspective" and recognise that psychological explanations are rarely singular or absolute.

The topic appears across all major UK exam boards: AQA dedicates approximately 15% of Paper 2 to Approaches, Edexcel integrates approaches throughout their specification, OCR includes it within their H567 specification as 'Psychological Themes through Core Studies', and WJEC Eduqas examines approaches as part of Component 1. Each board emphasises critical comparison between approaches and the ability to apply theoretical perspectives to novel scenarios.

## The Six Major Psychological Approaches

### 1. The Behaviourist Approach

The behaviourist approach, pioneered by Watson (1913) and developed by Skinner and Pavlov, assumes that all behaviour is learned through interaction with the environment. As Cardwell and Flanagan (2016) emphasise, behaviourism marked psychology's emergence as a scientific discipline through its focus on observable, measurable phenomena rather than introspective methods.

**Key principles:**
- Psychology should be scientific and study only observable behaviour
- All behaviour is learned through classical and operant conditioning
- Animals and humans learn through the same basic processes (principle of parsimony)
- Behaviour is determined by environmental stimuli and reinforcement contingencies

**Classical conditioning** (Pavlov, 1927) involves learning through association:
- Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS) → Unconditioned Response (UCR)
- Neutral Stimulus + UCS → UCR
- Conditioned Stimulus (CS) → Conditioned Response (CR)

**Operant conditioning** (Skinner, 1938) involves learning through consequences:
- Positive reinforcement: Adding pleasant stimulus to increase behaviour
- Negative reinforcement: Removing unpleasant stimulus to increase behaviour
- Positive punishment: Adding unpleasant stimulus to decrease behaviour
- Negative punishment: Removing pleasant stimulus to decrease behaviour

**Teaching implications:** Banyard and Grayson (2017) recommend using practical demonstrations such as the "ping-pong ball experiment" or analysing classroom behaviour management through operant conditioning principles. Students often confuse negative reinforcement with punishment—explicit comparison tables and real-world examples (parking fines, revision schedules) support conceptual understanding.

**Evaluation points:**
- Strength: Scientific credibility through controlled laboratory experiments (e.g., Skinner box)
- Strength: Real-world application in token economy systems, phobia treatment (systematic desensitisation)
- Limitation: Environmental determinism ignores free will and cognitive processes
- Limitation: Ethical concerns regarding animal research and generalisation to humans
- Limitation: Reductionist—oversimplifies complex human behaviour to stimulus-response units

### 2. Social Learning Theory (SLT)

Bandura's (1977) Social Learning Theory bridges behaviourism and cognitive psychology, proposing that learning occurs through observation and imitation within a social context. Flanagan describes SLT as representing a "cognitive revolution within behaviourism."

**Key principles:**
- Learning occurs through observation, imitation and modelling
- Mediational processes occur between stimulus and response
- Vicarious reinforcement influences likelihood of imitation
- Identification with role models increases observational learning

**Mediational processes** (Bandura identified four):
1. **Attention:** Noticing the behaviour
2. **Retention:** Remembering the behaviour
3. **Motor reproduction:** Being physically capable of replicating it
4. **Motivation:** Having the will/reason to perform it

**Key research:** Bandura, Ross & Ross (1961) - Bobo doll experiment demonstrated that children observationally learned aggressive behaviour, particularly from same-sex models. The 1963 variation showed vicarious reinforcement effects: children who observed models being rewarded for aggression were more likely to imitate.

**Teaching implications:** Students find SLT intuitive but may struggle distinguishing it from pure behaviourism. Cardwell (2017) suggests using contemporary examples like social media influencers, gaming behaviour, or apprenticeship models. Video analysis of the original Bobo doll studies proves particularly engaging.

**Evaluation points:**
- Strength: Explains cultural transmission and individual differences better than behaviourism
- Strength: Empirical support from Bandura's controlled experiments
- Strength: Real-world application in media violence debates, advertising, education
- Limitation: Causality issues—Bobo dolls designed to be hit; demand characteristics
- Limitation: Underestimates biological influences (e.g., hormonal factors in aggression)
- Limitation: Reciprocal determinism acknowledged but difficult to test empirically

### 3. The Cognitive Approach

Emerging in the 1950s-60s during the "cognitive revolution," this approach studies internal mental processes that behaviourists had dismissed as unscientific. As Billingham notes in *The Complete Companion for AQA Psychology A-Level* (2017), cognitive psychology transformed the discipline by making mental processes scientifically testable through inference.

**Key assumptions:**
- Internal mental processes should be studied scientifically
- Mind operates like a computer: input → processing → output (information processing approach)
- Use of theoretical models and schemas to understand mental processes
- Scientific methods including controlled experiments and cognitive neuroscience

**Key concepts:**

**Schemas** (Bartlett, 1932): Mental frameworks of beliefs and expectations developed through experience that help organise and interpret information. Schemas enable rapid processing but can distort recall and produce stereotypes.

**Theoretical models:** The Multi-Store Model (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968) and Working Memory Model (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974) exemplify how cognitive psychologists use diagrams to represent internal processes.

**Cognitive neuroscience:** Modern integration of biology and cognition using fMRI, PET scans, and lesion studies to locate and understand mental processes in the brain.

**Teaching implications:** Flanagan and Davies (2019) recommend interactive demonstrations of cognitive phenomena: Stroop effect for attention, memory experiments using word lists for capacity/duration, optical illusions for perception. Drawing process diagrams reinforces the information-processing metaphor.

**Evaluation points:**
- Strength: Scientific, objective methods produce reliable, replicable findings
- Strength: Real-world application in cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), artificial intelligence, education
- Strength: Integration with neuroscience provides biological validity
- Limitation: Machine reductionism—humans have emotions, motivations unlike computers
- Limitation: Low ecological validity in many laboratory experiments
- Limitation: Inference-based—cannot directly observe mental processes

### 4. The Biological Approach

The biological approach assumes that psychological processes have physical, biological bases. Banyard and Grayson emphasise this approach's growing dominance in contemporary psychology, particularly with advances in neuroimaging and genetics.

**Key assumptions:**
- Behaviour and experience have biological causes
- Evolution and genes significantly influence behaviour
- Brain structures, neurochemistry, and hormones affect psychological functioning
- Humans share biological mechanisms with other species (comparative psychology justified)

**Neurochemistry:** Neurotransmitters like serotonin (mood regulation), dopamine (motivation, pleasure), and noradrenaline (alertness) influence behaviour. Imbalances associate with psychological disorders: low serotonin with depression (serotonin hypothesis), excess dopamine with schizophrenia (dopamine hypothesis).

**Genetic basis:** Twin studies demonstrate genetic influence. Concordance rates for conditions like schizophrenia show higher rates in monozygotic (identical) twins than dizygotic twins, suggesting genetic component. However, concordance never reaches 100%, indicating environmental factors matter (diathesis-stress model).

**Evolution and behaviour:** Evolutionary psychology explains behaviours as adaptations selected for reproductive advantage. Examples include attachment (survival), mate selection preferences, and universal emotions.

**Teaching implications:** Cardwell recommends using case studies like Phineas Gage or patient HM to illustrate brain-behaviour relationships. Diagrams of neurons, synaptic transmission, and brain regions prove essential. The biological approach provides opportunities for cross-curricular links with Biology A-level.

**Evaluation points:**
- Strength: Scientific methods using advanced technology (fMRI, genetic analysis)
- Strength: Real-world application in drug therapy development
- Strength: Determinist predictions enable effective interventions
- Limitation: Biological reductionism ignores social and psychological context
- Limitation: Biological determinism—limited role for free will
- Limitation: Nature-nurture debate oversimplified; interaction typically operates

### 5. The Psychodynamic Approach

Originating with Freud (1900-1940s), the psychodynamic approach emphasises the influence of unconscious mind, childhood experiences, and innate drives on behaviour. Despite controversy, Flanagan notes it remains "culturally significant and clinically influential."

**Key assumptions:**
- Unconscious mind drives much behaviour
- Childhood experiences critically shape personality
- Personality comprises three parts: id, ego, superego
- Psychological development occurs through psychosexual stages
- Defence mechanisms protect the ego from anxiety

**Personality structure:**
- **Id:** Primitive, pleasure principle, unconscious, present from birth
- **Ego:** Reality principle, mediates between id and superego, conscious and unconscious, develops around age 2
- **Superego:** Morality principle, internalised societal standards, develops around age 5

**Psychosexual stages:** Oral (0-1 years), Anal (1-3 years), Phallic (3-5 years), Latency (6-puberty), Genital (puberty onwards). Fixation at any stage due to frustration or overindulgence produces characteristic adult personality traits.

**Defence mechanisms:** Unconscious strategies protecting ego from anxiety:
- Repression: Forcing traumatic memories into unconscious
- Denial: Refusing to acknowledge reality
- Displacement: Redirecting emotions to substitute target
- Projection: Attributing one's unacceptable thoughts to others

**Teaching implications:** Billingham suggests students find psychodynamic concepts abstract and difficult. Using case studies (Little Hans, Anna O), film analysis (e.g., defence mechanisms in everyday life), and comparison with humanistic approach improves understanding. Clear timelines of Freud's life and theory development provide context.

**Evaluation points:**
- Strength: Introduced idea of unconscious mind, now widely accepted
- Strength: Real-world application in psychotherapy, literary criticism, advertising
- Strength: Case study richness provides detailed insights
- Limitation: Unfalsifiable—concepts like unconscious cannot be scientifically tested
- Limitation: Psychic determinism leaves no role for free will
- Limitation: Sample bias—based on middle-class Viennese women with psychological problems
- Limitation: Gender bias—phallic stage and Oedipus complex reflect Victorian patriarchy

### 6. The Humanistic Approach

Developed by Rogers (1959) and Maslow (1943) as the "third force" in psychology, rejecting determinism of behaviourism and psychoanalysis. Flanagan describes it as emphasising "human capacity for self-determination and psychological growth."

**Key assumptions:**
- Humans have free will and are self-determining
- Subjective experience is central to understanding behaviour
- Each person is unique; idiographic approach preferred
- Humans are inherently good with innate tendency toward self-actualisation
- Context matters—behaviour cannot be meaningfully studied in laboratories

**Maslow's hierarchy of needs:** Five-level pyramid:
1. Physiological needs (food, water, shelter)
2. Safety needs (security, stability)
3. Love and belongingness needs (relationships, affection)
4. Esteem needs (achievement, recognition)
5. Self-actualisation (reaching full potential, creativity, peak experiences)

Lower needs must be satisfied before higher needs emerge. Self-actualisation represents full psychological health—achieving one's potential.

**Rogers' person-centred approach:** Focuses on congruence between self-concept (how we see ourselves) and ideal self (who we want to be). Incongruence produces psychological distress.

**Conditions of worth:** Conditional positive regard from parents (e.g., "I'll love you if you succeed") creates conditions of worth—standards that must be met for acceptance—leading to incongruence.

**Teaching implications:** Banyard recommends applying humanistic concepts to students' own experiences: goal-setting exercises, self-reflection on hierarchy of needs, comparison with school reward systems. Debates about free will versus determinism prove engaging.

**Evaluation points:**
- Strength: Optimistic, positive approach emphasising human potential
- Strength: Real-world application in counselling (person-centred therapy), education
- Strength: Holistic—considers whole person rather than reducing to components
- Limitation: Unscientific—vague concepts like "self-actualisation" difficult to test
- Limitation: Limited evidence base compared with other approaches
- Limitation: Cultural bias—individualistic values may not generalise to collectivist cultures
- Limitation: Self-actualisation perhaps luxury of economically privileged societies

## Comparing and Contrasting Approaches

### Key Debates Across Approaches

**Nature versus nurture:**
- Biological and evolutionary approaches emphasise nature
- Behaviourist and SLT emphasise nurture
- Interactionist approaches (diathesis-stress model) recognise both
- Teaching point: Flanagan emphasises that modern psychology generally rejects extreme positions, favouring interaction

**Determinism versus free will:**
- Hard determinism: Biological (genes, neurochemistry), behaviourist (environment), psychodynamic (unconscious)
- Soft determinism: Cognitive (influenced by mental processing) and SLT (reciprocal determinism)
- Free will: Humanistic approach
- Teaching point: Cardwell suggests exploring implications for moral responsibility and legal system

**Reductionism versus holism:**
- Reductionist: Biological (genes, neurones), behaviourist (S-R), cognitive (information processing)
- Holistic: Humanistic (whole person), Gestalt principles
- Teaching point: Neither extreme is always preferable; depends on research aims

**Idiographic versus nomothetic:**
- Idiographic: Humanistic (case studies, uniqueness), psychodynamic (detailed case histories)
- Nomothetic: Behaviourist, cognitive, biological (general laws through experiments)

### Clinical Applications Across Approaches

Each approach generates specific therapeutic interventions:
- **Behaviourist:** Systematic desensitisation, flooding, token economies
- **Cognitive:** Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)
- **Biological:** Drug therapy, ECT
- **Psychodynamic:** Psychoanalysis, dream analysis, free association
- **Humanistic:** Person-centred therapy, Gestalt therapy

## Practical Teaching Strategies

### Differentiation and Misconceptions

**Common misconceptions** identified by Flanagan et al. (2016):
1. Negative reinforcement is punishment (it's removal of unpleasant stimulus to increase behaviour)
2. Approaches are mutually exclusive (actually complementary)
3. Behaviourism ignores mental processes entirely (it focuses on observable behaviour but doesn't deny cognition exists)
4. Psychodynamic approach is Freud alone (post-Freudian developments significant: Jung, Erikson, Anna Freud)
5. Humanistic approach lacks evidence (research exists but different methodology)

**Differentiation strategies:**
- Foundation: Focus on describing each approach's key assumptions and one strength/limitation
- Core: Add comparisons between two approaches and application to scenarios
- Extension: Evaluate debates (nature-nurture, free will-determinism), analyse research methods associated with approaches, synthesise approaches in understanding complex behaviour

### Assessment Guidance

**AQA-specific requirements (Flanagan, 2019):**
- 6-mark questions: Describe or outline specific approaches (AO1)
- 4-mark questions: Apply approaches to novel scenarios (AO2)
- 8-mark questions: Compare two approaches (AO1 and AO3)
- 16-mark questions: Evaluate approaches or discuss debates (AO1 and AO3)

Mark schemes reward:
- Accurate, detailed knowledge (AO1)
- Effective application to unfamiliar contexts (AO2)
- Thorough, balanced evaluation with justified conclusions (AO3)

**Command words matter:**
- Outline = Brief description without detail
- Describe = Detailed account without evaluation
- Discuss = AO1 + AO3 (describe and evaluate)
- Evaluate = AO3 only (strengths, limitations, evidence)
- Compare = Similarities and differences between approaches
