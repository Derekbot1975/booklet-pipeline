# Research Methods

## Overview and UK Curriculum Context

Research Methods forms a fundamental component of Psychology education in the UK, representing approximately 25-30% of assessment weighting across A-Level specifications. The British Psychological Society (BPS) emphasises research methods as a core competency area, recognising that understanding how psychological knowledge is generated is as important as the knowledge itself.

All major UK exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) mandate substantial coverage of research methods throughout their specifications, typically integrating methodological content across all substantive topic areas whilst also requiring explicit teaching of research methods as a discrete component.

## Key Pedagogical Approaches

### Spiral Curriculum Design

Cara Flanagan and Philip Banyard, in their influential textbooks for AQA Psychology, advocate for a spiral curriculum approach where research methods are revisited with increasing sophistication throughout the course. This aligns with Bruner's pedagogical theory and recognises that students require multiple exposures to complex methodological concepts before achieving mastery.

### Contextualised Learning

Mike Cardwell and Mark Billingham emphasise the importance of teaching research methods in context rather than as abstract concepts. Their approach, widely adopted in UK classrooms, involves introducing methodological concepts through classic studies (e.g., teaching controlled observations through Ainsworth's Strange Situation, or experimental design through Loftus and Palmer's eyewitness testimony research).

## Experimental Methods

### True Experiments and Quasi-Experiments

Students must understand the distinction between true experimental designs (where the independent variable is directly manipulated) and quasi-experiments (where pre-existing differences are studied). AQA specification explicitly requires knowledge of laboratory, field, and natural experiments.

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Laboratory experiments offer high control but potentially low ecological validity (e.g., Milgram's obedience studies)
- Field experiments increase mundane realism whilst maintaining some control (e.g., Hofling's hospital study)
- Natural experiments exploit naturally occurring variables (e.g., Romanian orphan studies by Rutter et al.)

### Experimental Design

All UK specifications require understanding of:
- **Independent groups design**: Different participants in each condition; controls for order effects but requires more participants and is vulnerable to participant variables
- **Repeated measures design**: Same participants in all conditions; controls for participant variables but susceptible to order effects
- **Matched pairs design**: Pairs of participants matched on key variables; attempts to balance advantages but is time-consuming and expensive

Flanagan and Banyard (2020) recommend teaching counterbalancing through practical demonstrations, allowing students to design studies where they implement ABBA or randomisation techniques.

## Control and Validity

### Variables

UK specifications require sophisticated understanding of variable types:
- **Independent variable (IV)**: The variable manipulated or naturally varying
- **Dependent variable (DV)**: The variable measured
- **Extraneous variables**: Variables that might affect the DV but are not the focus of study
- **Confounding variables**: Uncontrolled variables that systematically vary with the IV, compromising internal validity

### Operationalisation

Students frequently struggle with operationalisation—the process of defining variables in measurable terms. Cardwell emphasises that this is a higher-order skill requiring explicit teaching through worked examples. For instance, operationalising "aggression" might involve counting physical acts, measuring verbal aggression through content analysis, or using physiological measures.

### Validity Types

The AQA specification (2015, updated 2021) requires detailed knowledge of:
- **Internal validity**: Whether observed effects genuinely result from IV manipulation (threatened by confounding variables, demand characteristics, investigator effects)
- **External validity**: Generalisability beyond the research setting
  - Ecological validity: Generalisability to real-world settings
  - Population validity: Generalisability to other populations
  - Temporal validity: Generalisability across time periods

### Reliability

Students must distinguish between reliability (consistency) and validity (accuracy). Key concepts include:
- **Internal reliability**: Consistency within a measure (assessed through split-half method)
- **External reliability**: Consistency across occasions (assessed through test-retest method)
- **Inter-rater reliability**: Agreement between observers (particularly relevant for observational research)

## Non-Experimental Methods

### Observational Research

Observational techniques are extensively covered, particularly in developmental psychology contexts. The OCR specification gives particular emphasis to observational methods.

**Key distinctions:**
- **Naturalistic vs controlled observations**: Ainsworth's Strange Situation (controlled) vs Sylva and Lunt's playground observations (naturalistic)
- **Participant vs non-participant observation**: Rosenhan's "On Being Sane in Insane Places" demonstrates participant observation
- **Overt vs covert observation**: Ethical considerations are paramount, as emphasised by BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct (2018)

**Behavioural categories** must be operationalised, exhaustive, and mutually exclusive. Flanagan recommends practical exercises where students develop coding schemes for observable behaviours (e.g., categorising children's play behaviours).

### Self-Report Methods

#### Questionnaires

Students must understand questionnaire construction principles:
- **Open questions**: Generate rich qualitative data but are difficult to analyse systematically
- **Closed questions**: Facilitate quantitative analysis but may restrict responses artificially
- **Likert scales**: Typically 5-point scales measuring attitudes (e.g., 1=strongly disagree to 5=strongly agree)
- **Rating scales**: Numerical scales measuring intensity
- **Fixed-choice options**: Multiple choice or checklist formats

Banyard and Flanagan (2013) emphasise teaching social desirability bias and response sets (acquiescence bias, tendency to agree regardless of content) as critical limitations.

#### Interviews

Interview formats include:
- **Structured interviews**: Standardised questions in fixed order (high reliability, potentially limited depth)
- **Unstructured interviews**: Conversation-style with flexible questions (rich data, potential for interviewer bias)
- **Semi-structured interviews**: Predetermined questions with flexibility to explore responses (balances structure and depth)

### Correlational Analysis

Correlations measure relationships between co-variables without establishing causation. This distinction is fundamental yet frequently misunderstood.

**Key teaching points:**
- **Positive correlation**: As one variable increases, so does the other (e.g., study time and exam performance)
- **Negative correlation**: As one variable increases, the other decreases (e.g., anxiety and performance on complex tasks)
- **Zero correlation**: No systematic relationship exists

Correlation coefficients range from -1.00 (perfect negative) through 0 (no correlation) to +1.00 (perfect positive). AQA requires students to interpret scattergrams and describe correlation strength and direction.

Cardwell emphasises the critical limitation: correlation does not establish causation. The relationship might be explained by: (a) A causing B, (b) B causing A, (c) a third variable causing both, or (d) chance.

## Case Studies

Case studies provide detailed investigation of individuals, groups, or organisations. Classic examples include:
- HM (Henry Molaison): Contributed fundamental knowledge about memory systems
- Genie: Language deprivation case (though ethically controversial)
- Phineas Gage: Historical case demonstrating brain localisation

**Strengths**: Rich, detailed data; can investigate rare phenomena; generate hypotheses for further research
**Limitations**: Cannot establish cause-and-effect; findings may not generalise; vulnerable to researcher bias

## Content Analysis and Thematic Analysis

Content analysis involves systematic examination of communication (text, media, speech). This method has become increasingly prominent in UK specifications following the 2015 reforms.

**Quantitative content analysis**: Counting instances of categories (e.g., frequency of gender stereotypes in advertisements)
**Qualitative content analysis/thematic analysis**: Identifying themes and patterns in data

Thematic analysis, popularised by Braun and Clarke (2006), involves:
1. Familiarisation with data
2. Generating initial codes
3. Searching for themes
4. Reviewing themes
5. Defining and naming themes
6. Producing the report

The Edexcel specification particularly emphasises thematic analysis, requiring students to conduct small-scale qualitative research.

## Sampling Methods

All specifications require detailed knowledge of sampling techniques:

### Probability Sampling
- **Random sampling**: Every member of target population has equal selection chance (e.g., names drawn from hat; requires complete population list)
- **Systematic sampling**: Every nth person selected (e.g., every 10th student on school register)
- **Stratified sampling**: Population divided into strata; random sampling from each stratum proportionally

### Non-Probability Sampling
- **Opportunity sampling**: Selecting readily available participants (most common in student practicals; criticized for bias)
- **Volunteer sampling**: Participants self-select (e.g., responding to advertisements; risk of volunteer bias)
- **Snowball sampling**: Existing participants recruit further participants (useful for hard-to-reach populations)

Flanagan and Banyard emphasise teaching the distinction between **target population** (group of interest) and **sample** (participants actually studied), alongside concepts of **generalisability** and **representativeness**.

## Ethical Considerations

The BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct (2018) provides the framework for ethical research practice in UK psychology. All exam boards require detailed knowledge of ethical principles:

### Core Ethical Principles

**Informed consent**: Participants must understand what participation involves. Special considerations apply to children, vulnerable adults, or those lacking capacity. Students should understand presumptive consent, prior general consent, and retrospective consent as alternatives where full informed consent is impractical.

**Deception**: Should be avoided unless scientifically justified and participants are debriefed. Classic debates involve Milgram's obedience research and Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment.

**Right to withdraw**: Participants can leave research at any time without penalty. This includes withdrawal of data after completion.

**Protection from harm**: Researchers must not expose participants to greater risk than everyday life. Psychological harm includes stress, anxiety, and loss of self-esteem.

**Confidentiality and privacy**: Personal data must be protected; anonymity preserved where possible. The Data Protection Act 2018 (UK implementation of GDPR) has legal implications.

**Debriefing**: Participants should be fully informed about the study's true nature, particularly if deception was involved, and monitored for adverse effects.

### Ethical Committees

Students should understand that research institutions have ethical review committees that evaluate proposed research before approval. The NHS Health Research Authority provides additional oversight for clinical research.

## Quantitative Data Analysis

### Descriptive Statistics

All specifications require calculation and interpretation of:

**Measures of central tendency**:
- **Mean**: Arithmetic average; sensitive to extreme scores
- **Median**: Middle value when ordered; appropriate for ordinal data or skewed distributions
- **Mode**: Most frequent value; only measure suitable for nominal data

**Measures of dispersion**:
- **Range**: Difference between highest and lowest scores; easily calculated but affected by extreme values
- **Standard deviation**: Average deviation from the mean; most informative measure but requires interval data

### Data Presentation

Students must construct and interpret:
- **Bar charts**: For categorical/nominal data with discrete categories
- **Histograms**: For continuous data showing frequency distributions
- **Scattergrams**: For correlational data showing relationship between variables
- **Line graphs**: For showing changes over time or conditions

Cardwell emphasises that many students confuse bar charts and histograms; explicit teaching of when each is appropriate is essential.

### Normal and Skewed Distributions

Understanding distribution shapes is required:
- **Normal distribution**: Symmetrical, bell-shaped curve where mean = median = mode
- **Positive skew**: Tail extends toward higher values; mean > median > mode
- **Negative skew**: Tail extends toward lower values; mode > median > mean

## Inferential Statistics (A-Level Only)

Since the 2015 specification reforms, A-Level Psychology requires understanding of inferential statistical tests. Students must know which test to use (though manual calculation is not required for all boards).

### Test Selection Criteria

Test selection depends on:
1. **Level of measurement**: Nominal, ordinal, or interval data
2. **Experimental design**: Independent groups, repeated measures, or correlation
3. **Whether testing difference or relationship**

### Specific Tests

**Tests of difference**:
- **Mann-Whitney U**: Independent groups, ordinal data
- **Wilcoxon Signed Ranks**: Repeated measures, ordinal data
- **Unrelated t-test**: Independent groups, interval data
- **Related t-test**: Repeated measures, interval data
- **Chi-square test**: Independent groups, nominal data (frequencies)

**Tests of correlation**:
- **Spearman's rho**: Ordinal data
- **Pearson's r**: Interval data

### Statistical Significance

Students must understand:
- **Null hypothesis (H₀)**: States no relationship or difference exists
- **Alternative hypothesis (H₁)**: States a relationship or difference does exist (can be directional/one-tailed or non-directional/two-tailed)
- **Significance levels**: Conventionally p ≤ 0.05 (5% probability results occurred by chance)
- **Type I error**: Accepting H₁ when H₀ is true (false positive)
- **Type II error**: Accepting H₀ when H₁ is true (false negative)

Flanagan recommends teaching significance through concrete examples, helping students understand that p ≤ 0.05 means "if the null hypothesis were true, these results would occur less than 5% of the time by chance alone."

## Qualitative Data Analysis

Qualitative approaches have gained prominence in UK specifications, reflecting psychology's methodological pluralism.

### Categorisation and Themes

Qualitative data (words, images, observations) must be systematically analysed. Key approaches include:
- **Coding**: Assigning labels to data segments
- **Categorisation**: Grouping codes into broader categories
- **Theme identification**: Recognizing patterns of meaning

### Evaluation of Qualitative Methods

**Strengths**: Rich, detailed data; captures complexity; gives voice to participants; generates new insights
**Limitations**: Time-consuming; subjective interpretation; difficult to replicate; findings may not generalise

Students should understand that qualitative research emphasises different quality criteria than quantitative research, including credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability (Lincoln & Guba's framework, frequently referenced in OCR materials).

## Features of Science

UK specifications require explicit teaching of psychology as a science. This meta-cognitive component asks students to evaluate psychology's scientific status.

### Characteristics of Science

**Objectivity**: Researchers should not let personal bias influence observations or interpretations. Double-blind procedures reduce experimenter bias.

**Empiricism**: Knowledge derives from direct observation and experience rather than reasoning alone. Psychology uses systematic, empirical investigation.

**Replicability**: Studies should be repeatable by other researchers achieving consistent findings. Detailed reporting of procedures enables replication.

**Theory construction and hypothesis testing**: Scientific theories generate testable predictions (hypotheses) that can be empirically investigated.

**Falsifiability**: Karl Popper's criterion states that scientific theories must be capable of being proven false. This distinguishes science from pseudoscience.

### Paradigms and Paradigm Shifts

Thomas Kuhn's concept of paradigms is relevant. Some argue psychology lacks a single dominant paradigm (is "pre-paradigmatic"), whilst others suggest multiple paradigms coexist (biological, cognitive, behavioural, etc.).

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Practical Work Requirements

All specifications require students to conduct practical investigations. AQA mandates a minimum of 10 practical activities including:
- Use of inferential statistics
- Qualitative data collection
- Observation techniques
- Self-report methods
- Correlation analysis

Whilst practical work is not directly assessed (following 2015 reforms removing coursework), students must demonstrate understanding of practical research through exam questions requiring them to design studies, identify improvements, or analyse methodological decisions.

### Common Student Misconceptions

**Correlation and causation**: Repeatedly reinforce that correlation does not establish causation. Use memorable examples (ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths—both caused by hot weather).

**Reliability vs validity**: Students frequently confuse these. Emphasise that a measure can be reliable (consistent) without being valid (accurate), but cannot be valid without being reliable.

**Population vs sample**: Clarify that findings from samples may not generalise to target populations, particularly if sampling is biased.

**Operationalisation confusion**: Students struggle to make variables measurable. Provide extensive practice with concrete examples.

### Assessment Approaches

Research methods questions constitute approximately 25-30% of A-Level marks. Question types include:
- **Design decisions**: "Explain why a repeated measures design would be appropriate..." (2-3 marks)
- **Methodological evaluation**: "Evaluate the use of questionnaires in this research..." (4-6 marks)
- **Statistical test selection**: "Which statistical test should be used? Explain your answer..." (3-4 marks)
- **Extended response**: "Discuss ethical issues in psychological research..." (12-16 marks)

Cardwell recommends regular low-stakes testing of research
