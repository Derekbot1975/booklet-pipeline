# Teaching Critical Evaluation

## Introduction to Critical Evaluation in Psychology

Critical evaluation is a cornerstone of A-level Psychology assessment in the UK, typically accounting for 40-50% of available marks across all major examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas). The skill involves systematic analysis of psychological theories, research studies, and methodologies, moving beyond mere description to demonstrate analytical thinking and judgement. As Flanagan and colleagues emphasise in their widely-used textbooks, evaluation is not simply about identifying strengths and weaknesses, but about demonstrating sophisticated psychological understanding through reasoned argument (Flanagan & Flanagan, 2015).

The ability to evaluate critically aligns with the broader educational aims outlined in Ofqual's subject-level guidance for GCE AS and A-level Psychology (2014), which requires students to "analyse, interpret and evaluate scientific information, ideas and evidence, including in relation to issues, making informed judgements."

## The Nature of Evaluation in Psychology

### Understanding Evaluation as a Skill

Cara Flanagan, whose Complete Companions series has significantly influenced UK Psychology teaching, distinguishes between "elaboration" and "evaluation" (Flanagan, Berry & Jarvis, 2016). Elaboration involves extending knowledge through examples and detail, whilst evaluation requires critical commentary on validity, reliability, and application. This distinction is crucial for teachers to convey, as many students conflate description with evaluation.

Philip Banyard's work emphasises that evaluation in Psychology must be evidence-based and contextualised within the scientific debate surrounding a topic (Banyard & Grayson, 2000). Students should reference empirical evidence, alternative theories, and methodological considerations rather than offering unsubstantiated personal opinions.

### The AO3 Assessment Objective

All UK Psychology specifications structure evaluation around Assessment Objective 3 (AO3): "Analyse, interpret and evaluate scientific information, ideas and evidence...to make judgements and reach conclusions." The weighting is typically:

- **AQA A-level Psychology (7182)**: 35 marks per paper for AO3 (approximately 35%)
- **Edexcel A-level Psychology (9PE0)**: 40% allocated to AO3
- **OCR A-level Psychology H567**: 45 marks per paper (approximately 36%)
- **WJEC Eduqas A-level Psychology**: 40% of total marks

Understanding these weightings helps teachers prioritise evaluation skills appropriately within curriculum time.

## Key Components of Effective Evaluation

### Research Evidence and Empirical Support

Mark Billingham's teaching resources emphasise the "three-point rule" for evaluation: students should provide the evaluative point, elaborate with specific evidence, and explain the significance (Billingham, 2018). For example, when evaluating Bowlby's maternal deprivation hypothesis, students might:

1. **Point**: The theory lacks temporal validity
2. **Evidence**: Modern research by Rutter (1981) demonstrated that privation rather than deprivation caused long-term harm
3. **Significance**: This challenges Bowlby's original conclusions and suggests the critical period may be less rigid than proposed

### Methodological Evaluation

Mike Cardwell's examination guidance stresses the importance of methodological critique (Cardwell, Flanagan & Clark, 2003). Students should evaluate:

- **Internal validity**: Confounding variables, demand characteristics, investigator effects
- **External validity**: Population validity, ecological validity, temporal validity
- **Reliability**: Test-retest reliability, inter-rater reliability, internal consistency
- **Ethical considerations**: BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct (2021) compliance

### Real-World Application

The criterion of "application to everyday life" appears consistently across mark schemes. Flanagan emphasises that students should consider both the practical utility and limitations of psychological findings when applied to real-world contexts such as education, therapy, or social policy (Flanagan et al., 2016).

## Teaching Strategies for Developing Evaluation Skills

### Scaffolding Evaluation in Key Stage 5

#### Year 12 Approaches

During AS or first-year A-level teaching, Banyard recommends beginning with structured evaluation frameworks:

**GRAVE**: An acronym developed for UK Psychology teaching:
- **G**eneralisability (population and ecological validity)
- **R**eliability (consistency of findings)
- **A**pplication (real-world utility)
- **V**alidity (does it measure what it claims?)
- **E**thics (compliance with ethical guidelines)

Teachers should provide evaluation paragraph frames initially, such as:
"One strength of [theory/study] is... This is because... This is important because..."

#### Year 13 Development

As students progress, Flanagan advocates for more independent evaluation requiring synthesis of multiple sources. Students should be encouraged to:

- Compare and contrast competing theories (e.g., biological vs. cognitive explanations)
- Evaluate theories using research evidence from multiple studies
- Consider cultural and historical context
- Engage with contemporary debates in Psychology

### Practical Classroom Techniques

#### The "Counter-Argument" Approach

Philip Banyard suggests teaching evaluation as a debate skill. For each theory or study, students should construct:
1. The strongest case FOR the approach
2. The strongest case AGAINST the approach
3. An overall judgement based on weight of evidence

This develops the dialectical thinking essential for high-level evaluation.

#### Evaluation Grids

Many UK teachers employ evaluation matrices where students systematically work through evaluation criteria:

| Criterion | Evidence | Conclusion |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| Supporting research | Identify specific studies | Explain what this shows |
| Contradicting research | Identify alternative findings | Explain implications |
| Methodology | Analyse research methods used | Assess impact on validity |
| Applications | Real-world examples | Evaluate effectiveness |

#### The "Golden Sentence" Technique

Developed by Mark Billingham, this involves students crafting single evaluative sentences that contain:
- An evaluative term (strength/limitation/however)
- Specific psychological content
- A justification

Example: "A limitation of Milgram's research is that the sample consisted entirely of American males, meaning the findings may not generalise to females or other cultures, thus reducing population validity."

### Developing Evaluation Through Writing

#### The PEE/PEEL Structure

Whilst originating from English teaching, the Point-Evidence-Explain (or Point-Evidence-Explain-Link) structure has been widely adopted in Psychology:

- **Point**: Clear evaluative statement
- **Evidence**: Specific psychological evidence (research, statistics, theories)
- **Explain**: Clarification of how evidence relates to point
- **Link**: Connection back to the original question or theory

Flanagan emphasises that the "Link" component is frequently omitted by students, yet it demonstrates the highest level of analytical thinking (Flanagan et al., 2016).

#### Modelling Expert Evaluation

Teachers should regularly model exemplar evaluation paragraphs, thinking aloud to demonstrate the thought processes involved. Cardwell suggests deconstructing examiner reports and published mark schemes to identify what differentiates Level 4 responses from Level 3 (Cardwell et al., 2003).

## Common Pitfalls and Misconceptions

### Surface-Level Evaluation

Many students produce "list-like" evaluations lacking depth. For example:
❌ "A strength is it has research support. A limitation is it is reductionist."

Flanagan identifies this as failing to meet AO3 criteria because it lacks elaboration and explanation. Teachers must emphasise that evaluation points require development, typically 3-4 sentences minimum.

### Confusing Description with Evaluation

Students frequently describe findings when asked to evaluate. For instance, when evaluating Loftus and Palmer's research, students might describe the verb conditions rather than analysing validity concerns. Explicit teaching of evaluative language is essential:

**Evaluative terms**: strength, limitation, however, conversely, furthermore, consequently, therefore, this suggests, alternatively, in contrast, significantly

### Unsubstantiated Opinions

Banyard warns against "common-sense" evaluation lacking psychological evidence (Banyard, 2016). Statements like "This wouldn't work in real life" or "Not everyone is like this" demonstrate weak evaluation unless supported by specific counter-evidence or methodological critique.

### The "Yet" Problem

Students often present contradictory evaluation without synthesis. Teaching the concept of "weighing evidence" helps students move beyond listing points to making justified overall judgements.

## Assessment and Feedback Strategies

### Formative Assessment Approaches

#### Evaluation-Only Practice

Billingham recommends regular "evaluation sprints" where students write only AO3 content for 8-10 minutes, allowing focused practice without the cognitive load of description. Teachers can then assess evaluation quality in isolation.

#### Peer Assessment Using Mark Schemes

Students should become familiar with mark scheme descriptors for evaluation. AQA's generic mark scheme levels for AO3 provide clear criteria:

- **Level 4** (10-12 marks): Effective and thorough evaluation; broad range of issues/evidence in depth
- **Level 3** (7-9 marks): Reasonable evaluation; range of issues/evidence, lacking depth
- **Level 2** (4-6 marks): Basic evaluation; superficial consideration of limited range
- **Level 1** (1-3 marks): Limited evaluation; isolated or muddled points

#### Live Marking of Evaluation

Given evaluation's complexity, Flanagan advocates for targeted live marking where teachers annotate evaluation paragraphs during lessons, providing immediate feedback on quality and suggesting improvements.

### Summative Assessment Considerations

#### Extended Writing Questions

All UK examination boards include extended writing questions (typically 8, 12, or 16 marks) requiring sustained evaluation. Teachers should ensure regular practice with:

- **AQA**: "Discuss" questions (16 marks: 6 AO1, 10 AO3)
- **Edexcel**: "Evaluate" questions (20 marks, split equally between description and evaluation)
- **OCR**: "Evaluate" questions (12 marks: 4 AO1, 8 AO3)

#### Mark Scheme Application

Teachers must familiarise students with how evaluation is assessed within context. For example, AQA awards marks for evaluation that is:
- Relevant to the question
- Supported by evidence
- Thorough (covers multiple evaluation points)
- Well-developed (elaborated and explained)

## Differentiation and Stretch

### Supporting Lower-Attaining Students

Cara Flanagan's differentiated resources include:
- Pre-written evaluation points requiring completion
- Gap-fill evaluation paragraphs
- Evaluation flashcards matching points to evidence
- Simplified GRAVE frameworks

### Challenging Higher-Attaining Students

For students targeting A/A* grades, Cardwell emphasises:
- Synthesis of evaluation from multiple perspectives
- Engaging with contemporary research and debates
- Demonstrating awareness of complexity and nuance
- Making sophisticated comparative judgements

Higher-level evaluation might involve considering paradigm shifts, meta-analyses, or philosophical debates about reductionism, determinism, and the nature of scientific knowledge in Psychology.

## Integration Across Topics

### Topic-Specific Evaluation Considerations

#### Cognitive Psychology
Emphasis on ecological validity (laboratory studies vs. real-world memory), reductionism, and machine analogy limitations.

#### Social Psychology
Cultural bias, ethical concerns in classic studies, temporal validity (1960s-70s research), demand characteristics in conformity/obedience studies.

#### Developmental Psychology
Methodological challenges (studying children), longitudinal vs. cross-sectional designs, nature-nurture debates.

#### Biological Psychology
Technological advancement in brain scanning, reductionism debates, determinism, ethical considerations in drug treatments.

### Cross-Curricular Connections

Banyard emphasises that evaluation skills developed in Psychology transfer to broader critical thinking applicable across A-level subjects and higher education (Banyard, 2016). Teachers should make explicit connections to:
- Scientific methodology (Biology, Chemistry)
- Statistical analysis (Mathematics)
- Argument structure (English, Philosophy)
- Research evaluation (Sociology)

## Conclusion

Teaching critical evaluation effectively requires systematic scaffolding, explicit modelling, and sustained practice throughout Key Stage 5. As Flanagan repeatedly emphasises, evaluation is not an add-on to Psychology teaching but is integral to thinking psychologically (Flanagan et al., 2016). By implementing structured approaches, providing regular feedback, and gradually removing scaffolding, teachers can develop students' capacity for sophisticated psychological analysis that meets the demands of UK examination boards and prepares students for higher education.

The most successful approach combines explicit teaching of evaluation frameworks, regular low-stakes practice, engagement with authentic psychological research, and consistent use of examination board mark schemes to clarify expectations. When students understand that evaluation involves reasoned judgement based on evidence rather than mere criticism, they can demonstrate the analytical skills that define high-quality Psychology education.
