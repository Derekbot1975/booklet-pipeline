# British Psychological Society and Education

## Overview of the British Psychological Society (BPS)

The British Psychological Society (BPS), founded in 1901, is the representative body for psychology and psychologists in the United Kingdom. With over 60,000 members, it is one of the largest organisations of its kind in Europe. The BPS plays a crucial role in shaping psychology education across all sectors, from secondary schools to universities, and maintains rigorous standards for both teaching and professional practice.

The Society's Education and Public Engagement Division works specifically to enhance the quality of psychology education, providing resources, guidance, and accreditation frameworks that directly influence curriculum design and delivery in UK schools and colleges.

## BPS Role in Secondary Education

### Curriculum Influence and Standards

The BPS has been instrumental in establishing psychology as a rigorous academic discipline within the secondary curriculum. Since psychology became available as an A-level subject in the 1970s, the BPS has worked closely with examination boards to ensure content validity and pedagogical appropriateness. The Society's Academic Board provides expert guidance to AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC examination boards on specification development and assessment practices.

Cara Flanagan, a prominent figure in UK psychology education and co-author of numerous A-level psychology textbooks (Flanagan & Berry, 2019; Flanagan et al., 2016), has emphasised the importance of the BPS in maintaining academic rigour whilst ensuring accessibility for students aged 16-18. Her work with AQA specifications has helped align examination content with BPS recommendations on core psychological knowledge.

### A-Level Psychology and BPS Alignment

The current A-level psychology specifications across all major exam boards reflect BPS guidance on essential psychological concepts, research methods, and ethical considerations. The BPS document "A-Level Psychology: Guidance for Examiners and Teachers" (2015, updated 2019) outlines recommended coverage across:

- Research methods and scientific methodology
- Biological psychology foundations
- Cognitive approaches
- Social influence and social psychology
- Developmental psychology
- Individual differences and psychopathology
- Contemporary debates and issues

Philip Banyard and his collaborators (Banyard et al., 2015) have noted that BPS involvement ensures that A-level content provides appropriate preparation for undergraduate study whilst remaining pedagogically suitable for Key Stage 5 learners. Their textbook "Essential AS & A2 Psychology" explicitly maps content against both exam board specifications and BPS competency frameworks.

## BPS Accreditation and Quality Assurance

### Graduate Basis for Chartered Membership (GBC)

The BPS's most significant impact on psychology education comes through its accreditation system. The Graduate Basis for Chartered Membership (GBC) represents the Society's quality standard for undergraduate psychology programmes. Accredited programmes must demonstrate comprehensive coverage across core areas defined by the BPS Curriculum and Qualifications Board:

1. Biological psychology
2. Cognitive psychology
3. Developmental psychology
4. Individual differences
5. Social psychology
6. Conceptual and historical issues
7. Research methods and statistics

This framework indirectly influences A-level teaching, as teachers often reference these core areas when preparing students for potential university study. Mike Cardwell, whose "Complete A-Level Psychology" series has been widely adopted across UK schools, structures content explicitly around these BPS-recognised domains (Cardwell et al., 2008).

### Implications for Teachers

Psychology teachers in UK secondary schools benefit significantly from BPS membership and resources:

**Professional Development**: The BPS Education Section runs regular conferences, workshops, and webinars specifically designed for psychology teachers. The annual "Psychology Teaching Review," published by the BPS, provides peer-reviewed articles on pedagogical innovations, assessment practices, and curriculum developments.

**Teaching Resources**: The BPS Psychology Teaching Hub offers downloadable resources including:
- Lesson plan templates aligned with exam specifications
- Assessment guidance and mark schemes
- Student research ethics guidance
- Careers information and progression pathways
- Subject-specific CPD materials

**Ethical Guidelines**: The BPS "Code of Ethics and Conduct" (2018) and "Code of Human Research Ethics" (2014, revised 2021) provide essential frameworks for teachers supervising student practical investigations. Mark Billingham has emphasised the importance of teachers familiarising students with these ethical principles early in their psychology education, particularly when designing coursework or internal assessments (Billingham, 2017).

## BPS Support for Practical Work and Research Skills

### Student Research and Ethics

The BPS provides specific guidance for A-level and undergraduate students conducting psychological research. The document "Conducting Research with Young People: A Guide for Teachers" (BPS, 2016) outlines appropriate methodologies, ethical safeguards, and supervision requirements for student-led investigations.

This guidance directly influences how teachers approach the mandatory practical components across examination boards:

- **AQA**: Requires minimum of 12 practical activities; BPS ethics framework must be applied
- **Edexcel**: Research methods assessment draws on BPS methodological standards
- **OCR**: Psychological investigations report assessed against BPS research conventions
- **WJEC**: Practical tasks must demonstrate understanding of BPS ethical principles

Cara Flanagan's "Research Methods Companion for A Level Psychology" (2018) explicitly references BPS ethical guidelines throughout, demonstrating practical application for student researchers.

## BPS Qualifications and Career Pathways

### Certificate in Applied Psychology

The BPS offers the Certificate in Applied Psychology Practice, a Level 4 qualification that bridges the gap between A-level study and professional psychology. Whilst not widely taken by school students, awareness of this pathway helps teachers provide comprehensive careers guidance.

### Supporting Careers Education

The BPS "Careers in Psychology" resources help teachers guide students through diverse career possibilities:

- Clinical and counselling psychology
- Educational psychology
- Forensic psychology
- Occupational psychology
- Sport and exercise psychology
- Health psychology
- Neuropsychology

Philip Banyard (2020) has argued that effective psychology teaching should include explicit discussion of professional pathways and the BPS's role in professional regulation through the Health and Care Professions Council (HCPC) registration.

## BPS Educational Publications and Resources

### Psychology Teacher Magazine

The BPS publishes "Psychology Teaching Review," formerly the "Psychology Teaching" journal, which provides evidence-based articles on:

- Innovative teaching approaches
- Assessment and feedback strategies
- Student misconceptions and how to address them
- Integration of contemporary research
- Use of technology in psychology teaching

Teachers can access this twice-yearly publication through institutional or individual BPS membership.

### Student Resources

The BPS "Psychologist" magazine, published monthly, includes accessible summaries of recent research suitable for A-level students. The "Research Digest" blog provides bite-sized research updates that teachers can incorporate into lessons, maintaining curriculum currency.

Mark Billingham has recommended that psychology departments establish a "psychology reading culture" using BPS resources to extend students beyond textbook content (Billingham, 2019).

## BPS and Diversity in Psychology Education

### Representation and Inclusion

The BPS Equality, Diversity and Inclusion (EDI) Committee provides guidance on ensuring psychology curricula reflect diverse perspectives and populations. The "Decolonising the Psychology Curriculum" initiative (BPS, 2020) challenges teachers to:

- Critically evaluate the cultural origins of psychological theories
- Include research from majority-world psychologists
- Address historical biases in psychological research
- Ensure case studies and examples represent diverse populations

This initiative responds to concerns raised by students and educators about the predominance of Western, male, and historical figures in psychology syllabi. Teachers are encouraged to balance coverage of classic studies with contemporary, diverse research.

### Widening Participation

The BPS supports initiatives to encourage students from underrepresented backgrounds to study psychology. The "Psychology in Schools" programme provides free workshops and resources to state schools, particularly those in areas of socioeconomic disadvantage.

## BPS Conferences and Professional Development

### Annual Conference

The BPS Annual Conference includes dedicated education sessions where psychology teachers can:

- Network with university lecturers and educational psychologists
- Attend workshops on specific curriculum topics
- Learn about cutting-edge research relevant to teaching
- Access exhibition resources from educational publishers

The Education Section typically hosts 15-20 sessions specifically for school teachers, with recent topics including teaching cognitive neuroscience, assessing practical work, and supporting students with anxiety disorders.

### Regional Networks

The BPS maintains regional branches across the UK, many of which host teacher networks. These local groups facilitate:

- Moderation meetings for internal assessments
- Resource sharing and collaborative planning
- Subject knowledge enhancement sessions
- Liaison with local universities for guest lectures and university visits

## BPS Guidance on Assessment

### Assessment Principles

The BPS "Good Practice Guidelines for the Conduct of Psychological Research within the NHS" (2023) and related assessment documents inform exam boards' approach to:

- Validity and reliability in assessment design
- Appropriate application of statistical tests
- Ethical considerations in assessment scenarios
- Scientific literacy and critical evaluation skills

Mike Cardwell's examination preparation materials (Cardwell, 2020) explicitly reference BPS assessment principles when guiding students through essay writing and research analysis questions.

### Mark Schemes and Examiner Reports

The BPS provides informal consultation to examination boards on mark scheme development, ensuring that assessment criteria reflect genuine psychological understanding rather than mere memorisation. Philip Banyard has noted that this collaboration has improved assessment quality, particularly in questions requiring critical evaluation and application (Banyard, 2018).

## Contemporary Challenges and BPS Response

### Digital Psychology Education

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated psychology teaching online, and the BPS responded with guidance on:

- Conducting remote practical investigations ethically
- Maintaining academic integrity in online assessments
- Supporting student wellbeing during remote learning
- Using digital tools to teach psychological concepts

The BPS "Teaching Psychology Online: Guidelines and Resources" (2020) has become essential reading for psychology teachers, addressing both pedagogical and ethical considerations.

### Mental Health Literacy

The BPS emphasises psychology teachers' unique position in promoting mental health literacy. The "Mental Health in Schools" initiative provides:

- Resources on teaching psychopathology sensitively
- Guidance on student disclosures during mental health topics
- Signposting to appropriate support services
- Training on trauma-informed teaching approaches

Cara Flanagan's recent work (2022) has highlighted the responsibility psychology teachers have in presenting mental health conditions accurately and reducing stigma, whilst maintaining appropriate professional boundaries.

## Practical Recommendations for Teachers

### Maximising BPS Resources

**Department Development**: Psychology departments should:

1. Establish at least one BPS member on staff to access resources
2. Subscribe to "Psychology Teaching Review" for the department library
3. Integrate BPS Research Digest articles into schemes of work
4. Display BPS ethical principles prominently in teaching spaces
5. Use BPS careers resources during enrichment activities

**Student Engagement**: Teachers can:

1. Encourage high-achieving Year 13 students to attend BPS undergraduate conferences
2. Use BPS case studies to illustrate theoretical concepts
3. Reference BPS position statements on controversial issues
4. Model critical thinking using BPS research summaries
5. Promote BPS student membership for university applicants

### Aligning Teaching with BPS Standards

When planning curriculum sequences, teachers should:

- Map topics against BPS core areas to ensure comprehensive coverage
- Integrate BPS ethical frameworks from the first lesson
- Use BPS methodological standards when teaching research methods
- Reference BPS position papers when addressing contentious issues
- Prepare students for BPS-accredited undergraduate study

## Conclusion

The British Psychological Society's influence on psychology education in the UK is profound and multifaceted. From shaping A-level specifications to providing professional development for teachers, from setting ethical standards to supporting student research, the BPS remains central to maintaining quality and relevance in psychology teaching.

As Mark Billingham (2021) notes, "effective psychology teaching requires engagement with the professional community, and the BPS provides the essential bridge between school classrooms and the wider world of psychological science." Teachers who actively utilise BPS resources, maintain awareness of Society initiatives, and align their practice with BPS standards ensure their students receive education that is both academically rigorous and professionally relevant.

The continued evolution of psychology as a discipline—incorporating neuroscience advances, digital methodologies, and diverse perspectives—makes ongoing engagement with the BPS essential for all psychology educators committed to excellence in their field.
