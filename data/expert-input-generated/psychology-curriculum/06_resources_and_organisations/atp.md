# Association for the Teaching of Psychology

## Overview and Organisational Background

The Association for the Teaching of Psychology (ATP) is the primary professional organisation supporting Psychology teachers and lecturers in the United Kingdom. Established in 2006, the ATP emerged from a recognition that Psychology teachers required dedicated professional support, particularly following Psychology's growth as one of the most popular A-level subjects. The association serves educators teaching across secondary schools, sixth form colleges, further education colleges, and higher education institutions.

The ATP operates under the auspices of the British Psychological Society (BPS) and works collaboratively with examination boards including AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC/Eduqas to support curriculum development and teaching practice. The organisation represents the interests of approximately 2,000 members nationwide, advocating for high-quality Psychology education and teacher professional development.

## Founding Rationale and Historical Context

The ATP was formed during a period of unprecedented expansion in Psychology education. Between 2000 and 2010, A-level Psychology entries increased by over 250%, creating an urgent need for subject-specific teaching support. Many teachers entering the field came from related disciplines (sociology, biology, or health studies) without formal training in psychology pedagogy.

Philip Banyard, a founding member and prominent Psychology educator, identified the crucial gap in professional support structures. Unlike established subjects such as English or Mathematics, Psychology lacked a dedicated teaching association to provide pedagogical guidance, resources, and professional community. The ATP filled this void, offering subject-specific expertise that general teaching organisations could not provide.

## Key Functions and Services

### Professional Development and Training

The ATP organises an annual conference, typically held in July, which attracts 300-400 Psychology teachers from across the UK. These conferences feature keynote presentations from leading Psychology educators and researchers, practical teaching workshops, and opportunities to engage with examination board representatives. Notable speakers have included **Cara Flanagan**, whose work on examination preparation and student cognitive load has profoundly influenced teaching practice, and **Mike Cardwell**, whose contributions to curriculum design and assessment have shaped multiple specifications.

The association also coordinates regional networks through local branches, facilitating grassroots professional learning communities. These branches organise termly meetings where teachers share resources, discuss pedagogical challenges, and engage with recent developments in psychological science and education policy.

### Teaching Resources and Publications

The ATP publishes *Psychology Teaching Review*, a peer-reviewed journal appearing twice annually. This publication bridges academic psychology and classroom practice, featuring articles on:

- Evidence-based teaching strategies specific to Psychology content
- Research into Psychology student learning and misconceptions
- Practical classroom activities and assessment approaches
- Commentary on curriculum changes and examination requirements
- Reviews of textbooks and digital resources

**Mark Billingham**, a long-standing contributor to ATP publications, has written extensively on practical classroom strategies for teaching research methods, consistently emphasising active learning approaches that develop students' methodological literacy rather than mere procedural knowledge.

The ATP website hosts a members-only resource library containing lesson plans, PowerPoint presentations, revision materials, and assessment resources aligned with all major UK examination specifications. These materials are quality-assured by experienced practitioners and regularly updated to reflect specification changes.

### Curriculum and Assessment Liaison

The ATP maintains formal relationships with all major examination boards, providing teacher input into specification development and assessment design. This ensures that qualification structures reflect both psychological science and pedagogical reality. Association representatives sit on examination board subject advisory groups, contributing expertise on appropriate content selection, assessment validity, and mark scheme clarity.

When AQA revised its A-level Psychology specification for first teaching in 2015, ATP representatives provided detailed feedback on content sequencing, assessment objectives, and practical work requirements. Similarly, the ATP contributed to OCR's H567 specification development and Edexcel's Pearson Salters Nuffield Advanced Biology specification psychology components.

## Support for Specific Key Stages

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE Level)

Whilst Psychology is not a statutory National Curriculum subject, GCSE Psychology is offered by AQA and OCR. The ATP supports teachers delivering these qualifications through:

- Age-appropriate teaching strategies recognising 14-16 year olds' developmental stage
- Guidance on managing sensitive topics (mental health, social influence, aggression) with younger learners
- Resources addressing the transition from descriptive to analytical thinking required in psychological explanation
- Support for schools introducing Psychology to diversify their Key Stage 4 curriculum offer

The ATP advocates for Psychology as an option subject that develops critical thinking, scientific literacy, and understanding of human behaviour—skills valuable across the curriculum and beyond.

### Key Stage 5 (A-level)

The ATP's primary focus remains A-level provision, reflecting where most Psychology teaching occurs in UK secondary education. Support includes:

**Specification-specific guidance**: Detailed mapping documents showing how topics can be sequenced across two years, with consideration of conceptual dependencies and cognitive load. **Cara Flanagan's** research on how students build schema in Psychology learning has directly informed ATP advice on curriculum planning, particularly regarding the optimal ordering of approaches and debates.

**Research methods teaching**: This represents a persistent challenge, with students often struggling to integrate methodological understanding with content knowledge. The ATP provides comprehensive resources on teaching experimental design, statistical concepts, and critical evaluation of research. **Philip Banyard** has repeatedly emphasised that research methods should not be taught as discrete topics but integrated throughout content delivery—a principle now widely adopted by ATP-trained teachers.

**Mathematical requirements**: Following the 2015 reforms that introduced minimum mathematical content requirements (10% of marks at A-level), the ATP developed specific resources addressing statistical analysis, graph interpretation, and probability. These materials help Psychology teachers—many from non-mathematical backgrounds—confidently deliver quantitative content.

**Practical work and investigations**: The ATP has produced exemplar practical activities meeting examination board requirements, including ethical approval templates, risk assessments, and student guidance documents. These resources ensure that practical work develops genuine research competence rather than becoming merely procedural exercises.

## Pedagogical Contributions to Psychology Teaching

### Evidence-Based Teaching Approaches

The ATP champions evidence-informed pedagogy specific to Psychology education. Key principles promoted through ATP professional development include:

**Cognitive Load Theory application**: **Cara Flanagan** has extensively written about managing intrinsic and extraneous cognitive load when teaching complex psychological concepts. Her work emphasises breaking down multi-component theories (e.g., the Working Memory Model) into manageable elements, using worked examples before independent application, and carefully sequencing information to avoid working memory overload.

**Retrieval practice**: The ATP advocates systematic use of low-stakes quizzing, with research demonstrating particular effectiveness for Psychology's substantial knowledge base. Teachers are encouraged to implement regular knowledge checks using software like Quizlet or paper-based systems, recognising that Psychology students must retain extensive factual information alongside conceptual understanding.

**Elaborative interrogation**: Encouraging students to explain 'why' psychological findings occur promotes deeper processing than simple memorisation. The ATP provides questioning frameworks that develop this skill, particularly valuable for evaluation (AO3) skills in examinations.

**Interleaving**: Rather than blocking topics (teaching all memory content together, then all attachment content), ATP guidance recommends interleaving related concepts to strengthen long-term retention and transfer.

### Addressing Common Misconceptions

**Mike Cardwell's** work on student misconceptions has informed ATP training materials identifying systematic errors in psychological thinking:

- **Correlation-causation confusion**: Students persistently interpret correlational findings as causal relationships
- **Reductionism misunderstanding**: Misapplying the term to any explanation that analyses components, rather than recognising it as problematic only when higher-level explanations are necessary
- **Research method confusion**: Conflating observations with experiments, or failing to distinguish controlled observations from naturalistic observations
- **Statistical interpretation errors**: Misunderstanding significance levels, confusing correlation coefficients with percentages, or reversing probability interpretations

The ATP provides diagnostic assessments and targeted teaching strategies addressing these specific misconceptions.

## Advocacy and Professional Representation

### Curriculum Development Advocacy

The ATP has consistently advocated for curriculum structures that balance breadth, depth, and accessibility. During the 2015 A-level reforms, the association submitted formal responses to government consultations, arguing for:

- Retention of synoptic assessment requiring integration across topics
- Preservation of diversity in psychological approaches (biological, cognitive, behavioural, psychodynamic, humanistic)
- Appropriate mathematical content that serves psychological understanding rather than mathematical complexity for its own sake
- Practical work requirements that develop authentic research competence

The ATP resisted pressures to reduce Psychology to purely biological or neuroscientific content, successfully maintaining the subject's multi-paradigmatic character that distinguishes it from Biology and distinguishes psychological from neuroscientific explanation.

### Teacher Retention and Workforce Development

The ATP has highlighted challenges in Psychology teacher retention and recruitment. Many Psychology teachers work in isolation as sole specialists in their institutions, lacking departmental colleagues for support. This isolation contributes to higher-than-average attrition rates, particularly among early-career teachers.

ATP initiatives addressing workforce sustainability include mentoring schemes pairing experienced practitioners with newly qualified teachers, online professional learning communities enabling year-round peer support, and advocacy for specialist initial teacher education routes in Psychology (currently, most Psychology teachers qualify through general science or social science routes without subject-specific training).

### University Liaison and Transition

The ATP facilitates dialogue between school/college Psychology teachers and university Psychology departments. Annual transition conferences examine:

- How A-level Psychology prepares (or fails to prepare) students for undergraduate study
- Alignment between school qualifications and university expectations
- Progression routes for students not continuing to Psychology degrees but applying psychological knowledge in nursing, education, business, or other fields

**Philip Banyard**, who straddles school and university contexts, has emphasised that A-level Psychology serves dual purposes: preparing future psychologists and providing psychological literacy for citizens. The ATP ensures both aims receive appropriate attention.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Membership Benefits and Access

ATP membership costs £40 annually (£20 for trainee teachers), providing access to:

- *Psychology Teaching Review* digital archive
- Members-only resource library
- Discounted conference registration
- Regional branch meetings
- Online discussion forums and email networks
- Job vacancy listings and career development information

For newly qualified Psychology teachers or those teaching Psychology without specialist background, ATP membership provides essential professional infrastructure that isolated practitioners otherwise lack.

### Implementing ATP-Recommended Practice

Teachers can operationalise ATP guidance through:

**Structured retrieval practice**: Implement ten-minute knowledge tests at the start of each lesson, covering material from previous weeks using ATP's spaced practice schedules. **Cara Flanagan's** research suggests optimal testing intervals of 1 week, 1 month, and pre-examination for maximum retention.

**Cognitive load management**: When introducing complex theories (e.g., Bowlby's Monotropic Theory), use ATP-provided worked examples showing evaluation construction before expecting independent evaluation. Break multi-component theories into sub-components taught across multiple lessons rather than single sessions.

**Research methods integration**: Rather than teaching methods in isolation, embed methodological analysis within every content topic. When teaching social influence, analyse Milgram's experimental design; when teaching attachment, critique Ainsworth's observational methodology. This integration approach, advocated by **Philip Banyard** and **Mark Billingham**, develops transferable methodological competence.

**Metacognitive development**: Use ATP reflection frameworks encouraging students to monitor their understanding, identify knowledge gaps, and plan learning strategies. Psychology, with its substantial content, requires students to develop sophisticated self-regulated learning approaches.

### Managing Sensitive Content

Psychology syllabuses necessarily include potentially sensitive topics: mental health disorders, childhood trauma, prejudice and discrimination, addiction, and criminal behaviour. The ATP provides ethical guidance on:

- Advance warning systems allowing students to prepare for potentially distressing content
- Alternative assignments for students with lived experience of discussed conditions
- Language choices that avoid stigmatisation (person-first language: "person with schizophrenia" rather than "schizophrenic")
- Signposting support services and recognising pastoral care boundaries
- Balancing scientific objectivity with empathetic understanding

**Mike Cardwell** has written extensively about creating psychologically safe classrooms where students can engage with challenging content without personal harm or reinforcing stereotypes.

## Research Evidence Supporting ATP Approaches

Empirical research validates ATP-promoted teaching strategies. Dunlosky et al.'s (2013) comprehensive review in *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* rated retrieval practice and distributed practice—both central to ATP guidance—as highly effective learning strategies, whilst highlighting that highlighting and rereading—methods students commonly prefer—show minimal effectiveness.

Roediger and Karpicke's (2006) research in *Psychological Science* demonstrated that repeated retrieval produces superior long-term retention compared to repeated studying, with effect sizes particularly large for complex material like Psychology content. The ATP's retrieval practice emphasis reflects this evidence base.

Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory research (2011, *Educational Psychology Review*) underpins ATP guidance on breaking complex information into manageable chunks. **Cara Flanagan** has adapted these principles specifically for Psychology teaching, recognising that topics like biopsychology involve unusually high intrinsic cognitive load requiring careful instructional design.

## Connections to Examination Board Requirements

### AQA Psychology (7182)

The ATP maintains particularly close relationships with AQA, the dominant A-level Psychology provider (approximately 65% market share). ATP representatives contributed to the current specification's design, particularly regarding:

- The tripartite topic structure (approaches, biopsychology, research methods) providing coherent conceptual organisation
- Synoptic assessment requirements in Paper 3 integrating approaches with option topics
- Mathematical requirements distribution ensuring statistical content serves psychological understanding
- Mark scheme clarity regarding evaluation depth and breadth

ATP resources explicitly map to AQA assessment objectives, providing mark scheme guidance and examiner perspective insights. **Cara Flanagan** co-authors the leading AQA textbook series, directly translating ATP pedagogical principles into student-facing materials.

### OCR Psychology (H567)

The ATP supported OCR's distinctive specification structure, which integrates core studies throughout thematic areas. ATP guidance helps teachers navigate this challenging but intellectually coherent approach, providing:

- Sequencing suggestions balancing historical chronology with conceptual difficulty
- Strategies for teaching classic studies in developmental context (understanding 1960s social psychology requires historical awareness)
- Integration frameworks showing how studies connect across themes

**Philip Banyard**, a longstanding OCR textbook author, represents ATP perspectives in OCR subject communities, ensuring teacher voice informs specification evolution.

### Edexcel (Pearson) Psychology (9PSO)

The ATP has worked with Edexcel on their distinctive topic approach (social psychology, cognitive psychology, biological psychology, learning theories, plus clinical and criminological applications). ATP training addresses:

- Managing the increased breadth compared to other specifications
- Integrating diverse topic areas into coherent psychological understanding
- Addressing the applications focus without sacrificing theoretical depth

### WJEC/Eduqas

For Wales-specific provision, the ATP collaborates with WJEC on specification design reflecting Welsh educational priorities whilst maintaining UK-wide standards compatibility. This includes considering Welsh-medium teaching materials and culturally appropriate examples.

## Future Directions and Current Priorities

The ATP's current strategic priorities include:

**Digital pedagogy**: Developing guidance on effective online and blended learning for Psychology, particularly following COVID-19 pandemic acceleration of digital education. This includes evaluating educational technology specifically for Psychology teaching, recognising that generic educational apps may not suit subject-specific requirements.

**Curriculum diversification**: Advocating for greater cultural diversity in psychological research representation within specifications. The ATP has formally recommended increasing non-Western research examples, female researcher representation, and contemporary studies addressing 21st-century issues.

**Mental health literacy**: Positioning Psychology education as contributing to student wellbeing and mental health awareness, whilst maintaining scientific objectivity and avoiding pathologisation. The ATP is developing guidance distinguishing educational discussion of mental health from therapeutic intervention.

**Climate psychology integration**: Supporting teachers incorporating environmental psychology and climate change psychological aspects into existing specifications, reflecting growing student interest and societal relevance.

## Conclusion

The Association for the Teaching of Psychology represents an essential professional infrastructure for UK Psychology teachers, providing pedagogical expertise, resources, advocacy, and community that isolated practitioners cannot generate independently. Through the contributions of key figures including **Cara Flanagan**, **Philip Banyard**, **Mark Billingham**, and **Mike Cardwell**, the ATP has established evidence-based teaching principles specifically adapted to Psychology's unique characteristics: substantial content, diverse methodological approaches, theoretical pluralism, and social sensitivity.

For curriculum leaders and teachers, ATP engagement offers immediate practical benefits through resources and professional development, alongside long-term career sustainability through professional community and pedagogical expertise. As Psychology continues evolving both as a science and as a curriculum subject, the ATP ensures teaching practice remains informed by both psychological science and educational research, maintaining the quality that has established Psychology as one of the UK's most popular and valuable educational disciplines.
