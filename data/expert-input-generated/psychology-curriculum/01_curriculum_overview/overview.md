# Psychology Curriculum Overview (A Level)

## Introduction to A Level Psychology in the UK

A Level Psychology has become one of the most popular subjects in UK secondary education, with approximately 70,000 students sitting examinations annually. The subject represents students' first formal encounter with psychology as a distinct academic discipline, bridging natural sciences and social sciences. Unlike GCSE, where Psychology is not part of the National Curriculum core provision, A Level Psychology is offered by all major examination boards and provides a robust foundation for further study and numerous career pathways.

## Examination Board Specifications

### AQA Specification (7182)

AQA is the dominant examination board for A Level Psychology, with approximately 60% market share. The specification, revised in 2015 for first teaching in 2015 and refined in subsequent years, emphasises the scientific nature of psychology whilst acknowledging its diverse methodological approaches.

The AQA specification comprises:
- **Paper 1: Introductory Topics in Psychology** (96 marks, 2 hours) covering Social Influence, Memory, Attachment, and Psychopathology
- **Paper 2: Psychology in Context** (96 marks, 2 hours) covering Approaches in Psychology, Biopsychology, and Research Methods
- **Paper 3: Issues and Options in Psychology** (96 marks, 2 hours) covering Issues and Debates plus three options from: Relationships, Gender, Cognition and Development, Schizophrenia, Eating Behaviour, Stress, Aggression, Forensic Psychology, and Addiction

Cara Flanagan and Mike Cardwell, longstanding contributors to AQA Psychology education, have produced extensive resources supporting this specification, including the widely-adopted "AQA Psychology for A Level" textbooks published by Illuminate Publishing.

### Edexcel/Pearson Specification (9PSO)

The Edexcel specification takes a more thematic approach, organising content around key psychological themes rather than discrete topics. This specification comprises:
- **Paper 1: Foundations in Psychology** examining social psychology, cognitive psychology, biological psychology, and learning theories
- **Paper 2: Applications of Psychology** focusing on clinical psychology, criminological psychology, and child psychology
- **Paper 3: Psychological Skills** emphasising research methods, mathematical skills, and synoptic assessment

Philip Banyard has been influential in shaping Edexcel resources, particularly emphasising the application of psychological theory to real-world contexts.

### OCR Specification (H567)

OCR offers two distinct approaches through their A Level (H567) specification:
- **Core Studies**: In-depth examination of 20 classic and contemporary studies across five approaches (cognitive, developmental, individual differences, physiological, and social)
- **Psychological Themes**: Investigation of how psychological understanding has developed over time
- **Applied Psychology**: Options including child development, environmental psychology, health and clinical psychology, psychology of sport and exercise, and forensic psychology

The core studies approach, unique to OCR, provides students with detailed knowledge of landmark research, fostering critical evaluation skills and methodological understanding.

### WJEC/Eduqas Specification

The WJEC specification serves primarily Welsh centres and some English schools seeking an alternative approach. It features:
- Component 1: Past to Present – covering approaches, research methods, and key studies
- Component 2: Investigating Behaviour – examining social, cognitive, developmental, and biological psychology
- Component 3: Implications in the Real World – applying psychology to education, criminology, health, and organisations

## Key Content Areas

### Research Methods and Mathematical Skills

All specifications emphasise research methods as fundamental to psychological literacy. Students must understand:

- **Experimental methods**: Laboratory, field, natural, and quasi-experiments, including understanding of independent variables (IV), dependent variables (DV), confounding variables, and control
- **Non-experimental methods**: Observations (naturalistic, controlled, participant, non-participant), self-report techniques (questionnaires, interviews), correlational analysis, and case studies
- **Scientific processes**: Hypothesis formulation, operationalisation, sampling techniques (random, systematic, stratified, opportunity, volunteer), pilot studies, and ethical considerations
- **Data handling**: Measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), measures of dispersion (range, standard deviation), presentation methods (tables, graphs, charts), and distributions (normal and skewed)
- **Inferential statistics**: Understanding probability and significance, including awareness of statistical tests (though calculation is not always required)

Mark Billingham's work on teaching research methods emphasises the importance of practical application and students conducting their own investigations to consolidate methodological understanding.

### Approaches in Psychology

The major theoretical approaches form the conceptual framework for A Level Psychology:

- **Biological approach**: Emphasising genetic and neurochemical explanations, evolution and behaviour, and the structure and function of the nervous system
- **Behaviourist approach**: Including classical conditioning (Pavlov) and operant conditioning (Skinner), with emphasis on observable behaviour and environmental determinism
- **Social learning theory**: Bandura's work on observational learning, identification, modelling, and vicarious reinforcement
- **Cognitive approach**: Information processing models, schema theory, and the role of inference in understanding behaviour
- **Psychodynamic approach**: Freud's theories of the unconscious mind, personality structure (id, ego, superego), defence mechanisms, and psychosexual development
- **Humanistic approach**: Rogers and Maslow's emphasis on free will, self-actualisation, and the hierarchy of needs

Cara Flanagan's textbooks particularly excel in presenting these approaches accessibly whilst maintaining academic rigour, helping students understand both the content and the synoptic connections between approaches.

### Core Content Topics

#### Memory
Students examine multi-store model (Atkinson & Shiffrin), working memory model (Baddeley & Hitch), explanations for forgetting (interference, retrieval failure), factors affecting eyewitness testimony, and cognitive interview techniques. This topic exemplifies psychology's scientific credentials through controlled experimental work.

#### Attachment
Coverage includes Schaffer's stages of attachment, types of attachment (secure, insecure-avoidant, insecure-resistant) identified through Ainsworth's Strange Situation, explanations of attachment (learning theory, Bowlby's monotropic theory), Romanian orphan studies, and influence of early attachment on later relationships. This demonstrates psychology's developmental perspective.

#### Psychopathology
Students study definitions of abnormality (statistical infrequency, deviation from social norms, failure to function adequately, deviation from ideal mental health), characteristics and explanations of phobias, depression, and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), plus biological and psychological treatments. This showcases psychology's practical applications in mental health.

#### Social Influence
This includes conformity (Asch's research), obedience to authority (Milgram's studies), situational and dispositional explanations, resistance to social influence, minority influence (Moscovici), and social change. Philip Banyard has written extensively about the ethical implications of classic social influence research and its contemporary relevance.

#### Biopsychology
Coverage spans the nervous system structure and function, endocrine system, fight or flight response, localisation of brain function, hemispheric lateralisation, neural plasticity, functional recovery after trauma, ways of studying the brain (fMRI, EEG, ERP, post-mortem), biological rhythms, and sleep. This content is demanding but demonstrates psychology's status as a natural science.

## Issues and Debates

A distinctive feature of A Level Psychology is the requirement to engage with broader issues and debates, promoting synoptic understanding:

### Gender and Culture
- **Gender bias**: Alpha bias (exaggerating differences) and beta bias (minimising differences), androcentrism in research and theory
- **Culture bias**: Ethnocentrism, cultural relativism, emic and etic approaches
- **Implications**: How bias affects the validity and generalisability of psychological research

### Free Will and Determinism
- **Hard determinism**: Behaviour entirely determined by external or internal forces
- **Soft determinism**: Some constraint on free will but scope for personal choice
- **Biological determinism**: Genetic and neurochemical influences
- **Environmental determinism**: Behaviourist emphasis on conditioning
- **Psychic determinism**: Freudian unconscious drives
- **Implications**: For legal system, moral responsibility, and therapeutic approaches

### Nature-Nurture Debate
- **Heredity and environment**: Genetic inheritance versus experience and learning
- **Interactionist approaches**: Diathesis-stress model, epigenetics
- **Research evidence**: Twin studies, adoption studies, cross-cultural research
- **Contemporary understanding**: Moving beyond dichotomous thinking

### Holism and Reductionism
- **Reductionist approaches**: Biological reductionism, environmental reductionism
- **Holistic approaches**: Humanistic psychology, Gestalt psychology
- **Levels of explanation**: Social, psychological, and biological
- **Implications**: For treatment approaches and understanding complexity

### Idiographic and Nomothetic Approaches
- **Idiographic**: In-depth study of individuals (case studies, unstructured interviews)
- **Nomothetic**: Establishing general laws through study of large groups
- **Examples**: Freud's case studies versus behaviourist experimental work
- **Complementarity**: How both approaches contribute to psychological understanding

### Ethical and Social Implications
- **Ethical issues in research**: Building on British Psychological Society (BPS) Code of Ethics and Conduct
- **Social sensitivity**: Implications of research for groups or individuals
- **Socially sensitive research**: Examples including intelligence testing, relationships research, and attachment theory

Cara Flanagan and Mike Cardwell emphasise that these debates should not be taught as discrete topics but woven throughout the course, enabling students to develop sophisticated evaluative commentary.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Designing a Two-Year Programme of Study

Most centres adopt a linear approach, teaching AS-equivalent content in Year 12 before advancing to A2 content in Year 13, though since 2015 AS qualifications are decoupled from A Level. Effective curriculum planning requires:

1. **Sequencing content appropriately**: Starting with concrete, accessible topics (e.g., memory, social influence) before progressing to more abstract content (e.g., issues and debates)
2. **Spiral curriculum approach**: Revisiting key concepts with increasing sophistication
3. **Integration of research methods**: Embedding methodological understanding throughout rather than teaching as isolated content
4. **Progressive skill development**: Building from description to evaluation to application
5. **Practical work**: Incorporating experiments and investigations to develop procedural understanding

### Assessment Objectives

All specifications assess three key objectives with similar weighting:

- **AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding)**: Approximately 35% - demonstrating knowledge of psychological ideas, processes, procedures, and research
- **AO2 (Application)**: Approximately 30% - applying knowledge to familiar and unfamiliar contexts, including novel scenarios and research design
- **AO3 (Analysis, Interpretation and Evaluation)**: Approximately 35% - analysing, interpreting and evaluating psychological information, including research evidence, issues, debates, and approaches

Mark Billingham's guidance on assessment emphasises that evaluation (AO3) causes greatest difficulty for students, requiring explicit teaching of evaluative frameworks including:
- Research evidence for and against
- Methodological strengths and limitations
- Practical applications and implications
- Ethical considerations
- Alternative explanations or theories
- Issues and debates relevance

### Developing Extended Writing Skills

A Level Psychology requires substantial extended writing, with 8-mark, 12-mark, and 16-mark questions common across specifications. Students must develop:

- **Structured essay technique**: Clear introduction, systematic coverage, integrated evaluation
- **Precision in terminology**: Accurate use of psychological vocabulary
- **Citation skills**: Referencing researchers and studies appropriately
- **Elaboration**: Explaining the significance of points rather than mere description
- **Synoptic thinking**: Making connections across topics and approaches

Teachers should provide regular practice with extended questions, using mark schemes transparently to develop students' understanding of assessment requirements.

### Teaching Controversial and Sensitive Content

Psychology necessarily addresses sensitive topics including mental health, criminal behaviour, substance abuse, and sexuality. Teachers must:

- **Establish classroom protocols**: Creating psychologically safe environments where students feel comfortable discussing potentially distressing material
- **Contextualise content appropriately**: Distinguishing clinical terminology from colloquial usage
- **Provide support resources**: Information about counselling services, helplines, and support organisations
- **Model critical thinking**: Examining psychological concepts objectively whilst acknowledging human experience
- **Follow safeguarding procedures**: Remaining alert to disclosures or concerns arising from curriculum content

Philip Banyard has written extensively about the ethical responsibilities of psychology teachers in handling sensitive content with appropriate professional boundaries.

### Practical Work and Investigations

Although A Level Psychology does not require a separate practical endorsement like sciences, practical work remains essential for developing methodological understanding. Effective practice includes:

- **Replication studies**: Students conducting simplified versions of classic experiments
- **Original investigations**: Planning and conducting research on ethically appropriate topics
- **Data handling exercises**: Analysing existing data sets to develop statistical literacy
- **Critical evaluation**: Examining published research for methodological strengths and limitations
- **Ethics approval**: Using departmental ethics procedures to reinforce understanding of ethical principles

The BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct (2018) and Code of Human Research Ethics (2014) provide frameworks for ensuring student investigations meet appropriate ethical standards.

## Resources and Professional Development

### Key Textbooks

Cara Flanagan and colleagues have authored the most widely adopted textbook series:
- "AQA Psychology for A Level Year 1 & AS" (Flanagan & Davies)
- "AQA Psychology for A Level Year 2" (Flanagan et al.)

These texts exemplify best practice through clear explanations, appropriate challenge, extensive research evidence, and integrated skill development.

Other significant resources include:
- Philip Banyard's "Essential Psychology" series
- OCR-specific texts focusing on core studies
- Mike Cardwell's contributions to examination practice materials

### Professional Organisations

- **Association for the Teaching of Psychology (ATP)**: UK organisation supporting psychology teachers through conferences, publications (Psychology Teaching Review), and professional networks
- **British Psychological Society (BPS)**: Provides teaching resources, ethical guidance, and opportunities for teachers to engage with contemporary psychological research
- **Psychology Teacher Network**: Online communities facilitating resource sharing and professional dialogue

### Continuing Professional Development

Effective psychology teaching requires ongoing engagement with:
- **Contemporary research**: Psychology is a rapidly evolving discipline requiring teachers to update subject knowledge regularly
- **Pedagogical approaches**: Research on effective teaching strategies specific to psychology education
- **Assessment practice**: Understanding mark schemes and assessment objectives deeply
- **Digital resources**: Evaluating and implementing technology-enhanced learning appropriately

## Progression Routes and Career Applications

A Level Psychology provides excellent preparation for:

### Higher Education
- **Psychology degrees**: BSc and BA programmes, requiring accreditation by the BPS for professional training
- **Related disciplines**: Neuroscience, cognitive science, education, criminology, sociology
- **Professional programmes**: Medicine, nursing, social work, teaching, occupational therapy

### Career Pathways
Direct career applications include:
- Clinical, counselling, educational, forensic, occupational, and sports psychology (requiring postgraduate training)
- Healthcare professions
- Education and teaching
- Human resources and organisational development
- Market research and consumer psychology
- Criminal justice system roles

Mike Cardwell has emphasised that psychology develops transferable skills valuable across employment sectors: critical thinking, research literacy, statistical reasoning, ethical awareness, and understanding of human behaviour.

## Current Developments and Future Directions

### Curriculum Evolution

Recent developments include:
- **Increased mathematical content**: Greater emphasis on statistical understanding and quantitative reasoning
- **Contemporary studies**: Updating specifications to include recent research alongside classic studies
- **Diversity and inclusion**: Addressing historical biases in psychological research and theory
- **Digital psychology**: Considering implications of technology for behaviour and mental health

### Research-Informed Practice

Cara Flanagan and others advocate for evidence-based teaching in psychology, including:
- Retrieval practice and spaced repetition for memory consolidation
- Elaborative interrogation to deepen understanding
- Interleaving of topics to enhance discrimination and application
- Feedback practices that promote metacognition

### Widening Participation

Psychology teachers should address:
- **Representation**: Highlighting contributions of diverse psychologists and research with varied populations
- **Cultural context**: Examining psychological concepts from multiple cultural perspectives
- **Accessibility**: Ensuring curriculum delivery meets needs of all learners
- **Aspirations**: Supporting students from underrepresented backgrounds to consider psychology higher education and careers

## Conclusion

A Level Psychology represents a rigorous, scientific qualification that develops critical thinking, methodological understanding, and insight into human behaviour. Successful teaching requires deep subject knowledge, pedagogical expertise, and commitment to evidence-based practice. By engaging with resources from experts like Cara Flanagan, Philip Banyard, Mark Billingham, and Mike Cardwell, teachers can deliver outstanding psychology education that prepares students for higher education, professional psychology careers, and informed citizenship in an increasingly complex world.
