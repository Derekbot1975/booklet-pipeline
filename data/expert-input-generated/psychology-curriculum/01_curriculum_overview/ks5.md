# Psychology at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview and Statutory Framework

Key Stage 5 Psychology represents a significant departure from the GCSE curriculum, introducing students (typically aged 16-18) to psychology as a rigorous academic discipline. Unlike Key Stages 1-4, A Level Psychology is not part of the statutory National Curriculum but falls under qualifications regulated by Ofqual and delivered through various examination boards. The subject is classified as a science at A Level, requiring institutions to provide appropriate laboratory access and practical investigation opportunities.

The A Level typically spans two years, structured as AS (one year) and A2 (second year), though most institutions now teach the full linear A Level following the 2015 reforms. Psychology consistently ranks among the most popular A Level choices, with over 70,000 students taking the subject annually in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

## Key Examination Boards and Specifications

### AQA Psychology (7182)

AQA is the most widely adopted specification, accounting for approximately 55% of A Level Psychology entries. The specification, developed with input from Cara Flanagan and other leading curriculum designers, comprises:

**Paper 1: Introductory Topics in Psychology**
- Social influence
- Memory
- Attachment
- Psychopathology

**Paper 2: Psychology in Context**
- Approaches in psychology
- Biopsychology
- Research methods

**Paper 3: Issues and Options in Psychology**
- Issues and debates in psychology
- Option topics (e.g., relationships, schizophrenia, aggression, forensic psychology)

The AQA specification explicitly requires 10% mathematical content, 25% practical skills assessment, and emphasises scientific methodology throughout.

### Edexcel Psychology (9PSO)

Edexcel's specification takes a distinctly thematic approach:

**Paper 1: Foundations in Psychology**
- Social psychology
- Cognitive psychology
- Biological psychology
- Learning theories

**Paper 2: Applications of Psychology**
- Clinical psychology
- Criminological psychology
- Child psychology
- Health psychology

**Paper 3: Psychological Skills**
- Research methods with practical applications
- Content review and synoptic assessment

### OCR Psychology (H567)

OCR offers both H167 (AS) and H567 (A Level) qualifications, structured around core studies:

**Component 1: Research Methods**
- Research design and implementation
- Data analysis and interpretation

**Component 2: Psychological Themes**
- Studies organised thematically across areas

**Component 3: Applied Psychology**
- Issues in mental health
- Criminal psychology
- Environmental psychology

### WJEC Eduqas Psychology

Popular in Wales, this specification emphasises:

**Component 1: Past to Present**
- Historical and contemporary approaches
- Research methods

**Component 2: Investigating Behaviour**
- Social psychology
- Cognitive psychology
- Biological psychology
- Developmental psychology

**Component 3: Implications in the Real World**
- Applied areas including clinical, criminological, and child psychology

## Core Content Areas

### Research Methods and Scientific Thinking

All specifications emphasise psychology as an empirical science. Students must demonstrate competence in:

- **Experimental methods**: Laboratory, field, natural, and quasi-experiments
- **Non-experimental methods**: Observations, self-reports (questionnaires and interviews), correlations, case studies
- **Data handling**: Descriptive statistics (measures of central tendency and dispersion), inferential statistics (appropriate tests for different data types)
- **Mathematical requirements**: Percentages, ratios, fractions, estimations, standard form, significant figures, graphs and charts

As Cara Flanagan and Mike Cardwell emphasise in their influential textbooks, practical research skills should be embedded throughout teaching rather than isolated. Students must complete a minimum of 12 practical activities across the two-year course, though these are not formally assessed through coursework (following 2015 reforms that removed the coursework component).

### Approaches in Psychology

This core area provides epistemological foundations:

**Biological approach**: Neural and hormonal mechanisms, genetic basis of behaviour, evolutionary explanations. Key researchers include Raine (brain abnormalities and violence), Gottesman (genetic concordance in schizophrenia).

**Behaviourist approach**: Classical and operant conditioning, role of environment in shaping behaviour. Founded on Pavlov's work with dogs, Skinner's operant chambers, and Watson's Little Albert study.

**Social learning theory**: Bandura's research on observational learning, vicarious reinforcement, identification and modelling processes.

**Cognitive approach**: Information processing models, schemas, theoretical and computer models, emergence of cognitive neuroscience. Links to artificial intelligence and machine learning increasingly relevant.

**Psychodynamic approach**: Freud's psychosexual stages, tripartite personality structure (id, ego, superego), defence mechanisms, influence of unconscious processes.

**Humanistic approach**: Maslow's hierarchy of needs, Rogers' person-centred therapy, emphasis on free will and self-actualisation.

Philip Banyard emphasises the importance of teaching these approaches as complementary perspectives rather than competing theories, encouraging students to apply multiple explanatory frameworks to psychological phenomena.

### Biopsychology

A distinctive feature of A Level Psychology is substantial neuroscience content:

- **Nervous system structure and function**: Central and peripheral nervous systems, somatic and autonomic divisions, sympathetic and parasympathetic systems
- **Endocrine system**: Glands, hormones, and their behavioural effects
- **Neurons**: Structure, function, synaptic transmission, excitation and inhibition
- **Brain localisation**: Historical evidence (Broca, Wernicke) and contemporary neuroimaging
- **Hemispheric lateralisation**: Split-brain research (Sperry)
- **Plasticity and functional recovery**: Neuroplasticity following trauma
- **Biological rhythms**: Circadian, infradian, and ultradian rhythms

This content requires specific practical demonstrations. Teachers should ideally have access to brain models, EEG demonstrations, or virtual laboratory software.

### Core Content Topics

Specific topics vary by specification but commonly include:

**Memory**: Multi-store model (Atkinson & Shiffrin), working memory model (Baddeley & Hitch), long-term memory types, explanations of forgetting including interference and retrieval failure, eyewitness testimony research (Loftus).

**Attachment**: Types of attachment (Ainsworth's Strange Situation), explanations (Bowlby's monotropic theory, learning theory), Romanian orphan studies (Rutter), influence on later relationships.

**Social influence**: Conformity (Asch), obedience (Milgram), social change, minority influence (Moscovici), resistance to social influence.

**Psychopathology**: Definitions of abnormality, characteristics and explanations of phobias, depression, and OCD, treatments including systematic desensitisation, cognitive behavioural therapy, and drug treatments.

## Key Debates and Issues

All specifications require explicit coverage of debates representing fundamental epistemological tensions in psychology. As Mark Billingham highlights, these debates should permeate teaching rather than being taught as isolated content:

### Free Will vs Determinism

The extent to which human behaviour is self-directed versus controlled by internal or external forces. Biological determinism contrasts with humanistic emphasis on personal agency.

### Nature vs Nurture

Genetic and evolutionary influences versus environmental and social learning. Contemporary teaching should emphasise the interactionist position and epigenetics.

### Holism vs Reductionism

Whether behaviour is best understood by examining complex systems or reducing to constituent elements. Biological reductionism contrasts with humanistic holistic approaches.

### Idiographic vs Nomothetic

Case study approaches seeking unique understanding versus experimental approaches establishing general laws.

### Ethical Implications of Research

Protection from harm, informed consent, deception, confidentiality, and privacy. Students must evaluate historical studies (e.g., Milgram, Watson) using contemporary BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct (2018).

### Gender and Culture Bias

Alpha and beta bias, androcentrism, ethnocentrism, cultural relativism. Increasing emphasis on psychology's universality assumptions and generalisability limitations.

### Socially Sensitive Research

Research with potential social consequences for participants or represented groups. Examples include research into race and IQ, genetic explanations for criminality.

## Pedagogical Approaches for A Level Psychology

### Development from GCSE

For students progressing from GCSE Psychology, teachers must recognise:

- Greater emphasis on **evaluation and critical analysis** (AO3 skills typically worth 30-35% of marks)
- Introduction of **synoptic assessment** requiring connections across topics
- Substantially increased **mathematical content** and statistical analysis
- Expectation of **independent research** and wider reading
- More sophisticated understanding of **research methodology**

For students without GCSE Psychology (the majority), initial scaffolding is essential, particularly for research methods understanding.

### Practical Work Requirements

Following 2015 reforms, practical work is assessed indirectly through examination questions rather than direct coursework assessment. However, Ofqual requires evidence that students have:

- Completed minimum 12 practical activities
- Covered required apparatus and techniques
- Maintained appropriate records

**Suggested practical activities include:**

1. Observation of conformity behaviour
2. Memory experiment (interference, levels of processing)
3. Correlation between variables (e.g., stress and illness)
4. Content analysis of media representations
5. Interview regarding attachment types
6. Questionnaire construction and analysis
7. Strange Situation observation (via video)
8. Stroop effect experiment
9. Reconstruction of automobile destruction (Loftus & Palmer replication)
10. Perceptual set investigation
11. Learning experiment (reinforcement schedules)
12. Hemispheric lateralisation task

Cara Flanagan advocates for embedding practical work throughout teaching sequences rather than concentrated practical blocks, enhancing both engagement and retention.

### Assessment Objectives

Understanding AO weighting is crucial for effective teaching:

**AO1: Demonstrate knowledge and understanding** (25-30%)
- Accurate description of theories, research, concepts
- Appropriate psychological terminology

**AO2: Apply knowledge and understanding** (30-35%)
- Application to novel scenarios
- Analysis of research methods
- Mathematical skills

**AO3: Analyse, interpret, and evaluate** (30-35%)
- Critical evaluation using evidence
- Discussion of strengths and limitations
- Comparison of approaches
- Consideration of issues and debates

Many students struggle with AO3 skills, particularly constructing sustained critical commentary. Mike Cardwell emphasises teaching explicit evaluation frameworks:

- **Research evidence**: Supporting or contradicting studies
- **Alternative explanations**: Competing theories
- **Methodological evaluation**: Strengths and limitations of research methods
- **Application**: Real-world relevance and practical value
- **Issues and debates**: Links to broader psychological questions

### Extended Writing Development

A Level Psychology requires substantial extended writing, with AQA including 16-mark essay questions and Edexcel 20-mark essays. Philip Banyard suggests scaffolding essay writing through:

1. **Modelling**: Teacher demonstration with think-aloud protocols
2. **Shared construction**: Collaborative essay planning
3. **Guided practice**: Writing with structured frameworks
4. **Independent application**: Gradual removal of scaffolds

Essay plans should emphasise:
- **Clear structure**: Introduction, thematic paragraphs, conclusion
- **Balanced coverage**: Description and evaluation integrated
- **Developed commentary**: Extended elaboration points rather than superficial lists
- **Specialist terminology**: Accurate psychological vocabulary
- **Coherence**: Logical progression and clear argument

### Mathematical Skills Development

The 10% mathematical requirement presents challenges for humanities-oriented students. Essential skills include:

- Calculating percentages, fractions, ratios
- Interpreting tables, charts, graphs
- Calculating means, medians, modes, ranges, standard deviations
- Understanding probability and significance
- Selecting and justifying appropriate statistical tests
- Interpreting statistical test results

Teachers should integrate mathematical practice throughout rather than isolating in research methods units. Real data from classic studies provides authentic contexts.

## Transition and Progression

### University Progression

A Level Psychology provides excellent preparation for psychology degrees, though universities increasingly require or prefer additional science A Levels (particularly Biology or Mathematics). The British Psychological Society (BPS) accreditation requirements for undergraduate programmes align closely with A Level content.

Psychology A Level also supports progression to:
- Medicine and healthcare professions
- Teaching and education
- Social work and counselling
- Business and management (organisational psychology applications)
- Law (forensic psychology connections)
- Marketing and consumer research

### Higher Education Entry Requirements

Russell Group universities typically require A*AA-AAB including Psychology. Top programmes (Cambridge, Oxford, UCL, Bath) usually require Biology or Mathematics A Level alongside Psychology.

### Beyond Psychology Degrees

The British Psychological Society (2019) notes that only approximately 15-20% of A Level Psychology students progress to psychology degrees. The subject develops transferable skills highly valued across sectors:

- **Critical evaluation**: Analysis of evidence quality
- **Research literacy**: Understanding empirical methodology
- **Statistical competence**: Data interpretation
- **Essay writing**: Constructing evidence-based arguments
- **Scientific thinking**: Hypothesis formulation and testing

## Contemporary Challenges and Developments

### Curriculum Reforms (2015-Present)

The 2015 specification reforms removed AS as a standalone qualification and eliminated coursework components. These changes aimed to increase rigour but created challenges:

- **Increased content demands** requiring careful curriculum planning
- **Loss of coursework** reducing opportunities for extended independent investigation
- **Assessment by examination only** potentially disadvantaging students who perform better through coursework

### Diversity and Representation

Contemporary psychology teaching must address historical biases. Cara Flanagan emphasises incorporating:

- **Diverse researchers**: Highlighting contributions from women and minority ethnic psychologists
- **Cross-cultural research**: Moving beyond WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, Democratic) populations
- **Contemporary issues**: Including LGBTQ+ psychology, neurodiversity, intersectionality

### Mental Health Awareness

Teaching psychopathology requires sensitivity to student mental health. Approximately 1 in 6 students aged 16-19 experiences mental health difficulties. Teachers should:

- Establish clear boundaries between academic study and personal experience
- Provide information about support services
- Avoid stigmatising language
- Recognise potential for distress when covering clinical topics

### Ethical Considerations

Beyond examining research ethics as content, teachers must model ethical practice:

- Informed consent for practical activities
- Right to withdraw from participation
- Confidentiality of personal disclosures
- Avoiding psychological harm through activities or discussions

## Conclusion

A Level Psychology represents a sophisticated introduction to psychological science, developing critical thinking, research literacy, and scientific understanding. Success requires careful curriculum planning, explicit skills development (particularly evaluation and mathematical competencies), and extensive examination practice.

The subject's popularity reflects both intrinsic interest in understanding human behaviour and excellent progression opportunities. Teachers must balance content coverage with deeper understanding, ensuring students develop genuine psychological literacy rather than superficial examination technique.

As Philip Banyard notes, excellent A Level Psychology teaching transforms students' understanding of themselves and others, developing scientifically informed perspectives on fundamental human questions. This transformative potential, combined with rigorous academic demands, makes Psychology one of the most rewarding subjects to teach at Key Stage 5.
