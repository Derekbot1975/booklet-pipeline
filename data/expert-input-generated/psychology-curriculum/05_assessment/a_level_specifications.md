# A Level Psychology Specifications

## Overview of A Level Psychology in the UK Education System

A Level Psychology is a two-year linear qualification typically studied by students aged 16-18 in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. It sits within the Key Stage 5 curriculum framework and is regulated by Ofqual. The qualification was reformed in 2015, with first teaching beginning in September 2015 and first assessment in summer 2017. This reform aligned Psychology with other A Level subjects, removing AS-level contributions to final A Level grades and introducing a more rigorous, knowledge-based curriculum.

Psychology is classified as a science subject by all major UK exam boards, requiring students to develop both scientific knowledge and research methods competencies. The subject attracts significant numbers of students annually, consistently ranking among the top ten most popular A Levels in the UK.

## Major Exam Board Specifications

### AQA A Level Psychology (7182)

AQA is the largest provider of A Level Psychology in the UK, with approximately 65% of entries. The specification, designed with input from psychology education experts including **Cara Flanagan** and **Mike Cardwell**, emphasises breadth and depth across core psychological approaches.

**Specification Structure:**

**Paper 1: Introductory Topics in Psychology (2 hours, 96 marks, 33.3%)**
- Social influence
- Memory
- Attachment
- Psychopathology

**Paper 2: Psychology in Context (2 hours, 96 marks, 33.3%)**
- Approaches in psychology
- Biopsychology
- Research methods

**Paper 3: Issues and Options in Psychology (2 hours, 96 marks, 33.3%)**
- Issues and debates in psychology (compulsory)
- Three optional topics from: Relationships, Gender, Cognition and development, Schizophrenia, Eating behaviour, Stress, Aggression, Forensic psychology, Addiction

**Key Features:**
- 10% of marks allocated to mathematical skills
- Minimum 25% of marks assess knowledge and understanding of research methods
- Scientific literacy required through critical analysis of research studies
- Synoptic assessment in Paper 3 integrating knowledge across specification

**Assessment Objectives:**
- AO1 (Demonstrate knowledge and understanding): 35-40%
- AO2 (Apply knowledge and understanding): 35-40%
- AO3 (Analyse, interpret and evaluate): 25-30%

### Edexcel (Pearson) A Level Psychology (9PS0)

Edexcel's specification, informed by practitioners including **Philip Banyard**, offers a distinctive approach focusing on applied psychology and contemporary issues.

**Specification Structure:**

**Paper 1: Foundations in Psychology (2 hours 30 minutes, 90 marks, 36%)**
- Social psychology
- Cognitive psychology
- Biological psychology
- Learning theories

**Paper 2: Applications of Psychology (2 hours 30 minutes, 90 marks, 36%)**
- Clinical psychology
- Criminological psychology
- Child psychology
- Health psychology

**Paper 3: Psychological Skills (2 hours, 70 marks, 28%)**
- Research methods
- Issues and debates
- Practical applications

**Key Features:**
- Strong emphasis on applied psychology across four contexts
- Integration of research methods throughout all papers
- Contemporary topics including neuropsychology and brain scanning
- Practical investigation requirement worth 15% of Paper 3
- Enhanced focus on ethical considerations in psychological research

### OCR A Level Psychology (H567)

OCR's specification, developed with contributions from **Mark Billingham**, provides a research-focused approach with particular emphasis on methodological rigour.

**Specification Structure:**

**Paper 1: Research Methods (2 hours, 90 marks, 35%)**
- Research design and response
- Data analysis and interpretation
- Quantitative and qualitative methods
- Ethical considerations
- Designing and conducting psychological investigations

**Paper 2: Core Studies (2 hours, 105 marks, 40%)**
- 15 classic and contemporary studies across five approaches:
  - Cognitive psychology
  - Developmental psychology
  - Individual differences
  - Physiological psychology
  - Social psychology

**Paper 3: Applied Psychology (2 hours, 80 marks, 25%)**
- Three from: Issues in mental health, Criminal psychology, Environmental psychology, Sport and exercise psychology

**Key Features:**
- Unique core studies approach examining seminal research in depth
- Students must conduct their own practical investigation
- Strong methodological foundation in Paper 1
- Explicit teaching of alternative explanations and reductionism
- Assessment of synoptic links between studies

### WJEC/Eduqas A Level Psychology

The Welsh specification provides comprehensive coverage suitable for both English and Welsh contexts, with particular attention to cultural and individual differences.

**Specification Structure:**

**Component 1: Past to Present (2 hours 30 minutes, 100 marks, 40%)**
- History and approaches
- Social psychology
- Cognitive psychology

**Component 2: Investigating Behaviour (2 hours 30 minutes, 100 marks, 40%)**
- Research methods
- Biological psychology
- Developmental psychology

**Component 3: Implications in the Real World (2 hours, 50 marks, 20%)**
- Applications across health, education, and organisational contexts
- Synoptic assessment

**Key Features:**
- Historical development of psychology as a discipline
- Welsh language assessment available
- Strong emphasis on practical applications
- Integrated approach to research methods

## Common Core Elements Across Specifications

### Research Methods Requirements

All UK A Level Psychology specifications require substantial coverage of research methods, reflecting the subject's scientific foundation. **Cara Flanagan** has extensively written about the importance of methodological understanding in psychology education, arguing that it develops critical thinking skills essential for higher education.

**Core methodological content includes:**

- **Experimental methods**: Laboratory, field, natural, and quasi-experiments
- **Non-experimental methods**: Observations (naturalistic, controlled, participant), self-reports (questionnaires, interviews), correlational studies, case studies
- **Data handling**: Descriptive statistics (measures of central tendency, dispersion), inferential statistics (sign test, Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon, chi-squared, Spearman's rho), graphical representations
- **Scientific processes**: Operationalisation, hypothesis formulation, experimental design (independent groups, repeated measures, matched pairs), controlling variables, reliability and validity
- **Ethical considerations**: BPS Code of Ethics and Conduct, informed consent, deception, protection from harm, privacy, confidentiality

### Mathematical Skills Requirements

Following the 2015 reforms, mathematical skills were explicitly incorporated into psychology specifications. A minimum of 10% of marks across all specifications assess mathematical competencies at Level 2 (GCSE Higher Tier equivalent).

**Required mathematical skills include:**

- Percentages, fractions, and ratios
- Calculating means, medians, modes, and ranges
- Interpreting tables, charts, and graphs
- Probability and significance levels
- Standard deviation calculations
- Understanding scattergrams and correlation coefficients
- Converting between different data formats

**Mike Cardwell** has emphasised that mathematical proficiency in psychology enables students to engage critically with empirical evidence and prepares them for university-level research.

## Assessment Objectives Framework

All specifications align with Ofqual's Assessment Objectives framework, ensuring consistency and comparability:

**AO1: Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of scientific ideas, processes, techniques and procedures**
- Recall and define psychological terminology
- Describe theories, studies, and research methods
- Outline key concepts and principles

**AO2: Apply knowledge and understanding of scientific ideas, processes, techniques and procedures**
- Apply psychological concepts to novel scenarios
- Use psychological knowledge to explain behaviour
- Relate research findings to real-world contexts

**AO3: Analyse, interpret and evaluate scientific information, ideas and evidence**
- Evaluate research studies and theories
- Analyse methodological approaches
- Assess validity, reliability, and generalisability
- Make informed judgements about applications of psychology

The weighting of these objectives varies slightly between boards but generally maintains AO1 and AO2 at 35-40% each, with AO3 at 25-30%.

## Issues and Debates: Synoptic Assessment

All specifications include explicit assessment of broader issues and debates in psychology, typically in final examination papers. This synoptic element requires students to integrate knowledge from across their course.

**Core issues and debates typically include:**

- **Gender and culture**: Universality vs. cultural relativism, gender bias (androcentrism, alpha and beta bias)
- **Free will vs. determinism**: Biological, environmental, and psychic determinism
- **Nature vs. nurture**: Genetic influences, environmental factors, interactionist approaches
- **Reductionism vs. holism**: Levels of explanation, biological, environmental, and machine reductionism
- **Idiographic vs. nomothetic approaches**: Individual case studies vs. general laws
- **Ethical implications**: Socially sensitive research, cost-benefit analysis
- **Scientific status**: Paradigms, falsifiability, theory construction, objectivity

**Philip Banyard** has written extensively on teaching these concepts, advocating for integration throughout the course rather than separate treatment in final weeks.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Scheme of Work Development

Teachers should develop comprehensive schemes of work that:

- Sequence topics logically, building methodological knowledge progressively
- Integrate research methods teaching throughout, not as isolated units
- Include regular opportunities for synoptic connections
- Plan for mathematical skills development across topics
- Build in revision and consolidation periods

**Cara Flanagan's** textbooks provide excellent sequencing models, particularly for AQA specifications.

### Differentiation Strategies

Given the academic demands of A Level Psychology, teachers must:

- Provide scaffolded support for extended writing (12- and 16-mark questions)
- Differentiate reading materials to accommodate varying literacy levels
- Use dual coding techniques (verbal and visual information)
- Offer tiered activities for research methods practice
- Support students with mathematical anxieties through concrete examples

### Assessment for Learning

Formative assessment strategies should include:

- Regular low-stakes testing to promote retrieval practice
- Peer assessment using mark schemes to develop evaluative skills
- Self-assessment checklists for essay responses
- Topic tests incorporating mixed AO1, AO2, and AO3 questions
- Mock examinations under timed conditions

**Mark Billingham** emphasises the importance of familiarising students with command words (e.g., "outline," "evaluate," "discuss") and their specific demands.

### Resource Selection

Quality resources are crucial for effective teaching:

- **Textbooks**: Ensure alignment with specific exam board specifications
- **Journal articles**: Introduce accessible research from journals like *Psychology Review* and *Psychology Teaching Review*
- **Digital resources**: Utilise exam board-provided materials, online simulations, and video demonstrations
- **Practical equipment**: Maintain resources for class investigations (reaction time tests, memory experiments)

The **Association for the Teaching of Psychology (ATP)** provides regularly updated resources and professional development opportunities.

## Preparing Students for Higher Education

A Level Psychology serves as preparation for undergraduate psychology degrees and related disciplines. Teachers should:

- Emphasise critical evaluation skills valued in higher education
- Introduce students to primary research literature
- Encourage independent reading beyond specification requirements
- Discuss careers in psychology requiring graduate or postgraduate qualifications
- Explain the distinction between A Level content and degree-level study

The British Psychological Society (BPS) provides guidance on accredited undergraduate programmes and career pathways.

## Quality Assurance and Standardisation

### Internal Assessment Practices

While A Level Psychology no longer includes coursework, teachers must:

- Ensure marking accuracy through regular standardisation exercises
- Use official mark schemes consistently
- Participate in exam board training events
- Review student work samples collaboratively
- Monitor progress against national standards

### External Quality Monitoring

Exam boards conduct regular monitoring through:

- Examiner reports published annually
- Grade statistics and comparative data
- Subject-specific guidance updates
- Training materials for new specifications

Teachers should regularly consult these resources to inform practice.

## Current Developments and Future Directions

### Curriculum Reviews

The psychology curriculum undergoes periodic review. Current discussions in the teaching community include:

- Diversifying the curriculum beyond Western, predominantly white male researchers
- Including more contemporary research methodologies (e.g., neuroimaging, big data)
- Expanding coverage of non-clinical applications
- Addressing representation in case studies and research participants

**Philip Banyard** and others have advocated for greater cultural diversity in psychological content.

### Assessment Innovation

Future developments may include:

- Enhanced use of technology in examinations (e.g., stimulus materials, data analysis)
- Greater emphasis on research design skills
- Modified examination formats to assess higher-order thinking
- Integration of practical skills assessment

### Professional Development

Teachers should engage with:

- Annual ATP conferences and regional networks
- Exam board-specific training days
- Subject specialist networks through social media
- Academic journals focused on psychology education
- BPS events and publications

## Conclusion

UK A Level Psychology specifications provide rigorous, scientifically grounded qualifications preparing students for higher education and careers requiring psychological knowledge. Success requires careful curriculum planning, robust assessment practices, and continuous professional development. Teachers should draw upon the expertise of established figures such as **Cara Flanagan**, **Philip Banyard**, **Mark Billingham**, and **Mike Cardwell**, whose contributions have shaped contemporary psychology education. By understanding the distinctive features of each exam board while maintaining focus on core scientific principles, teachers can deliver outstanding outcomes for their students.
