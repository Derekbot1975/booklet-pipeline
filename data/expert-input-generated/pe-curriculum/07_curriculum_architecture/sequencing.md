# Sequencing PE Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in Physical Education

Sequencing in Physical Education refers to the systematic ordering of learning experiences across Key Stages to ensure progressive skill development, conceptual understanding, and the cultivation of physical literacy. As Margaret Whitehead's seminal work on physical literacy emphasises, effective sequencing must recognise PE as a developmental journey where learners acquire embodied knowledge, confidence, and motivation to engage in physical activity throughout their lives (Whitehead, 2010, *Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse*).

David Kirk (2010, *Physical Education Futures*) argues that curriculum sequencing must move beyond traditional multi-activity models towards pedagogical models that develop deep understanding and transferable movement competencies. This requires careful consideration of progression principles, pedagogical approaches, and assessment frameworks across the 3-18 age range.

## Principles of Effective Sequencing

### Building Blocks of Movement Competence

Len Almond's research on fundamental movement skills provides essential foundations for curriculum sequencing. The progression should follow:

**Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS):**
- Gross motor skill development through unstructured play
- Basic locomotor skills (running, jumping, hopping, skipping)
- Object control fundamentals (throwing, catching, striking)
- Balance and coordination activities

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7):**
- Refinement of fundamental movement skills
- Introduction to basic games principles
- Development of spatial awareness and body management
- Beginning of structured activity experiences in gymnastics, dance, and games

### Spiral Curriculum Model

Following Bruner's spiral curriculum concept, adapted for PE by Dawn Penney and colleagues (Penney & Chandler, 2000), sequencing should revisit concepts with increasing complexity:

- **Revisiting:** Core concepts like tactics, compositional principles, and fitness components return across Key Stages
- **Deepening:** Each return engages learners at more sophisticated cognitive and physical levels
- **Connecting:** Links between activity areas strengthen understanding of transferable principles

## Key Stage Specific Sequencing Frameworks

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The National Curriculum 2014 requires pupils to develop competence in a broad range of physical activities. Sequencing considerations include:

**Year 3-4 Focus:**
- Consolidation of fundamental movement patterns
- Introduction to small-sided modified games
- Development of simple tactics and strategies
- Basic swimming competence (statutory requirement)
- Introduction to outdoor and adventurous activities

**Year 5-6 Focus:**
- Application of combined movement skills in game situations
- Understanding of invasion, net/wall, and striking/fielding game principles
- Enhanced compositional skills in gymnastics and dance
- Development of leadership and teamwork capabilities
- Swimming proficiency and water safety (minimum 25 metres)

**Practical Implications:**
Teachers should employ Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) approaches, as advocated by Almond and colleagues at Loughborough University, to develop tactical awareness alongside technical competence.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

This transitional phase requires sophisticated sequencing to maintain engagement while deepening competence. The National Curriculum emphasises development of technique, performance analysis, and understanding of health and fitness.

**Sequencing Considerations:**

**Year 7:**
- Baseline assessment of prior learning and physical literacy
- Reintroduction of activity areas with increased technical demand
- Introduction to performance analysis concepts
- Development of peer assessment capabilities
- Understanding components of fitness related to specific activities

**Year 8:**
- Application of advanced techniques in competitive contexts
- Introduction to officiating and leadership roles
- Health-related exercise knowledge development
- Specialisation opportunities in preferred activity areas
- Cross-curricular links with science (biomechanics, physiology)

**Year 9:**
- Refined performance in chosen activity pathways
- Independent planning and evaluation of performance
- Preparation for GCSE PE (where offered)
- Advanced tactical understanding and decision-making
- Personal fitness programming

**Research Evidence:**
Kirk and Macdonald's (1998) research on situated learning suggests that KS3 sequencing should create authentic performance contexts where learners understand the cultural and social significance of activities, not merely technical execution.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

Sequencing at KS4 diverges between Core PE and examination PE, requiring differentiated approaches.

**Core PE Sequencing:**
- Personalised activity pathways based on interest and competence
- Leadership and coaching qualification opportunities (e.g., Sports Leaders UK awards)
- Health and lifestyle education integration
- Community sport connections
- Preparation for lifelong participation

**GCSE PE Sequencing (AQA 8582, Edexcel 1PE0, OCR J587, WJEC Eduqas C620U):**

All specifications require integration of:

1. **Practical Performance** (40-50% depending on board)
   - Progressive development in three activities from different categories
   - Sequential skill acquisition followed by competitive application
   - Video analysis and self-reflection

2. **Theoretical Understanding** (50-60%)
   - **Applied anatomy and physiology**: Sequential progression from basic body systems to exercise physiology
   - **Movement analysis**: Building from fundamental movement patterns to biomechanical principles
   - **Physical training**: Progressive understanding from fitness components to periodisation
   - **Use of data**: Developing analytical skills through fitness testing sequences
   - **Sports psychology**: Introduction to mental preparation and motivation theories
   - **Socio-cultural influences**: Understanding factors affecting participation

**Sequencing Strategy:**
Dawn Penney's research (Penney, 2008) on assessment in PE emphasises that theoretical and practical learning should be deliberately integrated rather than taught separately. For example, anatomy lessons should directly connect to performance in practical activities.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

Advanced level sequencing requires sophisticated integration of theoretical knowledge and practical application.

**A-Level PE Specifications (AQA 7582, Edexcel 9PE0, OCR H555, WJEC A620U):**

**Year 12 Sequencing:**
- Detailed physiological knowledge building on GCSE foundations
- Introduction to research methods and critical evaluation
- Biomechanical analysis using appropriate technology
- Advanced skill acquisition theories (schema theory, information processing)
- Historical and contemporary issues in sport

**Year 13 Sequencing:**
- Applied physiology (altitude training, ergogenic aids)
- Advanced biomechanics and performance analysis
- Exercise and sport psychology (arousal, anxiety, leadership)
- Contemporary issues and synoptic connections
- Independent investigation (coursework/NEA)
- Performance excellence in chosen practical activity

**Practical Implications:**
Susan Capel's work (Capel & Whitehead, 2015, *Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School*) emphasises that A-Level sequencing should develop independent learners who can critique sports science literature and apply theoretical concepts to their own performance.

## Vertical and Horizontal Sequencing

### Vertical Sequencing (Progression Across Key Stages)

Effective vertical sequencing ensures cumulative learning:

**Movement Competence Progression:**
- EYFS/KS1: Fundamental movement patterns
- KS2: Combined movement skills in modified contexts
- KS3: Refined techniques in authentic game/performance situations
- KS4/5: Specialised performance with analytical understanding

**Tactical Understanding Progression:**
- KS1: Awareness of space and others
- KS2: Simple tactical principles (width, depth, support)
- KS3: Complex decision-making in competitive contexts
- KS4/5: Strategic planning and adaptation

**Physical Development Progression:**
- Early Years: Natural physical development through play
- KS2: Introduction to structured fitness activities
- KS3: Understanding of health-related fitness
- KS4: Personal exercise programming
- KS5: Advanced training principles and periodisation

### Horizontal Sequencing (Breadth Within Key Stages)

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework emphasises the importance of varied movement experiences within each Key Stage:

**Activity Area Balance:**
The National Curriculum requires exposure to:
- Invasion games (football, basketball, rugby, netball, hockey)
- Net/wall games (tennis, badminton, volleyball)
- Striking/fielding games (cricket, rounders, softball)
- Gymnastics (floor work, apparatus, vaulting)
- Dance (creative, choreographic, cultural forms)
- Athletics (running, jumping, throwing)
- Outdoor and adventurous activities (orienteering, problem-solving)
- Swimming and water safety (KS2 statutory)

**Sequencing Within a Key Stage:**
Research by Kirk and colleagues suggests organising units to build conceptual understanding. For example, at KS3:

*Autumn Term:* Invasion games focus (developing principles applicable across football, hockey, basketball)
*Spring Term:* Gymnastics and dance (compositional skills, aesthetic appreciation)
*Summer Term:* Net/wall and striking/fielding games, athletics

This allows deep engagement with similar tactical or compositional concepts before transitioning to different activity types.

## Pedagogical Models and Sequencing

### Sport Education Model

Developed by Daryl Siedentop and researched extensively by David Kirk in UK contexts, Sport Education provides an alternative sequencing structure:

- Extended units (20+ lessons) focusing on single activities
- Progressive roles: player → captain → coach → official
- Authentic sport experiences including competition seasons
- Development of affiliation and sporting culture

**Sequencing Considerations:**
- Introduce in upper KS2 or KS3 when social maturity supports team affiliation
- Begin with inclusive activities (ultimate frisbee, newcombe ball)
- Progress to traditional sports with established cultures

### Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)

Len Almond and Rod Thorpe's TGfU model suggests sequencing based on game categories:

**Progression:**
1. **Sampling games** within categories (e.g., multiple invasion games)
2. **Modified games** that emphasise tactical problems
3. **Full games** with refined techniques

**Key Stage Application:**
- KS1: Pre-game activities and simple tag games
- KS2: Modified games introducing tactical concepts
- KS3: Full games with tactical emphasis
- KS4/5: Advanced tactical analysis and strategic planning

### Health-Related Exercise Approaches

Sequencing fitness education requires age-appropriate approaches:

**KS1-2:** Active play and enjoyment focus
**KS3:** Introduction to FITT principles (Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type)
**KS4:** Personal programme design using training principles
**KS5:** Advanced training methodologies, periodisation, and scientific justification

Jo Harris's research (Harris, 2000) emphasises that fitness education should be integrated across all activities rather than isolated in 'fitness lessons', ensuring progressive understanding through varied contexts.

## Assessment-Informed Sequencing

### Formative Assessment Integration

Dawn Penney's work on assessment emphasises that sequencing should incorporate regular assessment opportunities that inform subsequent teaching:

**Assessment Sequence Pattern:**
1. **Initial assessment:** Establish baseline competence
2. **Developmental feedback:** Regular formative assessment during learning
3. **Summative evaluation:** Demonstrate progress at unit end
4. **Feed-forward:** Use assessment to plan next learning steps

### End-of-Key-Stage Expectations

The National Curriculum 2014 provides end-of-Key-Stage expectations that inform sequencing:

**End of KS1:** Pupils should:
- Master basic movements
- Develop balance, agility, and coordination
- Engage in cooperative and competitive activities

**End of KS2:** Pupils should:
- Use running, jumping, throwing, and catching in isolation and combination
- Play competitive games with understanding of tactics
- Develop flexibility, strength, technique, and control
- Perform dances using a range of movement patterns
- Take part in outdoor activities
- Swim competently, confidently, and proficiently (minimum 25m)

**End of KS3:** Pupils should:
- Develop techniques with consistency in a broad range of activities
- Develop physical capacity (fitness, strength, stamina, speed, flexibility)
- Analyse performances and demonstrate improvement
- Take part in competitive sports and activities

**End of KS4:** Pupils should tackle complex and demanding contexts:
- In athletics, fitness, health and wellbeing contexts
- Through leadership and officiating opportunities

## Planning Schemes of Work for Progressive Sequencing

### Long-Term Planning (Multi-Year Sequencing)

Curriculum leaders should develop overarching sequences that map:

**Conceptual Development:**
- Tactical concepts across invasion/net-wall/striking-fielding games
- Compositional principles across gymnastics/dance/trampolining
- Movement quality and efficiency across all activities

**Example Three-Year KS3 Sequence (Invasion Games):**

*Year 7:* 
- Autumn: Basketball (maintaining possession, supporting player with ball)
- Spring: Football (creating space, width/depth)
- Summer: Touch rugby (forward momentum, continuity)

*Year 8:*
- Autumn: Basketball (zone defence, transition)
- Spring: Hockey (penalty corners, set plays)
- Summer: Ultimate frisbee (vertical stack, handler movements)

*Year 9:*
- Autumn: Student choice invasion game (independent tactical planning)
- Spring: Combined invasion games (transferable principles)
- Summer: Tournament management and officiating

### Medium-Term Planning (Unit Sequencing)

Individual units (typically 6-12 lessons) should follow pedagogical progressions:

**Typical Unit Structure:**
1. **Orientation phase** (Lessons 1-2): Assessment, game awareness, problem identification
2. **Development phase** (Lessons 3-8): Skill refinement, tactical understanding, practice design
3. **Performance phase** (Lessons 9-12): Competition, performance evaluation, reflection

**Differentiated Pathways:**
Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework suggests providing multiple pathways:
- Challenge-based differentiation (different success criteria)
- Role differentiation (performer, coach, analyst, official)
- Activity differentiation (personal choice within movement categories)

### Short-Term Planning (Lesson Sequencing)

Individual lessons should follow logical progressions:

**Recommended Structure:**
1. **Engaging warm-up** (connecting to previous learning)
2. **Skill/concept introduction** (explicit teaching)
3. **Practice opportunities** (deliberate practice with feedback)
4. **Application in context** (game/performance situation)
5. **Plenary and reflection** (assessment and feed-forward)

Susan Capel's research emphasises that every lesson should connect explicitly to prior learning and preview future development, making the learning journey transparent to students.

## Special Considerations for Sequencing

### Inclusion and Differentiation

Sequencing must accommodate diverse learner needs:

**Physical Ability:**
- Parallel activity progressions (modified activities alongside standard versions)
- Equipment modification sequenced appropriately
- Alternative success criteria at each stage

**SEND Considerations:**
- Additional repetition cycles within sequences
- Multi-sensory learning approaches
- Personalised progression rates

### Gender and Cultural Sensitivity

David Kirk's research on pedagogies of difference highlights the importance of:
- Offering culturally diverse activity options
- Avoiding gender stereotyping in activity sequencing
- Providing single-sex and mixed-sex learning contexts appropriately

### Transition Points

Critical transitions require careful sequencing:

**Primary-Secondary Transition (KS2-KS3):**
- Liaison with feeder schools to understand prior learning
- Initial assessment units to establish baseline
- Bridging units that connect familiar content with new challenges

**KS3-KS4 Transition:**
- Preparation for GCSE theoretical demands
- Introduction to examination practical assessment requirements
- Development of independent learning skills

## Technology-Enhanced Sequencing

Modern sequencing should incorporate digital technologies progressively:

**KS2:** Simple recording devices for performance review
**KS3:** Tablet-based analysis apps, fitness tracking devices
**KS4:** Video analysis software (e.g., Coach's Eye, Hudl Technique), heart rate monitoring
**KS5:** Advanced biomechanical analysis tools, data interpretation software

Dawn Penney's research on assessment suggests that technology integration should support, not replace, embodied learning and should be sequenced to develop digital literacy alongside physical literacy.

## Monitoring and Evaluating Curriculum Sequencing

Curriculum leaders should regularly evaluate sequencing effectiveness:

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Are learners making expected progress across Key Stages?
- Do assessment outcomes show cumulative learning?
- Are learners developing physical literacy attributes (motivation, confidence, knowledge, understanding)?
- Does the sequence support transition to lifelong participation?

**Data Sources:**
- End-of-Key-Stage assessments
- Examination results (GCSE/A-Level PE)
- Student voice feedback
- Participation rates in extra-curricular and community sport
- Physical literacy assessments (e.g., CAPL-2 adaptations)

Margaret Whitehead argues that the ultimate measure of effective sequencing is whether learners develop the disposition to pursue physical activity throughout their lives, not merely their performance at any single point.
