# Progression in PE Skills and Knowledge

## Introduction

Progression in Physical Education represents the systematic development of pupils' physical competence, cognitive understanding, and affective engagement across their educational journey. The UK National Curriculum for PE (2013) establishes statutory requirements for progression from Key Stage 1 through Key Stage 4, emphasising that pupils should "develop competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities" and "be physically active for sustained periods of time." Understanding how skills and knowledge develop over time is fundamental to effective PE curriculum design and pedagogical practice.

Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy provides the theoretical foundation for progression in PE, defining it as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2010). This holistic approach requires teachers to consider progression across multiple domains simultaneously rather than focusing solely on technical skill acquisition.

## Theoretical Frameworks for Progression

### Physical Literacy as a Foundation

Whitehead's physical literacy framework (2013) positions progression not as linear skill accumulation but as the development of embodied capability across four interconnected domains: physical, cognitive, social, and affective. This challenges traditional models that prioritise measurable technical outcomes over broader developmental processes. The International Physical Literacy Association (IPLA) definition, widely adopted across UK education, emphasises that physical literacy is developed through the lifespan, making progression a continuous rather than terminal process.

### Developmental Models

David Kirk (2005) argues in "Physical Education, Youth Sport and Lifelong Participation" that progression in PE must connect with pupils' lives beyond school. His critique of multi-activity, sport-based curricula suggests that authentic progression requires embedding skills within meaningful contexts that promote sustained participation. Kirk's advocacy for models-based practice (2013) provides alternative frameworks for progression, including Sport Education, Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), and Health-Based Physical Education.

Dawn Penney's work on curriculum development (Penney & Chandler, 2000) emphasises that progression must be explicitly planned across year groups and key stages, with clear criteria for advancement. Her research highlights that implicit assumptions about progression often lead to repetition rather than genuine development, particularly in secondary PE where pupils may repeat similar activities annually without meaningful challenge.

## Dimensions of Progression in PE

### Physical Competence Progression

Physical competence progresses from fundamental movement skills (FMS) to context-specific applications and refined techniques. Len Almond's work on fundamental movement (1989) identifies core patterns—locomotor, stability, and manipulative skills—that provide the foundation for all subsequent physical activity.

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7):**
- Master basic movements: running, jumping, throwing, catching
- Develop balance, agility and coordination
- Perform simple sequences and patterns
- Explore equipment with increasing control

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11):**
- Consolidate and apply FMS in combination
- Develop technique in broader range of activities
- Understand tactical principles in modified games
- Perform increasingly complex sequences with accuracy

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14):**
- Refine technique across different activity categories
- Apply skills under pressure and in competitive situations
- Analyse and improve performance
- Develop activity-specific fitness

**Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16):**
- Specialise and personalise skill development
- Demonstrate advanced technique and tactical awareness
- Evaluate performance using sophisticated criteria
- Prepare for lifelong participation or performance pathways

The Association for Physical Education (afPE) "Health Position Paper" (2015) emphasises that physical competence must progress alongside health-related learning, with pupils developing increasingly sophisticated understanding of how physical activity contributes to wellbeing.

### Cognitive Knowledge Progression

Cognitive progression encompasses developing knowledge about movement, health, tactics, rules, and performance analysis. Susan Capel's "Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School" (2016) identifies three knowledge types that progress throughout PE: declarative knowledge (knowing what), procedural knowledge (knowing how), and conditional knowledge (knowing when and why).

At Key Stage 1, cognitive learning focuses on simple action words, understanding space, and recognising feelings during activity. By Key Stage 4, pupils should demonstrate sophisticated understanding of biomechanical principles, energy systems, psychological factors affecting performance, and socio-cultural influences on participation.

The GCSE PE specifications from all major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC) codify this cognitive progression explicitly:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582):**
- Applied anatomy and physiology
- Movement analysis
- Physical training
- Use of data and socio-cultural influences

**OCR GCSE PE (J587):**
- Physical factors affecting performance
- Socio-cultural issues and sports psychology
- Health and fitness and use of data

This academic progression complements practical development, though Penney and colleagues (2009) caution against false dichotomies between "doing" and "thinking" in PE.

### Social and Affective Progression

Progression in social competence moves from simple cooperation and rule-following to leadership, conflict resolution, and inclusive practice. Whitehead (2010) emphasises that affective progression—developing motivation, confidence, and positive attitudes—is as important as physical and cognitive domains.

**Early progression (KS1-2):**
- Taking turns and sharing equipment
- Working safely alongside others
- Showing respect for rules and opponents
- Experiencing success and building confidence

**Middle progression (KS3):**
- Collaborating in teams with different roles
- Officiating and organising activities
- Supporting others' development
- Evaluating own performance honestly

**Later progression (KS4-5):**
- Leading activities and coaching others
- Resolving conflicts and making ethical decisions
- Understanding social justice issues in sport
- Taking responsibility for own participation pathway

The Youth Sport Trust's "TOP Programme" provides structured progression for social and personal development through PE, emphasising leadership, volunteering, and community engagement as advanced manifestations of social competence.

## Principles of Effective Progression

### Coherence and Continuity

Kirk and Macdonald (1998) identify curriculum coherence as essential for progression, requiring vertical integration (building across year groups) and horizontal integration (connections between activities). The National Curriculum statutory guidance requires that schools ensure pupils "progress to their potential," but Penney's research (2008) demonstrates wide variation in how schools interpret and implement progression.

Effective progression requires:

1. **Clear learning intentions** at each stage, articulated in pupil-friendly language
2. **Explicit success criteria** that define what advancement looks like
3. **Assessment frameworks** that identify current capability and next steps
4. **Spiral curriculum design** revisiting concepts with increasing complexity
5. **Differentiation strategies** allowing pupils to progress at appropriate rates

### Breadth and Depth Balance

The National Curriculum specifies activity categories that must be covered: games, dance, gymnastics, athletics, outdoor and adventurous activities, and swimming. At KS3, pupils must experience "a range of activities" including at least two from games. This breadth requirement can tension with depth needed for genuine progression.

David Kirk's critique (2010) suggests that excessive breadth without depth prevents authentic skill development, creating a "smorgasbord curriculum" where pupils sample activities superficially. Conversely, early specialisation risks narrowing physical literacy development. Balancing these requires strategic curriculum planning that provides sufficient time for meaningful progression within activity categories whilst maintaining breadth across domains.

### Developmentally Appropriate Practice

Research by Bailey et al. (2009) in "The Educational Benefits Claimed for Physical Education and School Sport" emphasises that progression must align with pupils' developmental readiness across physical, cognitive, and social dimensions. This challenges one-size-fits-all approaches, requiring teachers to understand:

- **Physical development variations:** Particularly during adolescence, when growth spurts affect coordination and strength
- **Cognitive development:** Progression from concrete to abstract thinking affects tactical understanding
- **Social development:** Peer relationships influence motivation and engagement patterns
- **Individual differences:** Disability, prior experience, cultural background shape progression pathways

The afPE guidance "Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity" (2020 edition) emphasises that progression must never compromise safety, with challenge matched to capability.

## Assessing Progression

### Assessment Frameworks

Effective assessment of progression requires looking beyond performance outcomes to underlying capabilities. Whitehead's physical literacy assessment approaches focus on holistic development rather than sport-specific performance. The "Physical Literacy Consensus Statement" (IPLA, 2017) cautions against reducing physical literacy to measurable competencies, though acknowledges the need for monitoring progression.

Assessment approaches include:

**Formative assessment:**
- Observation of application in varied contexts
- Questioning to reveal understanding
- Peer and self-assessment developing metacognitive awareness
- Video analysis enabling reflection on progression

**Summative assessment:**
- National Curriculum level descriptors (legacy) or age-related expectations
- GCSE specifications with graded criteria (1-9 scale)
- Progress 8 measures for secondary accountability
- Personal development portfolios demonstrating journey over time

### Progress Indicators

The National Curriculum states pupils should "make progress" but does not specify standardised measures. Schools typically use:

- **Skill-based rubrics** defining progression in specific techniques
- **Game performance assessment instruments (GPAI)** measuring tactical understanding
- **Physical competence batteries** such as Dragon Challenge (Wales) or fundamental movement assessments
- **Self-report measures** of confidence, motivation, and understanding
- **Participation tracking** monitoring engagement patterns over time

Susan Capel (2007) argues that assessment literacy among PE teachers is crucial for supporting progression, requiring professional development in assessment design, implementation, and interpretation.

## Barriers to Progression

### Structural Barriers

Research by Penney and colleagues identifies several structural barriers to effective progression in PE:

1. **Curriculum time reduction:** Many schools provide less than two hours weekly PE at KS3-4, limiting opportunity for development
2. **Facility constraints:** Access to specialist spaces affects activity range and progression opportunities
3. **Teacher expertise:** Non-specialist PE teaching, particularly at primary level, may limit progression quality
4. **Transition issues:** Weak liaison between primary and secondary phases causes repetition or inappropriate challenge
5. **Assessment systems:** Accountability measures may prioritise participation over progression quality

### Pedagogical Barriers

Kirk's work on pedagogical models (2013) suggests traditional multi-activity approaches often fail to provide sufficient engagement time for meaningful progression. Directive teaching styles may develop narrow competencies whilst limiting transfer and understanding. The dominance of competitive games can marginalise pupils who don't progress in traditional sports, creating disengagement.

### Individual Barriers

Pupils experience varied barriers to progression:
- **Fixed mindset** about physical capability (Dweck, 2006 applied to PE)
- **Previous negative experiences** creating anxiety or avoidance
- **Social comparison** with high-performers reducing motivation
- **Cultural factors** affecting participation in specific activities
- **Body image concerns** particularly during adolescence
- **Special educational needs** requiring adapted approaches

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning for Progression

Effective planning requires:

1. **Long-term curriculum mapping** showing how activities build across terms and years
2. **Medium-term schemes** with explicit progression steps identified
3. **Lesson planning** with differentiated progressions and regression options
4. **Assessment integration** monitoring and responding to actual progression
5. **Flexible grouping** enabling pupils to work at appropriate challenge levels

The afPE "National Curriculum for Physical Education Guidance" (2013) provides exemplification of progression across key stages, though emphasises schools' autonomy in interpretation.

### Differentiation Strategies

Supporting all pupils' progression requires sophisticated differentiation:

- **Task modification:** STEP principle (Space, Task, Equipment, People) adjusting challenge
- **Support variation:** Scaffolding, peer support, teacher intervention intensity
- **Outcome openness:** Setting goals matched to individual starting points
- **Pace adjustment:** Allowing different time for skill consolidation
- **Representation variety:** Visual, verbal, kinaesthetic instruction methods

### Feedback for Progression

Research on feedback in PE (Mouratidis et al., 2008) demonstrates that feedback quality significantly affects progression. Effective feedback:

- **Focuses on learning intentions** directly related to progression criteria
- **Provides specific improvement points** rather than general praise
- **Encourages self-regulation** developing pupils' own assessment capability
- **Balances challenge and support** maintaining motivation whilst promoting development
- **Uses varied formats** immediate verbal, delayed written, video-based, peer-generated

## Progression Beyond School

### Lifelong Participation

Kirk's work emphasises that progression in PE should ultimately enable lifelong engagement. This requires progression towards:

- **Independent participation skills:** Finding and accessing opportunities
- **Intrinsic motivation:** Self-determined engagement rather than compliance
- **Adaptive competence:** Transferring skills to new contexts and activities
- **Health literacy:** Making informed decisions about physical activity
- **Social capital:** Building relationships through physical activity communities

The Chief Medical Officers' Physical Activity Guidelines (2019) recommend progression from structured PE towards increasingly self-directed activity aligned with personal interests and capabilities.

### Post-16 Pathways

Progression pathways at Key Stage 5 include:

**Academic routes:**
- A-level PE (AQA 7582, Edexcel 9PE0, OCR H555, WJEC A620QS) providing advanced theoretical knowledge
- BTEC Sport qualifications emphasising vocational competence
- Level 3 Cambridge Technical Sport and Physical Activity

**Vocational routes:**
- Coaching qualifications progressing from Level 1 assistant to Level 3 coach
- Officiating pathways in specific sports
- Sports leadership awards (Level 2-3)
- Active IQ fitness instruction qualifications

**Performance routes:**
- Talent pathways in specific sports
- Academy and scholarship programmes
- National governing body performance centres

## Conclusion

Progression in PE skills and knowledge represents far more than linear technical improvement. Drawing on Whitehead's physical literacy framework, Kirk's pedagogical models, and Penney's curriculum development research, effective progression requires holistic development across physical, cognitive, social, and affective domains. The UK National Curriculum provides the statutory framework, whilst exam board specifications codify cognitive progression expectations at Key Stage 4.

For teachers and curriculum leaders, ensuring genuine progression requires careful planning that balances breadth and depth, aligns with developmental readiness, employs varied assessment approaches, and overcomes structural and pedagogical barriers. Ultimately, progression in PE should equip all young people with the competence, confidence, and commitment to maintain physically active lives, recognising that optimal progression pathways vary according to individual circumstances and aspirations.
