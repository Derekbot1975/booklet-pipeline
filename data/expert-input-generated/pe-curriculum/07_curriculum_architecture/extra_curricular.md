# Extra-Curricular Sport and PE

## Introduction and Definitional Framework

Extra-curricular sport and physical education represents the provision of physical activity opportunities beyond the formal curriculum timetable. As defined by the Association for Physical Education (afPE), extra-curricular activities encompass "all organised physical activity that takes place outside the formal PE curriculum, including lunchtime and after-school clubs, competitive fixtures, outdoor education trips, and holiday provision" (afPE, 2015). This distinction is crucial for curriculum architects, as Margaret Whitehead's work on physical literacy emphasises that learning occurs across multiple contexts, with extra-curricular provision offering vital opportunities for developing movement competence, confidence, and motivation beyond the constraints of curriculum time (Whitehead, 2010, 2013).

David Kirk (2010) argues in *Physical Education Futures* that extra-curricular sport has historically been conflated with curriculum PE, particularly in independent schools, creating tension around the purpose and value of each. He contends that whilst curriculum PE should focus on education *through* the physical, extra-curricular provision can legitimately emphasise performance, competition, and specialisation. This architectural separation allows schools to serve different purposes through each strand whilst maintaining coherence across the broader physical education offer.

## Policy Context and National Frameworks

### The PE and Sport Premium

Since 2013, the Primary PE and Sport Premium has provided £320 million annually to improve PE and sport provision in English primary schools. Ofsted's research report *Beyond 2012 – Outstanding Physical Education for All* (2013) established that effective schools use premium funding to enhance extra-curricular provision alongside curriculum improvements. The Department for Education's guidance (DfE, 2019) explicitly identifies "increased participation in extra-curricular activities" as a key impact indicator, requiring schools to demonstrate how additional clubs and competitive opportunities reach previously inactive pupils.

The Premium's five key indicators include offering "a broader experience of a range of sports and activities" and increasing "participation in competitive sport" – both primarily delivered through extra-curricular provision. Dawn Penney's research (2013) on policy enactment demonstrates that schools interpret these requirements variably, with some maintaining traditional team-sport dominance whilst others diversify provision to include lifestyle activities like fitness, dance, and outdoor adventure.

### Youth Sport Trust and School Games

The School Games framework, coordinated by the Youth Sport Trust, provides structural architecture for competitive extra-curricular sport across four levels: intra-school (Level 1), inter-school local (Level 2), county/area (Level 3), and national (Level 4). This pyramid model, developed from the PESSCL strategy (2002-2008) and continued through subsequent School Sport Partnerships, creates clear pathways from grassroots participation to elite performance (Youth Sport Trust, 2020).

However, Len Almond's critique (2014) highlights that competition-focused extra-curricular provision risks excluding less confident participants. He advocates for "inclusive extra-curricular design" that balances competitive fixtures with recreational clubs, emphasising participation over performance for the majority whilst maintaining excellence pathways for talented individuals.

## Theoretical Underpinnings

### Physical Literacy Development

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework positions extra-curricular provision as essential for developing "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2013: 29). Extra-curricular contexts offer extended time for practice, enhanced instructor-participant ratios, and voluntary attendance that signals intrinsic motivation – all crucial for physical literacy development.

Whitehead distinguishes between *acquiring* physical literacy through diverse movement experiences and *applying* it through chosen activities. Extra-curricular provision serves both functions: introductory clubs expose pupils to new activities, building movement vocabularies, whilst sustained participation clubs allow deeper engagement and competence development. This architectural principle should inform extra-curricular programme design across key stages.

### Models-Based Practice

Kirk's advocacy for models-based practice (Kirk, 2013) extends to extra-curricular contexts. Whilst Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), developed by Almond and Bunker (1986), originated for curriculum use, its principles apply to extra-curricular coaching: understanding tactical problems before technical execution, modified games promoting engagement, and learner-centred approaches. Similarly, Sport Education (Siedentop, 1994) – with its emphasis on seasons, teams, and authentic roles – provides pedagogical architecture particularly suited to extra-curricular clubs where extended timeframes enable deeper immersion than curriculum units allow.

The Cooperative Learning model (Casey & Goodyear, 2015) offers structure for inclusive extra-curricular provision, using positive interdependence and group processing to create welcoming environments for less confident participants. Research by Sport England's *Active Lives* survey (2020) demonstrates that 11-16 year-olds cite "feeling judged" and "lack of confidence" as primary barriers to sport participation; pedagogically informed extra-curricular design can address these concerns.

## Curriculum Architecture Considerations

### Strategic Alignment and Breadth

Effective extra-curricular provision architecturally complements rather than simply extends curriculum PE. Susan Capel's work on PE curriculum design (Capel & Blair, 2019) emphasises "constructive alignment" – ensuring extra-curricular opportunities connect with curriculum learning whilst offering additional breadth. This might mean: curriculum units introducing activity categories (invasion games, aesthetic activities, outdoor adventurous activities) whilst extra-curricular clubs enable specialisation; or curriculum focusing on fundamental movement skills with extra-curricular provision applying these in sport-specific contexts.

The National Curriculum for PE (DfE, 2013) requires pupils to "take part in competitive sports and activities" and "lead healthy, active lives" – objectives significantly dependent on extra-curricular provision given limited curriculum time (typically 2 hours weekly at Key Stage 3-4, often less at Key Stage 4). Curriculum architects must therefore view extra-curricular and curriculum provision as interdependent components of a coherent physical education architecture.

### Progression and Differentiation Across Key Stages

#### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Ages 5-11)

Early extra-curricular provision should prioritise breadth, inclusivity, and fundamental movement skill development. Multi-skills clubs serve foundation purposes, developing agility, balance, and coordination (ABCs) identified by Youth Sport Trust's TOP programmes. Competitive opportunities at this stage should emphasise participation and personal bests rather than inter-school fixtures.

The PE and Sport Premium requirement for "engagement of all pupils in regular physical activity" (DfE, 2019) necessitates lunchtime and before-school provision in addition to after-school clubs, recognising that younger pupils may face transport barriers to post-school attendance. Active break-time initiatives, supervised by Sports Leaders or coaches, extend provision accessibility.

#### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Dawn Penney's research on secondary transition (Penney & Evans, 1999) identifies Year 7 as critical for establishing extra-curricular participation habits. Comprehensive provision at Key Stage 3 should include: introductory clubs for curriculum activities, enabling skill consolidation; pathway clubs for sports requiring extended development (gymnastics, swimming, athletics); representative teams for competitive performers; and recreational options (fitness, recreational games) for participation-focused pupils.

The "PE and school sport cliff edge" documented by Women in Sport (2012) shows 41% of girls become disengaged by age 14. Extra-curricular architecture addressing this requires: single-sex options where preferred; non-competitive alternatives; and diverse activities including dance, fitness, and lifestyle sports. Kirk's call for "alternative curriculum possibilities" (Kirk, 2010) applies equally to extra-curricular contexts.

#### Key Stage 4 and 5 (Ages 14-18)

Extra-curricular provision for older pupils serves multiple functions: supporting GCSE/A-level PE coursework and analysis requirements; maintaining physical activity during examination pressures; and developing leadership capacity. Students studying PE with AQA (8582), Edexcel (1PE0), OCR (J587), or WJEC (C650YQ) specifications benefit from extra-curricular participation for practical performance assessment and analytical understanding of tactics, techniques, and training principles.

Leadership programmes like Sports Leaders UK qualifications (Level 1 and 2) integrate with extra-curricular provision, enabling older pupils to deliver primary school festivals or Key Stage 3 clubs. This addresses National Curriculum requirements to "take on leadership roles" whilst building employability skills and social responsibility identified in Whitehead's physical literacy domains.

### Inclusion and Equity

Len Almond's work on inclusion (Almond & Myers, 2017) establishes that equal opportunity requires more than open access – it demands proactive architectural design removing participation barriers. This includes:

- **Transport provision**: After-school clubs disadvantage pupils dependent on school transport; before-school and lunchtime provision improves equity
- **Financial accessibility**: Subscription-free clubs, loan equipment schemes, and subsidised trips prevent economic exclusion
- **Disability inclusion**: Parallel or integrated provision for SEND pupils, with adapted equipment and specialist coaching where beneficial
- **Cultural sensitivity**: Single-sex options, alternative activities, and modified kit requirements respecting diverse backgrounds
- **Confidence-building pathways**: Introductory sessions, peer mentoring, and visible role models encouraging previously inactive participants

Sport England's *Active Lives* data (2021) demonstrates persistent inequalities: 48% of children from affluent families meet activity guidelines versus 36% from less affluent backgrounds; 47% of boys versus 40% of girls are active; and ethnicity, disability, and family circumstances create further disparities. Extra-curricular architecture must explicitly address these equity challenges.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Coaching vs Teaching Paradigms

Kirk and others distinguish between pedagogical approaches appropriate for education (curriculum PE) versus performance development (competitive sport). However, extra-curricular contexts benefit from hybrid approaches. Even competitive teams require educational pedagogy: TGfU principles develop tactical understanding; questioning enhances decision-making; and modified games promote engagement for substitutes and developing players.

Conversely, recreational extra-curricular clubs benefit from sport coaching's emphasis on practice design, progressions, and skill acquisition. Effective extra-curricular provision architecture recognises this pedagogical complexity, requiring staff with both teaching qualifications and coaching expertise, or partnerships between PE departments and qualified external coaches who understand educational contexts.

### Developing Physical Literacy Through Extra-Curricular Contexts

Whitehead's physical literacy framework (2013) identifies four interrelated capacities: physical competence, confidence, motivation, and knowledge/understanding. Extra-curricular provision offers distinctive advantages for each:

**Physical competence** develops through extended practice time, lower participant-instructor ratios enabling individualised feedback, and voluntary attendance suggesting readiness to learn. Clubs offering progressive skill development rather than just organised play maximise competence gains.

**Confidence** emerges from mastery experiences in supportive environments. Psychological safety – freedom to attempt skills without judgment – requires explicit creation through group norms, positive coaching language, and appropriate challenge levels. Fixed membership clubs (rather than drop-in sessions) build social confidence through peer familiarity.

**Motivation** is inherent in voluntary extra-curricular participation but requires nurturing. Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000) suggests autonomy, competence, and relatedness sustain motivation. Extra-curricular architecture supporting these includes: pupil voice in activity selection (autonomy); appropriate challenge and clear progression (competence); and team/club identity development (relatedness).

**Knowledge and understanding** develop through tactical discussions, performance analysis, leadership roles, and officiating opportunities more feasible in extra-curricular than curriculum contexts given extended time and engaged participants.

## Organisational Models and Delivery Structures

### Staffing Configurations

Effective extra-curricular provision requires sustainable staffing models. Options include:

1. **PE Department Delivery**: Teachers deliver extra-curricular clubs, often through directed time (1265 hours annually for teachers in England) though voluntary contribution remains common. This ensures pedagogical quality and curriculum alignment but risks teacher workload and burnout concerns identified by the National Education Union (2019).

2. **External Coach Provision**: School Sport Partnerships, National Governing Body coaches, or commercial providers deliver clubs. This broadens expertise and reduces teacher workload but requires quality assurance, safeguarding verification, and integration with curriculum aims.

3. **Hybrid Models**: PE staff coordinate programmes with shared delivery. This combines pedagogical expertise with specialist coaching, though requires careful coordination.

4. **Leadership Development Models**: Older pupils or trained volunteers deliver supervised sessions. This develops leadership capacity whilst extending provision, though requires significant preparation and oversight.

afPE's *Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity* (2020) emphasises that all extra-curricular providers require appropriate qualifications, DBS clearance, and supervision arrangements proportionate to activity risk and participant age.

### Timetabling and Scheduling

Strategic timetabling maximises participation:

- **Varied timing**: Before-school, lunchtime, and after-school options accommodate different circumstances
- **Consistent scheduling**: Regular weekly sessions enable habit formation
- **Block programming**: Intensive periods (e.g., six-week blocks) allow rotation of activities, breadth of exposure, and manageable commitments for participants and staff
- **Seasonal variation**: Outdoor summer activities, indoor winter alternatives, and cross-curricular residential experiences create diverse annual offers

### Competition Structures

Competitive extra-curricular provision should architect multiple pathways. The School Games framework provides structure, but Kirk (2010) cautions against exclusive focus on inter-school competition. Effective architecture includes:

- **Intra-school competition** (house systems, ladder tournaments) enabling inclusive participation
- **Developmental fixtures** against similar-standard opposition, prioritising participation over results
- **Performance pathways** for talented athletes, including district/county representation
- **Festival formats** celebrating participation and skill demonstration without winners/losers
- **Alternative competitions** (personal best challenges, adventure activities, fitness goals) for non-traditional competitors

## Links to Examination Specifications

### GCSE Physical Education

All GCSE PE specifications include practical performance assessment, typically 30-40% of final grades. Extra-curricular participation directly supports this:

- **AQA (8582)**: Students assessed in three activities from different categories (team sports, individual sports, activities). Regular extra-curricular participation develops competence in chosen activities.
- **Edexcel (1PE0)**: Students select from approved activity list with moderated performance. Extra-curricular clubs provide coaching and competitive experience enhancing performance standard.
- **OCR (J587)**: Practical performance worth 30%, assessed through skills in isolation, competitive situations, and effectiveness. Extra-curricular competitive fixtures provide authentic assessment contexts.
- **WJEC Eduqas (C650YQ)**: 40% practical assessment in three activities. Extra-curricular participation provides practice, coaching, and competitive experience underpinning assessment performance.

Additionally, examination specifications require analysis of performance (AoP) based on personal activity involvement. Regular extra-curricular participation provides experiences for reflection, tactical analysis, and evaluation required in written examinations.

### A-Level Physical Education

A-level specifications similarly include practical performance, though reduced to 15-30% of qualification. More significantly, theoretical content on skill acquisition, sports psychology, and exercise physiology becomes meaningful through personal participation experience. Extra-curricular involvement provides concrete examples for abstract concepts: skill classification, practice structures, motivational theories, and training principles.

Leadership and officiating opportunities within extra-curricular provision can contribute to A-level coursework requirements in some specifications, whilst competitive participation at higher standards provides experiences for personal performance evaluations.

## Impact and Evidence

### Research Evidence on Extra-Curricular Participation

Systematic research demonstrates multiple benefits of extra-curricular sport participation:

**Academic attainment**: Bailey et al.'s (2009) review for DCSF found positive correlations between physical activity/sport participation and academic performance, with extra-curricular sport showing stronger associations than curriculum PE alone.

**Social development**: Studies compiled by the Institute of Youth Sport (2013) identify extra-curricular sport contexts as significant for developing leadership, teamwork, and social capital, particularly when structured around Sport Education or cooperative learning models.

**Physical health**: Sport England's *Active Lives* survey (2021) shows that pupils participating in extra-curricular sport are significantly more likely to meet Chief Medical Officer activity guidelines (60 minutes daily moderate-to-vigorous physical activity).

**Sustained participation**: Kirk's longitudinal research (Kirk & MacPhail, 2003) demonstrates that positive extra-curricular sport experiences during schooling predict adult physical activity levels more strongly than curriculum PE experiences alone.

### Monitoring and Evaluation

Effective extra-curricular provision architecture includes systematic evaluation mechanisms:

- **Participation data**: Tracking attendance by demographic factors (gender, ethnicity, SEND, disadvantage) identifies equity gaps
- **Breadth analysis**: Monitoring individual pupils' participation across activity categories ensures diverse movement experience
- **Progression tracking**: Recording skill development, competitive achievements, and leadership roles demonstrates impact
- **Pupil voice**: Regular consultation on preferences, barriers, and experiences informs programme adaptation
- **Outcome measures**: Assessing impacts on physical literacy, curriculum PE performance, examination results, and sustained participation validates provision

afPE's Quality Mark framework provides standards against which schools can evaluate extra-curricular provision quality, whilst the School Games Mark recognises competitive sport provision specifically.
