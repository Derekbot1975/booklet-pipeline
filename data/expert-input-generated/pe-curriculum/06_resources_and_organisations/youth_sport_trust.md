# Youth Sport Trust

## Overview and Historical Context

The Youth Sport Trust (YST) is a leading UK charity established in 1994 with a mission to build a brighter future for young people through the power of sport and physical education. Founded by Sir John Beckwith, the organisation has become instrumental in shaping PE policy and practice across the UK, working in partnership with schools, multi-academy trusts, and national sporting bodies to ensure high-quality physical education and school sport provision.

The Trust's formation coincided with growing concerns about children's physical activity levels and the quality of PE provision in schools. The organisation has been particularly influential in developing frameworks that align with Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy, emphasising the development of motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding that enables young people to maintain purposeful physical activity throughout their lives.

## Core Programmes and Resources

### National School Sport Week

The Youth Sport Trust coordinates National School Sport Week, an annual celebration typically held in June that encourages schools to showcase their PE and sport provision. This initiative provides ready-made resources, lesson plans, and activity ideas across all key stages, supporting teachers in delivering engaging sessions that promote participation and highlight the breadth of sporting opportunities available to young people.

### Primary Premium Support

Following the introduction of the PE and Sport Premium in 2013 (£150 million annually, increased to £320 million), the Youth Sport Trust developed extensive guidance materials to help primary schools maximise the impact of this funding. Their framework aligns directly with the Department for Education's key indicators:

1. Engagement of all pupils in regular physical activity
2. Profile of PE and sport being raised across the school
3. Increased confidence, knowledge and skills of staff teaching PE
4. Broader experience of a range of sports and activities
5. Increased participation in competitive sport

The Trust's impact measurement tools enable schools to demonstrate accountability and evidence-based improvements, addressing concerns raised by education researchers including David Kirk regarding the quality and sustainability of PE provision.

### Change Starters

This flagship programme develops young leadership and advocacy skills through sport and physical activity. Aligned with the National Curriculum requirements for developing personal and social skills, Change Starters empowers young people aged 7-18 to identify and address issues that matter to them, using sport as a vehicle for positive change. The programme embodies Dawn Penney's research on student voice and agency in PE curriculum development.

## Alignment with UK National Curriculum

### Key Stage 1 and 2 Provisions

The Youth Sport Trust's resources directly support the statutory requirements outlined in the National Curriculum for Physical Education (2013). At Key Stage 1, where pupils should "develop fundamental movement skills" and "become increasingly competent and confident," the Trust provides progressive skill development frameworks and assessment tools.

At Key Stage 2, their resources support the statutory requirement for all pupils to participate in swimming and water safety education, providing guidance on achieving the minimum expectation of swimming 25 metres competently using various strokes.

### Key Stage 3 and 4 Frameworks

For secondary phases, the Youth Sport Trust offers resources supporting the National Curriculum aim for pupils to "analyse their performances compared to previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve their personal best." Their differentiation strategies and inclusive practice guides help teachers implement Len Almond's principles of pedagogical models that prioritise understanding and application over mere technical competence.

The Trust's materials also support GCSE PE specifications across all major exam boards (AQA 8582, Edexcel 1PE0, OCR J587, and WJEC), particularly in the practical performance components and analysis of performance requirements.

## Impact on Teacher Development

### Continuing Professional Development

The Youth Sport Trust operates as a major provider of CPD for PE teachers and generalist primary teachers. Their training programmes address the persistent challenge identified by Susan Capel and colleagues regarding non-specialist teachers delivering PE in primary schools. Specific CPD strands include:

- **Primary PE Specialist Training**: Multi-day programmes developing subject knowledge and pedagogical approaches
- **Inclusion in PE**: Strategies for adapting activities for SEND pupils, aligned with the Equality Act 2010 requirements
- **Assessment Without Levels**: Following the 2014 removal of National Curriculum levels, providing frameworks for formative and summative assessment

### Teacher Networks and Communities of Practice

The Trust facilitates regional networks enabling teachers to share best practice and develop collaborative approaches to curriculum design. This model reflects Kirk's advocacy for professional learning communities that challenge traditional, sport-technique-focused PE in favour of more educationally purposeful approaches.

## Inclusion and Equality Initiatives

### Paralympic Legacy Programmes

Building on London 2012's legacy, the Youth Sport Trust developed extensive resources promoting disability sport and inclusive PE practice. Their "Inclusive PE Framework" provides practical strategies for adapting activities, ensuring compliance with the Equality Act 2010 and supporting schools in meeting their Public Sector Equality Duty.

The Trust's work in this area exemplifies Whitehead's assertion that physical literacy must be achievable for all individuals, regardless of physical or cognitive ability. Resources include differentiated activity cards, equipment recommendations, and guidance on classification systems used in Paralympic sports.

### Girls Active Programme

Recognising persistent gender disparities in physical activity participation (with girls significantly less active than boys by Key Stage 4), the Youth Sport Trust launched the Girls Active programme. This evidence-based initiative addresses barriers including body confidence, menstruation concerns, and cultural factors. The programme provides:

- Whole-school approach frameworks involving senior leadership
- Peer leadership opportunities specifically for girls
- Curriculum audit tools identifying gender-biased provision
- Community engagement strategies involving parents and families

Research evaluation demonstrates significant improvements in girls' engagement, attendance and attitudes towards PE in participating schools, supporting Penney's research on gender equity in PE curriculum implementation.

## School Games Framework

The Youth Sport Trust manages delivery of the School Games, a government-funded competition framework providing competitive opportunities from intra-school through to national level. The framework comprises:

- **Level 1**: Intra-school competition
- **Level 2**: Inter-school competition (local area)
- **Level 3**: County/area finals
- **Level 4**: National finals

This tiered structure enables schools to meet the National Curriculum requirement for competitive sport whilst ensuring inclusive participation opportunities. School Games Organisers (SGOs), typically funded through partnerships between local authorities, schools, and sports organisations, coordinate local delivery.

Critics, including Kirk, have questioned whether competition-focused models genuinely serve educational purposes or simply reproduce elite sport structures. The Youth Sport Trust has responded by emphasising inclusive virtual competitions, personal challenge formats, and non-traditional activities alongside conventional sports.

## Physical Literacy Framework

The Youth Sport Trust has been instrumental in promoting physical literacy concepts within UK education. Their framework, directly informed by Whitehead's International Physical Literacy Association definition, provides assessment tools and progression frameworks that help teachers develop:

- **Physical competence**: Fundamental movement skills and sport-specific techniques
- **Confidence and motivation**: Self-efficacy and intrinsic motivation for physical activity
- **Knowledge and understanding**: Tactical awareness, health benefits, and safety considerations
- **Engagement in physical activity for life**: Establishing lifelong active habits

This holistic approach challenges traditional PE curricula that prioritise performance and technique, advocating instead for Almond's educational gymnastics and games concepts that emphasise problem-solving and creative application of skills.

## Digital Resources and Innovation

### Online Platform

The Youth Sport Trust maintains an extensive digital resource library accessible to member schools, including:

- Video demonstration libraries with high-quality footage
- Downloadable lesson plans aligned with National Curriculum objectives
- Assessment and tracking tools
- CPD webinars and online courses
- Impact measurement frameworks

### Virtual School Games

Accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, the Trust developed virtual competition formats enabling participation from home or school. These innovations have persisted post-pandemic, providing additional engagement opportunities and supporting pupils unable to participate in traditional competitive formats due to disability, illness, or anxiety.

## Partnership Approach

The Youth Sport Trust operates through strategic partnerships with:

- **National Governing Bodies of Sport**: Developing sport-specific resources and competition pathways
- **Multi-Academy Trusts**: Providing strategic PE and sport leadership across trust schools
- **Local Authorities**: Supporting School Games delivery and teacher networks
- **Active Partnerships**: Aligning school provision with community sport opportunities
- **UK Sport and Sport England**: Connecting elite sport talent pathways with school provision

This ecosystem approach reflects research by Penney and others emphasising that sustainable PE and school sport systems require cross-sector collaboration and alignment of policy, funding, and practice.

## Research and Evidence Base

The Youth Sport Trust commissions and publishes research examining PE and school sport effectiveness. Their annual "PE Pulse" survey provides data on provision, funding, and perceived impact across UK schools. Key findings consistently highlight:

- Variations in curriculum time allocated to PE (typically 90-120 minutes weekly at primary, 120+ minutes at secondary)
- Workforce challenges, particularly primary teachers' confidence in delivering PE
- Impact of funding, particularly PE and Sport Premium, on provision breadth and quality
- Persistent inequalities in participation related to gender, socioeconomic status, and disability

This evidence base informs the Trust's programme development and advocacy work, contributing to policy debates about statutory PE time, workforce development, and sustainable funding models.

## Critical Perspectives and Limitations

Whilst the Youth Sport Trust has undoubtedly influenced UK PE provision positively, academic researchers including Kirk and Penney have raised important considerations:

- **Competition emphasis**: Questions whether School Games focus reflects educational values or elite sport priorities
- **Sustainability concerns**: Reliance on time-limited government funding (particularly PE Premium) creates uncertainty
- **Professional autonomy**: Potential tension between prescribed programmes and teacher professional judgment
- **Measurement challenges**: Difficulty evidencing long-term health and wellbeing outcomes

These critiques encourage reflective practice among teachers using Youth Sport Trust resources, ensuring programmes serve genuinely educational purposes aligned with pupils' holistic development rather than reproducing potentially exclusionary sporting cultures.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Accessing Resources

Teachers can access Youth Sport Trust materials through membership schemes, with different tiers for individual teachers, schools, and multi-academy trusts. Many resources are freely available, particularly those linked to government-funded initiatives like the PE and Sport Premium.

### Implementing Programmes

Effective implementation requires:

1. **Senior leadership engagement**: Securing strategic support and resource allocation
2. **Needs analysis**: Auditing current provision against Trust frameworks to identify priorities
3. **Staff development**: Ensuring teachers have confidence and competence to deliver programmes effectively
4. **Pupil voice**: Involving young people in programme design, reflecting Change Starters principles
5. **Impact measurement**: Using Trust evaluation tools to demonstrate improvements and inform ongoing development

### Curriculum Integration

Youth Sport Trust resources complement rather than replace teacher-designed curricula. Effective practice involves selecting specific programmes addressing identified needs whilst maintaining curriculum coherence and progression. Teachers should critically evaluate resources against Capel's criteria for quality PE: educational purpose, inclusive practice, and contribution to lifelong physical activity participation.

## Conclusion

The Youth Sport Trust represents a significant force in UK PE and school sport provision, providing resources, training, and frameworks that support teachers in delivering high-quality, inclusive physical education. Their work exemplifies the application of physical literacy principles advocated by Whitehead, whilst addressing practical challenges of implementation identified by researchers including Kirk, Penney, Almond, and Capel. For teachers and curriculum leaders, engaging critically and selectively with Youth Sport Trust programmes offers valuable support in developing PE provision that genuinely serves all young people's educational and developmental needs.
