# Association for Physical Education (afPE)

## Overview and Organisational Purpose

The Association for Physical Education (afPE) is the only UK-wide professional association for all those involved in the delivery of physical education (PE) and school sport. Established in 2006 following the merger of the British Association of Advisers and Lecturers in Physical Education (BAALPE) and the Physical Education Association of the United Kingdom (PEA UK), afPE serves as the authoritative voice for PE practitioners across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

afPE's primary mission is to promote and maintain high standards and safe practice in all aspects of physical education, school sport, and physical activity (PESSPA). The organisation operates as a membership body with over 7,000 individual and organisational members, including teachers, teaching assistants, sports coaches, local authority advisers, higher education lecturers, and school sport coordinators.

## Core Functions and Services

### Professional Standards and Guidance

afPE produces the definitive guidance document *Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity* (commonly known as *Safe Practice*), currently in its 2020 edition. This publication is recognised by the Health and Safety Executive as the standard reference for safe practice in PE and is cited in numerous legal cases involving accidents in educational settings. The guidance covers all aspects of risk management, from curriculum activities to extra-curricular provision, and is regularly updated to reflect changes in legislation, equipment standards, and pedagogical approaches.

The organisation's *Quality Mark* accreditation system enables schools to benchmark their PE provision against national standards. This framework aligns with Ofsted's inspection criteria and provides evidence for school self-evaluation processes, particularly relevant given Ofsted's increased focus on curriculum quality since the 2019 Education Inspection Framework.

### Professional Learning and Development

afPE delivers extensive professional development opportunities through its annual National Conference, regional events, and webinar programmes. These events frequently feature contributions from leading PE academics including Professor Margaret Whitehead (developer of the physical literacy concept), Professor David Kirk (whose work on quality PE has influenced policy internationally), and Professor Dawn Penney (renowned for her research on curriculum and assessment in PE).

The organisation's professional learning portfolio includes specialist courses on:
- Teaching the National Curriculum for PE (2013) across all key stages
- Assessment without levels following the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014
- Implementing physical literacy principles within secondary school contexts
- Supporting pupils with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND) in PE
- Developing high-quality primary PE provision, particularly relevant given the Primary PE and Sport Premium funding requirements

### Policy Advocacy and Representation

afPE actively engages with government departments, including the Department for Education (DfE) and the Department for Culture, Media and Sport (DCMS), to shape PE policy. The organisation contributed significantly to the development of the National Curriculum for PE (2013) and continues to provide expert advice on curriculum matters. afPE maintains close working relationships with Youth Sport Trust, Sport England, and UK Sport to ensure coherence across the school sport landscape.

## Key Publications and Resources

### Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity (2020)

This comprehensive resource remains the cornerstone of afPE's output. The 2020 edition addresses contemporary issues including:
- COVID-19 related safety considerations and adaptations
- Updated guidance on trampolining, gymnastics, and swimming in line with current governing body specifications
- Risk management in adventure activities and outdoor education
- Safeguarding protocols aligned with *Keeping Children Safe in Education* (2023)
- Legal responsibilities under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and associated regulations

The publication explicitly references the work of researchers like Professor Susan Capel, whose extensive research on beginning teachers and professional learning in PE informs the guidance on professional development and safe practice.

### Health Position Papers

afPE has published several authoritative position statements addressing critical issues in contemporary PE practice:

**Physical Literacy Statement**: Drawing extensively on Margaret Whitehead's conceptual framework, this document clarifies how physical literacy should be interpreted and implemented in UK educational contexts. It challenges narrow, fitness-focused interpretations and emphasises the holistic development of motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge, and understanding. This aligns with the National Curriculum's aim to ensure all pupils develop competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities.

**Health-Related Learning Position Paper**: This guidance addresses the teaching of health and fitness within PE, reflecting concerns raised by David Kirk and others about the dominance of health imperatives potentially narrowing curriculum experiences. The paper advocates for a balanced approach integrating health knowledge across physical activities rather than treating it as a discrete topic.

### Assessment Guidance

Following the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014, afPE produced extensive guidance supporting schools in developing fit-for-purpose assessment systems in PE. This work acknowledges Dawn Penney's research on assessment in PE, which demonstrates how assessment practices profoundly shape curriculum enactment and pupil learning experiences.

The assessment resources address:
- Formative assessment strategies appropriate for practical learning contexts
- Meaningful reporting to parents without levels
- Supporting Progress 8 measures at Key Stage 4
- Assessment approaches that align with GCSE PE specifications from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas

## Curriculum Support Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Primary)

afPE provides extensive resources supporting primary PE delivery, particularly crucial given that many primary teachers lack specialist PE qualifications. Resources address the National Curriculum programmes of study requiring pupils to develop fundamental movement skills, apply them in range of activities, and engage in competitive sport and activities.

Since the introduction of the Primary PE and Sport Premium in 2013 (£320 million annually across England's primary schools), afPE has been instrumental in providing guidance on effective use of this funding. The organisation's *Primary PE and Sport Premium Toolkit* helps schools demonstrate sustainable impact and value for money, addressing five key indicators:
1. Engagement of all pupils in regular physical activity
2. Profile of PE and sport being raised across the school
3. Increased confidence, knowledge and skills of staff teaching PE
4. Broader experience of activities offered to pupils
5. Increased participation in competitive sport

The resources reflect Len Almond's pioneering work on fundamental movement skills and his contribution to the development of the Youth Sport Trust's TOP programmes, which many primary schools use to structure their PE curriculum.

### Key Stage 3

At Key Stage 3, afPE resources support the National Curriculum requirement that pupils develop technique and improve performance in a broad range of activities including invasion games, net/wall games, striking/fielding games, gymnastics, dance, athletics, and outdoor and adventurous activities (OAA).

The organisation provides guidance on:
- Curriculum sequencing and progression models that build on primary experiences
- Differentiation strategies ensuring access for all learners
- Developing pupils' tactical understanding and decision-making capabilities
- Embedding evaluation and improvement skills across activities
- Addressing the participation decline often observed in early secondary years, particularly among girls

Resources explicitly reference David Kirk's research on pedagogical models in PE, including Sport Education, Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), and Cooperative Learning approaches. Kirk's work on the "hidden curriculum" in PE has influenced afPE's guidance on creating inclusive learning environments where all pupils can experience success.

### Key Stage 4 and 5

afPE provides support for both core PE and examination PE at Key Stages 4 and 5. For core PE, resources address the challenge of maintaining engagement when PE is non-examined, promoting activity choices that develop lifelong participation habits.

For GCSE PE (typically taught across Years 10-11), afPE resources support teachers delivering specifications from:
- **AQA GCSE PE (8582)**: Including practical performance, analysis and evaluation of performance (AEP), and written papers covering applied anatomy and physiology, movement analysis, physical training, use of data, sport psychology, socio-cultural influences, and health, fitness and well-being
- **Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)**: Covering fitness and body systems, health and performance, and practical performance
- **OCR GCSE PE (J587)**: Addressing physical factors affecting performance, socio-cultural issues and sports psychology, and practical performance
- **WJEC Eduqas GCSE PE**: Including the role of physical activity and sport in society, applied anatomy and physiology, and practical performance

At Key Stage 5, afPE supports teachers delivering A-level PE specifications across the same examination boards, addressing more advanced content in exercise physiology, biomechanics, skill acquisition, sport psychology, and sport and society topics.

Susan Capel's research on subject knowledge development in PE teachers is particularly relevant here, as examination PE requires teachers to possess and teach substantial theoretical content alongside practical coaching expertise.

## Supporting Inclusive Practice

afPE's commitment to inclusive PE is evident across its publications and professional learning offerings. The organisation's resources address:

### SEND Provision

Guidance on meeting requirements of the Children and Families Act 2014 and the SEND Code of Practice (2015) within PE contexts. Resources provide practical strategies for adapting activities, using appropriate equipment, and employing inclusive pedagogies that enable pupils with physical, sensory, cognitive, and social-emotional-mental health needs to access high-quality PE.

The *Including All Learners* publication series provides activity-specific guidance addressing conditions including autism spectrum disorder, physical disabilities, visual impairment, and hearing impairment. This work draws on the legacy of inclusion advocates within PE, recognising that PE environments can be particularly challenging for pupils with SEND but also offer unique opportunities for development and achievement.

### Gender and Participation

Resources address the persistent gender gap in PE participation and attainment, particularly evident in secondary schools. Drawing on extensive research showing girls' declining participation post-primary transition, afPE guidance suggests:
- Offering gender-neutral activity choices
- Challenging stereotypical activity associations
- Addressing body image concerns sensitively
- Providing female role models and mentors
- Creating psychologically safe learning environments
- Considering single-sex teaching for specific activities where evidence supports improved outcomes

### Cultural and Religious Considerations

Guidance addresses requirements under the Equality Act 2010, providing practical advice on accommodating religious observances (including Ramadan), culturally appropriate clothing, and addressing language barriers for EAL pupils. Resources emphasise proactive communication with families and communities to remove barriers to participation.

## Research and Evidence Engagement

afPE actively promotes evidence-informed practice in PE, bridging the research-practice gap that often characterises educational practice. The organisation's journal *Physical Education Matters* publishes accessible summaries of current research alongside practitioner case studies, creating dialogue between academic inquiry and classroom reality.

### Physical Literacy Debate

afPE has been central to debates around physical literacy implementation in UK schools. While embracing Margaret Whitehead's holistic, phenomenologically-rooted concept emphasising embodied capability and disposition toward physical activity, the organisation has cautioned against reductive interpretations that treat physical literacy as merely a synonym for physical competence or a checklist of motor skills.

The organisation's position recognises tensions highlighted by researchers like David Kirk, who has questioned whether physical literacy can be meaningfully assessed in educational contexts or whether attempts to measure it inevitably distort Whitehead's original philosophical conception. afPE's guidance suggests teachers focus on creating conditions fostering physical literacy development rather than claiming to directly assess or measure it.

### Pedagogical Models

Following Dawn Penney's research on curriculum models and David Kirk's extensive work on pedagogical models in PE (including his seminal 2013 paper "Educational Value and Models-Based Practice in Physical Education"), afPE promotes informed selection of teaching approaches matched to learning intentions.

Resources explain and provide practical implementation guidance for:
- **Sport Education**: Extended units organised as seasons with team affiliation, competitions, and authentic roles
- **Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)**: Game-centred approach emphasising tactical awareness before technique refinement
- **Cooperative Learning**: Structured peer interaction promoting positive interdependence and individual accountability
- **Inquiry-Based Learning**: Pupil-led exploration and problem-solving within movement contexts

This pedagogical pluralism reflects research evidence that no single teaching approach serves all learning intentions, activities, or learners equally well.

## Professional Recognition and Career Development

afPE offers professional recognition through its **Chartered Teacher (Physical Education)** status, achieved through portfolio submission demonstrating sustained excellence and professional contribution. This status aligns with the Society of Education and Training's professional standards and provides formal recognition of expertise beyond qualified teacher status (QTS).

The organisation's career development resources address:
- Transitioning from initial teacher education (recognising Susan Capel's extensive research on beginning PE teachers)
- Developing subject leadership capacity at Key Stage 1/2 and Key Stage 3/4
- Subject leadership in primary schools without specialist qualifications
- Moving into advisory, higher education, or national-level roles

## Response to Contemporary Challenges

### COVID-19 Pandemic Impact

afPE provided critical guidance throughout the COVID-19 pandemic, producing regularly updated safety protocols enabling schools to maintain PE provision while managing infection risks. Resources addressed ventilation, equipment hygiene, activity selection, and modifications enabling physical distancing where required. The organisation's rapid response demonstrated its central role in UK PE infrastructure.

Post-pandemic, afPE has supported schools addressing pupils' reduced fitness levels, diminished motor competence from extended school closures, and increased anxiety around physical activity participation documented in post-lockdown research.

### Mental Health and Wellbeing

Recognising PE's potential contribution to whole-school mental health strategies (referenced in the DfE's *Promoting Children and Young People's Mental Health and Wellbeing*, 2021), afPE resources explore:
- Creating psychologically safe PE environments
- Using physical activity therapeutically without medicalising PE
- Supporting pupils experiencing anxiety, depression, or stress
- Training staff to recognise mental health concerns manifesting in PE contexts
- Signposting appropriate support services

### Social Justice and Anti-Racism

Following the Black Lives Matter movement's increased prominence in 2020, afPE committed to addressing racial inequality in PE and school sport. Resources examine:
- Representation in curriculum content (role models, activity origins)
- Addressing unconscious bias in teaching and assessment
- Creating culturally responsive PE curricula
- Challenging stereotyping in activity selection and grouping
- Supporting Black and minority ethnic teachers in PE careers

## Partnerships and Collaborative Working

afPE maintains strategic partnerships with numerous organisations across the physical education and school sport landscape:

- **Youth Sport Trust**: Collaboration on primary PE development and school sport partnerships
- **Sport England**: Alignment with the *Uniting the Movement* strategy (2021-2031) emphasising reducing inequalities
- **National Governing Bodies (NGBs)**: Ensuring curriculum resources align with sport-specific good practice
- **English Schools' Football Association, English Schools' Athletic Association**, and other school sport organisations
- **Teaching Schools Councils** and **Subject Associations Network**: Supporting curriculum development and teacher education

These partnerships ensure afPE's guidance reflects diverse perspectives and current developments across the sector.

## Implications for School Leaders and Teachers

### Curriculum Leaders

Subject leaders should utilise afPE membership and resources to:
- Ensure departmental practice aligns with current safe practice standards
- Access professional development for department members, particularly non-specialist teachers
- Benchmark provision against national standards through Quality Mark processes
- Inform curriculum planning with current research and effective practice examples
- Develop assessment systems reflecting removal of National Curriculum levels
- Evidence PE's contribution to whole-school priorities including SMSC development, British Values, and literacy/numeracy strategies

### Classroom Teachers

Individual teachers benefit from afPE membership through:
- Accessing practical teaching resources reducing planning burden
- Maintaining currency with safety requirements protecting both pupils and professional standing
- Professional learning opportunities supporting career progression
- Networking with peers facing similar challenges
- Building evidence portfolios for performance management and career advancement
- Accessing expertise supporting examination PE teaching where theoretical subject knowledge may require development

### Initial Teacher Education

Universities delivering PGCE and undergraduate PE teacher education programmes should ensure trainees engage with afPE resources, particularly *Safe Practice*, as part of meeting the Teachers' Standards (2011). Familiarity with afPE's evidence base supports development of critical, research-engaged practitioners rather than technicians simply replicating existing practice.

## Critical Perspectives

While afPE serves as PE's professional voice, critical engagement with its positions remains important. Some researchers, including David Kirk, have questioned whether professional associations sometimes reinforce conservative, activity-focused conceptions of PE rather than promoting transformative practice addressing contemporary challenges like sedentary lifestyles and health inequalities.

Dawn Penney's work reminds us that curriculum guidance always reflects particular values and interests; teachers should engage critically with all resources, including those from authoritative bodies, considering whose interests are served and what alternative approaches might exist.

The Association for Physical Education represents an essential resource for anyone involved in delivering physical education in UK schools. Its authoritative guidance, professional learning opportunities, policy advocacy, and evidence-informed resources support teachers in providing high-quality, safe, and inclusive PE experiences for all pupils.
