# Ofsted and PE

## Introduction

Ofsted (Office for Standards in Education, Children's Services and Skills) is the non-ministerial government department responsible for inspecting and regulating services that care for children and young people in England. For Physical Education, Ofsted inspection plays a crucial role in evaluating the quality of provision, curriculum intent, implementation, and impact. The current inspection framework (introduced September 2019) has significantly influenced how PE is taught, assessed, and valued within schools.

## The Ofsted Education Inspection Framework (EIF)

### The Three-Pillar Approach

The current Education Inspection Framework operates on three core judgements that directly affect PE provision:

**Intent**: What is the school trying to achieve through its PE curriculum? This includes:
- The vision and rationale for PE within the broader curriculum
- How the curriculum is sequenced from EYFS through to Key Stage 4 (and Key Stage 5 where applicable)
- How the curriculum addresses the National Curriculum requirements for PE
- Links to the whole-school curriculum and British values

**Implementation**: How is the PE curriculum being delivered? This encompasses:
- The quality of teaching and pedagogy
- Teacher subject knowledge and expertise
- Assessment practices and their effectiveness
- Resources, facilities, and time allocation
- Extra-curricular provision and enrichment

**Impact**: What are pupils achieving through PE? This includes:
- Pupils' knowledge, skills, and competencies in physical activities
- Progress across year groups and key stages
- Participation rates and engagement
- Pupils' physical literacy development (drawing on Margaret Whitehead's conceptualisation)
- Contribution to pupils' health, wellbeing, and character development

## Ofsted's Subject-Specific Guidance for PE

### The PE Research Review (2022)

In May 2022, Ofsted published its **'Research review series: PE'**, which represents the most comprehensive examination of PE curriculum and pedagogy in an inspection context. This document draws on international research and provides clear expectations for high-quality PE provision.

#### Key Findings from the Research Review

The review identifies that high-quality PE curricula should develop:

1. **Physical competence**: The ability to perform motor skills with efficiency, control, and coordination
2. **Tactical and strategic knowledge**: Understanding of how and when to apply skills in game contexts
3. **Fitness knowledge**: Understanding of health, fitness principles, and how to maintain an active lifestyle
4. **Evaluative and reflective capabilities**: Ability to analyse and improve performance

The research review emphasises that effective PE teaching requires careful curriculum sequencing that builds on prior learning. This aligns with David Kirk's work on curriculum models in PE, particularly his critique of traditional multi-activity models that lack progression and depth.

### Curriculum Sequencing and Progression

Ofsted's research review draws attention to the importance of **hierarchical knowledge** in PE. Inspectors now look for evidence that curricula are built on clear progression principles:

- **Foundation skills** taught systematically in Key Stage 1 (e.g., running, jumping, throwing, catching)
- **Skill refinement and application** in Key Stage 2 with increasing complexity
- **Advanced tactical understanding** and specialisation in Key Stage 3
- **Personal development and lifelong participation** emphasis in Key Stage 4

Dawn Penney's research on curriculum construction and power relations in PE is relevant here, as Ofsted's framework requires schools to justify their curriculum choices and demonstrate how they meet diverse pupil needs.

## Deep Dive Methodology in PE Inspections

### What is a Deep Dive?

Since 2019, Ofsted inspections include 'deep dives' into selected subjects, including PE. A PE deep dive typically involves:

1. **Discussion with curriculum leaders**: Exploring curriculum intent, rationale, and design
2. **Lesson observations**: Watching teaching across different year groups and key stages
3. **Work scrutiny**: Examining pupils' work, though this differs in PE from traditional written subjects
4. **Pupil discussions**: Talking to pupils about what they have learned and can remember
5. **Teacher discussions**: Speaking with PE staff about planning, assessment, and professional development

### Specific Questions Inspectors May Ask

During PE deep dives, inspectors typically explore:

**Curriculum Intent Questions:**
- How have you designed your PE curriculum to build knowledge and skills progressively?
- How does your curriculum address the needs of all pupils, including SEND and disadvantaged learners?
- What is your rationale for the activities chosen at each key stage?
- How does PE contribute to personal development, including character and resilience?

**Implementation Questions:**
- How do teachers check pupils' understanding during lessons?
- What assessment information do teachers use to plan sequences of lessons?
- How do you ensure all pupils are appropriately challenged?
- What CPD do staff receive to develop their subject and pedagogical knowledge?

**Impact Questions:**
- How do you know pupils are developing physical competence?
- What evidence shows pupils are retaining knowledge and skills over time?
- How are pupils prepared for lifelong physical activity?
- What is the take-up of extra-curricular PE and competitive opportunities?

## National Curriculum Requirements and Ofsted Expectations

### Key Stage 1 and 2

The **National Curriculum for PE (2014)** sets out statutory requirements that Ofsted evaluates during inspections. For Key Stages 1 and 2, schools must provide:

- **Key Stage 1**: Master basic movements including running, jumping, throwing and catching; develop balance, agility and coordination; participate in team games; perform dances using simple movement patterns
- **Key Stage 2**: Apply and develop broader range of skills; play competitive games; develop flexibility, strength, technique, control and balance; perform dances; take part in outdoor and adventurous activity challenges; compare performances and demonstrate improvement

Ofsted expects to see clear progression between Key Stages 1 and 2, with Year 3 building directly on Year 2 knowledge and skills. Len Almond's work on physical literacy provides a useful framework here, emphasising motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding as interconnected elements.

### Key Stage 3

At Key Stage 3, the National Curriculum requires pupils to:

- Use a range of tactics and strategies to overcome opponents in direct competition
- Develop technique and improve performance in other competitive sports
- Take part in outdoor and adventurous activities
- Analyse performances to inspire improvement
- Take on leadership and coaching roles

Ofsted expects schools to demonstrate how the curriculum has been designed to deepen pupils' understanding beyond Key Stage 2, introducing more complex tactical knowledge and developing pupils as independent learners and leaders.

### Key Stage 4

Key Stage 4 PE provision includes both:

**Core PE** (statutory for all pupils):
- Continuation of physical development and participation
- Preparation for lifelong involvement in physical activity

**Examination PE** (optional qualifications including):
- **AQA GCSE PE (8582)**: Focuses on applied anatomy and physiology, movement analysis, physical training, use of data, socio-cultural influences, sports psychology, and health, fitness and well-being
- **Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)**: Covers anatomy and physiology, movement analysis, physical training, health and performance, socio-cultural influences, and sports psychology and performance
- **OCR GCSE PE (J587)**: Includes physical factors affecting performance, socio-cultural issues and sports psychology, health and fitness, and practical performance
- **WJEC GCSE PE**: Comprises health, training and exercise, the role of diet and nutrition, psychology, biomechanics, and socio-cultural influences

Ofsted evaluates whether examination specifications are taught rigorously and whether core PE provision ensures all pupils, including those not taking examination courses, continue to develop physically and access meaningful physical activity.

## Common Ofsted Findings and Areas for Development

### Strengths Often Identified

Research from Ofsted inspection reports reveals common strengths in high-quality PE provision:

1. **Clear curriculum rationale**: Schools articulate precisely what they want pupils to learn and why
2. **Strong subject leadership**: Specialist PE coordinators and heads of department who drive improvement
3. **Inclusive practice**: Effective adaptation for SEND pupils and creative use of inclusive teaching approaches
4. **Links to health and wellbeing**: Explicit teaching about fitness, health, and lifelong participation
5. **Rich extra-curricular offer**: Extensive clubs, competitions, and leadership opportunities

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

Ofsted reports frequently identify these weaknesses in PE provision:

1. **Lack of curriculum coherence**: Multi-activity programmes that rotate activities without clear progression (echoing David Kirk's critique of the "multi-activity curriculum")
2. **Weak assessment practice**: Inadequate monitoring of pupils' skill development and knowledge retention
3. **Insufficient subject knowledge**: Non-specialist teaching without adequate support or training
4. **Limited implementation of National Curriculum breadth**: Particularly in swimming, outdoor and adventurous activities, and dance
5. **Inadequate SEND provision**: Failure to adapt curriculum and teaching appropriately for pupils with special educational needs
6. **Poor planning and sequencing**: Lessons that don't build systematically on prior learning
7. **Overemphasis on activity rather than learning**: Lessons focused on participation without clear learning objectives

Susan Capel's extensive work on learning to teach PE and developing PE teachers is particularly relevant to addressing many of these weaknesses, particularly around subject knowledge and pedagogical approaches.

## Practical Implications for PE Teachers and Leaders

### Curriculum Design and Documentation

To meet Ofsted expectations, PE departments should:

1. **Create comprehensive curriculum maps** showing:
   - What is taught in each year group and unit
   - How knowledge and skills build across key stages
   - Links to National Curriculum requirements
   - Assessment opportunities and success criteria

2. **Develop clear progression documents** identifying:
   - Threshold concepts in PE (e.g., space creation, application of pressure, adaptation)
   - How these concepts develop from EYFS to Key Stage 4
   - Specific vocabulary introduced at each stage

3. **Articulate curriculum intent explicitly** by:
   - Writing a clear rationale for curriculum choices
   - Explaining how PE supports whole-school aims
   - Demonstrating consideration of local context and pupil needs

### Assessment and Evidence

Ofsted's focus on pupil outcomes requires robust assessment approaches:

1. **Use formative assessment effectively**:
   - Regular checks for understanding during lessons
   - Use of questioning to probe pupils' knowledge
   - Responsive teaching that adapts based on assessment information

2. **Develop appropriate evidence of learning**:
   - Video recordings of practical performances
   - Written or verbal reflections on performance
   - Knowledge assessments on fitness, tactics, and rules
   - Pupil self and peer assessment materials

3. **Track pupil progress systematically**:
   - Clear systems for recording skill development
   - Monitoring of participation and engagement
   - Analysis of data by pupil groups (SEND, disadvantaged, gender, etc.)

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework provides valuable assessment principles here, emphasising holistic development across physical, psychological, social, and cognitive domains.

### Lesson Observations and Teaching Quality

Inspectors evaluate PE teaching against general teaching standards but with subject-specific considerations:

1. **Effective PE lessons demonstrate**:
   - Clear learning objectives focused on knowledge and skills
   - High expectations for all pupils
   - Appropriate challenge and differentiation
   - Efficient organisation maximising active learning time
   - Skilful questioning to develop understanding
   - Safe practice with explicit attention to risk management
   - Use of demonstration, practice, and feedback cycles

2. **Pedagogical approaches valued by Ofsted**:
   - Explicit instruction where appropriate
   - Guided discovery and problem-solving
   - Use of peer learning and leadership opportunities
   - Integration of literacy (PE-specific vocabulary)
   - Technology to enhance learning (e.g., video analysis)

### Professional Development

Addressing Ofsted's emphasis on subject knowledge requires strategic CPD:

1. **For non-specialist teachers**:
   - Subject knowledge development in specific activities
   - Understanding of progression in motor skills
   - Assessment literacy in PE contexts

2. **For specialist staff**:
   - Latest pedagogical research and approaches
   - Curriculum design and sequencing
   - Subject leadership and quality assurance
   - Working with external organisations (afPE, Youth Sport Trust)

3. **Whole-school understanding**:
   - Educating senior leaders about high-quality PE
   - Sharing PE curriculum rationale with all staff
   - Demonstrating PE's contribution to whole-school priorities

## PE Premium and Primary School Sport

### Accountability for PE Premium Spending

Primary schools receive **PE and Sport Premium** funding and Ofsted evaluates its use during inspections. The DfE specifies that funding should be used to:

1. Develop or add to PE and sport activities that the school already offers
2. Build capacity and capability within the school to ensure improvements are sustainable
3. Make improvements to meet the broader outcomes (e.g., improved health, wellbeing, participation)

Ofsted inspectors examine:
- **Strategic use of funding**: Clear rationale for spending choices
- **Impact on provision**: Evidence of improved teaching, curriculum, or participation
- **Sustainability**: Plans to maintain improvements beyond funding period
- **Additionality**: Ensuring funding supplements rather than replaces core provision

### Common Weaknesses in PE Premium Use

Ofsted has identified these issues in PE Premium spending:

1. Using funding for existing rather than additional provision
2. Over-reliance on external coaches without staff development
3. Lack of monitoring of impact on pupil outcomes
4. Insufficient focus on sustainability and building teacher expertise

## Preparing for an Ofsted PE Deep Dive

### For Subject Leaders

1. **Know your curriculum intimately**:
   - Be able to explain the rationale for every unit and activity
   - Articulate how learning builds across years and key stages
   - Reference National Curriculum requirements explicitly
   - Explain how your curriculum reflects research-informed best practice

2. **Have evidence readily available**:
   - Curriculum plans and progression documents
   - Assessment information showing pupil outcomes
   - Examples of pupil work (video, written work, photographs)
   - Records of professional development and its impact
   - Data on participation, engagement, and extra-curricular involvement
   - Evidence of inclusive practice and support for diverse learners

3. **Engage with current discourse**:
   - Familiarity with Ofsted's PE Research Review (2022)
   - Understanding of physical literacy concept (Whitehead)
   - Awareness of debate around curriculum models (Kirk, Penney)
   - Knowledge of examination specifications if teaching Key Stage 4

### For Teachers

1. **Understand the curriculum plan**:
   - What pupils should learn in your unit
   - What prior knowledge pupils bring from previous units
   - Where this learning leads in subsequent years

2. **Plan for learning, not just activity**:
   - Clear, specific learning objectives
   - Opportunities to check understanding
   - Structured skill development and practice
   - Time for reflection and consolidation

3. **Be prepared to discuss**:
   - What pupils have learned in recent lessons
   - How you adapt teaching for different needs
   - How you assess pupils' progress
   - What pupils will learn next and why

### For Pupils

Ofsted conversations with pupils might explore:

1. **What they have learned**: Can they articulate specific skills, tactics, or knowledge?
2. **What they remember**: Can they recall prior learning and make connections?
3. **Why they are learning**: Do they understand the purpose and value of PE?
4. **Their experience**: Do they feel challenged, supported, and engaged?

## The Broader Context: Ofsted and Physical Activity Policy

### Links to Government Policy

Ofsted's evaluation of PE sits within broader government policy aims:

1. **School Sport and Activity Action Plan (2019)**: Commitment to all children getting 60 minutes of physical activity daily (30 minutes in school)
2. **Childhood Obesity Strategy**: Recognition of schools' role in promoting active lifestyles
3. **Health and Wellbeing**: Ofsted's personal development judgement includes attention to physical health

### Ofsted's Role in Accountability

While Ofsted does not grade individual subjects, PE quality significantly influences:

- **Quality of Education judgement**: Strong or weak curriculum design and implementation
- **Personal Development judgement**: Contribution to character, resilience, and health education
- **Behaviour and Attitudes**: Engagement in lessons and extra-curricular activities
- **Leadership and Management**: Strategic use of PE Premium in primary schools

## Contemporary Debates and Tensions

### Curriculum Breadth vs. Depth

Ofsted's research review has intensified debate about PE curriculum models. The tension exists between:

- **Traditional multi-activity approach**: Sampling many activities but potentially lacking progression
- **Depth-focused models**: Fewer activities studied in greater detail with better progression
- **Physical literacy approach**: Holistic development emphasised by Margaret Whitehead, focusing on motivation, confidence, competence, and knowledge

David Kirk's research on "block curriculum" models suggests that teaching activities in concentrated blocks (e.g., entire terms) rather than short rotations may improve learning outcomes.
