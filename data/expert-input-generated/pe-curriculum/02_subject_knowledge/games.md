# Games and Sport

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Games and sport constitute a fundamental component of the UK Physical Education curriculum, appearing as a statutory requirement across all key stages in the National Curriculum for Physical Education (NCPE) in England. The Department for Education's statutory guidance (2013, revised 2021) mandates that pupils should "play competitive games, modified where appropriate, and apply basic principles suitable for attacking and defending." This area of activity encompasses invasion games (football, rugby, hockey, basketball, netball), net/wall games (tennis, badminton, volleyball), striking/fielding games (cricket, rounders, softball), and target games (golf, bowls, archery).

David Kirk's influential work on "games-based approaches" has significantly shaped contemporary PE pedagogy, moving away from traditional technique-focused instruction toward more holistic, contextualised learning. Kirk (2010) argues that games teaching should prioritise tactical awareness and decision-making alongside physical competence, a perspective now widely adopted across UK schools.

## National Curriculum Requirements by Key Stage

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The NCPE requires pupils to "participate in team games, developing simple tactics for attacking and defending." At this foundational stage, the focus is on:

- Master basic movements including running, jumping, throwing and catching
- Develop balance, agility and coordination
- Participate in simple competitive situations
- Understand basic concepts of space, equipment sharing and turn-taking

Teachers should employ modified games with reduced numbers (typically 2v2 or 3v3), simplified rules, and adapted equipment. Len Almond's "Teaching Games for Understanding" (TGfU) model, developed originally in the 1980s at Loughborough University, remains particularly relevant here, emphasising game appreciation before technical proficiency.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Pupils should "play competitive games, modified where appropriate...and apply basic principles suitable for attacking and defending." This stage develops:

- Tactical understanding across different game categories
- Communication and collaboration skills within teams
- Application of skills under pressure in competitive contexts
- Understanding of rules, fair play and sporting values

Dawn Penney's research (2006) highlights the importance of ensuring breadth and balance across game types during this phase, avoiding over-emphasis on traditional team games like football at the expense of net/wall or striking/fielding games.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The NCPE states pupils should "develop their technique and improve their performance in competitive sport" and "take part in competitive sports and activities outside school through community links or sports clubs." Key developments include:

- Refinement of advanced technical skills specific to chosen activities
- Complex tactical understanding and strategic application
- Leadership roles including officiating, coaching and team captaincy
- Analysis and evaluation of performance using appropriate terminology

Susan Capel's work on PE teacher development (2007) emphasises the importance of subject knowledge depth at this stage, with teachers requiring sophisticated understanding of game structures, tactics and progression frameworks.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

While PE remains compulsory, the curriculum allows schools greater flexibility. Many pupils pursue GCSE PE (AQA, Edexcel, OCR or WJEC), where games and sport feature prominently in both practical assessment and theoretical study.

## Pedagogical Approaches and Teaching Models

### Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)

Developed by Bunker and Thorpe (1982) and championed in the UK by Len Almond, this model prioritises:

1. Game appreciation
2. Tactical awareness
3. Decision-making
4. Skill execution
5. Performance

The TGfU model groups games into categories (invasion, net/wall, striking/fielding, target) based on similar tactical problems, enabling transfer of learning. Research by Griffin and Butler (2005) demonstrates improved engagement and cognitive development compared to traditional technique-first approaches.

### Sport Education Model

Kirk and colleagues (1996, 2006) adapted Siedentop's Sport Education model for UK contexts. This approach organises units as "seasons" where pupils remain in persistent teams, assuming roles (player, coach, official, statistician) and competing in authentic competition formats. The model develops:

- Affiliation and team identity
- Understanding of sport culture and values
- Authentic application of skills in meaningful contexts
- Student autonomy and leadership

Research by Hastie et al. (2011) shows Sport Education significantly increases pupil engagement and maintains higher activity levels compared to traditional multi-activity units.

### Constraints-Led Approach

Drawing on ecological dynamics theory, this contemporary approach manipulates task, environmental and individual constraints to facilitate skill acquisition. Particularly relevant for games teaching, coaches and teachers can modify:

- **Task constraints**: rules, equipment size, scoring systems
- **Environmental constraints**: playing area dimensions, weather, surfaces
- **Individual constraints**: player numbers, ability groupings, roles

This approach aligns with Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy, emphasising adaptability and problem-solving in varied contexts.

## Classification of Games

### Invasion Games

Games where teams score by invading opponents' territory (football, rugby, hockey, basketball, netball, lacrosse). Common tactical problems include:

- Maintaining possession
- Penetrating defence
- Creating and using space
- Defending space and preventing scoring

### Net/Wall Games

Games where opponents are separated by a barrier (tennis, badminton, volleyball, table tennis, squash). Key tactical elements include:

- Court positioning and coverage
- Shot selection and placement
- Creating opponent movement
- Exploiting space

### Striking/Fielding Games

Games involving striking an object and running to score (cricket, rounders, softball, baseball). Tactical considerations include:

- Batting placement and timing
- Fielding positions and backing up
- Running between bases/wickets
- Bowling/pitching strategy

### Target Games

Games aiming to propel objects toward targets (golf, bowls, archery, curling). Emphasis on:

- Precision and consistency
- Environmental reading
- Strategic shot selection
- Concentration and composure

## Physical Literacy and Games Education

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy concept (2010, 2019) emphasises that games education should develop "motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life." This philosophical framework challenges traditional performance-focused approaches, instead prioritising:

- Embodied dimension: developing capability across varied game contexts
- Affective dimension: fostering confidence and motivation in competitive situations
- Cognitive dimension: understanding tactical principles and strategic thinking
- Social dimension: collaboration, communication and ethical participation

The Association for Physical Education (afPE) formally adopted physical literacy as a guiding principle in 2015, significantly influencing how games are conceptualised and taught across UK schools.

## Assessment in Games and Sport

### Formative Assessment Approaches

Kirk and MacPhail (2002) advocate assessment that supports learning rather than merely measuring outcomes. Effective strategies include:

- Peer observation using structured frameworks
- Video analysis with focused criteria
- Tactical awareness questioning during game play
- Self-assessment against learning objectives
- Teacher observation of decision-making in context

### GCSE Physical Education Requirements

All major UK exam boards assess practical performance in games:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582)**: Students assessed in three activities from different categories. Performance in games activities marked against standardised criteria considering technical proficiency, tactical awareness, and performance quality under pressure (worth 30% of total qualification).

**Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)**: Similar structure with practical assessment across three activities (30%). Moderators assess isolated skills, sequential performance, and conditioned competitive situations.

**OCR GCSE PE (J587)**: Practical performance worth 30%, with students demonstrating skills in isolation and applied context. Games activities feature in paper-based questions examining rules, tactics and skill application.

**WJEC GCSE PE**: Welsh examination board with similar assessment structure, ensuring UK-wide comparability.

### A-Level Assessment

Games feature in theoretical examination content, particularly within biomechanical, physiological and psychological contexts. Students analyse skill classification, information processing, motivation in sport, and leadership in team environments.

## Differentiation and Inclusion

Dawn Penney's extensive work on inclusion (2002, 2008) highlights persistent challenges in games teaching, particularly regarding:

- Gender stereotypes limiting activity choice
- Ability grouping creating hierarchies
- Cultural assumptions about game preferences
- Physical access for disabled pupils

The STEP principle (Space, Task, Equipment, People) provides a practical framework for inclusive differentiation:

- **Space**: Modify playing area size, boundaries, zones
- **Task**: Adjust rules, scoring, roles, objectives
- **Equipment**: Use different sized balls, adapted implements
- **People**: Vary team sizes, mix abilities, assign specific roles

The Inclusion Spectrum (Black and Williamson, 2011) offers five approaches: open activities (all participate equally), modified activities, parallel activities, separate activities, and disability sport. Research demonstrates that well-designed modified games can maintain challenge while ensuring meaningful participation for all.

## Competition and Values Education

David Kirk's critique (2004) of competitive games raises important questions about how competition is framed. While the NCPE mandates competitive opportunities, research suggests the quality of competitive experience matters significantly:

- **Process-oriented competition**: Emphasising improvement, effort and teamwork
- **Outcome-oriented competition**: Focusing solely on winning and superiority

Bailey and Dismore (2004) distinguish between "competition" (striving for excellence) and "competitiveness" (desire to defeat others), arguing that PE should develop healthy attitudes toward competition without promoting win-at-all-costs mentalities.

The Youth Sport Trust's "Step into Sport" programme and Sport England's emphasis on positive sporting experiences reflect sector-wide recognition that games teaching must explicitly address values including:

- Respect for opponents, officials and rules
- Fair play and integrity
- Resilience and gracious responses to outcomes
- Teamwork and collective responsibility

## Links to Health and Wellbeing

Games and sport contribute significantly to the government's childhood obesity strategy and broader public health objectives. The Chief Medical Officer's guidelines (2019) recommend children achieve 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity daily, with games lessons providing structured opportunities.

Research by Fairclough and Stratton (2005) examining activity levels during secondary PE lessons found games activities generated substantial physical activity, though with considerable variation based on teaching approach, organisation and game format. Small-sided games consistently produced higher activity levels than full-sided matches with extended waiting times.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning Effective Games Units

Susan Capel and colleagues (2015) emphasise that effective planning requires:

- Clear learning objectives beyond simple participation
- Logical progression of tactical complexity
- Varied organisational strategies maintaining high activity levels
- Differentiation supporting all learners
- Explicit attention to cognitive as well as physical development

### Managing Learning Environments

Games teaching presents specific management challenges:

- **Safety considerations**: Risk assessment for contact activities, appropriate equipment, surface conditions
- **Equipment organisation**: Efficient distribution and collection systems
- **Grouping strategies**: Flexible approaches balancing social, tactical and skill development needs
- **Space utilisation**: Maximising activity through multiple playing areas

### Subject Knowledge Development

Kirk and colleagues (2002) identify subject matter knowledge as crucial for effective games teaching, requiring understanding of:

- Technical models for skills across game categories
- Tactical frameworks and principles
- Appropriate progressions and regressions
- Common errors and corrective strategies
- Rules and their educational modification

Professional development should focus on pedagogical content knowledge—knowing how to teach games effectively—rather than merely playing ability.

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Curriculum Time and Activity Selection

Debate continues regarding which games deserve curriculum time. Traditional team games dominate many programmes, reflecting teachers' own experiences and perceived community expectations. However, Penney and Evans (1999) argue for more diverse offerings reflecting broader definitions of games, including:

- Lifetime activities (tennis, golf, badminton)
- Alternative invasion games (ultimate frisbee, tchoukball)
- Modified versions promoting creativity
- Culturally diverse game forms

### Performance versus Participation

Tensions exist between developing elite performers and ensuring broad engagement. Kirk's "two-world" concept describes the divide between curriculum PE and extra-curricular sport, potentially creating hierarchies where less-skilled pupils feel marginalised. Research by Flintoff and Scraton (2006) demonstrates this particularly affects girls and certain ethnic groups.

### Assessment Authenticity

Green (2008) questions whether isolated skill assessment reflects genuine game performance. Authentic assessment should evaluate decision-making, adaptation and performance under genuine competitive pressure, aligning assessment practices with stated learning outcomes.

## Conclusion and Future Directions

Games and sport remain central to UK PE provision, though contemporary approaches emphasise tactical understanding, inclusion and physical literacy over traditional performance models. Effective games teaching requires sophisticated pedagogical content knowledge, commitment to inclusive practice, and recognition of games' potential for developing broader capacities including critical thinking, collaboration and ethical decision-making.

Future directions include greater use of technology for performance analysis, continued development of evidence-based pedagogies, and renewed attention to ensuring equitable access regardless of gender, ability, cultural background or prior experience. As Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy concept continues influencing practice, games teaching must balance skill development with nurturing lifelong engagement in physical activity.
