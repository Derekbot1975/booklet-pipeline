# Athletics

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Athletics forms a fundamental component of the UK Physical Education curriculum, representing a core activity area that develops fundamental movement skills and physical literacy. Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy—the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life—is particularly well-served through athletics, which builds upon and refines fundamental movement patterns (Whitehead, 2010).

The National Curriculum for Physical Education (DfE, 2013) explicitly requires that pupils develop competence in a broad range of physical activities, including athletics. David Kirk (2010) argues that athletics provides an ideal vehicle for developing what he terms "pedagogical models" that emphasise both skill development and cognitive engagement with movement.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2

At Key Stage 1, whilst athletics is not explicitly named, the curriculum requires pupils to "master basic movements including running, jumping, throwing and catching, as well as developing balance, agility and co-ordination" (DfE, 2013). These fundamental athletic skills form the foundation for later specialisation.

Key Stage 2 specifically states that pupils should "use running, jumping, throwing and catching in isolation and in combination" and should "develop flexibility, strength, technique, control and balance". Athletics activities must enable pupils to "compare their performances with previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve their personal best" (DfE, 2013).

### Key Stage 3 and 4

At Key Stage 3, pupils should "develop technique and improve their performance in athletics" whilst Key Stage 4 students should be able to "analyse their performances compared to previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve their personal best" (DfE, 2013). Dawn Penney's work on curriculum policy highlights that athletics provides opportunities for differentiation and personalised learning that few other activities can match (Penney, 2008).

## Fundamental Skills and Technique

### Running Events

Athletics encompasses three primary running categories: sprints (60m, 100m, 200m, 400m), middle-distance (800m, 1500m), and long-distance events. Susan Capel et al. (2016) in "Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School" emphasise that effective running technique involves:

- **Sprint technique**: Appropriate starting position (set position in blocks for older pupils), acceleration phase, drive phase, and maintenance of running form with high knee lift, arm drive, and upright posture
- **Middle and long-distance technique**: Economical running action, appropriate stride length and frequency, breathing patterns, and pacing strategies
- **Hurdles technique**: Lead leg action (straight at knee over hurdle), trail leg action, maintenance of rhythm and speed between hurdles, and three-stride pattern

### Jumping Events

The four principal jumping events—long jump, triple jump, high jump, and pole vault (typically only in specialist settings)—each require specific technical understanding:

**Long Jump**: Len Almond's work on fundamental movement emphasises that long jump combines speed on the approach, an effective penultimate stride, vertical impulse at take-off, flight phase management, and landing technique. Progressive teaching moves from standing jumps through short approach jumps to full approach (Almond, 1997).

**Triple Jump**: The hop-step-jump sequence requires balance, rhythm, and maintenance of horizontal velocity whilst achieving sufficient vertical displacement at each phase.

**High Jump**: The Fosbury Flop technique, now universally adopted, requires curved approach run (typically 8-10 strides), inside leg take-off, rotation and clearance of bar, and landing on upper back.

### Throwing Events

Athletics offers four throwing disciplines, each with distinct technical requirements:

**Shot Put**: The O'Brien technique (glide) or rotational technique requires initiation from the rear of the circle, acceleration across the circle, delivery position (power position), and release with appropriate projection angle (typically 38-42 degrees).

**Discus**: Rotational technique involving 1.5 turns within the circle, torque generation through hip-shoulder separation, and release with appropriate flight angle.

**Javelin**: The run-up approach, withdrawal phase, cross-over steps, delivery stride, and release with appropriate projection angle (typically 30-35 degrees) and technical legality (over the shoulder, point first landing).

**Hammer** (typically only at specialist clubs): Similar rotation to discus but with extended implement requiring specific safety protocols.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Teaching Athletics Effectively

David Kirk's work on pedagogical models in Physical Education (Kirk, 2013) identifies several approaches particularly effective for athletics teaching:

**Task-based learning**: Athletics lends itself to individualised practice with specific, measurable targets. Dawn Penney and Michael Jess (2004) advocate for differentiated task cards that allow pupils to progress at individual rates whilst maintaining common learning objectives.

**Peer assessment and coaching**: Athletics events provide clear performance criteria enabling effective peer assessment. Pupils can observe, analyse and provide feedback on technique, developing both performer and observer capabilities.

**Personal best orientation**: Rather than competitive comparison, Whitehead's physical literacy framework emphasises individual progress and improvement, perfectly aligned with athletics' capacity for personal target-setting.

### Athletics and Physical Literacy

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy concept (Whitehead, 2010) identifies four key domains particularly well-addressed through athletics:

1. **Physical domain**: Development of fundamental movement skills and physical capacities
2. **Affective domain**: Self-confidence, motivation, and enjoyment from achieving personal goals
3. **Cognitive domain**: Understanding biomechanical principles, tactical awareness in races, and performance analysis
4. **Behavioural domain**: Regular engagement, practice habits, and commitment to improvement

## Progression and Development

### Primary School Athletics

Youth Sport Trust resources and the Change4Life Sports Clubs programme emphasise multi-skills approaches in Key Stages 1 and 2. Progressive activities might include:

- **Foundation stage**: Basic running, jumping on two feet, throwing underarm
- **Year 1-2**: Running for distance and speed, jumping for distance, throwing overarm
- **Year 3-4**: Introduction to specific athletic events, relay changeover technique, basic competition formats
- **Year 5-6**: Refined technique in specific events, understanding of rules, Sports Hall Athletics formats

Sports Hall Athletics, developed by English Schools Athletic Association (ESAA), provides age-appropriate indoor competition formats highly suitable for UK primary schools.

### Secondary School Athletics

Progressive skill development through secondary years should follow technical, physical and cognitive development:

**Key Stage 3**: Foundation technical models in all event groups, understanding of basic biomechanical principles, introduction to event-specific training, Athletics 365 award scheme (UK Athletics).

**Key Stage 4**: Refinement of technique, understanding of training principles specific to different athletic events, performance analysis using video technology, preparation for examination specifications.

## Assessment in Athletics

### Formative Assessment

Susan Capel's work emphasises continuous assessment focused on technique development rather than purely outcome measures. Effective assessment strategies include:

- Video analysis of technique with pupil self-assessment
- Peer assessment using criteria sheets focused on specific technical elements
- Training diary maintenance demonstrating understanding of progressive training
- Performance comparisons against personal benchmarks rather than peer comparisons

### Summative Assessment

National Curriculum level descriptors and GCSE/A-Level specifications provide clear assessment criteria. Athletics units enable assessment against multiple PE objectives simultaneously.

## Examination Specifications

### GCSE Physical Education

All major examination boards include athletics within their specifications:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582)**: Requires knowledge of sprint, middle and long-distance running, hurdles, relay running, long jump, high jump, triple jump, shot put, discus, javelin, and hammer. Students must analyse performance, identify strengths and weaknesses, and suggest improvement strategies.

**Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)**: Similar event coverage with emphasis on biomechanical analysis including Newton's Laws, projectile motion in throwing and jumping, and force application in running events.

**OCR GCSE PE (J587)**: Practical performance assessment alongside theoretical understanding of techniques, rules, and performance analysis strategies.

**WJEC GCSE PE**: Includes analysis of technique, training methods specific to different athletic events, and understanding of factors affecting performance.

### A-Level Physical Education

A-Level specifications require deeper biomechanical and physiological analysis:

**AQA A-Level PE (7582)**: Application of biomechanical principles (angular motion in throwing events, projectile motion, fluid mechanics affecting javelin flight), physiological demands of different events (ATP-PC system for sprints, aerobic system for distance events), and performance analysis methodologies.

**Edexcel A-Level PE (9PE0)**: Similar content with additional emphasis on historical and socio-cultural factors affecting athletics participation and development.

**OCR A-Level PE (H555)**: Detailed biomechanical analysis, training programme design specific to athletic events, and performance analysis using motion analysis software.

## Health and Safety Considerations

Safe athletics teaching requires rigorous attention to environmental, equipment and organisational factors:

### Equipment Safety

- **Throwing implements**: Appropriate weight for age and ability (e.g., 3kg shot for Year 7-9 girls, 4kg for Year 7-9 boys)
- **Landing areas**: Adequate depth and condition of landing pits for jumping events
- **Starting blocks**: Secure positioning and appropriate spike use
- **Measurement equipment**: Accurate and well-maintained tape measures, non-elastic for competitive measurements

### Organisational Safety

Association for Physical Education (afPE) guidance "Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity" (2020) provides essential safety protocols:

- Designated throwing sectors with clear marshalling zones
- Retrieval procedures preventing pupils entering throwing areas until signalled
- Appropriate spacing for sprint lanes and adequate run-off areas
- High jump landing area positioning and appropriate bar materials (fiberglass, not metal)
- Weather considerations (lightning protocols, extreme heat procedures)

### Warm-up and Preparation

Progressive warm-up specific to athletic events is essential, including:

- Pulse-raising activities with progressive intensity
- Dynamic stretching appropriate to subsequent activity demands
- Event-specific preparatory drills
- Gradual intensity build-up in throwing and jumping practices

## Differentiation and Inclusion

### Adapting for Different Abilities

Athletics offers excellent opportunities for meaningful differentiation:

- **Task modification**: Varied implement weights, approach run lengths, hurdle heights, target distances
- **STEP principle** (Space, Task, Equipment, People): Adjusting these variables enables appropriate challenge for all learners
- **Competition formats**: Personal best competitions, team points systems, handicap systems

### SEND Considerations

Athletics can be effectively adapted for pupils with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities:

- **Visual impairment**: Use of guide runners (tethered running), audible targets, textured approaches
- **Physical disabilities**: Wheelchair racing classifications, seated throwing events, adapted implements
- **Sensory processing differences**: Clear, predictable routines, visual task cards, individual workstations

UK Athletics' "Inclusive Activity Cards" provide specific adaptations for various disabilities whilst maintaining core skill development objectives.

## Athletics Beyond the Curriculum

### School Sport Competitions

UK School Games programme provides structured competition pathways from Level 1 (intra-school) through Level 4 (national finals). English Schools' Athletics Association (ESAA) organises national championships providing aspirational targets for talented athletes.

Sports Hall Athletics (October-March) and outdoor athletics (April-July) provide complementary competition structures enabling year-round participation.

### Athletics Clubs and Talent Development

Effective PE teaching identifies and nurtures athletic talent. Links with UK Athletics affiliated clubs enable progression pathways. Teacher awareness of England Athletics' Athlete Development Model helps appropriate signposting.

### Cross-Curricular Links

Athletics provides rich opportunities for cross-curricular learning:

- **Mathematics**: Time measurement, distance calculation, performance data analysis, statistical comparison
- **Science**: Biomechanics, physics of projectile motion, physiology of different energy systems
- **Technology**: Video analysis software, performance tracking applications, timing systems
- **Personal development**: Goal-setting, resilience, handling success and failure

## Contemporary Issues in Athletics Education

### Technology in Athletics Teaching

Modern athletics teaching increasingly incorporates technology:

- **Video analysis**: iPad apps (Coach's Eye, Hudl Technique) enabling immediate technical feedback
- **Performance tracking**: Google Sheets or Excel for maintaining performance databases
- - **Timing systems**: Electronic timing gates providing accurate performance data
- **Motion analysis software**: A-Level practical analysis using Dartfish or Quintic

### Talent Development and Ethical Considerations

Dawn Penney's research highlights tensions between participation and performance agendas in school sport (Penney, 2006). Teachers must balance:

- Identifying and developing talent whilst maintaining inclusive participation
- Competition opportunities without creating performance pressure
- Personal best orientations alongside competitive experience
- Long-term athlete development principles versus short-term competitive success

UK Athletics' "Athlete Development Model" provides age-appropriate guidance preventing premature specialisation whilst developing fundamental competencies.

### Gender and Athletics

Historical gender differences in athletics participation and achievement remain relevant. Research by Sport England's "Active Lives" survey (2021) identifies athletics as an activity showing positive engagement across genders when taught effectively. Teachers should:

- Challenge stereotypes about event suitability (particularly throwing events for girls)
- Ensure equal access to facilities, equipment and coaching expertise
- Recognise and celebrate diverse physical achievements
- Address body image concerns sensitively, particularly in running events

## Training Principles Specific to Athletics

### Event-Specific Training

Understanding different athletic events' physiological demands enables appropriate training design:

**Sprints (100m-400m)**: Predominantly ATP-PC and anaerobic glycolytic systems. Training emphasises maximum speed development, acceleration technique, and lactate tolerance (400m specifically).

**Middle-distance (800m-1500m)**: Combination of aerobic and anaerobic systems. Training includes VO2 max development, lactate threshold improvement, and tactical racing skills.

**Long-distance (3000m+)**: Primarily aerobic system. Training emphasises aerobic capacity, running economy, and pacing strategies.

**Jumps**: Power development combining strength and speed (force × velocity). Plyometric training, technical practice, and approach run consistency.

**Throws**: Maximal strength and explosive power. Technical drilling, implement-specific strength work, and competition simulation.

### Training Methods

Effective athletics coaching employs varied training methods appropriately matched to event demands:

- **Interval training**: Structured work:rest ratios developing specific energy system capacities
- **Fartlek training**: Varied pace running developing multiple energy systems and race adaptability
- **Continuous training**: Sustained aerobic exercise developing aerobic capacity
- **Plyometric training**: Explosive jumping exercises developing elastic strength
- **Technical drilling**: Repeated practice of specific movement patterns developing movement efficiency

## Performance Analysis in Athletics

### Biomechanical Analysis

A-Level specifications require detailed biomechanical understanding applicable to athletics:

**Sprint running**: Analysis of ground contact time, stride length, stride frequency, centre of mass displacement, and force application angles. Newton's Third Law (action-reaction) explaining forward propulsion. Air resistance considerations in 100m/200m.

**Jumping events**: Projectile motion equations applied to long jump and high jump. Take-off angle optimization balancing horizontal velocity maintenance with vertical displacement. Conservation of angular momentum in Fosbury Flop rotation.

**Throwing events**: Projectile motion with optimal release angles (shot 38-42°, discus 35-37°, javelin 30-35°). Magnus effect creating lift on discus and javelin. Torque generation through sequential body segment acceleration (kinetic chain).

### Physiological Analysis

Understanding energy system contributions enables event-specific training:

- **100m sprint**: 95% ATP-PC, 5% anaerobic glycolysis (approximately 10-second duration)
- **400m sprint**: 60% anaerobic glycolysis, 40% ATP-PC (45-55 second duration)
- **800m**: 60% anaerobic, 40% aerobic (critical lactate tolerance event)
- **1500m**: 75% aerobic, 25% anaerobic (strategic race management critical)
- **5000m+**: 95%+ aerobic (efficient running economy essential)
