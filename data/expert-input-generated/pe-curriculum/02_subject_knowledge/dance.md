# Dance in PE

## Introduction to Dance in Physical Education

Dance is a statutory requirement within the National Curriculum for Physical Education in England at Key Stages 1, 2, and 3. The subject represents a unique art form that combines physical activity with creative expression, cultural understanding, and aesthetic appreciation. As Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy emphasises, dance contributes significantly to developing "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2010).

Dance in PE differs fundamentally from recreational or performance dance by emphasising educational outcomes, physical development, and inclusive participation. David Kirk (2010) in 'Physical Education Futures' argues that dance provides opportunities for bodily expression that are less readily available in competitive games, making it essential for a balanced PE curriculum.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

Pupils should be taught to:
- Perform dances using simple movement patterns
- Develop fundamental movement skills through dance activities

At this stage, dance focuses on exploring basic actions (travelling, turning, jumping, gesturing, stillness) and developing spatial awareness. Teachers should integrate counting, pattern-making, and story-based dance to engage young learners.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Pupils should be taught to:
- Develop flexibility, strength, technique, control and balance through dance
- Perform dances using a range of movement patterns
- Compare their performances with previous ones and demonstrate improvement

The programme of study expects pupils to create and perform more complex dance phrases and sequences, often working collaboratively. Cultural dance forms (e.g., traditional British, European, or world dances) should be introduced.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Pupils should be taught to:
- Use a range of techniques and tactics confidently and effectively
- Develop technique and improve performance in dance
- Take part in competitive dance (where appropriate) and cooperative physical activities

At KS3, dance becomes more sophisticated, incorporating choreographic devices, performance quality, and critical appreciation. Students should experience various dance styles including contemporary, street dance, and cultural forms.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

Dance is optional at KS4 but features prominently in GCSE and BTEC qualifications. The National Curriculum states that schools should provide "a broad and ambitious physical education curriculum" which may include dance as a practical activity option.

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Physical Literacy and Dance

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy concept (Whitehead, 2019, 'Physical Literacy Across the World') positions dance as crucial for developing:
- **Physical competence**: Control, coordination, and kinesthetic awareness
- **Motivation and confidence**: Self-expression and personal interpretation
- **Knowledge and understanding**: Understanding movement vocabulary and dance terminology
- **Engagement**: Lifelong participation in dance and physical activity

### Dance as Embodied Learning

Dawn Penney and colleagues have researched curriculum-making in PE, emphasising that dance offers unique embodied learning experiences (Penney & Chandler, 2000). Dance requires students to interpret, create, and communicate through movement, engaging cognitive, affective, and physical domains simultaneously.

## Core Content Areas

### Dance Styles and Genres

Teachers should expose students to diverse dance forms:

**Creative/Contemporary Dance**: Emphasis on original choreography, exploring themes and ideas through movement. This approach, influenced by Rudolf Laban's movement analysis framework, focuses on body, space, dynamics, and relationships.

**Cultural and Traditional Dance**: Including British folk dance, European forms, and world dance traditions. This addresses cultural literacy objectives and promotes inclusivity.

**Street Dance**: Hip-hop, breaking, popping, and locking. Particularly engaging for secondary students and addresses youth culture authenticity concerns raised by Kirk (2010).

**Social Dance**: Partner dances, ballroom, and contemporary social forms. Develops cooperation and communication skills.

**Musical Theatre Dance**: Jazz-based movement combining performance quality with storytelling.

### Movement Components (Laban Framework)

Rudolf Laban's movement analysis provides pedagogical structure:

**Body**: What moves (whole body, body parts, body shapes, body actions)
**Space**: Where movement occurs (levels, directions, pathways, size)
**Dynamics**: How movement is performed (time, weight, flow, space)
**Relationships**: With whom or what (solo, partner, group, objects, audience)

This framework, widely adopted in UK PE (as referenced in AfPE guidance), provides vocabulary for observation, feedback, and choreographic development.

### Choreographic Devices

Students should learn to manipulate movement through:
- Repetition and variation
- Canon and unison
- Climax and contrast
- Motif development
- Formation changes
- Use of stimulus (music, poetry, visual art, narrative)

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Teaching Models

**Spectrum of Teaching Styles** (Mosston & Ashworth, 2002): Dance education benefits from varied approaches:
- **Command and practice styles**: For teaching specific techniques and sequences
- **Reciprocal style**: Peer observation and feedback enhances performance quality
- **Self-check**: Developing independent evaluation skills
- **Guided discovery and divergent discovery**: Essential for creative choreography
- **Learner-designed programmes**: Advanced choreographic projects

**Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)** principles, promoted by Len Almond, can transfer to dance through problem-solving approaches where students address choreographic challenges rather than simply replicating teacher-led sequences.

### Inclusive Practice

Dance offers particular opportunities for inclusive PE, as Susan Capel notes in 'Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School' (Capel & Whitehead, 2019). Adaptations include:

- **Differentiation by outcome**: Open-ended tasks allowing varied responses
- **Physical adaptations**: Seated or supported dance for students with SEND
- **Cultural inclusivity**: Representing diverse dance traditions
- **Gender-neutral approaches**: Challenging stereotypes about "appropriate" dance participation
- **Assessment for learning**: Formative feedback emphasising individual progress

### Lesson Structure

Effective dance lessons typically include:

1. **Warm-up** (8-12 minutes): Pulse-raising activities, dynamic stretches, and movement exploration
2. **Skill development** (15-20 minutes): Teaching specific techniques, introducing movement vocabulary
3. **Creative application** (15-20 minutes): Choreographic tasks, rehearsal, refinement
4. **Performance and evaluation** (8-12 minutes): Sharing work, peer feedback, reflection
5. **Cool-down** (5 minutes): Static stretching, calming activities

## Assessment in Dance

### Formative Assessment

Continuous assessment through:
- **Teacher observation**: Using success criteria linked to learning objectives
- **Peer assessment**: Structured feedback using movement vocabulary
- **Self-reflection**: Video analysis, learning journals, verbal reflection

Assessment should address three key areas identified by the National Curriculum:
- **Developing performance**: Technical proficiency and performance quality
- **Evaluating and improving**: Analysis and refinement capabilities
- **Knowledge and understanding**: Movement concepts and contextual knowledge

### Summative Assessment

For KS3, schools design their own assessment frameworks, typically evaluating:
- Technical execution (control, coordination, accuracy)
- Performance quality (expression, dynamics, projection)
- Choreographic skills (originality, structure, effectiveness)
- Understanding (vocabulary, terminology, evaluation)

### GCSE Dance Assessment

Dance appears in PE GCSE specifications and as a separate GCSE subject:

**AQA GCSE Dance (8236)**: 
- 50% practical (performance and choreography)
- 50% written examination (anthology appreciation, choreography, critical appreciation)

**OCR GCSE Dance (J803)**:
- 60% practical (performance in two styles, choreography)
- 40% written examination (knowledge and understanding of choreography, performance, appreciation)

**Edexcel GCSE Dance (1DA0)**:
- Similar structure with professional work appreciation and practical components

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Dance**:
- Welsh examination board offering equivalent qualifications

### A-Level Dance

Available through AQA, OCR, and Edexcel, typically requiring:
- Solo and group performance (classical and contemporary techniques)
- Original choreography (solo or group composition)
- Written examination (critical appreciation of professional repertoire)

## Health and Safety Considerations

Dance-specific safety guidance from the Association for Physical Education (AfPE, 2020) includes:

**Physical Environment**:
- Appropriate flooring (sprung floors ideal; avoid slippery surfaces)
- Adequate space (minimum 2m² per student)
- Temperature control and ventilation
- Removal of hazards and clear boundaries

**Warm-up and Cool-down**:
- Essential for injury prevention
- Age-appropriate stretching protocols
- Gradual intensity increase/decrease

**Appropriate Clothing**:
- Footwear suitable for style (bare feet, jazz shoes, trainers)
- Clothing allowing movement observation
- Jewellery removal policies

**Medical Considerations**:
- Awareness of students with asthma, epilepsy, or other conditions
- Modified activities for students with injuries
- Consultation with parents/guardians regarding participation

## Research Evidence and Effective Practice

### Benefits of Dance Education

Research demonstrates multiple benefits:

**Physical Development**: Improved flexibility, strength, endurance, and coordination (Bailey et al., 2009, 'The Educational Benefits of Physical Education and School Sport'). Dance uniquely develops kinesthetic awareness and fine motor control.

**Cognitive Development**: Enhanced spatial reasoning, memory, and executive function. The creative problem-solving inherent in choreography develops higher-order thinking skills (Hanna, 2008).

**Social and Emotional Learning**: Collaborative choreography develops communication, cooperation, and empathy. Performance builds confidence and resilience (Connolly et al., 2011, 'The role of the PE teacher in promoting physical literacy').

**Cultural Understanding**: Exposure to diverse dance traditions promotes cultural awareness and challenges stereotypes (Risner & Stinson, 2010).

### Addressing Barriers

Research by Kirk and colleagues identifies barriers to dance participation:

**Gender Stereotyping**: Perception of dance as "feminine" reduces male participation, particularly at secondary level. Strategies include:
- Introducing street dance and culturally diverse forms
- Male role models and visiting artists
- Challenging stereotypes explicitly through curriculum content
- Mixed-gender groupings and assessment criteria emphasising individual progress

**Teacher Confidence**: Many non-specialist PE teachers lack dance training. Professional development should focus on:
- Movement vocabulary and observation skills
- Progression in choreographic complexity
- Assessment literacy in dance education
- Practical teaching strategies

**Curriculum Time**: Competing demands reduce dance provision. Schools should audit curriculum balance, ensuring statutory requirements are met and recognising dance's unique contribution to physical literacy.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning Schemes of Work

Effective dance curricula feature:

**Progression**: 
- KS1: Exploring basic actions, simple sequences
- KS2: Developing motifs, partner work, cultural forms
- KS3: Complex choreographic devices, performance projection, style-specific techniques
- KS4: Advanced composition, critical appreciation, technical proficiency

**Breadth**: Multiple dance styles across key stages
**Balance**: Creative and cultural dance experiences
**Coherence**: Clear learning sequences building skills systematically

### Teaching Strategies

**Demonstration**: Teacher and student demonstrations clarify expectations. Video resources supplement live demonstration.

**Scaffolding**: Provide structures for creative work:
- Stimuli (images, music, poetry, themes)
- Choreographic frameworks (ABA structure, narrative arc)
- Movement suggestions (action words, quality descriptors)
- Success criteria for self/peer assessment

**Differentiation**:
- **By task**: Different complexity levels for choreographic challenges
- **By outcome**: Open-ended tasks producing varied responses
- **By support**: Additional adult support, peer mentoring, simplified instructions
- **By resource**: Music selection, spatial arrangements, equipment

**Questioning**: Develop critical thinking through:
- Descriptive questions: "What actions did you observe?"
- Analytical questions: "How did the dynamics create contrast?"
- Evaluative questions: "Which choreographic device was most effective?"
- Creative questions: "How could you develop this motif?"

### Using Music and Accompaniment

Music selection significantly impacts engagement and learning:
- Age-appropriate and culturally relevant choices
- Varied tempos, styles, and structures
- Copyright compliance (schools should hold appropriate licences)
- Alternatives to music: percussion, voice, silence

### External Partnerships

Collaboration enhances provision:
- **Dance artists**: Professional choreographers and performers bring expertise
- **Cultural organisations**: Theatres, dance companies offer workshops and performance opportunities
- **Community groups**: Traditional dance groups, youth dance companies
- **Higher education**: Links with university dance departments provide resources and specialist input

Such partnerships address workforce development concerns raised by Penney (2008) regarding specialist knowledge in dance education.

## Cross-Curricular Links

Dance naturally connects with multiple subjects:

**Music**: Rhythm, structure, tempo, genre
**Drama**: Expression, character, narrative
**Art**: Visual stimuli, design elements, composition
**English**: Poetry, storytelling, literary themes
**History**: Historical periods, cultural movements
**PSHE**: Body image, relationships, cultural diversity
**Science**: Anatomy, physiology, physics of movement

The Youth Sport Trust and other organisations provide cross-curricular dance resources supporting whole-school literacy and numeracy objectives.

## Current Developments and Future Directions

### Digital Technology

Technology enhances dance education through:
- Video analysis apps (e.g., Coach's Eye, Hudl Technique) for feedback
- Choreographic software (DanceForms, MotionBuilder) for visualisation
- Digital portfolios documenting creative processes
- Virtual reality and motion capture exploring movement innovation

### Qualification Developments

Recent developments include:
- BTEC Sport options incorporating dance components
- Core PE programmes emphasising wellbeing and lifelong participation
- Dance leader awards (e.g., Sports Leaders UK) developing student leadership

### Policy Context

The government's 'School Sport and Activity Action Plan' (2019) emphasises breadth of activity. Dance contributes to 60-minute daily activity targets whilst offering alternatives to competitive sport.

AfPE's 'Health Position Paper' (2015) recognises dance's contribution to physical and mental health, social connection, and creative expression—aligning with broader wellbeing agendas in education.

## Conclusion

Dance in PE represents essential provision for holistic physical education. It uniquely develops physical literacy through creative expression, cultural engagement, and embodied learning. For teachers, dance requires pedagogical approaches balancing structured skill development with creative freedom, technical proficiency with personal interpretation.

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework positions dance as integral to developing confident, motivated, physically competent individuals. David Kirk's curricular analysis demonstrates that dance provides learning experiences unavailable in other activity areas. Dawn Penney's research emphasises inclusive pedagogy and meaningful engagement—principles particularly accessible through dance education.

Effective dance teaching requires subject knowledge, pedagogical skill, and commitment to inclusive practice. With appropriate planning, professional development, and creative approaches, all PE teachers can deliver high-quality dance education meeting National Curriculum requirements whilst inspiring lifelong participation in physical activity.
