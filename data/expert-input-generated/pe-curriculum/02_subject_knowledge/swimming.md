# Swimming and Water Safety

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Swimming and water safety represents one of the statutory requirements of the National Curriculum for Physical Education in England. The 2014 National Curriculum explicitly states that all schools must provide swimming instruction in either Key Stage 1 or Key Stage 2, with the expectation that all pupils should be able to swim competently, confidently and proficiently over a distance of at least 25 metres by the end of Key Stage 2. This statutory provision reflects the life-saving importance of aquatic competence and positions swimming as a fundamental component of physical literacy, a concept championed by Margaret Whitehead, who emphasises the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding necessary for a physically active life.

The Association for Physical Education (afPE) has consistently advocated for quality swimming provision, highlighting that swimming is not merely a curriculum requirement but an essential life skill. The tragic reality of drowning statistics in the UK—with the Royal Life Saving Society UK (RLSS UK) reporting approximately 400 accidental drowning deaths annually—underscores the critical importance of comprehensive swimming and water safety education.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2 Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Physical Education (DfE, 2013) stipulates that pupils should be taught to:

- Swim competently, confidently and proficiently over a distance of at least 25 metres
- Use a range of strokes effectively (for example, front crawl, backstroke and breaststroke)
- Perform safe self-rescue in different water-based situations

These attainment targets represent minimum expectations. The Swim England (formerly the Amateur Swimming Association) School Swimming Charter advocates for a more comprehensive approach, recommending that children should be able to perform safe entries into water, swim 25 metres unaided without stopping, and demonstrate basic water safety knowledge.

### Key Stage 3 and 4

Whilst swimming is not statutory beyond Key Stage 2, many secondary schools incorporate swimming within their PE curriculum. David Kirk's pedagogical models emphasise the importance of maintaining and extending aquatic competence throughout adolescence, particularly as this life stage often sees declining participation in swimming activities. GCSE PE specifications from AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas all include swimming as an optional practical activity, with assessment criteria covering technical proficiency, tactical understanding, and performance under competitive conditions.

## Fundamental Swimming Skills and Stroke Development

### Water Confidence and Familiarisation

Before formal stroke instruction, establishing water confidence is paramount. This progressive approach, endorsed by Swim England's Learn to Swim Framework, involves:

- Submersion and breath control
- Floating (prone and supine positions)
- Gliding and streamlining
- Movement through water using various body actions
- Entry and exit techniques

Research by Stallman et al. (2017) in the International Journal of Aquatic Research and Education emphasises that water competence extends beyond stroke technique to encompass water orientation, safety skills, and adaptability to different aquatic environments.

### Core Swimming Strokes

**Front Crawl**: The most efficient swimming stroke, characterised by alternating arm pulls, flutter kick, and rotary breathing. Technical focus includes body position (horizontal and streamlined), arm entry at shoulder width, high elbow catch, and rhythmic breathing pattern.

**Backstroke**: Performed supine, with alternating arm actions and flutter kick. Key teaching points include head position (looking upward with water at ear level), shoulder rotation, and straight-arm recovery.

**Breaststroke**: The most technically complex stroke, involving simultaneous and symmetrical arm and leg actions. The "pull, breathe, kick, glide" sequence requires careful coordination. The modern wave-action breaststroke, as opposed to the older flat-style technique, promotes greater efficiency.

**Butterfly**: Typically taught as an extension skill, involving simultaneous over-water arm recovery and dolphin kick. This stroke demands significant core strength and coordination.

### Starting and Turning Techniques

Competitive swimming components include dive starts (track and grab starts), tumble turns for front crawl and backstroke, and open turns for breaststroke and butterfly. Safety considerations dictate that diving instruction should only occur in appropriately deep water (minimum 1.8 metres) and under qualified supervision, as specified by Swim England guidance.

## Water Safety Education

### The Water Safety Code

The RLSS UK and the Royal Society for the Prevention of Accidents (RoSPA) promote comprehensive water safety education encompassing:

**Recognition of hazards**: Understanding risks in various aquatic environments (pools, rivers, lakes, coastal waters), including cold water shock, currents, tides, and submerged objects.

**Safe behaviour**: Never swimming alone, understanding personal limitations, avoiding alcohol before swimming, and respecting warning signs and flags.

**Emergency response**: The RLSS UK advocates teaching the "Reach, Throw, Don't Go" principle, emphasising that rescuers should use aids (branches, ropes, flotation devices) rather than entering water themselves.

### Self-Rescue Skills

Dawn Penney's work on curriculum design emphasises meaningful, context-relevant learning. Self-rescue instruction should include:

- **HELP position** (Heat Escape Lessening Posture): Minimising heat loss by drawing knees to chest
- **Treading water**: Maintaining position using sculling actions and gentle leg movements
- **Floating for survival**: Conserving energy whilst awaiting rescue
- **Swimming in clothing**: Understanding the impact of waterlogged garments on buoyancy and mobility
- **Cold water shock management**: Controlling breathing during initial immersion

The Swim England Survival Sequence provides a structured approach to teaching these competencies, progressing from controlled pool environments to simulated open-water scenarios.

## Pedagogical Approaches to Swimming Instruction

### Teaching Styles and Methods

Len Almond's advocacy for developmentally appropriate practice in PE applies particularly to aquatic education. The traditional command-style teaching approach, whilst maintaining safety through direct control, should be balanced with guided discovery and problem-solving methods that promote autonomous learning.

**Whole-Part-Whole Method**: Introducing the complete stroke, isolating specific components for refinement, then reintegrating elements. This approach aligns with constraints-led coaching, where task, environment, and individual constraints are manipulated to facilitate skill acquisition.

**Differentiation Strategies**: Susan Capel's work on inclusive PE practice highlights the necessity of adaptive teaching. In swimming contexts, this includes:

- Equipment variation (floats, kickboards, pull buoys, fins)
- Task modification (distance, stroke requirements, time constraints)
- Environmental adjustment (water depth, lane configuration, sensory considerations)
- Grouping strategies based on ability rather than age

### Assessment and Progression Frameworks

Swim England's Learn to Swim Framework provides a seven-stage progression pathway:

1. **Stage 1-2**: Water confidence, basic movement, and safety awareness
2. **Stage 3-4**: Development of stroke technique over increasing distances
3. **Stage 5-6**: Refinement of strokes, introduction of starts and turns
4. **Stage 7**: Competitive skills and advanced water safety

The Swim England Distance Awards (5m, 10m, 20m, 50m, 100m, etc.) provide structured achievement recognition. Many schools supplement these with internal assessment frameworks aligned to National Curriculum expectations.

### Lesson Structure and Pool Management

Effective swimming lessons typically follow this structure:

**Pre-entry routine**: Register, health checks, and safety briefing (including emergency procedures)

**Pool entry**: Controlled entry appropriate to water depth and pupil confidence

**Warm-up**: Gentle mobility exercises and preliminary skills (5-10 minutes)

**Main activity**: Focused skill development with differentiated tasks (25-30 minutes)

**Cool-down and exit**: Calmer activities and organised exit procedures (5 minutes)

The afPE publication *Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity* (2020 edition) provides comprehensive guidance on pupil-to-teacher ratios, lifeguard requirements, and emergency action plans specific to swimming provision.

## Challenges in Swimming Provision

### The 25-Metre Attainment Gap

Despite statutory requirements, many pupils fail to achieve the 25-metre competency target. The Amateur Swimming Association's Swimming in Schools report (2018) identified that approximately 30-40% of primary school leavers cannot swim the statutory minimum distance. Contributing factors include:

- **Curriculum time allocation**: Many schools provide only one term of swimming instruction, insufficient for skill acquisition
- **Transport and facility access**: Cost and logistics of accessing suitable pools
- **Teacher confidence**: Primary generalist teachers often lack aquatic-specific pedagogy training
- **Parental engagement**: Limited reinforcement of skills outside school provision

### Inclusion and Cultural Sensitivity

David Kirk's research on equity in PE highlights particular challenges in swimming participation. Some pupils face barriers including:

- **Cultural and religious considerations**: Modest swimwear requirements, single-sex provision preferences
- **Socioeconomic factors**: Access to appropriate swimwear and family swimming experiences
- **Special educational needs**: Physical disabilities, sensory sensitivities, or anxiety disorders requiring specialist support

The Equality Act 2010 requires schools to make reasonable adjustments. Strategies include providing private changing facilities, offering single-sex sessions, sourcing adaptive equipment (pool hoists, flotation devices), and training staff in disability swimming instruction techniques.

### Facility and Resource Considerations

Many UK schools lack on-site pools, necessitating partnerships with local authority or commercial facilities. This introduces logistical and financial challenges. The Sport England Facilities Planning Model provides guidance on facility requirements, but school budgets increasingly constrain swimming provision. Some multi-academy trusts have adopted cluster approaches, sharing facility access and specialist instruction across multiple schools.

## Open Water Swimming and Natural Environments

### Outdoor Swimming Safety

The growing popularity of open water swimming, triathlon, and outdoor education programmes requires specific safety knowledge. The RLSS UK and Swim England Open Water Committee provide guidance on:

- **Environmental hazards**: Cold water (temperatures below 15°C), currents, tides, underwater obstacles, and wildlife
- **Equipment requirements**: Brightly coloured swim caps, wetsuits, tow floats
- **Supervision protocols**: Qualified open water safety personnel, emergency evacuation procedures

### Curriculum Integration

Outdoor and adventurous activities (OAA), a National Curriculum requirement, can incorporate open water experiences. However, these must adhere to stringent safety protocols outlined in the Outdoor Education Advisers' Panel (OEAP) National Guidance, including specific leader qualifications (e.g., RLSS UK Open Water Lifeguard, Swim England Open Water Coaching awards).

## Examination Specifications and Academic Study

### GCSE Physical Education

Swimming features as an optional practical activity across all major exam boards:

**AQA (8582)**: Assesses swimming over 100m individual medley or four strokes (25m each), with evaluation of technique, tactics, and performance analysis.

**Edexcel (1PE0)**: Requires demonstration of strokes, starts, turns, and racing skills, assessed against level-specific criteria.

**OCR (J587)**: Includes swimming within Activity 1 options, evaluating technical proficiency and competitive performance.

**WJEC Eduqas**: Integrates swimming within practical assessment, emphasising stroke efficiency and tactical application.

### A-Level Physical Education

Swimming biomechanics, physiology, and psychology feature in theory components:

- **Biomechanical analysis**: Application of Newton's Laws, fluid dynamics, drag reduction through streamlining
- **Physiological demands**: Aerobic and anaerobic energy systems, thermoregulation in water, respiratory adaptations
- **Psychological factors**: Anxiety management, performance pressure in competitive swimming

## Professional Development and Teacher Competence

### Qualification Requirements

The afPE recommends that teachers delivering swimming instruction hold:

- **Minimum requirement**: Swim England Teacher Award (formerly ASA Level 1)
- **Enhanced provision**: Swim England Teaching Swimming (Level 2) or UK Coaching Certificate in Swimming

Primary generalist teachers benefit from CPD provided by Swim England, local authority advisory services, or commercial swim school partnerships. The National College for Teaching and Leadership does not mandate specific swimming qualifications for initial teacher training, representing a gap identified by swimming sector stakeholders.

### Continuing Professional Development

Effective CPD should address:

- **Technical knowledge**: Stroke analysis, fault correction, progression pathways
- **Pedagogical skills**: Differentiation, assessment for learning, inclusive practice
- **Safety competence**: Emergency action plans, rescue techniques, risk assessment

The Youth Sport Trust and afPE offer networks and resources supporting swimming provision, whilst Swim England's regional development teams provide school-facing support.

## Future Directions and Policy Considerations

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework emphasises lifelong engagement with physical activity. Swimming, as a low-impact, accessible activity across the lifespan, represents an ideal vehicle for developing physical literacy. However, systemic challenges require policy-level intervention:

- **Primary curriculum time**: Extending swimming provision beyond single-term blocks
- **Secondary continuation**: Maintaining aquatic competence through Key Stages 3-5
- **Facility investment**: Addressing the decline in school pools through capital funding
- **Workforce development**: Embedding aquatic pedagogy within initial teacher education

The All-Party Parliamentary Group on Swimming's reports have consistently advocated for these measures, recognising swimming's unique contribution to health, safety, and wellbeing. As Dawn Penney's curriculum theory emphasises, meaningful PE experiences must connect to students' lives beyond school; swimming's dual role as recreation and survival skill exemplifies this principle.

## Conclusion

Swimming and water safety education represents a non-negotiable component of quality Physical Education provision. The statutory nature of swimming in the National Curriculum reflects its unique status as both a physical competence and life-saving imperative. Effective delivery requires qualified, confident teachers; adequate curriculum time; appropriate facilities; and pedagogical approaches that promote genuine skill acquisition rather than mere curriculum compliance. By embracing the principles of physical literacy advocated by Margaret Whitehead and the inclusive, learner-centred pedagogies championed by Kirk, Penney, and Capel, schools can ensure that all young people develop the aquatic competence necessary for safe, confident, and lifelong engagement with water-based activities.
