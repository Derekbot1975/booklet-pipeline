# Gymnastics

## Introduction to Gymnastics in UK Physical Education

Gymnastics forms a statutory component of the National Curriculum for Physical Education in England, appearing within the "Gymnastics Activities" category. The subject develops fundamental movement competencies, body management skills, and physical literacy – concepts strongly advocated by Margaret Whitehead through her seminal work on physical literacy as the foundation for lifelong physical activity. Gymnastics uniquely contributes to children's understanding of their bodies in space, developing what Whitehead describes as "motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life."

David Kirk has emphasised the pedagogical importance of gymnastics in developing children's movement vocabulary and spatial awareness, arguing that gymnastics provides essential foundations for broader physical education experiences. Dawn Penney's research on curriculum implementation highlights gymnastics as a critical site for developing inclusive practice, where differentiation and progression must be carefully constructed to accommodate diverse learner needs.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum for Physical Education (DfE, 2013) states that pupils should develop fundamental movement skills and become increasingly competent and confident to access a broad range of opportunities to extend their agility, balance and coordination. Specifically for gymnastics:

- Pupils should master basic movements including running, jumping, throwing and catching
- They should develop balance, agility and co-ordination, and begin to apply these in a range of activities
- Master basic body management skills including rolls, jumps, balances and travelling actions
- Develop awareness of space, levels, directions, pathways and body shapes

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

At KS2, the curriculum requires pupils to:

- Develop flexibility, strength, technique, control and balance through gymnastics
- Compare their performances with previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve personal bests
- Create and perform movement sequences both individually and with partners
- Develop increasingly complex combinations of actions including counterbalance and counter-tension
- Apply compositional principles including canon, unison, matching and mirroring

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The KS3 curriculum expects students to:

- Continue to develop their technique and improve their performance in gymnastics
- Take part in competitive gymnastics (where appropriate) and evaluate and recognise their own success
- Develop more complex sequences incorporating partner work, group work and apparatus
- Understand the biomechanical principles underlying gymnastic movements

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At KS4, gymnastics may form part of core PE provision or appear within examination specifications. Students should continue developing their performance whilst also developing analytical and evaluative capabilities.

## Fundamental Gymnastic Actions and Themes

### Core Actions

British Gymnastics, the national governing body, identifies fundamental gymnastics actions that align with educational gymnastics frameworks developed by pioneers such as Len Almond:

**Travelling Actions:**
- Walking, running, hopping, skipping, galloping, sliding, crawling, rolling

**Static Positions:**
- Balances on different body parts (1-5 points of contact)
- Inverted positions (shoulder stands, headstands, handstands with appropriate progressions)
- Arch, tuck, pike, straddle and twisted body shapes

**Rotational Actions:**
- Forward rolls, backward rolls, teddy bear rolls, log rolls
- Cartwheels and round-offs (at appropriate developmental stages)

**Flight Actions:**
- Jumps incorporating different take-off and landing patterns
- Vault-type activities appropriate to age and development
- Leaps with various body shapes in flight

**Swinging and Circling Actions:**
- Swinging on apparatus (ropes, bars, rings where available)
- Circling actions around bars or using body parts as pivots

### Educational Gymnastics Themes

Drawing on the work of Rudolf Laban and developed for British contexts, educational gymnastics is organised around movement themes:

**Body Awareness:** What the body can do (curl, stretch, twist, turn, balance)

**Space Awareness:** Where the body moves (levels, directions, pathways, extensions)

**Effort Awareness:** How the body moves (speed, force, flow)

**Relationship Awareness:** With whom or what the body moves (partners, groups, apparatus)

## Progression in Gymnastics

Susan Capel's work on learning to teach PE emphasises the importance of systematic progression. In gymnastics, progression operates across several dimensions:

### Skill Progression

1. **Simple to Complex:** Single actions → combinations → sequences
2. **Supported to Independent:** Teacher/peer support → independent performance
3. **Floor to Apparatus:** Master on floor → transfer to low apparatus → progress to higher apparatus
4. **Symmetrical to Asymmetrical:** Matching movements → contrasting movements

### Apparatus Progression

- Large apparatus (mats, benches, boxes, bars, beams) introduced systematically
- Small apparatus (balls, ribbons, hoops) may be incorporated for rhythmic gymnastics elements
- Safety protocols must be rigorously maintained at all stages

## Safety Considerations and Risk Management

The Association for Physical Education (afPE) publishes "Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity" (2020 edition), which provides comprehensive guidance for gymnastics teaching. Key principles include:

### Equipment Safety
- Regular inspection and maintenance of all apparatus
- Appropriate matting for all activities involving height or rotation
- Correct storage and positioning of equipment
- Student involvement in safe equipment handling under supervision

### Pedagogical Safety
- Appropriate warm-up emphasising wrist, ankle and core preparation
- Progressive skill development with no "skipping steps"
- Spotting techniques taught and implemented appropriately
- Clear differentiation to ensure challenge without excessive risk
- Awareness of students' medical conditions and previous injuries

### Safeguarding
- Clear lines of sight maintained at all times
- Appropriate changing and supervision arrangements
- Professional boundaries maintained when providing physical support

## Inclusive Practice in Gymnastics

Dawn Penney's research emphasises that inclusion requires proactive curriculum design rather than reactive adaptation. In gymnastics:

### STEP Framework (Youth Sport Trust)

**Space:** Modify working areas, apparatus height and arrangement
**Task:** Alter complexity, duration or composition requirements
**Equipment:** Use different sizes of mats, varied apparatus heights
**People:** Organise groupings strategically, use peer support

### SEND Considerations

- Gymnastics offers excellent opportunities for students with movement difficulties to experience success
- Visual demonstrations, peer modelling and task cards support diverse learners
- Wheelchair gymnastics and adapted apparatus enable full participation
- Close liaison with specialist staff ensures appropriate provision

### Gender and Cultural Sensitivity

- Sensitivity to cultural requirements regarding physical contact and clothing
- Challenge gender stereotypes about gymnastics as a "female" activity
- Provide diverse role models including male and female gymnasts
- Ensure language and examples are inclusive and non-stereotyped

## Assessment in Gymnastics

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Assessment should align with David Kirk's emphasis on meaningful engagement and skill development:

- Observation of specific movement criteria
- Peer assessment using structured frameworks
- Video analysis and self-reflection
- Target-setting linked to personal improvement
- Skill cards or "gymnastics passports" tracking progressive achievement

### Summative Assessment

At examination level, gymnastics features in GCSE and A-Level PE specifications:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582):**
- Practical performance in gymnastics as one performance activity
- Assessment of technical skills, compositional quality and performance quality
- Analysis and evaluation of performance through video analysis

**Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0):**
- Similar practical performance assessment framework
- Integration with anatomy, biomechanics and movement analysis theory

**OCR GCSE PE (J587):**
- Core knowledge of movement classification and biomechanical principles applied to gymnastics
- Practical assessment incorporating individual and paired elements

**WJEC GCSE PE:**
- Assessment of performance and compositional abilities
- Analysis of factors affecting performance

### Assessment Criteria

Typically include:
- Technical proficiency and execution
- Compositional originality and variety
- Aesthetic quality and presentation
- Difficulty level appropriate to performer
- Consistency and control throughout performance

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Teaching Styles

Drawing on Len Almond's work on developmentally appropriate practice and Mosston and Ashworth's Spectrum of Teaching Styles:

**Command Style:** Appropriate for safety instructions and specific skill instruction

**Practice Style:** Students practice prescribed skills with teacher feedback

**Reciprocal Style:** Students work in pairs with criteria sheets providing peer feedback

**Self-Check Style:** Students assess own performance against criteria

**Guided Discovery:** Teacher guides students to discover optimal techniques through questioning

**Divergent Production:** Students create unique solutions to movement challenges – particularly relevant in educational gymnastics

### Task Design

Effective gymnastics tasks should:

- Be developmentally appropriate and progressively challenging
- Offer choice and creativity within safe parameters
- Include clear success criteria understood by students
- Enable multiple attempts and practice opportunities
- Facilitate transfer of learning across contexts

## Links to Physical Literacy

Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy is exemplified through gymnastics teaching that develops:

**Physical Competence:** Fundamental movement skills, body management, spatial awareness

**Confidence and Motivation:** Success in achievable challenges, visible progression, inclusive practice

**Knowledge and Understanding:** Anatomical awareness, movement vocabulary, compositional principles

**Engagement in Activity:** Foundation skills applicable to dance, sport, recreation and lifelong physical activity

## Cross-Curricular Links

Gymnastics offers rich opportunities for cross-curricular learning:

**Mathematics:** Symmetry, angles, rotation, sequencing, counting, patterns

**Science:** Forces, balance, levers, momentum, Newton's laws

**English:** Movement vocabulary, descriptive writing, peer feedback communication

**PSHE:** Risk management, perseverance, supporting others, body confidence

**Arts:** Aesthetic awareness, composition, creativity, performance

## Contemporary Issues and Research

### Competition vs. Participation Debate

David Kirk's research raises questions about competitive sport models in PE. In gymnastics, teachers must balance:

- Skill development for all vs. elite pathway development
- British Gymnastics competition frameworks vs. inclusive educational gymnastics
- Performance outcomes vs. process-focused learning

### Curriculum Time Allocation

Research by the Youth Sport Trust indicates gymnastics provision varies significantly between schools. Dawn Penney's curriculum analysis suggests gymnastics is sometimes marginalised in favour of team games, despite its statutory curriculum status and unique contribution to movement development.

### Teacher Confidence and CPD

Susan Capel's research on PE teacher education identifies gymnastics as an area where many teachers lack confidence. Effective CPD should address:

- Technical subject knowledge of gymnastics skills and progressions
- Pedagogical approaches appropriate to educational gymnastics
- Safety management and risk assessment procedures
- Observation and assessment skills specific to gymnastics

## Conclusion

Gymnastics remains a vital component of UK Physical Education, offering unique opportunities to develop physical literacy, fundamental movement competencies and aesthetic appreciation. Effective gymnastics teaching requires secure subject knowledge, appropriate pedagogical approaches, rigorous safety management and inclusive practice that enables all students to experience success and progression. When taught well, gymnastics provides foundations for lifelong physical activity and embodies the principles articulated by leading PE thinkers including Whitehead, Kirk, Penney, Almond and Capel.
