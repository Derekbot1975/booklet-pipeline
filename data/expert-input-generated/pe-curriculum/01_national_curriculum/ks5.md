# PE at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview of Key Stage 5 Physical Education

Key Stage 5 Physical Education represents the culmination of statutory PE provision in England and Wales, though students choosing A Level PE engage with a substantially different academic discipline. Unlike Key Stages 3 and 4, KS5 is not defined by National Curriculum requirements; instead, students typically follow either examination specifications (A Level or BTEC qualifications) or continue with enrichment PE programmes. A Level Physical Education combines theoretical study with practical performance, requiring students to demonstrate both academic rigour and physical competence.

The specification content draws heavily on exercise physiology, biomechanics, sport psychology, and socio-cultural studies of sport and physical activity. As David Kirk (2010) argues in *Physical Education Futures*, post-16 PE must move beyond performance-focused approaches to embrace critical engagement with sport as a social and cultural phenomenon.

## Examination Specifications and Awarding Bodies

### AQA A Level Physical Education (7582)

AQA's specification, the most widely taught in England, divides content into two examined papers and one non-examined assessment:

**Paper 1: Factors affecting participation in physical activity and sport**
- Applied anatomy and physiology (cardiovascular, respiratory, neuromuscular systems)
- Skill acquisition (information processing, memory models, motor programmes)
- Sport psychology (arousal theories, aggression, attribution theory, social facilitation)

**Paper 2: Factors affecting optimal performance in physical activity and sport**
- Exercise physiology and biomechanics (energy systems, linear and angular motion, fluid mechanics)
- Sport and society (historical and contemporary issues, commercialisation, ethics, technology)
- The role of technology in physical activity and sport

**Non-Examined Assessment (NEA)**
- Students assessed as performer or coach in one physical activity
- Analysis and evaluation of performance (AEP) coursework linking theory to practice

### OCR A Level Physical Education (H555)

OCR's specification offers similar content but with distinct emphases:

**Paper 1: Physiological factors affecting performance**
- Applied anatomy and physiology
- Exercise physiology (energy systems, recovery, altitude training)

**Paper 2: Psychological factors affecting performance**
- Skill acquisition
- Sport psychology (personality, motivation, stress management)

**Paper 3: Socio-cultural issues in physical activity and sport**
- Sport and society (sport in pre-industrial, industrial and post-industrial Britain)
- Contemporary issues (ethics, violence, drugs, technology)
- Sport and society and the role of technology in physical activity and sport

**NEA: Performance and Evaluation**
- Practical performance in one activity
- Evaluation and Analysis of Performance (EAEP) linking theoretical knowledge

### Pearson Edexcel A Level Physical Education (9PE0)

Edexcel structures content across two papers with integrated NEA:

**Paper 1: Factors affecting participation in physical activity and sport**
- Applied anatomy and physiology
- Skill acquisition
- Sport psychology
- Sport and society

**Paper 2: Factors affecting optimal performance in physical activity and sport**
- Biomechanical movement
- Sport and society and technology in sport
- Exercise physiology

**NEA: Practical performance in physical activity and sport**
- Performance in one activity from approved list
- Analysis of performance

## Theoretical Content: Key Concepts and Pedagogical Approaches

### Applied Anatomy and Exercise Physiology

Students must develop sophisticated understanding of human biological systems during exercise. This includes:

- **Cardiorespiratory function**: cardiac cycle, stroke volume, cardiac output, oxygen transport, spirometry, tidal volume, minute ventilation
- **Neuromuscular systems**: muscle fibre types (Type I, Type IIa, Type IIx), sliding filament theory, motor units, all-or-none law
- **Energy systems**: ATP-PC system, glycolytic system, aerobic system, energy continuum, lactate sampling, VO₂ max testing
- **Training adaptations**: cardiac hypertrophy, increased capillarisation, mitochondrial density, lactate tolerance

**Practical implications for teachers**: Link theoretical content to practical activities students know. When teaching energy systems, use specific sporting examples (100m sprint for ATP-PC, 400m for glycolytic, marathon for aerobic). Incorporate practical laboratory sessions where facilities permit—lactate testing, spirometry, heart rate monitoring during exercise protocols.

Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy, whilst primarily applied to younger learners, remains relevant here. Understanding physiological principles enables students to make informed decisions about their own physical development throughout life.

### Biomechanics

Students analyse movement using Newtonian mechanics:

- **Linear motion**: distance, displacement, speed, velocity, acceleration, Newton's laws of motion
- **Angular motion**: moment of inertia, angular velocity, conservation of angular momentum
- **Projectile motion**: flight paths, optimum angles of release, factors affecting projectiles
- **Fluid mechanics**: drag, lift, Bernoulli principle, boundary layer, streamlining

**Teaching approach**: Use video analysis software (Dartfish, Quintic, Coach's Eye) to analyse performances. Students can calculate velocities, identify force applications, and suggest biomechanical improvements to technique. Link to students' own practical performances in NEA activities.

### Skill Acquisition

This area synthesises cognitive psychology and motor learning theory:

- **Information processing models**: Welford's model, Whiting's model, display, effector mechanism, feedback loop
- **Memory systems**: short-term sensory store, short-term memory, long-term memory, selective attention
- **Motor programmes**: executive motor programmes, subroutines, schema theory (Schmidt, 1975), open-loop and closed-loop control
- **Learning theories**: operant conditioning (Skinner), observational learning (Bandura's social learning theory), cognitive learning, Gestalt theory
- **Transfer of learning**: positive, negative, bilateral, proactive, retroactive
- **Practice structures**: massed, distributed, fixed, varied, whole, part, progressive part

Dawn Penney's work on curriculum futures emphasises the importance of students understanding *how* they learn physical skills, not merely performing them. A Level PE should develop metacognitive awareness of learning processes.

**Practical implications**: Design practical sessions demonstrating skill acquisition principles. Show students variable practice schedules, guidance methods (visual, verbal, manual, mechanical), and feedback timing (immediate, delayed, terminal, concurrent). Encourage students to coach younger students, applying learning theories practically.

### Sport Psychology

Students explore psychological factors affecting performance:

- **Personality theories**: trait theory (Eysenck), social learning theory, interactionist approach
- **Arousal and anxiety**: drive theory, inverted-U hypothesis, catastrophe theory (Hardy & Fazey, 1987), zones of optimal functioning (Hanin)
- **Motivation**: intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, achievement motivation theory (Atkinson & McClelland), achievement goal theory
- **Stress management**: cognitive and somatic techniques, imagery, positive self-talk, biofeedback, centering
- **Aggression**: instinct theory, social learning theory, frustration-aggression hypothesis
- **Attribution theory**: Weiner's model, locus of causality, stability, controllability, learned helplessness
- **Social facilitation**: Zajonc's model, evaluation apprehension, home advantage
- **Group dynamics**: cohesion, Steiner's model of group productivity, social loafing, group formation (Tuckman)
- **Leadership**: trait approach, behavioural approach, Chelladurai's multi-dimensional model

**Teaching considerations**: Use contemporary sporting examples—elite athlete interviews discussing psychological preparation, documented cases of choking under pressure, analysis of team cohesion in successful squads. Students should apply psychological theories to their own performance in NEA activities.

### Sport and Society

This content area reflects David Kirk's advocacy for critical pedagogies in PE that enable students to understand sport as a contested social institution. Students examine:

- **Historical development of sport**: popular recreation (pre-1780), rational recreation (post-1850), public school athleticism, development of National Governing Bodies
- **Social and cultural factors**: class, gender, ethnicity, disability, age affecting participation
- **Commercialisation**: media influence, sponsorship, advertising, commodification of sport
- **Ethical issues**: drugs and doping (WADA code, biological passport), violence in sport (player violence, spectator violence), gamesmanship versus sportsmanship
- **Technology in sport**: performance enhancement (altitude training, cryotherapy, hypoxic tents), analysis (GPS tracking, Hawkeye, VAR), equipment developments, ethical considerations
- **UK Sport and Sport England**: talent identification, World Class Performance Programmes, Active Lives survey

Len Almond's work on health-focused physical education connects here—students should understand the broader social determinants of physical activity participation, not merely individual motivation.

**Practical implications**: Encourage critical analysis of contemporary sporting issues through debates, case studies, and extended writing. Use recent Olympic Games to analyse commercialisation, technology, and ethical issues. Students could investigate participation barriers in their local communities, applying theoretical understanding to real contexts.

## Non-Examined Assessment (NEA): Practical Performance

### Performance Requirements

All specifications require students to demonstrate practical competence in one activity as either performer or coach. Assessment typically constitutes 15-30% of total A Level grade.

**AQA requirements**:
- One activity from approved list (team games, individual sports, outdoor activities)
- Assessed in competitive/authentic context
- Skills assessed under pressure with appropriate decision-making
- Moderated through visiting moderation or video evidence

**Performance standards**: Students assessed across skill acquisition, technical execution, tactical understanding, and competitive application. Assessment criteria typically differentiate between:
- **Basic level (0-10 marks)**: Limited technical competence, poor decision-making
- **Moderate level (11-20 marks)**: Sound technical ability, appropriate tactical choices
- **High level (21-30 marks)**: Excellent technique under pressure, sophisticated tactical understanding

**Practical implications**: Teachers must maintain awareness of approved activity lists and specific assessment criteria. Video evidence requirements demand high-quality recording showing full context of performance. Students benefit from performing in activities where authentic competitive situations exist—school fixtures, club competitions, athletics meetings.

Susan Capel's research on trainee teacher development in PE emphasises the importance of understanding assessment processes. Teachers must apply consistent standards whilst recognising diverse activity contexts.

### Analysis and Evaluation of Performance (AEP/EAEP)

Students produce written/verbal analysis linking theoretical knowledge to practical performance. This typically involves:

- **Identifying strengths and weaknesses**: Specific technical/tactical elements requiring improvement
- **Applying theoretical knowledge**: Linking identified weaknesses to specification content (biomechanics, physiology, psychology, skill acquisition)
- **Planning improvement**: Designing training programmes incorporating periodisation, progressive overload, FITT principles
- **Justifying decisions**: Explaining *why* specific practices/interventions improve performance

**Example**: A basketball player identifies weak free-throw percentage. Analysis might incorporate:
- **Biomechanical analysis**: Insufficient knee flexion reducing force generation, inconsistent release angle
- **Skill acquisition**: Need for fixed practice to develop motor programme consistency, use of mental practice/imagery
- - **Sport psychology**: Performance anxiety during pressure situations, application of progressive muscular relaxation and positive self-talk
- **Training programme**: Massed practice sessions with varied pressure conditions, use of video feedback (augmented information)

**Teaching approach**: Scaffold AEP writing through structured templates initially. Model high-quality analysis using exemplar performances. Provide formative feedback on draft analyses. Encourage students to use specification content terminology precisely.

## Curriculum Planning and Delivery

### Balancing Theory and Practice

A Level PE demands sophisticated integration of academic study and practical engagement. Teachers face challenges balancing:

- **Curriculum time allocation**: Typically 5 hours weekly contact time, split between classroom theory and practical sessions
- **Practical activity breadth**: NEA requires depth in one activity, but enrichment value of broader practical participation
- **Examination pressure**: Theory papers constitute 70-85% of final grade, potentially marginalising practical work

David Kirk argues against narrow, performance-focused curricula that privilege examination results over holistic physical education. However, accountability pressures and university entrance requirements mean teachers must achieve examination success whilst maintaining meaningful practical engagement.

**Recommended approach**:
- Dedicate approximately 60-70% curriculum time to theory teaching
- Maintain weekly practical sessions where students engage in NEA activity plus broader enrichment
- Integrate theory within practical sessions—teach biomechanics during technique development, psychology during competitive situations
- Use practical experiences as case studies for theory application

### Differentiation and Inclusion

A Level PE attracts students with diverse practical abilities and academic backgrounds. Differentiation strategies include:

- **Tiered practical assessments**: Students select activities matching their competence level
- **Coaching alternative**: Students unable to perform at required level can assess as coaches
- **Academic support structures**: Additional literacy support for extended writing, visual learning resources for complex physiological concepts
- **Stretch and challenge**: Extension activities for higher-attaining students—reading primary research literature, analysing professional performances

Margaret Whitehead's inclusive vision of physical literacy emphasises that all students can develop physical competence appropriate to their potential. A Level PE should not exclude students with physical impairments or lower sporting proficiency.

### Assessment and Feedback

**Formative assessment strategies**:
- Regular knowledge-retrieval practice using flashcards, low-stakes quizzes, spaced repetition
- Extended writing practice with examiner-style feedback
- Peer assessment using mark schemes to develop assessment literacy
- Video analysis of practical performances with self-reflection

**Summative assessment**:
- Mock examinations following specification format and timing
- Practical moderation events replicating final assessment conditions
- Marked AEP drafts with specific improvement targets

**Feedback principles**: Research by Wiliam and Black on formative assessment applies equally to A Level PE. Effective feedback should:
- Identify specific strengths and misconceptions
- Provide actionable next steps
- Encourage student self-regulation and metacognition
- Focus on progress towards specification requirements rather than comparison with peers

## Progression and Career Pathways

### University Progression

A Level PE facilitates entry to various degree programmes:

- **Sport and Exercise Science**: Physiotherapy, sports therapy, strength and conditioning
- **Sport Studies/PE degrees**: Secondary PE teaching (PGCE route), sports development, sports coaching
- **Sport Management/Business**: Sports marketing, event management, facility management
- **Psychology**: Sport and exercise psychology postgraduate training

Universities value A Level PE's combination of scientific knowledge and practical application. Russell Group institutions increasingly accept PE alongside traditional academic subjects, recognising rigorous physiological and biomechanical content.

### Vocational Pathways

Students not pursuing university can progress to:

- **Apprenticeships**: Personal training, sports coaching, outdoor education
- **Employment**: Leisure centre operations, gym instruction, community sports development
- **Elite performance**: Professional sport (recognising this applies to very small percentage), sports academies

### Teacher Knowledge Requirements

Teachers delivering A Level PE require:

- **Academic qualifications**: Degree-level study in sport science, physical education, or related discipline. Many successful A Level PE teachers hold MSc qualifications in exercise physiology, biomechanics, or sport psychology
- **Specification knowledge**: Detailed understanding of examination requirements, mark schemes, assessment criteria
- **Pedagogical expertise**: Ability to teach complex scientific concepts accessibly whilst maintaining academic rigour
- **Practical credibility**: Continued engagement with physical activity and sport, though not necessarily elite performance level
- **Assessment literacy**: Understanding of moderation processes, standardisation requirements, NEA marking criteria

Susan Capel's research highlights that effective PE teachers combine subject knowledge with pedagogical content knowledge—knowing *how* to teach specific content, not merely possessing content knowledge.

## Contemporary Issues and Future Directions

### Technology Integration

Digital technologies increasingly enhance A Level PE delivery:

- **Video analysis software**: Real-time feedback on practical performances
- **Virtual learning environments**: Specification content delivery via platforms like Google Classroom, Microsoft Teams
- **Wearable technology**: Heart rate monitors, GPS trackers providing data for physiological analysis
- **3D motion capture**: Sophisticated biomechanical analysis in well-resourced departments

However, Dawn Penney warns against technological determinism—technology should enhance pedagogical practice, not drive it. Teachers must critically evaluate digital tools' educational value.

### Research-Informed Practice

A Level PE should expose students to contemporary research methodologies and findings. This includes:

- Interpreting research papers on training adaptations, psychological interventions, biomechanical analyses
- Understanding research design—experimental studies, longitudinal research, qualitative investigations
- Evaluating evidence quality and applicability to sporting practice
- Recognising that sport science knowledge evolves—current understanding may be refined through future research

### Equity and Participation

Despite increased accessibility of A Level PE, participation patterns reveal inequities:

- **Gender imbalance**: Male students disproportionately represented in many schools
- **Cultural capital**: Students from families with sporting backgrounds more likely to select PE
- **Resource implications**: Schools with superior facilities, coaching expertise, competitive opportunities better placed to support high NEA achievement
