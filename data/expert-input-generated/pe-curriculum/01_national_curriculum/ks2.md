# PE at Key Stage 2

## Overview and Statutory Requirements

Physical Education at Key Stage 2 forms a crucial developmental period for children aged 7-11 years. The **National Curriculum for Physical Education in England (DfE, 2013)** establishes the statutory framework that all maintained schools must follow. Key Stage 2 builds upon the foundation movement skills developed in Key Stage 1, progressing towards more complex movement patterns, competitive situations, and sophisticated tactical awareness.

According to the National Curriculum, pupils should continue to apply and develop a broader range of skills, learning how to use them in different ways and to link them to make actions and sequences of movement. They should enjoy communicating, collaborating and competing with each other whilst developing an understanding of how to improve in different physical activities and sports.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Statutory Areas of Activity

The Programme of Study for Key Stage 2 (DfE, 2013) mandates that pupils should be taught to:

- **Use running, jumping, throwing and catching in isolation and in combination**
- **Play competitive games**, modified where appropriate, including football, netball, rounders, cricket, hockey, basketball, badminton and tennis, applying basic principles suitable for attacking and defending
- **Develop flexibility, strength, technique, control and balance** through gymnastics and athletics
- **Perform dances** using a range of movement patterns
- **Take part in outdoor and adventurous activity** challenges both individually and within a team
- **Compare their performances with previous ones and demonstrate improvement** to achieve their personal best

Additionally, **swimming and water safety** is statutory at Key Stage 2. All schools must provide swimming instruction either in Key Stage 1 or Key Stage 2. By the end of Key Stage 2, pupils should be able to:
- Swim competently, confidently and proficiently over a distance of at least 25 metres
- Use a range of strokes effectively (e.g., front crawl, backstroke and breaststroke)
- Perform safe self-rescue in different water-based situations

## Developmental Considerations

### Physical Development at Ages 7-11

Margaret Whitehead's concept of **physical literacy** is particularly relevant at Key Stage 2. In her seminal work *Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifespan* (2010), Whitehead emphasises that this age phase represents a critical window for developing movement competence, confidence and motivation to engage in physical activity. Children at this stage are refining fundamental movement skills and beginning to apply them in increasingly complex contexts.

**David Kirk** (2010) in *Physical Education Futures* argues that Key Stage 2 represents a "teachable moment" where children are developmentally ready to grasp tactical concepts, understand rules systems, and develop the cooperative skills necessary for team games. Kirk emphasises that this stage should focus on **meaningful participation** rather than elite performance models.

### Cognitive and Social Development

At Key Stage 2, children develop:
- **Increased attention span** enabling longer activity sequences and game play
- **Enhanced problem-solving abilities** supporting tactical understanding
- **Greater social awareness** facilitating cooperative learning and teamwork
- **Developing self-regulation** allowing for improved behaviour management and personal responsibility

**Len Almond**, instrumental in developing the Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) approach, identified Key Stage 2 as optimal for introducing game appreciation and tactical awareness (Almond, 1986). His work suggests that children at this stage benefit from modified games that emphasise decision-making over technical perfection.

## Progression Throughout Key Stage 2

### Lower Key Stage 2 (Years 3-4)

**Movement Skills Development:**
- Consolidation of fundamental movement skills with improved quality and consistency
- Introduction to basic sport-specific techniques (e.g., chest pass in netball, dribbling in football)
- Development of simple sequences in gymnastics (3-4 movements)
- Basic dance motifs and short choreographed sequences

**Tactical Understanding:**
- Grasp basic principles of attack (creating and using space)
- Understand basic defending concepts (marking, intercepting)
- Begin to identify strengths and areas for improvement in performance
- Follow simple rules and demonstrate fair play

**Physical Capacity:**
- Sustained aerobic activity for 5-10 minutes
- Development of core strength through gymnastics and athletics
- Improved coordination in multi-skill activities

### Upper Key Stage 2 (Years 5-6)

**Movement Skills Development:**
- Refinement of sport-specific techniques with increasing consistency
- Complex gymnastics sequences (5-6 movements) incorporating levels, directions and speed variations
- Extended dance compositions demonstrating character, narrative or theme
- Application of skills under pressure in competitive situations

**Tactical Understanding:**
- Sophisticated game principles including width/depth in attack, pressure/cover in defence
- Team strategies and positional play
- Evaluate and recognise own and others' success
- Understand and apply rules consistently

**Physical Capacity:**
- Sustained aerobic activity for 15+ minutes
- Enhanced muscular strength and endurance
- Improved flexibility and body control
- Understanding of why physical activity is important for health

## Core Content Areas

### Games

**Dawn Penney** and **John Evans** in their analysis *Policies, Politics and Practice in Physical Education* (1999) highlight that games dominate the PE curriculum in England, occupying approximately 50-70% of curriculum time at Key Stage 2. They advocate for a balanced approach that ensures breadth whilst developing depth of understanding.

**Invasion Games** (e.g., football, hockey, netball, basketball, rugby):
- Passing and receiving under pressure
- Creating and denying space
- Supporting play and covering defensively
- Transitioning between attack and defence
- Understanding roles and positions

**Net/Wall Games** (e.g., tennis, badminton, volleyball):
- Serving with accuracy and variety
- Returning to base position
- Placing the ball/shuttle to create difficulty for opponents
- Cooperative rally skills
- Singles and doubles strategies

**Striking and Fielding Games** (e.g., cricket, rounders):
- Batting techniques and shot selection
- Bowling/pitching with accuracy
- Fielding positions and tactical placement
- Running between bases/wickets
- Team fielding strategies

### Gymnastics

Gymnastics at Key Stage 2 should emphasise:
- **Travelling**: Using different body parts, directions, levels and speeds
- **Balance**: Still and dynamic, individual and partner/group
- **Rolling**: Forward, backward, and sideways variations
- **Jumping and Landing**: From different heights, with control and extension
- **Flight**: Taking weight on hands, vaulting activities
- **Sequence Work**: Linking movements with smooth transitions
- **Partner and Group Work**: Matching, mirroring, canon, counterbalance and counter-tension

**Safety remains paramount**: appropriate matting, progressive skill development, and thorough warm-up procedures must always be followed.

### Dance

**Susan Capel** in *Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School* (7th edition, 2020) emphasises that dance develops creativity, expression and cultural awareness. At Key Stage 2, dance should include:

- **Dance styles**: Creative, contemporary, folk, cultural, historical periods (e.g., Victorian, Tudor)
- **Stimulus-based work**: Stories, music, images, poetry, emotions
- **Movement vocabulary**: Gestures, travel, turn, jump, balance, stillness
- **Choreographic devices**: Unison, canon, repetition, levels, formation
- **Performance skills**: Projection, facial expression, spatial awareness
- **Appreciation**: Watching and evaluating own and others' work

### Athletics

Athletics introduces children to track and field events:

**Running**:
- Sprinting (40-60m with proper starting technique)
- Middle distance (600-800m with pacing strategies)
- Relay techniques (baton passing, changeover zones)
- Hurdling (modified heights and distances)

**Jumping**:
- Long jump (developing run-up, take-off and landing)
- High jump (scissors technique appropriate for this age)
- Triple jump (hop, step, jump coordination)

**Throwing**:
- Shot put (standing and simple glide technique)
- Javelin (foam javelins, focusing on run-up and release)
- Discus (modified equipment, rotational technique basics)

### Outdoor and Adventurous Activities (OAA)

OAA develops problem-solving, teamwork and risk assessment. Activities include:

- **Orienteering**: Map reading, control finding, route planning
- **Problem-solving challenges**: Team-building tasks, equipment-based challenges
- **Trust activities**: Developing group cohesion and communication
- **Environmental awareness**: Countryside code, leave no trace principles
- **Residential experiences**: Many schools offer Year 6 residential trips incorporating climbing, canoeing, high ropes courses

### Swimming and Water Safety

The **Amateur Swimming Association (now Swim England)** provides the National Curriculum Swimming Framework. Key Stage 2 swimming should:

- Progress from water confidence to stroke development
- Ensure 25m competency in at least two strokes
- Teach safe entry and exit from water
- Develop personal survival techniques (floating, treading water)
- Address different water environments and dangers
- Many schools provide swimming in Years 3-4, though some continue throughout Key Stage 2

## Physical Literacy and Long-term Development

**Margaret Whitehead's** conception of **physical literacy** (2001, 2010) has profoundly influenced Key Stage 2 PE philosophy. Whitehead defines physical literacy as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2010, p.12).

At Key Stage 2, developing physical literacy requires:

### Motivation and Confidence
- Providing appropriately challenging tasks (Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development)
- Celebrating effort and improvement, not just winning
- Offering choice within activities to develop autonomy
- Creating inclusive environments where all children experience success

### Physical Competence
- Systematic skill development from fundamental to sport-specific movements
- Varied contexts requiring skill adaptation
- Regular opportunities for practice and refinement
- Progressive challenges that extend capability

### Knowledge and Understanding
- Teaching *why* as well as *how* to move
- Developing tactical awareness and decision-making
- Understanding health benefits of physical activity
- Learning rules, etiquette and sporting values

### Engagement and Value
- Connecting PE to children's lives and interests
- Introducing diverse activities including alternative sports
- Developing intrinsic motivation through meaningful experiences
- Building habits and attitudes for lifelong participation

## Assessment at Key Stage 2

### Assessment Principles

The removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014 gave schools greater autonomy in assessment. **Association for Physical Education (afPE)** guidance (2020) recommends assessment should be:

- **Valid**: Measuring genuine PE outcomes, not just behavioural compliance
- **Reliable**: Consistent across teachers and contexts
- **Formative**: Informing teaching and learning, promoting improvement
- **Inclusive**: Recognising diverse abilities and progress rates
- **Manageable**: Not creating excessive workload

### What to Assess

Following **Dawn Penney's** work on assessment in PE (2008), effective Key Stage 2 assessment should evaluate:

**Physical Competence**:
- Quality and consistency of skill execution
- Application of skills in varied contexts
- Development of physical capacity (strength, endurance, flexibility)

**Cognitive Understanding**:
- Tactical awareness and decision-making
- Rules knowledge and game appreciation
- Understanding of health and fitness concepts
- Ability to evaluate performance

**Social and Personal Development**:
- Cooperation and leadership
- Communication and teamwork
- Respect for rules, opponents and officials
- Resilience and determination

**Creativity and Expression**:
- Original responses to tasks (particularly in dance and gymnastics)
- Use of space, dynamics and relationships

### Assessment Methods

**Observation**: Systematic observation during lessons using criteria-based frameworks

**Video Analysis**: Recording performances for detailed evaluation and pupil self-assessment

**Peer and Self-Assessment**: Developing children's evaluative skills through structured activities

**Questioning**: Checking understanding of concepts, rules and tactics

**Performance Tasks**: Specific challenges designed to reveal particular competencies

## Differentiation and Inclusion

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND)

The **Equality Act 2010** requires schools to make reasonable adjustments. **Susan Capel** (2020) identifies strategies including:

- **Task modification**: Adapting equipment (lighter bats, larger balls), rules (simpler games), or space (smaller areas)
- **Grouping strategies**: Ability grouping for skill development, mixed ability for games
- **Additional support**: Teaching assistants, peer buddies, visual demonstrations
- **Differentiated expectations**: Setting individual targets based on starting points

### Gifted and Talented Pupils

Identified talented pupils require:
- **Extended challenges**: Leadership roles, complex tactical problems, performance goals
- **Enrichment opportunities**: School teams, external clubs, competition pathways
- **Specialisation**: Developing depth alongside breadth
- **Mentoring**: Linking with older pupils or external coaches

### Gender Considerations

Research by **Youth Sport Trust** (2018) shows declining activity levels, particularly among girls, during upper Key Stage 2. Strategies include:

- Reviewing gender stereotypes in activity selection
- Offering choice and variety
- Considering single-sex groupings where appropriate
- Using positive female role models
- Addressing cultural sensitivities around dress and participation

## Health and Wellbeing

### Physical Activity Levels

**UK Chief Medical Officers' Physical Activity Guidelines (2019)** recommend children engage in:
- At least 60 minutes moderate-to-vigorous physical activity daily
- Activities that strengthen muscle and bone at least 3 days per week
- Minimised sedentary time

PE lessons alone cannot achieve this, but they contribute to overall activity and establish positive attitudes and habits.

### Health-Related Learning

Key Stage 2 PE should develop understanding of:

- **Cardiovascular health**: How heart and lungs respond to exercise
- **Muscular fitness**: Strength and endurance development
- **Flexibility**: Range of motion and safe stretching
- **Body composition**: Healthy weight and nutrition basics
- **Mental wellbeing**: PE's role in stress reduction, mood improvement and resilience

**PSHE Links**: PE significantly contributes to Personal, Social, Health and Economic education, particularly around healthy lifestyles, managing risk, and relationships.

## Cross-Curricular Connections

Key Stage 2 PE offers rich opportunities for cross-curricular learning:

**Mathematics**: Measuring distances, recording times, calculating scores, geometry in dance/gymnastics formations, data handling in personal bests

**English**: Technical vocabulary, writing match reports, dance narratives, instructional language, reading rules

**Science**: Human body systems, forces and motion, nutrition, scientific enquiry through performance analysis

**Geography**: Orienteering map skills, OAA in different environments, international sporting cultures

**History**: Traditional games, sport through history, Olympic Games development

**Computing**: Video analysis, fitness tracking apps, creating presentations

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Planning Effective Lessons

**David Kirk** (2010) advocates for **meaningful PE** organised around three pedagogical principles:

1. **Relevance**: Activities connecting to pupils' lives and interests
2. **Challenge**: Appropriately demanding tasks promoting engagement
3. **Student Voice**: Involving pupils in decision-making and evaluation

**Lesson Structure**:
- **Warm-up (5-8 minutes)**: Pulse-raising, mobility, skill-related preparation
- **Skill Development (10-15 minutes)**: Teaching, practicing, refining specific skills
- **Application (15-20 minutes)**: Using skills in game/performance contexts
- **Cool-down (3-5 minutes)**: Reducing intensity, stretching, reflection

### Maximising Activity Time

Research shows pupils are often active for only 30-40% of PE lessons. Strategies to increase activity include:

- **Efficient organisation**: Pre-prepared equipment, established routines
- **Avoiding lines/queues**: Multiple activities, small groups
- **Active instruction**: Demonstrating with pupils moving, checking understanding quickly
- **Modified games**: Smaller teams (3v3 rather than 11v11), more equipment (multiple balls)

### Behaviour Management

**Susan Capel** (2020) emphasises that effective behaviour management in PE requires:

- **Clear expectations**: Establishing rules for equipment, behaviour, safety
- **Consistent routines**: Entry procedures, register, equipment distribution
- **Positive reinforcement**: Praising desired behaviours and effort
- **Strategic positioning**: Maintaining sightlines to all pupils
- **Appropriate interventions**: Matching consequences to behaviours
- **Relationship building**: Knowing pupils and demonstrating care

### Safety Considerations

Teachers have a **duty of care** and must:

- Conduct **risk assessments** for all activities and environments
- Check equipment regularly and remove defective items
- Ensure appropriate clothing (remove jewellery, tie long hair)
- Maintain appropriate supervision ratios
- Match activities to pupils' capabilities
- Teach safe practice (falling, landing, collision avoidance)
- Have emergency procedures in place
- Hold relevant qualifications for specialist activities
