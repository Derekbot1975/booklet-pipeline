# PE National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction to the National Curriculum for Physical Education

The National Curriculum for Physical Education in England establishes the statutory requirements for what pupils should be taught from Key Stage 1 through to Key Stage 4. First introduced in 1992 and most recently revised in 2013 (implemented from September 2014), the current curriculum represents a significant shift towards greater professional autonomy for teachers whilst maintaining core entitlements for all pupils.

The curriculum framework is deliberately non-prescriptive, allowing schools to design their own programmes of study whilst ensuring all pupils receive a broad and balanced physical education. As David Kirk (2010) argues in *Physical Education Futures*, this flexibility enables schools to respond to local contexts whilst maintaining national standards and expectations.

## Statutory Requirements and Legal Framework

Physical Education is a statutory foundation subject within the National Curriculum for England, meaning all maintained schools must teach PE at Key Stages 1-4. Academies and free schools are not legally required to follow the National Curriculum but must teach a 'broad and balanced curriculum' which typically includes PE.

The curriculum is established under the Education Act 2002, with the current programmes of study published by the Department for Education (DfE, 2013). Schools must provide a minimum of two hours of high-quality PE per week, though this is guidance rather than statutory requirement. The Association for Physical Education (AfPE) consistently advocates for protected curriculum time and recommends at least 120 minutes per week at primary level and 90-120 minutes at secondary level.

## National Curriculum Aims

The National Curriculum for PE aims to ensure that all pupils:

- Develop competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities
- Are physically active for sustained periods of time
- Engage in competitive sports and activities
- Lead healthy, active lives

These aims reflect what Margaret Whitehead (2010) describes as the development of 'physical literacy' - the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life. Whilst the term 'physical literacy' does not appear in the statutory documentation, Whitehead's conceptual framework has profoundly influenced contemporary PE philosophy and practice in the UK.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Programmes of Study Requirements

Pupils should develop fundamental movement skills, become increasingly competent and confident, and access a broad range of opportunities to extend their agility, balance and coordination (the 'ABC' of movement).

**Statutory requirements specify pupils should be taught to:**

- Master basic movements including running, jumping, throwing and catching
- Develop balance, agility and coordination, and begin to apply these in a range of activities
- Participate in team games, developing simple tactics for attacking and defending
- Perform dances using simple movement patterns

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Dawn Penney and colleagues (2009) in their work on curriculum making emphasise that Key Stage 1 establishes the movement vocabulary upon which all future physical development depends. Teachers should focus on:

- Multi-skills approaches rather than early sport specialisation
- Movement exploration and discovery learning
- Short, varied activities maintaining high engagement
- Cross-curricular links, particularly with mathematics (counting, patterns) and literacy (vocabulary of movement)

Len Almond's work on fundamental movement skills (Almond, 1997) remains influential, advocating for systematic development of locomotor, stability and manipulative skills before introducing complex game forms.

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Programmes of Study Requirements

Pupils should continue to apply and develop a broader range of skills, learning how to use them in different ways and to link them to make actions and sequences of movement.

**Statutory requirements specify pupils should be taught to:**

- Use running, jumping, throwing and catching in isolation and in combination
- Play competitive games, modified where appropriate, and apply basic principles suitable for attacking and defending
- Develop flexibility, strength, technique, control and balance through gymnastics
- Perform dances using a range of movement patterns
- Take part in outdoor and adventurous activity challenges both individually and within a team
- Compare their performances with previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve their personal best

**Swimming and water safety** becomes statutory at Key Stage 2. All schools must provide swimming instruction, and by the end of Key Stage 2, pupils should be able to swim competently, confidently and proficiently over a distance of at least 25 metres, use a range of strokes effectively, and perform safe self-rescue.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

The Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) model, developed by Almond and Thorpe at Loughborough University (Bunker & Thorpe, 1982), provides an effective pedagogical framework. Rather than technique-first approaches, TGfU emphasises:

- Game appreciation and tactical awareness
- Decision-making within modified games
- Skill execution in context
- Performance improvement through understanding

David Kirk's research (Kirk, 2013) demonstrates that games-centred approaches lead to greater engagement and transfer of learning across activity contexts.

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### Programmes of Study Requirements

Pupils should build on and embed the physical development and skills learned in Key Stages 1 and 2, becoming more competent, confident and expert in their techniques.

**Statutory requirements specify pupils should be taught to:**

- Use a range of tactics and strategies to overcome opponents in direct competition through team and individual games
- Develop their technique and improve their performance in other competitive sports (e.g., athletics, gymnastics)
- Perform dances using advanced dance techniques in a range of dance styles and forms
- Take part in outdoor and adventurous activities which present intellectual and physical challenges
- Analyse their performances compared to previous ones and demonstrate improvement
- Take part in competitive sports and activities outside school through community links or sports clubs

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Susan Capel and Margaret Whitehead (2015) in *Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School* emphasise that Key Stage 3 represents a critical period for maintaining engagement, particularly for girls and lower-skilled pupils.

Effective practice includes:

- Differentiated pathways offering breadth and depth
- Leadership and officiating opportunities
- Performance analysis using technology
- Clear progression from Key Stage 2
- Links to community sports clubs and facilities

The curriculum's requirement for competitive sport reflects governmental priorities articulated in the 2012 School Games legacy agenda, though researchers like Penney and Evans (2013) caution against narrow interpretations that exclude non-competitive activities.

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

### Programmes of Study Requirements

The Key Stage 4 programme of study is notably brief, requiring only that all pupils continue to be taught the Key Stage 3 programme in a manner appropriate to their age. Schools have substantial flexibility in provision, which may include:

- Core PE (non-examined)
- GCSE Physical Education
- BTEC Sport qualifications
- Other accredited courses

### Examination Specifications

For pupils pursuing accredited qualifications, examination boards offer detailed specifications:

**GCSE Physical Education** is offered by:

- **AQA** (Specification 8582): Focuses on applied anatomy and physiology, movement analysis, physical training, use of data, socio-cultural influences, sports psychology and health, fitness and well-being. Practical performance accounts for 40% (three activities from set lists), with 60% theory examined through two written papers.

- **Edexcel/Pearson** (Specification 1PE0): Similar content structure with emphasis on anatomy and physiology, movement analysis, physical training, health and performance, and socio-cultural influences. 40% practical assessment across three activities, 60% theory.

- **OCR** (Specification J587): Distinctive approach including components on physical factors affecting performance, socio-cultural issues and sports psychology, and performance and leadership. 60% practical assessment (three activities plus analysis and evaluation of performance), 40% theory.

- **WJEC/Eduqas** (used primarily in Wales): Comparable structure focusing on health, training and exercise, skills, strategies and performance, and socio-cultural influences affecting participation and performance.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

David Kirk's research on models-based practice (2013) suggests Key Stage 4 benefits from differentiated pedagogical approaches. For core PE, emphasis should be placed on:

- Lifelong participation and physical literacy
- Authentic choices reflecting pupil interests
- Leadership, coaching and officiating pathways
- Health-related exercise and personal fitness planning

For examination PE, teachers must balance practical skill development with theoretical understanding, using integrated approaches where possible.

## Cross-Phase Considerations

### Assessment Without Levels

Following the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014, schools have developed diverse assessment approaches. Susan Capel and colleagues (2016) identify effective practice includes:

- Clear learning objectives and success criteria
- Formative assessment embedded in lessons
- Pupil self and peer assessment
- Progress measured against individual starting points
- End of key stage expectations rather than age-related levels

The Association for Physical Education provides guidance through its 'Assessment for Learning in Physical Education' framework, emphasising assessment **of**, **for** and **as** learning.

### Inclusion and SEND Provision

The National Curriculum inclusion statement requires teachers to set suitable learning challenges, respond to pupils' diverse needs, and overcome potential barriers to learning. In PE, this means:

- Adapting activities through the STEP framework (Space, Task, Equipment, People)
- Ensuring accessibility for pupils with physical, sensory or cognitive needs
- Challenging stereotypes and promoting equity across gender, ethnicity and ability
- Providing alternative activities where religious or cultural considerations apply

### Breadth and Balance

Whilst the curriculum specifies activity areas (games, dance, gymnastics, athletics, outdoor and adventurous activities, swimming), the extent and balance are not prescribed. Dawn Penney's research (2008) on curriculum construction emphasises that achieving genuinely broad and balanced provision requires deliberate planning to avoid:

- Over-emphasis on traditional team games
- Gender-stereotyped activity allocation
- Neglect of aesthetic or adventurous activities
- Failure to progress learning across key stages

## Curriculum Design and Implementation

### Planning Frameworks

Effective curriculum planning requires consideration at three levels, as articulated by Kirk and Macdonald (2001):

**Intended curriculum**: The written programmes of study and schemes of work outlining what should be taught.

**Implemented curriculum**: The enacted curriculum in lessons, influenced by teacher interpretation, resources, facilities and expertise.

**Experienced curriculum**: What pupils actually learn, shaped by their prior experiences, engagement and social contexts.

### Schemes of Work and Units of Learning

The non-prescriptive nature of the 2014 curriculum places responsibility on schools to design coherent schemes of work. Effective practice includes:

- Clear progression maps across key stages
- Units of work (typically 6-12 lessons) allowing depth of learning
- Learning intentions aligned with National Curriculum aims
- Assessment opportunities integrated throughout
- Flexibility to respond to weather, facilities and pupil needs

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework provides a useful structure, with units developing:

- **Physical competence**: Motor skills, fitness, movement patterns
- **Cognitive understanding**: Tactics, rules, health concepts
- **Affective engagement**: Confidence, motivation, enjoyment
- **Behavioural habits**: Participation patterns, active lifestyle choices

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Physical Literacy vs Sport Performance

The tension between developing lifelong participation (physical literacy) and achieving sporting excellence runs through contemporary PE discourse. Margaret Whitehead (2013) argues that authentic physical literacy enables both outcomes, but cautions against curriculum models prioritising elite performance over inclusive participation.

Research by Kirk and colleagues (2014) suggests effective curricula integrate multiple objectives through varied pedagogical models including Sport Education, Teaching Games for Understanding, Cooperative Learning, and Health-Based Physical Education.

### Health and Obesity Agenda

Government policy increasingly positions PE as a public health intervention, particularly following the 2012 childhood obesity strategy. However, researchers including Evans, Rich and Davies (2009) caution that narrow focus on weight and fitness risks:

- Alienating pupils, particularly adolescent girls
- Perpetuating body dissatisfaction and disordered eating
- Oversimplifying complex health behaviours
- Reducing PE to mere exercise

Len Almond's work on health-related exercise emphasises empowerment through understanding rather than prescription, developing pupils' ability to plan, perform and evaluate their own health-enhancing physical activity.

### Competitive Sport and School Games

The 2013 curriculum explicitly requires participation in competitive sport, reflecting the School Games agenda and Sport England's youth sport strategy. Whilst competitive experiences can develop resilience, teamwork and aspiration, Dawn Penney and colleagues (2013) argue for inclusive interpretations including:

- Competition against personal bests
- Inter-house and intra-school formats
- Alternative competitive frameworks (dance competitions, outdoor challenges)
- Cooperative-competitive hybrid formats

## Professional Resources and Continuing Development

### Key Professional Organisations

**Association for Physical Education (AfPE)**: The subject association providing guidance, resources and professional development. Publications include *Physical Education Matters* journal and various policy statements.

**Youth Sport Trust**: Focuses on primary PE and school sport, particularly supporting the PE and Sport Premium in primary schools (£320 million annually since 2013).

**Sport England**: Delivers the School Games programme and provides strategic direction for youth sport development.

## Conclusion

The National Curriculum for Physical Education provides a flexible framework enabling teachers to design programmes meeting local contexts whilst ensuring all pupils develop the competence, confidence and motivation for lifelong physical activity. Effective implementation requires understanding not only statutory requirements but also the pedagogical principles and research evidence that underpin high-quality physical education.

As Margaret Whitehead consistently argues, the ultimate aim transcends curriculum compliance - it is to nurture physically literate individuals who value and engage with physical activity throughout their lives. This requires teachers who are curriculum makers rather than mere deliverers, thoughtfully interpreting policy through evidence-informed practice.
