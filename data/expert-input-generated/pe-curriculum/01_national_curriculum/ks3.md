# PE at Key Stage 3

## Overview of Key Stage 3 Physical Education

Key Stage 3 (KS3) encompasses Years 7, 8 and 9, typically serving pupils aged 11-14. The National Curriculum for Physical Education in England (DfE, 2013) establishes the statutory framework for PE during this crucial transitional phase. This period represents a critical juncture in physical literacy development, as articulated by Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2010).

## National Curriculum Requirements for Key Stage 3

### Statutory Framework

The National Curriculum (DfE, 2013) states that all pupils should:

- **Develop competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities**
- **Be physically active for sustained periods of time**
- **Engage in competitive sports and activities**
- **Lead healthy, active lives**

### Specific Programme of Study Requirements

At KS3, pupils should be taught to:

1. Use a range of tactics and strategies to overcome opponents in direct competition through team and individual games (including football, rugby, hockey, netball, basketball, badminton, tennis, cricket and rounders)
2. Develop their technique and improve their performance in other competitive sports (such as athletics and gymnastics)
3. Perform dances using advanced dance techniques within a range of dance styles and forms
4. Take part in outdoor and adventurous activities which present intellectual and physical challenges
5. Analyse their performances compared to previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve their personal best
6. Take part in competitive sports and activities outside school through community links or sports clubs

## Pedagogical Approaches and Key Thinkers

### Margaret Whitehead and Physical Literacy

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework provides the philosophical foundation for much contemporary PE practice at KS3. Whitehead (2013) emphasises that physical literacy development during adolescence should focus on:

- **Motivation**: Maintaining engagement during a period when activity levels typically decline
- **Confidence**: Supporting pupils through physical and social changes
- **Physical competence**: Building on primary skills to develop sport-specific techniques
- **Knowledge and understanding**: Developing cognitive aspects of movement

### David Kirk's Models-Based Practice

David Kirk (2013) advocates for models-based practice at KS3, moving beyond traditional multi-activity, sport-based curricula. Kirk's research suggests that models such as Sport Education, Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), and Cooperative Learning prove more effective for sustained engagement. At KS3, Sport Education involves:

- Extended units (typically 15-20 lessons rather than 6-8)
- Authentic roles (captains, coaches, officials, statisticians)
- Formal competition structures
- Festivity and culminating events

### Dawn Penney's Curriculum Frameworks

Dawn Penney's work on curriculum design and assessment emphasises the need for coherent progression from KS2 to KS3. Penney and Chandler (2000) identify the "transition gap" where pupils often repeat content from primary school. Effective KS3 curricula should:

- Build explicitly on KS2 foundation skills
- Establish clear progression frameworks
- Employ varied pedagogical approaches
- Consider pupils' wider physical activity contexts

### Len Almond and Health Focus

Len Almond's contribution to health-focused PE remains particularly relevant at KS3, given declining physical activity levels during adolescence. Almond and Whitehead (2012) advocate for embedding health literacy throughout PE, including:

- Understanding physical activity guidelines (Chief Medical Officer recommends 60 minutes daily for young people)
- Developing exercise programming knowledge
- Connecting movement experiences to lifelong participation

## Curriculum Design Considerations

### Activity Areas

The statutory curriculum requires breadth across activity areas. A balanced KS3 curriculum typically includes:

**Invasion Games**: Football, rugby (union/league), hockey, basketball, netball, handball
**Net/Wall Games**: Badminton, tennis, table tennis, volleyball
**Striking/Fielding Games**: Cricket, rounders, softball
**Gymnastics**: Floor work, vaulting, partner/group work, apparatus
**Athletics**: Running (sprints, middle-distance), jumping, throwing events
**Dance**: Contemporary, street dance, cultural forms
**Swimming**: Developing competence, personal survival (if not achieved at KS2)
**Outdoor and Adventurous Activities (OAA)**: Orienteering, problem-solving, expeditions
**Health and Fitness**: Understanding training principles, fitness testing, activity planning

### Curriculum Time Allocation

While not statutorily prescribed, the Association for Physical Education (AfPE, 2020) recommends a minimum of two hours weekly curriculum PE at KS3. Many schools provide:

- 2 x 60-minute lessons (typical model)
- 3 x 50-minute lessons (allows greater breadth)
- 100 minutes delivered through various timetable structures

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Susan Capel's work on inclusion in PE (Capel & Whitehead, 2015) emphasises that effective KS3 provision must address:

**Physical differentiation**: Modifying activities for varied abilities, SEND pupils, and physical disabilities
**Social differentiation**: Managing friendship groups, gender considerations, and grouping strategies
**Cognitive differentiation**: Varying complexity of tactics, strategies and decision-making
**Cultural differentiation**: Respecting religious and cultural requirements

## Assessment at Key Stage 3

### Assessment Framework

The removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014 granted schools autonomy in assessment design. Effective KS3 assessment frameworks typically incorporate:

**Formative Assessment**: Ongoing feedback within lessons through questioning, observation, peer and self-assessment
**Summative Assessment**: End-of-unit evaluations measuring progress against curriculum objectives
**Holistic Assessment**: Evaluating across multiple domains (physical, cognitive, social, affective)

### Assessment Models

Leading PE departments employ various models:

**Competency-based assessment**: Evaluating technique execution and tactical application
**Process-focused assessment**: Assessing participation, effort, leadership and teamwork
**Progress-focused assessment**: Measuring individual improvement against baseline
**Hybrid models**: Combining elements to provide comprehensive evaluation

Dawn Penney's work (Penney et al., 2009) advocates for assessment that:
- Makes learning intentions explicit
- Involves pupils in assessment processes
- Provides meaningful, actionable feedback
- Recognises achievement beyond physical performance

## Progression from Key Stage 2

### The Transition Challenge

Research by Kirk (2005) identifies significant challenges in KS2-KS3 transition:

**Curriculum discontinuity**: Repetition of primary content without progression
**Pedagogical regression**: Return to teacher-centred approaches after active primary experiences
**Motivational decline**: Reduced enthusiasm, particularly among girls
**Competence perception**: Decreased confidence in ability during early adolescence

### Effective Transition Practice

Outstanding practice includes:

1. **Primary liaison**: Communication with feeder schools about prior learning
2. **Baseline assessment**: Early evaluation of competencies in Year 7
3. **Bridging units**: Activities consolidating KS2 skills whilst introducing KS3 expectations
4. **Progressive planning**: Ensuring clear advancement in complexity, tactics and understanding

## Specific Developmental Considerations

### Physical Development

At KS3, pupils experience:

**Growth spurts**: Particularly in Year 8/9, affecting coordination and movement efficiency
**Puberty variations**: Wide ranges in physical maturity requiring sensitive management
**Motor skill refinement**: Transition from fundamental movement skills to sport-specific techniques
**Physiological changes**: Improved aerobic capacity, strength development (particularly boys)

### Cognitive Development

Piaget's formal operational stage (typically emerging 11-15 years) enables:

- Abstract tactical thinking
- Understanding complex game strategies
- Analysing performance analytically
- Hypothetical reasoning about movement problems

### Social and Emotional Development

Erikson's identity development stage requires PE to:

- Provide success experiences maintaining self-efficacy
- Manage peer relationships and social hierarchies
- Address body image concerns sensitively
- Create psychologically safe learning environments

## Contemporary Issues in Key Stage 3 PE

### Engagement and Retention

The Youth Sport Trust's PE and School Sport Survey (2020) identifies declining participation rates, particularly among:

**Girls**: Significant drop-off beginning Year 7, accelerating through KS3
**SEND pupils**: Lower participation in both curricular and extra-curricular PE
**Disadvantaged pupils**: Reduced engagement in extended opportunities

Evidence-based strategies addressing these challenges include:

- Pupil voice in curriculum design
- Varied activity offerings beyond traditional team sports
- Single-sex groupings where appropriate
- Alternative activities (fitness classes, outdoor pursuits, lifestyle sports)

### Technology Integration

Digital technology offers opportunities to enhance KS3 PE:

**Video analysis**: Apps like CoachMyVideo, Hudl Technique enabling immediate performance feedback
**Fitness tracking**: Wearable technology (heart rate monitors, pedometers) supporting health education
**Gamification**: Apps and platforms increasing motivation through competition and progress tracking
**Online resources**: Supporting independent learning and homework tasks

### Health and Wellbeing

The Chief Medical Officer's guidelines (2019) recommend 60 minutes daily moderate-to-vigorous physical activity for young people. KS3 PE must:

- Maximise activity time within lessons
- Educate about activity recommendations
- Promote extra-curricular and community participation
- Address mental health benefits of physical activity

### Safeguarding and Safety

Following the Association for Physical Education's Safe Practice guidelines (afPE, 2020, 10th edition), KS3 PE teachers must:

- Conduct risk assessments for all activities
- Ensure appropriate supervision ratios
- Maintain suitable facility and equipment standards
- Address changing room safeguarding
- Manage outdoor education risks appropriately

## Preparing for Key Stage 4

### Building Foundation Knowledge

For pupils selecting GCSE PE (typically in Year 9), KS3 must develop:

**Practical competence**: Proficiency in activities offered as GCSE assessment options
**Theoretical understanding**: Foundation knowledge in anatomy, physiology, training principles
**Analytical skills**: Performance analysis abilities required for coursework
**Vocabulary**: Subject-specific terminology across activity areas

### Examination Board Specifications

All major boards offer GCSE PE beginning KS4:

**AQA (8582)**: Most popular specification; requires competence in three activities from different categories
**Edexcel/Pearson (1PE0)**: Emphasises practical application of theoretical concepts
**OCR (J587)**: Offers breadth across socio-cultural and biomechanical topics
**WJEC Eduqas**: Popular in Wales; includes contemporary issues in sport

Effective KS3 programmes anticipate these requirements, establishing foundational content whilst maintaining inclusive, engaging experiences for all pupils.

## Quality Assurance and Curriculum Evaluation

### Ofsted Expectations

The Ofsted PE Subject Report (2022) identifies outstanding KS3 practice as including:

- Coherent, well-sequenced curricula building systematically on prior learning
- High participation rates with minimal non-participation
- Pupils developing competent, confident movement across activity areas
- Strong extra-curricular programmes extending curriculum learning
- Explicit teaching of knowledge alongside practical skill development

### Self-Evaluation

David Kirk and colleagues (2006) recommend regular curriculum review examining:

**Breadth**: Sufficient variety across activity categories
**Balance**: Appropriate time allocation between areas
**Progression**: Clear advancement across Years 7-9
**Relevance**: Connection to pupils' lives and interests
**Inclusion**: Equitable access and achievement for all groups

## Resources and Professional Development

Key professional organisations supporting KS3 PE:

**Association for Physical Education (afPE)**: Professional body offering guidance, resources and training
**Youth Sport Trust**: Providing CPD, research and programme support
**Sport England**: Funding and strategic direction for school sport
**National Governing Bodies**: Sport-specific resources and qualifications

Essential reading for KS3 PE practitioners:

- Capel, S. & Whitehead, M. (2015) *Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School* (4th ed.), Routledge
- Kirk, D. (2010) *Physical Education Futures*, Routledge

## Conclusion

Effective Key Stage 3 Physical Education requires sophisticated pedagogical understanding, combining statutory curriculum requirements with evidence-based practice informed by leading researchers. Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework, David Kirk's models-based practice, and Dawn Penney's curriculum design principles provide complementary lenses through which to construct engaging, progressive and inclusive KS3 programmes. As pupils navigate significant physical, cognitive and social changes during early adolescence, PE must maintain motivation, develop competence across varied activities, and establish foundations for lifelong participation in physical activity. The transition from KS2 and preparation for KS4 require careful curriculum sequencing, whilst contemporary issues including technology integration, health education and declining participation rates demand responsive, innovative practice. By attending to these multiple dimensions, KS3 PE can fulfil its potential as a vital component of adolescent education, developing physically literate young people equipped to lead active, healthy lives.
