# PE at Key Stage 1

## Overview of Key Stage 1 Physical Education

Physical Education at Key Stage 1 (ages 5-7, Years 1 and 2) forms the foundation of children's physical literacy and movement competence within the National Curriculum for England (DfE, 2013). This crucial phase establishes fundamental movement skills, develops positive attitudes towards physical activity, and introduces children to the essential concepts that underpin lifelong participation in sport and exercise.

The statutory National Curriculum states that pupils should develop fundamental movement skills, become increasingly competent and confident, and access a broad range of opportunities to extend their agility, balance and coordination, individually and with others. As Margaret Whitehead (2010) emphasises in her seminal work on physical literacy, this stage is critical for developing the "motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life."

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Statutory Content

The Department for Education's National Curriculum (2013) specifies that Key Stage 1 pupils should be taught to:

- Master basic movements including running, jumping, throwing and catching, as well as developing balance, agility and co-ordination, and begin to apply these in a range of activities
- Participate in team games, developing simple tactics for attacking and defending
- Perform dances using simple movement patterns

The programme of study emphasises breadth and progression rather than depth in specific sports, contrasting with later key stages. Schools must provide at least two hours of high-quality PE per week, though this is guidance rather than statutory requirement (afPE, 2015).

### Non-Statutory Guidance

While swimming is not mandatory at Key Stage 1, some schools choose to introduce water confidence activities. The statutory requirement for children to swim 25 metres competently using various strokes applies by the end of Key Stage 2, but early introduction can support progression (ASA, 2014).

## Fundamental Movement Skills Development

### The Foundation of Physical Literacy

David Kirk (2005) argues that Key Stage 1 represents a "critical window" for developing fundamental movement skills (FMS), which he categorises as the building blocks for more complex, sport-specific movements encountered in later schooling. Research by Morley et al. (2015) demonstrates that children who develop competence in FMS during early primary years are significantly more likely to maintain physical activity levels into adolescence.

### Core Movement Categories

**Locomotor Skills:**
- Walking, running, hopping, skipping, galloping, sliding, leaping
- Progression should focus on control, rhythm and spatial awareness
- Teachers should observe and assess movement quality rather than simply completion

**Stability Skills:**
- Balancing on different body parts, at different levels, in static and dynamic contexts
- Twisting, turning, rolling, stopping and starting
- Core strength development through age-appropriate activities

**Manipulative Skills:**
- Sending: throwing, striking, kicking
- Receiving: catching, collecting, trapping
- Travelling with equipment: dribbling, carrying

Len Almond's (1997) work on developmentally appropriate practice emphasises that Key Stage 1 children benefit most from exploratory learning with varied equipment rather than rigid, adult-imposed techniques.

## Curriculum Areas and Implementation

### Games Activities

At Key Stage 1, games education should focus on fundamental game skills rather than specific sports. Dawn Penney (2006) critiques traditional approaches that introduce sport-specific rules prematurely, advocating instead for "invasion games principles" that develop transferable concepts.

**Invasion Games Principles:**
- Maintaining possession (keeping the ball)
- Creating and using space
- Attacking goals or targets
- Defending space and intercepting

**Net/Wall Games Principles:**
- Sending objects to make opponents move
- Positioning to receive
- Simple rally situations

**Striking and Fielding Principles:**
- Sending objects into space
- Retrieving and returning quickly
- Simple scoring concepts

Teachers should use small-sided games (2v2, 3v3) with modified equipment, ensuring maximum participation and skill repetition rather than prolonged waiting times characteristic of traditional sports (Bailey et al., 2009).

### Gymnastics

Gymnastics at Key Stage 1 develops body management, spatial awareness, and movement quality. Susan Capel and Margaret Whitehead (2015) emphasise that gymnastics uniquely contributes to physical literacy through its focus on controlled, aesthetic movement.

**Key Elements:**
- Travelling actions: different ways of moving on feet, hands and feet, other body parts
- Flight: jumping for distance, height, with turns, over equipment
- Balance: stillness on different body parts, at different levels
- Rotation: rolling along different body surfaces, safely and with control
- Weight on hands: bunny hops, cartwheels (progressions), handstands against walls

**Teaching Approach:**
The National Curriculum emphasises creating sequences by linking actions, developing quality of movement, and exploring contrasts (fast/slow, high/low, curled/stretched). Teachers should provide differentiated tasks allowing various solutions rather than prescriptive "one correct way" approaches (Wright, 2002).

### Dance

Dance education at Key Stage 1 integrates artistic, expressive, and physical development. The National Curriculum requires pupils to "perform dances using simple movement patterns," which typically involves:

**Dance Elements:**
- Body awareness: what the body can do (stretch, curl, twist, turn)
- Spatial concepts: where the body moves (levels, directions, pathways, formations)
- Dynamic qualities: how the body moves (speed, force, flow)
- Relationships: with whom (partners, groups, mirroring, matching, contrasting)

**Thematic Approaches:**
Key Stage 1 dance commonly uses stimulus material including stories, music, objects, and images to motivate and structure movement. For example, traditional tales, weather phenomena, or animal movements provide accessible starting points for young children.

Research by Pickard and Bailey (2009) demonstrates that regular dance participation at Key Stage 1 significantly enhances creativity, self-expression, and social skills alongside physical competencies.

### Outdoor and Adventurous Activities (OAA)

Whilst not explicitly mentioned in the Key Stage 1 National Curriculum, many schools incorporate age-appropriate OAA experiences to develop problem-solving, cooperation, and spatial awareness.

**Appropriate Activities:**
- Orienteering games in school grounds
- Trust activities and simple team challenges
- Exploring different environments (playgrounds, fields, woodland areas where available)
- Treasure hunts and trail activities

## Pedagogical Approaches for Key Stage 1

### Developmentally Appropriate Practice

Margaret Whitehead (2013) stresses that Key Stage 1 pedagogy must recognise children's developmental characteristics:

**Physical Considerations:**
- Short attention spans requiring activity changes every 10-15 minutes
- Developing fine and gross motor skills at varying rates
- Limited muscular strength and endurance
- Ongoing development of hand-eye and foot-eye coordination

**Cognitive Considerations:**
- Concrete thinking requiring visual demonstrations and clear, simple language
- Limited capacity for complex rules or abstract strategies
- Learning through play and exploration rather than formal instruction
- Egocentric perspective affecting cooperative activities

**Social-Emotional Considerations:**
- Developing sharing and turn-taking skills
- Strong need for praise and positive reinforcement
- Varied confidence levels requiring sensitive differentiation
- Parallel play transitioning to interactive cooperative play

### Effective Teaching Strategies

Susan Capel et al. (2016) in "Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School" includes principles equally applicable to primary contexts:

**Organisation and Management:**
- Clear routines for entry, equipment collection, listening signals, and transitions
- Maximum participation through appropriate equipment quantities (ideally one piece per child)
- Efficient formations (lines, circles, scatter) appropriate to activities
- Emphasis on safe practice including spatial awareness and controlled movement

**Instruction and Feedback:**
- Demonstrations paired with verbal cues (show and tell)
- Whole-part-whole approaches for skill introduction
- Abundant positive, specific feedback emphasising effort and improvement
- Use of questioning appropriate to developmental level

**Differentiation Strategies:**
- Task modification (STEP principles: Space, Task, Equipment, People)
- Outcome variety allowing multiple successful responses
- Ability grouping where appropriate, whilst avoiding stigmatisation
- Extension challenges for more competent movers

### Assessment at Key Stage 1

The removal of statutory levelling following the Commission on Assessment without Levels (DfE, 2015) requires schools to develop assessment approaches aligned with their curricula. David Kirk and colleagues (2006) advocate assessment focusing on:

**Formative Assessment:**
- Teacher observation of movement quality using skill-specific criteria
- Pupil self-assessment using age-appropriate language and visual supports
- Peer observation developing evaluative skills

**Summative Assessment:**
- End-of-unit assessments recording achievement against learning objectives
- Photographic or video evidence of sequences or performances
- Records tracking progress in fundamental movement skills

The Association for Physical Education (afPE, 2020) provides exemplification materials showing progression expectations across Key Stage 1, though emphasising these should inform rather than constrain professional judgement.

## Linking Physical Literacy Theory to Key Stage 1 Practice

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework encompasses four interrelated domains particularly relevant to Key Stage 1:

### Physical Competence

Developing fundamental movement skills across stability, locomotor, and manipulative categories forms the primary focus. Teachers should ensure breadth of experience rather than specialisation, recognising that competence develops through quality practice over time.

### Cognitive Understanding

Key Stage 1 pupils begin understanding basic movement vocabulary (stretch, curl, travel, balance), safety principles, and simple tactical concepts. Questioning techniques help develop thinking: "Why did you choose to move that way?" "How could we work together better?"

### Affective Engagement

Fostering positive attitudes towards physical activity requires success-oriented climates where effort is celebrated, mistakes are reframed as learning opportunities, and all children experience genuine achievement. Whitehead (2010) emphasises that negative experiences during early years can establish lifelong avoidance of physical activity.

### Behavioural Habits

Key Stage 1 establishes foundations for active lifestyles through regular participation, understanding that activity makes bodies stronger and healthier, and experiencing the enjoyment physical activity provides. Links to initiatives like the Daily Mile or active playtime programmes reinforce messages beyond timetabled PE.

## Inclusive Practice at Key Stage 1

### SEND Considerations

The SEND Code of Practice (DfE/DoH, 2015) requires reasonable adjustments ensuring all children access high-quality PE. At Key Stage 1, this involves:

**Physical Disabilities:**
- Modified equipment (lighter balls, larger targets, adapted apparatus)
- Alternative activities achieving similar learning outcomes
- Specialist TA support where appropriate
- Collaboration with physiotherapists or occupational therapists

**Learning Difficulties:**
- Simplified language and additional visual supports
- Breaking skills into smaller components
- Additional repetition and practice time
- Use of buddies or peer support

**Social, Emotional, and Mental Health Needs:**
- Clear, predictable routines reducing anxiety
- Opportunities for individual work before group activities
- Positive behaviour management strategies
- Quiet spaces for regulation if needed

### Gender Considerations

While Key Stage 1 classes typically work together, teachers should be mindful of developing gender stereotypes. Penney and Evans (1999) highlight that gendered messages about "appropriate" activities begin early. Best practice includes:

- Varied activity selection avoiding stereotyped associations
- Diverse role models through resources and examples
- Challenging language that limits participation ("girls can't...", "boys don't...")
- Ensuring all children access all activities equally

## Links to Other Curriculum Areas

### Cross-Curricular Opportunities

Key Stage 1 PE naturally integrates with other subjects:

**Mathematics:**
- Counting repetitions, scoring points
- Shape recognition through body shapes and floor patterns
- Spatial language (above, below, beside, between)
- Timing and sequencing

**English:**
- Following instructions and rules
- Describing movements and actions
- Storytelling through dance and drama
- Developing specialist vocabulary

**Science:**
- Understanding bodies (muscles, bones, heart, breathing)
- Observing changes during exercise
- Exploring materials and forces through equipment use

**PSHE:**
- Cooperation and teamwork
- Healthy lifestyles and choices
- Managing emotions and behaviour
- Respecting differences

### Literacy Development Through PE

Dawn Penney's research (2008) demonstrates that PE provides authentic contexts for literacy development. Teachers can enhance this through:

- Movement vocabulary walls displaying key terms with images
- Story-making through movement sequences
- Reading activity cards developing comprehension
- Writing movement descriptions or evaluations

## Health and Safety Considerations

### Safe Practice in Physical Education

The Association for Physical Education's "Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity" (afPE, 2020) provides comprehensive guidance. Key Stage 1-specific considerations include:

**Supervision:**
- Higher adult-to-child ratios than older age groups
- Line-of-sight supervision during all activities
- Particular vigilance during apparatus work
- Clear protocols for toileting or first aid requiring adult departure

**Equipment and Environment:**
- Age-appropriate, well-maintained equipment
- Adequate space for vigorous movement
- Appropriate footwear (trainers for most activities, bare feet for gymnastics/dance where appropriate)
- Temperature considerations for outdoor lessons

**Risk-Benefit Assessment:**
- Recognising appropriate challenge vs. excessive risk
- Progressive introduction to apparatus at suitable heights
- Teaching falling and landing techniques
- Developing children's own risk-assessment capabilities

### Safeguarding

All PE teaching must comply with safeguarding requirements including appropriate supervision in changing areas, maintaining professional boundaries during physical support, and vigilance for signs of abuse or neglect potentially visible during PE.

## Professional Development and Resources

### Key Publications

- **Association for Physical Education (afPE):** Regular publications, online resources, and CPD opportunities specifically addressing primary PE
- **Youth Sport Trust:** Complete PE schemes of work including Key Stage 1 units with detailed lesson plans
- **Real PE:** Commercially available scheme developing physical literacy through progressive skill development
- **TOP programmes:** Activity cards and equipment packages supporting fundamental movement skill development

### Continuing Professional Development

Len Almond and colleagues (2012) emphasise that effective Key Stage 1 PE requires ongoing professional learning, particularly for generalist primary teachers who may lack specialist knowledge. Recommended approaches include:

- Subject association membership (afPE) providing access to conferences, webinars, and publications
- Local authority PE advisor support and network meetings
- School Sport Partnerships facilitating inter-school collaboration
- Online resources from organisations like the Association for Physical Education and Youth Sport Trust

## Current Developments and Future Directions

### The School Sport and Activity Action Plan

The UK Government's School Sport and Activity Action Plan (DfE, 2019) emphasises primary PE quality, with specific focus on:

- Primary PE and Sport Premium funding accountability
- Supporting generalist teachers through specialist support
- Increasing active time beyond formal PE lessons
- Embedding active travel and active play

### COVID-19 Legacy

The pandemic significantly impacted Key Stage 1 PE delivery. Emerging research suggests some children entering Year 1 in 2020-2021 showed delayed fundamental movement skill development. Best practice now emphasises:

- Baseline assessment identifying COVID-related gaps
- Increased focus on FMS "catch-up"
- Outdoor learning where feasible
- Enhanced hygiene protocols as appropriate

### Physical Literacy Assessment

Margaret Whitehead's ongoing work developing valid, reliable physical literacy assessment tools includes age-appropriate Key Stage 1 measures. The Canadian Assessment of Physical Literacy (CAPL) and UK-adapted versions provide frameworks schools may adopt, though implementation remains variable.

## Conclusion

Physical Education at Key Stage 1 establishes foundations upon which all subsequent physical development, sports participation, and active lifestyle habits are built. As David Kirk (2010) argues, this phase represents "the most important yet most neglected" element of PE provision. Excellence at Key Stage 1 requires understanding child development, implementing varied, engaging activities developing fundamental movement skills, and creating positive, inclusive environments where all children experience success and enjoyment.

Teachers should view Key Stage 1 PE not as preparation for later sporting achievement, but as intrinsically valuable in developing physically literate young people who are "motivated and confident with physical competence to value and take responsibility for maintaining purposeful physical pursuits throughout life" (Whitehead, 2010).
