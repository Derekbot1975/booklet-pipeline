# Physical Development in EYFS

## Overview and Statutory Framework

Physical Development is one of the three prime areas within the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) framework, which is statutory for all schools and Ofsted-registered early years providers in England. The current EYFS Statutory Framework (DfE, 2021) establishes Physical Development as fundamental to children's overall development and future learning, alongside Communication and Language, and Personal, Social and Emotional Development.

The prime areas are described as universal and particularly crucial for developing secure foundations for children aged from birth to five years. Physical Development specifically focuses on developing children's physical capabilities, their understanding of physical health and wellbeing, and their capacity for learning through physical activity.

### Statutory Requirements

Under the EYFS framework, practitioners must consider the individual needs, interests, and development of each child, using the prime and specific areas to plan learning experiences. Physical Development is assessed through two Early Learning Goals (ELGs) at the end of Reception year:

**Gross Motor Skills**: Children demonstrate strength, balance and coordination when playing. They move energetically, such as running, jumping, dancing, hopping, skipping and climbing.

**Fine Motor Skills**: Children hold a pencil effectively in preparation for fluent writing, using the tripod grip in almost all cases. They use a range of small tools, including scissors, paintbrushes and cutlery. They begin to show accuracy and care when drawing.

## Key Components and Educational Ranges

### Gross Motor Development

Gross motor skills involve large muscle movements and whole-body coordination. Development in this area follows a broadly predictable sequence, though individual children progress at different rates. Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy (Whitehead, 2010) emphasises that physical development in early years establishes the foundation for lifelong engagement with physical activity.

#### Birth to Three Years

During this period, children develop fundamental movement capabilities including:
- Rolling, sitting, crawling and eventually walking
- Developing balance and spatial awareness
- Learning to climb, run with increasing control
- Beginning to show awareness of others when moving

#### Three and Four-Year-Olds

The Development Matters guidance (Early Education, 2021) identifies typical developmental milestones including:
- Running, jumping, climbing and balancing with increasing confidence
- Developing ball skills (throwing, catching, kicking)
- Moving in different ways (sideways, backwards, at different speeds)
- Beginning to show preference for dominant hand
- Negotiating space and obstacles safely

#### Reception Year (4-5 Years)

Children typically progress to:
- More complex movements involving coordination of multiple body parts
- Demonstrating balance and control in still and moving positions
- Travelling with confidence in different directions and heights
- Understanding and following movement instructions
- Beginning to show accuracy in movements

### Fine Motor Development

Fine motor skills involve precise movements using small muscles, particularly in hands and fingers. Research by the Royal College of Occupational Therapists (2018) has highlighted increasing concerns about children's fine motor development, with many children entering school without adequate finger strength and dexterity.

#### Progressive Development

Fine motor development typically follows this progression:
- Palmer grasp (whole hand grasping)
- Pincer grip (thumb and finger)
- Tripod grip (three-finger pencil hold)
- Dynamic tripod grip with fluid movement

Development Matters emphasises that children need extensive opportunities for mark-making, manipulative play, and using tools before formal writing instruction begins.

## Links to Physical Literacy

Margaret Whitehead's influential work on physical literacy (Whitehead, 2001, 2010) has significant implications for EYFS practice. Physical literacy is defined as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (IPLA, 2017).

### Foundation Phase

The EYFS represents the crucial foundation phase for physical literacy development. Whitehead argues that early experiences of movement fundamentally shape children's relationship with physical activity throughout their lives. Key elements during EYFS include:

**Motivation**: Creating positive, enjoyable movement experiences that build children's intrinsic motivation to be active.

**Confidence**: Providing appropriately challenging activities where children experience success and develop positive self-perception regarding their physical capabilities.

**Physical Competence**: Developing fundamental movement skills (FMS) including locomotor skills (running, jumping, hopping), stability skills (balancing, twisting, turning), and object control skills (throwing, catching, striking).

**Knowledge and Understanding**: Beginning to understand how their bodies move and respond to physical activity.

## Connection to National Curriculum Key Stage 1

Physical Development in EYFS provides essential foundations for National Curriculum Physical Education at Key Stage 1. The National Curriculum for PE (DfE, 2013) states that pupils should develop fundamental movement skills, become increasingly competent and confident, and access a broad range of activities.

### Transition and Progression

David Kirk's work on quality physical education (Kirk, 2005) emphasises the importance of developmentally appropriate practice during transition periods. The progression from EYFS to KS1 should be seamless, with Year 1 teachers building directly upon physical development achieved in Reception.

Key progression points include:
- From free exploration of movement to more structured PE lessons
- From teacher-supported movements to greater independence
- From basic movement patterns to more complex combinations
- From individual activities to partner and group activities

Len Almond's research on child development in PE contexts (Almond, 1997) stresses that children need hundreds of hours of varied movement experiences to establish motor competence, beginning in EYFS and continuing throughout primary years.

## Health and Self-Care

The EYFS framework emphasises that Physical Development includes children's understanding of physical health and their capacity to manage their own needs. This encompasses:

### Health Awareness

- Understanding the importance of physical activity for health
- Recognising changes in their bodies during exercise (breathing, heart rate)
- Understanding basic safety principles
- Beginning to understand the importance of healthy eating

### Independence and Self-Care

- Managing toileting needs independently
- Dressing and undressing for PE activities
- Understanding and managing personal hygiene
- Using equipment safely and appropriately

The 2021 EYFS reforms placed increased emphasis on healthy lifestyles, reflecting concerns about childhood obesity and sedentary behaviour documented by Public Health England.

## Learning Environment and Provision

### Indoor Provision

Effective indoor provision for Physical Development includes:

**Continuous Provision Areas**:
- Creative areas with varied mark-making materials and tools
- Construction areas with different sized blocks and materials
- Malleable materials (playdough, clay) for hand strength
- Small world play requiring precise hand movements
- Threading, weaving, and other manipulative activities

**Structured Sessions**:
- Daily physical activities (Wake Up Shake Up, movement songs)
- Dance and creative movement
- Yoga and mindfulness activities
- Obstacle courses and movement circuits

### Outdoor Provision

The EYFS framework states that providers must provide access to outdoor space or, if that is not possible, ensure outings are planned and taken daily. Research by Play England and numerous early years specialists emphasises that outdoor learning is particularly valuable for Physical Development.

**Outdoor Continuous Provision**:
- Large-scale construction materials
- Climbing equipment and balance beams
- Wheeled toys (bicycles, scooters, wheelbarrows)
- Balls and throwing targets of various sizes
- Natural materials for exploration and transport
- Digging and gardening equipment
- Water play requiring pouring and manipulating

**Challenging Physical Activities**:
Susan Capel's work on physical education pedagogy (Capel, 2007) supports the importance of appropriate challenge. Gill Connell's research on risky play demonstrates that children need opportunities for physical challenge to develop competence, risk assessment skills, and resilience.

## Assessment and Observation

### Formative Assessment

The EYFS operates on a primarily formative assessment model, with practitioners observing children during child-initiated and adult-led activities. Effective assessment of Physical Development includes:

**Observation Strategies**:
- Naturalistic observation during free play
- Focused observations of specific movements or skills
- Tracking sheets for developmental milestones
- Video recording for detailed analysis
- Photographs with annotations

**Assessment Documentation**:
- Learning journeys or portfolios
- Online learning journal platforms (Tapestry, Learning Book)
- Physical Development tracking grids
- Communication with parents about development at home

### Summative Assessment

The EYFS Profile is completed at the end of Reception year. For Physical Development, practitioners make best-fit judgements about whether children have met the two Early Learning Goals:

- **Emerging**: Not yet met the ELG
- **Expected**: Has achieved the ELG
- **Exceeding**: No longer used following 2021 reforms; assessment focuses on secure attainment of ELGs

Data from EYFS Profile results (2022) showed that Physical Development is typically one of the prime areas where highest percentages of children achieve expected levels, though this varies significantly by socioeconomic background and SEND status.

## Inclusive Practice and SEND

### Identifying Developmental Concerns

Dawn Penney's research on inclusive pedagogy in PE (Penney, 2002) emphasises that effective early years practice accommodates diverse physical abilities and identifies concerns early. Indicators that may suggest developmental delay or additional needs include:

**Gross Motor Concerns**:
- Significantly delayed achievement of movement milestones
- Poor balance and coordination compared to peers
- Difficulty with spatial awareness and navigation
- Persistent difficulty with activities like climbing or jumping

**Fine Motor Concerns**:
- Limited hand strength or finger isolation
- Persistent difficulty with grasping or manipulating objects
- Unable to use tools (scissors, pencils) when peers can
- Poor hand-eye coordination

### Intervention and Support

Early identification allows for timely intervention, which might include:

**Universal Strategies**:
- Differentiated activities with varied challenge levels
- Additional adult support during physical activities
- Breaking skills into smaller steps
- Extended time for skill development

**Targeted Support**:
- Specialist programmes (e.g., Fizzy, Jiggy Wrigglers)
- Occupational therapy input
- Physiotherapy support
- Regular focused practice sessions

**Specialist Provision**:
- Referral to Child Development Centre
- Assessment for Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD/Dyspraxia)
- Multi-agency planning and support
- Education, Health and Care Plans (EHCP) where appropriate

## Parental Engagement and Home Learning

Research consistently demonstrates that parental engagement enhances developmental outcomes. Effective strategies for supporting Physical Development through home-school partnership include:

### Communication Strategies

- Regular updates about physical development progress
- Sharing photographs and videos of physical activities
- Home learning suggestions aligned with current provision
- Workshops demonstrating fine motor activities
- Information about local physical activity opportunities

### Home Activities

Practitioners should guide parents towards developmentally appropriate activities:

**Gross Motor**:
- Walking, running, climbing at parks and playgrounds
- Swimming and water play
- Cycling, scooting, balancing activities
- Active games and sports
- Dance and movement to music

**Fine Motor**:
- Cooking activities (mixing, cutting, spreading)
- Arts and crafts (cutting, sticking, threading)
- Playing with construction toys and puzzles
- Mark-making and drawing
- Fastening clothes, zips, and buttons

## Current Issues and Research

### Screen Time and Sedentary Behaviour

Public Health England and the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health (2019) have published guidelines highlighting concerns about excessive screen time in early years. Research indicates associations between high screen time and:
- Reduced physical activity levels
- Delayed motor skill development
- Decreased time for active play
- Impact on sleep, which affects physical development

EYFS practitioners should work with families to promote active play over sedentary screen-based activities.

### Pre-Writing Skills

The 2021 EYFS reforms maintained emphasis on holding a pencil effectively "in preparation for fluent writing". However, research by occupational therapists and early years specialists emphasises that children need extensive fine motor development before formal writing instruction. The National Handwriting Association advocates for:

- Strength-building activities (playdough, climbing)
- Hand-eye coordination development
- Bilateral coordination activities
- Crossing the midline exercises
- Mark-making in various media before pencil work

### Physical Activity Levels

Research by the British Heart Foundation (2020) indicates that many children are insufficiently active even in early years. The UK Chief Medical Officers' guidelines recommend that children under five who can walk should be physically active for at least 180 minutes daily, spread throughout the day.

## Links to Wider Curriculum

Physical Development connects substantially with other EYFS areas:

### Communication and Language

Physical activity contexts provide rich opportunities for vocabulary development, following instructions, and social interaction. Movement activities often incorporate songs, rhymes, and positional language.

### Personal, Social and Emotional Development

Physical play develops turn-taking, sharing, risk assessment, resilience, and confidence. Team games and partner activities build cooperation skills.

### Literacy

Fine motor development directly supports emergent writing. Gross motor activities (forming letters with bodies, obstacle courses following story sequences) support phonics and comprehension.

### Mathematics

Physical activities develop spatial awareness, positional language, counting, pattern-making, and understanding of properties like size, weight, and distance.

### Understanding the World

Physical exploration enables children to investigate their environment, understand cause and effect, and learn about their bodies and health.

### Expressive Arts and Design

Dance, creative movement, and manipulating creative materials integrate Physical Development with artistic expression.

## Conclusion

Physical Development in EYFS establishes crucial foundations for lifelong physical literacy, health, and wellbeing. As Margaret Whitehead's research demonstrates, early experiences fundamentally shape children's capacity and motivation for physical activity throughout life. Quality provision requires:

- Rich, varied opportunities for gross and fine motor development
- Indoor and outdoor environments that enable challenging physical activity
- Inclusive practice meeting diverse developmental needs
- Strong partnerships with parents and carers
- Informed practitioners who understand developmental progressions
- Assessment practices that identify and respond to individual needs
- Recognition of Physical Development as a prime area deserving equal emphasis with communication and personal development

By providing high-quality Physical Development opportunities in EYFS, practitioners establish foundations that will support children's success not only in Key Stage 1 PE but throughout their educational journey and adult life.
