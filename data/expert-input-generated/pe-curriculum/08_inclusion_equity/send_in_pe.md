# SEND in PE

## Introduction and Policy Context

Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) provision in Physical Education represents a fundamental aspect of inclusive practice within UK schools. The Children and Families Act 2014 and the SEND Code of Practice (DfE, 2015) establish the legal framework requiring schools to make reasonable adjustments to ensure all pupils can access PE meaningfully. The Equality Act 2010 further mandates that schools must not discriminate against disabled pupils and must make reasonable adjustments to prevent them being disadvantaged.

The National Curriculum for Physical Education (DfE, 2013) states that "teachers should set high expectations for every pupil" and that they should plan "to address potential areas of difficulty and to remove barriers to pupil achievement." This commitment to inclusion aligns with Margaret Whitehead's conception of physical literacy, which emphasises that all individuals, regardless of ability, have the capacity to develop confidence, competence, knowledge and understanding to maintain physical activity throughout their lives.

## Understanding SEND in PE Context

### Categories of Need

The SEND Code of Practice identifies four broad areas of need relevant to PE:

**Communication and Interaction**: Including Autism Spectrum Conditions (ASC), where pupils may struggle with team-based activities, non-verbal communication during game play, and understanding abstract tactical concepts.

**Cognition and Learning**: Including moderate learning difficulties (MLD), severe learning difficulties (SLD), and specific learning difficulties such as dyslexia and dyspraxia. Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD/dyspraxia) affects approximately 5-6% of school-age children and significantly impacts motor skill development and movement competence.

**Social, Emotional and Mental Health Difficulties**: Including ADHD, attachment disorders, and anxiety, which can affect participation, behaviour management in competitive situations, and risk-taking in physical activities.

**Sensory and/or Physical Needs**: Including visual impairment (VI), hearing impairment (HI), physical disabilities, and sensory processing difficulties affecting proprioception, vestibular processing, and tactile sensitivity.

### The Challenge-Skill Framework

David Kirk and colleagues have argued that meaningful PE must balance challenge with capability. For SEND pupils, this requires sophisticated pedagogical approaches that differentiate without diminishing challenge or creating what Penney and Evans (1999) describe as "curriculum stratification" where certain groups experience a diluted curriculum.

## Pedagogical Approaches for SEND in PE

### The STEP Framework

The STEP framework (adapted for UK contexts from Australian models) provides a systematic approach to differentiation:

**Space**: Modifying playing areas (smaller courts, zoned areas, safe spaces for sensory breaks)

**Task**: Simplifying or complexifying rules, skills, and objectives (allowing catches on bounce, alternative scoring systems)

**Equipment**: Adaptive equipment (larger/softer balls, lighter bats, visual markers, audible balls for VI pupils)

**People**: Grouping strategies, peer buddies, adult support ratios, and teaching assistant deployment

### Adapted and Inclusive Models

**Unified Sports Approach**: Based on Special Olympics principles, mixed-ability teams of SEND and non-SEND pupils working collaboratively. Research by Haegele and Sutherland (2015) demonstrates positive impacts on social inclusion and peer attitudes.

**Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)**: Originally developed by Len Almond and Rod Thorpe at Loughborough University, this model emphasises tactical awareness over technical perfection, enabling SEND pupils to contribute strategically even when motor skills are developing.

**Sport Education Model**: Providing structured roles (coach, statistician, manager, referee) allows SEND pupils with physical limitations to participate meaningfully in extended units of work, developing leadership and organisational skills.

**Physical Literacy Approach**: Whitehead's (2010) holistic model emphasises motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding. This framework supports SEND pupils by valuing progress across multiple domains rather than focusing solely on performance outcomes.

## Key Stage-Specific Considerations

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Ages 5-11)

Early identification of movement difficulties is crucial. The Movement Assessment Battery for Children (MABC-2) can identify pupils at risk of DCD. At these stages, focus on:

- Fundamental movement skills (FMS) development through multi-sensory approaches
- Visual supports (task cards, demonstration videos, visual timetables)
- Clear, chunked instructions with checking for understanding
- Sensory circuit activities for pupils with sensory processing needs
- Small-sided games reducing social and spatial demands

**Practical Implication**: Susan Capel emphasises the importance of "teaching by invitation" where pupils can self-select challenge levels, promoting autonomy particularly for SEND pupils who may experience learned helplessness.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Transition to secondary PE presents particular challenges for SEND pupils, including changing facilities, larger groups, and increased emphasis on competitive performance. Strategies include:

- Extended induction programmes with familiarisation to facilities
- Social stories and visual guides for routines (changing, showering)
- Activity passports documenting successful strategies from primary school
- Flexible grouping arrangements avoiding permanent "bottom sets"
- Technology integration (video analysis, fitness apps) providing alternative success pathways

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

SEND pupils often disengage from PE during these years. Dawn Penney's research highlights how curriculum narrowing and GCSE PE options can marginalise SEND pupils. Inclusive practice requires:

- Alternative accreditation pathways (Dynamic New Discovery, ASDAN Personal Development programmes)
- Leadership and coaching qualifications (Sports Leaders UK awards)
- Community sports links and adapted sports opportunities
- Careful consideration of GCSE PE access, with appropriate differentiation and reasonable adjustments

## GCSE and Exam Board Provisions

### Reasonable Adjustments and Access Arrangements

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC/Eduqas) provide guidance on reasonable adjustments for SEND pupils taking GCSE PE:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582)**: Permits modified activities for practical assessments where pupils cannot perform standard activities due to disability. Requires evidence of impairment and normal way of working.

**Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)**: Allows alternative activities from prescribed lists, extra time for written papers (25-50%), use of assistive technology, and practical assessment with adaptations documented through Notification of Modified Assessment.

**OCR GCSE PE (J587)**: Provides extensive guidance on differentiation by task, outcome, and support. Permits video evidence where live moderation is problematic.

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE PE**: Allows readers, scribes, and modified practical assessments with prior approval through Access Arrangements Online system.

### Alternative Accreditation

For pupils unable to access GCSE PE, consider:

- **BTEC Tech Award in Sport** (Pearson): More coursework-based, reducing time-pressured examination stress
- **Entry Level PE qualifications**: Available from all major boards
- **Award Scheme Development and Accreditation Network (ASDAN)**: Offers personal and social development programmes valued by many SEND pupils

## Specific Impairment Strategies

### Autism Spectrum Conditions (ASC)

Research by Grenier and Yeaton (2011) identifies key barriers including unpredictability, sensory overload, and social demands. Effective strategies include:

- Structured, predictable lessons with visual timetables
- Advance warning of changes (substitute teachers, different venues)
- Reduced sensory stimuli (quieter spaces, smaller groups)
- Explicit teaching of social skills and unwritten rules
- Special interests incorporated into PE activities
- Concrete language avoiding idioms and ambiguity

### Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD/Dyspraxia)

The Dyspraxia Foundation and Movement Matters UK provide evidence-based guidance:

- Task analysis breaking skills into component parts
- Cognitive approaches (verbal self-instruction, mental rehearsal)
- Reduced demonstration speed with repeated viewing opportunities
- Additional practice time and over-learning
- Success criteria focused on personal improvement not peer comparison
- Collaboration with occupational therapists

### Visual Impairment (VI)

British Blind Sport resources and the Guide to Physical Education for Pupils with Visual Impairment (RNIB, 2016) recommend:

- Audible equipment (balls with bells, talking cricket balls)
- Tactile markers and high-contrast equipment
- Verbal descriptions supplementing demonstrations
- Sighted guides and peer support
- Goalball and other VI-specific sports
- Environmental modifications (improved lighting, reduced glare)

### Physical Disabilities

Activity Alliance (formerly English Federation of Disability Sport) champions the "inclusion spectrum" approach:

- **Open activities**: All participate in same format with equipment/rule modifications
- **Modified activities**: Amended to allow meaningful participation
- **Parallel activities**: Separate but equal activities in same space
- **Disability sport**: Wheelchair basketball, boccia, sitting volleyball
- **Separate provision**: Specialist activities when inclusion is not beneficial

Wheelchair users require accessible facilities, smooth surfaces for chairs, and activities designed for wheelchair participation from the outset rather than adapted retrospectively.

## The Role of Support Staff

Teaching Assistants (TAs) and Learning Support Assistants (LSAs) are crucial but research by Webster and Blatchford (2013) cautions against over-reliance creating dependency and reducing teacher-pupil interaction. Effective deployment includes:

- TAs briefed on learning objectives, not just behaviour management
- Facilitation rather than direct instruction
- Encouraging peer interaction not TA-pupil isolation
- Fading support as pupils develop independence
- Specialist training in physical intervention (Team Teach, MAPA) where required

## Assessment and Progress Monitoring

### Assessment Principles

Sue Capel's work on assessment in PE emphasises that for SEND pupils:

- **Ipsative assessment** (measuring progress against own previous performance) is more meaningful than normative comparisons
- Process-focused assessment values effort, strategy, and understanding alongside performance
- Multiple assessment methods accommodate different abilities and learning styles
- Assessment for learning strategies (self-assessment, peer assessment) develop metacognitive skills

### Tools and Approaches

- **Personalised Learning Checklists**: Visual progress trackers for FMS and sport-specific skills
- **Video Analysis**: Enables pupils to see own progress over time
- **Verbal Feedback**: Immediate, specific, actionable feedback during lessons
- **Photographic Evidence**: Documentation of participation and achievement for non-verbal pupils
- **Observation Schedules**: Systematic tracking of engagement, participation quality, and social interaction

## Parental Engagement and Multi-Agency Working

The SEND Code of Practice emphasises co-production with parents/carers. For PE specifically:

- Share strategies that work, creating consistency between school and home
- Communicate PE timetables to enable appropriate kit and preparation
- Involve parents in activity selection and target-setting
- Signpost to community inclusive sports opportunities
- Collaborate with health professionals (physiotherapists, occupational therapists, paediatricians)

## Inclusive PE and Physical Literacy Development

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework offers particular value for SEND pedagogy. Rather than deficit-focused approaches, physical literacy emphasises:

- Every individual's capacity for movement development
- Intrinsic motivation through autonomy, competence, and relatedness (Self-Determination Theory)
- Holistic development across physical, psychological, cognitive, and social domains
- Lifelong engagement rather than elite performance

For SEND pupils, this means celebrating progress in confidence, social skills, and understanding alongside physical competence. A physically literate SEND pupil might demonstrate limited technical skill but show enthusiasm, persistence, understanding of health benefits, and commitment to regular activity.

## Barriers and Facilitators: Research Evidence

Research by Fitzgerald (2005, 2009) and Coates and Vickerman (2008, 2013) identifies systemic barriers:

- **Teacher confidence and competence**: Many PE teachers report inadequate initial teacher education for SEND
- **Facility limitations**: Inaccessible changing rooms, outdoor-only spaces affecting pupils with mobility or sensory needs
- **Curriculum pressures**: Examination focus marginalising non-traditional activities
- **Attitudinal barriers**: Low expectations and deficit perspectives

Conversely, facilitators include:

- Continuing professional development (CPD) focused on inclusive pedagogy
- Collaborative planning with SENCOs and specialist teachers
- Access to adapted equipment and specialist coaching
- Whole-school culture valuing diversity and inclusion
- Strong links with disability sport organisations

## The Youth Sport Trust and Inclusion Charter

The Youth Sport Trust's Inclusion 2020 programme (now incorporated into broader work) established principles including:

- Every child's right to high-quality PE
- Personalised approaches recognising individual differences
- Skilled, confident workforce through professional development
- Strong partnerships between education, sport, and health sectors
- Celebration of diversity and challenge to discrimination

## Future Directions and Emerging Practice

### Technology-Enhanced Inclusion

Emerging technologies offer new possibilities:

- **Wearable fitness trackers**: Providing objective, personalised feedback
- **Adaptive gaming**: Seated dance platforms, wheelchair-accessible motion sensors
- **Augmented reality**: Virtual demonstrations and tactical scenarios
- **Communication apps**: Supporting non-verbal pupils in team activities

### Disability Sport Development

British Paralympic Association and Activity Alliance promote sports such as:

- Boccia (target sport for severe physical disabilities)
- Goalball (VI-specific team sport)
- Wheelchair rugby and basketball
- Frame football (modified football for mobility needs)
- Sitting volleyball (for various disabilities)

Exposing all pupils to disability sports challenges deficit perspectives and provides authentic sporting experiences for SEND pupils.

### Pedagogical Research

Current research directions include:

- Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles in PE curriculum design
- Social justice perspectives challenging ableism in PE
- Pupil voice research foregrounding SEND pupils' experiences
- Intersectionality examining how SEND intersects with other identities

## Practical Recommendations for Teachers

1. **Know your pupils**: Understand specific needs, triggers, and successful strategies through comprehensive transition information and ongoing dialogue
2. **Co-plan adaptations**: Involve SEND pupils in designing appropriate modifications, promoting agency and ownership
3. **Differentiation by default**: Plan inclusive activities from the start rather than retrofitting adaptations
4. **Celebrate diversity**: Use diverse role models (Paralympic athletes, disability sport ambassadors) and challenge negative stereotypes
5. **Assess appropriately**: Use multiple assessment methods focusing on personal progress and broader outcomes beyond performance
6. **Develop peer support**: Train peer buddies and create inclusive class cultures where diversity is valued
7. **Maintain high expectations**: Challenge ableist assumptions about what SEND pupils can achieve while providing necessary support
8. **Seek expertise**: Collaborate with SENCOs, specialist teachers, allied health professionals, and disability sport organisations
9. **Continuous professional development**: Engage with research, attend training, and reflect critically on inclusive practice
10. **Monitor impact**: Systematically evaluate inclusion strategies, using pupil feedback and engagement data

## Conclusion

Inclusive PE for SEND pupils represents both an ethical imperative and a pedagogical challenge requiring sophisticated knowledge, reflective practice, and collaborative working. As David Kirk argues, meaningful PE must be accessible to all pupils, with curriculum, pedagogy, and assessment designed to accommodate diversity from the outset. Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework provides a philosophical foundation emphasising capability, development, and lifelong engagement over narrow performance metrics. When effectively implemented, inclusive PE develops not only SEND pupils' physical competence and confidence but also non-SEND pupils' understanding of diversity, empathy, and collaborative skills, ultimately creating more socially just learning environments and physically literate communities.
