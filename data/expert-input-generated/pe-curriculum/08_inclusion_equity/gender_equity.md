# Gender Equity in PE

## Introduction and Context

Gender equity in Physical Education remains a critical concern within UK schools, despite legislative frameworks promoting equality. The Equality Act 2010 provides the legal foundation, requiring schools to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of opportunity, and foster good relations between different groups. However, research by **Dawn Penney** and her colleagues demonstrates that PE remains one of the most heavily gendered subjects in the curriculum, with persistent inequalities in participation, engagement, and outcomes.

The National Curriculum for Physical Education (2014) in England states that all pupils should "develop competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities" and "lead healthy, active lives." This applies equally to all genders, yet achievement of this vision requires deliberate pedagogical and curricular strategies to address deeply embedded gendered practices.

## Historical Context and Key Theoretical Perspectives

### Evolution of Gender in PE

Historically, PE in UK schools was explicitly segregated by sex, with boys participating in team games and competitive sports whilst girls engaged in gymnastics, dance, and aesthetics-focused activities. **David Kirk's** historical analysis in *Schooling Bodies* (2020) traces how these divisions reflected Victorian ideals of masculinity and femininity that persisted well into the late 20th century.

The move towards mixed-sex PE from the 1970s onwards was intended to promote equality, but research by **Dawn Penney and John Evans** (1999) in *Politics, Policy and Practice in Physical Education* demonstrates that simply mixing groups did not eliminate gendered experiences. Their work reveals how curriculum content, teaching approaches, and assessment practices continue to privilege traditionally masculine activities and behaviours.

### Key Theoretical Frameworks

**Margaret Whitehead's** concept of **physical literacy** provides a valuable framework for promoting gender equity. Physical literacy—defined as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life"—is explicitly gender-neutral and focuses on individual development rather than performance in traditionally gendered activities.

**Len Almond's** advocacy for Health-Related Exercise (HRE) and broader definitions of physical competence similarly challenges narrow, competitive, and gendered conceptions of PE. His work emphasises that PE should develop lifelong physical activity habits rather than elite sporting performance, which has particular relevance for addressing gender gaps in post-school physical activity.

## Gender Inequalities: Evidence Base

### Participation Patterns

The Youth Sport Trust's *Girls Active* research programme (2015-2021) identified significant gender disparities in PE and school sport participation:

- Girls are twice as likely as boys to be inactive by age 14
- 45% of girls avoid or skip PE at secondary school compared to 25% of boys
- Key drop-off points occur during Year 8 (age 12-13) for girls
- Girls cite body confidence, menstruation, and curriculum content as barriers

The Women's Sport and Fitness Foundation (now Women in Sport) research demonstrates that these patterns have long-term consequences, with adult women significantly less likely to meet physical activity guidelines than men who were more engaged in school PE.

### Curriculum Content Analysis

**Dawn Penney's** analysis of National Curriculum implementation reveals persistent gender bias in activity selection. Research across UK secondary schools shows:

- Football, rugby, and cricket dominate boys' PE (70-80% curriculum time)
- Netball, hockey, and dance feature prominently in girls' PE
- Mixed-sex PE often defaults to traditionally masculine activities
- "Lifetime activities" (e.g., fitness, outdoor adventure) receive insufficient time for both genders

This gendered curriculum fails both girls and boys. Girls miss opportunities to develop competence in activities valued in wider society, whilst boys are often denied access to aesthetic and expressive movement forms that could enhance their physical literacy.

## Key Stage-Specific Considerations

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Ages 5-11)

Research by **Susan Capel** and colleagues indicates that gender stereotypes in PE begin early. Primary teachers (often non-specialists) may unconsciously reinforce gendered expectations through:

- Language choices (e.g., "throw like a girl")
- Activity selection and organisation
- Differentiation of expectations
- Use of gendered examples and role models

**Practical implications:**
- Ensure balanced representation in demonstrations and examples
- Select gender-neutral equipment (e.g., various-coloured bibs, not "boys v girls")
- Explicitly challenge stereotypes through discussion
- Present diverse role models from all sports
- Focus on individual progress against personal benchmarks
- Teach fundamental movement skills to all pupils equally

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

This critical phase sees the most significant gender divergence in PE engagement. The Association for Physical Education (AfPE) Position Statement on Gender Equity (2020) identifies particular challenges:

- Body image concerns intensify, especially for girls
- Peer dynamics and fear of judgement increase
- Traditional gendered activities become more entrenched
- Physical development differences become more apparent

**Practical implications:**
- Offer genuine choice within activity areas
- Consider varied grouping strategies (mixed, single-sex, ability, interest-based)
- Address changing room and kit anxieties proactively
- Include fitness and health-focused activities that don't require public performance
- Develop inclusive assessment practices that value diverse achievements
- Create "safe spaces" for discussion about bodies, menstruation, and physical activity
- Explicitly teach about women's achievements in sport history

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE PE uptake shows persistent gender imbalances. Department for Education statistics (2022) indicate:

- Boys are 1.5 times more likely to choose GCSE PE than girls
- This gap has remained consistent despite curriculum reforms
- Similar patterns exist for Level 2 qualifications in Sport

**Exam board considerations:**

**AQA GCSE PE** (9-1): The specification includes socio-cultural issues including commercialisation, media, and participation barriers by gender. Teachers should explicitly address how these issues affect different genders differently.

**Edexcel GCSE PE** (9-1): The "Sport Psychology" component includes motivation and confidence—key areas where gender differences emerge. This provides opportunities to explore why girls' confidence in PE often declines.

**OCR GCSE PE** (9-1): Component 2 includes "Socio-cultural influences"—requiring understanding of gender participation patterns and barriers.

**WJEC GCSE PE**: Includes requirements to understand factors affecting participation, providing scope for detailed gender analysis.

**Practical implications for KS4:**
- Ensure coursework/NEA (Non-Examined Assessment) allows diverse activity choices
- Consider whether practical assessment criteria inadvertently favour traditionally masculine attributes
- Use socio-cultural content to critically examine gender issues
- Provide female role models in leadership and officiating roles
- Ensure careers education includes diverse pathways in sport, fitness, and PE

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-Level PE continues to show gender disparities, though less pronounced than GCSE. **David Kirk** notes that A-Level specifications have historically privileged scientific and physiological content over socio-cultural analysis, potentially making the subject less attractive to students interested in critical examination of sport's social dimensions.

## Pedagogical Approaches for Gender Equity

### Curriculum Design

**Dawn Penney's** work on curriculum policy emphasises that equity requires deliberate planning, not just good intentions. Recommendations include:

1. **Audit curriculum breadth**: Ensure all students experience invasion games, net/wall games, striking/fielding games, aesthetic activities, fitness activities, and outdoor adventure activities
2. **Rotation systems**: Implement half-term rotations that prevent permanent gendered tracking
3. **Parallel activities**: Offer genuine choice rather than prescription by gender
4. **Co-educational design**: When teaching mixed groups, select activities less associated with gender stereotypes (e.g., ultimate frisbee, handball, parkour)
5. **Alternative activities**: Include emerging sports and activities without gendered histories (e.g., dodgeball, bench ball, martial arts, yoga)

### Grouping Strategies

Research evidence on mixed-sex versus single-sex PE is nuanced. **Susan Capel** and **Margaret Whitehead** note that neither approach is universally superior; effectiveness depends on activity, age group, school context, and teaching approach.

**Arguments for single-sex grouping:**
- Can increase girls' confidence in traditionally male-dominated activities
- Reduces social comparison and peer pressure
- Allows tailored approach to physiological differences
- May increase participation rates for less confident girls

**Arguments for mixed-sex grouping:**
- Reflects real-world participation contexts
- Challenges gender stereotypes directly
- Prevents implicit hierarchy between boys' and girls' PE
- Supports social cohesion

**Practical recommendation**: Use flexible grouping strategies that vary by activity, unit, and pupil need. AfPE (2020) recommends regular consultation with pupils about grouping preferences and careful monitoring of engagement across different arrangements.

### Language and Communication

Teachers' language powerfully shapes gendered experiences. Research by **Claire Hanlon** (2014) demonstrates that PE teachers unconsciously use different language with boys and girls:

- Boys receive more technical feedback focused on performance
- Girls receive more supportive but less instructionally specific feedback
- Teachers use gendered metaphors ("man up," "hit like a girl")
- Different standards applied to appearance and behaviour

**Strategies for equitable communication:**
- Audit your own language patterns through video self-analysis
- Use names rather than "boys" and "girls" to organise groups
- Provide equally technical and demanding feedback to all pupils
- Challenge pupils who use gendered put-downs
- Avoid using gender as a motivational tool ("beaten by a girl!")

### Assessment Practices

Traditional PE assessment often privileges attributes associated with masculinity: competitive success, physical dominance, aggression, speed, and power. **Dawn Penney and Richard Bailey's** work on assessment in PE argues for broader conceptions of achievement that recognise:

- Improvement relative to starting points
- Leadership and cooperation
- Strategic thinking and decision-making
- Creative and aesthetic qualities
- Commitment and perseverance
- Support for others' learning

The current National Curriculum's focus on developing "competence to excel" should be interpreted broadly to encompass these diverse achievements, supporting **Margaret Whitehead's** holistic physical literacy framework.

## Addressing Specific Barriers for Girls

### Body Confidence and Kit

The Girls Active research identified body confidence as the primary barrier for girls in PE. Women in Sport (2020) research found:

- 75% of girls worry about how they look in PE
- Uniform concerns intensify from Year 7 onwards
- Fear of judgement from peers (especially boys) reduces participation

**Practical strategies:**
- Review PE kit policies—consider allowing options (shorts/tracksuit bottoms, t-shirts/polo shirts)
- Ensure changing facilities provide adequate privacy
- Don't mandate tight or revealing clothing
- Allow pupils to keep t-shirts on during swimming (rash vests)
- Never use physical appearance as a teaching point
- Separate fitness testing from peer observation where possible

### Menstruation

Research by Plan International UK (2017) found that one in seven girls has missed school due to period poverty, with PE often the first lesson skipped. Girlguiding UK research (2018) indicated that menstruation significantly affects PE participation but teachers rarely address it.

**Practical strategies:**
- Normalise discussion of menstruation in PE context
- Ensure sanitary products available in PE changing areas
- Allow flexible participation options during menstruation (e.g., officiating, coaching roles)
- Educate all pupils (including boys) about menstrual cycle effects on exercise
- Never question or challenge menstruation-related absence in front of peers
- Consider menstrual cycle in timing of challenging physical activities or assessments where possible

### Hair and Religious/Cultural Considerations

AfPE's *Religious Observance and Physical Education* guidance (2019) notes particular issues affecting girls from some religious and cultural backgrounds:

- Swimming participation concerns
- Modesty requirements for clothing
- Mixed-sex participation issues
- Hair and hijab considerations

**Practical strategies:**
- Provide single-sex swimming sessions where possible
- Allow full-body swimwear (available from mainstream retailers)
- Accept hijabs and other religious headwear in all activities
- Enable participation in all activities without compromising religious requirements
- Engage families in dialogue about PE participation
- Educate all pupils about religious diversity and respect

## Addressing Gender Issues for Boys

Gender equity is not solely about girls' participation. Research by **Becky Beasley** and others demonstrates that traditional PE can also disadvantage boys who don't conform to hegemonic masculine stereotypes:

- Boys who are less physically competent experience shame and ridicule
- Aesthetic activities (dance, gymnastics) are stigmatised as "feminine"
- Emotional expression and vulnerability are discouraged
- Narrow definitions of sporting success exclude many boys

**Len Almond's** work emphasises that PE should serve all pupils' lifelong health rather than identifying and developing elite performers—a shift that benefits boys who struggle in competitive, traditional PE environments.

**Practical strategies:**
- Challenge homophobic language and behaviour that polices masculinity
- Ensure boys access full curriculum breadth, including dance and aesthetic activities
- Use male role models in traditionally "feminine" activities
- Value cooperation, support, and leadership alongside competitive success
- Create safe spaces for boys to discuss body image and physical confidence
- Recognise diverse body types and physical abilities in teaching examples

## Leadership and Representation

### Staffing and Role Models

The PE teaching profession remains heavily gendered. Youth Sport Trust data shows:

- Secondary PE departments are predominantly male-led
- Female PE teachers are under-represented in senior leadership
- Primary PE premium funding often employs male coaches

**Susan Capel's** research demonstrates that pupils benefit from seeing diverse role models in leadership positions. Female pupils particularly benefit from female PE staff, but all pupils benefit from understanding that sporting expertise and leadership are not gendered.

**Practical implications:**
- Ensure leadership opportunities in PE (sports captains, young leaders, etc.) are equitable
- Use diverse examples when discussing sporting careers and achievements
- Invite female coaches, athletes, and officials to work with pupils
- Highlight women's sporting achievements in displays and discussions
- Ensure female pupils see women in positions of authority in PE

### Extra-Curricular Provision

AfPE research (2019) found significant gender imbalances in extra-curricular sports provision:

- More clubs offered for boys than girls in most secondary schools
- Boys' clubs receive more funding and coaching expertise
- Girls' clubs more likely to be recreational rather than competitive
- Timing and format of clubs may not suit girls' preferences

**Practical strategies:**
- Audit extra-curricular provision for equity
- Consult pupils (especially less active girls) about preferred activities and formats
- Offer girls-only spaces for skill development before mixed participation
- Provide varied formats (recreational, competitive, social)
- Schedule clubs at times that work for diverse pupils
- Ensure equal resource allocation across provision

## Whole-School Approaches

**David Kirk** emphasises that PE cannot address gender equity in isolation from wider school culture. Effective approaches include:

1. **Whole-school equality policy**: Ensure PE features explicitly in equality objectives
2. **Staff training**: Provide CPD on gender equity for all PE staff
3. **Pupil voice**: Regularly consult pupils about their experiences and preferences
4. **Cross-curricular links**: Connect PE with PSHE, citizenship, and equality education
5. **Parent/carer engagement**: Communicate about PE's purposes and challenge gendered assumptions
6. **Governor oversight**: Include PE gender equity in school self-evaluation

The Association for Physical Education's *Curriculum Guidance for Secondary Education* (2021) recommends establishing a PE equity working group including staff, pupils, and governors to monitor and improve practice.

## Monitoring and Evaluation

Effective gender equity work requires systematic monitoring. Key data includes:

- Participation rates in curriculum PE by gender (including absence, non-participation, and notes from home)
- Engagement levels (using systematic observation or pupil surveys)
- Extra-curricular participation by gender
- GCSE/A-Level PE uptake by gender
- Attainment data by gender (both qualifications and curriculum levels)
- Pupil voice data on experiences and perceptions
- Staff deployment and leadership roles

**Dawn Penney** argues that data collection must move beyond simple "counting" to understanding pupils' qualitative experiences. Regular pupil surveys and focus groups provide essential insights into whether equity policies translate into equitable experiences.

## Conclusion and Future Directions

Gender equity in PE requires sustained, deliberate effort to challenge deeply embedded cultural assumptions about bodies, sport, and physical activity. **Margaret Whitehead's** physical literacy framework offers a valuable conceptual foundation, but implementation requires attention to curriculum design, pedagogical practices, assessment approaches, and whole-school culture.

The Women's Sport and Fitness Foundation's *Changing the Game for Girls* report (2012) demonstrated that girls who have positive PE experiences are significantly more likely to be active adults. Given the public health challenges facing the UK—with gender gaps in physical activity contributing to health inequalities—ensuring equitable PE is not merely a matter of fairness but of societal importance.

Future developments should focus on:
- Initial teacher education that foregrounds gender equity
- Continued research on effective pedagogical practices
- Development of curriculum models that transcend gendered activities
- Greater attention to intersectionality (how gender intersects with race, class, disability, and sexuality)
