# Adaptive PE and Inclusive Practice

## Introduction and Policy Context

Adaptive PE and inclusive practice represent fundamental components of quality physical education in the UK, underpinned by both educational philosophy and legal requirements. The Equality Act 2010 mandates that schools must make 'reasonable adjustments' to ensure pupils with disabilities or special educational needs (SEN) can access the full PE curriculum alongside their peers. This legislative framework aligns with the educational principles advocated by Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy, which emphasises that "all individuals have the potential to develop physical literacy" regardless of ability or disability.

The UK National Curriculum for PE (2014) explicitly states that high-quality PE should be "inclusive and accessible to all pupils." This commitment requires teachers to adapt activities, environments, and pedagogical approaches to ensure meaningful participation for pupils with diverse needs, including those with physical disabilities, sensory impairments, autism spectrum conditions, and social, emotional, and mental health (SEMH) difficulties.

The Special Educational Needs and Disability Code of Practice (DfE & DoH, 2015) provides statutory guidance, establishing the graduated approach of 'assess, plan, do, review' that PE teachers must implement when supporting pupils with SEN. This cyclical process ensures continuous refinement of adaptive strategies.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Physical Literacy and Inclusion

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework provides a philosophical foundation for inclusive PE practice. Whitehead (2010, 2013) defines physical literacy as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life." Crucially, this definition is capability-focused rather than performance-focused, recognising that physical literacy develops uniquely in each individual according to their potential.

Whitehead's work emphasises the importance of embodied experiences and the affective domain in PE. For pupils with disabilities or SEN, this means focusing on personal progress, intrinsic motivation, and positive movement experiences rather than comparative performance or standardised outcomes.

### The STEP Framework

The STEP framework (Space, Task, Equipment, People) provides a practical tool for differentiating PE activities. Originally developed by the Youth Sport Trust and refined through practice, STEP enables teachers to systematically adapt lessons:

- **Space**: Modify playing areas, distances, boundaries, or zones
- **Task**: Adjust rules, scoring systems, complexity, or time constraints
- **Equipment**: Change size, weight, texture, colour, or type of equipment
- **People**: Vary groupings, peer support, adult assistance, or number of participants

This framework aligns with David Kirk's (2010) research on curriculum models in PE, which emphasises that effective pedagogy requires flexible, context-responsive teaching approaches rather than rigid, one-size-fits-all methodologies.

## Legal and Statutory Requirements

### Equality Act 2010

The Equality Act establishes three key duties relevant to PE:

1. **Non-discrimination**: Schools cannot treat pupils with disabilities less favourably
2. **Reasonable adjustments**: Schools must take proactive steps to remove barriers to participation
3. **Accessibility planning**: Schools must increase access to the curriculum, physical environment, and information

In PE contexts, reasonable adjustments might include providing specialist equipment (e.g., audible balls for visually impaired pupils), modifying activities (e.g., adapted rules in games), or ensuring accessible facilities (e.g., wheelchair-accessible changing rooms).

### National Curriculum Requirements

The National Curriculum for PE states that teachers should "set high expectations for every pupil" and "plan challenging work for... those with low levels of prior attainment or come from disadvantaged backgrounds" as well as those with SEN and disabilities. At each Key Stage, teachers must ensure access to the statutory requirements whilst recognising that progression will vary between individuals.

## Key Stage Considerations

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Primary)

In primary PE, Dawn Penney's research (2013, 2017) on curriculum design emphasises the importance of establishing inclusive cultures early. At KS1 and KS2, adaptive approaches should focus on:

**Fundamental Movement Skills**: All pupils, including those with disabilities, should develop competence in basic movements (running, jumping, throwing, catching, balance, coordination). Adaptations might include:
- Larger, lighter balls for pupils with coordination difficulties
- Visual cues and demonstrations for pupils with processing difficulties
- Reduced distances or simplified sequences for pupils with physical disabilities
- Peer partnerships for pupils requiring additional support

**Differentiated Success Criteria**: Rather than uniform expectations, teachers should establish personalised learning objectives that recognise starting points and individual potential. Len Almond's work on Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) supports this approach by emphasising tactical awareness and decision-making over technical perfection, making games more accessible to pupils with varying physical abilities.

### Key Stage 3 and 4 (Secondary)

At secondary level, inclusive practice becomes increasingly complex as curriculum content becomes more specialised and social dynamics intensify.

**Key Stage 3**: The statutory requirements include developing technique and performance in activities from athletics, dance, games, gymnastics, outdoor and adventurous activities, and swimming. Susan Capel's research (2007, 2016) on learning to teach PE emphasises the importance of teacher subject knowledge in adapting activities effectively. Teachers must understand both the core concepts of each activity area and the potential barriers for different pupils.

Practical adaptations at KS3 might include:
- Modified invasion games (e.g., wheelchair basketball, sitting volleyball)
- Graded gymnastics sequences with varying complexity levels
- Adapted athletics events (e.g., seated throws, wheelchair racing)
- Differentiated dance stimuli and movement vocabulary
- Accessible outdoor activities with appropriate challenge and support

**Key Stage 4**: When pupils begin GCSE PE (typically Year 10-11), inclusion presents additional considerations. All major examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) permit reasonable adjustments and access arrangements for practical assessments.

## Examination Board Provisions

### AQA GCSE PE (8582)

AQA allows pupils to be assessed in adapted or disability sports for their practical assessment. Activities such as boccia, goalball, polybat, table cricket, and wheelchair basketball are explicitly recognised. The specification states that pupils with disabilities should be assessed "on their ability to demonstrate the relevant skills, techniques and tactics" within their chosen adapted activity, with marks awarded on the same criteria as mainstream activities.

### Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)

Edexcel permits access arrangements including modified equipment, additional time for written examinations, and assessment in adapted activities. The specification emphasises that "all students should be given the opportunity to demonstrate their skills in a competitive situation" and provides guidance on selecting appropriate activities that match pupils' capabilities.

### OCR GCSE PE (J587)

OCR's specification explicitly addresses inclusion, stating that practical activities "should be selected to allow all students to perform to their maximum potential." The board provides detailed guidance on evidence requirements for adapted activities and encourages video evidence where live moderation may be challenging.

### WJEC Eduqas GCSE PE

WJEC Eduqas recognises a wide range of adapted activities and provides specific assessment criteria for disability sports. The specification emphasises that inclusive provision should enable pupils to demonstrate the "application of skills under pressure" regardless of the specific activity format.

## Classification of Special Educational Needs in PE

### Physical Disabilities

Pupils with physical disabilities may include those with cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy, spina bifida, limb deficiencies, or conditions affecting mobility, coordination, or stamina. Adaptive strategies include:

- **Environmental modifications**: Ensuring wheelchair access, appropriate surface selection (e.g., smooth indoor surfaces for wheelchair users)
- **Equipment adaptations**: Velcro straps for equipment attachment, adapted grips, ball bearings on benches for sliding movements
- **Rule modifications**: Allowing additional contacts in volleyball, permitting mobility aids in activities, adjusting court/field dimensions
- **Alternative activities**: Introducing boccia, wheelchair basketball, table cricket, or seated exercise variations

The English Federation of Disability Sport (EFDS), now rebranded as Activity Alliance, provides extensive resources and training for teachers working with pupils with physical disabilities.

### Sensory Impairments

**Visual Impairment**: Adaptations for pupils with partial sight or blindness include:
- Audible balls with bells or beepers
- High-contrast equipment (e.g., yellow balls against blue backgrounds)
- Tactile boundaries and markers
- Verbal descriptions of demonstrations and environments
- Guide runner partnerships in athletics
- Goalball as an inclusive activity specifically designed for visually impaired participants

**Hearing Impairment**: Strategies for deaf or hearing-impaired pupils include:
- Visual signals and demonstrations rather than verbal instructions only
- Positioning pupils to see teacher's face for lip-reading
- British Sign Language (BSL) support where appropriate
- Written instruction cards with diagrams
- Vibration-based starting signals in athletics

### Autism Spectrum Conditions (ASC)

Research by the Autism Education Trust provides PE-specific guidance for supporting pupils with ASC. Key strategies include:

- **Predictable structures**: Clear routines, visual timetables, advanced warnings of changes
- **Sensory considerations**: Awareness of sensory sensitivities (noise levels in sports halls, texture of equipment, physical contact in games)
- **Social communication support**: Explicit teaching of unwritten rules in games, structured peer interactions, social stories about PE scenarios
- **Modified activities**: Reducing unpredictability in games, providing individual activities alongside team options, allowing breaks when overwhelmed

### Social, Emotional, and Mental Health (SEMH) Difficulties

Pupils with SEMH needs may experience anxiety, attachment difficulties, or behavioural challenges. Inclusive approaches include:

- Building positive relationships and trust
- Providing choice and autonomy within structured boundaries
- Recognising that competition may trigger anxiety for some pupils
- Offering cooperative rather than competitive activities when appropriate
- Creating safe, supportive environments where mistakes are normalised
- Being alert to potential trauma responses and de-escalation strategies

## Pedagogical Approaches for Inclusion

### Differentiation Strategies

David Kirk and colleagues' research (2006, 2012) on situated learning in PE emphasises that differentiation must go beyond simple task modification. Effective differentiation encompasses:

**Differentiation by Outcome**: Setting open-ended tasks where pupils demonstrate learning at their own level (e.g., "create a sequence showing changes of direction" allows varied complexity)

**Differentiation by Task**: Providing tiered activities with increasing challenge (e.g., bronze, silver, gold levels in gymnastics)

**Differentiation by Support**: Varying levels of scaffolding, peer assistance, or adult intervention based on individual needs

**Differentiation by Resource**: Offering different equipment options that pupils can select based on their confidence and competence

### Collaborative Learning and Peer Support

Research demonstrates that inclusive PE benefits all pupils when structured appropriately. Strategies include:

- **Peer tutoring**: Pairing more competent pupils with those requiring support (benefits both parties through reinforced learning and social development)
- **Cooperative learning structures**: Activities requiring interdependence where all pupils contribute meaningfully regardless of ability level
- **Mixed-ability grouping**: Avoiding ability-based streaming that can marginalise pupils with SEN
- **Inclusive team formations**: Ensuring teams are balanced and all pupils have meaningful roles

### Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

UDL principles, originally developed in the United States but increasingly influential in UK education, suggest designing learning experiences with diversity in mind from the outset rather than retrofitting adaptations. In PE, this means:

- **Multiple means of representation**: Providing information through visual, auditory, and kinaesthetic channels
- **Multiple means of action and expression**: Allowing varied ways to demonstrate learning (verbal explanation, physical demonstration, written work, video analysis)
- **Multiple means of engagement**: Offering choices, relevance, and authentic challenges that motivate diverse learners

## Assessment and Progress Monitoring

### Formative Assessment in Inclusive PE

Assessment practices must recognise individual starting points and progress rather than only measuring attainment against universal standards. Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy assessment framework emphasises holistic evaluation across four domains:

1. **Physical competence**: Motor skills and movement capabilities relative to individual potential
2. **Confidence and motivation**: Self-efficacy, enjoyment, intrinsic motivation to participate
3. **Knowledge and understanding**: Tactical awareness, rules, health and fitness concepts
4. **Engagement in physical activity**: Participation patterns, lifestyle choices, sustained involvement

For pupils with SEN, assessment should focus particularly on the affective domain (confidence, motivation) as this often determines long-term participation more than physical competence alone.

### Individualised Target Setting

Effective inclusive practice requires personalised learning objectives linked to Education, Health and Care Plans (EHCPs) where applicable. Targets should be:

- **Specific**: Clearly defined, observable behaviours or skills
- **Measurable**: With criteria for success that can be objectively assessed
- **Achievable**: Challenging yet realistic given the pupil's current capabilities
- **Relevant**: Meaningful to the pupil's broader development and life goals
- **Time-bound**: With clear timeframes for review and adjustment

Teachers should collaborate with Special Educational Needs Coordinators (SENCOs), teaching assistants, parents, and external specialists (physiotherapists, occupational therapists) to set appropriate targets.

## Inclusive Practice in Specific Activity Areas

### Games Activities

Dawn Penney's research on curriculum formation highlights that traditional competitive games can create barriers for pupils with disabilities or low confidence. Inclusive games teaching involves:

- Modified small-sided games with adapted rules (e.g., everyone must touch the ball before scoring)
- New Age Kurling, boccia, or polybat as alternatives to traditional striking/fielding games
- Unified Sports approaches where pupils with and without disabilities play together in meaningful roles
- Emphasising tactical understanding and decision-making over physical prowess (TGfU approach)

### Dance

Dance offers particularly rich opportunities for inclusion due to its creative, expressive nature. Strategies include:

- Wheelchair dance and integrated dance techniques
- Task cards with differentiated movement vocabulary (from simple to complex)
- Allowing varied interpretations of stimuli based on individual movement capabilities
- Celebrating diversity in movement rather than seeking uniformity
- Using dance to explore disability awareness and challenge stereotypes

### Athletics

Athletics can be adapted extensively while maintaining core principles of personal best performance:

- Seated throws and wheelchair racing
- Measured distances appropriate to individual abilities
- Multi-event formats where pupils compete in different events based on capabilities
- Peer assessment and timing roles for pupils unable to participate physically in all events
- Focus on personal improvement rather than competitive ranking

### Gymnastics

Gymnastics presents particular challenges but also opportunities for differentiation:

- Floor-based work for pupils unable to use apparatus
- Graded sequences with varying complexity and apparatus heights
- Body management and core stability work adapted to individual needs
- Partner and group work where pupils contribute according to their capabilities
- Use of specialist equipment like physio balls, wedges, and crash mats

### Outdoor and Adventurous Activities (OAA)

OAA often proves highly inclusive when appropriately designed:

- Orienteering with visual or tactile maps, shortened courses, or team formats
- Problem-solving initiatives where cognitive and social skills are as important as physical abilities
- Accessible outdoor environments and adapted equipment (e.g., all-terrain wheelchairs)
- Emphasis on teamwork, communication, and leadership rather than physical prowess alone

## Teacher Development and Training

Susan Capel's extensive research on PE teacher education emphasises that many teachers feel inadequately prepared to teach pupils with SEN effectively. Professional development should address:

### Initial Teacher Education (ITE)

PGCE and undergraduate PE programmes must include substantial SEN and disability content, including:
- Placements in inclusive settings or specialist schools
- Disability awareness training and simulation experiences
- Practical workshops on adaptive PE techniques
- Understanding of relevant legislation and SEND Code of Practice

### Continuing Professional Development (CPD)

Practising teachers benefit from:
- Specialist qualifications such as those offered by Activity Alliance
- In-school training from SEND specialists or external agencies
- Collaborative planning with SENCOs and teaching assistants
- Action research projects focusing on inclusive practice
- Networks and communities of practice sharing inclusive PE strategies

## Role of Teaching Assistants

Teaching assistants (TAs) frequently support pupils with SEN in PE lessons. Effective deployment requires:

- **Pre-lesson briefing**: TAs must understand learning objectives, their specific role, and how to fade support to promote independence
- **Training**: TAs benefit from PE-specific training on activity adaptations and safety considerations
- **Positioning**: TAs should facilitate peer interaction rather than creating dependency or social isolation
- **Communication**: Regular dialogue between teachers and TAs to evaluate effectiveness and adjust approaches

Research by Webster, Russell, and Blatchford (2016) warns against over-reliance on TA support, which can inadvertently reduce curriculum access and peer interaction. The goal should be enabling independence and participation rather than constant adult proximity.

## Practical Resources and Equipment

### Specialist Equipment

Investment in adapted equipment significantly enhances inclusion:
- Audible and textured balls of various sizes
- Adapted bats, rackets, and implements with modified grips
- Lightweight and foam alternatives to traditional equipment
- Sports wheelchairs (if budget permits) or adapted games that can be played from personal wheelchairs
- Visual markers, cones, and boundaries in contrasting colours
- Communication cards and visual timetables
