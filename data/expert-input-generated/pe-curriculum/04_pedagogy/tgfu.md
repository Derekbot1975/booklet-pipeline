# Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)

## Introduction and Historical Context

Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) is a pedagogical approach that emerged in the 1980s at Loughborough University through the work of David Bunker and Rod Thorpe. This approach fundamentally challenged traditional technique-based games teaching by prioritising tactical awareness and decision-making before technical skill development. TGfU represents a significant shift from the 'skill-drill-game' model that had dominated physical education for decades.

Len Almond, a key figure in UK physical education pedagogy, was instrumental in developing and disseminating TGfU principles throughout British schools. The approach gained significant traction following Bunker and Thorpe's seminal 1982 paper 'A Model for the Teaching of Games in Secondary Schools', published in the Bulletin of Physical Education. This model placed game appreciation and tactical awareness at its core, arguing that children often left schools with well-rehearsed techniques but limited understanding of when and how to apply them in authentic game situations.

## The TGfU Model

### Core Components

The original TGfU model consists of six interconnected stages arranged in a pedagogical sequence:

1. **Game**: Modified or small-sided games appropriate to learners' developmental stage
2. **Game Appreciation**: Understanding rules, tactics and strategies within game contexts
3. **Tactical Awareness**: Recognising problems within games requiring solutions
4. **Making Appropriate Decisions**: Selecting what to do (decision-making) and how to do it (skill execution)
5. **Skill Execution**: Developing and refining techniques within meaningful game contexts
6. **Performance**: Applying learned concepts back to game situations

This cyclical model emphasises that technique development emerges from understanding tactical problems, rather than being taught in isolation before game play begins.

### Game Categories

TGfU classifies games into four primary categories, each sharing similar tactical principles:

- **Invasion Games**: Football, rugby, basketball, hockey, netball
- **Net/Wall Games**: Tennis, badminton, volleyball, squash, table tennis
- **Striking/Fielding Games**: Cricket, rounders, softball
- **Target Games**: Golf, bowls, archery

Understanding these categories enables teachers to develop transferable tactical knowledge across similar games, a principle David Kirk has emphasised in his work on curriculum planning and pedagogical innovation.

## UK National Curriculum Alignment

### Key Stage 2

The National Curriculum for Physical Education (DfE, 2013) requires pupils to "play competitive games, modified where appropriate, and apply basic principles suitable for attacking and defending". TGfU directly addresses this requirement by teaching tactical concepts through modified games that emphasise understanding over premature technical refinement.

At KS2, TGfU approaches might involve:
- Reducing playing areas to increase decision-making opportunities
- Modifying rules to emphasise specific tactical problems
- Using smaller teams (3v3 or 4v4) to maximise participation and tactical engagement
- Introducing games with simplified scoring systems that reward tactical play

### Key Stage 3

The KS3 curriculum states pupils should "develop their technique and improve their performance in competitive sports". TGfU interprets 'technique' broadly to include tactical decision-making and game understanding alongside motor skills. Susan Capel, in her extensive work on learning to teach PE (particularly 'Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School'), emphasises that technique divorced from context becomes meaningless.

### Key Stage 4

At GCSE level, all major examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) require students to demonstrate both practical performance and theoretical understanding of games. TGfU provides a framework for connecting these domains:

- **AQA GCSE PE**: Assessment criteria include "the application of skills" and "decision-making in competitive situations"
- **OCR GCSE PE**: Specification explicitly references "tactics and strategies" within practical assessment
- **Edexcel GCSE PE**: Requires demonstration of "the ability to select and apply appropriate skills, tactics and compositional ideas"

### Key Stage 5

A-Level specifications increasingly recognise TGfU principles within sports psychology and skill acquisition content:

- **AQA A-Level PE**: Section 3.1.1.3 covers "teaching styles" including discovery learning approaches aligned with TGfU
- **OCR A-Level PE**: Content on "principles and theories of learning and performance" connects to TGfU pedagogy
- **Edexcel A-Level PE**: Units on "skill acquisition" examine how tactical understanding develops

## Theoretical Underpinnings

### Constructivism and Discovery Learning

TGfU is fundamentally rooted in constructivist learning theory, where learners actively construct knowledge through problem-solving rather than passively receiving information. This aligns with Jerome Bruner's discovery learning principles and Jean Piaget's emphasis on cognitive development through active engagement.

David Kirk's work, particularly in 'Physical Education Futures' (2010), positions TGfU within broader educational reform movements emphasising student-centred learning and authentic assessment. Kirk argues that TGfU addresses the "relevance gap" between decontextualised skill practice and authentic game performance.

### Situated Learning Theory

TGfU embodies situated learning principles articulated by Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger, where learning occurs within authentic contexts that mirror real-world application. Rather than practising chest passes in isolation, students learn passing within game situations requiring tactical decisions about timing, direction and force.

### Connection to Physical Literacy

Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy—"the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2010)—finds natural expression through TGfU. By emphasising understanding and decision-making alongside movement competence, TGfU develops the cognitive and affective dimensions of physical literacy that purely technical approaches neglect.

Whitehead's work, particularly 'Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse' (2010), argues that meaningful engagement in physical activity requires understanding its purposes and pleasures. TGfU's game-centred approach makes these meanings explicit and accessible to learners.

## Pedagogical Strategies

### Questioning Techniques

Effective TGfU implementation relies heavily on strategic questioning that develops tactical awareness. Teachers should employ:

- **Convergent questions**: "Where is the space?" "Which player is unmarked?"
- **Divergent questions**: "What might happen if we spread out wider?" "How could we create more space?"
- **Evaluative questions**: "Did that tactic work? Why/why not?"

Dawn Penney's research on assessment in physical education emphasises that such questioning makes learning visible and enables formative assessment of understanding, not just performance.

### Game Modification Principles

Effective game modifications follow the "STEP" framework (Space, Task, Equipment, Players):

- **Space**: Adjusting playing area size to increase/decrease tactical complexity
- **Task**: Modifying rules to emphasise specific tactical problems (e.g., must complete three passes before shooting)
- **Equipment**: Using different balls, goal sizes or targets to match learner capabilities
- **Players**: Varying team sizes or introducing numerical advantages/disadvantages

Len Almond's work emphasises that modifications should always maintain the essential tactical structure of the game category whilst being developmentally appropriate.

### Exaggeration Games

These deliberately exaggerate specific tactical problems to make them explicit:

- **Width Attack (Football)**: Extra points for goals scored from crosses, emphasising width in attack
- **Endball (Invasion games)**: Scoring by passing to teammates in endzones, emphasising support play
- **King/Queen Court (Net games)**: Players advance through courts, emphasising consistency and placement

## Practical Implementation

### Lesson Structure

A typical TGfU lesson follows this sequence:

1. **Introduction** (5 minutes): Present tactical problem through questioning or demonstration
2. **Initial Game** (8-10 minutes): Modified game highlighting the tactical problem
3. **Freeze/Questioning** (3-5 minutes): Stop play to discuss tactical solutions
4. **Skill Practice** (8-10 minutes): Contextualised practice of techniques needed to solve the problem
5. **Game Application** (10-12 minutes): Return to modified game to apply solutions
6. **Plenary** (3-5 minutes): Review tactical learning and transfer to other games

### Differentiation Strategies

TGfU naturally accommodates diverse abilities through:

- **By task**: Different game modifications for different groups
- **By outcome**: Same game, different expectations for tactical sophistication
- **By support**: Peer coaching, questioning individuals at appropriate cognitive levels
- **By grouping**: Mixed-ability groupings where tactical understanding can compensate for technical limitations

Susan Capel's work on inclusive PE emphasises that TGfU can be particularly effective for less physically skilled students, as tactical intelligence and decision-making are equally valued alongside motor execution.

## Research Evidence and Effectiveness

### Academic Research Base

Substantial research supports TGfU's effectiveness:

- **Light (2013)**: 'Game Sense: Pedagogy for Performance, Participation and Enjoyment' provides comprehensive evidence that TGfU increases student motivation and understanding
- **Harvey and Jarrett (2014)**: Research in Sport, Education and Society demonstrated improved decision-making and game performance compared to traditional approaches
- **Kirk and MacPhail (2002)**: 'Teaching Games for Understanding and Situated Learning' showed enhanced transfer of tactical knowledge across similar game categories

### Comparative Studies

UK-based research comparing TGfU with traditional technique-first approaches consistently shows:

- Equivalent or superior skill development within game contexts
- Significantly improved tactical decision-making and game understanding
- Higher levels of student enjoyment and motivation
- Better retention of learning over time
- Enhanced transfer of tactical concepts across games

However, researchers including Richard Bailey (in 'Physical Education and Sport in Schools', 2001) note that TGfU requires greater pedagogical expertise and may initially challenge teachers accustomed to direct instruction methods.

## Challenges and Criticisms

### Practical Challenges

Teachers implementing TGfU commonly encounter:

- **Class management**: Game-based learning with multiple groups requires sophisticated organisational skills
- **Noise and activity levels**: Active games create environments some schools perceive as 'chaotic'
- **Assessment complexity**: Evaluating tactical understanding is more complex than measuring isolated skill execution
- **Time constraints**: Developing understanding through discovery requires patience and extended engagement

Dawn Penney's research on curriculum and pedagogy in physical education highlights that school cultures emphasising control and quietness can inadvertently discourage TGfU approaches.

### Theoretical Criticisms

Critics, including some sport pedagogy researchers, argue that:

- TGfU may delay technical mastery for talented performers who require early specialisation
- Some skills are sufficiently complex to require isolated practice before game application
- The approach assumes all students can access tactical concepts cognitively, potentially disadvantaging some learners

David Kirk addresses these criticisms in 'Physical Education Futures', arguing that TGfU is not dogmatic—technique practice remains important but is sequenced after establishing tactical purpose.

## Contemporary Developments

### Game-Centred Approaches (GCA)

TGfU has evolved into a family of related pedagogical models collectively termed Game-Centred Approaches:

- **Play Practice**: Launder's Australian adaptation emphasising game focus
- **Game Sense**: Australian interpretation with explicit focus on questioning
- **Tactical Games Approach**: North American model by Griffin, Mitchell and Oslin emphasising tactical problems
- **Invasion Games Competence Model**: Ellis's framework specifically for invasion games

These variations share TGfU's core philosophy whilst adapting to different contexts and preferences.

### Sport Education Model

David Kirk and Daryl Siedentop's Sport Education model complements TGfU by embedding game-centred learning within authentic sporting contexts featuring seasons, team affiliations, and student roles (coaches, managers, statisticians). Research by Penny and colleagues demonstrates that combining TGfU with Sport Education enhances both tactical development and broader educational outcomes including leadership and social skills.

### Digital Technology Integration

Contemporary TGfU practice increasingly incorporates technology:

- **Video analysis**: Tablets enabling immediate game footage review for tactical discussion
- **Movement tracking**: GPS and accelerometry data informing tactical positioning
- **Interactive whiteboards**: Visualising tactical concepts and game scenarios

However, as Susan Capel notes in recent editions of 'Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School', technology should enhance rather than replace the fundamental pedagogical relationships and physical engagement at TGfU's core.

## Assessment Considerations

### Formative Assessment

TGfU naturally supports assessment for learning through:

- **Observation**: Monitoring decision-making within game situations
- **Questioning**: Assessing tactical understanding through dialogue
- **Peer assessment**: Students evaluating teammates' tactical decisions using criteria
- **Self-assessment**: Reflective journals documenting tactical learning

Dawn Penney's work on assessment in physical education emphasises that TGfU makes cognitive learning visible and assessable, addressing the historic over-emphasis on purely physical performance.

### Summative Assessment

For GCSE/A-Level assessment:

- **Practical performance**: Can assess both execution and decision-making using criteria referencing tactical appropriateness
- **Coursework**: Students analyse tactics and strategies, demonstrating game understanding
- **Examination questions**: AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications include questions on tactical decision-making and game analysis

## Professional Development Implications

### Teacher Knowledge Requirements

Effective TGfU teaching requires:

- Deep understanding of game categories and their tactical similarities
- Sophisticated questioning skills to scaffold tactical thinking
- Flexibility to modify games responsively based on student understanding
- Confidence to embrace productive 'chaos' whilst maintaining purposeful learning

The Association for Physical Education (afPE) provides CPD resources specifically addressing TGfU pedagogy, including the 'Health Position Paper' (2015) which positions TGfU within broader health and wellbeing objectives.

### Initial Teacher Education

Susan Capel's research on learning to teach PE highlights that many beginning teachers default to traditional technique-focused approaches because:

- Their own school experiences modelled such approaches
- Direct instruction feels safer for behaviour management
- TGfU's complexity requires pedagogical confidence that develops with experience

ITE programmes must therefore provide sustained, supported practice in TGfU implementation, not merely theoretical introduction.

## Conclusion and Future Directions

Teaching Games for Understanding represents a mature, research-evidenced pedagogical approach fundamentally aligned with contemporary UK National Curriculum requirements and examination specifications. Its emphasis on tactical understanding, decision-making, and authentic game engagement addresses both motor competence and cognitive development dimensions of physical literacy that Margaret Whitehead emphasises as essential for lifelong physical activity engagement.

As David Kirk argues, TGfU's future lies not in replacing all other approaches but in forming part of a pedagogically diverse curriculum where different models serve different purposes. Teachers must become "pedagogical innovators" selecting and adapting approaches to their specific contexts, students, and learning intentions.

The approach's emphasis on understanding over mere performance, participation over exclusion, and transferable tactical concepts over isolated skills positions it as particularly relevant for 21st-century PE that must justify its educational contribution beyond simple activity provision. As Dawn Penney's curriculum research demonstrates, TGfU exemplifies the kind of intellectually rigorous, student-centred pedagogy that establishes physical education as a legitimate educational endeavour rather than merely supervised play.

For UK teachers, TGfU provides a practical framework for delivering National Curriculum requirements whilst developing the tactical awareness, decision-making capabilities, and game understanding that examination specifications increasingly demand and that authentic sports participation requires.
