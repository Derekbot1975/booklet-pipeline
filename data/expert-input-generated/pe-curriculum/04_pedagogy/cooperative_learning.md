# Cooperative Learning in PE

## Introduction and Theoretical Foundations

Cooperative learning represents a pedagogical approach that structures physical education to promote interdependence, shared responsibility, and collective achievement among learners. Unlike traditional competitive models that dominated UK PE throughout much of the 20th century, cooperative learning emphasises students working together toward common goals whilst developing both motor competencies and social skills.

Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy provides a robust framework for understanding cooperative learning's value in PE. Physical literacy encompasses motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life. Cooperative learning addresses all these domains simultaneously, particularly the affective and social dimensions that Whitehead argues are fundamental to lifelong physical activity participation.

David Kirk's work on democratic pedagogy in PE aligns closely with cooperative learning principles. In his influential text *Physical Education Futures* (2010), Kirk critiques traditional multi-activity, sport-based programmes that privilege the already-skilled and advocates for more inclusive approaches. Cooperative learning structures challenge the hierarchies Kirk identifies, redistributing participation and success opportunities more equitably across ability levels.

## Defining Characteristics of Cooperative Learning in PE

Cooperative learning is distinguished from mere group work by five essential elements, adapted for PE contexts from the foundational work of Johnson and Johnson:

### Positive Interdependence

Students perceive they need each other to complete tasks successfully. In PE, this might involve creating games where all team members must touch the ball before scoring, or designing fitness challenges where group success depends on collective improvement rather than individual performance. Len Almond's advocacy for modified games and Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) naturally incorporates positive interdependence through rule structures that necessitate cooperation.

### Individual Accountability

Each student remains responsible for contributing and learning. This prevents the common pitfall where more skilled students dominate whilst others become passive. Teachers might assign specific roles (e.g., defender, encourager, recorder), use coloured bibs to track individual contributions, or implement reflection journals where students document personal learning within cooperative contexts.

### Face-to-Face Promotive Interaction

Students actively support each other's learning through encouragement, sharing resources, and providing feedback. In PE, this manifests through peer coaching, reciprocal teaching episodes, and structured observation tasks where students help partners refine technique or tactical understanding.

### Social Skills Development

Explicit teaching of communication, conflict resolution, leadership and trust-building. Dawn Penney's research emphasises that social learning outcomes deserve equal status with physical outcomes in curriculum planning. Cooperative learning makes these skills visible and assessable rather than leaving them as incidental by-products.

### Group Processing

Structured time for groups to reflect on how effectively they worked together and identify improvements. This metacognitive element distinguishes cooperative learning from unstructured play or poorly planned group activities.

## National Curriculum Connections

The National Curriculum for Physical Education in England (DfE, 2013) provides explicit opportunities for cooperative learning across all key stages:

### Key Stage 1 and 2

The curriculum requires pupils to "participate in team games, developing simple tactics for attacking and defending" and to "perform dances using simple movement patterns." Cooperative learning structures support these aims whilst also addressing the broader purpose statement that PE should help pupils "develop competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities" and "lead healthy, active lives."

Cooperative games at KS1 might include parachute activities requiring synchronised movement, or modified invasion games where passing sequences must be completed before scoring attempts. At KS2, more complex cooperative challenges emerge: orienteering tasks requiring navigation and communication, gymnastics compositions demanding coordinated sequences, or athletic relay events emphasising smooth baton exchanges and strategic runner positioning.

### Key Stage 3

The KS3 programme of study requires students to "develop their technique and improve their performance in competitive sports" whilst also learning to "analyse their performances compared to previous ones and demonstrate improvement to achieve their personal best." Cooperative learning facilitates both requirements through peer assessment protocols, cooperative practice structures, and group goal-setting.

Susan Capel's extensive work on PE pedagogy, particularly in *Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School* (2020, 7th edition), provides practical guidance for implementing cooperative approaches at KS3. She emphasises how cooperative structures support differentiation, allowing mixed-ability groups to work productively on common tasks with scaffolded roles.

### Key Stage 4

At GCSE level, all major examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) include analysis and evaluation of performance as core assessment objectives. Cooperative learning provides authentic contexts for developing these analytical skills. For example:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582)** requires students to "analyse and evaluate performance" and "understand the key socio-cultural influences that can affect people's involvement in physical activity and sport." Cooperative learning addresses both through structured peer analysis tasks and by creating inclusive environments that challenge traditional participation barriers.

**Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)** emphasises "applying knowledge to evaluate and improve performance." Cooperative practice structures where students work in pairs or small groups to refine technique or solve tactical problems directly support this objective.

**OCR GCSE PE (J587)** includes "socio-cultural influences" as a major topic area. Cooperative learning provides lived experience of positive group dynamics, fair play, and inclusive practice that enriches theoretical understanding.

### Key Stage 5

A-Level PE specifications include deeper psychological and sociological analysis. Cooperative learning provides practical contexts for exploring:

- **Group dynamics and cohesion** (Steiner's model, Tuckman's stages) through observing how cooperative groups form and function
- **Social facilitation and inhibition** by comparing individual versus cooperative performance contexts
- **Attribution theory and motivation** through structured reflection on cooperative successes and challenges

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Cooperative Learning Structures for PE

Several specific structures have proven effective in PE contexts:

**Jigsaw Method**: Each group member becomes an "expert" in one aspect of a complex skill or tactic (e.g., in basketball: shooting technique, defensive positioning, passing options, rebounding). Students teach their specialty to groupmates, ensuring everyone contributes essential knowledge.

**Think-Pair-Share**: After a demonstration or task, students individually consider a question (e.g., "What made that pass effective?"), discuss with a partner, then share with a larger group. This structure increases participation beyond traditional teacher-led questioning where only one student responds.

**Reciprocal Teaching**: Students work in pairs with defined roles—performer and observer/coach. The observer uses criteria sheets to provide feedback, then roles reverse. This develops analytical skills whilst maximising productive practice time.

**Cooperative Base Groups**: Stable, heterogeneous groups of 4-5 students who work together throughout a unit or term. Base groups provide social support, accountability, and continuity particularly valuable for less confident students.

**Numbered Heads Together**: Each group member receives a number. After a task or question, groups confer to ensure everyone can respond, then the teacher calls a random number to answer. This maintains individual accountability within group work.

### Planning Considerations

Len Almond's work on curriculum planning emphasises that pedagogical approach must align with learning intentions. When planning cooperative learning episodes, teachers should consider:

**Task Design**: Activities must genuinely require cooperation rather than allowing students to work independently in proximity. Poorly designed tasks undermine cooperative structures. For example, a relay race may simply queue students rather than promote cooperation; modifying rules so all runners must stay within 2 metres of each other transforms the task into a genuine cooperative challenge.

**Group Composition**: Research suggests heterogeneous grouping (mixing abilities, genders, backgrounds) typically produces stronger learning outcomes than homogeneous groups, though this requires careful facilitation to prevent dominance patterns. Single-sex groupings may sometimes be appropriate when addressing specific participation barriers, particularly at KS3 and KS4 where research by Dawn Penney and others has identified gendered engagement patterns.

**Assessment Alignment**: If cooperative skills are learning objectives, they require assessment. This might include peer assessment rubrics, self-reflection tools, teacher observation of group interactions, or video analysis of cooperative episodes. The assessment must value cooperative processes, not merely outcomes.

### Differentiation Within Cooperative Learning

Cooperative structures inherently support differentiation by allowing students to contribute according to their current capabilities whilst working toward common goals. Specific strategies include:

**Role Differentiation**: Assigning roles with varying physical demands (e.g., in modified cricket: bowler, wicket-keeper, fielder, scorer, coach) allows all students to participate meaningfully.

**Task Complexity Variation**: Groups tackle the same challenge type but at different complexity levels (e.g., all groups create gymnastics sequences, but requirements vary: some must include 3 balances, others must include 5 balances plus transitions).

**STEP Framework**: Using Space, Task, Equipment, and People variables, teachers can modify activities to maintain cooperative structure whilst adjusting challenge. For instance, changing court size (Space), requiring specific passes before shooting (Task), using different sized balls (Equipment), or adjusting group size (People).

## Research Evidence and Effectiveness

Substantial research evidence supports cooperative learning in PE, though as David Kirk notes, context matters significantly. What works in one setting may require adaptation elsewhere.

### Academic and Motor Learning Outcomes

Studies compiled in systematic reviews (e.g., Casey and Goodyear's work published in *Sport, Education and Society*, 2015) demonstrate that cooperative learning produces comparable or superior motor skill development relative to traditional direct instruction when implemented with fidelity. Importantly, cooperative approaches simultaneously develop tactical understanding and decision-making capabilities often neglected in technique-focused instruction.

### Social and Affective Outcomes

Research consistently shows cooperative learning enhances:

**Peer relationships and social inclusion**: Students develop stronger cross-group friendships and more positive attitudes toward classmates with disabilities or from different backgrounds. This aligns with Margaret Whitehead's emphasis on confidence and positive motivation as physical literacy foundations.

**Self-esteem and perceived competence**: Particularly for lower-skilled students who experience greater success and recognition in cooperative versus competitive structures. This addresses persistent participation inequalities that Dawn Penney's research has documented.

**Communication and leadership skills**: Explicit development of these capabilities supports broader educational goals beyond PE-specific outcomes.

### Challenges and Limitations

Research also identifies implementation challenges:

**Teacher expertise requirements**: Effective cooperative learning demands sophisticated classroom management, task design, and facilitation skills. Susan Capel's work emphasises this in initial teacher education contexts—student teachers often struggle with the apparent chaos of multiple groups working simultaneously.

**Time intensity**: Establishing cooperative norms, teaching social skills explicitly, and allowing sufficient processing time requires sustained investment. This tensions with packed curricula and external pressures for measurable performance outcomes.

**Student resistance**: Students acculturated to competitive PE structures may initially resist cooperative approaches, particularly high-skilled students who enjoyed advantaged positions. Careful introduction and clear rationale are essential.

**Assessment complexity**: Assessing both individual and group dimensions whilst maintaining validity and reliability challenges teachers. Traditional assessment tools designed for individual performance require adaptation.

## Connection to Broader Pedagogical Approaches

Cooperative learning intersects with several significant movements in contemporary PE:

### Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)

Len Almond and colleagues' TGfU approach, which emphasises tactical understanding and decision-making over isolated technique, naturally incorporates cooperative learning. Game appreciation and tactical awareness develop most effectively through collaborative problem-solving rather than individual drill.

### Sport Education

Daryl Siedentop's Sport Education model, adapted for UK contexts by researchers including David Kirk, structures PE units as "seasons" with stable teams, assigned roles (players, coaches, referees, statisticians), and culminating events. This model embeds cooperative learning within authentic sport participation contexts.

### Physical Literacy Development

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework positions motivation, confidence, and social interaction as equally important to physical competence. Cooperative learning addresses these affective domains whilst developing movement capabilities, making it particularly aligned with physical literacy aims.

### Models-Based Practice

The broader movement toward Models-Based Practice in PE, advocated by researchers including Kathleen Armour, positions cooperative learning as one pedagogical model among several that teachers might deploy strategically depending on learning objectives and context. This represents a shift from seeking single "best" approaches toward building pedagogical flexibility.

## Practical Applications Across Activity Areas

### Invasion Games

Cooperative learning aligns naturally with invasion games' inherent team structure. Specific applications include:

- Modified games requiring minimum passes before shooting
- Small-sided games with rotating positions ensuring all students experience varied roles
- Tactical problem-solving tasks where groups develop solutions to specific game situations (e.g., "How can we maintain possession against pressure?")
- Peer coaching partnerships during skill practice stations

### Gymnastics and Dance

These aesthetic activities benefit from cooperative approaches:

- Partner and group sequences requiring synchronisation and interdependence
- Compositional tasks where groups create sequences meeting specified criteria
- Peer feedback protocols using observation criteria sheets
- Mirror and canon work developing spatial awareness and cooperative timing

### Athletics

Individual-focused athletics can incorporate cooperation through:

- Relay events emphasising smooth exchanges and strategic placement
- Team athletics formats combining individual performances toward group totals
- Peer coaching during technique practice
- Cooperative goal-setting where groups identify collective improvement targets

### Outdoor and Adventurous Activities

These inherently cooperative activities include:

- Orienteering tasks requiring navigation, communication and shared decision-making
- Problem-solving initiatives (e.g., crossing rivers with limited equipment)
- Trust activities developing group cohesion
- Expedition planning requiring coordinated preparation and execution

### Health-Related Exercise

Fitness contexts benefit from cooperative structures:

- Circuit training with partner encouragement and form monitoring
- Group fitness challenges with collective improvement goals
- Cooperative competition between groups rather than individuals
- Peer-led warm-ups and cool-downs with rotating leadership

## Assessment and Monitoring Progress

Assessment of cooperative learning requires attention to both process and product dimensions:

### Formative Assessment Approaches

**Teacher observation**: Using structured observation tools focusing on specific cooperative behaviours (e.g., encouraging communication, sharing equipment, rotating roles fairly). Susan Capel recommends focusing on 3-4 students per lesson rather than attempting to observe all simultaneously.

**Video analysis**: Recording cooperative episodes allows students to analyse their own interactions, identifying effective cooperation and improvement areas.

**Student self-assessment**: Reflection journals or structured prompts where students assess their own cooperative contributions and set goals.

**Peer assessment**: Using rubrics, students provide feedback on groupmates' cooperative behaviours, developing evaluative skills whilst promoting accountability.

### Summative Assessment

For GCSE and A-Level contexts where formal assessment is required:

**AQA GCSE PE**: Coursework components assessing "analysis and evaluation of performance" can include cooperative dimensions—students might analyse how effective cooperation contributed to team performance or evaluate their own role within group contexts.

**Practical performance assessment**: Where examination boards require demonstrated performance in team activities (e.g., football, netball), cooperative capabilities (communication, supporting teammates, tactical awareness) form part of assessment criteria alongside individual technique.

**Written examinations**: Questions about group dynamics, cohesion, social facilitation, and cooperation appear regularly across all boards' theory papers, with students' practical cooperative experiences enriching theoretical understanding.

## Inclusion and Equity Considerations

Cooperative learning offers particular promise for advancing inclusion and equity in PE, longstanding concerns in research by Dawn Penney, David Kirk, and others:

### Gender Equity

Traditional competitive PE structures often perpetuate gendered participation patterns. Cooperative approaches can challenge these by:

- Valuing diverse contributions beyond dominant physicality
- Creating mixed-gender groups with structured roles preventing male dominance
- Emphasising communication, strategy and mutual support alongside physical performance
- Providing alternatives to traditionally masculine competitive models

### Ability Inclusion

Students with disabilities or lower physical competence often experience marginalisation in competitive PE. Cooperative learning addresses this through:

- Task structures where varied abilities contribute to group success
- Emphasis on personal improvement within group contexts rather than comparative performance
- Roles matched to individual capabilities within interdependent structures
- Positive status creation for typically marginalised students through expertise development

### Cultural Responsiveness

Cooperative learning can honour diverse cultural orientations toward individual versus collective achievement, though this requires cultural sensitivity rather than stereotypical assumptions.

## Professional Development and Teacher Learning

Implementing cooperative learning effectively requires substantial professional expertise. Initial teacher education programmes increasingly incorporate cooperative approaches, but research by Susan Capel and others indicates significant challenges:

### Knowledge Requirements

Teachers need understanding of:
- Cooperative learning theory and evidence base
- Specific cooperative structures and their appropriate application
- Group dynamics and management strategies
- Assessment approaches for cooperative processes and outcomes

### Skill Development

Practical capabilities include:
- Designing tasks that genuinely require cooperation
- Forming productive groups and managing dynamics
- Facilitating reflection and processing
- Providing feedback on both physical and social learning
- Managing complex, active learning environments

### Continuing Professional Development

School-based professional learning might include:
- Collaborative planning of cooperative units
- Peer observation focusing on cooperative learning implementation
- Action research investigating cooperative approaches' impact in specific contexts
- Engagement with academic literature and evidence

## Future Directions and Contemporary Challenges

Cooperative learning in UK PE faces several contemporary considerations:

### Accountability Pressures

School accountability measures emphasising attainment and progress in academic subjects can marginalise PE, creating pressure for measurable outcomes potentially misaligned with cooperative learning's broader goals. David Kirk's work highlights tensions between educational purposes and performance metrics.

### Curriculum Time Constraints

Reduced PE curriculum time in many schools limits opportunities for approaches requiring sustained engagement. Cooperative learning's time requirements for establishing norms, teaching social skills, and allowing processing may seem incompatible with pressures to "cover content."
