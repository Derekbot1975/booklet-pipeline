# Teaching for Physical Literacy

## Introduction and Conceptual Framework

Physical literacy represents a fundamental shift in how we conceptualise and deliver Physical Education in the UK. Margaret Whitehead, the leading theorist in this field, defines physical literacy as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life" (Whitehead, 2010). This holistic philosophy moves beyond traditional skill-focused PE towards developing the whole person, incorporating physical, cognitive, affective and behavioural domains.

The concept emerged from phenomenological and monist philosophy, particularly the work of Merleau-Ponty, emphasising the embodied nature of human existence. Whitehead's International Physical Literacy Association (IPLA), established in 2014, has positioned physical literacy as a universal human entitlement, fundamentally reshaping PE curriculum design across the UK.

## Alignment with UK National Curriculum

The 2014 National Curriculum for Physical Education in England explicitly reflects physical literacy principles, though the term itself appears sparingly. The curriculum's aim that all pupils should "lead healthy, active lives" and develop "competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities" (DfE, 2013) directly aligns with physical literacy's holistic vision.

Key Stage 1 expectations emphasise fundamental movement skills (FMS) – the building blocks of physical literacy. Pupils should "develop balance, agility and co-ordination, and begin to apply these in a range of activities" (DfE, 2013). This foundation phase is critical; research by Stodden et al. (2008) demonstrates that motor competence in early years predicts lifelong physical activity participation.

At Key Stage 2, the curriculum requires pupils to "develop competence to excel" and "communicate, collaborate and compete with each other", reflecting the social and cognitive dimensions of physical literacy. The breadth requirement – swimming, gymnastics, dance, games, athletics and outdoor adventure – ensures varied movement experiences essential for comprehensive physical literacy development.

Key Stages 3 and 4 emphasise "analysing performance" and selecting "appropriate strategies, tactics or compositional ideas", developing the metacognitive aspects of physical literacy that enable autonomous, lifelong participation.

## Key Theoretical Perspectives

### Margaret Whitehead's Conceptualisation

Whitehead's (2010, 2013) monist-existential-phenomenological foundations position physical literacy as inseparable from human flourishing. Her definition emphasises four core elements:

1. **Motivation and confidence**: The affective disposition to engage in movement
2. **Physical competence**: The motor skills and fitness to participate effectively
3. **Knowledge and understanding**: Cognitive awareness of movement principles and health benefits
4. **Engagement for life**: The behavioural commitment to lifelong physical activity

Whitehead argues that physical literacy should be "nurtured through a positive physical education experience" (2013, p.29), placing immense responsibility on PE pedagogy. Unlike literacy and numeracy, physical literacy cannot be fully "achieved" but exists on a continuum throughout the lifespan.

### David Kirk's Pedagogical Critique

Kirk (2010, 2013) has both championed and critiqued physical literacy discourse. Whilst acknowledging its potential to revitalise PE, Kirk cautions against "conceptual confusion" and the risk of physical literacy becoming merely "old wine in new bottles" – repackaging traditional PE without substantive pedagogical change. In 'Physical Education Futures' (2010), Kirk advocates for teaching models that genuinely prioritise learning over performance, such as Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) and Sport Education, which better align with physical literacy's holistic aims.

Kirk emphasises that physical literacy requires fundamental shifts in teacher beliefs and practices, moving from transmission models towards facilitative, student-centred pedagogies that develop autonomous learners.

### Len Almond's Advocacy and Application

Len Almond, instrumental in bringing TGfU to the UK, has been a passionate advocate for physical literacy since the late 1980s. His work emphasises the "capability" dimension – ensuring young people can adapt movement skills across contexts (Almond, 2013). Almond argues that traditional multi-activity models often fail to develop transferable competencies, advocating instead for deeper learning in fewer activities with explicit teaching of tactical understanding and decision-making.

## Pedagogical Implications for Teaching

### Creating an Optimal Learning Environment

Teaching for physical literacy requires careful attention to learning climate. Dawn Penney's research (Penney and Jess, 2004; Penney, 2013) emphasises "inclusive pedagogies" that recognise diverse starting points and learning trajectories. This contrasts with traditional PE's often narrow conception of ability, which Penney argues marginalises many learners.

Practical strategies include:

- **Differentiation by outcome**: Setting open-ended tasks that allow multiple success routes
- **Individual goal-setting**: Enabling pupils to track personal progress against their own baselines
- **Psychological safety**: Establishing class norms that celebrate effort, improvement and risk-taking rather than innate talent
- **Varied grouping strategies**: Moving beyond ability-based grouping which can entrench perceptions of competence

### Developing Physical Competence

Physical competence forms the observable foundation of physical literacy. The Association for Physical Education (afPE) advocates a developmental approach, building from fundamental movement skills (FMS) through applied skills to complex, context-specific performance.

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)**: Focus on FMS acquisition across stability (balance, twist), locomotion (run, jump, skip) and manipulation (throw, catch, strike) categories. Use of equipment, space and practice structures should prioritise maximum practice opportunities and immediate feedback. The Youth Sport Trust's TOP programmes provide exemplar activities aligned with this approach.

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)**: Consolidation and application of FMS within increasingly game-like contexts. Introduction of fundamental tactical concepts (creating space, defending space) develops the cognitive dimension alongside physical skill. Assessment should focus on movement quality and adaptability rather than outcome measures alone.

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)**: Refinement of skills within activity categories. Teaching should explicitly develop "performance intelligence" – the ability to read situations and select appropriate responses. Video analysis, peer assessment and guided discovery methods enhance metacognitive awareness essential for autonomous learning.

**Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)**: Consolidation and specialisation, with emphasis on personal choice and self-directed learning. GCSE specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) include practical performance alongside theoretical knowledge, reflecting physical literacy's integrated nature. Teachers should support students in identifying activities they can realistically continue post-16.

### Fostering Motivation and Confidence

Self-Determination Theory (Deci and Ryan, 1985), extensively applied to PE by researchers including Joan Duda and Nikos Ntoumanis, provides crucial insights. Three psychological needs – autonomy, competence and relatedness – must be satisfied for intrinsic motivation to flourish.

**Autonomy-supportive practices** include:
- Providing meaningful choices in activities, roles and challenge levels
- Explaining rationales for learning tasks
- Acknowledging students' perspectives and feelings
- Minimising controlling language ("you must") in favour of informational feedback

**Competence-building practices** include:
- Appropriate challenge levels (flow theory)
- Mastery-oriented feedback focusing on improvement and effort
- Visible progress indicators (skill charts, personal portfolios)
- Private rather than public performance of developing skills

**Relatedness-enhancing practices** include:
- Cooperative learning structures
- Peer teaching and reciprocal feedback
- Celebrating collective achievements
- Anti-bullying and inclusion-focused class cultures

Susan Capel's extensive work on learning to teach PE (Capel and Blair, 2019) emphasises that trainee teachers must be explicitly taught these pedagogical strategies, as they often contradict their own experiences as successful performers in competitive sport contexts.

### Building Knowledge and Understanding

The cognitive dimension of physical literacy is frequently neglected in practice. Whitehead (2013) argues that knowledge and understanding encompass:

- **Movement vocabulary**: Language to describe and analyse movement quality
- **Strategic/tactical awareness**: Understanding principles transferable across activities
- **Health and fitness concepts**: Knowledge of how physical activity benefits wellbeing
- **Social and ethical understanding**: Appreciating fair play, respect and responsibility

Pedagogical approaches that develop this dimension include:

**Questioning techniques**: Higher-order questions that promote analysis, evaluation and creation rather than recall. For example: "What movements helped you create space?" rather than "Did you score?"

**Explicit teaching of concepts**: Direct instruction on principles such as weight transfer, force production or defensive positioning, followed by application in varied contexts.

**Reflective practice**: Use of learning journals, video analysis and verbal reflection to develop metacognitive awareness. Almond and others advocate the "freeze replay" technique where teachers stop activity to highlight tactical or technical elements.

**Integrated assessment**: GCSE PE specifications across all exam boards (AQA 8582, Edexcel 1PE0, OCR J587, WJEC) combine practical performance with theoretical examination, potentially modelling integrated physical-cognitive development, though Kirk warns against instrumentalism that values knowledge only for examination.

## Assessment for Physical Literacy

Traditional PE assessment often focuses on performance outcomes (times, scores, success rates), which Whitehead argues can undermine physical literacy by privileging those with advantageous genetics or early specialisation. Assessment for physical literacy should:

1. **Be developmental**: Tracking progress from individual baselines
2. **Include all domains**: Assessing motivation, confidence, knowledge and behaviour alongside physical competence
3. **Support learning**: Formative assessment that guides next steps
4. **Involve the learner**: Self and peer assessment developing metacognitive skills

AfPE's Assessment for Learning in Physical Education resources (afPE, 2020) provide practical tools, including observation schedules that assess decision-making and adaptability alongside technique.

Whitehead proposes that physical literacy assessment should address:
- Commitment and enthusiasm for physical activity
- Self-perception of physical competence
- Breadth of physical competence across environments (land, water, air, ice)
- Knowledge of health benefits and activity opportunities
- Movement vocabulary and analytical capability

The Canadian Physical Literacy Assessment (PLAY) tools, whilst developed outside the UK, offer validated approaches that some UK schools have adopted, including fundamental movement skill assessments and self-report questionnaires addressing confidence and motivation.

## Challenges and Critiques

### Conceptual Clarity

Despite widespread adoption, significant confusion remains about physical literacy. Kirk (2013) and Jurbala (2015) note definitional inconsistencies, with some practitioners simply renaming traditional PE as "physical literacy" without changing practice. The term's relative newness to many UK teachers creates implementation challenges.

### Policy Translation Gaps

Whilst implicit in the National Curriculum, physical literacy's absence as an explicit term creates varied interpretations. Ofsted frameworks evaluate PE provision but without specific physical literacy criteria, potentially missing opportunities to assess holistic development beyond performance.

### Performativity Pressures

Dawn Penney (2013) highlights tensions between physical literacy's process-oriented philosophy and educational systems' outcome-focused accountability. School sport partnerships, league tables and performance indicators may inadvertently privilege elite performance over inclusive development.

### Teacher Education

Capel and colleagues (Capel and Blair, 2019) identify significant challenges in preparing teachers to deliver physical literacy-informed PE. Many Initial Teacher Education programmes remain rooted in sport performance paradigms, and practising teachers may lack the pedagogical content knowledge to implement physical literacy principles effectively.

### Equity and Inclusion

Whilst physical literacy discourse emphasises universality, Penney and others question whether current implementations adequately address structural inequalities. Gender, social class, ethnicity, disability and other factors significantly influence physical literacy development, requiring pedagogies that explicitly address these dimensions rather than assuming neutral implementation.

## Moving Forward: Recommendations for Practice

### Whole-School Approach

Physical literacy extends beyond timetabled PE. Successful implementation requires:
- Active travel initiatives
- Active breaktime provision
- Extra-curricular clubs emphasising participation over selection
- Whole-school physical activity policies
- Links with community sport and activity providers

### Teacher Professional Development

Sustained, collaborative professional learning focused on pedagogical change rather than one-off training events. Subject associations including the Association for Physical Education provide resources and networks supporting physical literacy implementation.

### Curriculum Planning

Move from multi-activity models towards deeper learning in carefully selected activities that develop transferable capabilities. Kirk's advocacy for "pedagogical models" (Sport Education, Cooperative Learning, Teaching Games for Understanding) offers alternatives to traditional block teaching.

### Partnership Working

Collaboration between schools, youth sport organisations, health services and community providers creates coherent pathways supporting lifelong physical activity. Youth Sport Trust and Sport England programmes increasingly reflect physical literacy principles.

### Research-Informed Practice

Engagement with ongoing research through academic journals (Physical Education and Sport Pedagogy, European Physical Education Review) and professional publications (afPE's Physical Education Matters) ensures practice remains evidence-informed.

## Conclusion

Physical literacy represents both a philosophy and a practical framework for reimagining Physical Education. Margaret Whitehead's vision, refined through scholarly debate involving Kirk, Penney, Almond, Capel and others, challenges teachers to move beyond sport technique transmission towards holistic development of motivated, confident, competent and knowledgeable movers for life. Successful implementation requires pedagogical transformation, sustained professional learning and systemic support, but offers the potential to fulfil PE's promise of lifelong health and wellbeing for all young people.
