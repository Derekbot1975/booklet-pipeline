# Health-Related PE

## Introduction and Definition

Health-Related Physical Education (HRPE) represents a pedagogical approach that prioritises the development of knowledge, understanding, and competence necessary for students to adopt and maintain physically active, healthy lifestyles throughout their lives. As Len Almond's seminal work in the 1980s established, HRPE moves beyond traditional fitness testing to emphasise education *about*, *through*, and *for* physical activity and health.

The UK National Curriculum for Physical Education (2014) embeds health-related learning within its aims, stating that a high-quality PE education should enable pupils to "lead healthy, active lives". This positions HRPE as a fundamental component of physical education rather than a discrete topic, though it requires explicit teaching to ensure comprehensive understanding.

## Historical Development and Key Thinkers

### Len Almond and the HRF Movement

Len Almond's work in the 1980s and 1990s fundamentally shaped health-related fitness (HRF) teaching in the UK. His criticism of traditional fitness testing approaches—which often emphasised performance over understanding—led to the development of educational models focused on empowering students with knowledge and positive attitudes towards physical activity. Almond advocated for teaching that helped students understand *why* physical activity matters, not merely *what* fitness components exist.

### David Kirk's Critical Perspective

David Kirk has provided important critical analysis of HRPE, particularly in his work on "physical education futures" and the relationship between PE and public health agendas. Kirk argues that whilst health is a legitimate concern for PE, the subject risks becoming narrowly focused on obesity prevention and disease reduction rather than broader educational goals. His research emphasises the importance of maintaining PE's educational integrity whilst addressing health concerns through pedagogically sound approaches.

### Margaret Whitehead and Physical Literacy

Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy has profoundly influenced contemporary HRPE. Physical literacy—defined as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life"—provides a holistic framework that naturally incorporates health-related learning. Whitehead emphasises the affective and cognitive domains alongside physical competence, ensuring HRPE addresses the whole person.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2

The National Curriculum states that pupils should "engage in competitive (both against self and against others) and co-operative physical activity" and "lead healthy, active lives". At primary level, HRPE typically involves:

- Understanding that physical activity makes the body feel different (heart beats faster, breathing changes)
- Recognising the importance of warm-ups and cool-downs
- Developing vocabulary to describe how the body responds to exercise
- Understanding basic hygiene practices related to physical activity
- Recognising that regular physical activity contributes to health

### Key Stage 3

At KS3, students should "understand what makes a performance effective and how to apply these principles to their own and others' work" and "develop the confidence and interest to get involved in exercise, sports and activities out of school". HRPE content typically includes:

- Understanding components of health-related fitness (cardiovascular endurance, muscular strength and endurance, flexibility, body composition)
- Planning and performing personal health-related exercise programmes
- Understanding immediate and long-term effects of exercise on body systems
- Evaluating personal fitness levels appropriately
- Understanding principles of training (FITT—Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type)
- Recognising links between physical activity, health, and wellbeing

### Key Stage 4

At KS4, HRPE becomes more sophisticated, with examination specifications providing detailed content. All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) include substantial health and fitness components in their GCSE PE specifications:

**AQA GCSE PE (8582)** includes "Physical training" covering health and fitness components, principles of training, and preventing injuries.

**Edexcel GCSE PE (1PE0)** covers "Health, fitness and wellbeing" including relationships between health, fitness, and physical activity.

**OCR GCSE PE (J587)** addresses "Health and performance" including physical, emotional, and social health benefits.

**WJEC GCSE PE** includes "Health, training and exercise" covering fitness components and training methods.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### The Five Key Messages Framework

Building on Almond's work, the Association for Physical Education (afPE) promoted five key health messages that should underpin HRPE teaching:

1. Physical activity is good for you in many ways
2. Physical activity should be enjoyable
3. Physical activity can be achieved in many different ways
4. We can all benefit from regular physical activity
5. Taking part in physical activity can help you improve at activities

These messages emphasise positive, inclusive approaches rather than prescriptive fitness regimes.

### Teaching Models for HRPE

#### Personal and Social Responsibility Model

Dawn Penney's research has highlighted how Hellison's Teaching Personal and Social Responsibility (TPSR) model effectively addresses health-related learning. This model develops students' capacity to make independent decisions about physical activity, progressing through levels from respecting others to self-direction and caring.

#### Sport Education and Health

David Kirk's advocacy for Sport Education model demonstrates how health-related learning can be embedded within authentic sporting contexts. Students develop fitness through extended seasons of participation whilst learning about training, roles, and team responsibilities—making health-related fitness purposeful rather than abstract.

#### Health-Based Physical Education (HBPE)

Some scholars distinguish HBPE from traditional PE by positioning health outcomes as the primary goal. However, this approach remains controversial. Susan Capel and colleagues argue that PE should maintain its educational breadth whilst incorporating health-related learning, rather than becoming narrowly focused on health outcomes.

## Key Content Areas

### Components of Health-Related Fitness

Effective HRPE teaching addresses five health-related fitness components:

**Cardiovascular endurance**: The ability of the heart, lungs, and blood vessels to deliver oxygen to working muscles during sustained physical activity. Teaching should connect this to everyday activities and sport performance.

**Muscular strength**: The maximum force a muscle can exert in a single contraction. Students should understand its relevance to posture, injury prevention, and functional movement.

**Muscular endurance**: The ability of muscles to perform repeated contractions over time. This connects to sustained sports performance and daily activities.

**Flexibility**: Range of movement around joints. Teaching should address myths about stretching whilst emphasising its role in movement quality and injury prevention.

**Body composition**: The ratio of fat mass to lean tissue. This requires sensitive, educational handling that avoids stigmatisation and emphasises healthy ranges rather than ideal weights.

### Understanding Physical Activity Benefits

Research-informed HRPE teaching addresses physical, mental, and social health benefits:

**Physical benefits**: Reduced risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, certain cancers, and osteoporosis; improved immune function and weight management.

**Mental health benefits**: Reduced anxiety and depression, improved self-esteem, better sleep quality, and enhanced cognitive function. This evidence base has strengthened significantly since the CMO's 2019 physical activity guidelines emphasised mental health.

**Social benefits**: Opportunities for social interaction, teamwork, and community belonging.

### Principles of Training

HRPE curricula typically cover training principles including:

**Specificity**: Training must be relevant to the activity or fitness component being developed.

**Progressive overload**: Gradually increasing training demands to stimulate adaptation.

**Reversibility**: Fitness gains are lost when training stops (use it or lose it).

**Tedium**: Varying training prevents boredom and maintains motivation.

**FITT principles**: Manipulating Frequency, Intensity, Time, and Type of exercise.

### Safe Practice

HRPE includes understanding risk management and injury prevention. AfPE's *Safe Practice in Physical Education, School Sport and Physical Activity* (2020 edition) provides authoritative guidance. Teaching should address:

- Appropriate warm-up and cool-down procedures
- Understanding personal limits
- Correct technique to prevent injury
- Environmental considerations (hydration, weather, appropriate clothing)
- Recognition of contraindications to exercise

## Assessment in HRPE

### Formative Assessment Approaches

Effective HRPE assessment goes beyond fitness testing. Susan Capel and colleagues emphasise that assessment should evaluate:

- **Knowledge and understanding**: Can students explain why physical activity benefits health?
- **Application**: Can students plan appropriate activities for health-related goals?
- **Evaluation**: Can students assess their own and others' fitness levels appropriately?
- **Attitude and motivation**: Are students developing positive dispositions towards active lifestyles?

### Challenges with Fitness Testing

Research by David Kirk and others has highlighted problems with traditional fitness testing approaches:

- Public performance of tests can humiliate less-fit students
- Testing often lacks educational context—students complete tests without understanding their purpose
- Norm-referenced standards can demotivate students who don't meet arbitrary thresholds
- Over-emphasis on testing can create negative associations with physical activity

**Alternative approaches** include:
- Self-referenced improvement rather than peer comparison
- Educational context explaining test purposes and limitations
- Private, supportive testing environments
- Focus on personal goal-setting rather than classification

### GCSE Assessment

At GCSE level, HRPE knowledge is assessed through written examinations. All major boards require students to:

- Analyse relationships between health, fitness, and physical activity
- Apply training principles to scenario-based questions
- Evaluate the effectiveness of training programmes
- Justify activity choices for different health-related goals

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### The Obesity Discourse

David Kirk and colleagues have critiqued the "obesity epidemic" rhetoric's impact on PE. Whilst childhood obesity is a public health concern, making obesity prevention PE's primary purpose risks:

- Stigmatising overweight students
- Reducing PE's educational breadth
- Creating negative associations with physical activity
- Focusing narrowly on energy expenditure rather than movement competence and enjoyment

Effective HRPE acknowledges health concerns whilst maintaining positive, educational approaches that benefit all students regardless of body size.

### Mental Health Integration

The Chief Medical Officer's 2019 physical activity guidelines strengthened the evidence base for mental health benefits. Contemporary HRPE increasingly addresses:

- Understanding how physical activity affects mood and anxiety
- Using physical activity as a coping strategy
- Recognising the relationship between physical activity and sleep
- Understanding social connections through sport and physical activity

### Physical Literacy and Lifelong Engagement

Margaret Whitehead's physical literacy framework emphasises that HRPE should develop the motivation, confidence, and competence for lifelong engagement. This challenges transmission models where teachers simply deliver fitness knowledge, instead requiring teaching that:

- Develops intrinsic motivation through autonomy, competence, and relatedness (Self-Determination Theory)
- Provides successful experiences across diverse activities
- Helps students find personally meaningful physical activities
- Addresses barriers to participation (time, access, confidence, cultural factors)

### Technology and HRPE

Contemporary practice increasingly incorporates technology:

- Heart rate monitors providing immediate feedback about exercise intensity
- Fitness tracking apps enabling self-monitoring
- Video analysis supporting technique improvement
- Online resources extending learning beyond lessons

However, Dawn Penney and colleagues caution that technology should enhance rather than replace quality teaching, and access inequalities must be considered.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Creating Positive Learning Environments

Research consistently shows that HRPE effectiveness depends on classroom climate. Teachers should:

- Avoid public comparison or humiliation related to fitness levels
- Use inclusive language that recognises diverse starting points
- Emphasise personal improvement rather than peer comparison
- Celebrate effort and progress, not just achievement
- Address body image sensitively, avoiding weight-focused language

### Embedding HRPE Across the Curriculum

Rather than isolating HRPE into discrete "fitness lessons", effective practice embeds health-related learning throughout PE:

- Explain fitness demands of different sports and activities
- Connect warm-ups to understanding body preparation
- Discuss training principles when preparing for competitions
- Help students understand fatigue and recovery
- Link skill development to physical preparation

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Len Almond emphasised that HRPE must be accessible to all students. Practical strategies include:

- Offering activity choices matching different interests and abilities
- Providing individualised goals based on personal starting points
- Using self-paced activities rather than group-paced tests
- Recognising that health benefits accrue from moderate activity, not just vigorous exercise
- Addressing specific needs of students with SEND, long-term conditions, or disabilities

### Connecting to Real-World Contexts

HRPE becomes meaningful when connected to students' lives:

- Explore local physical activity opportunities
- Invite community sports providers into lessons
- Help students overcome personal barriers to activity
- Address social and cultural factors affecting participation
- Discuss critical evaluation of health messages in media and advertising

### Cross-Curricular Links

HRPE naturally connects with other subjects:

- **Science**: Body systems, effects of exercise, nutrition
- **PSHE**: Mental health, making healthy choices, managing risk
- **Mathematics**: Data collection and analysis from fitness testing
- **Food Technology**: Nutrition and hydration for physical activity

## Conclusion

Health-Related PE represents a fundamental dimension of quality physical education. When implemented effectively—through pedagogically sound approaches informed by thinkers such as Almond, Kirk, Whitehead, Penney, and Capel—HRPE develops students' capacity and motivation to lead active, healthy lives. The approach must balance legitimate health concerns with PE's broader educational purposes, ensuring that all students, regardless of current fitness or ability, develop positive relationships with physical activity. As contemporary challenges including mental health, sedentary lifestyles, and health inequalities intensify, thoughtful, evidence-informed HRPE becomes increasingly vital for supporting young people's wellbeing and life chances.
