# A Level PE Specifications

## Overview of A Level Physical Education

A Level Physical Education in England and Wales represents the culmination of school-based physical education study, typically undertaken by students aged 16-18 in Years 12 and 13. The specifications combine theoretical knowledge with practical performance, reflecting the holistic nature of physical education advocated by Margaret Whitehead's concept of physical literacy, which emphasises the development of physical competence alongside cognitive understanding and affective engagement.

The current A Level PE specifications were reformed in 2016 as part of wider curriculum changes, with first teaching from September 2016 and first examinations in summer 2018. These specifications reflect contemporary thinking in physical education, drawing on the work of key theorists including David Kirk's advocacy for curriculum models that connect physical activity to broader educational outcomes, and Dawn Penney's emphasis on curriculum as a contested space requiring critical examination.

## Exam Board Specifications

### AQA A Level Physical Education (7582)

AQA's specification is the most popular in England, structured around three principal components:

**Component 1: Factors Affecting Participation in Physical Activity and Sport (35% of A Level)**
- Applied anatomy and physiology
- Skill acquisition
- Sport and society

This 105-mark written examination (2 hours) includes multiple-choice, short-answer and extended-writing questions. The applied anatomy and physiology content includes biomechanical principles, exercise physiology, and the cardio-respiratory and neuromuscular systems. The skill acquisition section draws heavily on information processing theory and schema theory, whilst sport and society examines socio-cultural factors affecting participation, reflecting Kirk's (2010) emphasis on understanding physical education within broader social contexts.

**Component 2: Factors Affecting Optimal Performance in Physical Activity and Sport (35% of A Level)**
- Exercise physiology and biomechanics
- Sport psychology
- Sport and society and technology in sport

This 105-mark written examination (2 hours) extends Component 1 content, incorporating energy systems, linear motion, and psychological theories including social learning theory and achievement motivation. The technology section addresses ethical debates around performance enhancement, connecting with Len Almond's work on promoting positive values in physical education.

**Component 3: Practical Performance in Physical Activity and Sport (30% of A Level)**

Students perform in one practical activity from a list of approved options (45 marks) and complete a written/verbal analysis of performance (15 marks). The analysis requires students to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses, demonstrating the reflective practice Susan Capel (2007) identifies as essential for developing pedagogical content knowledge in PE teachers.

### Pearson Edexcel A Level Physical Education (9PE0)

Edexcel's specification adopts a slightly different structure:

**Component 1: Factors Affecting Participation in Physical Activity and Sport (35%)**

This 90-mark, 2-hour examination covers:
- Applied anatomy and physiology
- Exercise physiology
- Biomechanics
- Skill acquisition
- Sport psychology

**Component 2: Factors Affecting Optimal Performance in Physical Activity and Sport (35%)**

A 90-mark, 2-hour examination addressing:
- Health, fitness and well-being
- Sport and society
- Sport and politics
- Commercialisation of physical activity and sport

Edexcel's second component distinctively emphasises contemporary issues in sport, including globalisation and commodification, reflecting Dawn Penney's (2008) argument for curriculum content that engages critically with sport as a cultural practice.

**Component 3: Practical Performance in Physical Activity and Sport (30%)**

Similar to AQA, comprising practical performance (36 marks) and a Performance Analysis Task (24 marks). Students analyse their own performance using appropriate analytical methods, demonstrating understanding of performance improvement strategies.

### OCR A Level Physical Education (H555)

OCR's specification emphasises integration between theoretical and practical components:

**Component 1: Physiological Factors Affecting Performance (30%)**

A 70-mark, 2-hour examination covering:
- Applied anatomy and physiology
- Exercise physiology

**Component 2: Psychological Factors Affecting Performance (20%)**

A 60-mark, 1-hour examination addressing:
- Skill acquisition
- Sports psychology

**Component 3: Socio-Cultural Issues in Physical Activity and Sport (20%)**

A 60-mark, 1-hour examination examining:
- Sport and society
- Contemporary issues in physical activity and sport

**Component 4: Performance in Physical Education (30%)**

This non-exam assessment comprises practical performance in one activity (45 marks) and the Evaluation and Analysis of Performance for Improvement (EAPI) (15 marks). The EAPI requires students to identify weaknesses and design improvement programmes, embodying the problem-solving approach Whitehead (2010) argues develops physical literacy.

### WJEC Eduqas A Level Physical Education

Available in Wales and some English centres, the WJEC specification includes:

**Component 1: Scientific Principles and the Application in Improving Performance (30%)**

Topics include anatomy, physiology, biomechanics, skill acquisition and psychology, examined through a 2-hour, 90-mark paper.

**Component 2: Issues in Sport and Contemporary Issues in Sport (30%)**

A 2-hour, 90-mark examination covering historical, social and contemporary sporting issues, including commercialisation, technology and ethics.

**Component 3: Performance (40%)**

This substantial practical component requires performance in two activities (60 marks total) plus a Theory into Practice oral examination (20 marks), where students discuss application of theoretical principles to their practical performance.

## Common Core Content Across Specifications

Despite structural variations, all specifications address core areas:

### Anatomical and Physiological Knowledge

All boards require detailed understanding of skeletal, muscular, cardiovascular and respiratory systems. Students must apply this knowledge to performance analysis, recognising how physiological adaptations enhance sporting capability. This reflects the scientific rigour Kirk and Macdonald (1998) argue should underpin quality physical education programmes.

### Biomechanical Principles

Linear motion, force, vectors, angular motion, and fluid mechanics feature across specifications. Students analyse movement efficiency and technique optimisation, developing analytical skills applicable beyond sport contexts.

### Skill Acquisition Theory

Information processing models, schema theory, observational learning, and feedback mechanisms provide frameworks for understanding motor learning. This content draws on cognitive psychology research whilst maintaining practical relevance to coaching and performance improvement.

### Sport Psychology

Motivation theories, arousal and anxiety management, leadership, and group dynamics enable students to understand psychological factors affecting performance. The specifications emphasise application to real sporting scenarios, consistent with Almond's (1997) advocacy for teaching that connects theory with practice.

### Socio-Cultural Analysis

Historical development of sport, social determinants of participation, commercialisation, and ethical issues engage students critically with contemporary sporting culture. This reflects Penney and Evans's (1999) argument that physical education should develop students' capacity to understand and critique sport's role in society.

## Assessment Objectives

All specifications assess three principal objectives, though weightings vary slightly:

**AO1: Demonstrate knowledge and understanding** (typically 30-35%)
Students recall, select and communicate knowledge of physical education concepts, using appropriate terminology.

**AO2: Apply knowledge and understanding** (typically 30-35%)
Students analyse and evaluate, making connections between theoretical concepts and practical contexts.

**AO3: Analyse and evaluate** (typically 30-40%)
Students interpret information, construct arguments, and make judgements about physical activity and sport.

The practical component assesses performance quality and the ability to analyse and evaluate performance critically, embodying the integration of physical, cognitive and affective domains Whitehead (2013) identifies as central to physical literacy.

## Practical Performance Assessment

### Activity Lists

Specifications include extensive approved activity lists, typically categorised as:
- Team sports (e.g., football, hockey, rugby)
- Individual sports (e.g., athletics, swimming, gymnastics)
- Combat sports (e.g., boxing, judo, taekwondo)
- Performance activities (e.g., dance, outdoor activities)

Students typically perform in one activity (AQA, Edexcel, OCR) or two activities (WJEC), assessed against published criteria describing performance standards from basic competence to high-level execution.

### Assessment Criteria

Performance is assessed using level descriptors addressing:
- Technical proficiency
- Tactical awareness
- Physical fitness appropriate to activity demands
- Performance consistency under pressure

Assessments occur in "full-sided" competitive contexts where possible, ensuring authenticity. This approach reflects Kirk's (2010) critique of decontextualised skill practices, emphasising instead situated learning within authentic sporting environments.

### Coursework Components

The analytical components require students to:
1. Identify strengths and weaknesses in performance
2. Apply theoretical knowledge to explain performance characteristics
3. Design and justify improvement strategies
4. Consider psychological, physiological and biomechanical factors

This integration develops the reflective practice Capel and Whitehead (2015) identify as distinguishing mature performers from novices.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Programme Planning

Delivering A Level PE requires careful curriculum planning to integrate theoretical and practical components. Teachers should:

**Create explicit theory-practice connections**: When teaching physiological concepts like lactate threshold, simultaneously demonstrate application through interval training sessions. This dual approach reflects Almond's (1997) advocacy for experiential learning in PE.

**Employ varied pedagogical strategies**: Combine didactic teaching for content delivery with inquiry-based approaches for deeper understanding. Kirk et al. (2006) demonstrate that students develop more sophisticated conceptual understanding when actively constructing knowledge rather than passively receiving information.

**Utilise technology appropriately**: Video analysis software, heart rate monitors, and motion analysis tools enable students to collect authentic performance data, supporting evidence-based analysis. However, technology should enhance rather than replace fundamental pedagogical relationships.

### Assessment Practices

**Formative assessment**: Regular low-stakes testing consolidates knowledge and identifies misconceptions. Research by Roediger and Karpicke (2006) demonstrates retrieval practice significantly improves long-term retention.

**Practice examination questions**: Familiarising students with command words (evaluate, analyse, discuss) and mark scheme expectations improves examination technique. Past papers and mark schemes are freely available from exam boards.

**Practical performance tracking**: Regular practical assessments throughout the course enable identification of performance improvements and areas requiring additional practice. Video recording performances facilitates retrospective analysis and student self-assessment.

### Differentiation Strategies

A Level PE attracts students with diverse backgrounds and capabilities:

**Support lower-attaining students** through scaffolded tasks, glossaries of key terminology, and structured note-taking frameworks. Breaking complex concepts into manageable components aids comprehension without reducing cognitive demand.

**Challenge higher-attaining students** through extension tasks requiring application to unfamiliar contexts, comparative analysis of competing theories, or critical evaluation of research evidence. Penney (2006) argues that challenging students to engage critically with curriculum content develops sophisticated thinking.

**Recognise diverse practical abilities**: Students may excel theoretically whilst finding practical performance challenging, or vice versa. Both domains offer opportunities for success, though integration remains the specification ideal.

## Research Evidence and Expert Opinion

### Theory-Practice Integration

Kirk (2010) argues that traditional physical education curricula often fragment theoretical and practical learning, treating them as separate domains. A Level specifications attempt integration, though the extent varies between boards. Teachers should actively construct connections, making theoretical principles explicit during practical sessions and using practical experiences to illustrate theoretical concepts.

### Physical Literacy Development

Whitehead's (2010) physical literacy concept, emphasising lifelong physical activity engagement, provides a philosophical foundation for A Level PE. The specifications develop physical literacy's cognitive dimension through theoretical study whilst maintaining practical engagement. However, critics including Lundvall (2015) suggest academic PE can inadvertently alienate students less comfortable with theoretical study, potentially undermining broader participation goals.

### Critical Pedagogy in PE

Penney (2008) advocates for physical education that develops critical thinking about sport's social role. The socio-cultural components across specifications partially achieve this, examining power relations, inequality and commercialisation. Teachers should encourage students to question taken-for-granted assumptions about sport, developing what Kirk calls "educated consumers" of sporting culture.

### Assessment Validity and Reliability

Practical performance assessment presents challenges regarding standardisation. Carroll (1994) identifies subjectivity in judging performance quality as problematic, though detailed criteria and moderation processes mitigate this. The shift toward assessment in authentic competitive contexts improves ecological validity but may disadvantage students with limited access to high-quality opposition.

## Progression and University Pathways

A Level PE provides foundation knowledge for undergraduate study in:
- Sport and exercise science
- Sports coaching
- Sports therapy and rehabilitation
- Sports management
- Physical education teacher training

Universities typically recognise A Level PE as demonstrating scientific knowledge and analytical capability. The Russell Group (2024) includes PE amongst facilitating subjects for sport-related degrees, though not for broader university access.

Research by Keech (2013) indicates A Level PE students pursuing sport-related degrees often demonstrate stronger initial understanding of physiological principles compared to students entering from pure science backgrounds, though may require additional support with research methodology.

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### Content Breadth vs Depth

Specifications attempt comprehensive coverage of sub-disciplines (physiology, psychology, sociology, biomechanics), but necessarily sacrifice depth. Some academics argue for greater specialisation, allowing deeper exploration of particular areas. However, Kirk (2014) suggests breadth provides essential foundation knowledge whilst maintaining subject coherence.

### Practical Component Weightings

At 30-40%, practical performance significantly influences final grades. This reflects physical education's distinctive practical character but raises questions about students who excel theoretically but struggle practically. Some stakeholders advocate increased theoretical weighting, whilst others defend practical performance as fundamental to subject identity.

### Technology and Innovation

Emerging technologies including wearable sensors, virtual reality training systems, and biomechanical analysis software offer new pedagogical possibilities. Specifications acknowledge technology's role, but rapid development challenges curriculum currency. Teachers must balance teaching enduring principles with contemporary applications.

### Equity and Access

Access to high-quality coaching, facilities and competition opportunities varies significantly between schools and socio-economic contexts. This potentially disadvantages students from less-resourced backgrounds in practical assessments. Examination boards acknowledge these equity concerns but solutions remain contested.

## Conclusion

A Level PE specifications represent sophisticated curricula integrating theoretical knowledge with practical performance. Drawing on established physical education scholarship, they develop students' capacities to understand, analyse and evaluate physical activity and sport across physiological, psychological and socio-cultural dimensions. Effective delivery requires teachers to construct explicit connections between theory and practice, employ varied pedagogical strategies, and develop students' critical thinking alongside physical competence. The specifications embody ongoing debates about physical education's purposes, balancing scientific knowledge, practical performance, and critical understanding of sport's social dimensions.
