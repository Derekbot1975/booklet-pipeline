# Formative Assessment in PE

## Introduction and Definition

Formative assessment in Physical Education represents the ongoing process of gathering, interpreting and using evidence to improve learning during the teaching and learning process, rather than merely at its conclusion. Often described as "assessment for learning" (AfL), formative assessment in PE enables teachers to make informed decisions about next steps in teaching and allows pupils to understand their current performance level and how to improve.

The Black and Wiliam (1998) seminal work "Inside the Black Box" fundamentally shaped formative assessment practice across all subjects, including PE, demonstrating that effective formative assessment could raise standards significantly. In PE specifically, researchers including **Margaret Whitehead**, **David Kirk**, and **Dawn Penney** have emphasised how formative assessment aligns with broader pedagogical aims, particularly physical literacy development and inclusive practice.

## Theoretical Framework

### Assessment for Learning in PE Context

**Dawn Penney** and colleagues have argued that assessment in PE must move beyond simple skill acquisition measurement towards holistic evaluation of physical literacy, encompassing physical competence, confidence, motivation, knowledge and understanding. This broader conception requires sophisticated formative assessment approaches that capture the multidimensional nature of learning in PE.

**Margaret Whitehead's** physical literacy framework (2010, 2013) emphasises that assessment should recognise individual journeys towards embodied capability, with formative assessment serving as the primary vehicle for understanding each pupil's developmental trajectory. Her work highlights that physical literacy is a lifelong journey, making formative feedback particularly crucial in establishing positive dispositions towards physical activity.

**David Kirk** (2005, 2010) has critiqued traditional approaches to PE assessment that focus narrowly on performance outcomes, advocating instead for assessment practices that recognise the situated, social and cultural dimensions of learning in PE. His models-based practice approach requires continuous formative assessment to ensure appropriate task modification and differentiation.

## UK National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage Requirements

The **National Curriculum for Physical Education in England** (DfE, 2013) does not prescribe specific assessment methods but establishes expectations that pupils should be able to evaluate and improve performance. This implicitly requires ongoing formative assessment practices.

**Key Stage 1 and 2** expectations include that pupils should "describe how their bodies feel during different activities" and "recognise their own success," both requiring formative assessment conversations and self-evaluation opportunities.

**Key Stage 3** requires pupils to "analyse their performances compared to previous ones and demonstrate improvement" – explicitly necessitating formative tracking and feedback mechanisms.

**Key Stage 4** expectations that pupils "evaluate their strengths and areas for development and work towards improving them" demand sophisticated formative assessment literacy from both teachers and students.

## Core Principles of Formative Assessment in PE

### Sharing Learning Intentions and Success Criteria

**Len Almond** and **Sue Capel** have emphasised the importance of transparent learning objectives in PE. Pupils must understand not only what they are learning (e.g., "developing defensive positioning in basketball") but also what successful performance looks like.

**Practical application:**
- Display learning intentions using accessible language and demonstrations
- Co-construct success criteria with pupils where appropriate
- Use video exemplars to illustrate quality performance
- Ensure success criteria address both observable performance and underlying understanding

### Effective Questioning

Quality questioning transforms passive activity completion into active learning. Effective PE questioning moves beyond simple recall ("What did we do last lesson?") towards higher-order thinking ("Why might you choose this tactic in this situation?").

**Susan Capel** and colleagues (in "Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School," 2016) identify questioning strategies specific to PE:
- Open questions that encourage tactical thinking
- Targeted questions to individuals during activity
- Whole-class questioning during demonstration phases
- Questions that prompt metacognition: "How did you know that wasn't working?"

### Quality Feedback

Research consistently demonstrates that feedback quality matters more than frequency. **Dylan Wiliam's** work indicates effective feedback should:
- Focus on the task, process and self-regulation rather than the person
- Provide specific guidance on improvement rather than merely praise
- Be timely enough to inform the next attempt
- Encourage dialogue rather than one-way communication

**In PE contexts, this translates to:**
- Immediate verbal feedback during performance
- Video feedback for complex skills enabling self-analysis
- Peer feedback structured through clear protocols
- Written feedback in assessed work (GCSE/A-level) that identifies specific improvement strategies

### Peer and Self-Assessment

**Margaret Whitehead** argues that developing metacognitive awareness is central to physical literacy. Peer and self-assessment develop this awareness whilst reducing teacher workload and increasing assessment frequency.

**Practical strategies include:**
- Structured peer observation sheets with specific focus points
- Traffic light self-assessment of confidence levels
- Video analysis apps enabling frame-by-frame self-review
- Student-led coaching relationships with clear assessment criteria
- Reflective journals or learning logs documenting progress

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Using Technology for Formative Assessment

Contemporary PE practice increasingly utilises technology to enhance formative assessment:

**Video analysis applications** (e.g., Coach's Eye, Hudl Technique) enable immediate replay and comparison with model performances, facilitating self-correction and peer coaching.

**Fitness tracking devices and apps** provide objective data that pupils can interpret formatively, understanding relationships between effort, adaptation and outcomes.

**Digital portfolios** enable longitudinal tracking of progress across multiple activities, supporting reflection on development over time.

### Differentiated Assessment Approaches

**Dawn Penney's** work on inclusive curriculum highlights that formative assessment must be accessible to all learners regardless of ability, disability, or prior experience.

**Strategies include:**
- Multiple modes of demonstration (verbal explanation, physical demonstration, diagram)
- Varied response options (verbal, written, physical demonstration)
- Differentiated success criteria with minimum, expected and extension levels
- Personalised targets based on individual starting points
- Assessment that values different dimensions of performance (tactical understanding, leadership, creativity, consistency)

### Assessment Within Different Activity Areas

Formative assessment approaches must be tailored to specific activity contexts:

**Games activities:** Tactical understanding can be assessed through questioning during play pauses, positioning analysis, and decision-making evaluation alongside technical skill execution.

**Gymnastics/Dance:** Performance quality assessed through peer observation against specific criteria (extension, control, synchronisation), with video replay enabling self-evaluation.

**Athletics:** Personal best tracking provides individualised formative data; technique analysis against key points; understanding of principles (e.g., force application, pacing strategies).

**Outdoor and Adventurous Activities:** Problem-solving processes, leadership qualities, and communication effectiveness assessed through structured observation and reflective discussion.

**Swimming:** Stroke technique assessed against specific teaching points; endurance improvement tracked personally; water confidence self-evaluated.

**Health and Fitness:** Understanding of principles assessed through explanation and application; personal fitness improvement tracked against baseline; knowledge of training methods evaluated through planning tasks.

## Formative Assessment at GCSE and A-Level

### Exam Board Approaches

**AQA GCSE PE (8582)** and **A-level PE (7582)** include both practical and theoretical components. Formative assessment in practical activities uses standardised assessment criteria across different activity areas, with detailed descriptors at each level enabling specific feedback on performance quality.

**Edexcel/Pearson GCSE PE (1PE0)** and **A-level PE (9PE0)** similarly require ongoing formative assessment against published criteria. Teachers must provide formative feedback on both isolated skill performance and full-context competitive performance.

**OCR GCSE PE (J587)** and **A-level PE (H555)** assessment requires documented evidence of performance quality. Formative assessment throughout the course enables pupils to select appropriate activities for final assessment and identify areas requiring additional development time.

**WJEC/Eduqas GCSE PE** and **A-level PE** specifications include analysis and evaluation of performance (AEP) components requiring pupils to demonstrate formative assessment skills in analysing their own or others' performances and planning improvement programmes.

### Integrating Theory and Practice

Effective formative assessment in examination PE connects theoretical knowledge with practical application. For example:
- Apply biomechanical principles (lever systems, force application) through practical skill analysis
- Use fitness testing data formatively to understand training principles
- Connect skill acquisition theory to personal performance development
- Apply tactical concepts from theory lessons in competitive contexts

## Evidence-Informed Practice

### Research Evidence on Effectiveness

**Hay and Penney's** research (2013) demonstrated that formative assessment in PE develops not only physical competence but also assessment capability that transfers across learning domains. Their work in Australian and UK contexts showed pupils who engaged regularly with self and peer assessment demonstrated greater metacognitive awareness and autonomous learning behaviours.

**Lorente-Catalán and Kirk** (2014) found that democratic assessment practices, including student voice in assessment criteria development, increased engagement and ownership of learning, particularly for traditionally marginalised groups in PE.

**López-Pastor et al.** (2013) conducted extensive research showing formative assessment significantly improved learning outcomes compared to traditional summative-only approaches, whilst simultaneously reducing teacher workload when peer and self-assessment were effectively implemented.

### Implementation Challenges

Research by **Hay and Macdonald** (2008) identified common barriers to effective formative assessment in PE:
- Time constraints within activity-based lessons
- Teacher confidence in facilitating assessment conversations during activity
- Lack of pupil assessment literacy
- Overemphasis on participation and effort at the expense of learning
- Difficulty capturing evidence in dynamic practical contexts

**David Kirk** has argued that many PE teachers conflate assessment with measurement, leading to over-reliance on quantifiable performance data (times, distances, scores) at the expense of qualitative formative assessment of understanding, tactics, and decision-making.

## Developing Assessment-Capable Pupils

### Building Assessment Literacy

**Len Almond's** work on Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU) emphasises that pupils must develop sophisticated understanding of performance quality. This requires explicit teaching of assessment processes:

**Strategies include:**
- Modelling self-assessment thinking aloud
- Providing assessment frameworks with clear language
- Gradually releasing responsibility from teacher to peer to self-assessment
- Teaching observation skills systematically
- Discussing what quality looks like in different contexts
- Connecting assessment language across different activities

### Metacognition and Self-Regulation

**Margaret Whitehead's** physical literacy concept positions self-awareness as fundamental. Pupils should understand:
- Their current capabilities across physical literacy domains
- Specific areas for development
- Strategies that work for their learning
- How to set realistic yet challenging goals
- How to monitor their own progress
- When to seek help or adjust approaches

## Integration with Summative Assessment

Whilst distinct in purpose, formative and summative assessment should be complementary rather than separate. **Dawn Penney** argues that summative judgements should emerge naturally from accumulated formative evidence rather than constituting separate assessment episodes.

**Practical approaches:**
- Use formative assessment data to inform interim reporting
- Enable pupils to track progress towards end-of-unit standards
- Provide formative feedback even on summative tasks
- Use summative results formatively to inform future planning
- Maintain ongoing records of formative observations that contribute to summative judgements

## Recommendations for Effective Practice

### For Classroom Teachers

1. **Plan assessment opportunities explicitly** within lesson plans, not as afterthoughts
2. **Limit feedback focus** to 2-3 key points per lesson to ensure manageability and clarity
3. **Develop observation routines** that enable systematic data gathering during activity
4. **Use structured formats** for peer assessment to ensure quality and focus
5. **Create assessment-rich environments** through visible success criteria, exemplars, and learning walls
6. **Record formative evidence systematically** using appropriate technology or paper-based systems
7. **Involve pupils in target-setting** based on formative assessment data
8. **Connect assessment across lessons** by referring back to previous feedback and tracking progress

### For Curriculum Leaders

1. **Develop whole-department assessment literacy** through collaborative professional development
2. **Create shared resources** including success criteria, observation sheets, and exemplar materials
3. **Establish assessment moderation practices** ensuring consistency across teachers
4. **Review curriculum design** to ensure sufficient time for formative assessment processes
5. **Monitor assessment workload** ensuring approaches are sustainable
6. **Evaluate impact** of formative assessment on pupil outcomes and engagement
7. **Ensure assessment policy reflects contemporary understanding** informed by researchers like Whitehead, Kirk, and Penney
8. **Develop coherent assessment frameworks** linking primary-secondary transition and progression across key stages

## Conclusion

Formative assessment represents the heart of effective PE pedagogy, transforming teaching from activity delivery to responsive, personalised learning facilitation. The work of **Margaret Whitehead**, **David Kirk**, **Dawn Penney**, **Len Almond**, and **Susan Capel** collectively demonstrates that quality formative assessment develops not only physical competence but also metacognitive awareness, autonomous learning capabilities, and lifelong physical literacy.

Effective implementation requires systematic planning, appropriate use of technology, development of pupil assessment literacy, and integration with broader pedagogical approaches including models-based practice and physical literacy development. When implemented thoughtfully, formative assessment enhances learning outcomes, increases engagement, and develops assessment-capable pupils who can self-regulate their learning journey in PE and beyond.
