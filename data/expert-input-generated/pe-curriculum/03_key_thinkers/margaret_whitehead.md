# Margaret Whitehead

**Full Name:** Professor Margaret Whitehead  
**Current/Recent Roles:** Emeritus Professor of Physical Education, University of Bedfordshire; Honorary Research Fellow, University of Roehampton; Founder and Past President, International Physical Literacy Association (IPLA)  
**Known For:** Developing and advancing the concept of physical literacy; pioneering holistic approaches to Physical Education; embedding existential phenomenology and monism in PE philosophy; transforming PE pedagogy through person-centred, lifelong participation frameworks

---

## Biography

### Academic and Professional Career

Professor Margaret Whitehead is internationally recognised as the founding architect of the contemporary physical literacy movement, which has fundamentally reshaped Physical Education discourse in the UK and globally since the early 2000s. Her academic career spans over four decades, during which she has held senior positions at several British universities including Liverpool John Moores University, the University of Bedfordshire, and the University of Roehampton.

Whitehead's initial training was in Physical Education, followed by extensive postgraduate study in philosophy, particularly existential phenomenology. This interdisciplinary background uniquely positioned her to challenge the dualistic, performance-oriented paradigms that dominated PE throughout much of the late 20th century. Her doctoral research explored the philosophical foundations of human movement, drawing heavily on continental philosophy, particularly the works of Merleau-Ponty, Sartre, and Marcel.

### Development of Physical Literacy Concept

Beginning in the 1990s, Whitehead began articulating what would become her seminal contribution: the concept of physical literacy. This work culminated in numerous publications throughout the 2000s and 2010s, with her edited collection *Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse* (2010, Routledge) serving as the foundational text for the field. She founded the International Physical Literacy Association (IPLA) in 2014 and served as its first President, establishing the organisation as the authoritative voice on physical literacy worldwide.

Her influence extended beyond academia into policy and practice. Whitehead served as an advisor to numerous national governing bodies, including Youth Sport Trust, the Association for Physical Education (afPE), and Sport England. Her conceptual framework was adopted by multiple countries, including Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, and influenced curriculum reforms across these nations.

### Recent Work and Legacy

In her role as Emeritus Professor, Whitehead continues to write, present, and advise on physical literacy implementation. She has been particularly focused on ensuring the philosophical integrity of physical literacy is maintained as the concept achieves widespread adoption, cautioning against reductionist interpretations that focus solely on physical competence whilst neglecting the affective, cognitive, and social dimensions.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Physical Literacy as Philosophical Foundation

Whitehead's central contribution is defining physical literacy as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life." This definition, established in her 2010 text and refined through IPLA, represents a paradigm shift from traditional PE objectives.

Crucially, Whitehead grounds physical literacy in **monist philosophy**, specifically existential phenomenology. This rejects Cartesian mind-body dualism, instead positioning humans as embodied beings for whom movement is fundamental to existence and meaning-making. Drawing on Merleau-Ponty's *Phenomenology of Perception*, she argues that physical capability and sense-making are inseparable—we understand our world through and as our bodies.

This philosophical foundation distinguishes physical literacy from mere 'physical activity' or 'fundamental movement skills'. It encompasses four interconnected domains: physical (competence in movement), affective (confidence and motivation), cognitive (understanding of movement and health), and behavioural (engagement in physical activity). Whitehead emphasises these domains are inseparable and mutually reinforcing.

### 2. Embodiment and Existential Phenomenology

Whitehead's application of **embodiment theory** to PE represents one of her most sophisticated contributions. She argues that humans are fundamentally 'embodied subjects'—not minds inhabiting bodies, but unified beings whose physical existence is integral to consciousness, identity, and agency.

This perspective, detailed in her chapter "The Concept of Physical Literacy" (2010) and numerous journal articles, has profound pedagogical implications. It suggests that PE should not view the body as an instrument to be trained, but as the fundamental medium through which learners experience, interpret, and act in the world. Movement becomes a form of knowledge and expression, not merely a skill set.

Whitehead draws on Gabriel Marcel's concept of the body as 'I am my body' (rather than 'I have a body'), and Sartre's notion of human freedom and responsibility. This positions learners as agents who continuously create meaning through movement, with PE serving to enhance this existential capacity throughout life.

### 3. Holistic Physical Education

Whitehead's holistic approach challenges reductionist models that dominated British PE, particularly those focused narrowly on skill acquisition, fitness testing, or competitive sport performance. Her framework, detailed in *Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse* and various policy documents for afPE and Youth Sport Trust, argues for PE that develops:

- **Physical competence:** movement skills, fitness, and control across diverse environments
- **Affective attributes:** intrinsic motivation, self-confidence, self-expression, and enjoyment
- **Cognitive capabilities:** understanding movement principles, tactical awareness, health knowledge, and critical thinking about physical culture
- **Behavioural habits:** committed, regular, lifelong engagement in physical activity

This holistic model explicitly opposes narrow interpretations that reduce physical literacy to a checklist of fundamental movement skills (FMS). Whitehead has consistently critiqued such approaches, particularly in Canadian and Australian contexts where assessment-driven FMS frameworks emerged, arguing they represent "a complete misunderstanding" of the concept's philosophical foundation.

### 4. Motivation and Intrinsic Value

Central to Whitehead's work is an emphasis on **intrinsic motivation** and the inherent value of movement. Drawing on Self-Determination Theory (Deci and Ryan), but extending beyond it, she argues physical literacy develops when individuals experience movement as inherently satisfying, meaningful, and connected to their sense of self.

This contrasts sharply with extrinsic motivation approaches (rewards, competition, health imperatives) that dominated traditional PE. Whitehead's framework, articulated in her 2013 article "What is Physical Literacy and How Does it Impact on Physical Education?" (*Advances in Physical Education*), suggests PE should cultivate:

- **Autonomy:** learners making informed choices about their physical activity engagement
- **Competence:** experiencing success and progressive mastery
- **Relatedness:** connecting with others through movement
- **Meaning:** understanding how physical activity contributes to a fulfilling life

She argues that when physical literacy is properly developed, individuals maintain physical activity participation throughout life because they value it for its own sake, not for external health or appearance benefits.

### 5. Lifelong Journey and Individual Pathways

Whitehead conceptualises physical literacy as a **lifelong journey** rather than an endpoint or achievement level. In her 2010 framework and subsequent IPLA publications, she emphasises that physical literacy develops from birth through old age, with each life stage presenting unique opportunities and challenges.

This perspective shifts PE's purpose from preparing children for adult sport participation to enabling all individuals to maintain appropriate physical activity engagement throughout their lives. Whitehead writes extensively about physical literacy in early years, adolescence, adulthood, and older age, arguing that educational provision must account for developmental, social, and environmental factors at each stage.

Crucially, she emphasises **individual pathways**—there is no single form of physical literacy. Cultural context, personal preferences, physical capabilities, and life circumstances create diverse expressions of physical literacy. This inclusive approach challenges normative standards and celebrates plurality in physical activity engagement.

### 6. Inclusive and Person-Centred Pedagogy

Whitehead's physical literacy framework is fundamentally **inclusive**, rejecting elitist models that prioritise talented performers. Her chapter "Physical Literacy and its Importance to Every Individual" (in *Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse*) argues that all humans, regardless of ability, have the capacity and entitlement to develop physical literacy appropriate to their circumstances.

This includes explicit consideration of:
- **Disability and SEND:** adapted activities that enable embodied agency
- **Gender equity:** challenging stereotypes about appropriate physical activities
- **Cultural diversity:** respecting different movement traditions and practices
- **Socioeconomic access:** addressing barriers to participation

Her person-centred approach positions the learner's subjective experience as central, requiring teachers to understand individual motivation, confidence levels, prior experiences, and aspirations. This contrasts with standardised, one-size-fits-all curriculum models.

### 7. Environmental Interaction and Adaptability

Drawing on ecological psychology and her phenomenological foundation, Whitehead emphasises physical literacy includes the capacity to **"read" and respond to diverse physical environments**. Her 2010 definition specifies that physical literacy enables individuals to demonstrate poise, confidence, and control across "a wide range of physical activities in multiple environments."

This environmental dimension, detailed in her chapter "Physical Literacy and the Young Child" (2017), includes:
- Natural environments (parks, forests, beaches)
- Built environments (playgrounds, sports facilities, urban spaces)
- Social environments (team settings, individual pursuits, informal recreation)
- Weather and terrain variations

She argues physical literacy develops through rich, varied movement experiences that challenge individuals to adapt movement patterns to changing contexts, fostering creativity, problem-solving, and kinesthetic intelligence.

### 8. Embodied Knowledge and Practical Wisdom

Whitehead articulates a sophisticated epistemology of movement, arguing that **physical capability constitutes a distinct form of knowledge**—what Aristotle termed *phronesis* (practical wisdom). In her philosophical writings, particularly "The Value of Physical Activity and Physical Literacy" (2014, *Debates in the Sociology of Sport*), she argues movement generates:

- **Tacit knowledge:** non-verbal understanding embedded in bodily experience
- **Proprioceptive awareness:** sophisticated understanding of body position and capability
- **Practical reasoning:** situated decision-making in movement contexts
- **Aesthetic appreciation:** recognition of quality, grace, and expression in movement

This positions PE as intellectual and aesthetic education, not merely physical training. She argues students should be recognised as generating genuine knowledge through movement, challenging hierarchies that privilege verbal/mathematical intelligence over embodied intelligence.

## Key Publications

### Books and Edited Collections

**Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse** (2010, Routledge)  
Whitehead's seminal edited collection establishing the theoretical and practical foundations of physical literacy. Includes her own chapters on philosophical foundations, definition, and application across age groups. This text remains the authoritative reference for physical literacy globally and includes contributions situating physical literacy within PE curriculum, health promotion, and sport development.

**Physical Literacy Across the World** (2019, Routledge, co-edited with Whitehead as lead editor)  
Comprehensive international survey of physical literacy implementation across 21 countries, demonstrating the concept's global reach and diverse interpretations. Includes critical analysis of policy adoption, curriculum integration, and assessment approaches. Whitehead's introductory and concluding chapters provide synthesis and address conceptual integrity concerns.

### Journal Articles

**"Definition of Physical Literacy and Clarification of Related Issues"** (2013, *ICSSPE Bulletin - Journal of Sport Science and Physical Education*)  
Authoritative statement refining the physical literacy definition and addressing common misconceptions. Establishes IPLA's official definition and clarifies distinctions between physical literacy and related concepts (physical competence, physical activity, sports participation).

**"Physical Literacy: Philosophical Considerations in Relation to Developing a Sense of Self, Universality and Propositional Knowledge"** (2007, *Sport, Ethics and Philosophy*, 1(3), 281-298)  
Scholarly exploration of physical literacy's philosophical foundations, examining how embodied capability contributes to identity formation and existential flourishing. Key text for understanding the phenomenological basis of Whitehead's work.

**"The Concept of Physical Literacy"** (2001, *European Journal of Physical Education*, 6(2), 127-138)  
Early articulation of physical literacy concept, predating the 2010 book. Establishes core philosophical principles and distinguishes physical literacy from fitness and skill-based models.

**"Developing Physical Literacy: A Cautionary Tale"** (2013, *Revista Observatorio del Deporte*)  
Critical commentary warning against reductionist interpretations of physical literacy, particularly assessment-driven approaches focusing narrowly on fundamental movement skills.

### Policy and Professional Documents

**"Physical Literacy: A Brief Introduction"** (afPE Health Position Paper, 2013)  
Accessible overview commissioned by the Association for Physical Education, aimed at teachers and curriculum leaders. Explains physical literacy principles and their application in British school PE.

**IPLA Definition and Position Statements** (2014-present)  
Series of official publications through the International Physical Literacy Association establishing definitions, assessment principles, and implementation guidance. Whitehead authored or co-authored most foundational statements.

### Chapters in Edited Volumes

Whitehead has contributed numerous chapters to edited collections on PE pedagogy, philosophy of sport, and health education, including works in:
- *Debates in the Sociology of Sport* (Routledge)
- *Routledge Handbook of Youth Physical Activity* (Routledge)
- *Children, Obesity and Exercise* (Routledge)

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### UK National Curriculum and Policy Documents

Whitehead's physical literacy framework has profoundly influenced UK PE policy since 2010, though not always with explicit curriculum adoption. The 2013 National Curriculum for Physical Education in England, whilst not using "physical literacy" terminology, reflects Whitehead's holistic approach through its emphasis on:
- Developing competence to excel in a broad range of physical activities
- Being physically active for sustained periods
- Leading healthy, active lives

**Youth Sport Trust**, a leading UK charity for school sport and PE, formally adopted physical literacy as a central framework from 2011 onwards. Whitehead served as advisor to Youth Sport Trust, and the organisation's publications, including *Start to Move* and *Physical Literacy Framework for Schools*, directly reference her work.

**Association for Physical Education (afPE)**, the UK professional body for PE teachers, embraced physical literacy through multiple position papers and professional development resources from 2013 onwards. Whitehead contributed to afPE publications including their Health Position Paper series, which established physical literacy as a guiding principle for health-focused PE.

**Sport England**, the government body responsible for grassroots sport, integrated physical literacy into its strategy documents from 2016, particularly in early years and primary school initiatives. The *Sporting Future* strategy (2015) and subsequent *Towards an Active Nation* documents reflect physical literacy principles, emphasising lifelong participation over competitive performance.

### Examination Specifications

Physical literacy concepts appear in GCSE and A-Level Physical Education specifications, particularly:
- **AQA GCSE PE** (2016 onwards): includes physical literacy in theoretical components examining participation, health, and wellbeing
- **OCR A-Level PE** (2016 onwards): addresses physical literacy within socio-cultural and psychological theory units

Whitehead's work is cited in examination board resources and recommended reading lists for advanced PE students.

### Initial Teacher Education

British universities offering PE teacher training widely incorporate physical literacy within ITE programmes. Whitehead's 2010 text is standard reading in most PGCE and undergraduate PE courses. Many institutions invite Whitehead to deliver guest lectures and keynotes, embedding her frameworks within professional preparation for PE teachers.

### International Influence

Beyond the UK, Whitehead's influence is substantial:
- **Canada:** Physical literacy adopted as national framework through PHE Canada (now PHE Canada - Physical and Health Education Canada), with Whitehead serving as advisor. Canadian Sport for Life's Long-Term Athlete Development model integrated physical literacy from 2010.
- **Australia:** Australian Sports Commission (now Sport Australia) adopted physical literacy framework in 2016, with implementation through schools and community sport.
- **New Zealand:** Sport New Zealand integrated physical literacy into national PE and youth sport strategy.
- **Wales:** Welsh Government's PE curriculum explicitly references physical literacy more directly than England's framework.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Teaching Principles Derived from Whitehead's Work

Whitehead's philosophical framework suggests specific pedagogical approaches, though she has been careful to note that physical literacy is a learning outcome rather than a teaching method. Nevertheless, her work implies:

#### 1. **Learner-Centred Approaches**
Teachers should prioritise individual learner experiences, motivations, and developmental needs over standardised performance expectations. This requires:
- Differentiated instruction acknowledging diverse starting points
- Student voice in activity selection and goal-setting
- Formative feedback focused on personal progress rather than comparative performance
- Recognition of subjective experience (enjoyment, confidence) as valid learning outcomes

#### 2. **Diverse Movement Experiences**
Physical literacy develops through breadth of experience, not specialisation. Whitehead advocates:
- Exposure to multiple activity categories (invasion games, aesthetic activities, adventure, athletic pursuits)
- Varied environments (indoor, outdoor, natural, built)
- Individual, partner, and group activities
- Creative movement and problem-solving tasks alongside structured skill development
