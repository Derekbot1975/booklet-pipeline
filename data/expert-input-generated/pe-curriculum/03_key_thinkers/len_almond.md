# Len Almond

**Full Name:** Leonard (Len) Almond

**Known For:** Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), Physical Literacy, Health-Related Physical Education, Sport Education

**Key Affiliations:** Loughborough University, Youth Sport Trust

**Period of Influence:** 1970s–2010s

---

## Biography and Career History

Len Almond is a pioneering figure in UK Physical Education, whose work has fundamentally shaped contemporary approaches to games teaching, health education, and physical literacy. His career spans over four decades, during which he has been instrumental in challenging traditional, technique-focused PE pedagogy and advocating for learner-centred, cognitively-engaging approaches.

Almond spent the majority of his academic career at Loughborough University, one of the UK's premier institutions for sport, exercise and health sciences. At Loughborough, he worked within the Department of Physical Education, Sport Science and Recreation Management, where he became a leading voice in PE pedagogy research and teacher education. His position at Loughborough provided him with a platform to influence generations of PE teachers and researchers, establishing the institution as a centre for innovative thinking in games teaching and health-focused PE.

During the 1980s and 1990s, Almond emerged as one of the most influential voices in British PE, particularly through his advocacy for Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), which he helped introduce to the UK from its origins in North America. He worked closely with other prominent PE academics including Rod Thorpe and David Bunker at Loughborough, forming a triumvirate that would revolutionise games teaching methodology.

Beyond his academic work, Almond played a significant role in policy development and curriculum reform. He was instrumental in establishing the Youth Sport Trust in the 1990s, serving as a key advisor and helping to shape its mission to improve the quality of PE and school sport. His influence extended to government policy, contributing to thinking around Active Schools initiatives and health-focused PE frameworks.

In the 2000s, Almond became closely associated with the development and promotion of physical literacy in the UK, working alongside Margaret Whitehead and other advocates to establish this concept as a foundation principle for PE and youth sport.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU)

Len Almond is best known in the UK as one of the primary advocates and interpreters of the Teaching Games for Understanding model. Whilst David Bunker and Rod Thorpe at Loughborough are credited with developing the TGfU framework in 1982, Almond was crucial in articulating its theoretical foundations, promoting its adoption, and demonstrating its practical applications.

TGfU represents a paradigm shift from traditional, technique-first approaches to games teaching. Instead of beginning with isolated skill drills, TGfU places learners in modified game situations where they must understand tactical problems before developing appropriate techniques. The model emphasises game appreciation, tactical awareness, and decision-making before technical execution.

Almond's particular contribution was in explaining *why* TGfU was necessary. He critiqued traditional approaches for producing learners who could perform skills in isolation but couldn't apply them in actual game contexts. He argued that conventional methods often led to disengagement, particularly among less naturally athletic students who spent lessons in repetitive drills without understanding their purpose.

In numerous publications and workshops throughout the 1980s and 1990s, Almond demonstrated how TGfU could make games more inclusive, cognitively engaging, and enjoyable. He emphasised that games teaching should develop 'thinking players' who understand when and why to use particular skills, not merely how to execute them.

Almond categorised games into four types (invasion, net/wall, striking/fielding, and target games), arguing that understanding the tactical similarities within each category could accelerate learning transfer. This classification system became widely adopted in UK PE curricula and remains influential today.

### 2. Physical Literacy

In the 2000s, Almond became one of the foremost advocates for physical literacy as a foundational concept for PE. Working closely with Margaret Whitehead, who provided the philosophical framework based on monism and existential phenomenology, Almond focused on the practical implications for PE practice and policy.

Almond articulated physical literacy as "the motivation, confidence, physical competence, knowledge and understanding to value and take responsibility for engagement in physical activities for life." This definition emphasised that PE should aim for lifelong participation rather than producing elite athletes or focusing solely on fitness outcomes.

He argued that physical literacy should replace the traditional focus on 'physical education' as the core purpose of the subject. Physical literacy, in Almond's view, represented a more holistic goal—developing the whole person through movement rather than merely educating about physical activity.

Almond was particularly influential in promoting physical literacy within policy circles. He contributed to reports and frameworks that positioned physical literacy as the foundation for the Youth Sport Trust's programmes and influenced government thinking about the purpose of school PE. His work helped establish physical literacy as a mainstream concept in UK sport and education policy by the 2010s.

### 3. Health-Related Physical Education (HRE/HRPE)

Almond was a leading figure in developing Health-Related Exercise (HRE), later termed Health-Related Physical Education (HRPE), as a distinct strand within PE curricula. During the 1980s and 1990s, he argued that PE had a crucial role in addressing public health concerns, particularly childhood obesity and sedentary lifestyles.

However, Almond's approach to health education in PE was nuanced and sophisticated. He critiqued purely fitness-focused approaches that emphasised testing and exercise prescription, arguing these could be alienating and counterproductive. Instead, he advocated for an educational approach that developed understanding, positive attitudes, and self-management capabilities.

In his 1989 publication "Health Related Fitness in the National Curriculum" (with others), Almond outlined principles for integrating health concepts throughout PE rather than treating it as a separate unit. He emphasised that young people should understand the relationship between physical activity and health, develop the competence to participate in various activities, and, most importantly, desire to be active.

Almond distinguished between 'exercise' and 'physical activity', arguing that PE should promote the latter—a broader concept encompassing active living rather than structured fitness regimens. This thinking anticipated modern public health approaches emphasising lifestyle activity rather than solely promoting sport or exercise.

He was critical of approaches that measured success through fitness test scores, arguing these could damage self-esteem and create negative associations with physical activity. Instead, he advocated for process-oriented goals focused on understanding, skill development, and fostering intrinsic motivation.

### 4. Sport Education

Almond played a significant role in introducing and adapting the Sport Education model to UK contexts. Originally developed by Daryl Siedentop in the United States, Sport Education structures PE around extended seasons where students take on various roles (players, coaches, officials, managers) and experience sport in a more authentic format.

Almond recognised that Sport Education addressed several limitations of traditional PE: lessons were often too short to develop meaningful skill or understanding; students rarely experienced sport in authentic contexts; and less skilled students had limited opportunities for significant roles.

He promoted Sport Education in UK schools from the 1990s onwards, demonstrating how extended units could develop not just physical skills but also social responsibility, leadership, and deeper game understanding. Almond showed how Sport Education aligned well with TGfU principles, as both emphasised understanding game contexts and developing thinking performers.

His adaptation of Sport Education for UK schools included considerations of curriculum constraints, assessment requirements, and British sporting culture. He provided practical guidance for teachers on managing extended seasons within timetable limitations and integrating Sport Education with National Curriculum requirements.

### 5. Curriculum Development and the National Curriculum for PE

Almond was actively involved in debates surrounding the National Curriculum for Physical Education (NCPE) introduced in England and Wales in 1992. He contributed to thinking about how PE should be structured and assessed within the broader educational framework.

Whilst supportive of establishing PE as a statutory National Curriculum subject, Almond was critical of elements he felt were too prescriptive or activity-focused rather than learning-focused. He argued for curricula that emphasised understanding, decision-making, and personal development rather than merely covering specific activities.

His influence can be seen in the NCPE's inclusion of 'planning, performing and evaluating' as core processes, which reflected TGfU principles of developing thinking performers. He also advocated for the prominence of health and fitness understanding within the curriculum.

Almond contributed to subsequent curriculum revisions, consistently arguing for approaches that would make PE more inclusive and relevant to all young people, not just the athletically gifted. He emphasised that curriculum design should consider lifelong participation as the primary outcome measure.

### 6. Reflective Practice and Teacher Education

Throughout his career at Loughborough, Almond emphasised the importance of reflective practice in PE teaching. He argued that effective teachers must continuously question their practice, evaluate student learning outcomes, and adapt their approaches accordingly.

He promoted action research methodologies for PE teachers, encouraging them to systematically investigate their own practice. This approach aligned with broader movements in teacher education emphasising teachers as researchers and professionals rather than merely technicians implementing prescribed curricula.

Almond's work in teacher education emphasised pedagogical content knowledge—the understanding of how to teach specific content effectively. He argued that PE teachers needed deep understanding not just of activities themselves but of how learners develop competence, common misconceptions, and effective progression strategies.

His influence on PE Initial Teacher Education (ITE) at Loughborough and beyond helped establish more critically reflective, research-informed approaches to teacher preparation.

## Key Publications

### Books and Edited Volumes

**Almond, L. (Ed.) (1989).** *The Place of Physical Education in Schools*. London: Kogan Page.
- This edited collection examined PE's role within the broader educational mission of schools, featuring contributions from leading UK PE academics. Almond's editorial framing positioned PE as essential for holistic education rather than merely extra-curricular.

**Bunker, D., Thorpe, R., & Almond, L. (1986).** *Rethinking Games Teaching*. Loughborough: Loughborough University.
- A seminal text that introduced TGfU to wider UK audiences. Almond contributed chapters on tactical approaches and practical implementation, providing detailed lesson examples and progressions.

**Almond, L., & Harris, J. (1997).** *The ABC of HRE/HRPE: A Practical Guide*. Loughborough: British Association of Advisers and Lecturers in Physical Education (BAALPE).
- A practical guide for teachers implementing health-related physical education, emphasising educational rather than fitness-testing approaches.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

**Almond, L. (1986).** "Reflecting on Themes: A Games Classification." In D. Bunker, R. Thorpe, & L. Almond (Eds.), *Rethinking Games Teaching* (pp. 71-72). Loughborough: Loughborough University.
- Introduced the influential classification of games into four categories, providing theoretical justification for teaching games in related groupings.

**Almond, L. (1983).** "A Rationale for Health-Related Fitness in Schools." *Bulletin of Physical Education*, 19(2), 5-10.
- An early articulation of why schools should incorporate health education within PE, establishing principles that would guide subsequent HRE/HRPE development.

**Almond, L. (1997).** "Physical Education in Schools." In L. Almond (Ed.), *Physical Education in Schools*, 2nd edition. London: Kogan Page.
- Examined PE's contribution to broader educational goals and young people's development.

**Almond, L. (2010).** "Physical Literacy and the Older Adult Population." In M. Whitehead (Ed.), *Physical Literacy: Throughout the Lifecourse* (pp. 116-129). London: Routledge.
- Explored how physical literacy concepts apply beyond school-age populations, demonstrating the concept's lifespan relevance.

**Almond, L. (2013).** "What is the Value of Physical Literacy and Why is Physical Literacy Valuable?" Paper presented at the ICSSPE Conference, Berlin.
- A detailed examination of physical literacy's conceptual foundations and its potential to transform PE practice and policy.

### Professional Reports and Curriculum Documents

Almond contributed to numerous Youth Sport Trust reports and curriculum guidance documents throughout the 1990s and 2000s, including materials supporting the TOP programmes and resources for implementing Sport Education in schools.

He also provided consultancy for various national and regional PE development initiatives, producing guidance documents on assessment, curriculum planning, and pedagogical approaches.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Impact

Almond's ideas significantly influenced the structure and philosophy of the National Curriculum for Physical Education (NCPE) in England. The emphasis on planning, performing, and evaluating as interconnected processes reflects TGfU principles he championed. The inclusion of health and fitness understanding as statutory content similarly reflects his advocacy for HRE/HRPE.

Subsequent curriculum revisions have maintained these emphases, with the current NCPE (2014) continuing to prioritise tactical understanding, health knowledge, and reflective practice—all areas where Almond's influence is evident.

### Youth Sport Trust

Almond's involvement with the Youth Sport Trust from its establishment in 1994 was particularly significant. He helped shape the philosophical foundations of programmes like TOP Play, TOP Sport, and TOP Link, which introduced developmentally appropriate games activities to thousands of UK primary and secondary schools.

These programmes embodied TGfU principles, using modified games and equipment to ensure all children could participate successfully. They also reflected Almond's emphasis on enjoyment and inclusion rather than early specialisation or elitism.

### Physical Literacy Movement

Almond's advocacy was crucial in establishing physical literacy as a mainstream concept in UK sport and education policy. His work influenced:

- Sport England's adoption of physical literacy as a foundation principle
- The Association for Physical Education's (afPE) embrace of physical literacy as central to PE's purpose
- UK Coaching's integration of physical literacy into coach education frameworks
- Public Health England's recognition of physical literacy in active living strategies

By translating Margaret Whitehead's philosophical work into accessible, practical guidance, Almond made physical literacy comprehensible and actionable for practitioners and policymakers.

### International Influence

Almond's work, particularly on TGfU, has had international impact. He contributed to conferences and publications in North America, Europe, Asia, and Australasia, helping establish TGfU as a globally recognised pedagogical approach. The International TGfU Association acknowledges his contributions to developing and promoting the model worldwide.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Learner-Centred Philosophy

Central to Almond's pedagogical approach is learner-centredness. He consistently argued that PE should start from students' perspectives, interests, and developmental needs rather than teachers' agendas or activity traditions. This meant:

- Prioritising understanding and enjoyment over performance standards
- Ensuring all students experience success and challenge appropriate to their abilities
- Recognising diverse motivations for physical activity participation
- Valuing student voice in activity selection and lesson design

### Constructivist Learning

Almond's advocacy for TGfU and similar approaches reflects constructivist learning principles. He believed students should construct understanding through guided discovery rather than receiving knowledge passively. This meant:

- Presenting tactical problems for students to solve
- Using questioning to stimulate thinking rather than merely demonstrating techniques
- Allowing students to discover effective solutions through experimentation
- Building progressively from simple to complex game situations

### Inclusivity and Differentiation

Throughout his work, Almond emphasised that PE should be inclusive and accessible to all students, regardless of ability level. His approaches inherently provided differentiation:

- Modified games allowed success at various skill levels
- Emphasis on understanding meant less physically capable students could still excel
- Variety of roles (in Sport Education) ensured different strengths could be valued
- Focus on personal improvement rather than comparative performance

### Critical Thinking and Autonomy

Almond advocated developing students' capacity for independent thinking and self-management. This included:

- Understanding principles rather than merely following instructions
- Making informed decisions about personal physical activity participation
- Critically evaluating health and fitness information
- Taking responsibility for own learning and development

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

Len Almond's influence on UK Physical Education remains profound and ongoing. TGfU principles are now mainstream in UK PE, reflected in curriculum documents, teacher education programmes, and classroom practice. The game classification system he promoted is standard in UK PE curricula.

Physical literacy has become the dominant framework for articulating PE's purpose in the UK, appearing in national governing body strategies, curriculum guidance, and policy documents. Almond's role in establishing this concept cannot be overstated.

His emphasis on health education within PE anticipated contemporary concerns about childhood obesity, mental health, and physical inactivity. Current approaches to health-focused PE continue to reflect his principles: educational rather than prescriptive, empowering rather than testing, and focused on lifelong participation rather than short-term fitness gains.

Contemporary challenges in PE—including engagement of disaffected students, relevance of traditional curricula, and demonstrating PE's contribution to broader educational outcomes—are precisely those Almond addressed throughout his career. His work provides both philosophical justification and practical strategies for meeting these challenges.
