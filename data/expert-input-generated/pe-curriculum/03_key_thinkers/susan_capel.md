# Susan Capel

**Full Name:** Susan Capel

**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Physical Education, Brunel University London

**Known For:** Leading expert in PE teacher education, pioneering research on learning to teach PE, mentoring in physical education, and comprehensive work on the theory and practice of physical education pedagogy in the UK.

---

## Biography

### Academic Career and Professional Background

Susan Capel is one of the most influential figures in UK physical education teacher education, with a career spanning over four decades. She has been based primarily at Brunel University London, where she established herself as a pre-eminent researcher and educator in the field of PE teacher education. Her work has fundamentally shaped how physical education teachers are prepared, supported, and developed throughout their careers in the UK and internationally.

Capel's academic journey positioned her at the forefront of research into the complex processes of learning to teach PE. She recognised early in her career that PE teacher education required distinct approaches that acknowledged the unique contexts, challenges, and pedagogical demands of the subject. Her research has consistently focused on understanding the experiences of student teachers, the role of mentors, and the development of pedagogical knowledge specific to physical education.

Throughout her career at Brunel, Capel has supervised numerous doctoral students, many of whom have become leading PE academics and teacher educators themselves. Her leadership in the field extended beyond research to include significant contributions to policy development and professional standards for PE teachers.

### Professional Leadership

Capel has held numerous leadership positions within the physical education academic community. She has been actively involved with the Association for Physical Education (afPE) and has contributed to various governmental and non-governmental consultations on PE teacher education and curriculum development. Her work has informed teacher training standards and quality assurance frameworks for initial teacher education (ITE) in physical education across England.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The Unique Nature of Learning to Teach PE

Capel's foundational contribution centres on articulating why learning to teach PE differs fundamentally from learning to teach other subjects. She identified that PE teachers must develop competence across multiple activity areas, manage complex safety considerations, work in varied and often challenging teaching spaces, and address the dual objectives of physical development and broader educational outcomes. Her research demonstrated that PE student teachers face particular challenges including managing changing facilities, dealing with mixed-ability groups in practical contexts, and addressing pupils' varied prior experiences and confidence levels in physical activity.

In her extensive research, Capel highlighted that PE teachers must simultaneously develop subject knowledge across diverse activities (games, gymnastics, dance, athletics, outdoor and adventurous activities, swimming), pedagogical content knowledge specific to teaching movement and physical skills, and the ability to create inclusive learning environments that account for significant variations in pupils' physical capabilities and body confidence.

### 2. School-Based Mentoring in PE

Capel's work on mentoring represents some of the most comprehensive research on supporting PE student teachers during school placements. She identified that effective mentoring in PE requires mentors who understand the specific pedagogical demands of the subject and can provide targeted support beyond generic teaching competences. Her research mapped the knowledge, skills, and dispositions required of effective PE mentors.

Key findings from her mentoring research include the critical importance of regular, focused observation and feedback sessions; the need for mentors to model effective PE pedagogy; and the value of creating opportunities for student teachers to gradually assume teaching responsibilities. Capel identified common problems in PE mentoring arrangements, including insufficient time for mentor-student teacher interactions, limited mentor training, and mentors who prioritised generic teaching skills over PE-specific pedagogical development.

Her work emphasised that mentoring relationships in PE are complicated by the practical, active nature of lessons, which can make observation and immediate feedback challenging. She advocated for structured mentoring frameworks that include pre-lesson planning discussions, systematic observation, and reflective post-lesson dialogues focused on PE-specific pedagogical decisions.

### 3. Student Teacher Concerns and Stress

Capel conducted pioneering research on the concerns and sources of stress experienced by PE student teachers. Drawing on Fuller and Bown's teacher concerns theory, she mapped how PE student teachers' concerns evolved from initial self-concerns (Can I control the class? Will I be accepted?) through task concerns (How do I plan effective lessons? How do I manage equipment?) to impact concerns (Are pupils learning? How can I differentiate effectively?).

Her longitudinal studies revealed that PE student teachers experienced particular stressors including anxiety about demonstrating activities, concerns about pupil safety, worries about managing behaviour in large practical spaces, and pressure to develop competence across multiple activity areas. This research informed recommendations for how ITE programmes should sequence and support student teachers' development, ensuring appropriate scaffolding and support at each stage.

Capel's work in this area challenged ITE providers to create more responsive support structures that acknowledged the emotional and psychological dimensions of learning to teach PE, not just the technical skills development.

### 4. Models of PE Teacher Education

Capel has been a leading voice in debates about the appropriate balance between school-based and university-based preparation for PE teachers. She has consistently argued for the importance of strong theoretical foundations alongside extensive practical experience. Her research has examined various models of ITE, from traditional Post Graduate Certificate in Education (PGCE) routes to School Direct and employment-based routes.

She has been critical of overly school-centred models that reduce university input, arguing that theoretical knowledge from exercise physiology, motor learning, psychology, sociology, and pedagogy is essential for developing adaptive experts who can respond to diverse contexts and evolving curriculum demands. Capel's position is that effective PE teachers require both practical teaching experience and the conceptual tools to analyse, evaluate, and continuously improve their practice.

Her comparative analyses of different ITE routes have demonstrated that partnership models—where universities and schools collaborate with clearly defined complementary roles—produce the most effective outcomes. She has advocated for ITE models where universities provide subject knowledge enhancement, introduce research and evidence-based practice, and develop critical reflection capabilities, whilst schools provide authentic teaching contexts, mentoring, and opportunities to apply and refine pedagogical skills.

### 5. Subject Knowledge in PE Teacher Education

Capel's research has examined the complex relationship between personal participation in sport and physical activity and the capacity to teach PE effectively. She challenged assumptions that being a competent performer automatically translates to effective teaching. Her work demonstrated that many PE student teachers enter ITE with strong capabilities in one or two sports but require significant subject knowledge development across the breadth of the National Curriculum for Physical Education.

She identified that developing pedagogical content knowledge (PCK)—knowing how to teach specific content to specific learners—is a lengthy process that extends well beyond initial teacher education. Her research has informed curriculum design in ITE programmes, highlighting the need for subject knowledge audits, targeted subject knowledge enhancement sessions, and systematic opportunities to develop PCK across activity areas.

Capel also examined the challenge of preparing generalist primary teachers to teach PE, highlighting the insufficient time and preparation that many primary ITE programmes devote to PE, despite its statutory curriculum status.

### 6. Reflective Practice in PE Teacher Development

Capel has been a strong advocate for developing reflective practitioners in PE. She introduced various models and frameworks for structured reflection, emphasising that reflection must move beyond superficial description to critical analysis of pedagogical decisions and their impacts on pupil learning. Her work drew on Schön's concepts of reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action, adapting these for the specific context of PE teaching.

She developed practical tools for supporting reflection, including structured observation schedules, reflective journals with targeted prompts, and frameworks for analysing video recordings of teaching. Her research demonstrated that reflective capacity develops over time and requires explicit teaching and support, rather than emerging naturally from teaching experience.

### 7. The Transition from Student Teacher to Newly Qualified Teacher

Capel's longitudinal research followed PE teachers from ITE through their early career years, documenting the "transition shock" many experience. She identified common challenges including increased teaching loads, limited support after the structured mentoring of ITE, pressure to contribute to extra-curricular programmes, and the reality of working in departments where established practices might conflict with the approaches learned during training.

This research informed recommendations for structured induction programmes, continued mentoring support, and realistic workload management for newly qualified PE teachers. Capel advocated for career-long professional development systems that recognise teaching expertise develops progressively through novice, advanced beginner, competent, proficient, and expert stages.

## Key Publications

### Core Textbooks

**Learning to Teach Physical Education in the Secondary School: A Companion to School Experience** (Various editions, Routledge)

This comprehensive textbook, which Capel edited and contributed to extensively, has become the definitive guide for PE student teachers in the UK. Now in its fourth edition (2016), co-edited with Margaret Whitehead, it systematically addresses all aspects of learning to teach PE, from understanding the purposes of PE through planning and assessment to professional development. The book integrates theoretical perspectives with practical guidance and has been adopted by virtually every secondary PE ITE programme in England. Each chapter includes tasks for student teachers, further reading, and connections to the Teachers' Standards.

**Issues in Physical Education** (Routledge, various editions)

Co-edited with Peter Breckon in earlier editions and later with Margaret Whitehead, this companion volume addresses contemporary debates and challenges in PE. It examines philosophical, sociological, and pedagogical issues including inclusion, assessment, cross-curricular dimensions, and the aims of PE. The book encourages critical engagement with contested aspects of PE rather than presenting simplistic solutions.

**A Practical Guide to Teaching Physical Education in the Secondary School** (Routledge, 2nd edition 2011)

This accessible text provides focused, practical guidance on essential teaching skills specific to PE. Co-edited with Peter Breckon, it addresses topics such as lesson planning, demonstration and explanation in PE, managing practical spaces, and teaching specific activity areas.

### Research Publications on PE Teacher Education

**Capel, S. (2007). "Moving beyond physical education subject knowledge to develop knowledgeable teachers of the subject." *The Curriculum Journal*, 18(4), 493-507.**

This influential paper articulated the distinction between subject content knowledge and pedagogical content knowledge in PE. Capel argued that ITE must explicitly address the transformation of subject knowledge into teachable forms, rather than assuming this occurs automatically. The paper provided frameworks for understanding the multiple knowledge bases PE teachers require.

**Capel, S. (2005). "Teachers, teaching and pedagogy in physical education." In K. Green & K. Hardman (Eds.), *Physical Education: Essential Issues* (pp. 111-127). London: SAGE.**

This chapter examined the professional knowledge base for PE teaching, distinguishing between craft knowledge (developed through experience), content knowledge (understanding activities and their educational applications), and pedagogical knowledge (knowing how to teach). Capel's analysis informed debates about appropriate preparation routes for PE teachers.

**Capel, S. (1997). "Changes in students' anxieties and concerns after their first and second teaching practices." *Educational Research*, 39(2), 211-228.**

This empirical study tracked changes in PE student teachers' concerns across their school placements, demonstrating that concerns evolved predictably but that some student teachers remained focused on survival concerns longer than others. The research informed recommendations for differentiated support during ITE.

**Capel, S. (1998). "An investigation into the effects of initial teacher education on the concerns of beginning physical education teachers." In *Proceedings of the Second International Conference of the Professional Preparation of Physical Education Teachers* (pp. 45-52).**

This paper extended her earlier work on teacher concerns, examining how different ITE structures influenced the rate and nature of concern development, with implications for programme design.

### Work on Mentoring

**Capel, S. (2001). "Secondary students' development as teachers over the course of a PGCE year." *Educational Research*, 43(3), 247-261.**

This longitudinal study provided detailed insights into the developmental trajectories of PE student teachers, identifying critical incidents, turning points, and the factors that supported or hindered development. The research highlighted significant individual variation in developmental pathways.

**Capel, S. & Blair, R. (2007). "Making physical education relevant: Increasing the impact of initial teacher training." *London: Institute of Education, Training and Development Agency for Schools.***

This TDA-funded research report examined effective practices in PE ITE partnerships, identifying factors that contributed to high-quality preparation including structured mentoring, university-school collaboration, and systematic curriculum design.

**Capel, S. (2008). "Debates and issues in PE." In S. Capel, M. Leask & T. Turner (Eds.), *Learning to Teach in the Secondary School: A Companion to School Experience* (5th ed.). Abingdon: Routledge.**

While part of a broader teacher education text, Capel's contributions on PE consistently emphasised the subject-specific pedagogical knowledge required and the distinctive contexts in which PE teaching occurs.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on Initial Teacher Education Standards

Capel's research has directly influenced the development of professional standards for PE teachers in England. Her work informed the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA) and later the Training and Development Agency for Schools (TDA) in their specifications for ITE in PE. Her articulation of subject-specific pedagogical knowledge requirements helped establish that generic teaching standards alone were insufficient for PE teacher preparation.

When the Department for Education (DfE) introduced the Teachers' Standards in 2012, Capel's published work provided evidence for how these standards should be interpreted and assessed specifically in PE contexts. Her textbooks systematically address how each standard applies to PE teaching.

### Contribution to Subject Association Work

Through her engagement with the Association for Physical Education (afPE, formerly the Physical Education Association of the United Kingdom), Capel contributed to professional guidance documents, quality assurance frameworks for ITE, and position statements on PE teacher education. Her research evidence supported afPE advocacy for maintaining strong university involvement in ITE, even as policy shifted towards more school-led routes.

### International Influence

Capel's work has influenced PE teacher education internationally, particularly in Europe and Commonwealth countries. Her textbooks are used in multiple countries, and her research on mentoring and teacher development has been cited extensively in international journals. She has contributed to International Council for Health, Physical Education, Recreation, Sport, and Dance (ICHPER-SD) conferences and publications.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Commitment to Evidence-Based Practice

Capel's pedagogical philosophy emphasises the importance of evidence-based practice in PE teacher education. She advocates for ITE programmes that develop teachers' capacity to engage with research, critically evaluate educational practices, and make informed pedagogical decisions grounded in both empirical evidence and sound educational theory.

Her approach integrates knowledge from multiple disciplines—exercise physiology, motor learning, developmental psychology, sociology of sport, and pedagogy—arguing that effective PE teachers must draw on this broad knowledge base to plan inclusive, developmentally appropriate, and educationally worthwhile learning experiences.

### Developing Adaptive Expertise

Rather than training teachers to follow prescribed methods, Capel's approach aims to develop adaptive expertise—the capacity to analyse contexts, diagnose learning needs, select and modify teaching strategies appropriately, and evaluate outcomes critically. Her work emphasises that teaching PE involves constant decision-making in complex, dynamic environments where pre-planned approaches must be adjusted responsively.

She advocates for ITE programmes that provide multiple, varied teaching experiences with systematic support for analysing and learning from each experience. Her pedagogical approach values both successful lessons and difficulties as learning opportunities when approached reflectively.

### Theory-Practice Integration

A consistent theme across Capel's work is the necessity of strong theory-practice integration in PE teacher education. She rejects false dichotomies between theoretical and practical knowledge, arguing instead that theoretical frameworks provide essential tools for understanding practice, whilst practical experience grounds theoretical understanding and reveals its applications.

Her textbooks model this integration, consistently linking practical guidance to research evidence and theoretical frameworks. For student teachers, she advocates pedagogies that require active application of theoretical concepts to analyse teaching episodes, plan lessons, and reflect on practice.

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

Susan Capel's contribution to PE teacher education in the UK is unparalleled in its scope and impact. Her research has provided the evidence base for understanding how PE teachers develop, what they need to learn, and how ITE programmes can most effectively support their development. Her textbooks have shaped the professional learning of thousands of PE teachers and continue to be core resources in ITE programmes.

At a time when teacher education policy has emphasised school-led training and reduced university involvement, Capel's research provides crucial evidence for the value of structured, research-informed, university-supported teacher preparation. Her work demonstrates that effective PE teaching requires sophisticated professional knowledge that develops through carefully designed learning experiences, expert mentoring, and sustained reflection.

The frameworks, concepts, and evidence Capel has provided continue to inform:
- ITE programme design and quality assurance
- Mentoring training and support systems
- Professional standards and assessment criteria
- Continuing professional development for PE teachers
- Policy debates about teacher education models

Her emphasis on subject-specific pedagogy has established PE teacher education as a distinct field of scholarship, not simply an application of generic teacher education principles. This recognition has been crucial for maintaining the quality and professional status of PE teaching in the UK.
