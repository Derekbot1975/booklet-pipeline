# David Kirk

**Full Name:** Professor David Kirk

**Current Role:** Formerly Professor of Physical Education at the University of Strathclyde (retired); previously held chairs at the University of Bedfordshire and Loughborough University

**Known For:** Models-based practice in Physical Education, situated learning theory applied to PE, critical analysis of PE futures, theorising physical education curriculum development, and sport education models

---

## Biography and Career History

Professor David Kirk is one of the most influential physical education scholars of the late 20th and early 21st centuries, with a career spanning over four decades. Born in Australia, Kirk completed his PhD at Loughborough University in the UK in the 1980s, where he developed his early interests in the sociology and pedagogy of physical education.

Kirk held professorial positions at several UK institutions, most notably at Loughborough University, the University of Bedfordshire (where he was Dean of the Faculty of Education and Sport), and finally at the University of Strathclyde in Glasgow, where he served as Alexander Chair in Physical Education and Sport. His tenure at Strathclyde (2006-2016) was particularly significant, as he established the institution as a major centre for PE research and developed many of his most influential ideas about models-based practice and the future of physical education.

Throughout his career, Kirk has maintained strong connections with international PE research communities, particularly in Australia, North America, and continental Europe. He has supervised numerous doctoral students who have themselves become leading PE researchers and educators, extending his influence across generations of scholarship.

Kirk's work has been characterised by a critical sociological lens, examining how physical education has evolved historically and how it might be reimagined for contemporary educational contexts. He has been particularly concerned with the disconnect between traditional PE curriculum models and the needs of young people in the 21st century.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Models-Based Practice in Physical Education

Kirk's most significant contribution to PE pedagogy is his development and advocacy of models-based practice (MBP). In his seminal 2013 article 'Educational value and models-based practice in physical education' (published in *Educational Philosophy and Theory*), Kirk argued that PE teachers should move away from multi-activity, technique-focused curricula toward coherent pedagogical models that emphasise educational values.

Models-based practice involves selecting and implementing complete pedagogical frameworks such as Sport Education, Teaching Games for Understanding (TGfU), Cooperative Learning, or Teaching Personal and Social Responsibility (TPSR). Kirk argued that these models provide theoretically coherent approaches that align learning activities, assessment, and intended outcomes in ways that fragmented, activity-based programmes cannot achieve.

Kirk distinguished between 'pedagogical models' (comprehensive frameworks with clear theoretical foundations) and mere 'teaching styles' or isolated activities. He emphasised that effective models must articulate: (1) critical learning outcomes, (2) developmentally appropriate content, (3) aligned pedagogical strategies, and (4) authentic assessment practices. This framework has fundamentally reshaped how PE teacher education programmes in the UK approach curriculum planning.

### 2. Situated Learning and Communities of Practice

Drawing heavily on the work of Jean Lave and Etienne Wenger, Kirk applied situated learning theory to physical education contexts. In his 2005 book *Physical Education Futures* (Routledge), Kirk argued that learning in PE is fundamentally social and occurs through participation in communities of practice, rather than through decontextualised skill acquisition.

This perspective challenged traditional approaches where skills were taught in isolation before being 'applied' in game contexts. Instead, Kirk advocated for authentic learning environments where students engage with the cultural and social dimensions of physical activities from the outset. For example, in Sport Education, students don't just learn volleyball techniques; they participate in the full culture of a sport season, including team affiliation, roles, records, and culminating events.

Kirk's situated learning perspective influenced his critique of 'Physical Education as Sport Techniques' (PEST), which he saw as an impoverished model that reduced rich movement cultures to decontextualised technical exercises. He argued that PE must engage students in meaningful participation within authentic communities of physical culture.

### 3. Critical Analysis of Physical Education Futures

Kirk has been a leading voice in analysing threats to and possibilities for physical education's future. His 2010 monograph *Physical Education Futures* represented a landmark critical analysis of PE's identity crisis in the 21st century. Kirk identified several challenges:

- The persistence of multi-activity, sport-techniques curriculum models developed in the 1950s-60s that no longer serve contemporary educational purposes
- The marginalisation of PE within increasingly academic-focused school curricula
- The rise of physical activity and health agendas that threaten to reduce PE to mere exercise prescription
- The growth of commercial and community sport providers potentially displacing school PE

Kirk argued that PE's survival depends on articulating clear educational values that distinguish it from both elite sport development and public health interventions. He advocated for a 'physical education-as-health' approach that is educationally defensible, focusing on physical literacy, embodied knowledge, and lifelong participation rather than short-term health outcomes.

### 4. Historical Sociology of Physical Education

Kirk's early work established him as a leading historical sociologist of PE. His 1992 book *Defining Physical Education: The Social Construction of a School Subject in Postwar Britain* (Falmer Press) provided a rigorous historical analysis of how PE became established as a school subject in the UK context.

This work revealed how PE curriculum was shaped by competing interest groups, including medical professionals, military establishments, women's physical education colleges, and educational reformers. Kirk demonstrated that curriculum decisions that appear 'natural' or 'common sense' are actually products of historical power struggles and social contexts.

This historical perspective informed Kirk's later advocacy for curriculum change. Understanding that the current PE curriculum is a historical construction, rather than an inevitable or optimal form, opened possibilities for reimagining PE for contemporary contexts.

### 5. Pedagogical Models as Curriculum

Kirk developed the concept of using pedagogical models themselves as curriculum frameworks, rather than simply as teaching methods within traditional multi-activity programmes. In his work with Australian colleague Ann MacPhail and others, Kirk argued that schools should organise their PE curriculum around a sequence of pedagogical models, each taught for extended blocks (6-12 weeks or longer).

For example, Year 7 might experience Sport Education (focused on invasion games), followed by an Adventure Education model (emphasising personal challenge and group cooperation), then a Health-Related Exercise model. This 'models as curriculum' approach ensures coherence, depth, and alignment with educational outcomes that multi-activity programmes cannot achieve.

Kirk's research showed that implementing pedagogical models requires substantial teacher professional development and institutional support. Teachers need deep understanding of each model's theoretical foundations, not just surface-level familiarity with activities. This recognition led Kirk to advocate for reformed PE teacher education programmes centred on models-based practice.

### 6. Physical Literacy Critique

While supporting the general movement toward physical literacy, Kirk provided important critical perspectives on how this concept has been interpreted and implemented. In several publications, he warned against reductionist versions of physical literacy that simply rebrand traditional skill-focused approaches.

Kirk distinguished between genuine physical literacy (as articulated by Margaret Whitehead, emphasising embodied dimension of human existence, motivation, and lifelong participation) and superficial applications that merely assess motor competencies. He argued that physical literacy must be understood holistically, incorporating affective, cognitive, physical, and social dimensions within authentic movement contexts.

This critique has been important in UK contexts, where physical literacy has been adopted by various policy documents and national governing bodies with varying degrees of theoretical coherence.

## Influence on UK Curriculum and Policy

### Initial Teacher Education Reform

Kirk's models-based practice framework has significantly influenced PE teacher education programmes across the UK. Universities including Loughborough, Edinburgh, Bedfordshire, and many others have restructured their PGCE and undergraduate PE courses to centre on pedagogical models rather than activity-specific modules.

The Association for Physical Education (afPE), the UK's professional body for PE practitioners, has incorporated models-based practice into its continuing professional development offerings and guidance documents. Kirk has delivered numerous keynote addresses and professional development workshops for afPE and other practitioner organisations.

### National Curriculum Influence

While Kirk has not directly written national curriculum documents, his critique of multi-activity programmes and advocacy for coherent pedagogical models has influenced how PE leaders interpret and implement the National Curriculum for Physical Education in England. His work has provided theoretical justification for schools moving away from traditional two-week activity rotations toward extended units using complete pedagogical models.

Kirk's emphasis on educational values in PE has influenced curriculum thinking in Scotland particularly, where Curriculum for Excellence's emphasis on capacities (successful learners, confident individuals, responsible citizens, effective contributors) aligns well with outcomes from models like Sport Education and Cooperative Learning.

### Youth Sport Policy

Kirk's research on the relationship between school PE and lifelong participation has informed policy discussions around youth sport development. His work has been cited in Sport England and UK Sport policy documents, particularly regarding the importance of quality early experiences in physical education for sustaining participation into adulthood.

His critical perspective on the commercialisation of school sport has provided counterbalance to policy initiatives that prioritise elite talent identification over broad-based participation. Kirk has consistently argued that PE's primary purpose is educational, not sport development, though positive sport participation can be a beneficial outcome.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Theoretical Foundations

Kirk's pedagogical approach is grounded in several interconnected theoretical frameworks:

**Constructivist Learning Theory:** Students construct knowledge through active engagement rather than passive reception. PE should provide rich environments for movement problem-solving and decision-making rather than prescribed technical execution.

**Situated Learning Theory:** Learning occurs through participation in authentic communities of practice. PE should engage students in complete cultural practices (games, sports, dance forms) rather than decontextualised skills.

**Critical Pedagogy:** Education should empower students to understand and potentially transform social conditions. PE should develop students' critical awareness of how physical culture reflects and reproduces social inequalities while providing tools for active citizenship.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Kirk's work suggests several practical approaches for PE teachers:

1. **Select appropriate pedagogical models** based on learning outcomes, student characteristics, and contextual factors. Teachers should develop deep expertise in 2-4 models initially rather than superficial familiarity with many approaches.

2. **Implement models with integrity** for sufficient duration (minimum 6-8 weeks) to achieve intended outcomes. Hybrid or abbreviated versions often lose theoretical coherence and educational value.

3. **Make educational values explicit** to students, parents, and colleagues. Articulate why specific models and activities are chosen in terms of educational outcomes, not just enjoyment or tradition.

4. **Create authentic learning environments** where students engage with full cultural practices, including social roles, responsibilities, and decision-making opportunities.

5. **Assess holistically** across physical, cognitive, social, and affective domains using authentic assessment tasks aligned with model outcomes.

### Teacher Professional Development

Kirk recognised that implementing models-based practice requires substantial professional development. Teachers educated in traditional multi-activity programmes may lack theoretical knowledge and practical experience with pedagogical models.

Effective professional development should include:
- Extended engagement with model theory and research evidence
- Opportunities to experience models as learners
- Supported practice implementation with observation and feedback
- Collaborative planning with colleagues
- Ongoing reflection and refinement

Kirk's work at Strathclyde included developing professional development programmes for practicing teachers transitioning to models-based practice, demonstrating the feasibility of curriculum reform with appropriate support.

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

Though now retired from full-time academic positions, Kirk's influence continues through:

- Former doctoral students holding influential academic and policy positions internationally
- Widespread adoption of models-based practice frameworks in teacher education programmes
- Ongoing citation of his theoretical work in PE research literature
- Professional development programmes based on his pedagogical models framework
- Critical scholarship examining PE futures that builds on his foundational analyses

Kirk's work represents a distinctive UK contribution to international PE scholarship, combining rigorous sociological analysis with practical pedagogical innovation. His insistence that PE must articulate clear educational values to justify its place in the curriculum has shaped contemporary debates about the subject's purpose and future.
