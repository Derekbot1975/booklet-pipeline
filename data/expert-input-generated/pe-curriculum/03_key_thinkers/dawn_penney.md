# Dawn Penney

**Full Name:** Dawn Penney

**Current Role:** Professor and Dean of the School of Education at Edith Cowan University, Western Australia (as of recent appointment)

**Previously:** Professor of Physical Education and Sport Pedagogy at various institutions including the University of Tasmania, Loughborough University, and the University of Waikato, New Zealand

**Known For:** Leading international scholar in physical education curriculum policy, equity and social justice in PE, curriculum development and implementation, policy sociology in education, and pedagogical innovation in physical education

---

## Biography and Career History

Dawn Penney is one of the most influential voices in international physical education scholarship, with particular impact on curriculum policy development and implementation in the UK, Australia, and New Zealand. Her academic career spans over three decades, during which she has established herself as a leading authority on the relationship between policy, curriculum, and pedagogy in physical education.

Penney completed her doctoral research at Loughborough University under the supervision of Professor John Evans, focusing on the development and implementation of the National Curriculum for Physical Education (NCPE) in England and Wales. This foundational work established her expertise in policy sociology and curriculum analysis, examining the political processes through which PE curriculum policy is constructed and enacted.

Her career has included significant appointments at Loughborough University (UK), the University of Waikato (New Zealand), Edith Cowan University (Australia), and the University of Tasmania (Australia). This international experience has enabled her to undertake comparative curriculum policy analysis across different national contexts, enriching her understanding of how policy operates in diverse educational systems.

Throughout her career, Penney has maintained a consistent focus on issues of equity, inclusion, and social justice in physical education. She has been particularly concerned with how curriculum policy and pedagogical practices can either reproduce or challenge educational and social inequalities related to gender, ability, social class, and cultural background.

Her work is characterised by a commitment to bridging the gap between policy, research, and practice. She has worked extensively with curriculum authorities, schools, and teachers to develop and implement innovative curriculum frameworks and pedagogical approaches that better serve diverse student populations.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Policy Sociology and Curriculum Development

Penney's work draws extensively on the policy sociology framework developed by Stephen Ball, applying it specifically to physical education contexts. She argues that curriculum policy must be understood not as a straightforward transmission from policymakers to practitioners, but as a complex process involving multiple contexts of influence, text production, and practice.

In her analysis of the NCPE, Penney demonstrated how policy texts are politically constructed through negotiation between competing interests and ideologies. She revealed how traditional sport-based interests dominated the development of the English NCPE, marginalising alternative visions of physical education focused on health, personal development, or social objectives.

Her concept of "policy as discourse" emphasises how curriculum documents shape what can be thought and said about physical education, privileging certain knowledge, activities, and pedagogies while marginalising others. This has profound implications for equity, as dominant discourses often reflect the interests of already-privileged groups.

### 2. Curriculum Texts and the Policy-Practice Gap

Penney has extensively examined the relationship between written curriculum policies and their enactment in schools. She challenges simplistic notions of "implementation" or "delivery," arguing instead that teachers actively interpret, mediate, and transform policy texts in practice.

Her research, particularly with John Evans, identified how the broad, outcomes-based language of curriculum documents creates spaces for different interpretations. While this flexibility can enable teacher agency and contextual responsiveness, it can also lead to inconsistency and the reproduction of traditional practices that policy intended to change.

She has demonstrated how factors such as teachers' subject knowledge, pedagogical beliefs, school resources, assessment systems, and community expectations all mediate how curriculum policy is translated into classroom practice. Understanding these mediating factors is essential for effective curriculum development and implementation.

### 3. Equity, Inclusion, and Social Justice in Physical Education

Throughout her career, Penney has maintained a sustained focus on equity issues in PE. She has examined how curriculum structures, pedagogical practices, and assessment systems can systematically disadvantage particular groups of students.

Her work on gender equity, developed with John Evans and others, demonstrated how the NCPE's emphasis on traditional team games and competitive sport privileged masculine forms of physicality and disadvantaged many girls. She has advocated for curriculum structures that provide genuine choice and ensure diverse pathways to success in PE.

Penney has been particularly critical of ability-based grouping and competitive structures in PE, arguing that these often reinforce social hierarchies and limit opportunities for students with lower perceived ability. She advocates for pedagogical approaches that recognise and value diverse forms of physical competence and provide multiple ways for students to demonstrate achievement.

Her work extends beyond gender to encompass issues of social class, ethnicity, disability, and body size. She has consistently argued that achieving equity requires more than simply providing access; it demands fundamental reconsideration of what counts as legitimate knowledge and achievement in PE.

### 4. Curriculum Integration and Cross-Curricular Connections

Penney has been a strong advocate for breaking down subject boundaries and developing integrated approaches to curriculum. She argues that the traditional isolation of PE from other learning areas limits its educational potential and marginalises it within schools.

Her work has explored connections between PE and health education, arguing for integrated approaches that help students understand the relationship between physical activity, health, and wellbeing. She has been critical of narrow, exercise-focused approaches to health in PE, advocating instead for socially critical perspectives that examine broader determinants of health and physical activity participation.

She has also examined connections between PE and other curriculum areas such as science, social studies, and literacy, demonstrating how PE can contribute to broader educational goals when positioned as part of integrated learning experiences.

### 5. Sport Education and Curriculum Innovation

Penney has made significant contributions to the development and research of Sport Education, a curriculum and pedagogical model originally developed by Daryl Siedentop. Sport Education structures PE around extended seasons of authentic sport experiences, with students taking on roles beyond player, such as coach, referee, and administrator.

Her research has examined how Sport Education can promote student engagement, develop social and organisational skills, and provide more meaningful sport experiences than traditional multi-activity PE curricula. She has explored how the model creates opportunities for students who may not excel as performers to find success in other roles.

However, Penney has also offered critical perspectives on Sport Education, examining whose interests the model serves and how it might be adapted to better address equity concerns. She has explored how different versions of Sport Education privilege different forms of knowledge and activity, with implications for which students benefit most.

### 6. Assessment and Standards in Physical Education

Penney has contributed significantly to debates about assessment in PE, particularly in the context of standards-based and outcomes-based education reforms. She has examined how assessment systems shape curriculum and pedagogy, often in ways that narrow the focus of teaching and learning.

Her work has critiqued assessment practices that privilege easily measurable performance outcomes while neglecting broader educational goals. She advocates for assessment approaches that recognise diverse achievements, support learning, and avoid reinforcing social inequalities.

She has been particularly concerned with how high-stakes assessment can pressure teachers to focus on certain students or activities, potentially widening equity gaps. Her research has examined how assessment requirements interact with other policy pressures to shape what teachers prioritise in practice.

### 7. Teacher Development and Professional Learning

Recognising that curriculum change depends fundamentally on teachers, Penney has conducted extensive research on teacher professional learning. She has examined what types of professional development effectively support teachers to implement innovative curriculum approaches.

Her work emphasises the importance of sustained, collaborative professional learning that goes beyond one-off training events. She advocates for approaches that enable teachers to critically examine their practice, experiment with new pedagogies, and develop their understanding of curriculum and equity issues.

Penney has also examined the role of pre-service teacher education in preparing future PE teachers. She has been critical of programmes that emphasise technical skill development at the expense of critical thinking about curriculum, pedagogy, and social justice.

## Key Publications

### Books

**Penney, D. and Evans, J. (1999).** *Politics, Policy and Practice in Physical Education.* London: E & FN Spon.

This seminal text presents their analysis of the development and early implementation of the NCPE in England and Wales. Drawing on extensive policy document analysis and interviews with key stakeholders, the book reveals the political processes through which curriculum policy is constructed. It demonstrates how traditional sport interests dominated policy development and examines the implications for equity and educational quality. The book established the policy sociology approach that has influenced subsequent PE curriculum research internationally.

**Penney, D. and Chandler, T. (2000).** *Physical Education: What Future(s)?* London: Routledge.

This edited collection brings together international scholars to examine future directions for physical education. Chapters explore issues including curriculum integration, assessment, technology, and professional development. The book challenges readers to imagine alternatives to traditional PE and consider how the subject might evolve to better serve diverse learners.

**Penney, D., Clarke, G., Quill, M. and Kinchin, G. (Eds.) (2005).** *Sport Education in Physical Education: Research Based Practice.* London: Routledge.

This book presents comprehensive research on Sport Education implementation in various contexts. It examines both the potential and limitations of the model, including chapters on equity issues, teacher development, and curriculum integration. The book demonstrates Penney's commitment to research-informed curriculum innovation.

**Penney, D. and Jess, M. (2004).** *Physical Education: A Reader.* London: Routledge.

This edited collection brings together influential papers examining key debates in physical education, including curriculum, pedagogy, assessment, and professional development. The book serves as an important resource for students and researchers in PE.

### Journal Articles and Book Chapters

**Penney, D. (2002).** "Equality, equity and inclusion in physical education and school sport." In A. Laker (Ed.), *The Sociology of Sport and Physical Education* (pp. 110-128). London: Routledge.

This chapter provides a comprehensive analysis of equity issues in PE, distinguishing between equality (treating everyone the same) and equity (addressing different needs and circumstances). Penney examines how curriculum structures and pedagogical practices can either challenge or reinforce inequalities.

**Penney, D. (2008).** "Playing a political game and playing for position: Policy and curriculum development in health and physical education." *European Physical Education Review*, 14(1), 33-49.

This article analyses health and physical education curriculum development in Australia, examining how different interests compete to shape policy. It demonstrates how curriculum texts reflect compromise between competing visions and how implementation depends on local interpretation.

**Penney, D. and Chandler, T. (2000).** "Physical education: What future(s)?" *Sport, Education and Society*, 5(1), 71-87.

This article challenges PE professionals to critically examine the subject's purposes and consider alternative futures. It questions taken-for-granted assumptions about PE and advocates for innovation and equity.

**Evans, J., Penney, D. and Bryant, A. (1993).** "Improving the quality of physical education? The Education Reform Act, 1988, and physical education in England and Wales." *Quest*, 45(3), 321-338.

This early article examines the impact of the Education Reform Act on PE in England and Wales, providing critical analysis of the NCPE and associated reforms. It established key themes that Penney would continue to develop throughout her career.

**Penney, D. and Harris, J. (1997).** "Extra-curricular physical education: More of the same for the more able?" *Sport, Education and Society*, 2(1), 41-54.

This article examines who participates in extra-curricular PE and sport, revealing how these programmes often privilege already-advantaged students rather than extending opportunities to all. The research highlights equity concerns with school sport structures.

**Penney, D., Clarke, G. and Kinchin, G. (2002).** "Developing physical education as a 'connective specialism': Is sport education the answer?" *Sport, Education and Society*, 7(1), 55-64.

This article explores Sport Education as a potential approach for connecting PE with broader educational goals and other curriculum areas. It examines both opportunities and limitations of the model.

**Penney, D. and Waring, M. (2000).** "The absent agenda: Pedagogy and physical education." *Journal of Sport Pedagogy*, 6(1), 4-37.

This article critiques the lack of attention to pedagogy in PE curriculum policy documents, arguing that curriculum frameworks often focus on content while neglecting how teaching and learning should occur. The authors advocate for policy that explicitly addresses pedagogical approaches.

**Penney, D. (2006).** "Curriculum construction and change." In D. Kirk, D. Macdonald and M. O'Sullivan (Eds.), *The Handbook of Physical Education* (pp. 565-579). London: SAGE.

This chapter provides comprehensive analysis of curriculum development processes in PE, drawing on examples from multiple countries. It examines theoretical frameworks for understanding curriculum and offers insights for practitioners and policymakers.

**Penney, D. and Jess, M. (2004).** "Physical education and physically active lives: A lifelong approach to curriculum development." *Sport, Education and Society*, 9(2), 269-287.

This article advocates for PE curricula structured around a lifelong participation focus rather than short-term performance goals. It presents frameworks for curriculum development that prioritise sustained engagement in physical activity.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Dawn Penney's influence on physical education curriculum policy has been substantial and international in scope. Her research has directly informed curriculum development processes in England, Wales, Australia, New Zealand, and other countries.

### Impact on UK National Curriculum for Physical Education

Penney's critical analysis of the NCPE during its development and early implementation provided important insights for policymakers and practitioners. While her research highlighted significant concerns about the NCPE's structure and emphases, it also informed subsequent revisions of the curriculum.

Her work demonstrated how the NCPE's activity-based structure and emphasis on traditional games privileged certain forms of knowledge and activity while marginalising others. These insights contributed to debates about curriculum structure in later NCPE revisions, though traditional structures have proven resistant to fundamental change.

Her collaboration with health educators helped strengthen the health-related exercise component of the NCPE and influenced the development of health and wellbeing frameworks. Her advocacy for cross-curricular connections informed policy discussions about PE's relationship to broader educational goals.

Penney's research on assessment in PE influenced debates about how student achievement should be measured and reported. Her critiques of narrow performance-based assessment contributed to discussions about developing more holistic assessment approaches.

### Australian Curriculum Influence

In Australia, Penney has been directly involved in curriculum development processes, serving on advisory groups and contributing to curriculum frameworks. Her research on curriculum integration and cross-curricular connections influenced the development of the Australian Curriculum: Health and Physical Education, which integrates these traditionally separate subjects.

Her advocacy for outcomes-based approaches that recognise diverse pathways to success has influenced Australian curriculum structures. The Australian Curriculum's emphasis on inclusive practices and differentiation reflects principles Penney has long advocated.

Her work on lifelong participation as a curriculum organiser has influenced how Australian curricula conceptualise PE's purposes and structure learning progressions. The focus on developing movement competence and confidence for sustained participation reflects her research.

### International Impact

Beyond the UK and Australia, Penney's work has influenced curriculum thinking internationally. Her theoretical frameworks for analysing curriculum policy have been applied in numerous countries. Her research on Sport Education has influenced its adoption and adaptation in many nations.

Her emphasis on equity and social justice has influenced how curriculum developers think about inclusion and differentiation. Her work has contributed to international discussions about PE's role in addressing health, education, and social goals.

## Pedagogical Approach

Penney's pedagogical philosophy emphasises equity, inclusion, and meaningful learning experiences. While not primarily a pedagogical theorist, her work has important implications for teaching and learning in PE.

### Student-Centred and Differentiated Approaches

Penney advocates for pedagogies that recognise and respond to student diversity. She argues that effective PE teaching requires differentiation that goes beyond simply modifying task difficulty to address different interests, cultural backgrounds, learning preferences, and starting points.

Her research supports pedagogical approaches that provide student voice and choice, enabling learners to have genuine input into what and how they learn. She argues this increases engagement and helps ensure curricula serve diverse needs.

### Curriculum Models and Pedagogical Innovation

Penney has researched and promoted various curriculum and pedagogical models, including Sport Education, Teaching Games for Understanding, and cooperative learning approaches. She views these models as having potential to make PE more educationally meaningful and inclusive than traditional multi-activity programmes.

However, she maintains a critical perspective, examining how different models can be implemented in more or less equitable ways. She emphasises that no model is inherently progressive; implementation matters enormously for determining whose interests are served.

### Critical Pedagogy and Social Justice

Penney's work aligns with critical pedagogy perspectives that view education as potentially transformative but also potentially reproductive of social inequalities. She advocates for PE that helps students understand how physical activity, sport, health, and body are shaped by social, cultural, and economic factors.

She supports pedagogies that develop critical thinking about physical culture, not just physical skills. This includes examining how gender, race, class, and other social categories shape participation patterns and movement practices.
