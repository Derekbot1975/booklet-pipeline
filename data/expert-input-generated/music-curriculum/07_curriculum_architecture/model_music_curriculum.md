# The Model Music Curriculum

## Overview and Context

The Model Music Curriculum (MMC), published by the Department for Education in March 2021, represents a significant landmark in UK Music education. Developed by a panel of music education experts including Dame Evelyn Glennie, the MMC provides detailed guidance for teaching music from Early Years Foundation Stage through to Key Stage 3, supporting schools in delivering the statutory National Curriculum for Music (2013).

Unlike the National Curriculum itself, which sets out broad aims and attainment targets, the MMC is non-statutory guidance offering a detailed progression model with specific repertoire recommendations, technical vocabulary, and suggested activities. It was commissioned following concerns about variable quality in music provision across English schools, particularly at primary level.

## Curriculum Philosophy and Underpinning Principles

### The Three Pillars of Musical Learning

The MMC is structured around three interconnected musical dimensions that teachers should develop progressively:

**Performing**: developing technical and expressive skills in singing, playing instruments, and ensemble work
**Composing**: creating and developing musical ideas through improvisation and composition
**Listening and Appraising**: developing musical perception, critical engagement, and contextual understanding

This tripartite structure reflects Keith Swanwick's influential model of musical understanding, which emphasises the importance of integrating practical music-making with critical reflection. Swanwick's work on musical development suggests that authentic musical learning occurs when students engage with music as performers, composers, and informed listeners simultaneously.

### Musical Building Blocks

The MMC identifies five fundamental 'building blocks' that underpin all musical learning:

1. **Pulse and Metre**: understanding steady beat and time signatures
2. **Rhythm**: patterns of duration and accent
3. **Pitch**: highness and lowness of sound, melody and harmony
4. **Dynamics and Articulation**: volume and how notes are played
5. **Tempo and Performance Directions**: speed and expressive instructions

This systematic approach echoes Zoltán Kodály's philosophy that musical literacy should be built upon clear, sequential foundations. Kodály emphasised that children should develop inner hearing and musical understanding through carefully sequenced activities, beginning with simple elements and gradually increasing complexity.

## Key Stage Progression

### Early Years Foundation Stage

The MMC connects music education to the EYFS framework through the Expressive Arts and Design area of learning. It emphasises:

- Singing nursery rhymes and simple songs with increasing control
- Exploring vocal sounds and body percussion
- Moving rhythmically to music of varying styles
- Using simple percussion instruments expressively
- Beginning to recognise repeated patterns in music

The guidance acknowledges that early musical experiences should be playful, exploratory, and integrated with other areas of learning, consistent with research by Lucy Green on informal learning approaches.

### Key Stage 1 (Years 1-2)

The MMC provides detailed repertoire suggestions organised by musical style and cultural origin. Students should:

**Performing**:
- Sing songs in unison with clear diction and good posture
- Begin to sing simple partner songs
- Play tuned and untuned percussion with increasing control
- Perform from simple graphic and conventional notation

**Composing**:
- Create simple rhythmic and melodic patterns
- Explore timbre through different sound sources
- Begin to use symbols to represent sounds

**Listening and Appraising**:
- Recognise basic instruments by sound
- Identify simple musical elements (loud/quiet, fast/slow, high/low)
- Begin to use appropriate musical vocabulary

The repertoire includes traditional British folk songs, music from diverse cultures, and accessible classical pieces. This reflects an important shift towards cultural inclusivity, addressing criticisms by Gary Spruce and others about the historically Eurocentric bias in UK music education.

### Key Stage 2 (Years 3-6)

The MMC shows clear progression across KS2, with increasingly sophisticated expectations:

**Years 3-4**:
- Singing in two parts with increasing independence
- Reading staff notation within a limited range
- Playing classroom instruments with developing fluency
- Composing simple pieces with clear structures (AB, ABA forms)
- Understanding basic musical terminology (forte, piano, allegro, etc.)
- Recognising major and minor tonalities

**Years 5-6**:
- Performing in three or more parts
- Reading more complex rhythmic patterns including syncopation
- Playing melodic instruments with greater range and expression
- Composing with awareness of musical devices (ostinato, call and response, sequence)
- Understanding more sophisticated musical concepts (texture, harmony, timbre)
- Engaging critically with music from diverse periods and traditions

Martin Fautley's research on assessment in music education emphasises the importance of formative feedback during this progression. The MMC encourages teachers to develop students' metacognitive awareness by helping them articulate their musical decision-making and evaluate their own work.

### Key Stage 3 (Years 7-9)

At KS3, the MMC supports transition to more specialised musical learning whilst maintaining breadth:

**Performing**:
- Developing technical proficiency on instruments or voice
- Performing music from diverse genres with stylistic authenticity
- Reading and interpreting standard notation with increasing fluency
- Performing as part of ensembles with awareness of roles

**Composing**:
- Creating compositions for specific purposes and audiences
- Using music technology effectively
- Working with form, harmony, and sophisticated structures
- Developing personal compositional voice

**Listening and Appraising**:
- Analysing music using appropriate technical vocabulary
- Understanding musical contexts (historical, social, cultural)
- Comparing and contrasting musical styles
- Developing critical independence in musical judgement

The repertoire suggestions span Western classical music from medieval to contemporary, jazz, popular music, world music traditions, and music from the British Isles. This breadth prepares students for potential GCSE study whilst ensuring all students develop cultural literacy.

## Repertoire Recommendations

A distinctive feature of the MMC is its extensive repertoire lists, organised by:

- **Historical period**: Medieval, Renaissance, Baroque, Classical, Romantic, 20th and 21st century
- **Musical tradition**: Western classical, jazz, folk, popular music, world music
- **Thematic connections**: helping teachers plan coherent units of work

Examples include works by Purcell, Handel, Mozart, Elgar, Holst, as well as traditional songs from Caribbean, African, Asian, and other musical cultures. The inclusion of popular music and contemporary works reflects Lucy Green's argument that music curricula should value students' existing musical preferences and experiences.

However, some critics have questioned whether the repertoire list, whilst diverse, risks becoming prescriptive or suggesting a canon that might limit teacher autonomy and responsiveness to local contexts.

## Practical Implementation Considerations

### Primary Schools

The MMC acknowledges that many primary teachers lack specialist music training. It therefore includes:

- Warm-up activities and teaching sequences
- Links to existing whole-class instrumental teaching programmes
- Guidance on developing children's singing voices
- Suggestions for connecting music with other curriculum areas

Schools might utilise the MMC alongside established schemes such as Charanga or Music Express, which provide detailed lesson plans and resources. The MMC can serve as a progression framework against which commercial schemes are evaluated.

### Secondary Schools

At secondary level, the MMC supports departments in:

- Designing a coherent three-year KS3 curriculum
- Ensuring progression from primary music education
- Preparing students for GCSE study (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC)
- Balancing breadth with developing musical depth

Subject leaders should note that whilst the MMC provides valuable structure, it should be adapted to reflect departmental strengths, student interests, and available resources. Martin Fautley emphasises that effective curriculum planning involves teachers making informed professional judgements rather than mechanically following prescribed content.

### Sequencing and Progression

The MMC presents a logical sequence where:

- Musical concepts are introduced simply and revisited with increasing complexity
- Practical skills develop systematically through regular practice
- Listening repertoire expands students' musical horizons progressively
- Theoretical understanding emerges from practical musical experiences

This spiral curriculum approach aligns with Swanwick's C(L)A(S)P model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition, Skill acquisition, Performance), which suggests that musical learning should integrate different modes of engagement repeatedly across a student's education.

## Assessment and the MMC

Whilst the MMC does not provide assessment criteria, it supports teachers in identifying progression by:

- Describing increasingly sophisticated musical behaviours
- Providing vocabulary for discussing musical learning
- Suggesting observable indicators of musical understanding

Teachers should develop assessment approaches that recognise musical development as multifaceted. Fautley's work highlights that assessment should capture students' growth in musical fluency, expressivity, and metacognitive awareness, rather than focusing narrowly on technical accuracy or factual knowledge.

The MMC's structure can inform formative assessment practices where teachers:

- Observe students' musical decision-making during performing and composing
- Question students about their musical choices and preferences
- Facilitate peer feedback using appropriate musical vocabulary
- Help students set personal musical goals

## Connections to National Curriculum Statutory Requirements

The MMC directly supports delivery of the National Curriculum for Music (2013), which states that students should:

**At KS1 and KS2**:
- Use their voices expressively and creatively
- Play tuned and untuned instruments musically
- Listen with concentration and understanding
- Experiment with, create, select, and combine sounds
- (At KS2 additionally) appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music from different traditions and great composers and musicians
- Develop an understanding of the history of music

**At KS3**:
- Perform, listen to, review and evaluate music
- Be taught to sing and to use their voices, improvise and compose music
- Use staff and other relevant notations appropriately and accurately
- Develop an understanding of the history of music

The MMC translates these broad statutory requirements into actionable teaching content with specific examples and progression indicators.

## Links to GCSE Specifications

Whilst focused on KS1-3, the MMC provides crucial foundation for GCSE Music study. All major examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) require students to:

- Perform as a soloist and in ensemble contexts
- Compose original music
- Analyse and evaluate music from prescribed areas of study

Students who have followed the MMC's progression model will have developed:

- Instrumental/vocal fluency supporting GCSE performance requirements
- Compositional skills and understanding of musical structures
- Broad listening experience spanning diverse musical traditions
- Technical vocabulary for musical analysis
- Music literacy enabling engagement with set works

The MMC's emphasis on diverse musical traditions is particularly relevant to GCSE specifications, which typically include Areas of Study spanning Western classical tradition, popular music, world music, and contemporary art music.

## Critique and Ongoing Debate

### Strengths Identified by Practitioners

- Provides clarity and structure, particularly valuable for non-specialist primary teachers
- Offers concrete repertoire suggestions addressing teachers' uncertainty about content
- Emphasises progression and cumulative musical development
- Promotes cultural diversity in musical learning

### Concerns Raised by Music Educators

**Prescriptiveness**: Some educators worry the MMC might be interpreted as mandatory rather than guidance, potentially constraining teacher creativity and responsiveness to context. Gary Spruce has argued that overly prescriptive curricula can undermine teachers' professional autonomy and their ability to make music education relevant to diverse student communities.

**Cultural assumptions**: Despite efforts toward inclusivity, questions remain about whose musical knowledge is validated. The predominance of Western classical music in the repertoire lists may perpetuate cultural hierarchies.

**Assessment implications**: The detailed progression model might encourage teachers to assess students against predetermined descriptors rather than recognising diverse pathways of musical development. Lucy Green's research on informal learning suggests that musical development doesn't always follow linear, predictable patterns.

**Resource requirements**: Full implementation assumes access to instruments, repertoire recordings, and teaching expertise that may not be available in all schools, potentially exacerbating existing inequalities.

### Academic Perspectives

Keith Swanwick's work suggests that effective music curricula should balance structure with creative freedom. The MMC's detailed progression framework provides valuable structure, but teachers must ensure this doesn't overshadow opportunities for students to pursue personal musical interests and informal learning pathways.

Fautley's research on curriculum design emphasises that the written curriculum (what is documented) differs from the enacted curriculum (what actually happens in classrooms) and the experienced curriculum (what students actually learn). The MMC should therefore be understood as a starting point for professional dialogue rather than a script to be followed mechanistically.

## Implementation Support and Resources

Schools implementing the MMC should consider:

- **CPD provision**: Particularly for primary generalists requiring support with musical skills and pedagogical approaches
- **Resource audit**: Identifying instruments, technology, and repertoire needed
- **Partnership working**: Engaging with Music Education Hubs, which provide instruments, ensemble opportunities, and specialist teaching support
- **Curriculum mapping**: Ensuring coherent progression from EYFS through KS3
- **Differentiation strategies**: Adapting the model to meet diverse student needs and local contexts

The Department for Education has supported MMC implementation through additional funding and the Music Education Hub network, though availability and quality of support varies regionally.

## Conclusion

The Model Music Curriculum represents an important attempt to strengthen music education in English schools by providing detailed, progressive guidance from Early Years through KS3. Drawing on established pedagogical principles from Kodály, Swanwick, and others, whilst incorporating contemporary concerns about cultural diversity and inclusion, it offers valuable support particularly for non-specialist teachers.

However, as with any curriculum model, its effectiveness depends on thoughtful implementation by informed practitioners who understand their students' needs and contexts. Music educators should engage critically with the MMC, using it as a framework for professional dialogue whilst exercising their judgement about how best to foster genuine musical learning. The curriculum should serve teachers and students, not the reverse.

As Lucy Green has consistently argued, the most powerful music education emerges when teachers honour students' existing musical identities whilst expanding their horizons through carefully structured new experiences. The MMC provides one possible roadmap for that journey, but the actual route must be navigated by teachers responding thoughtfully to the unique communities they serve.
