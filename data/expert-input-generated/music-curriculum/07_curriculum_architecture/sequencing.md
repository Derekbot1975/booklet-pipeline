# Sequencing Music Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in Music

Sequencing in music education refers to the deliberate and strategic ordering of musical learning experiences across Key Stages to ensure progression, coherence and cumulative knowledge building. As Martin Fautley and colleagues argue in *Music Education in the 21st Century in the United Kingdom* (2017), effective sequencing must balance systematic skill development with authentic musical engagement, avoiding what Keith Swanwick termed the 'C-L-A-S-P trap' – where composition, listening, appraising, skill acquisition and performing become disconnected activities rather than integrated musical experiences.

The National Curriculum for Music (2013) provides a framework requiring pupils to perform, compose, listen, and develop musical understanding, but deliberately leaves sequencing decisions to schools. This professional autonomy demands sophisticated curriculum architecture that draws upon research-informed principles whilst responding to local contexts.

## Theoretical Foundations for Sequencing

### Swanwick's Developmental Spiral

Keith Swanwick's developmental model (1994), based on extensive research documented in *Musical Knowledge: Intuition, Analysis and Music Education*, identifies eight developmental modes organised into four layers: Materials, Expression, Form, and Value. This spiral suggests that musical development is neither strictly linear nor age-bound, but rather involves recursive engagement with increasingly sophisticated manifestations of these layers.

**Implications for sequencing:**
- Revisiting musical concepts at increasing levels of sophistication across key stages
- Recognising that younger pupils can engage with 'advanced' concepts intuitively before formal understanding
- Planning for spiral progression where materials (sounds), expression (character), form (structure) and value (judgement) are continually revisited

### Kodály's Sequential Principles

Zoltán Kodály's pedagogical approach, extensively adapted for UK contexts by organisations including the British Kodály Academy, emphasises systematic sequencing based on developmental appropriateness. The Kodály progression moves from known to unknown, simple to complex, and follows linguistic developmental patterns (sound before symbol).

**Sequencing principles:**
- Progressing from pentatonic to diatonic to chromatic tonalities
- Introducing rhythmic values systematically (ta, ti-ti, ta-a)
- Ensuring aural experience precedes theoretical understanding
- Building from folk song repertoire outward to diverse musical styles

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Model

Lucy Green's research, particularly *How Popular Musicians Learn* (2002) and subsequent *Music, Informal Learning and the School* (2008), challenges purely linear sequencing models. Her Musical Futures pedagogy demonstrates that purposeful, self-directed learning can accelerate progression when pupils choose repertoire and learn through copying and improvisation.

**Sequencing considerations:**
- Balancing systematic instruction with exploratory learning episodes
- Planning for non-linear pathways where pupil choice drives progression
- Integrating informal learning approaches across Key Stages 3-5, not only at KS3

## Key Stage 1 Sequencing (Ages 5-7)

### National Curriculum Requirements

The KS1 programme of study requires pupils to use voices expressively, play tuned and untuned instruments, listen with concentration, and experiment with sounds. Sequencing at this stage must prioritise:

**Foundation skills:**
- Pulse and rhythm discrimination (steady beat before rhythmic patterns)
- Pitch awareness (high/low, up/down before specific pitches)
- Dynamic and tempo variation (loud/quiet, fast/slow)
- Timbre exploration (contrasting sound qualities)

**Pedagogical sequencing:**
As Fautley emphasises in *Assessment in Music Education* (2010), KS1 sequencing should progress from experiential learning to emerging conscious control. Begin with movement responses to music, progress to vocal play and sound exploration, then structured singing and instrument playing.

**Typical progression within KS1:**
1. **Year R-1 Autumn:** Pulse, beat, rhythm of words, high/low pitch games
2. **Year 1 Spring:** Simple ostinato patterns, call-and-response singing, untuned percussion exploration
3. **Year 1 Summer:** Melodic shape awareness, simple tuned percussion, graphic notation introduction
4. **Year 2 Autumn:** More complex rhythms (including rests), pitch matching, simple songs with verses
5. **Year 2 Spring:** Two-note melodies (so-mi), simple compositions, responding to musical features
6. **Year 2 Summer:** Extended pitch range (la-so-mi-re-do), conventional notation introduction, simple accompaniments

## Key Stage 2 Sequencing (Ages 7-11)

### National Curriculum Requirements

KS2 demands pupils sing and play musically with increasing control, improvise and compose, listen with attention to detail, and develop understanding of musical history. Gary Spruce, in *Debates in Music Teaching* (2016), argues that KS2 sequencing must avoid the 'activities trap' where lessons become entertaining but disconnected experiences lacking cumulative progression.

### Systematic Musical Literacy Development

**Notation progression:**
- Year 3: Consolidate graphic notation, introduce staff notation basics (treble clef, crotchets, minims)
- Year 4: Extend to quavers, note names (C-G), simple time signatures (2/4, 3/4, 4/4)
- Year 5: Semibreves, dotted rhythms, accidentals, key signatures (up to 2 sharps/flats)
- Year 6: Semiquavers, ties, dynamic and tempo markings, reading simple vocal scores

**Performance progression:**
Swanwick's research suggests that technical mastery and expressive performance must develop simultaneously. Sequence instrumental/vocal skills to enable progressively sophisticated repertoire:
- Years 3-4: Unison singing with developing pitch accuracy, simple instrumental parts (bordun, ostinato)
- Years 5-6: Part-singing (rounds, simple harmonies), melodic instrumental lines, ensemble coordination

### Composition and Improvisation Sequencing

Fautley's research in *Developing Creativity in the Curriculum* (2007) demonstrates that composition skills develop through structured scaffolding followed by increasing independence:

**Progression model:**
1. **Controlled composition:** Teacher-defined parameters (use these three notes, create a 4-beat pattern)
2. **Guided composition:** Some parameters fixed, others chosen (create an ABA structure using contrasting dynamics)
3. **Scaffolded composition:** Brief provided, decisions guided through questioning
4. **Independent composition:** Open brief with success criteria
5. **Extended composition:** Personal interpretation of stimulus with self-evaluation

### Listening and Appraising Sequencing

Progress from musical elements identification to contextual understanding:
- Years 3-4: Identify and describe musical elements (pitch, duration, dynamics, tempo, timbre, texture)
- Years 5-6: Understand how elements combine for effect, recognise instruments and ensembles, place music in historical and cultural contexts

## Key Stage 3 Sequencing (Ages 11-14)

### National Curriculum Requirements

KS3 requires pupils to perform with technical control and expression, compose with increasing sophistication, and develop critical engagement with diverse musical traditions. The challenge, as Spruce articulates in *Music Education in the 21st Century*, is sequencing content that builds systematically whilst maintaining musical authenticity and relevance.

### Common Sequencing Challenges at Transition

Research by Fautley and Daubney (*National Plan for Music Education: A Single Year Study*, 2019) identifies significant variation in KS2 provision affecting KS3 baseline assumptions. Effective KS3 sequencing must:

- **Audit and baseline:** Assess prior learning without assuming uniformity
- **Bridge gaps:** Provide catch-up opportunities without patronising pupils with strong foundations
- **Build progressively:** Ensure Year 9 content genuinely advances beyond Year 7

### Year-by-Year KS3 Sequencing Framework

**Year 7: Consolidation and Exploration**
- **Autumn 1:** Baseline assessment through practical music-making; consolidate notation reading/writing
- **Autumn 2:** Rhythm and metre deepening (compound time, syncopation, polyrhythms)
- **Spring 1:** Melody and harmony foundations (scales, triads, chord progressions)
- **Spring 2:** Texture and timbre (ensemble arrangements, technology-based composition)
- **Summer 1:** Structure and form (binary, ternary, rondo, verse-chorus)
- **Summer 2:** Cultural and historical contexts (introducing diverse traditions)

**Year 8: Deepening and Application**
- Build upon Year 7 foundations with increasing technical demand
- Introduce genre-specific conventions (blues progression, riff composition, minimalist techniques)
- Develop critical listening using specialist vocabulary
- Extended composition projects (multi-track recording, ensemble pieces)
- Historical depth studies (e.g., development of popular music, classical traditions)

**Year 9: Sophistication and Specialisation**
- Pre-GCSE preparation for continuing pupils
- Advanced harmonic concepts (seventh chords, modulation, chromaticism)
- Genre mastery projects (students develop expertise in chosen styles)
- Technology integration (DAW proficiency, MIDI programming, mixing)
- Analytical essays and extended listening responses
- Performance portfolios demonstrating technical and interpretative development

### Genre and Style Sequencing

Lucy Green's research challenges the classical-first sequencing model. Effective KS3 progression should:

- **Introduce diverse genres from Year 7:** Popular, world, classical, jazz, electronic
- **Increase stylistic sophistication:** From recognition and imitation to creative fusion and innovation
- **Develop cultural contextualization:** From descriptive accounts to critical analysis of music's social meanings
- **Enable specialisation:** By Year 9, students pursue depth in preferred styles whilst maintaining breadth

## Key Stage 4 Sequencing (Ages 14-16)

### GCSE Specifications and Sequencing

All major examination boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure GCSE Music around performing, composing and appraising. Effective sequencing must balance content coverage with skill refinement.

**AQA GCSE Music (8271):**
- Areas of Study: Western Classical Tradition, Popular Music, Traditional Music, Western Classical Tradition since 1910
- Sequencing strategy: Introduce all four AoS in Year 10, revisit with increasing depth in Year 11

**Edexcel GCSE Music (1MU0):**
- Areas of Study: Instrumental Music, Vocal Music, Music for Stage and Screen, Fusions
- Sequencing strategy: Pair AoS thematically (e.g., Instrumental with Vocal; Stage/Screen with Fusions)

**OCR GCSE Music (J536):**
- Areas of Study: My Music, Concerto Through Time, Rhythms of the World, Film Music, Conventions of Pop
- Sequencing strategy: Begin with 'My Music' to validate prior experience, progress chronologically through historical topics

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Music (C660):**
- Musical Forms and Devices, Music for Ensemble, Film Music, Popular Music
- Sequencing strategy: Frontload 'Forms and Devices' as analytical foundation for subsequent topics

### Integrated Sequencing Model

Fautley's research in *GCSE Music Teaching and Learning* (2016) recommends integrated rather than compartmentalised sequencing:

**Half-termly integration example:**
- **Weeks 1-2:** Listening focus (Area of Study content delivery, exemplar analysis)
- **Weeks 3-4:** Composition application (creating in studied style/form)
- **Weeks 5-6:** Performance connections (repertoire from Area of Study)
- **Week 7:** Consolidation and assessment

This model ensures theoretical understanding emerges from practical engagement rather than existing as abstract knowledge.

### Composition Sequencing for GCSE

Progress from scaffolded exercises to independent portfolios:

**Year 10:**
- Autumn: Pastiche compositions (imitating set work characteristics)
- Spring: Technique-focused briefs (developing use of specific musical devices)
- Summer: First portfolio composition with teacher guidance

**Year 11:**
- Autumn: Second portfolio composition with increasing independence
- Spring: Refinement and submission preparation
- Summer: Final performance recording and examination preparation

## Key Stage 5 Sequencing (Ages 16-18)

### A Level Specifications

A Level Music demands sophisticated integration of practical and analytical skills. Sequencing must facilitate deep musical thinking rather than surface coverage.

**AQA A Level Music (7272):**
- Study of six Areas of Study across Western Classical and Popular/Jazz traditions
- Sequencing consideration: Chronological vs thematic organisation
- Extended essay component requires progressive development of musicological skills

**Edexcel A Level Music (9MU0):**
- Six Areas of Study with prescribed works
- Performing and composing briefs with specific technical requirements
- Sequencing strategy: Introduce all AoS in Year 12 with foundational analysis, deepen in Year 13

**OCR A Level Music (H543):**
- Historical study (Baroque to Modernism) plus two optional Areas of Study
- Sequencing strategy: Complete historical chronology before option specialisation

**WJEC Eduqas A Level Music (A670):**
- Four Areas of Study including Western Classical Tradition and Musical Theatre
- Integrated appraising tasks throughout course

### Vertical Sequencing: Y12 to Y13

**Year 12 focus:**
- Foundational content coverage (introduce all Areas of Study)
- Analytical technique development (formal analysis, harmonic analysis, contextual understanding)
- Performance technique refinement (minimum difficulty Level 6/Grade 6)
- Composition skills building (pastiche before originality)

**Year 13 focus:**
- Depth over breadth (detailed study of prescribed works)
- Independent analysis and extended writing
- Advanced performance preparation (difficulty Level 7-8/Grade 7-8)
- Original composition portfolios demonstrating stylistic command
- Synthesis across components (performing, composing and appraising interconnections)

### Sequencing Musical Analysis Skills

Progress from description to critical evaluation:

1. **Identification:** Recognising musical elements, devices and structures
2. **Description:** Articulating musical features using specialist vocabulary
3. **Contextualisation:** Placing music within historical, cultural and generic frameworks
4. **Interpretation:** Explaining compositional choices and their musical effects
5. **Evaluation:** Critical judgement supported by evidence and musical understanding
6. **Synthesis:** Connecting analytical insights across works, genres and traditions

## Cross-Phase Sequencing Principles

### The Progression Problematic

Fautley's research (*Progression in Music Education*, 2019) highlights persistent challenges in defining and assessing musical progression. Effective sequencing addresses this through:

**Multiple progression pathways:**
- **Technical progression:** Increasing instrumental/vocal facility
- **Conceptual progression:** Deepening theoretical understanding
- **Creative progression:** Compositional sophistication
- **Critical progression:** Analytical and evaluative refinement
- **Cultural progression:** Broadening stylistic knowledge and appreciation

**Non-linear recognition:**
Swanwick's spiral model reminds us that progression isn't purely accumulative. Pupils may demonstrate advanced understanding in one domain whilst requiring foundational work in another.

### Threshold Concepts in Musical Learning

Drawing on Meyer and Land's threshold concepts theory, certain musical understandings represent 'portals' that transform subsequent learning:

- **Metre vs rhythm:** Understanding independence of pulse and rhythmic pattern
- **Tonality:** Grasping pitch organisation systems (major/minor/modal/chromatic)
- **Functional harmony:** Recognising chord relationships and progressions
- **Texture:** Understanding independent musical layers
- **Genre as convention:** Recognising music as culturally constructed rather than universal

Sequencing should position these thresholds appropriately, providing time for troublesome knowledge to resolve.

### The Role of Practical Music-Making

Green's research demonstrates that practical engagement accelerates conceptual understanding. Effective sequencing ensures:

- **Concepts emerge from practice:** Theory follows practical experience
- **Regular performance opportunities:** Not reserved for 'end products'
- **Diverse practical modes:** Singing, playing, improvising, composing, arranging
- **Ensemble integration:** Chamber music, bands, choirs, orchestras, technology ensembles

## Sequencing for Inclusion and Differentiation

### Mixed-Prior-Attainment Contexts

Research by Spruce (*Inclusion and Diversity in Music Education*, 2015) demonstrates that music classrooms often contain the widest attainment range in schools. Sequencing must accommodate:

- **Flexible entry points:** Tasks with variable challenge levels
- **Multiple success criteria:** Excellence defined across different musical dimensions
- **Enrichment and support:** Simultaneous provision without fixed groupings
- **Cultural validation:** Recognising diverse musical heritages as assets
