# Progression in Musical Skills

## Introduction

Progression in musical skills represents one of the most complex aspects of music education, as musical development is rarely linear and operates across multiple domains simultaneously. As Martin Fautley (2000) argues in his seminal work on assessment in music education, musical progression involves the "increasing sophistication of musical behaviours" rather than simple accumulation of knowledge. The challenge for UK music educators lies in recognising and nurturing progression across performing, composing, listening, and appraising—the four key domains identified in the National Curriculum for Music (2013).

Keith Swanwick's developmental spiral model (1994) remains foundational to understanding musical progression in UK contexts. His research demonstrates that musical development moves through materials, expression, form, and value stages, but crucially, this development is spiral rather than linear—students revisit earlier modes of engagement at increasingly sophisticated levels throughout their education.

## National Curriculum Framework for Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum for Music (2013) establishes that pupils should be taught to:
- Use their voices expressively and creatively by singing songs and speaking chants and rhymes
- Play tuned and untuned instruments musically
- Listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music
- Experiment with, create, select and combine sounds using the inter-related dimensions of music

At this stage, progression manifests through increasing control of musical elements. Students move from exploring sounds randomly to purposeful selection and combination. Zoltán Kodály's principles, widely adopted in UK primary schools, emphasise that musical literacy should develop through singing and movement before instrumental work, establishing secure foundations for later progression.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to:
- Play and perform in solo and ensemble contexts, using their voices and playing musical instruments with increasing accuracy, fluency, control and expression
- Improvise and compose music for a range of purposes using the inter-related dimensions of music
- Listen with attention to detail and recall sounds with increasing aural memory
- Use and understand staff and other musical notations
- Appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians
- Develop an understanding of the history of music

Progression at KS2 involves increasing technical competence, expanding musical vocabulary, and developing understanding of musical structures. Students should demonstrate growing independence in musical decision-making and ability to articulate musical intentions. The introduction of formal notation represents significant cognitive progression, though as Lucy Green (2008) emphasises in 'Music, Informal Learning and the School', informal approaches to music-making remain equally valid progression routes.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum maintains the same programme of study as KS2 but expects greater depth, sophistication, and independence. Gary Spruce (2015) notes that progression at KS3 should involve students developing "critical autonomy"—the ability to make informed musical judgements independent of teacher direction.

Progression indicators at KS3 include:
- Technical facility on instruments or voice showing secure tone production and articulation
- Compositional work demonstrating structural coherence and purposeful manipulation of musical elements
- Analytical listening that identifies style markers, compositional techniques, and cultural contexts
- Ensemble skills including appropriate balance, awareness of others, and stylistic authenticity
- Growing ability to evaluate and refine their own work using appropriate musical vocabulary

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At GCSE level, progression becomes more explicitly defined through exam board specifications. All major boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) structure progression through three components: performing, composing, and appraising, each weighted at approximately 30%, 30%, and 40% respectively.

**AQA Music GCSE** (8271) specifies progression through grade descriptors that move from "basic" to "sophisticated" manipulation of musical elements. Performance progression is measured against grade expectations (typically Grade 3-5 standards by end of course), whilst compositional progression involves increasing structural sophistication and stylistic control.

**Edexcel Music GCSE** (1MU0) emphasises progression in "musical fluency"—the ability to deploy musical skills flexibly and appropriately. Their specifications note that progression involves students moving from "reproducing" musical ideas to "generating and developing" original musical material.

**OCR Music GCSE** (J536) structures progression through its "Integrated Portfolio" approach, where students demonstrate progression by combining performance, composition, and critical reflection in authentic musical contexts.

**WJEC/Eduqas Music GCSE** maintains similar expectations but emphasises progression in cultural understanding and the ability to connect musical learning across different traditions and styles.

## The Inter-Related Dimensions of Music: Tracking Progression

The National Curriculum identifies seven inter-related dimensions: pitch, duration, dynamics, tempo, timbre, texture, and structure. Martin Fautley and Regina Murphy (2015) argue in 'Assessment in Music Education' that progression should be observable across each dimension:

### Pitch
- **Early progression**: Matching pitches accurately, distinguishing high/low
- **Intermediate progression**: Using wider pitch ranges, understanding scalar patterns, controlling intonation
- **Advanced progression**: Manipulating pitch for expressive effect, understanding harmonic relationships, precise tuning in context

### Duration (Rhythm)
- **Early progression**: Maintaining steady pulse, copying simple rhythmic patterns
- **Intermediate progression**: Reading and performing syncopated rhythms, understanding note values
- **Advanced progression**: Complex polyrhythmic patterns, rubato, advanced metrical concepts

### Dynamics
- **Early progression**: Distinguishing loud/quiet, sudden dynamic changes
- **Intermediate progression**: Gradual dynamic change (crescendo/diminuendo), appropriate dynamic range for style
- **Advanced progression**: Sophisticated dynamic shaping, micro-dynamics for expression, dynamic balance in ensemble

### Tempo
- **Early progression**: Fast/slow distinction, maintaining consistent tempo
- **Intermediate progression**: Tempo changes, understanding tempo markings, accelerando/ritardando
- **Advanced progression**: Subtle tempo flexibility, stylistically appropriate rubato, ensemble tempo negotiation

### Timbre
- **Early progression**: Distinguishing instrumental families, vocal/instrumental differentiation
- **Intermediate progression**: Recognising specific instruments, understanding tone colour in orchestration
- **Advanced progression**: Manipulating tone colour expressively, understanding timbral fusion and blend

### Texture
- **Early progression**: Recognising layers of sound, thick/thin texture
- **Intermediate progression**: Identifying melodic/accompaniment relationships, understanding monophonic/homophonic/polyphonic textures
- **Advanced progression**: Creating sophisticated textural contrasts, understanding contrapuntal techniques

### Structure
- **Early progression**: Recognising same/different sections, verse/chorus patterns
- **Intermediate progression**: Understanding standard forms (binary, ternary, rondo, strophic)
- **Advanced progression**: Analysing complex forms, creating original structural designs, understanding developmental processes

## Swanwick and Tillman's Developmental Model

Keith Swanwick and June Tillman's (1986) developmental sequence remains influential in UK music education. Their model identifies eight developmental layers organised into four modes:

### Materials (0-9 years typically)
**Sensory Mode**: Exploration of sound properties, interest in tone colour and dynamic extremes
**Manipulative Mode**: Control development, technical mastery focus, pleasure in skill acquisition

### Expression (4-14 years typically)
**Personal Expression**: Music-making as emotional outlet, imitative of expressive gestures
**Vernacular**: Adoption of conventional musical styles, pleasure in stylistic authenticity

### Form (10-15 years typically)
**Speculative**: Experimentation with structural possibilities, surprise and novelty
**Idiomatic**: Understanding and manipulation of established structural conventions

### Value (15+ years typically)
**Symbolic**: Personal meaning-making, understanding music's role in identity and society
**Systematic**: Critical reflection, valuing musical heritage, commitment to particular musical values

Crucially, Swanwick emphasises that these modes spiral—older students may work in materials mode when learning new instruments, whilst young children occasionally produce work showing formal or even symbolic awareness. Progression involves increasing time spent in higher modes, not abandonment of earlier ones.

## Lucy Green's Informal Learning Pathways

Lucy Green's research (2002, 2008) challenges traditional progression models by demonstrating that popular musicians develop expertise through different pathways than classical musicians. Her work, implemented through Musical Futures in many UK schools, identifies informal learning progression:

1. **Copying recordings by ear** (progressing from fragments to complete pieces)
2. **Independent peer-directed learning** (progressing from reliance on teacher to autonomous group work)
3. **Personal choice of material** (progressing from simple, familiar music to more challenging repertoire)
4. **Improvisation within known frameworks** (progressing from tentative additions to confident elaboration)
5. **Integration of composition, performance, and listening** (progressing from separated activities to unified music-making)

Green's model demonstrates that progression in popular music contexts may prioritise aural skills, collaborative creativity, and stylistic authenticity over notation and formal analysis, challenging educators to recognise multiple valid progression pathways.

## Kodály Principles and Sequential Learning

Zoltán Kodály's influence on UK primary music education establishes clear sequential progression through:

- **Preparation**: Experiencing musical concepts aurally before labelling
- **Presentation**: Conscious identification and labelling of musical concepts
- **Practice**: Reinforcement through varied contexts

This sequence applies to each musical element. For example, syncopation progression:
1. **Experience**: Singing songs with syncopation unconsciously
2. **Identify**: Teacher draws attention to the "off-beat" feeling
3. **Label**: Introduction of terminology and rhythmic notation
4. **Create**: Students compose patterns using syncopation
5. **Transfer**: Students recognise and perform syncopation in new contexts

The Kodály approach emphasises that progression must be sequential, moving from sound to symbol, and that each step must be secure before advancing.

## Performing Skills Progression

### Vocal Progression
**Foundation Stage/KS1**:
- Developing singing voice distinct from speaking voice
- Matching pitch within limited range (typically D-A)
- Singing simple songs with repetitive melodic patterns
- Beginning to sing with others, maintaining independent part in unison

**KS2**:
- Extending vocal range (typically C-C')
- Maintaining independent part in rounds and simple two-part songs
- Developing breath control for longer phrases
- Singing with clear diction and appropriate tone quality
- Beginning dynamic and expressive control

**KS3**:
- Secure pitch control across full vocal range
- Part-singing in three or more parts
- Stylistic authenticity across genres (classical, jazz, popular, world music)
- Advanced breath control, phrasing, and dynamics
- Performance confidence and stage presence

**KS4/GCSE**:
- Performance standard typically Grade 4-5 ABRSM/Trinity equivalent
- Secure technical control allowing expressive interpretation
- Stylistic versatility demonstrated across contrasting pieces
- Professional presentation skills
- Ability to rehearse independently and self-evaluate

### Instrumental Progression

Progression on instruments follows similar trajectories but varies by instrument family. Martin Fautley (2010) notes that instrumental progression assessment must account for the vastly different technical demands of different instruments—comparing a Grade 3 pianist with a Grade 3 timpanist requires contextual understanding.

**Technical Control**:
- Posture and instrument hold
- Tone production
- Articulation
- Facility (speed and accuracy)
- Dynamic range
- Extended techniques (where appropriate)

**Musical Understanding**:
- Appropriate phrasing
- Stylistic awareness
- Ensemble awareness
- Interpretive choices
- Memorisation capacity

## Composing Skills Progression

Compositional progression presents particular assessment challenges, as Gary Spruce (2012) discusses. Unlike performance, where grade systems provide external benchmarks, composition assessment remains largely criterion-referenced.

### Early Years/KS1
- Exploration and discovery: creating soundscapes
- Question-and-answer structures
- Graphic notation to record ideas
- Simple ostinati and repetitive patterns
- Musical "stories" with beginning, middle, end

### KS2
- Extended compositions with clear structural organisation
- Development of musical motifs
- Use of staff notation where appropriate
- Compositions for specified purposes (e.g., film music, advertising)
- Beginning to evaluate and refine work
- Understanding of compositional techniques (repetition, variation, contrast)

### KS3
- Sophisticated structural designs
- Purposeful harmonic choices
- Effective instrumentation/orchestration
- Style-specific composition (e.g., Baroque pastiche, blues composition)
- Integration of technology where appropriate
- Critical evaluation and systematic refinement

### KS4/GCSE
All exam boards require two compositions (or one extended composition with multiple movements/sections):
- **Technical Control**: Accurate notation (staff or technology), idiomatic writing for instruments
- **Musical Coherence**: Effective structure, thematic development, harmonic direction
- **Stylistic Understanding**: Appropriate use of genre conventions
- **Originality**: Personal creative input beyond reproduction of models
- **Complexity**: Appropriate to student's technical level, typically 3-5 minutes total composition time

## Listening and Appraising Skills Progression

The National Curriculum emphasises that listening skills develop progressively through active, focused engagement with music. Janet Mills (2005) argues that listening progression involves both increasing sophistication of perception and expanding critical vocabulary.

### KS1
- Recognising basic elements: fast/slow, high/low, loud/quiet
- Distinguishing vocal/instrumental sounds
- Identifying simple repetition and contrast
- Beginning to use musical vocabulary
- Expressing personal responses to music

### KS2
- Detailed identification of instruments and families
- Recognition of standard musical forms
- Understanding of historical and cultural contexts
- Using expanded musical vocabulary accurately
- Comparing and contrasting musical examples
- Beginning critical evaluation using musical evidence

### KS3
- Detailed analytical listening identifying compositional techniques
- Understanding stylistic conventions across diverse traditions
- Recognising harmonic features (major/minor, simple progressions)
- Making connections between musical features and expressive effects
- Extended written analysis using appropriate terminology
- Critical evaluation of music considering historical, social, and cultural contexts

### KS4/GCSE
The appraising component (approximately 40% of GCSE) assesses progression through:
- **Aural Perception**: Identifying instruments, tonality, time signatures, compositional devices
- **Musical Context**: Understanding of set works and their historical/cultural backgrounds
- **Critical Analysis**: Extended writing analysing unfamiliar music using appropriate terminology
- **Comparative Analysis**: Drawing connections between different musical styles and traditions

**AQA** requires study of six Areas of Study covering Western Classical, Popular Music, Traditional Music, and Film Music.
**Edexcel** structures listening around four Areas of Study with detailed set works.
**OCR** uses five Areas of Study integrating practical music-making with analytical work.
**WJEC/Eduqas** requires study of four Areas of Study with specific set works for detailed analysis.

## Technology and Progression

The integration of music technology creates additional progression pathways. Ofsted's research review of music education (2021) emphasises that technology should enhance rather than replace traditional skills.

### Sequencing and Production
**Foundation**: Recording live sounds, using pre-made loops, basic editing
**Intermediate**: MIDI sequencing, multitrack recording, mixing basics, effects processing
**Advanced**: Mastering, synthesis, complex automation, production techniques across genres

### Notation Software
**Foundation**: Inputting simple melodies, understanding basic notation symbols
**Intermediate**: Creating full scores with multiple parts, using articulations and dynamics
**Advanced**: Advanced notation including contemporary techniques, professional score presentation

## Assessment of Progression

Martin Fautley and Regina Murphy (2015) identify three key principles for assessing musical progression:

### 1. Ipsative Assessment
Comparing students against their own previous work rather than external standards. Particularly appropriate in creative subjects where development may be highly individual.

### 2. Criterion-Referenced Assessment
Judging work against defined criteria. GCSE specifications provide detailed grade descriptors enabling consistent assessment across different musical contexts.

### 3. Norm-Referenced Assessment
Comparing student achievement against age-appropriate expectations. Grade examinations (ABRSM, Trinity, Rock School) provide external benchmarks, though these represent only one progression pathway.

### Practical Implications for Assessment

**Formative Assessment Strategies**:
- Regular performance opportunities with specific technical/musical foci
- Composition portfolios showing development over time
- Listening journals documenting expanding repertoire knowledge
- Peer and self-assessment using appropriate criteria
- Teacher feedback focusing on specific next steps

**Summative Assessment**:
- End-of-unit performances assessed against technical and musical criteria
- Finished compositions with written evaluations
- Listening tests assessing aural perception and analytical understanding
- Written work demonstrating contextual knowledge
