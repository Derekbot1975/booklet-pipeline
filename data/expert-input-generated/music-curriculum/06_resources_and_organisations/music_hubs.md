# Music Hubs and Music Education Partnerships

## Introduction and Policy Context

Music Education Hubs (MEHs) represent the cornerstone of England's music education infrastructure, established in 2012 following the publication of the National Plan for Music Education (NPME). These partnerships bring together Local Authorities, Arts Council England, schools, music services, and professional organisations to ensure that children and young people have access to high-quality music education. The 2022 refresh of the NPME reinforced the central role of Music Hubs in delivering the three key pillars: curriculum music, co-curricular provision, and progression routes.

Martin Fautley and colleagues have extensively researched the impact of Music Hubs, noting that whilst they have provided essential infrastructure, their effectiveness varies significantly depending on local leadership, funding, and partnership coherence. The Model Music Curriculum (2021), whilst non-statutory, provides a framework that Hubs increasingly reference when supporting schools in meeting National Curriculum requirements at Key Stages 1-3.

## The Music Hub Core and Extension Roles

### Core Roles (as defined in NPME 2022)

Music Hubs are required to deliver four core roles:

**1. Curriculum Music**: Support schools to deliver high-quality curriculum music teaching aligned with the National Curriculum programmes of study. This includes providing or brokering CPD for generalist primary teachers and specialist secondary educators. Research by Gary Spruce emphasises that effective curriculum support must address the confidence crisis among primary practitioners, with only 17% feeling adequately prepared to teach music (ISM/University of Sussex, 2019).

**2. Wider Opportunities and Enrichment**: Ensure children can access instrumental and vocal tuition, ensembles, and performance opportunities beyond the classroom. This reflects Keith Swanwick's principle that musical development requires both skill acquisition and creative expression in authentic contexts. Hubs typically offer whole-class ensemble teaching (WCET) programmes, often in Year 4, introducing instruments such as strings, brass, woodwind, or world music instruments.

**3. Progression and Pathways**: Create clear routes for pupils to continue musical learning, including access to county/area ensembles, youth choirs, and specialist provision for talented young musicians. Lucy Green's research on informal learning pedagogies informs many Hub progression programmes, which increasingly blend formal and informal approaches.

**4. Inclusion and SEND**: Ensure music education is accessible to all children, including those with special educational needs and disabilities, looked-after children, and those from disadvantaged backgrounds. This aligns with the Equality Act 2010 and Ofsted's emphasis on curriculum breadth for all learners.

### Extension Roles

Beyond core provision, many Hubs deliver extension activities including: Music Technology programmes, composition and songwriting projects, music therapy referrals, community music initiatives, and partnerships with professional orchestras and ensembles. The BBC Ten Pieces programme, widely delivered through Hub networks, exemplifies how partnerships can bring world-class repertoire into schools.

## Partnership Models and Governance

### Lead Organisations

Each of England's 120+ Music Hubs is managed by a Lead Organisation, typically:
- Local Authority Music Services (most common historical model)
- Multi-Academy Trusts or Teaching School Hubs
- Independent music education charities
- Arts organisations or orchestras

The Lead Organisation receives Arts Council England funding (approximately £75-85 million annually distributed across all Hubs) and coordinates partner contributions. Fautley's research highlights that effective Lead Organisations demonstrate strong strategic leadership, transparent governance, and genuine partnership working rather than merely subcontracting services.

### Partnership Composition

Effective Hub partnerships typically include:
- **Schools and Academy Trusts**: Curriculum delivery, venue hosting, strategic needs identification
- **Music Services**: Instrumental/vocal teaching, ensemble direction, resource provision
- **Higher Education Institutions**: Initial Teacher Training, research partnerships, specialist expertise
- **Professional Music Organisations**: Orchestras, opera companies, contemporary music organisations providing inspiring encounters
- **Examination Boards**: ABRSM, Trinity College London, Rock & Pop boards supporting progression frameworks
- **Community Groups**: Saturday music schools, cultural organisations, faith groups extending reach

Zoltán Kodály's emphasis on music as a community endeavour resonates with the Hub partnership model, though critics note that partnerships can sometimes lack genuine collaboration, defaulting to transactional relationships.

## Key Stage Implications and Curriculum Support

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Primary)

Music Hubs support primary schools in meeting National Curriculum requirements that pupils should:
- Use voices expressively and creatively through singing
- Play tuned and untuned instruments musically
- Listen with concentration and understanding
- Experiment with, create, select, and combine sounds

Practical Hub provision includes:
- **First Access programmes**: Often provided free in Year 3 or 4, introducing whole classes to instrumental families
- **Singing strategies**: Partnership with organisations like Sing Up or Music Mark's Sing Together programme
- **CPD for generalist teachers**: Training days, online resources, and co-teaching models addressing the confidence gap identified by Fautley and Daubney
- **Resource libraries**: Instrument loans, digital resources, and scheme-of-work materials supporting Model Music Curriculum implementation

### Key Stage 3 (Secondary)

At KS3, Hubs support delivery of the National Curriculum's requirement that pupils:
- Play and perform confidently in ensemble contexts
- Improvise and compose using interrelated dimensions of music
- Listen with increasing discrimination
- Develop understanding of musical history and cultural context

Hub support mechanisms include:
- **Specialist teacher networks**: Subject-specific CPD addressing pedagogy, curriculum sequencing, and assessment
- **Ensemble extension opportunities**: Area choirs, orchestras, jazz bands, and rock/pop groups providing progression beyond school provision
- **Composition partnerships**: Links with contemporary composers and music technology specialists
- **Curriculum resources**: Schemes aligned with Model Music Curriculum or examination board specifications

### Key Stage 4 and 5 (GCSE and A-Level)

Whilst KS4/5 music is optional, Hubs support schools offering qualifications from AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas by:
- Facilitating **ensemble performance opportunities** meeting coursework requirements
- Providing **specialist instrumental/vocal tuition** at appropriate levels
- Arranging **visiting professional workshops** enriching composition/appraisal components
- Coordinating **area performances and masterclasses** supporting progression to conservatoires

Research indicates that prior Hub engagement significantly impacts GCSE uptake, with schools offering sustained instrumental programmes showing higher take-up rates (Youth Music, 2020).

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### For Primary Generalists

Teachers should:
- **Engage proactively with Hub CPD**: Many Hubs offer twilight sessions, online modules, and in-school support addressing confidence issues
- **Utilise First Access programmes strategically**: Integrate WCET into broader curriculum sequencing rather than viewing as isolated provision
- **Access resource libraries**: Many Hubs provide instruments, software licences, and teaching materials free or subsidised
- **Connect singing programmes to curriculum**: Use Hub-supported initiatives like Sing Together as vehicles for exploring pitch, pulse, and musical structure

Lucy Green's research suggests that teachers benefit most when Hub support models informal learning approaches accessible to non-specialists, including learning by ear and peer teaching.

### For Secondary Specialists

Music teachers should:
- **Contribute to Hub strategy**: Participate in Hub forums identifying local needs and shaping provision
- **Coordinate ensemble pathways**: Ensure pupils understand progression from school ensembles to Hub area groups to national opportunities
- **Leverage partnership resources**: Access professional musicians, contemporary repertoire, and technology resources enhancing curriculum delivery
- **Support feeder primary schools**: Collaborate with Hub partners to improve transition and curriculum continuity from KS2 to KS3

Gary Spruce emphasises that secondary specialists must advocate for broader access to music education rather than focusing exclusively on examination cohorts, a role Hubs can support through partnership working.

### For Senior Leaders

Headteachers and curriculum leaders should:
- **Understand statutory requirements**: The National Curriculum mandates music teaching at KS1-3; Hubs support schools in meeting these obligations
- **Budget strategically**: Hub subsidies significantly reduce costs of instrumental provision, CPD, and resources
- **Monitor impact**: Evaluate Hub partnership contributions through pupil progression data, participation rates, and curriculum quality metrics
- **Advocate for sustainability**: Engage with Hub governance consultations ensuring local provision meets school needs

Ofsted's Research Review: Music (2021) emphasises that effective schools integrate Hub provision within well-sequenced, knowledge-rich curricula rather than treating it as peripheral enrichment.

## Research Evidence and Impact

### Participation and Equity

Youth Music's research demonstrates that Hub funding has maintained instrumental teaching infrastructure during austerity, with approximately 700,000 children receiving tuition annually. However, significant equity challenges persist:
- **Socioeconomic gaps**: Children from disadvantaged backgrounds remain under-represented in instrumental learning despite Hub prioritisation (ISM, 2019)
- **Geographical variation**: Hub provision quality varies substantially between areas, creating postcode lotteries (Fautley & Whyton, 2020)
- **Workforce diversity**: Teaching workforces remain predominantly white and middle-class, potentially limiting cultural responsiveness (Birmingham City University, 2018)

Fautley's work highlights that whilst Hubs have prevented wholesale collapse of music education infrastructure, they cannot fully compensate for reduced school budgets and curriculum time.

### Pedagogical Approaches

Research indicates effective Hub partnerships embrace pedagogical diversity:
- **Kodály-influenced approaches**: Many Hub singing programmes incorporate Kodály principles of sequential skill development through quality repertoire
- **Informal learning**: Following Green's work, Hubs increasingly support popular music, learning by ear, and learner-directed projects
- **Swanwick's C(L)A(S)P model**: Effective programmes balance Composition, Listening/Appraising, Skill acquisition, and Performance within authentic musical contexts

The Music Commission's 2019 report 'Protecting Our Music Education Services' emphasised that Hub pedagogies must evolve beyond instrumental silos towards holistic musical development addressing National Curriculum breadth.

### Curriculum Impact

Evidence on curriculum music impact remains mixed:
- **Positive indicators**: Schools with strong Hub partnerships report improved curriculum confidence, resource access, and pupil progression (Music Mark, 2021)
- **Challenges**: Primary curriculum music quality remains inconsistent, with many schools lacking specialist leadership and adequate curriculum time (Ofsted, 2021)
- **COVID-19 disruption**: Pandemic-related challenges highlighted Hub adaptability but also exposed digital divide issues and vulnerability of peripatetic workforce

Fautley and Daubney's longitudinal research suggests that sustainable curriculum improvement requires systemic change beyond Hub capacity, including Initial Teacher Training reform and protected curriculum time.

## Funding and Sustainability Challenges

### Funding Streams

Hub income typically derives from:
- **Arts Council England Core Grants**: Approximately £75-85 million nationally (2023-24)
- **Local Authority contributions**: Increasingly constrained by budget pressures
- **School payments**: Parental contributions, school subscriptions, examination preparation fees
- **Trust and foundation grants**: Project-specific funding from organisations like Youth Music, PRS Foundation

The funding model has been criticised for creating financial precarity and incentivising cost-recovery activities potentially conflicting with equity objectives. Fautley notes this risks reverting to middle-class privilege models contrary to Hub inclusion principles.

### Workforce Sustainability

Instrumental and vocal teachers face significant challenges:
- **Precarious employment**: Predominantly self-employed or zero-hours contracts lacking employment protections
- **Low pay**: Median hourly rates often below teacher pay scales, with travel time unremunerated
- **Professional isolation**: Limited access to CPD, mentoring, and career progression
- **Pension implications**: Self-employment status excludes many from Teachers' Pension Scheme

The Incorporated Society of Musicians and Musicians' Union have advocated for improved conditions, with some Hubs pioneering employed models and progressive contractual arrangements.

## Future Developments and Policy Directions

### The 2022 National Plan Refresh

The revised NPME emphasises:
- **Curriculum primacy**: Strengthening classroom music teaching as foundation for broader engagement
- **Progression clarity**: Better-articulated pathways from curriculum to co-curricular to specialist routes
- **Technology integration**: Embracing music technology as creative medium and accessibility tool
- **Partnership coherence**: Improved alignment between Hubs, schools, and other educational partnerships (Teaching School Hubs, Sport Partnerships)

### Emerging Models

Innovative approaches being piloted include:
- **MAT-Hub partnerships**: Multi-Academy Trusts and Hubs co-designing curriculum and progression frameworks
- **Technology-enabled access**: Online ensembles, composition platforms, and hybrid learning models expanding reach
- **Community partnership**: Deeper integration with libraries, youth services, and community centres
- **Diverse repertoire**: Expanded offerings in world music, contemporary styles, and music technology reflecting cultural plurality

Gary Spruce argues that future Hub development must address fundamental questions about music education's purposes—whether primarily cultural transmission, creative development, or social inclusion—with implications for partnership priorities and pedagogical approaches.

## Practical Resources and Engagement

Teachers and school leaders can engage with Music Hubs through:

### Finding Your Hub
- **Music Mark Hub Directory**: Comprehensive listing with contact details and area coverage (www.musicmark.org.uk)
- **Arts Council England Hub Map**: Geographic tool identifying local provision
- **Local Authority websites**: Education sections typically provide Hub contact information

### Accessing Support
- **School subscriptions**: Many Hubs offer tiered membership providing CPD, resources, and preferential rates
- **CPD programmes**: Subject-specific and generalist training, often qualifying for continuing professional development accreditation
- **Resource portals**: Digital platforms providing lesson plans, recordings, and assessment materials aligned to National Curriculum and Model Music Curriculum
- **Partnership brokerage**: Hubs facilitate connections between schools and professional musicians, community groups, and cultural organisations

### Contributing to Strategy
- **Hub forums and consultations**: Regular opportunities for schools to shape local provision
- **Governance representation**: Some Hubs include school representatives on boards and steering groups
- **Research participation**: Universities and Hubs frequently seek school partners for practice-based research

## Conclusion

Music Education Hubs represent an ambitious partnership model addressing the complex infrastructure required for comprehensive music education. Whilst research by Fautley, Green, Spruce, and others identifies significant challenges—funding sustainability, workforce conditions, equity gaps, and variable quality—Hubs remain essential in maintaining instrumental teaching, supporting curriculum delivery, and creating progression pathways. 

For teachers, effective Hub engagement requires proactive partnership, strategic curriculum integration, and advocacy for sustainable provision. As the policy landscape evolves, informed professional dialogue between practitioners, Hub leaders, and policymakers will determine whether Hubs fulfil their potential as vehicles for musical entitlement or remain constrained by systemic limitations. The principles articulated by Swanwick, Kodály, and contemporary scholars provide compass points for this ongoing journey, emphasising music education as creative endeavour, cultural inheritance, and fundamental human capacity deserving of sustained commitment and resource.
