# ISM and Music Education Bodies

## Introduction

The landscape of music education in the UK is supported by a complex network of professional organisations, advocacy bodies, and educational institutions that shape policy, provide professional development, and champion standards. The Incorporated Society of Musicians (ISM) stands as the UK's professional body for musicians, whilst numerous other organisations collectively influence curriculum development, pedagogy, and access to music education. Understanding these bodies is essential for music educators navigating professional development, policy changes, and resource allocation.

## The Incorporated Society of Musicians (ISM)

### History and Remit

Founded in 1882, the ISM is the UK's oldest professional body for musicians, representing over 10,000 members across all sectors of the music profession. The organisation serves performers, teachers, composers, and those working in music education at all levels, from peripatetic instrumental teachers to secondary school heads of department.

The ISM's remit extends beyond simple membership services to encompass advocacy work that directly influences government policy on music education. Their research publications, particularly the 'Music Education in Crisis' reports and subsequent monitoring, have provided empirical evidence of the decline in curriculum music provision since 2010.

### ISM Services for Music Educators

The ISM provides extensive professional support including:

**Legal and Professional Guidance**: The ISM offers comprehensive advice on employment contracts, safeguarding requirements, self-employment issues, and GDPR compliance—particularly valuable for peripatetic teachers navigating multiple employment relationships.

**Insurance Services**: Members receive professional indemnity and public liability insurance, essential for private teaching and school-based work.

**Pay and Conditions Research**: The ISM publishes regular reports on recommended pay rates for private teaching, ensemble work, and examination fees, which influence sector-wide standards.

**Policy Advocacy**: The ISM actively lobbies government regarding curriculum changes, funding for music services, and the EBacc's impact on arts subjects. Their evidence has been cited in Parliamentary Select Committee inquiries.

### ISM Research Contributions

Research published by the ISM has documented the measurable impact of the English Baccalaureate (EBacc) on music uptake at Key Stage 4. Their 2019 report revealed a 21% decline in pupils taking GCSE Music between 2010 and 2019, providing evidence that informed subsequent National Plan for Music Education revisions.

## Music Education Organisations: The Wider Ecosystem

### Music Mark

Music Mark (formed in 2012 from the merger of the Federation of Music Services and the National Association of Music Educators) represents music education hubs, music services, and organisations delivering music education. With over 3,500 members, Music Mark connects directly to the infrastructure delivering the National Plan for Music Education (2022).

Music Mark's role includes:

- **Advocacy for Music Services**: Representing the hub system and campaigning for sustainable funding models
- **Professional Development**: Providing CPD aligned with Teachers' Standards and music-specific pedagogical approaches
- **Best Practice Sharing**: Facilitating networks where practitioners share inclusive teaching strategies consistent with researchers like Lucy Green's informal learning approaches

The organisation's connection to the hub system means it directly influences how the National Curriculum's statutory requirements are delivered beyond classroom teaching.

### Arts Council England and Music Education

Arts Council England administers the Music Education Hub funding on behalf of the Department for Education, distributing approximately £75 million annually (as of 2023). The Arts Council's investment principles emphasise:

- Quality teaching aligned with the National Curriculum's requirements for performing, composing, and listening
- Progression routes from Key Stage 1 through to specialist provision
- Inclusive practice reaching children from disadvantaged backgrounds

The Arts Council's 'Let's Create' strategy (2020-2030) explicitly connects to music education's role in developing creativity—an aim that resonates with Keith Swanwick's emphasis on musical understanding through creative engagement.

### Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music (ABRSM)

The ABRSM provides graded music examinations that, whilst not part of statutory requirements, significantly influence instrumental teaching pedagogy and progression pathways. Understanding ABRSM's framework helps teachers:

- Map instrumental progress alongside National Curriculum levels
- Provide recognised accreditation complementing GCSE and A-Level work
- Structure ensemble work using repertoire from ABRSM syllabuses

Martin Fautley's research on assessment in music education acknowledges graded examinations whilst cautioning against allowing them to dominate broader musical development—a balance classroom teachers must navigate when students pursue parallel instrumental study.

### Youth Music

Youth Music focuses specifically on music-making for children and young people facing barriers, distributing National Lottery funding to projects supporting disadvantaged communities. Their research consistently demonstrates music's impact on social inclusion, attainment, and wellbeing—evidence that supports the National Curriculum's broader aims for cultural capital and personal development.

Youth Music's emphasis on contemporary, accessible musical styles reflects Lucy Green's research on informal learning pedagogies, where students engage with music reflecting their cultural contexts rather than exclusively formal Western classical traditions.

## Subject Associations and Pedagogical Networks

### National Association of Music Educators (NAME)

NAME provides subject-specific professional development, conferences, and publications that disseminate current research into classroom practice. Their journal, *Music Education Now*, publishes practitioner research bridging academic theory and practical application.

NAME's conference programme regularly features workshops on:

- Implementing National Curriculum requirements across key stages
- Technology integration using Digital Audio Workstations
- Inclusive practice for SEND pupils
- Composing pedagogies informed by John Paynter's creative music-making traditions

### Music Teachers' Associations

Specialist associations for Kodály, Dalcroze, and Orff-Schulwerk approaches maintain pedagogical traditions that inform UK practice:

**British Kodály Academy**: Promotes Zoltán Kodály's philosophy emphasising singing, inner hearing, and musical literacy through folk song repertoire. This approach informs Key Stage 1 and 2 practice where curriculum requirements emphasise singing and pitch development.

**Dalcroze UK**: Advocates eurhythmics—learning music through movement—particularly effective at Key Stage 1 for developing pulse, rhythm, and phrasing understanding.

**Orff Society UK**: Promotes Carl Orff's approach using tuned percussion, speech patterns, and ensemble improvisation, widely adopted in primary settings where curriculum requirements emphasise exploration and experimentation.

These pedagogical traditions offer practical methodologies for achieving National Curriculum aims, though Gary Spruce and others caution against uncritically importing approaches from different cultural contexts without adaptation.

## Examination Boards and Qualification Bodies

### GCSE and A-Level Providers

The four main examination boards shape secondary music education through their specifications:

**AQA**: Their GCSE Music specification emphasises integrated learning across performing, composing, and appraising, with 60% coursework allowing extended creative development. The specification's Areas of Study include Western Classical Tradition, Popular Music, Traditional Music, and Film Music.

**Edexcel (Pearson)**: Offers flexibility in instrument choice and composition briefs, with technological composition routes acknowledged. Their A-Level specification includes options for jazz, music technology, and composition portfolios.

**OCR**: Provides particularly detailed support for integrated musical learning, with specifications emphasising connections between performing, composing, and contextual understanding—reflecting Keith Swanwick's CLASP model.

**WJEC/Eduqas**: Offers specifications used predominantly in Wales and some English schools, with strong emphasis on world music traditions and appraising skills development.

Understanding these specifications helps teachers align Key Stage 3 curriculum design with progression routes, ensuring pupils develop appropriate skills, knowledge, and understanding for qualification study.

### Music Technology Qualifications

BTECs in Music and Music Technology (Pearson), alongside Cambridge Nationals (OCR), provide vocational pathways emphasising practical skills and portfolio development. These qualifications address different learner needs whilst maintaining rigour, supporting the National Curriculum's aim for musical inclusivity.

## Professional Development Networks

### Music education organisations facilitate various CPD models:

**Subject Knowledge Enhancement**: Addressing gaps in curriculum areas (e.g., contemporary music genres, technology skills, world music)

**Pedagogical Development**: Exploring teaching strategies informed by research, including Lucy Green's informal learning approaches or formative assessment practices recommended by Martin Fautley

**Leadership Development**: Supporting middle and senior leaders through music-specific leadership programmes addressing curriculum design, inspection preparation, and strategic planning

The ISM, Music Mark, and subject associations collectively provide this CPD, often aligned with Teachers' Standards and the Early Career Framework.

## Policy Influence and Advocacy

### National Plan for Music Education

Multiple organisations contributed to the revised National Plan for Music Education (2022), including:

- ISM's evidence on workforce sustainability
- Music Mark's hub system expertise
- Arts Council England's investment strategy
- Youth Music's inclusion research

The Plan's emphasis on progression from early years through specialist training reflects collective advocacy from these bodies, addressing previous criticisms from researchers like Gary Spruce regarding fragmentation and inequality in provision.

### Responding to Curriculum Changes

Music education bodies collectively responded to challenges including:

**EBacc Impact**: Co-ordinated campaigns evidencing music's decline at Key Stage 4, resulting in government acknowledgment and mitigation strategies

**Funding Pressures**: Advocacy for music services and hub funding during austerity, though success has been partial

**Teacher Recruitment**: Campaigns highlighting recruitment challenges, with ISM data showing music teacher vacancy rates exceeding other subjects

These efforts demonstrate how professional bodies translate grassroots concerns into policy dialogue, though researchers including Martin Fautley note ongoing challenges in securing music's curriculum position.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Professional Membership Decisions

Music teachers benefit from understanding which organisations serve their specific needs:

- **ISM membership** provides professional services, insurance, and policy representation
- **Music Mark** offers network connections and hub liaison
- **Subject associations** provide pedagogical development in specific traditions

Many teachers maintain multiple memberships, recognising complementary rather than competing services.

### Accessing Resources and Support

These bodies collectively provide:

**Curriculum Resources**: Schemes of work, assessment frameworks, and repertoire suggestions aligned with National Curriculum requirements

**Research Access**: Publications translating academic research into practical application, including work by Fautley, Green, and contemporary researchers

**Advocacy Support**: Evidence for local negotiations regarding curriculum time, funding, and staffing

**Professional Networks**: Connections to colleagues facing similar challenges, facilitating collaborative problem-solving

### Engaging with Policy Development

Teachers can influence music education policy through:

- Contributing to organisational consultations on curriculum reforms
- Sharing practice through publications and conferences
- Participating in research projects evidencing effective pedagogy
- Using organisation-provided evidence in local advocacy with senior leadership teams and governors

## Contemporary Challenges and Future Directions

### Current Priorities Across Organisations

Music education bodies currently focus on:

**Post-Pandemic Recovery**: Addressing disrupted musical learning, particularly ensemble work and practical music-making

**Diversity and Inclusion**: Expanding curriculum repertoire beyond Western classical traditions, reflecting Lucy Green's cultural diversity research

**Teacher Retention**: Improving workload and conditions to address recruitment challenges

**Technology Integration**: Supporting teachers in incorporating music technology whilst maintaining core musical understanding—a balance Keith Swanwick emphasised in his later work

**Progressive Pedagogy**: Moving beyond transmission models toward student-centred approaches informed by contemporary research

### Research Informing Practice

Current research priorities include:

- Assessment validity in creative subjects (continuing Martin Fautley's work)
- Curriculum models balancing formal and informal learning (extending Lucy Green's findings)
- Music's contribution to broader educational outcomes
- Effective models for inclusive practice with SEND pupils

These organisations disseminate such research, bridging the theory-practice gap that can otherwise limit research impact.

## Conclusion

The ISM and wider network of music education organisations provide essential infrastructure supporting UK music education. For classroom teachers, heads of department, and curriculum leaders, engaging with these bodies offers professional protection, pedagogical development, resource access, and collective advocacy power. Understanding their distinct roles enables strategic engagement, whether accessing ISM's professional services, Music Mark's hub connections, or subject associations' pedagogical expertise. As music education faces ongoing challenges regarding curriculum position, funding, and workforce sustainability, these organisations remain crucial in translating research, policy, and practice into improved outcomes for young musicians across all key stages.
