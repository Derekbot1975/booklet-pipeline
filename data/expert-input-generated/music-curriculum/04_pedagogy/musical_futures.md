# Musical Futures and Informal Learning

## Introduction and Theoretical Foundations

Musical Futures represents one of the most significant pedagogical movements in UK music education over the past two decades. Developed initially as a research project funded by the Paul Hamlyn Foundation in 2003, Musical Futures introduced informal learning approaches derived from how popular musicians learn outside formal educational settings. The approach was pioneered by Professor Lucy Green of the Institute of Education, University College London, whose seminal work *How Popular Musicians Learn* (2001) and *Music, Informal Learning and the School: A New Classroom Pedagogy* (2008) provided the theoretical and empirical foundations for this pedagogical shift.

Green's research revealed that popular musicians typically learn through peer-directed activity, self-chosen repertoire, purposive listening and copying recordings, integration of listening, performing, improvising and composing, and an emphasis on personal enjoyment and ownership. These principles fundamentally challenged the traditional transmission model of music education prevalent in UK secondary schools, where teachers typically controlled repertoire selection, directed activities, and separated musical skills into discrete lessons.

## Key Principles of Informal Learning

### Self-Directed Learning and Student Agency

The central tenet of Musical Futures is student ownership of the learning process. Rather than teacher-prescribed activities, students select their own repertoire, decide on learning strategies, and direct their own progress. This represents what Martin Fautley and Victoria Kinsella describe in *Teaching Music Creatively* (2019) as a shift from "teacher as deliverer" to "teacher as facilitator". The National Curriculum for Music (2013) explicitly supports this approach through its emphasis on students learning to "play and perform confidently in a range of solo and ensemble contexts using their voice, playing instruments musically, fluently and with accuracy and expression".

### Peer-Directed Learning

Informal learning privileges peer interaction over teacher-student transmission. Students work in friendship groups, teaching each other, sharing discoveries, and solving musical problems collaboratively. Research by Green (2008) demonstrated that students made significant progress when working in self-selected groups without direct teacher instruction. Gary Spruce, in *Teaching Music in Secondary Schools* (2013), argues that this approach validates students' existing musical identities and social relationships, making music education more inclusive and relevant.

### Aural/Oral Transmission

Unlike traditional notation-based approaches championed by Keith Swanwick's developmental model, Musical Futures prioritises learning by ear. Students learn from recordings, copying and imitating what they hear, rather than reading from scores. This aligns more closely with how the majority of contemporary musicians work and develops aural skills that Swanwick himself acknowledged as fundamental in his C(L)A(S)P model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition, Skill acquisition, Performance).

## Practical Implementation in UK Classrooms

### The Whole-Class Ensemble Model

Musical Futures' most widely adopted model involves whole-class instrumental work where students learn to play popular music repertoire together. The typical structure includes:

1. **Initial immersion**: Students listen repeatedly to a chosen song, identifying instruments, structure, and key features
2. **Instrumental exploration**: Using classroom instruments (keyboards, guitars, drums, bass guitars), students experiment with recreating what they've heard
3. **Group work**: Small groups work independently to learn different parts of the song
4. **Teacher facilitation**: The teacher circulates, responds to questions, and provides targeted support rather than whole-class instruction
5. **Informal performance**: Regular opportunities to share work-in-progress with peers

Martin Fautley, in *Assessment in Music Education* (2010), emphasises that this model requires teachers to reconceptualise their role, moving from "sage on the stage" to "guide on the side", which can be professionally challenging but pedagogically rewarding.

### Resource Requirements and Practical Considerations

Successful implementation requires adequate instrumental resources: ideally multiple keyboards, guitars, bass guitars, drum kits, and amplification equipment. Many UK schools have invested in "Musical Futures classroom" setups through funding from organisations including the Arts Council England and regional Music Education Hubs. The approach works across Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14) and can be adapted for Key Stage 4 GCSE work, particularly for AQA's ensemble performance requirements and Edexcel's "Music Technology" pathway.

### Repertoire Selection

While student choice is paramount, teachers must balance autonomy with curriculum requirements. Popular choices typically include contemporary chart music, classic rock songs, and film/game music. The Musical Futures organisation provides extensive resources including simplified arrangements and backing tracks for songs by artists such as Oasis, The Beatles, Ed Sheeran, and Stormzy. Schools must ensure appropriate licensing through organisations like the Copyright Licensing Agency (CLA) and Performing Right Society (PRS) for Schools scheme.

## Theoretical Debates and Critical Perspectives

### The Swanwick-Green Debate

Keith Swanwick's influential work, particularly *A Basis for Music Education* (1979) and *Musical Knowledge: Intuition, Analysis and Music Education* (1994), emphasised developmental progression through increasingly sophisticated engagement with musical materials. His model prioritised structured curriculum sequences and musical "literacy" through notation. Lucy Green's informal learning approach represents a significant departure from this tradition, prioritising authenticity and student relevance over prescribed developmental sequences.

Swanwick critiqued informal learning approaches for potentially lacking rigour and systematic skill development. However, Green's research demonstrated measurable progress in students' musical skills, confidence, and engagement. The debate reflects broader tensions in UK music education between classical/formal and popular/informal traditions.

### Integration with Formal Learning

Martin Fautley and Regina Murphy, in *What is Music Education For?* (2016), argue for a "both/and" rather than "either/or" approach. They suggest that informal learning principles can be integrated with systematic skill development, theoretical understanding, and exposure to diverse musical traditions. This integrated approach is reflected in the National Curriculum's requirement that students "appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians".

### Zoltán Kodály's Influence and Contrast

Zoltán Kodály's philosophy, influential in UK music education through organisations such as the British Kodály Academy, emphasised structured sequential learning, folk music as cultural foundation, and singing as the basis of musicality. The Kodály approach represents a contrasting pedagogical model: highly teacher-directed, notation-based, and culturally specific. However, both approaches share commitment to making music accessible to all students and developing intrinsic musical understanding rather than mere technical facility.

## Curriculum and Assessment Applications

### National Curriculum Alignment

The 2013 National Curriculum for Music aligns well with Musical Futures principles. The requirement that students "identify and use the inter-related dimensions of music expressively and with increasing sophistication" can be achieved through informal learning's integrated approach to listening, performing, and composing. However, the curriculum's expectation that students "develop an understanding of the history of music" requires teachers to supplement informal learning with contextual studies.

### GCSE Applications

Musical Futures approaches have been successfully integrated into GCSE Music across all major exam boards:

**AQA Music (8271)**: The ensemble performance component (30% of GCSE) aligns directly with Musical Futures methodology. Students can perform popular music repertoire learned informally, and the board explicitly values authentic performance styles developed through aural learning.

**Edexcel Music (1MU0)**: The "Performing as part of an ensemble" component allows students to demonstrate skills developed through informal learning. Edexcel also offers "Music Technology" qualification pathways that embrace contemporary production methods aligned with informal learning principles.

**OCR Music (J536)**: The integrated portfolio approach, where students create a body of work combining performance, composition, and reflection, suits the holistic nature of informal learning. OCR explicitly values student-directed projects and contemporary music styles.

**WJEC Eduqas Music (C660QS)**: The performing component allows pop/rock ensemble work, and the composing component permits briefs based on popular music genres, supporting progression from informal learning approaches at Key Stage 3.

### Assessment Challenges

Martin Fautley's research on assessment in music education highlights particular challenges when assessing informal learning. In *Assessment in Music Education* (2010), he notes that informal learning's emphasis on process, group work, and holistic musical engagement can conflict with examination requirements for individual achievement and clearly defined criteria. Teachers must balance formative assessment strategies that support informal learning (observation, peer feedback, recording progress) with summative requirements for GCSE and beyond.

## Research Evidence and Impact

### Lucy Green's Research Studies

Green's empirical work involved extensive classroom observations and teacher interviews across diverse UK schools. Her research, published in *Music, Informal Learning and the School* (2008), demonstrated:

- Significantly improved student engagement and motivation, particularly among previously disaffected learners
- Development of functional musical skills (ability to play by ear, work collaboratively, solve musical problems)
- Enhanced inclusivity, with students from non-traditional musical backgrounds feeling validated
- Challenges for teachers in relinquishing control and managing apparently chaotic learning environments

### Subsequent UK Research

Research by Fautley and Daubney, published in their report *Music Education in England, 2019: A New Decade* for the All-Party Parliamentary Group on Music Education, found that Musical Futures had become "embedded practice" in many UK secondary schools. However, they noted significant variation in implementation quality, with some schools adopting superficial elements without fundamental pedagogical change.

Studies by Georgina Barton and Margaret Barrett have examined long-term impacts, finding that students who experienced informal learning approaches demonstrated sustained engagement with music beyond school, contrasting with declining participation rates among students receiving traditional instruction.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Professional Development Requirements

Implementing Musical Futures effectively requires significant professional development. The Musical Futures organisation provides extensive CPD through regional training days, online resources, and certificated courses. Key professional competencies include:

1. **Facilitation skills**: Learning to support without directing, asking questions rather than providing answers
2. **Multi-instrumental competency**: Basic functional skills across popular music instruments to provide targeted demonstrations
3. **Aural skills development**: Enhanced ability to learn by ear and model this process for students
4. **Classroom management**: Strategies for managing apparently chaotic environments while maintaining purposeful learning

### Addressing Common Challenges

**Managing noise and apparent chaos**: Teachers must distinguish between productive and unproductive noise, establish clear expectations for purposeful work, and develop tolerance for environments that appear less controlled than traditional classrooms.

**Supporting struggling students**: While peer-learning is central, some students need additional scaffolding. Gary Spruce recommends strategic interventions: providing simplified versions, creating mixed-ability groupings, and offering targeted mini-lessons.

**Ensuring progression**: Martin Fautley emphasises the importance of documenting progress through recordings, student reflections, and teacher observations. Without formal notation or prescribed sequences, teachers must develop alternative methods for tracking musical development.

**Balancing coverage and choice**: The National Curriculum requires exposure to diverse musical traditions. Teachers must integrate repertoire from classical, world, and contemporary art music alongside student-chosen popular music.

### Integration with Music Education Hubs

Since the 2011 National Plan for Music Education, Music Education Hubs have become central to UK music provision. Many Hubs have adopted Musical Futures as core pedagogy, providing instrumental resources, CPD, and partnership opportunities. Schools should engage with their local Hub to access Musical Futures support, instrumental loans, and connections with professional musicians who can model informal learning approaches.

## Inclusive Practice and Equity Considerations

### Addressing Diverse Needs

Lucy Green's research demonstrated that informal learning can be particularly effective for students who struggle in traditional music education settings. Students with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND) often benefit from:

- Absence of notation requirements that can create barriers
- Peer support within inclusive grouping
- Multiple entry points and differentiated challenge
- Validation of diverse musical preferences and prior learning

However, Gary Spruce cautions that without careful planning, informal learning can inadvertently exclude students who lack confidence, cultural capital in popular music, or experience in social collaborative work.

### Gender and Cultural Considerations

Research by Fautley and Green has examined gender dynamics in Musical Futures classrooms, finding that careful attention to grouping, repertoire diversity, and instrument access is essential. Teachers must challenge stereotypes about "masculine" and "feminine" instruments, ensure diverse repertoire representing multiple cultures and genres, and monitor group dynamics to prevent marginalisation.

## Future Directions and Contemporary Developments

The Musical Futures approach continues to evolve. Recent developments include:

**Digital integration**: Incorporating music production software, online collaboration tools, and digital resources that reflect contemporary music-making practices.

**Just Play resources**: Simplified materials supporting complete beginners, addressing concerns about progression and accessibility.

**Cross-curricular applications**: Adapting informal learning principles for other subject areas, recognising broader pedagogical implications beyond music education.

**Teacher research networks**: Supporting practitioner enquiry and evidence-based refinement of approaches through organisations including the Music Education Research Group UK and the Incorporated Society of Musicians.

## Conclusion

Musical Futures and informal learning represent a fundamental reconceptualisation of music education, challenging traditional assumptions about teaching, learning, and musical knowledge. While debates continue about the balance between formal and informal approaches, the substantial evidence base and widespread adoption in UK schools demonstrate the approach's significant contribution to making music education more engaging, inclusive, and relevant. Teachers implementing these approaches must engage critically with the underlying principles, access appropriate professional development, and thoughtfully adapt practices to their specific contexts while maintaining the core commitment to student agency, peer-directed learning, and authentic musical experience.
