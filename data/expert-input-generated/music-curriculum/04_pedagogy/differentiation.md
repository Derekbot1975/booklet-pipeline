# Differentiation in Music

## Introduction

Differentiation in music education refers to the pedagogical practice of tailoring teaching approaches, learning activities, resources, and assessment methods to meet the diverse needs, abilities, interests, and learning styles of all pupils. Within the UK music classroom, effective differentiation ensures that every pupil, regardless of their starting point, can access high-quality musical learning and make meaningful progress. As Martin Fautley (2010) argues in 'Assessment in Music Education', differentiation is not simply about producing different worksheets, but about fundamentally understanding how pupils learn music and responding appropriately to their individual needs.

The UK National Curriculum for Music (2013) requires that teachers should 'set high expectations for every pupil' and 'plan stretch and challenge for all groups'. This statutory requirement places differentiation at the heart of music teaching across all key stages.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Multiple Intelligences and Musical Learning

Howard Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences, which identifies musical intelligence as a distinct cognitive domain, provides a theoretical framework for differentiation in music. However, Fautley and Savage (2014) in 'Assessment in Music Education' caution against rigid application of learning styles theory, instead advocating for varied approaches that recognise the complex, multifaceted nature of musical learning.

### Constructivist Approaches

Keith Swanwick's (1979, 1999) developmental spiral model, incorporating materials, expression, form, and value, suggests that pupils progress through musical understanding at different rates and may be at different stages simultaneously across various musical parameters. This model implies that differentiation must be responsive to where pupils are in their musical development rather than simply based on chronological age or year group.

### Informal Learning Pedagogy

Lucy Green's (2008) research into informal learning practices, particularly her Musical Futures project, demonstrates how differentiation can occur naturally when pupils are given choice, autonomy, and peer-directed learning opportunities. Green's work shows that allowing pupils to select their own music and learning pathways can be a powerful differentiating strategy, as pupils naturally pitch their learning at an appropriate level and support one another's development.

## Models of Differentiation in Music

### Differentiation by Task

This involves designing different activities or versions of activities matched to pupils' abilities. In music, this might include:

- **Performing**: Providing simplified or extended versions of a piece, different instrumental parts of varying complexity, or alternative notation formats (graphic, standard notation, chord charts, tablature)
- **Composing**: Offering different compositional briefs with varying constraints (e.g., some pupils compose using a pentatonic scale while others work with chromatic materials)
- **Listening**: Providing differentiated listening activities with varying levels of analytical demand

Gary Spruce (2012) in 'Ways of Thinking about Music Education' argues that task differentiation must be carefully managed to avoid 'ability grouping' that can limit expectations and create self-fulfilling prophecies about musical achievement.

### Differentiation by Outcome

This approach involves setting an open-ended task that allows pupils to respond at their own level. This is particularly effective in music due to the subject's creative nature. Examples include:

- Free composition tasks where success criteria allow for different levels of sophistication
- Improvisation activities where pupils can demonstrate understanding within their capability
- Group performances where pupils can take on roles matched to their current attainment

Fautley (2010) emphasises that differentiation by outcome requires carefully constructed success criteria that recognise different levels of achievement without creating ceilings on attainment.

### Differentiation by Support

Also known as 'scaffolding', this involves providing different levels of support to enable all pupils to access the same task. In music, this includes:

- Teaching assistant support for pupils with SEND
- Peer mentoring and mixed-ability grouping
- Technology-mediated support (e.g., recording software, auto-accompaniment, notation software with playback)
- Visual supports, including colour-coded notation, visual cues, and video demonstrations
- Structured frameworks (e.g., composition templates, writing frames for analysis)

The Kodály approach, widely used in UK music education, inherently incorporates differentiation by support through its sequential, carefully scaffolded methodology that allows pupils to secure understanding at each stage before progressing.

### Differentiation by Resource

This involves providing different resources to support learning. In music education, this includes:

- Instruments of varying difficulty (e.g., tuned percussion, keyboards, orchestral instruments)
- Different forms of notation (graphic scores, number notation, traditional staff notation)
- Technology (apps, digital audio workstations, online resources)
- Aural, visual, and kinaesthetic learning materials
- Modified or adapted instruments for pupils with physical disabilities

## Key Stage-Specific Differentiation Strategies

### Key Stage 1 and 2

At primary level, the National Curriculum requires pupils to 'play and perform in solo and ensemble contexts', 'improvise and compose', and 'listen with concentration and understanding'. Differentiation strategies include:

**Performing**: 
- Providing instruments requiring different levels of motor skill development
- Using peer demonstration and modelling
- Employing call-and-response techniques allowing graduated complexity
- Offering optional extension parts for more advanced pupils

**Composing**:
- Using limited pitch sets (pentatonic scales work particularly well as Kodály demonstrated)
- Providing composition templates with varying degrees of structure
- Allowing choice in how compositions are notated and recorded

**Listening**:
- Using multi-sensory approaches (movement, visual representations, verbalisation)
- Providing differentiated questioning (from simple recall to analytical and evaluative responses)
- Offering graphic organisers and listening maps of varying complexity

Fautley and Daubney (2019) in 'Teaching Music Creatively' emphasise the importance of maintaining high expectations whilst acknowledging different starting points, particularly in primary contexts where prior musical experience varies enormously.

### Key Stage 3

The National Curriculum at KS3 requires deeper engagement with musical elements, contexts, and traditions. Differentiation becomes more sophisticated:

**Performing**:
- Offering repertoire at different grade levels (using ABRSM, Trinity, or Rockschool frameworks as benchmarks)
- Providing opportunities for solo, small group, and whole-class performance
- Differentiating by musical role (melody, bass, harmony, percussion)
- Using technology to support performance (backing tracks, looping, click tracks)

**Composing**:
- Setting briefs with varying parameters and constraints
- Offering choice in compositional genre and style
- Providing differentiated compositional stimuli
- Using technology to enable access (notation software, DAWs with varying complexity)

**Appraising**:
- Differentiated written tasks with scaffolding for pupils requiring support
- Tiered questioning approaches
- Choice in how responses are presented (written, recorded, performed)
- Varied repertoire selection considering cultural backgrounds and interests

Lucy Green's (2008) informal learning strategies are particularly effective at KS3, as they allow natural differentiation through pupil choice and peer learning. Her research demonstrates that pupils often push themselves further when given ownership of their learning.

### Key Stage 4 and 5

At GCSE and A-Level, differentiation must address examination requirements whilst supporting diverse needs. The major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) all require differentiated teaching approaches to enable all pupils to access higher-level study.

**GCSE Level**:
- AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC specifications all require composing, performing, and appraising
- Differentiation in composition: allowing choice of brief, providing compositional frameworks, offering technology support
- Differentiation in performance: pupils select repertoire at appropriate levels (typically Grade 3-5 for higher achievement)
- Differentiation in listening: providing tiered resources, worked examples, and scaffolded analytical frameworks

**A-Level**:
- Extended composition portfolios allow for differentiation through choice of genre and scale
- Performance recitals can be differentiated through repertoire selection (typically Grade 6-8)
- Appraising requires sophisticated differentiation in essay writing support and analytical techniques

Martin Fautley's work on assessment in music education (2010) is particularly relevant here, as he argues that differentiation at KS4 and 5 must balance examination requirements with genuine musical development, avoiding 'teaching to the test' whilst ensuring pupils can meet assessment objectives.

## Differentiation for Specific Groups

### Pupils with Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND)

The Equality Act 2010 and the SEND Code of Practice (2015) require reasonable adjustments to ensure access for all pupils. In music, this includes:

**Physical Disabilities**:
- Adapted instruments and equipment (e.g., one-handed instruments, adapted beaters, switches for technology)
- Alternative notation systems
- Technology to enable access (soundbeam, eye-gaze technology, adaptive software)

**Sensory Impairments**:
- For hearing impairment: visual and tactile approaches, amplification, vibration-based activities
- For visual impairment: aural and tactile learning, braille music notation, large print resources, audio description

**Autism Spectrum Condition**:
- Clear routines and structures
- Visual timetables and task boards
- Reduced sensory overload (consider volume, number of participants, lighting)
- Concrete, explicit instruction
- Opportunities for individual work where group activities are challenging

**Speech, Language and Communication Needs**:
- Music can be an accessible medium for pupils with these needs
- Non-verbal communication through music-making
- Structured language development through singing

Ockelford (2000, 2013) has written extensively on music education for pupils with learning difficulties and disabilities, emphasising that music can be a particularly accessible curriculum area when appropriately differentiated.

### Gifted and Talented Pupils

Differentiation for more able musicians must provide appropriate stretch and challenge:

- Extension tasks that deepen musical understanding rather than simply adding volume
- Opportunities to explore complex repertoire and advanced techniques
- Leadership roles in ensemble work
- Engagement with professional musicians and higher education pathways
- Independent research and extended projects
- Cross-curricular links to mathematics, physics, languages, and history

Swanwick (1999) cautions against conflating technical virtuosity with musical understanding, arguing that differentiation for able pupils should address both dimensions.

### English as an Additional Language (EAL)

Music can be a particularly accessible subject for pupils with EAL:

- Music-making transcends language barriers
- Visual and aural learning approaches support understanding
- Singing supports language acquisition and pronunciation
- Cultural repertoire can value pupils' musical heritage
- Key vocabulary teaching embedded in practical contexts
- Visual supports, gestures, and demonstration

### Socio-Economic Disadvantage

The Pupil Premium requires schools to address attainment gaps. In music, this includes:

- Ensuring access to instruments (loan schemes, budget instruments)
- Providing time in school for practice where home practice is not possible
- Removing financial barriers to trips, performances, and enrichment
- Valuing diverse musical cultures beyond Western classical tradition
- Addressing cultural capital gaps through broad repertoire exposure

Green (2008) and Spruce (2012) both emphasise the importance of inclusive curriculum design that values the musical cultures pupils bring from home, rather than exclusively promoting particular musical traditions.

## Practical Strategies for the Music Classroom

### Flexible Grouping

Research by Fautley and Savage (2014) supports flexible grouping strategies in music:

- Mixed-ability groups for ensemble work (peer learning)
- Similar-ability groups for targeted skill development
- Friendship groups for informal learning approaches
- Random grouping to prevent fixed mindsets about 'musical' and 'non-musical' pupils
- Choice in whether to work individually, in pairs, or groups

### Tiered Success Criteria

Creating success criteria with 'all, most, some' or 'bronze, silver, gold' frameworks:

- Ensures all pupils have accessible entry points
- Provides clear progression routes
- Avoids ceiling effects by including 'beyond' criteria
- Must be carefully constructed to maintain high expectations
- Should focus on quality of musical understanding, not just technical achievement

### Choice and Autonomy

Drawing on Lucy Green's informal learning research:

- Allowing pupils to choose repertoire within parameters
- Offering choice in how learning is demonstrated (perform, compose, notate, record)
- Providing choice in instrumental timbres and ensemble roles
- Supporting pupils to make informed choices through modelling and exemplars

### Whole-Class Ensemble Teaching (WCET)

Programmes like Wider Opportunities (now often called First Access) incorporate differentiation through:

- Part differentiation in ensemble arrangements
- Progression through increasingly complex material
- Peer support and collaborative learning
- Gradual transition from imitation to notation reading

### Technology-Enhanced Differentiation

Digital tools offer powerful differentiation opportunities:

- Notation software with playback for aural learners and checking work
- Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs) enabling complex composition for those without instrumental skills
- Apps providing scaffolded learning (e.g., GarageBand, Bandlab, Soundtrap)
- YouTube and streaming services for accessing diverse repertoire
- Recording technology allowing repeated listening and self-assessment

Savage (2012) in 'Pedagogy and ICT in Music Education' explores how technology can enable access whilst caution is needed to ensure it enhances rather than replaces direct musical engagement.

### Differentiated Questioning

Using Bloom's Taxonomy or similar frameworks:

- Knowledge/Recall: "What instruments can you hear?"
- Understanding: "How does the music create a sad mood?"
- Application: "Can you use similar techniques in your composition?"
- Analysis: "How does the structure support the musical narrative?"
- Synthesis: "How might you combine these styles?"
- Evaluation: "Which version is most effective and why?"

## Assessment and Differentiation

Martin Fautley's extensive work on assessment in music education (2010, 2013) emphasises the intrinsic link between assessment and differentiation. Key principles include:

### Assessment for Learning (AfL)

- Using assessment information to inform next teaching steps
- Differentiated feedback addressing individual needs
- Peer and self-assessment developing metacognitive awareness
- Questioning as assessment tool to gauge understanding and inform differentiation

### Formative Assessment

- On-going assessment during lessons enabling responsive teaching
- Observation of practical music-making informing groupings and task selection
- Verbal feedback in the moment allowing differentiated support
- Low-stakes assessment reducing anxiety and enabling focus on learning

### Success Criteria and Progression

- Clear learning objectives with differentiated success criteria
- Reference to grade criteria (using ABRSM or similar frameworks appropriately)
- Musical progression should be understood through frameworks like Swanwick's developmental spiral
- Avoiding age-related expectations that create ceilings or floors

Fautley (2013) warns against over-reliance on National Curriculum levels (now removed) or numeric grades that can label pupils and limit expectations, instead advocating for rich, descriptive feedback that identifies specific next steps for each pupil.

## Challenges and Considerations

### Avoiding Labelling and Fixed Mindsets

Gary Spruce (2012, 2013) and Lucy Green (2008) both challenge assumptions about musical ability:

- The myth of 'musical' and 'non-musical' pupils
- Danger of differentiation becoming de facto streaming
- Importance of growth mindset approaches
- Recognising diverse musical intelligences and prior learning

### Cultural Responsiveness

Differentiation must be culturally responsive:

- Valuing diverse musical traditions (acknowledging pupils' cultural backgrounds)
- Avoiding assumption that Western classical music represents 'high' culture
- Using repertoire that reflects school population diversity
- Recognising that 'informal' learning approaches may be formal in other traditions

### Practical Constraints

Real-world challenges include:

- Large class sizes limiting individual attention
- Limited resources and instruments
- Timetabling constraints (single lessons versus double periods)
- Mixed classes with huge attainment ranges
- Limited instrumental skills among non-specialist teachers

Fautley and Daubney's research (2019) into the decline of music education in English schools highlights how resource constraints can impact differentiation quality, though they argue that pedagogical skill remains the most significant factor.

### Maintaining High Expectations

Differentiation should never mean lowering expectations:

- Stretch and challenge for all groups, including lower attainers
- Avoiding 'differentiation as deficit' thinking
- Recognising that pupils may progress rapidly with appropriate support
- Using differentiation to enable access, not to categorise pupils

## Conclusion

Effective differentiation in music education is complex, requiring deep understanding of musical learning, individual pupil needs, and appropriate pedagogical strategies. The work of Fautley, Green, Swanwick, and Spruce provides theoretical and practical frameworks for differentiating music teaching in ways that are inclusive, ambitious, and musically authentic.

Differentiation should be understood not as a separate activity or additional workload, but as integral to high-quality music teaching. When implemented effectively, it ensures that all pupils—regardless of starting point, background, or learning need—can access the transformative power of musical engagement and make meaningful progress in their musical learning.
