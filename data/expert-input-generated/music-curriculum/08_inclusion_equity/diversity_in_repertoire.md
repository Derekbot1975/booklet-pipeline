# Diversity in Musical Repertoire

## Introduction and Rationale

Diversity in musical repertoire represents a fundamental principle of inclusive music education in the UK, ensuring that all pupils encounter a broad spectrum of musical traditions, styles, and cultural perspectives throughout their learning journey. The National Curriculum for Music (2013) explicitly requires that pupils should "listen with direction to a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians."

Professor Lucy Green's seminal work on informal learning and musical cultures has demonstrated that pupils' musical identities are intricately connected to the repertoire they encounter (Green, 2008). When curriculum content reflects diverse musical traditions, pupils from all backgrounds can see themselves represented in their music education, whilst simultaneously developing intercultural understanding and respect.

Martin Fautley and Regina Murphy (2015) argue that repertoire choices carry profound pedagogical consequences, noting that "the music we choose to teach with is never neutral—it carries cultural values, historical contexts, and implicit messages about whose music matters." This understanding positions repertoire diversity as central to equity and inclusion in music education.

## National Curriculum Framework and Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2 Requirements

The National Curriculum stipulates that primary pupils should be taught to "listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians." The non-statutory guidance emphasises exposure to music from:

- Classical traditions (Western art music)
- Popular music across decades
- World music traditions
- Jazz and blues
- Folk music (British and international)
- Contemporary and experimental music

### Key Stage 3 Requirements

At KS3, the curriculum requires pupils to "develop an understanding of the history of music" whilst engaging with "a wide range of live and recorded music from different times and cultures." This necessitates deliberate planning to include music from diverse geographical regions, historical periods, and cultural contexts.

Gary Spruce (2017) highlights that whilst the National Curriculum provides statutory requirements, it "leaves significant interpretive space for teachers to determine which specific repertoires fulfil these obligations," placing responsibility on practitioners to make informed, inclusive choices.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Kodály's Approach to Folk Music

Zoltán Kodály advocated strongly for incorporating national folk music as the foundation of music education, believing that "folk song is the musical mother tongue of a people" (Kodály, 1974). However, in the diverse context of contemporary UK classrooms, this principle must be expanded beyond a single national tradition to encompass the multiple musical mother tongues present in our schools.

Contemporary interpretations of Kodály principles in the UK now recognise that classrooms contain pupils from numerous cultural backgrounds, each bringing their own musical heritage. The British Kodály Academy acknowledges this by encouraging teachers to incorporate folk materials from pupils' diverse backgrounds whilst maintaining the pedagogical principles Kodály established.

### Swanwick's Musical Development and Cultural Contexts

Keith Swanwick's developmental spiral (1994) emphasises that musical understanding develops through engagement with materials, expression, form, and value. Crucially, Swanwick recognised that this development occurs within specific cultural contexts. His later work acknowledged that Western-centric models of musical development risked marginalising non-Western musical thinking and that diverse repertoire enables pupils to develop multiple "musical literacies" (Swanwick, 1999).

### Green's Informal Learning and Musical Cultures

Lucy Green's groundbreaking Musical Futures project (2008) demonstrated that pupils engage more authentically with music when repertoire reflects their own musical cultures and preferences. Her research showed that incorporating popular music, including genres like grime, hip-hop, and electronic dance music, significantly increased engagement, particularly amongst pupils who had previously felt alienated from formal music education.

Green argues that the "delineation" between classical music as 'serious' or 'educational' and popular or world music as somehow lesser creates artificial hierarchies that perpetuate exclusion. She advocates for genuinely treating all musical traditions with equal pedagogical validity.

## Practical Frameworks for Repertoire Diversity

### Geographical and Cultural Representation

Effective repertoire diversity requires systematic planning across geographical regions:

**African musical traditions**: Incorporating West African drumming (djembe, dundun), South African choral music (isicathamiya, mbube), North African maqam systems, and East African taarab traditions. Organisations like SOAS (School of Oriental and African Studies) provide authentic resources and professional development.

**Asian musical traditions**: Including Indian classical music (raga, tala systems), Indonesian gamelan, Chinese traditional opera and instrumental music, Japanese taiko drumming, and Middle Eastern musical traditions. The Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan Institute and various gamelan ensembles across the UK offer educational partnerships.

**Latin American and Caribbean**: Samba, salsa, reggae, calypso, tango, and indigenous traditions. These genres offer rich opportunities for ensemble work and rhythmic development.

**European traditions**: Beyond Western classical music, incorporating Balkan folk music, klezmer, flamenco, and contemporary European art music from diverse composers.

### Historical Period Representation

Fautley (2010) emphasises that temporal diversity matters alongside cultural diversity. Effective curriculum planning includes:

- **Medieval and Renaissance music**: Introducing modal systems and early polyphony
- **Baroque period**: Bach, Handel, Vivaldi, but also lesser-known composers like Élisabeth Jacquet de la Guerre
- **Classical and Romantic periods**: Expanding beyond the canonical "great composers" to include figures like Florence Price, Samuel Coleridge-Taylor, and Amy Beach
- **20th and 21st century**: Contemporary classical, minimalism, electronic music, and experimental traditions

### Genre Diversity

The Music Mark (formerly the National Association of Music Educators) Standards for Music Education explicitly call for breadth across genres:

- **Popular music**: Rock, pop, hip-hop, grime, electronic dance music, indie
- **Jazz traditions**: From early jazz through bebop, cool jazz, and contemporary fusion
- **Folk traditions**: British folk revival, Irish traditional music, Scottish pibroch
- **Gospel and spiritual traditions**: Historical spirituals, gospel music, contemporary Christian music
- **Film and game music**: Increasingly significant in pupils' musical experience

## Addressing Historical Imbalances

### Decolonising the Music Curriculum

The movement to decolonise education, articulated by researchers like June Boyce-Davies and applied to music by scholars including Elizabeth Clendinning, requires critical examination of whose music has been privileged in educational settings. This involves:

**Interrogating canonical assumptions**: The notion of "great composers" has historically centred Western European men. Deliberate inclusion of composers from underrepresented groups—women, composers of colour, non-Western composers—is essential rather than supplementary.

**Addressing compositional representation**: Research by Donne (2021) found that in GCSE and A-Level specifications, composers from ethnic minority backgrounds comprised less than 5% of set works. Recent exam board revisions have begun addressing this, but teachers must actively seek diverse repertoire in their curriculum planning.

### Gender Representation

Women composers remain significantly underrepresented. The BBC's feminist audio project *Hear Her* (2020) found that only 13% of music played on UK radio was composed by women. Educational repertoire should actively counter this imbalance by including:

- Historical figures: Hildegard von Bingen, Clara Schumann, Ethel Smyth, Florence Price
- 20th century: Éliane Radigue, Pauline Oliveros, Kaija Saariaho, Judith Weir
- Contemporary: Anna Meredith, Kerry Andrew, Errollyn Wallen, Anna Clyne

Gary Spruce (2015) notes that "simply adding women composers to existing frameworks is insufficient; we must critically examine why they were excluded and how this shapes our understanding of musical value."

## Exam Board Specifications and Diverse Repertoire

### GCSE Level Provision

**AQA GCSE Music** (9-1, 8271): The Areas of Study include Western Classical Tradition, Popular Music, Traditional Music, and Contemporary Music, with specific requirements for cultural and temporal breadth. The anthology includes pieces from diverse traditions, though teachers should supplement with additional repertoire.

**Edexcel GCSE Music** (1MU0): Features eight Areas of Study including Film Music, Rhythms of the World, and Contemporary Traditional Music, providing structured frameworks for diverse repertoire. However, Fautley (2020) notes that the breadth requirements can sometimes lead to superficial coverage unless teachers deliberately deepen engagement with each tradition.

**OCR GCSE Music** (J536): Offers integrated study across Areas of Study with explicit requirements for music from different times and cultures. The specimen materials include gamelan, Afro-Cuban, and Indian classical examples.

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Music** (C660QS): Welsh specifications include particular emphasis on Welsh musical traditions alongside broader requirements for cultural diversity.

### A-Level Specifications

All major exam boards now include requirements for diverse repertoire at A-Level:

**AQA A-Level Music** (7272): Requires study of six Areas of Study spanning 1650 to the present, including prescribed works from diverse traditions. Recent additions include works by Caroline Shaw and Anna Clyne.

**Edexcel A-Level Music** (9MU0): Features six Areas of Study with requirements for contextual understanding of diverse musical cultures and traditions.

**OCR A-Level Music** (H543): Includes flexible Areas of Study enabling teachers to select repertoire reflecting their pupils' interests whilst meeting breadth requirements.

### Challenges with Examination Specifications

Fautley and Daubney (2019) critique examination boards for sometimes treating "world music" as a single category, potentially homogenising vastly different traditions. They advocate for specifications that require specific, sustained engagement with particular traditions rather than superficial sampling.

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Audit and Planning Tools

Teachers should conduct regular repertoire audits examining:

- Geographical representation across curriculum units
- Gender balance in composers, performers, and studied works
- Historical period distribution
- Genre breadth
- Representation of pupils' own musical cultures

The Music Mark Equality, Diversity and Inclusion Framework (2021) provides audit templates specifically designed for music departments.

### Authentic Representation vs. Tokenism

Lucy Green (2011) warns against "musical tourism"—brief, decontextualised encounters with non-Western traditions that may reinforce stereotypes rather than develop genuine understanding. Effective practice requires:

**Sufficient curriculum time**: Deep engagement with traditions rather than superficial exposure
**Contextual understanding**: Teaching the cultural, social, and historical contexts from which music emerges
**Authentic materials**: Using recordings by tradition bearers and authentic practitioners
**Avoiding exoticisation**: Treating all musical traditions as equally sophisticated and worthy of serious study
**Community connections**: Partnering with local musicians from diverse backgrounds

### Partnership and Community Engagement

Building relationships with musicians from diverse backgrounds enriches curriculum authenticity. Organisations facilitating such connections include:

- **SOAS Centre for Music and Dance**: Traditional music and dance from Africa, Asia, and the Middle East
- **Gamelan organisations**: Various UK-based gamelan groups offer educational workshops
- **Birmingham Conservatoire**: PIPA (Professional Integration Project for Asylum seekers) connects professional musicians from refugee backgrounds with educational settings
- **Drake Music**: Inclusive music-making with disabled musicians
- **Local cultural organisations**: Gurdwaras, mosques, temples, and cultural centres often have musicians willing to engage with education

## Key Stage Specific Applications

### Early Years and Key Stage 1

At this stage, diversity can be embedded through:

- **Singing games from multiple cultures**: Not only traditional British nursery rhymes but singing games from Caribbean, South Asian, African, and other traditions
- **Diverse instrumentation**: Introducing percussion instruments from various traditions alongside standard classroom instruments
- **Stories and music**: Using picture books and stories from diverse cultures that incorporate music and song

### Key Stage 2

Building systematic repertoire diversity:

- **Listening portfolios**: Structured programmes introducing specific traditions with increasing depth (e.g., six-week units on Brazilian samba, Indian classical music, West African drumming)
- **Themed approaches**: Topics like "music and celebration" explored across multiple cultures
- **Composer studies**: Including diverse composers in biographical learning
- **Charanga Primary Scheme**: Recent updates include expanded global music units, though teachers should critically evaluate representations

### Key Stage 3

Developing critical understanding:

- **Musical elements across traditions**: Comparing how rhythm, melody, texture, and structure function differently in various musical cultures
- **Historical context**: Understanding how music reflects and shapes social movements (e.g., hip-hop and social justice, punk and political dissent, protest songs across cultures)
- **Composition projects**: Creating music that draws on multiple traditions, encouraging fusion and creative synthesis
- **Critical listening**: Developing pupils' ability to articulate responses to unfamiliar musical traditions

### Key Stage 4 and 5

Examination preparation with diversity:

- **Set work contextualisation**: Deep study of cultural contexts for examination repertoire
- **Performance repertoire choices**: Encouraging diverse choices in solo and ensemble performance
- **Composition influences**: Studying compositional techniques from various traditions
- **Extended project work**: Using NEA (Non-Examined Assessment) opportunities to explore underrepresented traditions

## Pedagogical Approaches for Diverse Repertoire

### Practical Music-Making

Fautley and Murphy (2015) emphasise that diversity is most effectively embedded through active music-making rather than passive listening. This includes:

- **Ensemble traditions**: African drumming circles, gamelan orchestras, samba bands, steel pan ensembles
- **Participatory music**: Traditions where boundaries between performer and audience are fluid
- **Call and response**: Found across many world music traditions
- **Improvisation**: Exploring improvisatory practices from jazz, Indian classical music, and other traditions

### Listening and Appraising

Structured listening frameworks should enable pupils to:

- **Suspend judgement**: Develop openness to unfamiliar musical aesthetics
- **Understand musical systems**: Recognise that different traditions have different internal logics (e.g., maqam systems, raga frameworks, modal jazz)
- **Identify musical elements**: Recognise how elements function across traditions
- **Cultural context**: Appreciate the social and cultural functions of music

### Integration with Other Curriculum Areas

Cross-curricular connections strengthen contextual understanding:

- **History**: Music reflecting historical periods and events (e.g., Renaissance madrigals, protest songs, wartime music)
- **Geography**: Music from regions studied in geography curriculum
- **English**: Poetry and music, song lyrics as literature
- **PSHE and Citizenship**: Music addressing social issues, identity, and community
- **Religious Education**: Music in worship and spiritual practice across faiths

## Addressing Practical Challenges

### Teacher Confidence and Expertise

Many teachers express concern about teaching musical traditions beyond their training. Strategies to address this include:

**Professional development**: Organisations like Music Mark, ISM (Incorporated Society of Musicians), and ABRSM (Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music) offer CPD on diverse repertoire.

**Guest musicians**: Inviting practitioners from specific traditions into classrooms brings authenticity and addresses teacher expertise gaps.

**Co-learning approaches**: Lucy Green's informal learning model suggests teachers can adopt facilitator roles, learning alongside pupils when exploring new traditions.

**Quality resources**: Using resources from reputable sources such as SOAS, Smithsonian Folkways, and specialist organisations ensures authenticity.

### Resource Constraints

Budget limitations can restrict access to diverse instruments and resources:

**Digital resources**: Platforms like Spotify, YouTube (with appropriate safeguarding), and BBC Sounds provide free access to diverse repertoire.

**Instrument loans**: Music hubs often have loan collections including world music instruments.

**Creative alternatives**: Body percussion, found sounds, and vocal techniques can explore rhythmic and melodic concepts from various traditions without specialised instruments.

**Progressive acquisition**: Building instrument collections over time through targeted bidding for funding (Arts Council, local trusts, PTA fundraising).

### Avoiding Misrepresentation

The risk of cultural appropriation or misrepresentation requires careful consideration:

**Consultation**: Where possible, consult with community members from traditions being taught.

**Authentic sources**: Use recordings and materials from tradition bearers rather than Western interpretations.

**Humility and acknowledgement**: Explicitly acknowledge limitations in teaching traditions outside one's own expertise.

**Focus on musical learning**: Avoid reducing traditions to costumes, superficial cultural artefacts, or stereotypes.

**Critical reflection**: Encourage pupils (particularly at KS3-5) to consider issues of representation, appropriation, and cultural respect.

## Research Evidence and Impact

### Engagement and Achievement

Research by Hallam and Creech (2010) demonstrates that pupils show increased engagement when curriculum repertoire reflects their own musical cultures. Their longitudinal study found statistically significant improvements in attendance, participation, and attainment in schools with diverse curriculum provision.

Wright and Kanellopoulos (2010) documented that pupils from minority ethnic backgrounds reported greater sense of belonging in music education when their musical traditions were valued within curriculum content. Conversely, Eurocentric curricula correlated with lower retention in music at KS4.

### Developing Cultural Competence

Fautley's research on musical thinking (2010) indicates that engagement with diverse repertoire develops pupils' capacity for flexible, culturally-responsive musical thinking. Pupils who studied diverse traditions demonstrated greater ability to identify and manipulate musical elements
