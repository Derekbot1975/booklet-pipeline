# SEND in Music

## Introduction

Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) provision in music education represents a fundamental commitment to inclusive practice, ensuring all pupils can access, participate in, and excel within musical learning. The 2014 SEND Code of Practice establishes that schools must make reasonable adjustments and provide appropriate support for pupils with SEND, whilst the National Curriculum for Music (2013) explicitly states that teachers should set high expectations for all pupils, including those with SEND.

Research by Martin Fautley and colleagues has consistently demonstrated that music education offers unique opportunities for SEND learners, providing multisensory learning pathways that can circumvent barriers present in other curriculum areas. Music's inherently practical and creative nature allows for differentiation by task, outcome, and resource, making it particularly suited to inclusive pedagogy.

## Legislative and Policy Framework

### SEND Code of Practice (2014)

The statutory framework distinguishes four broad areas of need:
- **Communication and interaction** (including autism spectrum conditions)
- **Cognition and learning** (including specific learning difficulties)
- **Social, emotional and mental health difficulties**
- **Sensory and/or physical needs**

Music teachers must understand how these categories manifest within musical learning contexts and implement appropriate graduated responses through the "Assess, Plan, Do, Review" cycle.

### Equality Act 2010

This legislation requires schools to make reasonable adjustments for disabled pupils, including providing auxiliary aids and services. In music, this might include adapted instruments, specialist software, or alternative notation systems.

## Theoretical Frameworks and Key Thinkers

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Approaches

Lucy Green's research on informal learning pedagogies, detailed in *Music, Informal Learning and the School* (2008), demonstrates particular efficacy for SEND learners. Her emphasis on aural/oral transmission, pupil choice, and peer-directed learning reduces barriers associated with traditional literacy-based approaches. Green's work shows how removing notation requirements can enable pupils with dyslexia, visual impairments, or processing difficulties to engage meaningfully with complex musical learning.

### Keith Swanwick's Developmental Theory

Swanwick's spiral of musical development (Swanwick and Tillman, 1986) provides a non-linear framework that accommodates varied developmental trajectories. His emphasis on mastery, imitation, imaginative play, and metacognition offers teachers multiple entry points for SEND learners working at different developmental stages, irrespective of chronological age.

### Zoltán Kodály's Inclusive Philosophy

Kodály's assertion that music education is a fundamental human right underpins inclusive practice. His sequential, child-centred methodology, using kinaesthetic learning through movement, visual learning through hand signs, and aural development through singing, provides multisensory access points particularly beneficial for learners with processing difficulties or dyslexia.

### Martin Fautley on Assessment and SEND

Fautley's extensive work on assessment in music education, particularly *Assessment in Music Education* (2010), emphasises the importance of formative assessment and multiple forms of evidence-gathering. He argues that traditional assessment methods often disadvantage SEND learners and advocates for diverse assessment approaches including practical demonstration, verbal explanation, and portfolio evidence.

### Gary Spruce on Social Justice

Spruce's writings on music education and social justice highlight how exclusive practices can marginalise SEND learners. In *Debates in Music Teaching* (2016), he challenges deficit models, arguing instead for strengths-based approaches that value diverse musical contributions and validate alternative ways of knowing and doing music.

## Specific Categories of Need

### Autism Spectrum Conditions (ASC)

**Characteristics in Musical Contexts:**
- May demonstrate exceptional pitch discrimination or rhythmic precision
- Might struggle with improvisation's open-ended nature
- Could find ensemble work socially challenging
- May be sensitive to volume, timbre, or acoustic environments
- Often respond well to structure, routine, and visual supports

**Practical Strategies:**
- Use visual timetables and lesson structure displays
- Provide advance notice of repertoire or activities
- Offer noise-reducing headphones during loud ensemble work
- Create structured improvisation frameworks with clear parameters
- Consider alternative participation modes (e.g., soundscaping, technological roles)
- Employ Social Stories™ to prepare for performances or new experiences

### Specific Learning Difficulties (SpLD)

**Dyslexia:**
Research by the British Dyslexia Association notes that 10% of the UK population has dyslexia. Musical notation presents particular challenges, though many successful musicians are dyslexic.

**Strategies:**
- Prioritise aural/oral learning before introducing notation
- Use coloured overlays or alternative notation systems (graphic scores, Figurenotes, Colour Music)
- Enlarge notation and increase spacing
- Provide recordings for home practice
- Allow extended processing time
- Consider technology solutions (notation software with playback, apps like NotateMe)
- Teach multisensory memory strategies (kinaesthetic, verbal, visual)

**Dyscalculia:**
Rhythmic notation's mathematical relationships can prove challenging.

**Strategies:**
- Use body percussion and movement to internalise rhythm before notation
- Employ Kodály rhythm names or alternative systems
- Provide manipulatives (rhythm cards, counters)
- Connect rhythmic learning to non-mathematical contexts

**Dyspraxia:**
Motor planning difficulties affect instrumental technique.

**Strategies:**
- Allow extended time for technical skill development
- Consider instruments with lower fine-motor demands (drums, bass, keyboards)
- Use task analysis to break techniques into component parts
- Provide visual demonstrations and video modeling
- Ensure good posture and positioning
- Reduce performance anxiety through varied demonstration opportunities

### Social, Emotional, and Mental Health Difficulties (SEMH)

Music can provide powerful therapeutic benefits whilst also presenting emotional vulnerabilities around performance and evaluation.

**Strategies:**
- Establish consistent routines and clear behavioural expectations
- Offer choice within structured parameters
- Use music as emotional regulation tool (calming activities, energetic releases)
- Build trust through low-stakes creative tasks
- Consider group dynamics carefully in ensemble formation
- Provide alternative performance contexts (recording, smaller audiences)
- Recognise trauma-informed practice principles
- Collaborate with pastoral support staff

### Hearing Impairment

A common misconception suggests hearing-impaired pupils cannot access music. Dame Evelyn Glennie's career demonstrates otherwise, as does research showing that profoundly deaf individuals process music through visual cortex and somatosensory pathways.

**Strategies:**
- Emphasise vibrotactile elements (bass frequencies, percussion)
- Use visual representations (waveforms, notation, movement)
- Ensure good acoustic environment and positioning
- Consider British Sign Language (BSL) integration (signing songs)
- Use technology (vibrotactile devices, visual tuners, loop systems)
- Explore Figurenotes system developed in Finland for inclusive music education
- Collaborate with Teachers of the Deaf
- Reference Music and the Deaf charity resources

### Visual Impairment

**Strategies:**
- Prioritise aural learning methods
- Use tactile notation systems (Braille music notation)
- Provide high-contrast enlarged scores
- Employ assistive technology (screen readers, notation software)
- Ensure safe navigation of music spaces
- Use clear verbal descriptions and physical modeling
- Record learning materials
- Consult specialist Teachers of Visual Impairment

### Physical Disabilities

**Strategies:**
- Adapt instruments (larger grips, modified beaters, stabilising devices)
- Consider alternative instruments (iPad apps, soundbeam, eye-gaze technology)
- Use adaptive notation and recording systems
- Ensure physical accessibility of music spaces
- Explore Drake Music's Assistive Music Technology resources
- Employ mouth-operated instruments or switches for profound physical disabilities

### Speech, Language and Communication Needs (SLCN)

Music provides alternative communication channels, particularly valuable for non-verbal learners.

**Strategies:**
- Use Makaton or Signalong alongside songs
- Emphasise instrumental and movement-based responses
- Reduce verbal instruction density
- Provide visual supports (symbols, photographs, demonstration)
- Allow processing time
- Use call-and-response structures
- Collaborate with Speech and Language Therapists

## Key Stage Considerations

### Key Stage 1 and 2

The National Curriculum states pupils should be taught to "play and perform... sing... listen... use and understand... improvise and compose." For SEND learners, primary music education establishes foundational accessibility:

**Practical Implications:**
- Multisensory approaches benefit all learners whilst specifically supporting SEND needs
- Songs with repetitive structures support memory and processing
- Movement-based learning accommodates kinaesthetic learners and those with literacy difficulties
- Small group work provides scaffolding for social communication development
- Untuned percussion enables participation without pitch or notation barriers

### Key Stage 3

Transition to secondary education presents particular challenges for SEND learners. Music departments should receive detailed transition information and implement bridging activities.

**Practical Implications:**
- Build on primary experiences whilst recognising varied prior learning
- Offer structured choice in project-based learning
- Explicitly teach ensemble and studio etiquette
- Introduce technology as access tool and creative medium
- Differentiate complexity within ensemble arrangements
- Consider instrumental lessons access (Pupil Premium funding, charitable trusts)

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) provide SEND access arrangements, yet music GCSE entries for SEND learners remain lower than for other subjects. Fautley and Daubney's research (*Music Education in England: An Investigation*, 2019) identifies accessibility concerns around composition portfolios and listening papers requiring extended writing.

**Access Arrangements:**
Boards permit:
- Extra time (25% standard, up to 100% for complex needs)
- Rest breaks
- Scribes for composition annotations and listening responses
- Use of word processors
- Modified papers (enlarged print, simplified language versions)
- Prompters for attention difficulties
- Separate invigilation
- Practical assistance (page turners, instrument holders)

**Course Adaptations:**
- OCR allows submission of composition realisations (recordings) rather than scores for certain SEND categories
- All boards accept graphic scores with clear annotation
- Technology-generated compositions acceptable with evidence of creative input
- Consider whether Music BTEC Tech Award offers more suitable assessment model (more coursework, less examination)

**Teacher Responsibilities:**
- Apply for access arrangements through SENCO following JCQ regulations
- Provide evidence of normal way of working
- Understand permitted adaptations and maintain examination integrity
- Differentiate teaching without compromising qualification demands

### Key Stage 5 (A-Level)

A-Level music (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) presents increased independence demands. SEND learners require scaffolded transition to autonomous composition, analytical writing, and extended performance preparation.

**Practical Implications:**
- Explicit teaching of essay structures and analytical frameworks
- Provide composition process documentation templates
- Use assistive technology for score production and analysis
- Consider BTEC Extended Certificate or Diploma as alternative pathways
- Ensure access to practice facilities and specialist support
- Collaborate with Learning Support departments for study skills

## Differentiation Strategies

### Differentiation by Task

- Offer varied complexity levels within ensemble arrangements
- Provide choice of instruments matching motor skill levels
- Create tiered composition briefs with shared learning objectives

### Differentiation by Outcome

- Value diverse responses to open-ended creative tasks
- Assess individual progress from starting points
- Recognise multiple forms of musical understanding

### Differentiation by Resource

- Provide modified notation, recordings, visual aids
- Use assistive technology appropriately
- Adapt instruments and equipment

### Differentiation by Support

- Deploy teaching assistants effectively (avoid over-dependence)
- Establish peer support structures
- Provide additional teacher guidance judiciously

## Technology as Access Tool

### Software and Applications

- **Notation Software:** Sibelius, Dorico, MuseScore with playback and accessibility features
- **DAWs:** GarageBand, Soundtrap, BandLab offering visual interfaces and loop-based composition
- **Apps:** ThumbJam, Bloom, Singing Fingers for alternative control systems
- **Accessibility-Specific:** Soundbeam, eye-gaze instruments, switch-operated interfaces

### Drake Music Resources

Drake Music champions disabled musicians and provides extensive resources for inclusive technology use, training programmes, and consultation services.

## Assessment and Evidence-Gathering

Fautley's research emphasises that assessment must be accessible, valid, and reliable. For SEND learners, this requires:

### Multiple Forms of Evidence

- Video/audio recordings of practical work
- Verbal explanations captured through audio notes
- Annotated scores using assistive technology
- Portfolios demonstrating process over time
- Peer and self-assessment with appropriate scaffolding

### Formative Assessment

- Regular, low-stakes feedback opportunities
- Clear success criteria in accessible language
- Modeling and exemplars
- Dialogue and questioning matched to communication needs

## Working with Teaching Assistants and Support Staff

Music's specialist nature requires careful TA deployment:

**Effective Practice:**
- Brief TAs on lesson content and musical terminology
- Clarify roles (facilitator vs. dependency creator)
- Enable TAs to develop musical confidence
- Value TAs' knowledge of individual pupils
- Consider whole-class support strategies over individual extraction

**Avoiding Velcro Effect:**
Research shows constant TA proximity can reduce peer interaction and teacher contact. In music, encourage independent engagement and peer collaboration.

## Instrumental and Vocal Tuition

The importance of equitable access to instrumental tuition for SEND learners cannot be overstated, yet systemic barriers persist:

**Barriers:**
- Financial costs despite Pupil Premium
- Selection processes favouring 'able' pupils
- Peripatetic teacher confidence with SEND learners
- Venue accessibility
- Inappropriate teaching methods

**Solutions:**
- Ring-fence SEND places in instrumental programmes
- Provide SEND training for peripatetic staff
- Consider whole-class instrumental teaching models
- Explore adapted instruments and alternative methods
- Use technology to support practice and progress

## The Musical Futures Approach

Musical Futures' informal learning pedagogy, influenced by Lucy Green's research, demonstrates significant benefits for SEND learners:

- Student-led learning reduces performance anxiety
- Aural learning circumvents notation barriers
- Friendship groupings support social communication
- Choice and autonomy increase engagement
- Real-world contexts provide meaningful learning

## Soundabout and Sounds of Intent

Soundabout provides specialist music resources for learners with profound and multiple learning difficulties (PMLD). The Sounds of Intent framework, developed by Adam Ockelford, offers a developmental progression model accommodating learners functioning at very early stages, recognising reactive, proactive, and interactive musical engagement.

## Cultural Responsiveness and SEND

Gary Spruce argues that music curriculum content can marginalise or empower. For SEND learners, consider:

- Representing disability within studied repertoire (disabled composers, performers)
- Challenging deficit narratives through successful disabled musician examples
- Ensuring diverse musical traditions reflect pupils' cultural backgrounds
- Recognising different musical values and practices globally

## Practical Classroom Strategies Summary

### Physical Environment

- Consistent seating arrangements supporting sensory needs
- Clear pathways for wheelchair users
- Acoustic considerations (damping, amplification)
- Visual supports displayed prominently
- Sensory regulation spaces if needed

### Lesson Structure

- Visual timetables and advance organisers
- Clear routines and transitions
- Balance of active and receptive activities
- Processing time built into questioning
- Flexible pacing responding to learner needs

### Behaviour Management

- Positive reinforcement specific to musical progress
- Recognition that SEND-related behaviours aren't defiance
- Consistent expectations with reasonable adjustments
- Alternative participation options preventing escalation
- Collaboration with pastoral and SEND teams

### Communication

- Clear, concise instructions
- Multi-modal delivery (verbal, visual, demonstration)
- Check understanding through practical demonstration
- Vocabulary pre-teaching
- Alternative communication modes validated

## Continuing Professional Development

Teachers should engage with:

- **SEND-specific music education training** (through Music Education Hubs, Drake Music)
- **General SEND CPD** (understanding conditions, strategies)
- **Collaboration with SEND specialists** within school
- **Research engagement** (Fautley, Green, Ockelford)
- **Professional networks** (Music Mark, NAME, ISM)

## Conclusion

SEND provision in music education represents both ethical imperative and pedagogical opportunity. When music teachers embrace inclusive practice—informed by research, legislation, and specialist knowledge—they enable all pupils to experience music's unique power to communicate, create, and connect. As Kodály asserted, music belongs to everyone; our responsibility is ensuring that promise becomes reality through thoughtful, informed, and committed inclusive practice.
