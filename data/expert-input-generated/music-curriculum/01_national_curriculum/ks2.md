# Music at Key Stage 2

## Overview of Key Stage 2 Music in the National Curriculum

Key Stage 2 (ages 7-11, Years 3-6) represents a crucial phase in music education where children build upon their Early Years and Key Stage 1 experiences to develop more sophisticated musical understanding and practical skills. The National Curriculum for Music in England (2013) outlines a compact but comprehensive programme of study that requires all pupils to develop competence in performing, composing, and listening/appraising.

The statutory requirements state that pupils should be taught to:

- Sing and play musically with increasing confidence and control
- Develop an understanding of musical composition, organising and manipulating ideas within musical structures and reproducing sounds from aural memory
- Listen with attention to detail and recall sounds with increasing aural memory
- Use and understand staff and other musical notations
- Appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music from different traditions and great composers and musicians
- Develop an understanding of the history of music

## Theoretical Foundations and Key Thinkers

### Keith Swanwick's Developmental Model

Keith Swanwick's developmental spiral (Swanwick and Tillman, 1986) remains highly influential in Key Stage 2 music education. His model identifies that children aged 7-11 typically progress through the 'Vernacular' stage (ages 4-9), characterised by conventional musical patterns and rhythmic coordination, into the 'Speculative' stage (ages 10-15), where experimentation and structural awareness emerge. Teachers should recognise these developmental patterns whilst acknowledging individual variation.

Swanwick's CLASP model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition, Skill acquisition, Performance) provides a balanced framework for curriculum planning at Key Stage 2, ensuring pupils experience music holistically rather than through isolated skill development.

### Martin Fautley's Perspectives on Assessment and Pedagogy

Martin Fautley's research on formative assessment in music education (Fautley, 2010, 'Assessment in Music Education') is particularly relevant to Key Stage 2 practice. He emphasises that music assessment should focus on musical learning rather than compliance, advocating for:

- Clear learning intentions shared with pupils in accessible language
- Success criteria co-constructed with children where appropriate
- Assessment integrated into learning activities rather than as separate events
- Recognition that musical understanding may be demonstrated through multiple modalities (performing, composing, listening, notating)

Fautley argues that the Key Stage 2 phase is critical for developing children's musical identity and self-efficacy, requiring careful, encouraging assessment practices that value progress over absolute attainment.

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Approaches

Lucy Green's work ('Music, Informal Learning and the School', 2008) has significantly influenced Key Stage 2 practice, particularly in upper Key Stage 2. Her research demonstrates that incorporating informal learning practices—such as learning by ear, peer-directed learning, and self-chosen music—can dramatically increase engagement and achievement.

In the Key Stage 2 context, this might include:

- Regular opportunities for pupils to learn songs and instrumental parts by ear
- Peer teaching and collaborative group work with minimal teacher intervention
- Allowing pupil choice in repertoire and musical styles
- Copying recordings and working out music independently

Green's approaches are particularly effective for engaging reluctant learners and those who don't identify with formal classical music traditions.

### Zoltán Kodály's Sequential Approach

The Kodály approach, though developed in Hungary, has been adapted extensively for UK Key Stage 2 teaching. Kodály emphasised:

- Sequential skill development based on developmental readiness
- Using the singing voice as the primary instrument
- Folk music from children's own culture as foundational repertoire
- Systematic development of inner hearing and musical literacy

At Key Stage 2, Kodály-inspired practice typically includes systematic introduction of sol-fa syllables (do, re, mi, etc.), rhythm names (ta, ti-ti), and progressive development of musical literacy through singing games and folk songs. The British Kodály Academy provides UK-specific training and resources aligned with the National Curriculum.

## Progression Across Key Stage 2

### Years 3 and 4 (Lower Key Stage 2)

**Performing Skills:**
Pupils should develop the ability to sing in tune with clear diction and appropriate expression. They should maintain their own part in simple two-part singing (partner songs, rounds, or call-and-response). Instrumental skills should progress to controlling dynamics, tempo, and timbre with increasing accuracy. The Charanga Musical School scheme, widely used across UK primary schools, typically introduces recorder or glockenspiel as class instruments at this stage.

**Composing Skills:**
Children should begin organising sounds into simple structures (e.g., ABA form, question-and-answer phrases). They should experiment with layering sounds and creating textures. Graphic scores and invented notations remain appropriate alongside introduction to standard notation. Gary Spruce ('Teaching Music in Secondary Schools', 2002) emphasises that composition at this stage should prioritise musical decision-making over technical complexity.

**Listening and Appraising:**
Pupils should identify repeated patterns (ostinati), recognise basic structures, and describe music using developing musical vocabulary (pitch, duration, dynamics, tempo, timbre, texture, structure). They should begin to place music in historical and cultural contexts, recognising that music serves different purposes across times and places.

### Years 5 and 6 (Upper Key Stage 2)

**Performing Skills:**
Pupils should sing increasingly challenging repertoire in two or more parts with confidence and musical expression. They should maintain independent parts in ensemble work and demonstrate more sophisticated control of phrasing, dynamics, and articulation. Many schools introduce band instruments or continue whole-class ensemble teaching (WCET) programmes established in Year 4, often supported by music hubs.

**Composing Skills:**
Compositions should demonstrate purposeful use of musical elements to create intended effects. Pupils should be able to develop musical ideas through repetition, contrast, and variation. They should compose in response to different stimuli (stories, images, emotions, cultural traditions) and use technology (e.g., GarageBand, Chrome Music Lab, Soundtrap) as compositional tools. Notation should include staff notation for simple melodies and rhythms.

**Listening and Appraising:**
Pupils should analyse how musical elements combine to create effects and express meaning. They should compare music from different times and places, understanding historical and cultural contexts. Repertoire should include Western classical music (composers such as Bach, Mozart, Beethoven, Elgar, Britten), music from the British Isles (folk traditions, sea shanties, national songs), and diverse global traditions (Gamelan, Indian classical, West African drumming, Caribbean music).

## Singing at Key Stage 2

### Pedagogy and Repertoire

The voice remains the most accessible and important instrument at Key Stage 2. Research by Welch ('The Developing Voice', 2006) demonstrates that children's singing continues to develop throughout this phase, with increasing vocal range (typically A below middle C to E above middle C by Year 6) and control.

Effective singing pedagogy includes:

- Daily or regular singing opportunities, not just in designated music lessons
- Vocal warm-ups addressing posture, breathing, and vocal health
- Repertoire spanning unison songs, rounds, partner songs, and two-part harmony
- Songs from diverse traditions and languages
- Integration with other curriculum areas (historical songs, number songs, language learning)

The **Sing Up** programme, established nationally in 2007 and continuing through regional delivery, provides extensive resources and CPD specifically for Key Stage 2 singing. Their 'Song Bank' offers thousands of songs organised by curriculum theme, singing technique, and cultural origin.

### Addressing the 'Out-of-Tune' Singer

By Key Stage 2, most children should sing tunefully, but some require continued support. Research by Rutkowski ('The Measurement and Evaluation of Children's Singing Voice Development', 1997) suggests a developmental continuum. Strategies include:

- Individual vocal exploration activities
- Use of sol-fa hand signs (Kodály approach) to visualise pitch relationships
- Singing in a comfortable range (avoid songs that sit too high)
- Small group work reducing self-consciousness
- Matching pitch activities using neutral syllables
- Never excluding children from singing opportunities

## Instrumental Teaching at Key Stage 2

### Whole Class Ensemble Teaching (WCET)

Since the Music Manifesto (2004) and subsequent National Plan for Music Education (2011), many schools deliver WCET programmes, often in Year 4. Supported by Music Education Hubs, these programmes provide free or subsidised instrumental tuition to whole classes, typically on orchestral instruments (strings, brass, woodwind) or world instruments (djembe, samba, ukulele).

The Model Music Curriculum (2021), published by the Department for Education as non-statutory guidance, suggests progression in instrumental skills including:

- Correct posture and instrument hold
- Sound production techniques specific to instrument families
- Reading simple notation appropriate to the instrument
- Ensemble skills (listening, watching conductor, maintaining pulse)
- Performing simple repertoire from memory and from notation

### Classroom Instruments

Untuned and tuned percussion remain essential resources at Key Stage 2:

- **Untuned percussion:** Developing precision in rhythm, ensemble skills, and timbral awareness
- **Tuned percussion (glockenspiels, xylophones, chime bars):** Accessible melody instruments for all pupils, particularly valuable for teaching staff notation
- **Ukuleles:** Increasingly popular as affordable, accessible instruments suitable for whole-class teaching
- **Recorders:** Traditional in UK primary schools, teaching breath control and notation reading, though sometimes criticised for limited musical appeal

Research by Hallam ('The Power of Music', 2015) demonstrates that regular instrumental teaching correlates with cognitive benefits, particularly in memory, attention, and executive function.

## Composition at Key Stage 2

### Compositional Frameworks

Gary Spruce emphasises that composition should be central to music education, not supplementary. At Key Stage 2, compositional activities should balance freedom and structure, providing frameworks that support creativity without constraining it.

Effective approaches include:

**Graphic Scores:** Particularly effective in lower Key Stage 2, allowing children to compose without notation barriers. Sounds can be represented through pictures, shapes, colours, and symbols.

**Structured Improvisations:** Using parameters such as limited pitch sets (e.g., pentatonic scales), specific structures (ABA), or extramusical stimuli (weather, journeys, emotions).

**Technology-Enhanced Composition:** Software such as Charanga, BandLab, GarageBand, and Chrome Music Lab enables sophisticated compositional work. The Model Music Curriculum acknowledges technology as both a compositional tool and a distinct area of study.

**Composition Portfolios:** Building collections of compositions across Key Stage 2 supports progression and metacognitive development. Fautley recommends audio or video recording of compositions to enable revisiting and reflection.

### Teaching Musical Elements Through Composition

Composition provides authentic contexts for understanding musical elements:

- **Pitch:** Creating melodies using limited pitch sets, scales, or modes
- **Duration:** Experimenting with different note values, polyrhythms, and tempo changes
- **Dynamics:** Using volume for expressive effect and structural clarity
- **Tempo:** Exploring how speed affects mood and character
- **Timbre:** Selecting instruments or sounds for specific effects
- **Texture:** Layering and combining sounds in different ways
- **Structure:** Organising musical ideas using repetition, contrast, and development

## Listening and Appraising

### Developing Active Listening

Listening at Key Stage 2 should be active, purposeful, and integrated with performing and composing. The National Curriculum requirement to "listen with attention to detail" implies developing specific listening skills rather than passive hearing.

Effective strategies include:

**Movement and Gesture:** Physical response to music (conducting, creating movements to represent structure) enhances engagement and understanding, particularly for kinaesthetic learners.

**Graphic Listening Scores:** Visual representations of music (listening maps) support focused attention on specific elements or structural features.

**Comparative Listening:** Comparing different performances, arrangements, or compositions develops critical thinking and refined discrimination.

**Historical and Cultural Context:** Learning about composers, historical periods, and cultural traditions enriches understanding and appreciation.

### Repertoire Selection

The National Curriculum specifies exposure to "a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians." Interpretation of "great" has evolved to encompass excellence across genres rather than exclusively Western classical canon.

Recommended repertoire might include:

**Western Classical:**
- Bach: Brandenburg Concertos, chorales
- Mozart: Eine Kleine Nachtmusik, operas
- Beethoven: Symphonies (particularly 5th, 6th, 9th)
- Vivaldi: The Four Seasons
- British composers: Elgar, Vaughan Williams, Holst, Britten, Judith Weir, Anna Meredith

**Traditional and Folk:**
- British folk songs and dances
- Sea shanties
- Celtic traditions
- Music hall and popular traditions

**World Music:**
- Indonesian Gamelan
- West African drumming (Ghana, Guinea)
- Indian classical (raga)
- Caribbean steel pan
- Aboriginal Australian didgeridoo traditions
- South American (samba, pan pipes)

**Contemporary and Popular:**
- Film music (John Williams, Hans Zimmer)
- Musical theatre
- Jazz and blues
- Contemporary composers (John Rutter, Karl Jenkins, Eric Whitacre)

## Musical Notation at Key Stage 2

### Progression in Notation

The National Curriculum requires that pupils "use and understand staff and other musical notations." This acknowledges both standard staff notation and alternative systems.

**Lower Key Stage 2:**
- Graphic scores and invented notations
- Standard notation symbols: stave, clef (treble), bar lines, note values (crotchet, minim, semibreve), rests
- Simple melodic notation on stave (five-note range)
- Rhythm grids and time signatures (2/4, 3/4, 4/4)

**Upper Key Stage 2:**
- Extended melodic range in treble clef
- Dotted rhythms, tied notes, quavers
- Basic dynamic markings (p, f, mp, mf, crescendo, diminuendo)
- Tempo markings
- Simple sharp and flat symbols
- Introduction to bass clef (particularly for instrumental learners)

### Alternative Notation Systems

**Sol-fa Notation:** Kodály-influenced practice uses letter names (d, r, m, s, l) representing scale degrees, supporting inner hearing development.

**Number Notation:** Particularly useful for tuned percussion and recorder, with numbers representing scale degrees or finger positions.

**Rhythm Names:** Kodály (ta, ti-ti) or French time names (ta-a, ta, ti-ti) provide verbalisation supporting rhythm reading and performance.

**Graphic Scores:** Continuing from Key Stage 1, supporting composition and contemporary music exploration.

Technology enables notation through apps such as Noteflight, MuseScore, and Sibelius First, though handwritten notation remains important for conceptual understanding.

## Cross-Curricular Links

Music at Key Stage 2 provides rich opportunities for curriculum integration, supporting the holistic primary curriculum approach:

**History:** Music from historical periods studied (Vikings, Romans, Tudors, Victorians), using music as historical evidence.

**Geography:** Music from countries and regions studied, understanding musical traditions and cultural identity.

**Mathematics:** Pattern recognition, fractions (note values), counting, symmetry, proportion.

**English:** Songwriting, analysing lyrics, poetry and music relationships, performance poetry.

**PE:** Dance-music integration, coordinated movement, rhythm and pulse.

**Languages:** Songs in foreign languages support pronunciation, vocabulary, and cultural understanding.

**Science:** Acoustics, sound waves, pitch and frequency, volume and amplitude.

**Computing:** Music technology, composition software, recording, digital manipulation.

The Model Music Curriculum (2021) emphasises these connections whilst warning against diluting music's distinct contribution to education.

## Assessment at Key Stage 2

### Principles of Effective Music Assessment

Martin Fautley's research emphasises that assessment in music should:

- Be formative rather than predominantly summative
- Recognise different modes of musical understanding
- Avoid over-reliance on written work or notation as evidence
- Value creative and interpretive qualities alongside technical accuracy
- Support pupils' developing musical identity positively

The National Curriculum provides non-statutory age-related expectations but does not prescribe levels. Schools typically develop assessment frameworks indicating whether pupils are working towards, at, or exceeding age-related expectations.

### Assessment Methods

**Performance Assessment:**
- Recording performances enables reflective listening and evidence collection
- Peer and self-assessment using success criteria
- Observation of technical skills, expression, ensemble contribution
- Recognition that performance anxiety affects some pupils

**Composition Assessment:**
- Audio/video recordings of compositions
- Annotation of scores or compositional processes
- Pupil commentary explaining choices
- Assessment of creative decision-making, not just technical correctness

**Listening Assessment:**
- Verbal discussion and questioning
- Written responses (where age-appropriate)
- Demonstration through movement or gesture
- Comparative analysis tasks
