# Music National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction

The Music National Curriculum in England provides the statutory framework for music education in maintained schools from Key Stage 1 through Key Stage 3. Established through the Education Act 2002 and most recently revised in 2013, it sets out the essential knowledge, skills and understanding that pupils should acquire during their compulsory education. Music is designated as a foundation subject, mandatory at Key Stages 1–3, whilst at Key Stage 4 it becomes optional, though schools must provide access to arts subjects as part of a broad and balanced curriculum.

The current National Curriculum framework emphasises performing, composing and listening as interrelated musical activities, reflecting what Keith Swanwick's research identified as fundamental modes of musical engagement. Swanwick's C(L)A(S)P model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition, Skill acquisition, and Performance) has significantly influenced curriculum design, though the statutory documentation adopts a simplified tripartite structure.

## Historical Context and Development

The inclusion of music as a foundation subject was established in the 1988 Education Reform Act. The curriculum has undergone several revisions: 1992, 1995, 2000, 2007, and most substantially in 2013. The 2013 revision, implemented from September 2014, marked a significant shift towards a more knowledge-focused curriculum with reduced prescription, granting schools greater autonomy in curriculum design whilst maintaining statutory requirements.

Martin Fautley has extensively critiqued curriculum developments, particularly noting in his work with Victoria Kinsella that the 2013 curriculum's brevity (approximately 200 words per key stage) creates both opportunities and challenges. Whilst permitting creative curriculum design, this brevity requires teachers to possess sophisticated pedagogical content knowledge to interpret and implement the framework effectively.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Statutory Requirements

Pupils should be taught to:
- Use their voices expressively and creatively by singing songs and speaking chants and rhymes
- Play tuned and untuned instruments musically
- Listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music
- Experiment with, create, select and combine sounds using the inter-related dimensions of music

### Pedagogical Considerations

At KS1, the emphasis is on experiential learning and exploration. Zoltán Kodály's principles remain influential here, particularly the use of folk songs, developmentally appropriate repertoire, and the integration of singing as the foundation of musical learning. The Kodály philosophy, adapted for the UK context by organisations such as the British Kodály Academy, emphasises that music education should begin with the voice, the most accessible instrument.

The 'inter-related dimensions of music' represent a crucial conceptual framework introduced in 2013: pitch, duration, dynamics, tempo, timbre, texture, and structure. These dimensions replace earlier terminology and provide a consistent vocabulary across key stages.

Lucy Green's research on informal learning, whilst primarily focused on secondary education, has implications for KS1 practice. Her emphasis on aural learning, peer-directed activity, and personally meaningful music suggests that even at this early stage, children's musical preferences and experiences outside school should inform classroom practice.

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Statutory Requirements

Pupils should be taught to:
- Play and perform in solo and ensemble contexts, using their voices and playing musical instruments with increasing accuracy, fluency, control and expression
- Improvise and compose music for a range of purposes using the inter-related dimensions of music
- Listen with attention to detail and recall sounds with increasing aural memory
- Use and understand staff and other musical notations
- Appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians
- Develop an understanding of the history of music

### Implementation and Progression

KS2 represents a critical phase for developing musical literacy and expanding creative capacities. The requirement for staff notation understanding marks a significant progression point, though the curriculum does not mandate specific notation systems. This allows schools to adopt various approaches including graphic notation, rhythm notation systems, or traditional staff notation.

Keith Swanwick and June Tillman's developmental spiral model (1986) remains influential in understanding progression at this stage. Their research identified that children aged 7-11 typically move through 'vernacular' and 'speculative' stages, characterised by developing personal expressiveness and experimentation with structural possibilities in composition.

The Model Music Curriculum (2021), a non-statutory framework published by the Department for Education and developed by a panel of experts including Nikki Merry and Natalie Kooij, provides detailed guidance for implementing these requirements. Whilst non-statutory, it offers sequenced learning objectives across year groups, suggesting repertoire and pedagogical approaches that many schools have adopted.

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### Statutory Requirements

Pupils should be taught to:
- Play and perform confidently in a range of solo and ensemble contexts using their voice, playing instruments musically, fluently and with accuracy and expression
- Improvise and compose; and extend and develop musical ideas by drawing on a range of musical structures, styles, genres and traditions
- Use staff and other relevant notations appropriately and accurately in a range of musical styles, genres and traditions
- Identify and use the inter-related dimensions of music expressively and with increasing sophistication, including use of tonalities, different types of scales and other musical devices
- Listen with increasing discrimination to a wide range of music from great composers and musicians
- Develop a deepening understanding of the music that they perform and to which they listen, and its history

### Critical Developments

KS3 represents the culmination of statutory music education for many pupils. Gary Spruce has argued extensively that this key stage faces particular challenges, noting in his 2017 work that music education at this level must balance preparation for GCSE study with providing meaningful musical experiences for the majority who will not continue with formal music education.

Martin Fautley's research on assessment in music education is particularly relevant at KS3. His work highlights that music's distinctive characteristics—its ephemerality, its existence across multiple domains (performance, composition, listening), and its subjective elements—require sophisticated assessment approaches. The National Curriculum's level descriptors were removed in 2013, requiring schools to develop their own progression models and assessment frameworks.

The introduction of 'tonalities' and 'different types of scales' at KS3 reflects an expectation that pupils will engage with music beyond Western tonal traditions. This aligns with research emphasising cultural diversity in music education, though implementation varies significantly between schools.

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

### Context

Music becomes optional at KS4, though the DfE's English Baccalaureate (EBacc) framework has created tensions, as music is not included in this performance measure. This has contributed to documented decline in KS4 music uptake, as analysed by researchers including Ally Daubney and Duncan Mackrill in their 2018 report for the Sussex University/University of Reading's 'Exchanging Notes' project.

### GCSE Qualifications

Four main awarding bodies offer GCSE Music:

**AQA Music (8271)**: Structured around three components: Composition (30%), Performance (30%), and Appraising (40%). The appraising component includes study of six Areas of Study spanning Western Classical, Rock and Pop, Film Music, and world music traditions.

**Edexcel Music (1MU0)**: Similarly structured with three components but with different weightings: Performance (30%), Composition (30%), and Appraising (40%). Includes four Areas of Study with prescribed works for detailed study.

**OCR Music (J536)**: Offers both 'Integrated Portfolio' (30% of composition and performance combined) and separate assessment components. Includes distinctive focus on free composition alongside composition to a brief.

**WJEC Music (C660)**: Wales-specific qualification with emphasis on performing, composing and appraising, incorporating Welsh language options.

All specifications reflect National Curriculum principles whilst adding examination-specific requirements including knowledge of musical elements, contexts, and theoretical understanding through written papers.

## Non-Statutory Guidance and Supporting Frameworks

### The Model Music Curriculum (2021)

Developed following the 2021 publication of the Department for Education's 'The importance of music: a national plan for music education', this guidance provides year-by-year programmes of study. It emphasises sequential skill development, systematic repertoire selection, and integration of performing, composing and listening activities.

### National Plan for Music Education (2022 Revision)

The refreshed National Plan, published in June 2022, established Music Hubs as central to curriculum delivery support. It specifies that every child should have opportunity to learn an instrument, sing regularly, and progress to the next level of excellence. The plan commits to funding and infrastructure supporting curriculum delivery, though implementation responsibility rests with schools.

## Theoretical Underpinnings

### Keith Swanwick's Developmental Theory

Swanwick's work remains foundational to understanding musical development in curriculum planning. His emphasis on music as a form of discourse, rather than merely skill acquisition, challenges reductive approaches. The developmental spiral model (1986) identifies eight modes of musical understanding from sensory response through to systematic and symbolic understanding, typically achieved through adolescence.

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Theory

Green's 2002 and 2008 publications on 'How Popular Musicians Learn' fundamentally challenged formal music education approaches. Her Musical Futures project, widely adopted across UK secondary schools, demonstrated that incorporating informal learning practices—particularly aural learning, peer-directed learning, and learner choice of repertoire—could re-engage pupils alienated by traditional approaches. Whilst not explicitly referenced in the National Curriculum, this pedagogy has significantly influenced KS3 and KS4 practice.

### Zoltán Kodály's Principles

The Kodály concept emphasises that music education should develop musical literacy through sequential, developmentally appropriate activities based on folk song. Whilst Kodály's Hungarian folk song repertoire requires cultural adaptation, his pedagogical principles—including the primacy of singing, use of solfa, and rhythm syllables—are extensively applied in UK primary schools.

### Martin Fautley on Assessment and Pedagogy

Fautley's extensive research on formative assessment in music has influenced how teachers implement curriculum requirements. His work distinguishes between assessment of composing (evaluating products) and assessment for composing (supporting compositional development), arguing that effective music education requires both. His collaboration with Swanwick and more recently with researchers including Regina Murphy has provided practical frameworks for implementing progression within the National Curriculum's structure.

## Inter-Related Dimensions of Music

The National Curriculum's adoption of 'inter-related dimensions' represents a conceptual shift from 'musical elements'. These dimensions are:

- **Pitch**: Highness or lowness of sound, including melody and harmony
- **Duration**: Length of sounds, including rhythm and pulse
- **Dynamics**: Volume, including changes in volume
- **Tempo**: Speed of the music
- **Timbre**: Quality or character of sound, 'tone colour'
- **Texture**: Layers within music, including melody and accompaniment relationships
- **Structure**: Organisation of music, including form and repetition

This framework provides consistency across key stages and facilitates progression in analytical understanding, though some music educators argue it oversimplifies musical understanding, particularly regarding expressive and cultural dimensions.

## Practical Implications for Curriculum Leaders

### Curriculum Design Autonomy

The 2013 National Curriculum's reduced prescription requires curriculum leaders to make sophisticated decisions about repertoire, teaching sequence, and skill development. Schools must ensure coverage of statutory requirements whilst creating coherent, progressive schemes of work. Many schools have adopted published schemes (e.g., Charanga, Kapow Primary, Music Express) or use the Model Music Curriculum as a starting framework.

### Resourcing and Specialist Teaching

Primary music education faces particular challenges regarding specialist teaching. The Musicians' Union and Incorporated Society of Musicians have highlighted that many primary teachers lack confidence in music teaching. Music Hubs provide some support, but curriculum delivery quality varies significantly. At secondary level, specialist teaching is normative, though recruitment challenges persist, as documented in the 2019 Department for Education Teacher Recruitment and Retention strategy.

### Integration with Wider Music Education

The National Curriculum represents only one dimension of children's musical education. Instrumental teaching (often provided by peripatetic teachers), ensemble participation, and out-of-school music learning all contribute to musical development. Effective curriculum leadership requires coordination between classroom music, instrumental teaching, and extra-curricular provision.

### Cultural Diversity and Inclusion

The National Curriculum's references to 'different traditions' and 'music drawn from different traditions' require thoughtful implementation. Gary Spruce's work emphasises that genuine cultural diversity in music education requires more than tokenistic inclusion of 'world music'. Teachers must engage substantively with musical traditions, understanding their cultural contexts and avoiding appropriation.

## Assessment Without Levels

Since 2013, schools have developed individualised assessment frameworks. Music education's distinctive characteristics require assessment approaches that accommodate:

- Multiple modes of engagement (performing, composing, listening)
- Both individual and collaborative work
- Process and product evaluation
- Subjective and objective elements

Many schools have adopted assessment frameworks based on musical behaviours or compositional devices rather than hierarchical levels, reflecting Martin Fautley's research advocating for assessment that supports learning rather than merely measuring attainment.

## Current Challenges and Future Directions

The National Plan for Music Education (2022) acknowledges several challenges:

- Declining uptake at GCSE level (down 19% between 2010 and 2020)
- Inequality of access, particularly regarding instrumental tuition
- Workforce capacity, especially in primary schools
- Funding pressures on Music Hub services

These challenges exist despite evidence, including from the 2019 'Music Education: State of the Nation' report by the All-Party Parliamentary Group for Music Education, demonstrating music education's broader educational benefits including improved literacy, numeracy, social skills, and wellbeing.

The future direction of music education policy remains subject to ongoing debate, with tensions between maintaining music as an entitlement for all children and pressures toward instrumentalist, outcome-focused education policy. The work of researchers including Martin Fautley, Lucy Green, and Gary Spruce continues to inform this debate, advocating for music education that values musical engagement for its intrinsic worth whilst demonstrating its broader educational value.

## Conclusion

The Music National Curriculum provides a foundational framework requiring sophisticated interpretation and implementation. Successful delivery depends on teachers' pedagogical content knowledge, adequate resourcing, and understanding of musical development. As Keith Swanwick argued throughout his career, effective music education engages pupils as musicians—performing, composing, and listening—rather than merely learning about music. This principle remains central to translating statutory requirements into meaningful musical experiences that develop pupils' musical understanding, skills, and lifelong engagement with music.
