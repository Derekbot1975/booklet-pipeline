# Music in EYFS

## Introduction and Statutory Framework

Music in the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) is a statutory requirement in England, embedded within the broader framework of early childhood education. The EYFS applies to children from birth to five years old, with particular focus on the Reception year (ages 4-5) as preparation for Key Stage 1. Music is primarily addressed through the 'Expressive Arts and Design' area of learning, though it permeates multiple areas of the EYFS framework.

The statutory framework for the EYFS (Department for Education, 2021) establishes that children should "safely use and explore a variety of materials, tools and techniques, experimenting with colour, design, texture, form and function" and "share their creations, explaining the process they have used". While not explicitly naming music throughout, the framework's Early Learning Goals (ELGs) for 'Creating with Materials' include musical creativity and exploration.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Developmental Perspectives

Keith Swanwick's developmental spiral model, though primarily focused on older children, has implications for EYFS practice. Swanwick and Tillman (1986) identified the early 'Mastery' and 'Imitation' modes (ages 0-4) where children explore sounds and begin to replicate musical patterns they hear. This research supports the EYFS emphasis on exploratory play and adult modelling.

The work of Zoltán Kodály, whilst developed in Hungary, has significantly influenced UK early years music education. Kodály emphasised that music education should begin as early as possible, ideally from birth, and that singing is fundamental to musical development. His pedagogical approach, using folk songs and pentatonic scales, aligns well with EYFS principles of using culturally relevant, accessible materials.

### Play-Based Learning and Music

Martin Fautley and colleagues have extensively researched the integration of music within early years settings. Fautley emphasises that musical learning in EYFS should be holistic, integrated, and play-based rather than formal or compartmentalised. His research (Fautley and Savage, 2007) argues that young children's musical development flourishes when music is embedded within their everyday activities and play experiences.

## The EYFS Framework and Musical Development

### Prime and Specific Areas of Learning

Music contributes significantly to all three Prime Areas and the Specific Areas of learning:

**Prime Areas:**
- **Communication and Language**: Singing nursery rhymes, action songs, and exploring vocal sounds develop phonological awareness, vocabulary, and listening skills
- **Physical Development**: Action songs, dance, and playing instruments develop gross and fine motor skills
- **Personal, Social and Emotional Development**: Group music-making builds collaboration, turn-taking, and emotional expression

**Specific Areas:**
- **Literacy**: Rhymes and songs support phonemic awareness, rhythm, and pattern recognition essential for reading
- **Mathematics**: Musical activities develop understanding of pattern, sequence, counting, and temporal concepts
- **Understanding the World**: Music from diverse cultures broadens children's experiences and cultural awareness
- **Expressive Arts and Design**: Music is centrally positioned here for creative expression and aesthetic development

### The Early Learning Goals for Music

The ELG for 'Creating with Materials' states that children should "safely use and explore a variety of materials, tools and techniques, experimenting with colour, design, texture, form and function." In musical terms, this translates to:

- Exploring instruments and sound-makers
- Experimenting with vocal sounds
- Creating simple compositions
- Responding to music through movement
- Sharing musical ideas with others

## Characteristics of Effective Teaching and Learning in Music

The EYFS emphasises three Characteristics of Effective Teaching and Learning that are particularly relevant to music education:

### Playing and Exploring

Musical play should involve:
- Free exploration of instruments and sound-makers
- Investigating how sounds can be changed (louder/quieter, faster/slower)
- Discovering different ways to make sounds
- Finding connections between sounds and emotions

Research by Susan Young (2003, 2009) has demonstrated that very young children engage in sophisticated musical play when given opportunities and appropriate resources. Young's work shows that children create their own musical narratives and sound explorations that constitute genuine musical thinking.

### Active Learning

In music, active learning includes:
- Sustained shared thinking during musical activities
- Children initiating their own musical explorations
- Concentration during listening activities
- Pride in musical achievements
- Resilience when learning new songs or techniques

### Creating and Thinking Critically

Musical activities that promote critical thinking:
- Making musical choices in composition
- Finding different solutions to musical problems
- Linking musical ideas together
- Developing strategies for remembering songs
- Making predictions about what sounds will be made

## Practical Approaches for EYFS Practitioners

### The Singing School

Regular singing should be central to EYFS provision. Research consistently demonstrates that singing benefits language development, social cohesion, and musical development. The Music Mark publication 'A Framework for Music Education' (2021) emphasises singing as foundational.

Practitioners should:
- Sing daily with children, both in planned sessions and spontaneously
- Build a repertoire of nursery rhymes, traditional songs, and contemporary material
- Use actions and props to support engagement
- Model expressive singing
- Adapt songs to children's interests and experiences

### Instrumental Exploration

Lucy Green's concept of informal learning, whilst developed for older learners, has principles applicable to EYFS. Green (2002, 2008) advocates for self-directed exploration and peer learning. In EYFS, this translates to:

- Providing continuous access to instruments in both indoor and outdoor environments
- Allowing unstructured exploration time
- Avoiding overly prescriptive activities that limit experimentation
- Creating 'music areas' where children can freely investigate sounds

### Listening and Responding

Active listening activities should include:
- A diverse range of musical styles and cultures
- Classical music (short extracts appropriate for young attention spans)
- Music from children's home cultures
- Live music whenever possible
- Movement and dance responses

### Integration Across the Curriculum

Music should not be isolated but woven throughout the EYFS day:
- Transition songs for routines
- Mathematical songs for counting and patterns
- Story songs linking to literacy work
- Outdoor music-making
- Music in role-play areas

## Progression in EYFS Music

### Birth to Three

The Development Matters guidance (2020) indicates that very young children:
- Turn towards familiar sounds
- Show interest in music
- Copy familiar actions and sounds
- Tap out simple rhythms
- Create movement in response to music

### Three and Four-Year-Olds

Children typically:
- Remember and sing entire songs
- Create their own songs
- Make up simple rhythms
- Explore different sound makers and instruments
- Listen with increased attention to sounds

### Reception Year (ELG)

By the end of Reception, children should:
- Sing a range of well-known nursery rhymes and songs
- Perform songs, rhymes, poems and stories with others
- Make simple compositions
- Remember and perform extended sequences
- Explore and engage with a wide range of media and materials

This progression should inform planning but must remain flexible and responsive to individual children's needs and interests.

## Assessment in EYFS Music

Assessment in EYFS is ongoing and observational, not formal. Musical assessment should focus on:

### Observation Opportunities

- Spontaneous musical play in continuous provision
- Engagement during adult-led musical activities
- Use of musical vocabulary
- Creative responses to music
- Social interactions during music-making

### Recording Evidence

Practitioners might document:
- Video and audio recordings of musical explorations
- Photographs of children engaged with instruments
- Transcriptions of children's own songs
- Annotations about musical interests and achievements

Martin Fautley's work on assessment emphasises that in early years, assessment should support learning rather than judge it. Formative assessment through observation enables practitioners to extend children's musical interests appropriately.

## Professional Development and Subject Knowledge

### Teacher Confidence

Research consistently identifies that many EYFS practitioners lack confidence in music (Hennessy, 2000; Stunell, 2006). Gary Spruce has written extensively about the need for professional development that addresses both practical musical skills and pedagogical understanding.

Effective professional development should include:
- Singing skills and repertoire building
- Understanding of child development in music
- Practical instrumental skills
- Confidence in improvisation and composition with young children
- Knowledge of diverse musical cultures

### Specialist Support

Schools and settings should consider:
- Partnerships with music education hubs
- Specialist music teachers working alongside EYFS practitioners
- Training from organisations like Youth Music or Music Mark
- Resources from the Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music (ABRSM) early years materials

## Inclusive Practice

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND)

Music is particularly valuable for children with SEND:
- Multi-sensory nature supports diverse learning needs
- Non-verbal communication opportunities
- Success experiences for all children
- Therapeutic benefits well-documented

Ockelford's (2000, 2013) research on music and children with learning disabilities demonstrates that music can be a primary mode of communication and learning for some children.

### Cultural Diversity

The EYFS framework requires practitioners to reflect children's home cultures. Musically, this means:
- Learning songs in children's home languages
- Incorporating instruments from various cultures
- Inviting families to share musical traditions
- Avoiding a Euro-centric repertoire
- Recognising and valuing all musical traditions equally

## Resources and Environment

### Indoor Provision

Music areas should include:
- Tuned percussion (xylophones, glockenspiels, chime bars)
- Untuned percussion (drums, shakers, tambourines, claves)
- Home-made instruments (shakers with rice, elastic-band guitars)
- Technology (simple recording devices, tablets with music apps)
- Visual prompts (song cards, pictures of instruments)

### Outdoor Provision

Outdoor music-making offers unique opportunities:
- Large-scale instruments (hanging pipes, outdoor xylophones)
- Natural sound-makers (sticks, stones, seed pods)
- Space for loud explorations
- Water sounds
- Den-building incorporating sound-making

### Technology

Appropriate technology use includes:
- Recording children's compositions and performances
- Apps that support composition (e.g., GarageBand for early years)
- Interactive musical stories
- Video recordings of diverse musical performances
- Careful use of backing tracks for singing

## Links to Key Stage 1

### Transition and Continuity

As children move from EYFS to Key Stage 1, continuity in music education is essential. The National Curriculum for Music at KS1 expects children to:
- Use their voices expressively and creatively
- Play tuned and untuned instruments musically
- Listen with concentration to a range of high-quality music
- Experiment with, create, select and combine sounds

These expectations build directly on EYFS experiences. Year 1 teachers should:
- Liaise with EYFS colleagues about children's musical experiences
- Maintain play-based approaches initially
- Continue informal music-making alongside more structured lessons
- Build on established repertoire and instrumental experiences

### The Model Music Curriculum

The Model Music Curriculum (Department for Education, 2021), whilst non-statutory, provides guidance bridging EYFS and KS1. It emphasises that musical foundations laid in EYFS should be built upon systematically, with singing, listening, and creating remaining central.

## Parental and Family Engagement

### Home Learning

Music is ideally suited to home-school partnerships:
- Sharing songs and rhymes for home practice
- Inviting families to contribute cultural music
- Suggesting everyday musical activities (kitchen percussion, singing while walking)
- Lending instruments for home exploration
- Celebrating musical achievements with families

### Community Links

Broader community engagement might include:
- Visits from musicians
- Trips to concerts or musical performances
- Partnerships with local music services
- Intergenerational music projects
- Community singing events

## Evidence Base and Research

Research demonstrates multiple benefits of high-quality music education in EYFS:

- **Cognitive Development**: Degé and Schwarzer (2011) found music training enhances phonological awareness and reading development
- **Social Development**: Kirschner and Tomasello (2010) demonstrated that joint music-making increases cooperation in young children
- **Language Development**: Anvari et al. (2002) found significant correlations between music skills and phonological awareness
- **Mathematics**: Spelke (2008) identified links between musical training and spatial-temporal reasoning

## Challenges and Solutions

### Common Barriers

EYFS settings commonly face:
- Limited practitioner confidence in music
- Insufficient instruments and resources
- Lack of time in busy schedules
- Uncertainty about progression in music
- Limited access to specialist support

### Strategic Solutions

Settings can address these through:
- Whole-staff training and collaborative planning
- Gradual resource acquisition, prioritising versatile instruments
- Integrating music throughout the day rather than as separate sessions
- Using the Development Matters guidance to plan progression
- Partnerships with local Music Education Hubs

## Conclusion

Music in EYFS is far more than entertainment or time-filler; it is a powerful vehicle for holistic development spanning communication, physical development, cognition, and social-emotional growth. When practitioners embrace music's potential, maintaining appropriate balance between adult-led and child-initiated experiences, they lay foundations for lifelong musical engagement and broader educational achievement. The evidence base, theoretical frameworks, and statutory requirements all converge to position music as essential within high-quality EYFS provision.
