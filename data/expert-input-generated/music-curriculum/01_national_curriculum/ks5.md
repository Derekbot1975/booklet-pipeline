# Music at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview of Key Stage 5 Music Provision

Key Stage 5 represents the final stage of statutory secondary education in England, Wales and Northern Ireland, typically covering students aged 16-18 in Years 12 and 13. Unlike Key Stages 1-4, Music is not a compulsory subject at KS5, making it an entirely elective qualification. Students choosing A Level Music generally demonstrate substantial prior attainment and commitment to musical study, though AS and A Level specifications are designed to accommodate students from diverse musical backgrounds and traditions.

The four principal awarding bodies offering Music qualifications at this level are AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. Each specification balances performing, composing, and appraising activities, though with varying emphases and structural approaches. All specifications align with Ofqual's reformed A Level requirements implemented from 2016, which emphasise greater academic rigour, linear assessment structures, and the removal of modularisation.

## Philosophical and Pedagogical Foundations

### Musical Understanding at Advanced Level

Keith Swanwick's developmental theory of musical understanding, articulated through his seminal work with June Tillman and later refined in *Musical Knowledge* (1994), provides valuable theoretical grounding for KS5 pedagogy. At this level, students should be operating primarily within Swanwick's later developmental stages: 'speculative' (ages 15+) and ideally moving toward 'symbolic' understanding, where music becomes a vehicle for exploring profound structural relationships and cultural meanings.

Lucy Green's research on informal learning pathways, particularly in *How Popular Musicians Learn* (2001) and *Music, Informal Learning and the School* (2008), has significant implications for KS5 teaching. Many students arriving at A Level have developed substantial musical competencies through informal means—garage bands, bedroom production, YouTube tutorials—that may not align with traditional conservatoire models. Green argues that effective teaching harnesses these informal learning practices whilst developing critical analytical frameworks. This is particularly relevant as contemporary A Level specifications increasingly accommodate popular music, jazz, and music technology alongside Western classical traditions.

Martin Fautley's work on assessment in music education, especially *Assessment in Music Education* (2010), emphasises that evaluation at KS5 must recognise the multi-dimensional nature of musical achievement. Fautley argues against simplistic, criterion-referenced approaches that fail to capture musical creativity's emergent and contextual nature. His research suggests that effective assessment at advanced levels requires sophisticated understanding of how technical competence, creative imagination, and stylistic awareness interact.

## Examination Specifications and Requirements

### AQA A Level Music (7272)

The AQA specification divides assessment into three components:

**Component 1: Appraising Music (40% of A Level, 2 hours 30 minutes)**
This written examination requires students to demonstrate knowledge of six Areas of Study, including Art Music c.1650-1910, Popular Music, and four optional areas selected by the centre. Students must study two set works in depth. The 2024-2026 cycle includes Mendelssohn's *Hebrides Overture* and Shostakovich's *Piano Concerto No. 2, Movement I* as compulsory set works. Questions assess listening skills, analytical understanding, contextual knowledge, and ability to make critical judgements.

**Component 2: Performance (35% of A Level)**
Students perform as soloist and/or ensemble member for a minimum of 10 minutes (A Level). Performances are internally assessed and externally moderated. AQA explicitly permits performance across diverse musical traditions—classical, jazz, rock, world music—recognising Gary Spruce's argument in *Debates in Music Teaching* (2013) that narrow stylistic gatekeeping undermines inclusive music education.

**Component 3: Composition (25% of A Level)**
Students submit two compositions: one free composition and one to a brief set by AQA, totalling at least 6 minutes. Compositions may employ conventional notation, graphic scores, sequencer screenshots, or lead sheets as appropriate to style and genre.

### Pearson Edexcel A Level Music (9MU0)

Edexcel's specification emphasises integrated musical study:

**Component 1: Performing (30%)**
A performance of one or more pieces, lasting 8 minutes minimum, demonstrating technical control, interpretative understanding, and stylistic awareness.

**Component 2: Composing (30%)**
Two compositions: one to a brief set by Edexcel and one free composition, combined duration minimum 6 minutes.

**Component 3: Appraising (40%)**
Written examination (2 hours 30 minutes) covering six Areas of Study, including Bach, late Romantic, and twentieth-century/contemporary music. Two set works change on a two-year cycle. 

### OCR A Level Music (H543)

OCR offers distinctive flexibility:

**Component 1: Performing or Composing (35%)**
Students choose either Performance or Composition as their first area of strength, submitting work totalling 10-12 minutes (performance) or 8-10 minutes (composition).

**Component 2: Composing or Performing (25%)**
The alternative practical component, reinforcing student choice in determining their assessment profile.

**Component 3: Listening and Appraising (40%)**
Students select three Areas of Study from six options. Questions include skeleton score completion, harmony analysis, and essay responses demonstrating contextual understanding.

### WJEC Eduqas A Level Music (A680QS)

**Component 1: Performing (35%)**
One solo and one ensemble performance, total minimum 10 minutes.

**Component 2: Composing (25%)**
Two contrasting compositions totalling minimum 6 minutes, one responding to a set brief incorporating specific techniques or devices.

**Component 3: Appraising (40%)**
Students study three Areas of Study: Western Classical Tradition, Rock and Pop, and Musical Theatre. Set works include pieces by Mozart, Queen, and Sondheim, demonstrating the specification's embrace of popular and commercial music forms.

## Curriculum Design and Pedagogical Approaches

### Structuring the Two-Year Course

Effective KS5 Music curriculum design requires careful sequencing that builds technical competence whilst developing independent musicianship. Research by Martin Fautley and Robin Lamont in *Creativity and Assessment in Music* (2020) suggests that successful A Level teaching avoids artificially separating performing, composing, and listening. Instead, pedagogical approaches should create coherent musical experiences where analytical understanding informs creative work, and creative exploration deepens analytical insight.

A typical Year 12 structure might prioritise:
- **Autumn Term**: Introduction to set works and key analytical techniques; establishment of performance repertoire; foundational composition projects exploring specified techniques
- **Spring Term**: Deeper engagement with selected Areas of Study; interim performance assessment; composition portfolio development
- **Summer Term**: Integration of learning; first complete mock examinations; extended composition projects

Year 13 typically involves:
- **Autumn Term**: Completing analytical coverage; advanced composition briefs; finalising performance repertoire
- **Spring Term**: Performance recordings; composition submissions; intensive examination preparation
- **Summer Term**: Final examinations

### Developing Analytical Skills

Analytical competence distinguishes A Level Music from GCSE study. Students must develop fluency with technical vocabulary encompassing harmony, tonality, structure, instrumentation, texture, and rhythmic organisation. However, as Swanwick argues in *Teaching Music Musically* (1999), analysis should never become an end in itself. Rather, analytical understanding should enhance musical appreciation and inform creative decision-making.

Effective analytical teaching at KS5 employs multiple approaches:
- **Score study**: Close reading of notated music, identifying structural features, harmonic progressions, and developmental techniques
- **Aural analysis**: Identifying musical features from recorded examples without scores
- **Comparative analysis**: Examining how different composers or performers approach similar musical challenges
- **Contextual analysis**: Understanding music within historical, cultural, and social frameworks

Lucy Green's concept of "inter-sonic meaning" (the inherent musical relationships within a piece) versus "delineated meaning" (the cultural and contextual associations) provides valuable analytical framework for KS5 students navigating diverse musical styles.

### Supporting Composition Development

Composition at A Level requires students to demonstrate sophisticated understanding of musical elements whilst developing individual creative voices. This dual requirement—technical competence and personal expression—creates particular pedagogical challenges. Fautley's research emphasises that effective composition teaching provides structured support whilst preserving creative agency.

Practical strategies include:
- **Compositional modelling**: Analysing exemplar compositions to identify effective techniques
- **Technical constraints**: Setting focused briefs that develop specific skills (e.g., "compose a 32-bar theme using compound melody technique")
- **Workshop culture**: Regular sharing of work-in-progress with peer feedback
- **Iterative development**: Encouraging revision and refinement rather than single-draft approaches
- **Technology integration**: Utilising notation software (Sibelius, Dorico), DAWs (Logic, Cubase, Ableton), and virtual instruments

Students composing in popular and commercial styles may employ different working methods—loop-based construction, sampling, multitrack recording—which are equally valid. Assessment criteria must recognise diverse creative processes, as Gary Spruce argues in his critique of Western classical hegemony in music education.

### Performance Development

Performance assessment at KS5 evaluates technical control, musical interpretation, and stylistic understanding. Students may perform as soloists, in ensembles, or combining both approaches. Unlike GCSE, where basic competence suffices, A Level performance requires sophisticated musical communication appropriate to degree-level entry or professional contexts.

Effective performance teaching at KS5 encompasses:
- **Technical development**: Systematic practice routines addressing specific technical challenges
- **Interpretative awareness**: Understanding performance conventions within specific styles and historical periods
- **Reflective practice**: Regular self-assessment and target-setting
- **Performing experience**: Frequent performance opportunities building confidence and stage presence
- **Recording skills**: Familiarity with recording processes and producing polished submissions

Teachers must accommodate diverse performance pathways. A student might perform Chopin nocturnes, jazz standards, death metal, or Carnatic classical music—each requiring distinct technical approaches and assessment criteria. The principle of "fitness for purpose" should guide assessment, evaluating whether performances demonstrate appropriate technical control, stylistic awareness, and expressive communication within their chosen idiom.

## Supporting Diverse Learners

### Widening Participation

Historical data demonstrates that A Level Music attracts disproportionately privileged cohorts. Students with private instrumental tuition, middle-class cultural capital, and access to instrument ownership are significantly overrepresented. This participation gap reflects broader equity issues within music education that researchers including Lucy Green, Pauline Adams, and Ally Daubney have extensively documented.

Effective strategies for widening participation include:
- **Instrumental loan schemes**: Providing free instrument access for KS5 students
- **Genre diversification**: Validating popular music, non-Western traditions, and music technology pathways
- **Financial support**: Subsidising examination fees, recordings, and performance opportunities
- **Flexible scheduling**: Accommodating students with part-time employment or caring responsibilities
- **Peer mentoring**: Establishing support networks reducing isolation

### Supporting Students with SEND

Music provision at KS5 must accommodate students with diverse special educational needs and disabilities. Access arrangements might include:
- **Extra time** for written examinations
- **Scribe or voice-recognition software** for students with physical disabilities
- **Modified instruments** enabling performance despite physical limitations
- **Alternative assessment formats** such as oral rather than written responses

The creative and practical nature of music assessment often provides opportunities for students with SEND to demonstrate capabilities that traditional academic examinations might obscure.

## Assessment and Moderation

### Internal Assessment Processes

Performance and composition components are internally assessed against published marking criteria. Teachers must develop robust assessment expertise, regularly engaging with:
- **Standardisation materials** provided by examination boards
- **Exemplar recordings and portfolios** demonstrating different grade boundaries
- **Moderation feedback** identifying assessment accuracy

Martin Fautley's research emphasises that reliable assessment in creative subjects requires multiple assessment episodes, clear evidence trails, and moderation processes ensuring national comparability. His concept of "assessment validity" questions whether assessment tasks genuinely measure intended learning outcomes or merely proxy indicators.

### External Moderation

Examination boards externally moderate internally assessed components to ensure national standards. Centres submit recordings, scores, and supporting documentation. Moderators may adjust centre marks if systematic over- or under-assessment is identified. Effective moderation preparation includes:
- **Clear documentation**: Comprehensive records evidencing student work
- **Technical quality**: High-quality recordings enabling accurate assessment
- **Annotation**: Detailed commentary explaining assessment decisions
- **Administrative accuracy**: Correct completion of all required documentation

## Progression Pathways

### Higher Education

A Level Music provides essential preparation for undergraduate music study. Universities typically require A Level Music (often at Grade B or above) for BMus degrees, though some institutions consider strong performers or composers without formal qualifications. Degree pathways include:
- **BMus (Performance)**: Conservatoire training for professional performers
- **BMus (Composition)**: Creative practice degrees
- **BA Music**: Academic study combining historical, analytical, and cultural approaches
- **Music Technology/Production**: Contemporary music-making with technological emphasis
- **Music Education**: Training for teaching and community music leadership
- **Combined honours**: Music with languages, sciences, or humanities

### Professional Pathways

The music industries offer diverse career opportunities:
- **Performance**: Orchestral players, soloists, session musicians, function bands
- **Composition**: Film/television scoring, game music, concert composition
- **Music education**: Classroom teaching, private tuition, workshop leadership
- **Music technology**: Recording engineers, producers, sound designers
- **Arts administration**: Concert promotion, artist management, venue management
- **Music therapy**: Clinical practice supporting health and wellbeing
- **Music journalism**: Criticism, broadcasting, podcasting

Research by Susan Hallam and Helena Gaunt demonstrates that music graduates develop transferable skills—creativity, collaboration, time management, communication—valued across employment sectors. Many music graduates pursue successful careers outside music-specific roles.

## Resources and Professional Development

### Essential Resources

Successful KS5 Music departments require:
- **Performance spaces**: Acoustically suitable rooms for practice and recording
- **Instruments**: Diverse inventory accommodating different musical pathways
- **Technology**: Computers with notation software, DAWs, and virtual instruments
- **Recordings**: Extensive library of recorded music spanning diverse styles and periods
- **Scores**: Collection of published scores including set works and repertoire exemplars
- **Reference materials**: Music dictionaries, theory textbooks, historical surveys

### Professional Development

KS5 Music teaching requires specialist expertise extending beyond QTS requirements. Recommended professional development includes:
- **Examination board training**: Specification updates and assessment standardisation
- **Subject associations**: Membership of MTA (Music Teachers' Association), NAME (National Association of Music Educators), or ISM (Incorporated Society of Musicians)
- **Specialist courses**: CPD addressing analytical techniques, composition pedagogy, or performance teaching
- **Conference attendance**: Annual conferences by MTA, NAME, or subject-specific organisations
- **Academic engagement**: Reading current research in music education journals

## Challenges and Contemporary Debates

### The Performance/Composition Balance

Ongoing debate concerns the relative weighting of performing and composing. Some argue that greater performance weighting better reflects most students' strengths and career aspirations. Others, including Martin Fautley, contend that composition develops deeper musical understanding and creative capabilities. Current specifications attempt compromise, though the 35%/25% typical split arguably privileges performance.

### Canon and Diversity

Lucy Green and Gary Spruce have extensively critiqued the Western classical canon's dominance in music education. Whilst contemporary A Level specifications increasingly embrace diverse musical traditions, the appraising component's content often remains weighted toward European art music. This raises questions about whose music "counts" as legitimate knowledge and whether specifications genuinely value diverse musical practices or merely tokenise them.

### Technology and Authenticity

Digital technology enables sophisticated music-making without traditional notational literacy or instrumental technique. This challenges assumptions about musical competence. Are sequenced compositions demonstrating harmonic and structural sophistication but minimal instrumental performance "less valid" than classically-trained piano pieces? These debates reflect broader tensions between traditional musicianship models and contemporary creative practices.

### Accessibility and Elitism

Despite efforts to broaden participation, A Level Music remains disproportionately accessed by privileged students. Structural barriers—instrument costs, private tuition requirements, limited genre diversity—restrict access. Addressing these inequities requires systemic changes extending beyond individual teacher practice to examination structures, funding models, and cultural assumptions about musical value.

## Conclusion

Music at Key Stage 5 represents the pinnacle of statutory music education, demanding sophisticated musical understanding encompassing performance, composition, and critical analysis. Effective provision recognises diverse musical pathways, balances technical rigour with creative freedom, and prepares students for varied higher education and professional destinations. Drawing on research by Fautley, Green, Swanwick, and others, contemporary KS5 Music teaching embraces pedagogical approaches that value informal learning, recognise diverse musical traditions, and develop both specialist expertise and transferable capabilities. As music industries evolve and musical practices diversify, A Level specifications and teaching approaches must continue adapting whilst maintaining standards and musical integrity.
