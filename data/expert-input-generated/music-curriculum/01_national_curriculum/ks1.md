# Music at Key Stage 1

## Overview of Key Stage 1 Music in the National Curriculum

The National Curriculum for Music in England, established under the Education Act 2011 and most recently updated in 2013, sets out the statutory requirements for music education at Key Stage 1 (pupils aged 5-7, Years 1 and 2). The programme of study emphasises that music is a universal language that embodies one of the highest forms of creativity, aiming to engage and inspire pupils to develop a love of music and their talent as musicians.

At Key Stage 1, the National Curriculum states that pupils should be taught to:
- Use their voices expressively and creatively by singing songs and speaking chants and rhymes
- Play tuned and untuned instruments musically
- Listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music
- Experiment with, create, select and combine sounds using the inter-related dimensions of music

These four statutory requirements form the foundation of all music teaching at this stage, establishing the pedagogical framework that continues through Key Stages 2 and 3.

## The Inter-Related Dimensions of Music

The National Curriculum specifically references the "inter-related dimensions of music" as fundamental elements that pupils must understand and manipulate. At Key Stage 1, these dimensions are introduced practically and experientially:

- **Pitch** (high and low sounds)
- **Duration** (long and short sounds, rhythm and pulse)
- **Dynamics** (loud and quiet)
- **Tempo** (fast and slow)
- **Timbre** (sound quality and texture)
- **Texture** (layers of sound)
- **Structure** (how music is organised)

Keith Swanwick's developmental spiral model (1994) suggests that musical development at this age is primarily at the 'sensory' and 'manipulative' stages, where children explore sounds for their immediate appeal and begin to gain control over instruments and their voices. This theoretical framework underpins much of the practical work expected at Key Stage 1, where tactile and immediate musical experiences are prioritised over abstract theoretical knowledge.

## Singing at Key Stage 1

### Vocal Development

Research by the British Voice Association and organisations such as Sing Up (established in 2007 as part of the National Singing Programme) emphasises that Key Stage 1 represents a critical period for vocal development. Children at this stage typically have a vocal range of approximately D4 to A5, though this varies considerably between individuals.

Martin Fautley and Regina Murphy (2015) in their work "Assessment in Music Education" note that singing at Key Stage 1 should focus on:
- Developing pitch-matching skills through call-and-response activities
- Building a repertoire of songs appropriate to the age range
- Encouraging confident solo and ensemble singing
- Exploring vocal expression through dynamics and timbre

### Practical Approaches

The Kodály approach, developed by Hungarian composer and educator Zoltán Kodály (1882-1967), has significant influence in UK primary music education. Key principles applicable to Key Stage 1 include:
- Using the pentatonic scale (initially solfa notes so, mi, la) as it is naturally accessible to young children
- Employing hand signs (Curwen hand signs) to represent pitch relationships
- Beginning with folk songs and nursery rhymes from the child's own culture
- Teaching rhythm through speech patterns before introducing notation

Teachers should select songs that:
- Have limited pitch ranges (five to six notes)
- Feature repetitive patterns and clear structures
- Connect to cross-curricular themes
- Represent diverse musical cultures and traditions

## Instrumental Work

### Untuned Percussion

At Key Stage 1, untuned percussion instruments form the backbone of instrumental work. These include:
- Claves, woodblocks and guiros (wood family)
- Tambourines, maracas and shakers (shaken instruments)
- Triangles, bells and cymbals (metal instruments)
- Hand drums and tambours (skin instruments)

Pupils should develop the ability to:
- Hold and play instruments with appropriate technique
- Maintain a steady pulse
- Play simple rhythmic patterns
- Respond to conducting gestures (start, stop, dynamics)
- Understand that different playing techniques produce different timbres

### Tuned Percussion

Introduction of tuned percussion at Key Stage 1 typically includes:
- Chime bars (individual pitched bars)
- Glockenspiels (soprano range is most appropriate)
- Xylophones (alto range)

Gary Spruce, in his work "Teaching Music in Secondary Schools" (2001), whilst focused on secondary education, establishes principles that originate in primary practice, emphasising that instrumental teaching must prioritise musical understanding over technical facility, particularly relevant in Key Stage 1 where the focus is on exploration rather than mastery.

### Classroom Organisation

Effective instrumental work at Key Stage 1 requires careful classroom management:
- Establish clear routines for distributing and collecting instruments
- Create "sound signals" for silence (e.g., raised hand, particular chime bar note)
- Organise instruments by timbre families to facilitate teaching about sound qualities
- Use "listening boxes" or "mystery sounds" to develop aural discrimination
- Implement turn-taking systems that ensure all pupils have instrumental experience

## Listening and Appraising

### Developing Listening Skills

The National Curriculum requirement to "listen with concentration and understanding" represents a significant cognitive demand for Key Stage 1 pupils. Research by Susan Hallam (Institute of Education, UCL) demonstrates that focused listening skills develop gradually and require structured teaching approaches.

Effective strategies include:
- Active listening tasks (e.g., counting how many times a pattern repeats)
- Movement responses to musical features (e.g., high/low, fast/slow)
- Visual representation through drawing or selection of images
- Comparative listening (same/different activities)
- Story-based listening where pupils follow a narrative in music

### Repertoire Selection

The 2013 National Curriculum explicitly states that pupils should experience "high-quality live and recorded music." At Key Stage 1, this should encompass:

**Western Classical Music**: Selected works by composers such as:
- Camille Saint-Saëns: "Carnival of the Animals"
- Sergei Prokofiev: "Peter and the Wolf"
- Benjamin Britten: "Young Person's Guide to the Orchestra"
- Edward Elgar: "Wand of Youth Suites"

**World Music**: Diverse traditions including:
- West African drumming (pulse and rhythm patterns)
- Indian classical music (raga and tala concepts)
- Caribbean steel pans (timbre and melody)
- Chinese traditional instruments (guzheng, erhu)

**Contemporary and Popular Music**: Age-appropriate examples that engage pupils' interest whilst maintaining educational value.

Lucy Green's influential research (2008) on informal learning in music emphasises the importance of including music that reflects pupils' own cultures and interests, increasing engagement and relevance. At Key Stage 1, this means incorporating music that children encounter in their daily lives alongside canonical classical works.

## Composition and Improvisation

### Creative Music-Making at Key Stage 1

The National Curriculum requirement to "experiment with, create, select and combine sounds" establishes composition as a core element of music education from the earliest stages. At Key Stage 1, composition activities are typically short, exploratory and closely guided by the teacher.

Swanwick and Tillman's (1986) developmental model identifies that children aged 5-7 are typically at levels 0-2 of musical development:
- **Sensory** (0-2 years normally, but relevant for initial exposure): exploring sounds for their immediate appeal
- **Manipulative** (3-4 years): controlling sounds and instruments
- **Personal Expressiveness** (4-9 years): using music to express personal emotions and ideas

### Practical Composition Activities

Appropriate composition tasks at Key Stage 1 include:

**Graphic Notation**: Creating visual representations of sounds using:
- Lines (high/low, long/short)
- Shapes (different colours or sizes representing dynamics or timbre)
- Pictures (representing programmatic ideas)

**Structured Improvisation**: Teacher-guided activities such as:
- Question-and-answer phrases using voices or instruments
- Completing unfinished melodies or rhythms
- Adding sound effects to stories or poems
- Creating soundscapes for different environments

**Group Composition**: Collaborative creation where pupils:
- Select instruments for particular effects
- Decide on order and structure
- Agree on dynamics and tempo
- Perform their creation to others

Martin Fautley's research (2005) on composing in the classroom emphasises that at Key Stage 1, the process of composition is more important than the product. Teachers should focus on pupils' decision-making, experimentation and refinement of ideas rather than expecting polished performances.

## Cross-Curricular Links

### Music Across the Curriculum

The National Curriculum frameworks for all subjects encourage schools to make meaningful connections between disciplines. Music at Key Stage 1 naturally connects with:

**Literacy**: 
- Exploring rhythm through poetry and rhyme
- Creating soundscapes for stories
- Learning songs that reinforce phonics patterns
- Understanding that written music is a form of communication

**Mathematics**:
- Counting beats and measures
- Recognising patterns and sequences
- Understanding fractions through note durations
- Exploring shapes in sound (ascending/descending patterns)

**Physical Education**:
- Moving to music with control and coordination
- Understanding pulse through movement
- Exploring dynamics through gesture
- Creating dance sequences to musical structures

**History and Geography**:
- Studying music from different periods
- Exploring songs and music from various cultures
- Understanding how music reflects time and place

### PSHE and Social Development

Music education at Key Stage 1 significantly contributes to personal, social and emotional development:
- Building confidence through performance
- Developing cooperation through ensemble work
- Respecting others' contributions and ideas
- Experiencing achievement and progression
- Understanding cultural diversity through varied repertoire

## Assessment at Key Stage 1

### Formative Assessment

Assessment at Key Stage 1 should be primarily formative, focusing on pupils' progress rather than attainment levels. The removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014 requires schools to develop their own assessment frameworks, though these must demonstrate progression.

Martin Fautley and Robin Harrison's work "Developing Understanding of Musical Assessment" (2020) emphasises that effective music assessment at Key Stage 1 should:
- Focus on what pupils can do rather than deficits
- Use musical terminology appropriately
- Consider the context of the musical activity
- Value creativity and individuality
- Recognise progress in small increments

### Observable Musical Behaviours

Teachers can assess progression through observation of:

**Singing**:
- Increasing pitch accuracy
- Growing repertoire of songs
- Developing expression and phrasing
- Contributing confidently to group singing

**Instrumental Work**:
- Improved control and technique
- Ability to maintain pulse
- Playing with others in time
- Creating and repeating patterns

**Listening**:
- Sustained concentration
- Accurate identification of musical elements
- Appropriate use of musical vocabulary
- Making connections between pieces

**Composing**:
- Intentional use of musical elements
- Refinement of initial ideas
- Use of graphic notation
- Explanation of musical choices

### Recording Progress

Schools typically record Key Stage 1 music progress through:
- Photographic and video evidence
- Audio recordings
- Samples of graphic notation
- Teacher observation notes
- Pupil self-assessment (age-appropriate formats)

## Inclusive Practice

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities

Music education at Key Stage 1 should be accessible to all pupils, including those with SEND. The Equality Act 2010 requires reasonable adjustments to ensure participation.

Adaptations might include:
- **Visual impairment**: Use of tactile notation, larger notation, instruments with clear tonal differences
- **Hearing impairment**: Focus on vibration and rhythm, use of visual cues, British Sign Language songs
- **Physical disabilities**: Adapted instruments, use of technology, alternative performance methods
- **Autism Spectrum Condition**: Clear structures, visual timetables, preparation for changes, noise-reducing headphones available
- **Communication difficulties**: Non-verbal musical activities, Makaton signing with songs, alternative communication devices

Research by Adam Ockelford at the Roehampton University demonstrates that music can be particularly effective for pupils with profound and multiple learning difficulties, offering modes of communication and expression that transcend verbal language.

### English as an Additional Language

Music provides unique opportunities for pupils with EAL:
- Songs and rhymes support language acquisition
- Musical activities reduce dependence on verbal language
- Shared cultural music validates pupils' backgrounds
- Rhythm and melody aid memory of new vocabulary

## Resources and Training

### Essential Resources for Key Stage 1 Music

Schools should ensure adequate provision of:
- Sufficient untuned percussion for class sets
- Range of tuned percussion instruments
- Audio playback equipment with good quality sound
- Age-appropriate music stands and storage
- Resource books with song collections and lesson plans

### Professional Development

The Incorporated Society of Musicians (ISM) and Music Mark (the UK Association for Music Education) provide professional development for primary music teaching. Key areas for Key Stage 1 teachers include:
- Vocal warm-ups and singing technique
- Instrumental techniques and classroom management
- Composition pedagogy
- Assessment practices
- Repertoire knowledge

Many teachers at Key Stage 1 are generalists rather than music specialists. The National Plan for Music Education (2011, refreshed 2022) recognises this and emphasises the importance of:
- Access to music hubs for support and training
- Partnership with specialist music teachers
- Continuing professional development opportunities
- Resource sharing and collaboration

## Planning and Progression

### Long-Term Planning

Schools typically plan music at Key Stage 1 ensuring coverage of all National Curriculum requirements across Years 1 and 2. Common approaches include:

**Cyclical Planning**: Revisiting all musical activities (singing, playing, listening, composing) each half-term with increasing complexity.

**Blocked Units**: Teaching specific musical concepts or projects in concentrated periods (e.g., 6-week units on rhythm, pitch, or particular musical styles).

**Integrated Approaches**: Combining musical learning with topic work or other curriculum areas.

### Progression from EYFS to Key Stage 1

The Early Years Foundation Stage Framework includes "Expressive Arts and Design" which forms the foundation for Key Stage 1 music. Progression involves:
- Moving from spontaneous to structured musical activities
- Developing from individual exploration to collaborative ensemble work
- Progressing from free experimentation to controlled use of musical elements
- Transitioning from entirely practical work toward simple symbolic representation

### Looking Ahead to Key Stage 2

Key Stage 1 establishes foundations for Key Stage 2, where the National Curriculum expectations increase to include:
- Staff notation (preparation through iconic and graphic notation at KS1)
- Performing in multiple parts (developing from unison work)
- Improvisation and composition with increased independence
- Historical and cultural understanding of music

## Contemporary Challenges and Opportunities

### Digital Technology

While not explicitly mentioned in the 2013 National Curriculum, technology offers significant opportunities for Key Stage 1 music:
- Simple music composition apps (e.g., GarageBand, Chrome Music Lab)
- Interactive listening resources
- Recording and playback for self-assessment
- Access to diverse musical repertoire

However, technology should supplement rather than replace practical music-making at this stage.

### Time Allocation

The National Curriculum does not specify time allocations for foundation subjects, leading to considerable variation. Music Mark recommends at least one hour per week for music at Key Stage 1, though many schools provide less. Research by Ofsted (2021) in their "Music Subject Research Review" highlighted that insufficient curriculum time remains a barrier to high-quality music education.

### The Role of Music Hubs

The 2011 National Plan for Music Education established Music Education Hubs across England, providing:
- Instrumental teaching (though primarily at Key Stage 2)
- Singing programmes (e.g., Sing Up)
- Professional development for teachers
- Resources and scheme of work support
- Partnership opportunities with professional musicians

Key Stage 1 teachers should engage with their local Music Hub to access these resources and opportunities.

## Conclusion

Music at Key Stage 1 establishes the foundations for lifelong musical engagement and learning. Effective teaching at this stage combines practical music-making with systematic development of musical understanding, drawing on established pedagogical approaches from educators like Kodály whilst remaining responsive to pupils' interests and contemporary contexts. Teachers must balance the four core requirements of the National Curriculum—singing, playing, listening and composing—whilst ensuring inclusive, engaging and progressive musical experiences for all pupils. Through careful planning, appropriate assessment and continued professional development, Key Stage 1 teachers can inspire the next generation of musicians, listeners and music enthusiasts.
