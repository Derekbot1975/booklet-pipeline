# Zoltan Kodaly

**Full Name:** Zoltán Kodály (1882-1967)

**Known For:** Developing the Kodály Method (or Kodály Concept); pioneering a singing-based approach to music education; advocating for folk music as the foundation of musical literacy; creating a sequential, developmentally appropriate music curriculum; championing the use of solfa (tonic sol-fa) and hand signs in music education.

**Historical Significance:** Hungarian composer, ethnomusicologist, educator, and philosopher whose pedagogical principles have profoundly influenced music education in the UK and internationally, particularly from the 1960s onwards.

---

## Biography and Career History

Zoltán Kodály was born on 16 December 1882 in Kecskemét, Hungary. He demonstrated musical ability from childhood, learning violin, piano, viola, and cello. He studied composition at the Franz Liszt Academy of Music in Budapest (1900-1904) and later earned a PhD in linguistics from Eötvös Loránd University in 1906, writing his dissertation on Hungarian folk song.

From 1905 onwards, Kodály collaborated extensively with Béla Bartók in collecting and studying Hungarian folk music, travelling throughout rural Hungary to record thousands of folk songs using phonograph cylinders. This ethnomusicological work profoundly shaped his educational philosophy, convincing him that authentic folk music provided the ideal foundation for developing musical literacy and cultural identity.

Kodály taught composition at the Franz Liszt Academy from 1907 until 1942, becoming deputy director in 1919. However, his most significant contribution lay not in his own compositional output (though substantial) but in his revolutionary approach to music education, which he developed and refined from the 1920s until his death in 1967.

His educational philosophy crystallised following his visit to England in 1927, where he observed John Curwen's tonic sol-fa method in use. This experience, combined with his research into folk music and his observations of poor musical literacy among Hungarian children, led him to advocate for comprehensive reform of music education in Hungary. By the 1940s and 1950s, his methods were being implemented systematically in Hungarian schools, producing remarkable results that attracted international attention.

Kodály received numerous honours, including the UNESCO International Music Prize (1960) and honorary doctorates from universities worldwide. He died in Budapest on 6 March 1967, leaving a legacy that continues to shape music education globally.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Music Education for All Children

Kodály believed passionately that music education was a fundamental right of every child, not a privilege reserved for the talented few. He famously stated: 'Music is the manifestation of the human spirit, similar to language. Its greatest practitioners have conveyed to mankind things not possible to say in any other language.' He argued that musical literacy should be as universal as verbal literacy, advocating for daily music lessons in schools taught by specialist music teachers. This democratic vision positioned music as essential to human development rather than as an optional extra or entertainment.

### 2. Singing as the Foundation

Kodály identified the human voice as the most accessible and natural musical instrument, declaring 'The voice is the only instrument available to everyone' and 'Let us take our start from the instrument that is the common property of all, the singing voice.' He argued that singing should form the absolute foundation of music education because it requires no financial investment, is always available, and provides direct experience of musical elements. This singing-based approach meant that instrumental teaching should follow, not precede, vocal training, ensuring all children could develop musical understanding regardless of socioeconomic background.

### 3. Folk Music as Mother Tongue

Central to Kodály's philosophy was the belief that a nation's folk music constitutes its musical 'mother tongue' and provides the most appropriate material for early music education. He wrote extensively about how children should first encounter music from their own cultural heritage—simple, authentic folk songs that have survived through oral tradition because of their inherent musical value and appropriateness for children. He argued that these songs, refined through generations, naturally contain the pentatonic scales, simple rhythms, and melodic patterns most suitable for young children. Only after establishing this foundation should children encounter art music and music from other cultures. In the UK context, this principle has been adapted to include British folk song traditions, nursery rhymes, and folk music from diverse communities.

### 4. Sequential, Developmentally Appropriate Curriculum

Kodály advocated for a carefully sequenced curriculum that moves from simple to complex, following children's natural developmental stages. Musical concepts should be experienced before being labelled, and new material should always build upon secure prior learning. This sequential approach means introducing rhythmic and melodic elements in a specific order (for example, teaching sol-mi before adding la, then do and re), with each element thoroughly explored through singing, reading, writing, and creating before introducing the next. This contrasts sharply with approaches that expose children to all musical elements simultaneously without systematic progression.

### 5. Tonic Sol-fa and Hand Signs

Kodály championed the use of tonic sol-fa (a system developed by Sarah Glover and popularised by John Curwen in 19th-century England) as a tool for developing relative pitch and musical literacy. In this system, the degrees of the major scale are named do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti, do, with the 'do' always representing the tonic (key note) regardless of absolute pitch. This is combined with Curwen-Kodály hand signs—physical gestures representing each pitch—which provide a kinaesthetic dimension to pitch learning. The hand signs help children internalise pitch relationships spatially and physically before reading from staff notation. This relative pitch approach, Kodály argued, develops more secure musical understanding than systems based solely on absolute pitch or fixed-do systems.

### 6. Inner Hearing and Musical Literacy

A distinctive feature of Kodály pedagogy is its emphasis on developing 'inner hearing'—the ability to hear music in one's mind without external sound. Through exercises in reading rhythm and melody silently before performing them, children develop the same kind of cognitive processing that fluent readers use with text. Kodály believed this inner hearing was essential to true musical literacy and created numerous exercises specifically designed to develop it, including rhythm and melodic dictation, sight-singing, and memory exercises.

### 7. Quality Musical Material

Kodály was uncompromising in his insistence that children should encounter only the highest quality musical material. He was highly critical of the poor-quality songs, with awkward text settings and banal melodies, that he found in many school music textbooks. He argued that 'It is much more important who the music teacher is than what building they teach in' and that 'Only the best is good enough for children.' This principle extends to folk song collection, where only authentic, well-constructed folk songs should be used, not simplified or corrupted versions.

### 8. Music Literacy Through Rhythm and Solfa Syllables

Before introducing staff notation, Kodály advocated teaching rhythm through duration syllables (in the UK context, often using 'ta' for crotchets and 'ti-ti' for quavers, though various systems exist) and pitch through solfa. Children first learn to read and write using stick notation for rhythm and solfa letter names or hand signs for pitch. This approach means children can be musically literate—reading, writing, and creating music—long before mastering the complexities of staff notation, which is introduced gradually once rhythmic and melodic concepts are secure.

## Key Publications

### Works by Kodály

Kodály's writings on music education were extensive, though many were originally published in Hungarian and required translation to reach English-speaking audiences.

**'Children's Choruses' (1929-1955):** A collection of children's choral works composed specifically for educational purposes, demonstrating his approach to repertoire suitable for young voices.

**'Fifty-Five Two-Part Exercises' (1954):** Progressive two-part singing exercises demonstrating his pedagogical sequencing and emphasis on part-singing.

**'Pentatonic Music' (multiple volumes, 1940s-1960s):** Collections of songs and exercises based on pentatonic scales, reflecting his belief in beginning music education with these simpler scale structures.

**'The Selected Writings of Zoltán Kodály' (1974):** Edited by Ferenc Bónis, translated by Lili Halápy and Fred Macnicol, published by Boosey & Hawkes. This crucial collection brought together Kodály's essays on music education, folk music, and cultural philosophy in English. Key essays include 'Music in Kindergarten' (1941), 'Children's Choirs' (1929), and 'Folk Song in Pedagogy' (1941).

**'Let Us Sing Correctly' (1941):** Kodály's manual for teaching singing to children, emphasising proper vocal technique from the earliest stages.

### Influential Works About Kodály Method in the UK Context

**'Kodály in the Infant School' (1974)** by Cecilia Vajda: One of the earliest UK publications applying Kodály principles to British primary education, published by Boosey & Hawkes.

**'The Kodály Method: Comprehensive Music Education from Infant to Adult' (1973)** by Lois Choksy: Though American, this became widely used in UK teacher training, providing systematic explanation of the Kodály approach. Published by Prentice Hall.

**'The Kodály Context: Creating an Environment for Musical Learning' (1981)** by Lois Choksy: Further developing practical applications for teachers, this became a standard text in UK music education courses.

**'Kodály in the Primary School' (1979)** by Cecilia Vajda: Building on her earlier work, this addressed implementation across the full primary age range in the UK system.

**'Music Education in Hungary' (1966)** by Geoffrey Russell-Smith: An early English-language account of the Hungarian system, alerting UK educators to Kodály's achievements.

**'Developmental and Practical Approaches to Music Education' (various editions from 1980s)** by the British Kodály Academy: Training materials specifically adapted for UK contexts, incorporating British folk song repertoire.

## Influence on UK Curriculum and Policy

### Historical Introduction to the UK

Kodály's influence on UK music education began in earnest during the 1960s and 1970s, following publication of research demonstrating the remarkable musical literacy achieved by Hungarian children. British music educators, including Geoffrey Russell-Smith and Arnold Bentley, visited Hungarian schools and returned advocating for adoption of Kodály principles.

The British Kodály Academy (BKA) was established in 1975 by Cecilia Vajda and others to promote Kodály pedagogy adapted for UK contexts. This organisation has provided continuous professional development for teachers and advocated for singing-based, sequential music education in policy discussions.

### Influence on National Curriculum

When the National Curriculum for Music was introduced in 1992 (implemented 1995), several Kodály principles were evident, though not always explicitly attributed:

**Singing as Core Activity:** The 1992 and subsequent iterations of the National Curriculum established singing as a central component of music education at all key stages, reflecting Kodály's emphasis on the voice as the primary instrument.

**Sequential Progression:** The curriculum's organisation into progressive levels of attainment reflected Kodály's insistence on developmental sequencing, moving from simple to complex musical concepts.

**Folk Music and Cultural Heritage:** Requirements to explore music from different times and cultures, including British musical traditions, echo Kodály's emphasis on folk music as foundational material, though implementation has varied.

### National Curriculum 2014 Onwards

The 2014 National Curriculum for Music in England, whilst more concise than previous versions, retained key Kodály-influenced principles:

- Expectation that pupils 'sing in tune with clear diction, control and expression' (Key Stage 1 and 2)
- Progression from listening and responding to creating and composing
- Development of musical notation understanding (though specific systems not mandated)
- Emphasis on musical vocabulary and literacy

However, the 2014 curriculum's reduced prescriptiveness meant that Kodály-based approaches compete with other pedagogies, and implementation depends heavily on individual schools' and teachers' choices.

### Influence on Initial Teacher Training

Kodály principles have significantly influenced music education modules in UK Initial Teacher Training (ITT) programmes, particularly for primary generalist teachers. Many PGCE and undergraduate teacher training courses include introduction to solfa, singing games, folk songs, and sequential approaches derived from Kodály pedagogy. The British Kodály Academy's summer courses have trained thousands of UK teachers since the 1970s.

### Model Music Curriculum (2021)

The Model Music Curriculum, published by the Department for Education in 2021 to provide optional guidance for schools in England, shows considerable Kodály influence:

- Strong emphasis on singing at all key stages
- Sequential introduction of musical elements
- Use of graphic and then staff notation
- Recommendation of singing games and folk songs
- Progressive development of pitch and rhythm literacy
- Though not prescriptively Kodály-based, many suggested activities and progressions reflect his principles

### Ofsted and Inspection Frameworks

Ofsted's evolving inspection frameworks, particularly the emphasis on 'curriculum intent, implementation, and impact' introduced in the 2019 Education Inspection Framework, have indirectly supported Kodály approaches. The framework's focus on progressive, sequential curriculum design aligns well with Kodály's developmental principles, and inspectors increasingly recognise the value of systematic, singing-based approaches in achieving musical outcomes.

## Pedagogical Approach

### The Kodály Concept in Practice

It is important to note that Kodály himself never prescribed a rigid 'method' but rather articulated principles that teachers should adapt to their contexts. What is often called the 'Kodály Method' is more accurately termed the 'Kodály Concept' or 'Kodály Approach'—a philosophy of music education rather than a fixed instructional system.

### Core Pedagogical Principles

#### Preparation-Presentation-Practice

The Kodály approach employs a three-stage learning sequence:

**Preparation:** Children experience a musical concept through singing songs and engaging in musical activities without labelling or conscious awareness of the specific element being prepared. Multiple experiences over weeks or months build aural familiarity.

**Presentation:** Once children can perform the concept confidently (even if unconsciously), the teacher helps them identify, label, and understand it. This might involve comparing known songs, discovering what they have in common, and learning the appropriate musical terminology or notation.

**Practice:** After presentation, children practise the newly conscious concept through reading, writing, creating, and performing in varied contexts, building fluency and mastery before the next concept is introduced.

This sequence ensures children always work from sound to symbol, from experience to understanding, reversing the traditional approach of explanation followed by practice.

### Lesson Structure

A typical Kodály-inspired lesson in UK contexts might include:

1. **Vocal warm-ups and exercises:** Using solfa patterns, hand signs, and singing games to prepare the voice and focus attention
2. **Review of known songs:** Reinforcing previously learned material and providing contexts for musical concepts
3. **Preparation or practice activities:** Either preparing new concepts through singing and movement or practising recently presented concepts
4. **Presentation (when appropriate):** Conscious teaching of a new musical concept previously prepared
5. **Part-singing and musicianship:** Developing harmonic awareness through rounds, partner songs, and simple two-part songs
6. **Literacy activities:** Reading and writing rhythm and melody using stick notation, solfa, and progressing to staff notation
7. **Creating and improvising:** Using learned musical elements to create original rhythms, melodies, or accompaniments

### Pedagogical Tools

#### Hand Signs

The Curwen-Kodály hand signs provide physical representation of pitch relationships:
- **Do:** Fist at waist level (tonic, 'home' note)
- **Re:** Flat hand, palm down, slightly higher
- **Mi:** Flat hand, palm down, waist level
- **Fa:** Thumb down
- **Sol:** Flat hand, palm down, chest level
- **La:** Flat hand, palm tilted down, shoulder level
- **Ti:** Pointing finger upward
- **High Do:** Fist at head level

These signs, always used in relative pitch space (low notes low, high notes high), help children internalise melodic contour and interval relationships kinaesthetically.

#### Rhythm Syllables

Various rhythm syllable systems are used in UK Kodály teaching, including:
- **Ta** (crotchet), **Ti-ti** (quavers), **Ta-a** (minim), with variants for other durations
- Alternatively, some UK teachers use **Ta** (crotchet), **Te-te** (quavers), **Too** (minim)
- Gordon's rhythm syllables are sometimes integrated in hybrid approaches

The key principle is consistent, system-based naming that allows children to decode and encode rhythm patterns.

#### Stick Notation

Before staff notation, children read and write rhythm using simple vertical lines (sticks), with flags or beams for shorter durations. Similarly, melodic contour can be represented with high and low placement of notes or solfa letters before introducing the five-line staff.
