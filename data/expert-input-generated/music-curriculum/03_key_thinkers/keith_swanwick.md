# Keith Swanwick

**Full Name:** Charles Keith Swanwick (1937–2024)

**Former Roles:** Emeritus Professor of Music Education, UCL Institute of Education (IOE); Dean of Research, Institute of Education, University of London

**Known For:** The CLASP model of music education; developmental spiral theory of musical understanding; advocacy for music as a liberal art in the curriculum; critiques of utilitarian justifications for music education

---

## Biography and Career History

Keith Swanwick was born in 1937 and became one of the most influential figures in British music education during the latter half of the twentieth century and early twenty-first century. He passed away in 2024, leaving an enduring legacy in music pedagogy worldwide.

Swanwick began his career as a classroom music teacher before transitioning into higher education. He served as a Lecturer in Music Education at the University of London Institute of Education (now UCL Institute of Education) from 1973, eventually becoming Professor of Music Education and later Dean of Research. His work at the IOE positioned him at the heart of curriculum development and educational policy discourse in the UK during critical periods of educational reform.

Throughout his career, Swanwick was deeply concerned with establishing music as a serious educational discipline with its own integrity, resisting what he termed 'instrumental' justifications that valued music primarily for its supposed benefits to other areas of learning or social development. He argued passionately that music education should be valued for developing musical understanding itself.

Swanwick's academic work combined philosophical inquiry with practical pedagogy, drawing on psychology, aesthetics, and curriculum theory. He was influenced by the developmental psychology of Piaget, the aesthetic philosophy of Susanne Langer, and the educational theories of Jerome Bruner. His international reputation led to consultancies and lectures worldwide, and his theories have been applied in music education contexts across numerous countries.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The CLASP Model

Swanwick's CLASP model, developed in the 1970s and published in his 1979 book *A Basis for Music Education*, represents one of his most enduring contributions to music pedagogy. CLASP is an acronym representing five modes of musical experience:

- **C (Composition):** Creating original music
- **L (Literature studies):** Learning about music history, context, and repertoire
- **A (Audition):** Active listening and aural perception
- **S (Skill acquisition):** Developing technical competence on instruments and voice
- **P (Performance):** Interpreting and presenting music

The model proposes that comprehensive music education must balance all five elements, with Composition, Audition, and Performance forming the 'central' activities (direct musical engagement), while Literature studies and Skill acquisition serve as 'peripheral' supporting activities. Swanwick emphasised that these elements are interdependent and mutually reinforcing, not isolated activities. The model explicitly challenged traditional approaches that over-emphasised skill acquisition and performance of existing repertoire at the expense of creative work and critical listening.

CLASP became widely influential in UK music education planning and curriculum design, particularly during the development of the National Curriculum for Music in the late 1980s and early 1990s, where its influence can be seen in the emphasis on composing, performing, and appraising.

### 2. The Developmental Spiral of Musical Understanding

In collaboration with June Tillman, Swanwick developed a theory of musical development based on empirical research into children's compositions. This work, published in the 1986 British Journal of Music Education article 'The Sequence of Musical Development: A Study of Children's Composition', proposed an eight-level developmental sequence organised into four main layers:

**Materials (ages 0-4):**
- Sensory: exploration of sound as physical sensation
- Manipulative: control and organisation of sound materials

**Expression (ages 4-9):**
- Personal expressiveness: music as personal expression
- Vernacular: adopting conventional musical styles and gestures

**Form (ages 10-15):**
- Speculative: experimentation with structural relationships
- Idiomatic: working confidently within established musical idioms

**Value (age 15+):**
- Symbolic: music taking on systematic, symbolic meaning
- Systematic: integration of personal and cultural values in music-making

The 'spiral' element of the model acknowledges that development is not simply linear; individuals may revisit earlier modes at higher levels of sophistication. For example, an advanced musician might return to 'sensory' exploration when encountering electronic music technology, but do so with accumulated understanding from higher developmental levels.

This spiral model has been critiqued for its stage-based approach and Western cultural assumptions, but it has nevertheless provided a valuable framework for teachers to recognise and support musical development.

### 3. Musical Knowledge as Distinct Forms of Understanding

Swanwick identified three distinct forms of musical knowledge, which he argued must all be addressed in education:

**Propositional Knowledge ('knowing that'):** Facts about music—historical information, theoretical concepts, terminology. This corresponds to the 'Literature studies' component of CLASP.

**Procedural Knowledge ('knowing how'):** Technical skills and competencies—how to play instruments, read notation, manipulate music technology. This relates to 'Skill acquisition' in CLASP.

**Acquaintance Knowledge ('knowing music'):** Direct, intuitive understanding gained through musical experience itself—the qualitative, aesthetic dimensions of musical engagement. This is developed through composition, performance, and deep listening (Audition).

Swanwick argued forcefully that music education had historically overemphasised propositional and procedural knowledge at the expense of acquaintance knowledge. He believed that genuine musical understanding requires direct encounter with music as aesthetic experience, not merely learning about music or acquiring technical skills in isolation from meaningful musical contexts.

This tripartite framework drew on philosophical distinctions in epistemology and connected to debates about 'knowledge-rich' versus 'skills-based' curricula that remain current in UK education policy.

### 4. Music as a Liberal Art

Throughout his career, Swanwick defended music education as a liberal art—valuable in its own right for developing human understanding and experience. He was critical of what he termed 'instrumental' justifications for music in schools: arguments that music education should be valued primarily because it improves mathematical ability, enhances literacy, supports social cohesion, or develops transferable skills.

In his 1999 book *Teaching Music Musically*, Swanwick argued that such justifications undermined music's educational legitimacy and made music education vulnerable to removal when evidence for these claims proved weak or when other subjects claimed similar benefits more persuasively.

Instead, Swanwick advocated for understanding music as a unique 'symbolic form' (drawing on Susanne Langer's philosophy) that allows humans to explore and articulate feeling and emotional life in ways that verbal language cannot. He argued that this distinctive contribution to human understanding justified music's place in a broad, humanistic education.

This position aligned him with advocates of arts education more broadly and positioned him against increasingly utilitarian trends in UK education policy from the 1980s onwards.

### 5. Critical Musicology and Music Education

Swanwick engaged seriously with questions of musical value and quality, arguing that music education must help students develop critical discrimination. He rejected both elitist hierarchies that dismissed popular music and relativist positions that treated all musical products as equally valuable.

He advocated for a position of 'critical pluralism', where students encounter diverse musical traditions and genres, but develop criteria for making informed aesthetic judgements within and across traditions. This approach required teachers to demonstrate genuine respect for students' musical preferences whilst also expanding their musical horizons and critical awareness.

This stance was controversial, particularly during debates about 'world music' and multiculturalism in the curriculum, where some interpreted his position as covertly Eurocentric. Swanwick maintained that acknowledging musical quality and tradition need not imply cultural supremacy, but rather represented taking music seriously as an art form across cultures.

### 6. The Four Principles of Music Education

In *Teaching Music Musically* (1999), Swanwick distilled his pedagogical philosophy into four fundamental principles:

**1. Care for music as aesthetic experience:** Prioritising the expressive, qualitative dimensions of musical engagement.

**2. Care for learners' musical development:** Understanding and supporting students' developmental trajectories.

**3. Care for breadth of musical discourse:** Ensuring encounters with diverse musical traditions and practices.

**4. Care for musical communities:** Recognising music's social dimensions and connections to cultural identity.

These principles were intended to guide curriculum planning, lesson design, and assessment practices. They represented an integration of his various theoretical contributions into practical guidance for teachers.

## Key Publications

### Books

**A Basis for Music Education** (1979, NFER-Nelson)
Swanwick's first major theoretical work, introducing the CLASP model and establishing his philosophical approach to music curriculum. This book argued for music education as developing musical understanding through balanced engagement with creating, performing, and listening.

**Music, Mind, and Education** (1988, Routledge)
A comprehensive theoretical treatise examining the psychological, philosophical, and sociological foundations of music education. Swanwick engaged with aesthetic philosophy (Langer, Meyer), developmental psychology (Piaget), and curriculum theory (Bruner). This book explored music as symbolic form and developed arguments about musical meaning and value.

**Musical Knowledge: Intuition, Analysis and Music Education** (1994, Routledge)
An exploration of epistemological questions in music education, developing the distinction between propositional, procedural, and acquaintance knowledge. This book examined how different forms of knowing interact in musical understanding and what this means for teaching and assessment.

**Teaching Music Musically** (1999, Routledge)
Perhaps his most accessible and practically-oriented work, this book synthesised Swanwick's theoretical perspectives into guidance for classroom practice. It presented the four principles of music education and explored their application across different educational contexts. The book became widely used in teacher education programmes internationally.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

'The Sequence of Musical Development: A Study of Children's Composition' (with June Tillman), British Journal of Music Education, 3(3), 1986, pp. 305-339.
This landmark empirical study presented the developmental spiral model based on analysis of 745 compositions by children aged 3-11. The research methodology involved detailed qualitative analysis of compositional products, identifying developmental patterns that informed the eight-level sequence.

'Music Education Liberated from New Praxis', International Journal of Music Education, 28(1), 1996, pp. 16-24.
A critical engagement with Christopher Small's concept of 'musicking', where Swanwick defended the value of aesthetic experience against what he saw as overly sociological approaches to music education.

'Musical Development Theories Revisited', Music Education Research, 3(2), 2001, pp. 227-242.
A retrospective reflection on the developmental spiral model, responding to criticisms and considering cross-cultural applications of developmental theory.

'The "Good-Enough" Music Teacher', British Journal of Music Education, 25(1), 2008, pp. 9-22.
A late-career reflection on teacher education and professional identity, drawing on Winnicott's concept of the 'good-enough' mother to argue for realistic, humanistic approaches to teacher development.

### Edited Collections

Swanwick also edited several important collections, including contributions to the *Routledge Research Companion to Music Education* and various international handbooks of music education.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### The National Curriculum for Music (1992)

Swanwick's influence on the original National Curriculum for Music in England was substantial, though indirect. The curriculum's three attainment targets—Performing, Composing, and Appraising—clearly reflected CLASP principles, particularly the emphasis on composition as a central activity rather than peripheral enrichment.

The inclusion of composition as a statutory requirement for all pupils represented a significant shift in UK music education, moving away from the performance-dominated tradition of school music. Swanwick had argued persistently for this balance through his publications and consultancy work with examination boards and curriculum authorities.

The developmental spiral influenced thinking about progression in the National Curriculum programmes of study, though the age-phase descriptors did not map directly onto Swanwick's levels. The emphasis on breadth of musical styles and traditions also reflected his advocacy for cultural pluralism in music education.

### GCSE and A-Level Music

Swanwick served as a consultant to examination boards during significant reforms of GCSE and A-Level Music specifications in the 1980s and 1990s. His influence can be seen in the balanced weightings given to composition, performance, and listening/appraisal components, and in the criteria for assessing compositional work at different levels.

The emphasis on creative work and critical listening, rather than solely on performance and theoretical knowledge, represented a shift that Swanwick actively promoted. However, he remained critical of assessment practices that he felt reduced musical understanding to measurable outcomes, losing sight of aesthetic engagement.

### Teacher Education Standards

Through his work at the IOE and involvement with initial teacher education policy, Swanwick influenced expectations for music teacher training. His emphasis on student teachers developing their own musical creativity and critical listening abilities, not just instrumental skills and pedagogical techniques, shaped approaches to ITE in many UK institutions.

### International Influence

Beyond the UK, Swanwick's work influenced curriculum development in Australia, Hong Kong, South Africa, and various European countries. The International Society for Music Education (ISME) provided a platform for his ideas to reach global audiences, and his books were translated into several languages.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Teaching Philosophy

Swanwick's pedagogical approach emphasised:

**Musical authenticity:** Students should engage with music as musicians do—creating, performing, and listening with attention to expressive and aesthetic qualities, not merely completing exercises or learning facts.

**Developmental appropriateness:** Teaching should connect with students' current developmental levels whilst providing appropriate challenge and opportunities for progression.

**Integration of musical activities:** Composition, performance, and listening should inform and enrich each other, not be taught in isolation. Technical skills should be developed in service of musical understanding, not as ends in themselves.

**Teacher as musician:** Swanwick believed effective music teachers must be musically engaged themselves, demonstrating genuine musicianship and enthusiasm for music as an art form. The teacher's musical identity was central to effective pedagogy.

### Classroom Practice

In practical terms, Swanwick advocated for:

- **Beginning with musical experience:** Starting lessons with listening, performing, or creating activities that engage students musically before introducing theoretical concepts or historical information.

- **Authentic musical tasks:** Composition projects that result in music worth hearing, not merely exercises; performances prepared for real audiences; listening that develops genuine aesthetic response.

- **Critical reflection:** Regular opportunities for students to discuss, evaluate, and refine their musical work, developing the vocabulary and concepts to articulate their musical thinking.

- **Cultural breadth:** Exposure to diverse musical traditions, but with sufficient depth to understand each tradition's internal coherence and values, avoiding superficial 'world music tourism'.

### Assessment Principles

Swanwick was cautious about assessment practices that fragmented musical understanding into separate components. He advocated for:

- **Holistic judgement:** Assessing musical understanding through comprehensive musical tasks rather than atomised skill checklists.

- **Formative emphasis:** Using assessment primarily to support learning and development, not merely to grade or rank students.

- **Respecting musical quality:** Maintaining standards of musical value whilst acknowledging diverse pathways to musical achievement.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

### Critiques and Debates

Swanwick's work generated considerable debate within music education:

**Cultural assumptions:** Critics, including Lucy Green, argued that the developmental spiral reflected Western art music values and might not adequately account for development in other musical traditions or informal learning contexts. The emphasis on 'form' and 'structure' was seen by some as privileging Western classical concepts.

**Stage theory limitations:** The developmental model's stage-based structure was challenged by those who saw musical development as more contextual and variable than a universal sequence suggests. Cross-cultural research questioned whether the proposed sequence held across all cultural contexts.

**Romantic individualism:** Some sociologically-oriented scholars argued that Swanwick's emphasis on individual aesthetic experience underplayed music's social dimensions and the importance of musical communities and identities. Christopher Small's concept of 'musicking' represented an alternative paradigm that Swanwick engaged with critically.

**Political implications:** During periods when arts education faced funding cuts, some felt Swanwick's resistance to 'instrumental' justifications left music education vulnerable, unable to make pragmatic cases for resources based on wider educational benefits.

### Enduring Contributions

Despite debates, Swanwick's influence remains substantial:

- The CLASP model continues to inform curriculum planning in UK schools and internationally, providing a comprehensive framework for balanced music education.

- His emphasis on composition as central to music education has become established orthodoxy in UK curriculum documents and examination specifications.

- The developmental spiral, whilst debated, provides teachers with a valuable lens for understanding progression and planning appropriate challenges.

- His advocacy for music as aesthetic education continues to influence those resisting purely utilitarian education policy.

In the 2020s, Swanwick's ideas remain pertinent to several current debates:
