# Lucy Green

**Full Name:** Professor Lucy Green  
**Current Role:** Emerita Professor of Music Education, UCL Institute of Education (IOE)  
**Known For:** Pioneering research into informal learning in music education, founding Musical Futures, transforming popular music pedagogy in UK secondary schools, challenging traditional music curriculum approaches

---

## Biography and Career History

Professor Lucy Green is one of the most influential figures in UK music education over the past three decades. Her research has fundamentally reshaped how educators understand and implement popular music teaching, informal learning strategies, and student-centred pedagogy in secondary schools.

Green completed her doctoral research at the Institute of Education, University of London (now UCL Institute of Education) in the 1980s, examining gender and music education. She joined the IOE as a lecturer in 1989 and spent her entire academic career there, becoming Professor of Music Education before taking emeritus status. Throughout her career, she has maintained close connections with classroom practice whilst developing robust theoretical frameworks for understanding how young people learn music outside formal educational settings.

Her early work focused on gender, ideology and musical meaning, but from the late 1990s onwards, she increasingly concentrated on the pedagogical implications of popular musicians' learning practices. This shift proved transformative for UK music education, particularly at Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14), where engagement and relevance had long been identified as problematic.

Between 2002 and 2006, Green directed the groundbreaking 'Informal Learning in the Music Classroom' research project, funded by the Economic and Social Research Council (ESRC). This longitudinal study involved working with secondary school teachers across England to implement informal learning approaches with Year 9 students. The findings directly led to the establishment of Musical Futures in 2003, a national initiative that has since influenced thousands of schools across the UK and internationally.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Informal Learning Practices in Music

Green's most significant contribution to music education is her detailed analysis and categorisation of how popular musicians learn outside formal educational contexts. Through extensive research, she identified five key principles of informal music learning:

**First**, learners begin with music they already know and love, choosing repertoire themselves rather than following a predetermined curriculum.

**Second**, the primary method of learning is through copying recordings by ear, rather than reading notation or following teacher demonstrations.

**Third**, learning occurs in friendship groups where participants have self-selected, creating intrinsically motivated peer-directed learning environments.

**Fourth**, skills are acquired in haphazard, holistic ways rather than through progressive, linear skill-building exercises. Knowledge integration happens organically through repeated engagement with whole pieces of music.

**Fifth**, listening, performing, improvising and composing are deeply integrated activities rather than separated curriculum components.

Green's articulation of these principles, particularly in her 2002 book *How Popular Musicians Learn*, provided a theoretical and practical foundation for bringing authentic popular music practices into classroom settings. She demonstrated that these principles could be adapted for classroom use without losing their essential characteristics, provided teachers were willing to relinquish some traditional control over repertoire choice, learning sequence and assessment methods.

### 2. Musical Meaning and Delineated/Inherent Meaning

Earlier in her career, Green developed an influential framework for understanding how music carries meaning, published in *Music on Deaf Ears* (1988). She distinguished between two interconnected types of musical meaning:

**Inherent meanings** reside in the materials of music itself—the relationships between sounds, the syntactical organisation of musical elements, and the purely sonic experience. These meanings are experienced through direct engagement with music's materials.

**Delineated meanings** are the associations, images, concepts and cultural values that music carries beyond its sonic materials. These include genre associations, social identities, cultural contexts and extra-musical symbolism.

Green argued convincingly that these two types of meaning are inseparable in lived musical experience. A listener cannot hear inherent meanings without some delineated associations, however minimal, and delineated meanings cannot exist without the inherent musical materials that carry them. This framework has proven invaluable for understanding why repertoire choice matters so profoundly in music education—the delineated meanings students attach to different musical styles fundamentally affect their ability and willingness to engage with inherent musical materials.

### 3. Musical Futures Pedagogical Approach

Musical Futures, which Green founded and theoretically underpinned, translates informal learning principles into practical classroom strategies. The approach centres on several key pedagogical shifts:

Teachers adopt a facilitator role rather than front-leading instruction, moving around the room to support groups rather than demonstrating from the front. Pupils work in small friendship groups on equipment they select themselves, typically popular band instruments (guitars, bass, drums, keyboards, vocals). Learning begins with pupils copying songs they choose from a provided 'menu' of accessible popular music, working by ear from recordings rather than notation.

The classroom becomes a workshop environment with multiple groups working simultaneously on different songs at different stages of learning. Mistakes are reframed as essential learning opportunities rather than failures. Assessment emphasises musical progress and personal achievement rather than comparison against predetermined standards.

Green's research demonstrated that this approach dramatically improved engagement, particularly among students previously disaffected with music, and successfully included pupils across different prior attainment levels. Significantly, students developed aural skills, ensemble skills and musical independence more effectively than through traditional transmission methods.

### 4. Critique of Formal Music Education and the 'Celebratory' Approach

Green has consistently critiqued the tendency of formal music education to privilege Western classical music and traditional pedagogical methods. She argues that music education has historically celebrated certain musical values, practices and repertoires whilst marginalising others, particularly popular music and vernacular traditions.

Her concept of the 'celebratory' approach to music education suggests that teachers should validate and build upon students' existing musical identities, preferences and informal learning experiences rather than requiring them to abandon these at the classroom door. This does not mean avoiding challenge or limiting students to familiar music, but rather using familiar music and learning practices as a foundation for musical development.

Green argues that when students' musical identities are celebrated rather than ignored or devalued, they develop stronger musical agency, deeper engagement and more sustainable musical learning. This approach has influenced the National Curriculum for Music in England, particularly the emphasis on students' own musical interests and starting points.

### 5. Authenticity and Fidelity in Classroom Pedagogy

A crucial aspect of Green's work concerns how informal learning practices can be adapted for classroom use whilst maintaining authenticity. She addresses tensions between the spontaneous, intrinsically motivated nature of informal learning and the structured, compulsory context of schooling.

Green distinguishes between 'bringing informal learning into the classroom' and 'teaching informally'. Teachers do not simply leave students alone or abdicate responsibility; rather, they consciously structure learning environments to enable informal learning processes to flourish. This requires careful planning of resources, room organisation, groupings and scaffolding strategies, even whilst allowing students significant autonomy.

She emphasises 'fidelity' to authentic musical practices, arguing that classroom music learning should reflect how musicians actually engage with music outside educational institutions. This includes respecting the primacy of aural transmission in popular music, the integration of different musical activities, and the social nature of much music-making.

### 6. Ear-Playing, Purposive Listening and Aural Development

Green's research challenges conventional assumptions about aural skill development and the role of notation in music learning. She demonstrates that popular musicians develop sophisticated aural abilities through purposive listening—listening with the specific intention of learning and reproducing what they hear.

This 'purposive listening' differs qualitatively from passive listening or analytical listening for academic purposes. Musicians engage actively with recordings, repeatedly listening to specific sections, experimenting on instruments to match sounds, and adjusting their playing through immediate aural feedback.

Green argues that music education has often approached aural training through decontextualised exercises (intervals, rhythms, dictation) rather than integrated, purposeful aural engagement with complete musical works. Her informal learning approach places ear-playing at the centre, demonstrating that students can develop functional aural skills more effectively when listening serves immediate musical purposes rather than abstract skill-building.

## Key Publications

### *Music on Deaf Ears: Musical Meaning, Ideology and Education* (1988)

Green's first major book established her as a significant voice in music education philosophy. The text examines how musical meaning operates and how ideology shapes music education. Her framework of inherent and delineated meanings remains widely cited. The book challenged the notion that music is a 'universal language' and demonstrated how musical experience is always culturally mediated. Published by Manchester University Press, this work laid philosophical foundations for her later practical pedagogical research.

### *Music, Gender, Education* (1997)

This influential book synthesised research on gender issues in music education, examining why girls and boys participate differently in musical activities and how gendered associations with instruments, genres and musical roles develop. Green analysed how music education reproduces gender inequalities and proposed strategies for more equitable practice. Published by Cambridge University Press, the book has been widely used in teacher education and prompted significant reflection on gendered practices in UK schools.

### *How Popular Musicians Learn: A Way Ahead for Music Education* (2002)

This seminal work, published by Ashgate (now Routledge), presented Green's detailed research into popular musicians' learning practices and explicitly proposed these as models for classroom music education. The book examined how garage bands, rock musicians, DJs and other popular musicians acquire skills through informal methods. Green interviewed and observed numerous popular musicians, identifying common learning patterns across diverse genres. Crucially, she argued these methods could and should inform classroom practice, directly challenging transmission-based pedagogies dominant in UK secondary schools. This book provided the theoretical foundation for Musical Futures and remains essential reading for music educators engaging with popular music pedagogy.

### *Music, Informal Learning and the School: A New Classroom Pedagogy* (2008)

Published by Ashgate, this book reported findings from Green's ESRC-funded research project trialling informal learning approaches in London secondary schools. She documented in detail how teachers implemented informal learning strategies, the challenges they faced, students' responses, and learning outcomes. The book provides extensive practical guidance alongside theoretical analysis, including descriptions of lesson structures, teacher roles, assessment approaches and student progression. Green presented compelling evidence that informal learning approaches improved engagement, particularly among previously disaffected students, and developed musical skills effectively. This book became a key text supporting the spread of Musical Futures nationally and internationally.

### *Hearing, Listening and Musical Learning* (2012)

In this book, published by Ashgate, Green developed her ideas about aural learning, examining different modes of listening and their relationship to musical development. She distinguished between 'hearing' (passive reception of sound), 'listening' (active attention to music) and 'purposive listening' (listening with intention to learn). The book synthesised research on auditory perception, musical cognition and learning, connecting these to practical pedagogy. Green argued for more emphasis on ear-playing and purposive listening in music education, demonstrating how these support musical understanding more effectively than notation-based approaches for many learners.

### *Music Learning and Teaching in Infancy, Childhood and Adolescence: An Oxford Handbook of Music Education, Volume 2* (2012)

As co-editor (with Gary McPherson and Graham Welch) of this Oxford University Press handbook, Green contributed to establishing music education as a rigorous academic discipline. She authored chapters on informal learning and popular music pedagogy whilst bringing together international scholars to examine music learning across developmental stages. The handbook has become a standard reference text in music education research and postgraduate study.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

Green has published extensively in peer-reviewed journals including the *British Journal of Music Education*, *Music Education Research*, *Research Studies in Music Education* and *Psychology of Music*. Notable articles include 'Meaning, Autonomy and Authenticity in the Music Classroom' (2006), examining tensions in implementing informal learning; 'Popular Music Education in and for Itself, and for "Other" Music' (2006), exploring relationships between popular and classical music in education; and 'Group Co-operation, Inclusion and Disaffected Pupils' (2011), reporting on how informal learning approaches support inclusive practice.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum for Music (England)

Green's research has profoundly influenced the National Curriculum for Music, particularly revisions implemented from 2007 onwards. The Key Stage 3 programmes of study increasingly emphasised starting from pupils' own musical interests, developing aural skills, and integrating performing, composing and listening. The 2014 National Curriculum retained these principles, stating that teaching should 'build on the musical interests that pupils bring to school'.

The curriculum's emphasis on musical styles beyond Western classical traditions, on creative music-making, and on developing musical independence all reflect Green's influence. Ofsted reports throughout the 2000s and 2010s increasingly recognised engagement with popular music and informal learning approaches as good practice, directly citing Musical Futures and Green's research.

### Musical Futures National Programme

Perhaps Green's most direct policy influence came through Musical Futures, which she founded in 2003 at the Paul Hamlyn Foundation. The programme began as a small-scale initiative but rapidly expanded across England with substantial funding and government backing. By 2010, over 2,000 schools had engaged with Musical Futures approaches.

Musical Futures provided curriculum resources, professional development, and a network supporting teachers implementing informal learning. The programme developed various pedagogical strands beyond the initial informal learning project, including whole-class ensemble teaching, composition pathways, and listening resources, but informal learning remained foundational.

The programme influenced policy by demonstrating practical viability and measurable outcomes. Evaluation studies showed improved engagement, attendance and attainment, particularly for students from disadvantaged backgrounds. This evidence convinced schools, local authorities and policymakers to adopt these approaches widely.

Musical Futures became a Delivery Partner within Music Education Hubs from 2012 onwards, embedding informal learning approaches within the new national infrastructure for music education in England. The programme expanded internationally, influencing practice in Australia, Singapore, Ireland and other countries.

### Teacher Education and Professional Development

Green's work transformed initial teacher education (ITE) and continuing professional development (CPD) for music teachers. Her books became core texts on PGCE and music education MA programmes across UK universities. Many teacher education programmes restructured their popular music pedagogy modules around her informal learning principles.

The Institute of Education (UCL) and other universities developed CPD courses based on Green's research. Musical Futures offered extensive professional development, training thousands of teachers in informal learning approaches. This systemic influence on teacher education ensured Green's ideas reached successive cohorts of music teachers.

### Music Education Hubs

When the government introduced Music Education Hubs in 2012 as the primary infrastructure for music education in England, Green's principles influenced the model. The National Plan for Music Education (2011) emphasised accessibility, relevance and progression from students' starting points—principles consistent with Green's advocacy for student-centred, inclusive approaches. Musical Futures' role as a Hub Delivery Partner ensured informal learning approaches were available to all Hub schools.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Teacher's Role Transformation

Green advocates fundamentally reconceiving the teacher's role from instructor to facilitator. Teachers structure learning environments, provide resources and support, but avoid front-leading instruction during initial learning phases. This requires confidence to allow students to struggle productively with musical challenges rather than immediately intervening with demonstrations.

Teachers circulate among groups, observing, asking questions, offering subtle suggestions and celebrating achievements. Intervention becomes responsive rather than predetermined, tailored to individual groups' needs at specific moments. This demands sophisticated pedagogical judgement and deep musical understanding, contrary to misconceptions that informal learning approaches require less teacher expertise.

### Classroom Organisation and Resources

Green's approach requires careful attention to physical environment. Classrooms need multiple rehearsal spaces, sufficient instruments for simultaneous group work, and sound isolation between groups. Schools typically use practice rooms, corridors, drama studios and outdoor spaces alongside the main music room.

Resources include quality instruments reflecting popular music traditions (electric guitars, bass guitars, drum kits, keyboards), amplification, and recording equipment. Green emphasises that students need access to authentic instruments rather than simplified alternatives. Digital technology, whilst valuable, supplements rather than replaces instrumental playing.

Song 'menus' provide starting points—carefully selected popular songs with accessible chord progressions, clear structures and appeal to young people. Green worked extensively on identifying appropriate repertoire that balances challenge, accessibility and cultural relevance.

### Progression and Differentiation

Green's approach addresses progression through cyclical deepening rather than linear skill-building. Students repeatedly engage with complete songs, each cycle developing deeper understanding, greater accuracy and more sophisticated interpretation. Differentiation occurs naturally as students choose appropriately challenging repertoire and work at their own pace.

Teachers support progression by gradually increasing expectations—encouraging attention to musical details, introducing more complex songs, suggesting extended techniques, proposing creative variations. Assessment focuses on individual progress rather than comparison against fixed standards.

Green demonstrates how students develop transferable musical skills (chord understanding, ensemble awareness, aural skills) through repeated engagement with diverse songs rather than through isolated technical exercises.

### Integration of Composition and Improvisation

Unlike pedagogies separating composing, performing and listening, Green's approach integrates these activities. Students improvise within performed songs, create arrangements, compose transitions and develop variations. This reflects authentic popular music practice where boundaries between composition, arrangement and performance blur.

Teachers encourage experimentation with song structures, instrumental timbres, and expressive interpretations. Students' creative decisions emerge from their developing understanding of the music they're learning, making composition purposeful rather than abstracted.
