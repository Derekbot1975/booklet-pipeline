# Gary Spruce

**Full Name:** Gary Spruce

**Current Role:** Senior Lecturer in Music Education, The Open University

**Known For:** Critical scholarship on music curriculum policy, advocacy for inclusion and cultural diversity in music education, and challenging the dominance of Western classical music traditions in school curricula

---

## Biography and Career History

Gary Spruce has been a leading figure in UK music education for over three decades, establishing himself as one of the most influential critical voices in music curriculum development and policy analysis. Based at The Open University, Spruce has consistently interrogated the cultural politics of music education, questioning whose music is valued in schools and advocating for genuinely inclusive pedagogical approaches.

Spruce's career has combined academic scholarship with practical engagement in curriculum policy. He has served as a Senior Lecturer in Music Education at The Open University, where he has been instrumental in developing distance learning programmes for music educators and contributed significantly to postgraduate music education courses. His work has directly influenced generations of music teachers through both his teaching and his extensive publications.

Throughout his career, Spruce has been actively involved in policy debates surrounding the National Curriculum for Music in England. He has been a vocal critic of what he perceives as cultural conservatism in music education policy, particularly the tendency to privilege Western classical music traditions over other musical cultures. His scholarship has consistently challenged music educators and policymakers to consider issues of cultural democracy, social justice, and inclusion.

Spruce's academic background encompasses both musicology and education, enabling him to bring a sophisticated understanding of musical practices alongside critical pedagogical theory. He has been particularly influenced by sociological perspectives on curriculum, drawing on the work of scholars such as Basil Bernstein and Pierre Bourdieu to analyse power relationships within music education.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Cultural Politics of Music Education

Spruce's most significant contribution to music education discourse has been his rigorous analysis of the cultural politics underlying music curriculum decisions. In his seminal work, he argues that curriculum choices are never neutral but always reflect particular cultural values and power relationships. Drawing on critical theory, Spruce demonstrates how the National Curriculum for Music has historically privileged Western classical music traditions, effectively marginalising other musical cultures including popular music, world music, and community music-making traditions.

In his 1996 article 'Teaching Music: What is the Relationship between the National Curriculum and the Community?', published in the *British Journal of Music Education*, Spruce critiqued the disconnect between school music curricula and the musical lives of young people in their communities. He argued that this disconnect contributed to the alienation many students experienced in music classrooms and perpetuated social inequalities through cultural exclusion.

### 2. Inclusion and Musical Diversity

Spruce has been a persistent advocate for genuinely inclusive music education that recognises and values musical diversity. His concept of inclusion extends beyond simplistic notions of 'adding on' different musical styles to a predominantly classical curriculum. Instead, he calls for fundamental reconceptualisation of what counts as musical knowledge and musical achievement in schools.

In his 2002 chapter 'Music Education, Social Justice and the "Student Voice"' in the Routledge volume *Issues in Music Teaching*, Spruce articulated a vision of inclusive music education grounded in respect for students' own musical cultures and identities. He argued that true inclusion requires teachers to critically examine their own cultural assumptions and to create pedagogical spaces where diverse musical practices are equally valued.

### 3. Musical Identity and Cultural Democracy

Central to Spruce's work is the concept of musical identity and how school music education either supports or undermines students' developing sense of themselves as musical beings. He has drawn attention to the ways in which narrow curriculum definitions can invalidate the musical experiences and competencies that many young people develop outside school, particularly those from working-class backgrounds or minority ethnic communities.

Spruce advocates for what he terms 'cultural democracy' in music education – an approach that recognises the legitimacy of diverse musical traditions and provides all students with opportunities to engage with music in ways that are meaningful within their own cultural contexts. This contrasts with what he sees as the 'cultural missionary' approach that has historically characterised much music education policy.

### 4. Critique of Musical Universalism

Spruce has challenged the notion of universal musical values and standards that has often underpinned music curriculum policy. In his 2013 article 'Culture, Knowledge and Power in Music Education' in *Debates in Music Teaching*, he critiques the assumption that Western classical music represents a superior form of musical achievement against which all other music should be measured.

Drawing on ethnomusicological research, Spruce argues that different musical cultures embody different, but equally valid, forms of musical understanding and achievement. He contends that curriculum frameworks based on universalist assumptions inevitably privilege certain cultural groups whilst disadvantaging others, thereby reproducing social inequalities.

### 5. The Musical Curriculum as Social Construct

Influenced by sociological perspectives, particularly the work of Michael Young on the sociology of knowledge, Spruce analyses music curricula as social constructs that reflect particular interests and power relationships rather than objective musical truths. His 1999 article 'Teaching Music in Secondary Schools' examines how curriculum categories such as 'composing', 'performing', and 'listening' are not natural divisions of musical activity but culturally specific constructions that shape how music is understood and taught.

This perspective has significant implications for curriculum development. Spruce argues that rather than asking 'what music should we teach?', educators should first examine 'whose music are we teaching and whose interests does this serve?' This reflexive approach encourages ongoing critical engagement with curriculum content and pedagogical approaches.

### 6. Music Education Policy Analysis

Spruce has produced detailed analyses of successive versions of the National Curriculum for Music, examining the ideological assumptions embedded within policy documents. His 2007 chapter 'Manifesto for Music' in the *Routledge International Companion to Music Education* traces the shifting political landscape of music education policy, identifying tensions between progressive educational ideals and cultural conservative agendas.

He has been particularly critical of policy developments that emphasise 'musical heritage' and 'great works' approaches, arguing these represent attempts to use music education as a vehicle for cultural restoration rather than cultural development. Spruce's policy analyses provide music educators with conceptual tools for reading policy documents critically and for understanding the broader political contexts within which curriculum decisions are made.

### 7. Pedagogy and Power

Spruce has explored how pedagogical relationships in music classrooms reflect and reproduce wider social power relationships. His work examines how teaching approaches can either empower students as musical agents or position them as passive recipients of authorised musical knowledge. He advocates for pedagogical approaches that recognise students' existing musical knowledge and provide genuine opportunities for creative agency.

In his contributions to *Aspects of Teaching Secondary Music* (2001), Spruce discusses how assessment practices in particular can reinforce cultural hierarchies, with criteria that implicitly favour students from middle-class backgrounds with access to Western classical music training. He calls for assessment frameworks that recognise diverse forms of musical achievement.

### 8. Community Music and Formal Education

Spruce has explored the relationship between community music practices and formal music education, arguing that the community music sector often demonstrates more inclusive and democratic approaches than school music education. His work examines what formal education might learn from community music practices, including their emphasis on collaborative music-making, respect for diverse musical traditions, and focus on music as a means of personal and social development rather than primarily as cultural transmission.

### 9. Teacher Identity and Professional Development

Spruce has addressed the challenges facing music teachers who may themselves have been educated within Western classical music traditions whilst teaching in culturally diverse contexts. He has written about the need for music teacher education programmes to support teachers in developing culturally responsive pedagogical approaches and in critically examining their own musical identities and assumptions.

His work at The Open University has involved developing professional development resources that encourage practising teachers to reflect critically on their pedagogical approaches and to develop strategies for more inclusive practice.

## Key Publications

### Books and Edited Collections

**'Teaching Music in Secondary Schools: A Reader'** (2002, co-edited with Jonathan Savage), published by RoutledgeFalmer as part of the Learning to Teach series. This influential collection brought together key articles on secondary music education, including Spruce's own contributions on inclusion and curriculum policy. The volume has been widely used in initial teacher education programmes across the UK.

**'Debates in Music Teaching'** (2013, co-edited with Chris Philpott), published by Routledge. This comprehensive volume addresses fundamental questions about the purposes and practices of music education. Spruce's chapters in this collection include 'Culture, Knowledge and Power in Music Education' and contributions to debates about curriculum and assessment.

**'Aspects of Teaching Secondary Music: Perspectives on Practice'** (2001, edited), published by RoutledgeFalmer. This edited collection addressed practical and theoretical aspects of secondary music teaching, with Spruce contributing chapters on inclusion and curriculum development.

**'Teaching Music: What is the Relationship between the National Curriculum and the Community?'** (1996), *British Journal of Music Education*, 13(3), 237-248. This early article established Spruce's critical approach to curriculum analysis and raised fundamental questions about cultural relevance in music education.

**'Music Education, Social Justice and the "Student Voice"'** (2002), in C. Philpott and C. Plummeridge (eds.), *Issues in Music Teaching*. London: RoutledgeFalmer. This chapter articulated Spruce's vision of socially just music education centred on respect for students' own musical cultures.

**'Manifesto for Music'** (2007), in L. Bresler (ed.), *International Handbook of Research in Arts Education*. Dordrecht: Springer. This significant contribution traced the political dimensions of music education policy development and argued for more democratic approaches to curriculum decision-making.

**'Whose Music? Whose Culture? Whose Curriculum?'** (2012), in G. McPherson and G. Welch (eds.), *The Oxford Handbook of Music Education*, Volume 1. Oxford: Oxford University Press. This chapter synthesised Spruce's key arguments about cultural politics and music curriculum development.

### Professional Publications

Spruce has contributed extensively to professional publications for music teachers, including articles in *Music Teacher* magazine and resources for examination boards. His work has aimed to make critical scholarship accessible to practising teachers and to support them in developing more inclusive pedagogical approaches.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Spruce's scholarship has significantly influenced debates about music education policy in England and beyond. His critical analyses of the National Curriculum for Music have been widely cited by other researchers and have contributed to ongoing discussions about curriculum reform. Whilst his calls for more radical reconceptualisation of music curricula have not been fully realised in policy, his work has helped to legitimise critical perspectives within music education discourse.

The 2014 revision of the National Curriculum for Music, which removed specific musical genres from the programmes of study and emphasised the importance of diverse musical traditions, reflected some of the concerns Spruce had articulated, though he and other critics argued the changes did not go far enough in addressing underlying cultural hierarchies.

Spruce's influence extends beyond formal curriculum documents to the broader culture of music education. His work has encouraged music educators to question taken-for-granted assumptions about what music should be taught and how. Many music teachers and teacher educators have drawn on his concepts to develop more culturally responsive approaches to music teaching.

Through his work at The Open University, Spruce has influenced the professional development of thousands of music teachers through distance learning courses. His teaching materials have encouraged practising teachers to engage critically with policy documents and to develop their own informed perspectives on curriculum issues.

Spruce has been involved in various curriculum development initiatives and has served as a consultant on music education projects. His expertise has been sought by organisations including examination boards, local education authorities, and arts education charities seeking to develop more inclusive approaches.

## Pedagogical Approach

Spruce's pedagogical approach, as articulated in his writing and teaching, emphasises:

### Critical Reflexivity

He advocates for music educators to engage in ongoing critical reflection about their own cultural assumptions and pedagogical practices. This involves questioning what is taken for granted in music education and examining whose interests are served by particular curriculum choices.

### Student-Centred Practice

Spruce calls for pedagogical approaches that begin with students' own musical interests and experiences rather than imposing predetermined curriculum content. This requires teachers to develop genuine understanding of and respect for the diverse musical cultures students bring to the classroom.

### Democratic Curriculum Development

He argues for more democratic processes of curriculum decision-making that involve teachers, students, and diverse communities rather than being dominated by policy elites. This includes creating spaces within schools for students to have meaningful input into what music is studied and how.

### Cultural Pluralism

Spruce advocates for pedagogical approaches that present diverse musical traditions as equally valid and valuable. This goes beyond superficial multiculturalism to involve deep engagement with different musical epistemologies and practices.

### Critical Media Literacy

He emphasises the importance of developing students' critical understanding of the music industries and media, enabling them to engage thoughtfully with the musical culture that surrounds them. This includes examining issues of representation, commercialisation, and cultural appropriation.

### Collaborative Music-Making

Drawing on community music practices, Spruce advocates for pedagogical approaches that emphasise collaborative creation and performance, where musical learning emerges through collective music-making rather than primarily through individual skill development.

### Authentic Assessment

He calls for assessment practices that recognise diverse forms of musical achievement and that are authentic to the ways music functions in real-world contexts. This challenges traditional examination approaches that may privilege certain musical skills whilst devaluing others.

## Critical Reception and Debates

Spruce's work has been influential but not without controversy. His critiques of Western classical music's dominance in music education have prompted vigorous debate within the music education community. Some critics have argued that his position risks devaluing important musical traditions and lowering standards. Spruce has consistently responded that his aim is not to exclude Western classical music but to question its privileged status and to ensure all musical traditions are treated with equal respect.

His emphasis on cultural relativism has been questioned by those who maintain that certain musical traditions embody more sophisticated forms of musical understanding. Spruce's response draws on ethnomusicological research demonstrating the complexity and sophistication of diverse musical cultures, arguing that perceptions of sophistication are themselves culturally conditioned.

Within the broader field of music education research, Spruce's sociologically informed approach has been influential in moving discussions beyond purely musical or pedagogical considerations to examine the social and political dimensions of music education. His work is frequently cited in critical studies of music education both within the UK and internationally.

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

Spruce's contributions remain highly relevant to contemporary debates about decolonising curricula, addressing educational inequality, and developing culturally responsive pedagogy. As UK schools become increasingly diverse and as questions of cultural representation become more prominent in public discourse, his work provides essential conceptual frameworks for addressing these challenges in music education.

His influence can be seen in:

- **Teacher Education**: Many initial teacher education programmes now include critical perspectives on curriculum policy influenced by Spruce's work
- **Research Agendas**: His scholarship has helped establish the legitimacy of sociological and critical approaches within music education research
- **Policy Discourse**: His concepts and terminology appear regularly in policy discussions, even where his more radical proposals have not been fully adopted
- **Professional Practice**: Many music teachers report that engagement with Spruce's ideas has transformed their pedagogical approaches and encouraged more inclusive practice

The tensions Spruce identified between cultural democracy and cultural heritage continue to animate curriculum debates. The 2021 Model Music Curriculum, published by the Department for Education, reignited many of these debates, with critics arguing it represented a return to cultural conservatism. Spruce's analytical frameworks remain essential tools for understanding and engaging with such policy developments.

## Connections to Wider Educational Debates

Spruce's work connects to broader educational debates about:

- **Curriculum Theory**: His application of sociological perspectives to music curriculum connects to wider discussions about the nature of educational knowledge pioneered by scholars such as Michael Young, Basil Bernstein, and Michael Apple

- **Social Justice Education**: His emphasis on inclusion and equity aligns with broader social justice movements within education, connecting music education to anti-racist pedagogy and critical pedagogy more generally

- **Cultural Studies**: His analysis of power and representation in music education draws on cultural studies scholarship, particularly work on cultural capital and distinction by Pierre Bourdieu

- **Postcolonial Theory**: Although Spruce has not explicitly framed his work in postcolonial terms, his critique of cultural imperialism in music education resonates with postcolonial approaches to curriculum
