# Martin Fautley

**Full Name:** Professor Martin Fautley

**Current Role:** Professor of Education at Birmingham City University (BCU)

**Known For:** Leading research in assessment in music education, contribution to Musical Futures pedagogy, development of inclusive music education practices, and extensive writing on formative assessment and creativity in music classrooms.

---

## Biography and Career History

Professor Martin Fautley is one of the UK's most influential voices in music education research and practice. Based at Birmingham City University's School of Education, he has spent over three decades working to improve music teaching and learning across primary and secondary sectors.

Fautley's career began as a classroom music teacher, giving him direct experience of the challenges facing music educators in diverse school settings. This grounding in practical pedagogy has informed all his subsequent research and writing. He moved into higher education to train music teachers and conduct research into music education practices, eventually becoming a professor and leading significant research projects funded by organisations including the Paul Hamlyn Foundation and Youth Music.

His work at Birmingham City University has positioned BCU as a significant centre for music education research in the UK. Fautley has supervised numerous doctoral students and worked extensively with practising teachers through continuing professional development programmes. He is a regular keynote speaker at national and international conferences and has advised government bodies and music education organisations on policy development.

Fautley's influence extends beyond academia through his work with Musical Futures, the innovative pedagogical approach developed through the Paul Hamlyn Foundation initiative. He served as a researcher and evaluator for Musical Futures projects and has written extensively about informal learning approaches in music education.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Assessment in Music Education

Fautley is perhaps best known for his groundbreaking work on assessment in music education. His research challenges traditional approaches to assessment that focus narrowly on measurable outcomes and technical competence. In his seminal book *Music in the School* (2010, co-authored with Jonathan Savage) and later in *Assessment in Music Education* (2010), Fautley argues that conventional assessment practices often fail to capture the complexity of musical learning and creativity.

He distinguishes between assessment *of* learning (summative), assessment *for* learning (formative), and assessment *as* learning (where pupils develop metacognitive skills). Fautley advocates strongly for formative assessment approaches that support learning rather than merely measuring it. He has developed practical frameworks for teachers to assess musical processes, creativity, and collaborative work—aspects of music education that are notoriously difficult to assess using traditional methods.

Fautley's work identifies common problems in music assessment, including the "shopping list" approach where teachers tick off isolated skills, and the over-reliance on written work that privileges literacy over musical understanding. He promotes continuous assessment embedded in learning activities, arguing that assessment should be an integral part of teaching rather than a bolt-on activity.

### 2. Formative Assessment and Feedback

Building on the work of Paul Black and Dylan Wiliam, Fautley has developed specific applications of Assessment for Learning (AfL) within music education contexts. His research demonstrates how effective feedback in music lessons must be timely, specific, and focused on improvement rather than grades or levels.

Fautley identifies several challenges specific to music: the ephemeral nature of performance, the subjectivity involved in aesthetic judgements, and the group nature of much music-making. He provides practical strategies for teachers, including the use of audio/video recording to enable reflection, peer assessment protocols that develop critical listening skills, and questioning techniques that promote deeper musical thinking.

His work emphasises the importance of dialogic approaches to assessment, where teachers and pupils engage in meaningful conversations about musical learning. This contrasts with transmission models where assessment is done *to* students rather than *with* them.

### 3. Musical Futures and Informal Learning

Fautley served as a key researcher for Musical Futures, working alongside Lucy Green to evaluate and develop this influential pedagogical approach. Musical Futures draws on how popular musicians learn informally—through listening, copying, experimenting, and collaborating—and brings these approaches into formal education settings.

Fautley's research documented the impact of Musical Futures on student engagement, particularly among pupils who were previously disengaged from traditional music education. He examined how informal learning approaches affected assessment practices, teacher roles, and curriculum design. His writing acknowledges both the successes and challenges of implementing informal learning in formal educational contexts.

He has explored how Musical Futures challenges traditional teacher-student relationships, requiring teachers to adopt facilitator roles and relinquish some control over the learning process. Fautley's balanced analysis recognises that informal learning is not a replacement for formal instruction but rather a complementary approach that broadens the music curriculum.

### 4. Inclusive Music Education

Fautley has consistently advocated for inclusive approaches to music education that recognise diverse musical identities, abilities, and backgrounds. His concept of inclusion extends beyond Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) provision to encompass cultural inclusion, recognising the validity of different musical traditions and practices.

He argues that music education has traditionally privileged Western classical music and staff notation literacy, potentially excluding pupils whose musical interests or cultural backgrounds lie elsewhere. Fautley promotes curriculum approaches that validate popular music, world music traditions, and music technologies as legitimate areas of study.

His work on inclusion addresses practical classroom strategies, including differentiation techniques, use of assistive technologies, and creating learning environments where all pupils can participate meaningfully. He emphasises that inclusive practice benefits all learners, not just those with identified additional needs.

### 5. Creativity in Music Education

Fautley has contributed significantly to understanding creativity in music education, distinguishing between "big-C" Creativity (historically significant creative acts) and "little-c" creativity (everyday creative thinking and making). His research examines how creativity can be fostered, taught, and assessed within classroom contexts.

He identifies tensions between curriculum requirements, assessment demands, and creative freedom. Fautley argues that genuine creativity requires appropriate constraints and support structures rather than complete freedom. He has developed frameworks for understanding different aspects of musical creativity, including compositional creativity, improvisational creativity, and interpretive creativity in performance.

His work challenges the notion that creativity cannot be taught, demonstrating how teachers can develop pupils' creative thinking through targeted pedagogical strategies, including modelling, scaffolding, and providing tools for creative exploration.

### 6. Concepts, Knowledge, and Skills in Music

Fautley makes important distinctions between musical concepts (abstract ideas like pitch, duration, dynamics), procedural knowledge (knowing how to do things musically), and declarative knowledge (knowing facts about music). He argues that effective music education integrates these elements rather than treating them separately.

His research criticises approaches that reduce music learning to lists of concepts to be covered or skills to be ticked off. Instead, Fautley promotes understanding music education as developing musical thinking and musicianship—broader capacities that integrate knowledge, skills, and understanding within meaningful musical contexts.

He has written extensively about the "threshold concepts" in music education—transformative ideas that change how learners understand music and cannot be easily reversed once grasped. This work connects with curriculum design, helping teachers identify what is most essential for pupils to learn.

## Key Publications

### Books

**Music in the School** (2010, co-authored with Jonathan Savage, published by Routledge)
This comprehensive textbook for trainee and practising music teachers covers curriculum design, pedagogy, and assessment. It addresses practical classroom issues while grounding practice in research and theory. The book examines the National Curriculum for Music, discusses different pedagogical approaches, and provides frameworks for planning and assessment.

**Assessment in Music Education** (2010, published by Oxford University Press)
Fautley's definitive work on assessment examines why assessment in music is problematic and offers practical solutions. The book reviews assessment theory, discusses validity and reliability in musical contexts, and provides detailed guidance on assessing composition, performance, and listening. It has become essential reading for music teacher training courses across the UK.

**Developing Creativity in Secondary School Music Education** (2016, co-authored with Regina Murphy, published by Routledge)
This book explores how creativity can be fostered in secondary school music contexts. It examines creativity from theoretical perspectives, considers assessment of creativity, and provides case studies of creative practice. The book addresses tensions between curriculum requirements and creative freedom.

**Musical Futures: A New Approach to Teaching and Learning** (multiple publications through Paul Hamlyn Foundation)
Fautley contributed research reports and evaluation documents examining Musical Futures' impact on teaching, learning, and student engagement. These publications documented implementation challenges and successes across different school contexts.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Fautley's work has significantly influenced UK music education curriculum and policy development, though often indirectly through shaping teacher practice and training rather than through direct policy authorship.

### National Curriculum Impact

His writing on assessment informed discussions around the removal of level descriptors from the National Curriculum in 2014. Fautley had long criticised the reductive nature of level-based assessment in music, arguing that it encouraged "teaching to the level" rather than focusing on musical learning. His work on formative assessment provided alternative frameworks that many schools adopted following the level descriptors' removal.

### Teacher Training

Fautley's textbooks, particularly *Music in the School*, have shaped Initial Teacher Training (ITT) across UK universities. His frameworks for understanding musical learning, assessment, and pedagogy are taught to music teacher trainees nationally. His emphasis on reflective practice and research-informed teaching has influenced how music teachers are prepared for the profession.

### Musical Futures Legacy

Through his research on Musical Futures, Fautley contributed to the widespread adoption of informal learning approaches across UK secondary schools. Musical Futures has been implemented in hundreds of schools and has influenced examination specifications, with GCSE and A-Level specifications increasingly accommodating informal learning approaches and popular music traditions that Fautley's research validated.

### Assessment Policy

Fautley has contributed to subject association discussions on assessment without levels, providing guidance to schools developing their own assessment systems. His distinction between formative and summative assessment purposes has helped schools navigate assessment policy changes while maintaining focus on supporting learning.

### Inclusion and Diversity

His advocacy for inclusive approaches has contributed to broader acceptance of diverse musical traditions within school curricula and examination specifications. Music examination boards now routinely include popular music, world music, and technology-based routes that Fautley's research helped legitimise.

## Pedagogical Approach

Fautley's pedagogical philosophy, derived from his research and practical experience, emphasises several interconnected principles:

### Research-Informed Practice

Fautley consistently argues that teaching should be informed by research evidence rather than tradition or assumption. He advocates for teachers engaging with research literature and conducting their own practitioner research. However, he also recognises that research must be translated into practical strategies that work in real classroom contexts.

### Learner-Centred Teaching

His work emphasises understanding learning from the pupil's perspective. This involves recognising pupils' existing musical knowledge and interests, including musical experiences from outside school. Fautley's approach values pupil voice and agency, seeing learners as active participants in their musical education rather than passive recipients.

### Formative Assessment Culture

Central to Fautley's pedagogy is embedding assessment into teaching and learning processes. This means continuous observation, questioning, and dialogue that helps pupils understand their progress and next steps. Assessment becomes a tool for learning rather than merely accountability.

### Balancing Structure and Freedom

Fautley recognises that effective music education requires balancing teacher guidance with pupil autonomy, formal instruction with informal exploration, and curriculum requirements with creative freedom. He rejects false dichotomies that suggest these are opposing forces, instead showing how they complement each other.

### Inclusive Practice as Standard

Rather than treating inclusion as an additional consideration, Fautley's approach makes it fundamental. This means designing curriculum and pedagogy that works for diverse learners from the outset, validating different musical traditions and pathways, and recognising multiple forms of musical intelligence and achievement.

### Reflective Teaching

Fautley emphasises teachers continuously reflecting on and evaluating their practice. This involves questioning assumptions, examining evidence of learning, and adapting approaches based on what works for particular learners in specific contexts.

## Impact on Specific Practices

### Composition Teaching

Fautley's work has influenced how composition is taught and assessed in UK schools. He advocates for composition as musical thinking made audible rather than focusing narrowly on notation. His frameworks help teachers assess compositional processes, creative decision-making, and collaborative work, not just final products.

### Performance Assessment

He has developed approaches to assessing performance that consider interpretation, expression, and musical communication alongside technical accuracy. This more holistic approach recognises performance as a complex musical activity rather than merely executing notation correctly.

### Listening and Appraising

Fautley's work challenges traditional approaches to teaching about music (often called "listening and appraising") that emphasise factual knowledge about composers, periods, and styles. He advocates for developing critical listening skills, aesthetic awareness, and musical understanding through active engagement rather than passive reception.

### Technology in Music Education

His research recognises music technology as enabling participation and creativity rather than replacing traditional musicianship. Fautley's work helps teachers integrate technology purposefully to support musical learning rather than using it superficially.

## Contemporary Relevance

Fautley's work remains highly relevant to current challenges in UK music education:

### Post-Level Descriptors Assessment

Following the 2014 National Curriculum changes removing level descriptors, schools have sought alternative assessment approaches. Fautley's formative assessment frameworks provide practical alternatives that focus on learning rather than arbitrary numerical levels.

### National Plan for Music Education

The National Plan for Music Education (revised 2022) emphasises broader access and diverse musical pathways. Fautley's advocacy for inclusive approaches and validating different musical traditions aligns with and informs these policy directions.

### Ofsted Curriculum Intent Focus

Ofsted's Research Review for Music (2021) emphasises curriculum coherence and progression in knowledge and skills. Fautley's work on concepts, knowledge, and skills in music provides frameworks for schools developing their curriculum narratives.

### Examination Reform Discussions

Ongoing discussions about GCSE and A-Level Music reform engage with questions about assessment validity, inclusion, and curriculum breadth—areas where Fautley's research provides evidence and frameworks.

### Teacher Recruitment and Retention

His work on teacher identity, professional development, and making music teaching sustainable and rewarding addresses factors affecting teacher recruitment and retention in music.

## Critical Reception and Debate

While Fautley's work is widely respected and influential, it exists within broader debates in music education:

Some traditional music educators question whether informal learning approaches adequately develop technical skills and musical literacy. Fautley's response acknowledges these concerns while arguing for broader conceptions of musical literacy and skill.

His emphasis on formative assessment has been embraced widely, but some schools struggle with implementation, particularly around recording and reporting pupil progress without levels or grades. Fautley's work continues to develop practical solutions to these challenges.

Debates about knowledge-rich curricula and cultural literacy intersect with Fautley's work on concepts and knowledge. While some advocate for core knowledge approaches, Fautley's frameworks suggest more complex relationships between knowledge, skills, and understanding.
