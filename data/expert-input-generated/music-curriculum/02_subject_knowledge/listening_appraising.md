# Listening and Appraising

## Overview and Statutory Requirements

Listening and appraising forms one of the three core interrelated dimensions of music education in the English National Curriculum, alongside performing and composing. The 2013 National Curriculum for Music states that pupils should 'listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music' and 'listen with attention to detail and recall sounds with increasing aural memory' (DfE, 2013). This dimension is not merely passive reception but involves active, critical engagement with musical materials.

Keith Swanwick's developmental theory positions listening and appraising as integral to musical understanding, arguing that audiation—the ability to hear and comprehend music in the mind—underpins all musical activity. His influential work with June Tillman established developmental stages showing how children's capacity to engage critically with music evolves from personal response through to systematic analysis (Swanwick & Tillman, 1986).

## Theoretical Foundations

### Swanwick's Model of Musical Knowledge

Keith Swanwick's (1994) model identifies critical evaluation as one of the three fundamental musical processes, alongside composition and performance. His 'Musical Criticism' model proposes four layers of musical understanding:

1. **Materials** - recognising elements such as pitch, duration, dynamics, tempo, timbre and texture
2. **Expression** - understanding musical gestures and their expressive character
3. **Form** - comprehending structural relationships and architectural design
4. **Value** - making judgements about musical worth within cultural contexts

This framework directly influences UK music education practice and is reflected in Model Music Curriculum guidance (2021) and examination specifications across all major awarding bodies.

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Approaches

Lucy Green's research (2002, 2008) demonstrates that listening takes fundamentally different forms in formal and informal musical contexts. Her work with Musical Futures highlighted how young people engage with music through purposeful, self-directed listening that prioritises aural copying and peer learning. Green argues that 'deep listening'—focused, repeated engagement with recordings—develops sophisticated aural skills that formal teaching often fails to cultivate. This has significant implications for pedagogical approaches, particularly at Key Stage 3 where engagement can decline.

### Zoltán Kodály's Pedagogical Philosophy

Kodály's principle that 'music education should begin nine months before the mother is born' emphasises the primacy of listening from the earliest stages. The Kodály approach systematically develops inner hearing through preparatory listening activities that precede notation. This methodology, widely adopted in UK primary education, uses hand signs, rhythm names and carefully sequenced listening repertoire to develop what Kodály termed 'musical literacy'—the ability to think directly in musical sound (Choksy, 1999).

## Key Stage Progression

### Early Years Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1

At EYFS, listening and appraising connects to the 'Expressive Arts and Design' area, where children 'listen attentively, move to and talk about music, expressing their feelings and responses' (DfE, 2021). 

At Key Stage 1, the National Curriculum requires pupils to:
- Listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music
- Use their voices expressively and creatively by singing songs and speaking chants and rhymes
- Experiment with, create, select and combine sounds using the inter-related dimensions of music

Martin Fautley's research (2010) emphasises that even young children can engage in sophisticated musical thinking when provided appropriate scaffolding. He argues against over-simplification, noting that six-year-olds can identify timbral changes, structural features and emotional character with surprising precision.

Practical approaches include:
- Daily listening routines featuring diverse musical styles
- Movement responses to musical elements (tempo, dynamics, pitch direction)
- Comparative listening tasks using simple Venn diagrams
- Picture selection activities linking visual imagery to musical character
- 'Musical dimensions' games focusing on one element at a time

### Key Stage 2

At Key Stage 2, the curriculum expects pupils to 'listen with attention to detail and recall sounds with increasing aural memory' and 'appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians' (DfE, 2013).

The Model Music Curriculum (2021) provides detailed progression, suggesting pupils should:
- Identify instruments by timbre
- Recognise musical forms including binary, ternary and rondo
- Understand how music reflects time, place and cultural context
- Develop musical vocabulary to articulate responses
- Make comparative judgements between pieces

Fautley and Murphy (2015) emphasise that Key Stage 2 represents a critical period for developing 'critical musical understanding' where pupils move from intuitive response towards analytical frameworks. They advocate for 'scaffolded listening' where teachers model expert listening strategies through think-aloud protocols.

Teaching strategies include:
- Structured listening journals with prompts for each dimension
- 'Listening maps' providing visual representations of musical structure
- Comparative analysis using specially designed graphic organisers
- Active listening tasks where pupils move, conduct or follow scores
- Cultural context research linked to geography and history curricula

### Key Stage 3

At Key Stage 3, pupils should 'listen with increasing discrimination to a wide range of music from great composers and musicians' and 'develop a deepening understanding of the music that they perform and to which they listen, and its history' (DfE, 2013).

Gary Spruce's work (2013) highlights the danger of Key Stage 3 becoming overly theoretical, disconnected from students' musical identities. He argues for 'critical musicology' approaches where students interrogate how music constructs meaning within social, political and economic contexts, rather than merely identifying technical features.

The major examination boards' specifications provide insight into expected standards:
- **AQA GCSE Music** - 40% of assessment through listening examination
- **Edexcel GCSE Music** - four Areas of Study covering Western Classical, popular music, film and world traditions
- **OCR GCSE Music** - integrated approach linking listening to performing and composing portfolios
- **WJEC Eduqas GCSE Music** - four Areas of Study with comparative analysis requirements

Key Stage 3 programmes should develop:
- Sophisticated musical terminology including Italian terms
- Understanding of tonality, keys, scales and harmonic progressions
- Recognition of instrumental techniques and performance practice
- Ability to follow written scores whilst listening
- Critical evaluation skills using subject-specific criteria
- Understanding of musical contexts including social, historical and cultural factors

### Key Stages 4 and 5

GCSE and A Level specifications embed listening and appraising as substantial assessment components:

**GCSE (typically 40% of qualification):**
- AQA: 8 set works spanning 150 years, stylistic comparative questions
- Edexcel: Four areas of study, unfamiliar music analysis
- OCR: Two components - 'Listening and Appraising' examining four areas of study
- WJEC Eduqas: Four areas of study requiring contextual understanding

**A Level (typically 40% of qualification):**
- AQA: Six areas of study including baroque, Western Classical tradition 1910-present, popular music
- Edexcel: Six areas of study from 1650 onwards
- OCR: Six areas of study with extended essay component
- WJEC Eduqas: Four areas of study plus unfamiliar analysis

Swanwick's (1999) concept of 'musical criticism' becomes fully realised at this level, where students engage in what he terms 'value discourse'—making informed judgements drawing on technical, expressive and contextual knowledge.

## The Interrelated Dimensions of Music

The National Curriculum identifies interrelated dimensions forming the foundation for listening and appraising:
- **Pitch** - high and low sounds, melody, harmony
- **Duration** - pulse, rhythm, tempo
- **Dynamics** - volume, accents
- **Tempo** - speed and changing speeds
- **Timbre** - quality of sound, instruments, voices
- **Texture** - layers of sound, monophonic, homophonic, polyphonic
- **Structure** - form, phrases, sections
- **Appropriate musical notations**

Fautley (2010) emphasises that these dimensions do not exist in isolation but interact dynamically. Effective listening pedagogy helps pupils understand these relationships—for example, how textural thinning creates dynamic impression, or how timbre changes articulate formal structure.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Active Listening Strategies

Research consistently demonstrates that passive listening produces minimal learning. Effective approaches include:

**Movement-based responses**: Physical engagement with beat, metre, melodic contour and structural sections activates kinaesthetic memory and deepens understanding (Fautley, 2010).

**Graphic notation and listening maps**: Visual representation of musical events provides scaffolding for working memory and supports pattern recognition.

**Purposeful repeated listening**: Green's research (2008) shows that musicians naturally listen repeatedly with different foci. Teachers should structure multiple hearings with specific listening focuses.

**Think-aloud protocols**: Teacher modelling of expert listening strategies makes tacit knowledge explicit (Fautley & Murphy, 2015).

### Developing Musical Vocabulary

Gary Spruce (2013) argues that vocabulary development must serve genuine communication needs, not become empty labelling. Effective approaches:

- Introduce terminology when pupils need it to articulate observations
- Build word banks progressively across key stages
- Use concept mapping to show relationships between terms
- Connect English vocabulary work to musical contexts
- Develop both technical and expressive descriptive language

The Model Music Curriculum provides detailed vocabulary progression from EYFS through Key Stage 3.

### Cultural Breadth and Repertoire Selection

The National Curriculum requirement to engage with 'different traditions' raises important pedagogical questions. Lucy Green's work warns against tokenistic 'world music tourism' that presents non-Western traditions superficially (Green, 2003).

Spruce (2013) advocates for:
- Studying traditions in depth rather than superficial surveys
- Examining music as social practice within communities
- Questioning Western-centric assumptions about musical value
- Including popular and vernacular music alongside art music
- Considering whose music is validated through curriculum choices

## Assessment of Listening and Appraising

Martin Fautley's extensive research on assessment in music education (2010, 2013) highlights particular challenges in assessing listening and appraising:

### Formative Assessment

Effective formative assessment strategies include:

**Listening logs and journals**: Regular structured responses building over time demonstrate progression better than isolated tests.

**Verbal response protocols**: Many pupils articulate musical understanding more effectively through speech than writing.

**Practical demonstration**: Pupils showing understanding through performance, body percussion or movement may demonstrate learning invisible in written responses.

**Self and peer assessment**: Using success criteria derived from Swanwick's dimensions helps develop metacognitive awareness.

Fautley emphasises that effective feedback must be 'process-oriented' rather than merely corrective, helping pupils develop autonomous listening strategies.

### Summative Assessment

Examination specifications provide detailed assessment criteria typically addressing:
- Technical identification (instruments, devices, structures)
- Contextual understanding (style, period, cultural background)
- Musical analysis (how elements create effects)
- Comparative evaluation (similarities and differences between works)

Common challenges include:
- Over-reliance on technical labelling without musical understanding
- Insufficient development of analytical writing skills
- Limited transfer from studied to unfamiliar repertoire
- Difficulty articulating implicit musical knowledge explicitly

## Special Educational Needs and Differentiation

Listening and appraising offers particular opportunities for pupils with diverse needs:

**Supporting pupils with literacy difficulties**: Musical understanding does not depend on written literacy. Alternative response modes (recording, movement, graphic representation) provide access.

**Working with hearing-impaired pupils**: Visual representations, vibrotactile experiences and residual hearing amplification enable meaningful engagement. Deaf musicians and scholars demonstrate that musical understanding transcends auditory perception alone.

**Supporting pupils on the autism spectrum**: The structural, pattern-based nature of musical analysis can engage pupils who find open-ended creative tasks challenging. Clear scaffolds and predictable frameworks support access.

## Digital Technology and Resources

Contemporary practice increasingly integrates technology:

**Streaming platforms**: Spotify, Apple Music and YouTube provide unprecedented access to diverse repertoire. Curated playlists support structured listening programmes.

**Interactive analysis tools**: Soundtrap, BandLab and similar platforms enable waveform analysis and visual representation supporting understanding.

**Score-following applications**: Apps like nkoda and Tomplay present synchronised scores, supporting literacy development.

**Virtual reality experiences**: Emerging VR concert experiences enable immersive engagement with performance practice.

However, Green's research (2008) reminds us that technology enables but does not replace informed pedagogical practice. Teachers must curate, contextualise and scaffold digital resources.

## Links to Performing and Composing

Swanwick's model emphasises that listening, performing and composing function as integrated musical processes. Effective pedagogy makes these connections explicit:

- Performing music studied through listening develops interpretative understanding
- Composing activities applying observed techniques deepen analytical insight
- Listening to peer compositions develops critical evaluation skills
- Performing develops the aural discrimination essential for appraising

The Model Music Curriculum structures units integrating these dimensions, exemplifying how 'Classroom Jazz 1' combines listening to jazz recordings, performing jazz repertoire and creating jazz-style compositions.

## Professional Development and Teacher Expertise

Fautley and Daubney's research (2019) identifies listening pedagogy as an area where non-specialist teachers particularly lack confidence. Effective professional development should:

- Develop teachers' own active listening skills
- Model scaffolding strategies suitable for different key stages
- Build subject knowledge across diverse musical styles
- Examine progression in listening and appraising across key stages
- Consider assessment approaches that recognise diverse forms of musical understanding

## Conclusion

Listening and appraising represents far more than passive reception or technical labelling. As Swanwick argues, it constitutes a form of musical knowledge equal in importance to performing and composing. When effectively taught, it develops sophisticated cognitive skills—pattern recognition, analytical thinking, aesthetic judgment and cultural understanding—that extend well beyond music education.

The challenge for UK music education lies in maintaining this breadth and depth amidst assessment pressures. As Gary Spruce reminds us, listening and appraising should enable young people to engage critically and creatively with the soundscapes shaping their lives, not merely to accumulate technical terminology. The most effective practice, as evidenced across research by Fautley, Green, Swanwick and others, integrates listening with making, connects musical learning to pupils' identities, and recognises multiple forms of musical intelligence.
