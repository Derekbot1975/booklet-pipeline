# World Music and Cultural Diversity

## Introduction and Statutory Framework

World music and cultural diversity form essential components of the UK Music National Curriculum, reflecting Britain's multicultural society and the government's commitment to promoting cultural understanding through education. The National Curriculum for Music (2013) explicitly states that pupils should "appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions and from great composers and musicians." This statutory requirement positions cultural diversity not as an optional addition but as a fundamental dimension of music education.

The term "world music" itself requires careful consideration. Ethnomusicologist John Blacking argued that all music is fundamentally "world music" because musical expression is a universal human capability. Lucy Green, in her influential work *Music, Informal Learning and the School* (2008), cautions against the implicit Western-centricity of categorising non-Western traditions as "other" or "exotic." Instead, she advocates for approaches that respect the integrity and sophistication of diverse musical cultures whilst acknowledging pupils' own cultural identities and musical preferences.

## Pedagogical Approaches and Key Thinkers

### Keith Swanwick's Developmental Model

Keith Swanwick's developmental spiral, outlined in *Music, Mind, and Education* (1988) and refined in *Teaching Music Musically* (1999), provides a framework for incorporating world music that respects children's musical development. Swanwick emphasises the importance of "mastery" and "imitation" phases where pupils engage directly with musical materials from various cultures before progressing to more complex understanding. His C(L)A(S)P model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition, Skill acquisition, Performance) offers a balanced approach to world music study that avoids tokenistic representation.

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Pedagogy

Lucy Green's research into informal music learning practices, developed through the Musical Futures project, demonstrates how world music can be taught more authentically by adopting learning methods used within those traditions. Her findings, published in *How Popular Musicians Learn* (2001), show that many non-Western musical traditions prioritise aural transmission, peer learning, and contextualised skill development—approaches that contrast with formal Western conservatoire methods. The Musical Futures pedagogy has been widely adopted across UK secondary schools, particularly for teaching popular music, but its principles apply equally to Indian classical music, African drumming traditions, and other world music genres.

### Zoltán Kodály's Philosophy

Whilst Zoltán Kodály's method originated in Hungarian folk music, his underlying philosophy—that music education should be rooted in a culture's own musical traditions—has profound implications for UK schools serving diverse communities. Kodály argued that children's musical education should begin with their "mother tongue" music before expanding to other traditions. In the multicultural British context, this suggests validating and incorporating the musical heritage of pupils from South Asian, Caribbean, African, Eastern European, and other backgrounds as primary source material rather than supplementary content.

### Gary Spruce and Social Justice

Gary Spruce's work on music education and social justice, including his chapter in *Debates in Music Teaching* (2016), argues that decisions about what music is taught and how it is valued carry significant ideological implications. Spruce contends that the historical dominance of Western art music in UK curricula has marginalised many pupils' cultural identities. He advocates for a critical pedagogy that examines power structures within music education and actively promotes musical diversity as a matter of educational equity and inclusion.

### Martin Fautley on Assessment

Martin Fautley's research on assessment in music education (*Assessment in Music Education*, 2010) highlights particular challenges when evaluating pupils' engagement with world music. Fautley argues that assessment criteria derived from Western musical values (such as traditional notation literacy or harmonic complexity) may not appropriately measure understanding of traditions that prioritise different musical elements—rhythmic complexity in West African music, melodic ornamentation in Arabic music, or improvisational skill in Indian raga. He advocates for culturally responsive assessment that recognises diverse forms of musical excellence.

## National Curriculum Implementation Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The Programme of Study requires that pupils "use their voices expressively and creatively by singing songs and speaking chants and rhymes" and "listen with concentration and understanding to a range of high-quality live and recorded music." At this stage, world music integration should emphasise:

**Practical Implementation:**
- Action songs and singing games from diverse cultures (e.g., "Che Che Koolay" from Ghana, "Al Citron" from Spain)
- Simple percussion patterns from African, Caribbean, and South American traditions
- Story-songs that introduce instruments from various cultures (e.g., sitar, djembe, steel pans)
- Call-and-response songs reflecting African and Caribbean traditions
- Movement and dance connected to musical traditions (e.g., Bhangra, Irish dancing)

Teachers should source authentic materials whilst ensuring age-appropriate difficulty. Resources such as Voiceworks (Oxford University Press) and Sing Up provide carefully selected world music repertoire for primary schools.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The National Curriculum states pupils should "play and perform in solo and ensemble contexts, using their voices and playing musical instruments with increasing accuracy, fluency, control and expression" and "appreciate and understand a wide range of high-quality live and recorded music drawn from different traditions." Implementation should include:

**Practical Implementation:**
- Gamelan ensemble work (increasingly available through local music services)
- Samba percussion units developing polyrhythmic skills
- African drumming circles teaching cyclical structures and improvisation
- Steel pan repertoire exploring Caribbean musical heritage
- Indian classical music introducing raga and tala concepts
- Celtic folk music connecting to UK cultural traditions
- Listening units featuring diverse genres: Qawwali, Flamenco, Aboriginal music

The Charanga Musical School scheme, widely used in UK primary schools, incorporates world music elements throughout its units. However, teachers should supplement this with deeper, sustained engagement with specific traditions rather than superficial "tourism."

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At secondary level, the Programme of Study requires pupils to "play and perform confidently in a range of solo and ensemble contexts" and "develop an understanding of the history of music." World music should be integrated throughout schemes of work:

**Practical Implementation:**
- Extended projects focusing on specific traditions (minimum 6-8 lessons)
- Composition tasks inspired by structural principles from world music (e.g., cyclic structures from Indian classical music, layered textures from Indonesian gamelan)
- Performance of authentic arrangements requiring technique specific to traditions
- Historical and cultural contextualisation of musical styles
- Critical listening developing vocabulary for describing non-Western musical elements
- Cross-curricular links with Geography, History, Religious Education, and Modern Foreign Languages

Music departments should establish links with local cultural organisations and musicians who can provide authentic teaching. Organisations such as SOAS (School of Oriental and African Studies) in London and the Royal Birmingham Conservatoire offer educational outreach programmes.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16) GCSE

All major exam boards now incorporate world music into GCSE specifications:

**AQA Music GCSE (8271):**
Area of Study 3, "Rhythms of the World," includes detailed study of four world music traditions:
- Indian classical music (Ravi Shankar's *Rag Desh* is set work)
- Punjab Bhangra
- Eastern Mediterranean and Middle Eastern music
- Central and South American music

**Edexcel Music GCSE (1MU0):**
Area of Study 4, "Film Music and World Music," requires study of:
- Traditional African drumming
- A cappella gospel music
- Music of the Middle East

**OCR Music GCSE (J536):**
Area of Study 4, "Conventions of Pop and World Music," addresses:
- Arabic music (Anouar Brahem featured)
- African music (including Koko by Koko Tay)

**WJEC Eduqas Music GCSE:**
Area of Study 3, "Music for Stage and Screen and World Music," includes:
- Samba and Bossa Nova
- African music traditions

**Teaching Implications:**
Teachers must develop subject knowledge in these traditions, understanding not merely surface features but underlying structural principles, cultural contexts, and performance practices. Professional development opportunities include courses offered by exam boards, university departments (such as the Ethnomusicology departments at Royal Holloway and Durham University), and specialist organisations like World Music Umbrella UK.

### Key Stages 4-5 (Ages 16-18) A-Level

A-Level specifications provide opportunities for deeper engagement:

**AQA A-Level Music (7272):**
Area of Study 6, "Into the Twentieth Century," includes study of jazz, which has African-American cultural roots and represents an important world music tradition.

**Edexcel A-Level Music (9MU0):**
Offers "World Music" as an optional Area of Study, permitting detailed investigation of traditions including Indian classical music, Indonesian gamelan, and African music.

**OCR A-Level Music (H543):**
"Popular Song and World Music" optional area includes study of Reggae, Latin Jazz, and Bhangra.

## Cultural Authenticity and Respectful Practice

### Avoiding Cultural Appropriation

Gary Spruce warns that superficial engagement with world music can perpetuate stereotypes and constitute cultural appropriation rather than appreciation. Teachers must:

- Present musical traditions within their cultural, historical, and social contexts
- Avoid "safari" approaches that treat non-Western cultures as exotic curiosities
- Use correct terminology and pronunciation (e.g., "raga" not "rag," "djembe" not "jembe")
- Acknowledge when simplification or adaptation occurs for pedagogical purposes
- Invite musicians from cultural communities to share their expertise
- Recognise that they themselves may not be cultural insiders and position themselves as co-learners

### Working with Cultural Communities

Effective practice involves genuine partnership with local cultural communities. The Music Mark organisation provides guidance on building relationships with diverse musical communities. Practical steps include:

- Establishing relationships with local temples, mosques, community centres, and cultural associations
- Inviting musicians as long-term partners rather than one-off "visitors"
- Compensating cultural practitioners appropriately for their expertise
- Attending community musical events to demonstrate genuine interest
- Involving families and community members in curriculum planning
- Creating performance opportunities that respect cultural protocols (e.g., appropriate contexts for sacred music)

## Assessment and Cultural Responsiveness

Martin Fautley's research emphasises that assessment criteria must be adapted when evaluating pupils' engagement with world music. Consider:

**Culturally Responsive Assessment Criteria:**

- **Aural skills:** Many traditions prioritise listening and imitation over notation reading
- **Improvisation:** Indian, Arabic, and jazz traditions value spontaneous creativity within structures
- **Ornamentation:** Middle Eastern and Asian music employs complex melodic embellishment
- **Rhythmic complexity:** African and Latin American traditions feature polyrhythms and syncopation
- **Timbral variety:** World music instruments produce sounds outside Western orchestral norms
- **Ensemble balance:** Gamelan music, for example, values collective blend rather than individual prominence
- **Cultural understanding:** Pupils should demonstrate knowledge of cultural context and significance

Assessment should recognise different types of musical excellence and avoid privileging Western musical values. For example, perfect maintenance of a complex polyrhythmic cycle in African drumming represents sophisticated musical achievement comparable to accurate performance of a Bach fugue.

## Inclusion and Pupil Identity

Lucy Green's research on music, gender, and education (*Music, Gender, Education*, 1997) extends to considerations of cultural identity. For pupils from minoritised communities, seeing their cultural heritage reflected in the curriculum provides validation and enhances engagement. Conversely, exclusively Western-focused curricula can alienate these pupils and diminish their musical confidence.

**Practical strategies include:**

- Beginning units by surveying pupils' cultural backgrounds and musical experiences
- Inviting pupils to share music from their heritage
- Creating composition briefs that allow pupils to incorporate their cultural musical knowledge
- Displaying instruments and imagery representing diverse traditions in music classrooms
- Ensuring concert programmes feature diverse musical traditions
- Celebrating cultural festivals through music (Eid, Diwali, Lunar New Year, etc.)

## Cross-Curricular Opportunities

World music provides rich opportunities for cross-curricular work:

**Geography:** Study of music in relation to climate, landscape, migration patterns, and trade routes
**History:** Exploration of colonialism, slavery, diaspora, and cultural exchange through music
**Religious Education:** Understanding of music's role in religious practices across faiths
**Modern Foreign Languages:** Songs in target languages; understanding of linguistic influences on melody
**Physical Education:** Dance traditions connected to musical styles
**Art and Design:** Visual arts associated with musical cultures; instrument decoration
**Citizenship:** Understanding cultural diversity, combating prejudice, promoting community cohesion

The Arts Council England's *Quality Principles for Arts Education* emphasises such integrated approaches, and Ofsted inspection frameworks recognise cross-curricular contributions to pupils' spiritual, moral, social, and cultural (SMSC) development.

## Professional Development and Subject Knowledge

Teachers require ongoing professional development to teach world music effectively. Many UK music educators trained primarily in Western classical or popular music traditions and need structured opportunities to develop knowledge and practical skills in other traditions.

**Professional development pathways:**

- Exam board training on specific GCSE/A-Level set works
- University short courses in ethnomusicology (Royal Holloway, Durham, Sheffield, SOAS)
- Workshops with specialist organisations (e.g., WOMAD education programme)
- Local authority music service continuing professional development
- Online courses through platforms such as FutureLearn and Coursera
- Attendance at cultural events and festivals
- Mentorship from musicians within cultural communities

The Music Teacher subject journal regularly features articles on world music pedagogy, and the National Association of Music Educators (NAME UK) organises conferences with world music strands.

## Conclusion: World Music as Core, Not Periphery

Keith Swanwick argued that musical understanding transcends cultural boundaries because music functions as symbolic discourse universally. However, this universality emerges from engagement with particular musical traditions, each with distinctive characteristics and cultural meanings. The UK National Curriculum's requirement to study diverse traditions is not about superficial multiculturalism but about developing genuine musical literacy—understanding that music serves fundamental human needs across all cultures, expressed through magnificently varied forms.

Gary Spruce's social justice perspective reminds us that curriculum choices reflect values about whose culture matters. In contemporary Britain, where over 30% of primary pupils come from minoritised ethnic backgrounds (DfE statistics), a curriculum dominated by dead European composers is educationally indefensible. World music and cultural diversity must be woven throughout music education—in listening, performing, composing, and critical studies—reflecting both the reality of pupils' lives and the breadth of human musical achievement.
