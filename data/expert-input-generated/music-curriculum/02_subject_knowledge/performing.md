# Performing: Singing and Instrumental

## Overview

Performing lies at the heart of music education in the UK, as established by the National Curriculum for Music (2013), which states that pupils should be taught to 'play and perform in solo and ensemble contexts, using their voices and playing musical instruments with increasing accuracy, fluency, control and expression'. Performance encompasses both vocal and instrumental work, and serves as a primary means through which pupils develop musicianship, express creativity, and understand musical concepts through embodied practice.

Keith Swanwick's influential model of musical development emphasises that performance is not merely technical reproduction but involves layered understanding from sensory response through to symbolic representation. His work with June Tillman (1986) demonstrated that children's musical development progresses through performing, composing, and responding in integrated ways rather than as separate activities.

## The Voice as the Primary Instrument

### Pedagogical Foundations

The voice represents the most accessible and immediate musical instrument available to all pupils. Zoltán Kodály's philosophy, which has significantly influenced UK music education, positions singing as the foundation of musical literacy. Kodály argued that 'the human voice is the most beautiful instrument of all, but it is the most difficult to play' and advocated for systematic vocal training from early childhood.

Research by Welch and colleagues (2010, 2014) at the UCL Institute of Education has provided substantial evidence for the developmental trajectory of children's singing. The 'Sing Up' programme (2007-2012), funded by the Department for Education, demonstrated measurable improvements in children's singing accuracy, vocal range, and musical engagement when systematic singing instruction was implemented across primary schools.

### Key Stage Progression in Singing

**Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7):**
- Developing pitch-matching ability and recognition of melodic contour
- Building a repertoire of simple songs, rhymes, and chants
- Understanding the difference between singing and speaking voice
- Breathing control and posture awareness
- Singing in unison with others

**Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11):**
- Extended vocal range (typically developing from approximately five notes to an octave or more)
- Introduction to part-singing, rounds, and simple two-part songs
- Developing dynamics, phrasing, and expression
- Understanding diction and text projection
- Singing with accompaniment and maintaining independent vocal lines

**Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14):**
- Navigating voice change, particularly in male pupils (as documented by Ashley, 2013)
- Part-singing in three or more parts
- Genre-specific vocal techniques (classical, popular, world music styles)
- Understanding vocal health and safe singing practices
- Solo and ensemble performance with increasing independence

**Key Stage 4 & 5 (Ages 14-18):**
All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC) require performance as a significant component at GCSE and A-level. Typically, performance constitutes 30% at GCSE and 35% at A-level, with pupils expected to demonstrate:
- Technical control appropriate to chosen repertoire
- Stylistic understanding and interpretation
- Fluency and confidence in performance
- The ability to communicate musical intention to an audience

### Practical Implications for Vocal Teaching

Martin Fautley's research (2010) emphasises that assessment of singing must consider both the process and product of performance. Teachers should:

- Regularly model good singing to establish aural expectations
- Create safe, inclusive environments where all pupils feel comfortable using their voices
- Address the 'missing males' phenomenon (Hall, 2005) by providing male role models and appropriate repertoire
- Understand physiological changes during adolescence, particularly the challenges facing changing voices
- Incorporate vocal warm-ups addressing posture, breathing, resonance, and articulation
- Use varied repertoire reflecting diverse musical traditions and cultures

## Instrumental Performance

### Whole-Class Ensemble Teaching (WCET)

The National Plan for Music Education (2011) and its refresh (2022) positioned whole-class instrumental teaching as a cornerstone of music education access. Programmes such as Wider Opportunities (now termed First Access) typically introduce Year 3 or 4 pupils to orchestral, band, or world music instruments through weekly lessons over an academic year.

Research by Hallam et al. (2012) demonstrated that WCET programmes develop not only musical skills but also concentration, self-confidence, and social cohesion. However, Ofsted reports (2013) highlighted variability in quality, noting that the most effective programmes integrate with the broader curriculum rather than operating as isolated activities.

### Instrumental Progression Frameworks

The progression in instrumental performance broadly follows Swanwick and Tillman's developmental spiral (1986):

**Mastery Level (Early stages):**
- Sound exploration and manipulation
- Developing physical control and technique
- Understanding basic musical elements through playing

**Imitation Level:**
- Copying simple melodic and rhythmic patterns
- Learning pieces by ear and from notation
- Developing ensemble awareness

**Imaginative Play:**
- Expressive use of dynamics and articulation
- Stylistic interpretation
- Improvisation within given structures

**Metacognitive Level:**
- Systematic practice strategies
- Self-evaluation and refinement
- Understanding of structural and harmonic relationships

### Technology and Performance

Lucy Green's work on informal music learning (2002, 2008) has significantly influenced UK curriculum design, particularly regarding popular music and technology integration. Her research demonstrated that young musicians often learn effectively through peer-directed, aural/oral methods, copying recordings and developing their own performance styles.

Contemporary performance encompasses digital instruments, music production software, and hybrid acoustic-electronic setups. The revised National Curriculum acknowledges that 'playing musical instruments' includes digital technologies. Exam boards now accept performances using DJ decks, music production software, and electronic instruments across all qualification levels.

### Ensemble Skills and Collaborative Performance

Gary Spruce (2015) argues that music education should develop pupils' understanding of music as a social practice. Ensemble performance teaches:

- Listening and responding to others in real-time
- Understanding individual responsibility within collective achievement
- Developing leadership and supportive roles
- Negotiating interpretation and musical decisions
- Managing rehearsal time and strategies

Research by Pitts (2007) on community music groups highlights how ensemble participation develops musical identity and belonging. The National Curriculum explicitly requires teaching pupils to 'perform in solo and ensemble contexts', recognising both individual and collective musical-making.

## Assessment of Performance

### Formative Assessment Approaches

Fautley's work (2010, 2015) on assessment in music education emphasises that performance assessment should be:

- **Criterion-referenced**: Judged against learning objectives rather than norm-referenced comparison
- **Ipsative**: Recognising individual progress and development
- **Holistic**: Considering technical, expressive, and communicative dimensions
- **Dialogic**: Involving peer and self-assessment alongside teacher feedback

Effective formative assessment in performance involves:
- Video/audio recording for reflective review
- Structured peer assessment using clear criteria
- Regular performance opportunities in low-stakes environments
- Verbal feedback focusing on specific, actionable improvements
- Recognition of practice habits and preparation processes

### Summative Assessment in Examinations

GCSE specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) assess performance against criteria typically including:

- **Technical control**: Accuracy, fluency, intonation, tone quality
- **Expression and interpretation**: Dynamics, phrasing, articulation, stylistic understanding
- **Communication**: Engagement, confidence, musical shaping
- **Difficulty level**: Complexity of repertoire selected

At A-level, specifications additionally assess:
- Coherence of recital programming
- Depth of technical and interpretative challenge
- Stylistic authenticity across different periods/genres
- Reflective understanding demonstrated through performance preparation documentation

## Addressing Inclusion and Diversity

### Widening Participation

The National Plan for Music Education (2022) explicitly addresses equity of access, acknowledging that instrumental learning opportunities have historically been unevenly distributed across socioeconomic groups. Research by Daubney and Fautley (2019) revealed concerning decline in curriculum music provision, particularly affecting disadvantaged pupils.

Teachers should:
- Provide varied instrumental options including non-Western instruments
- Validate diverse musical traditions and popular music styles
- Ensure adaptive instruments and approaches for pupils with SEND
- Challenge gender stereotypes in instrument selection
- Create multiple performance contexts (formal/informal, small/large group)

### Special Educational Needs and Disability (SEND)

The Music Mark Standards (2020) emphasise that all pupils should access performance opportunities. Practical adaptations include:

- Simplified or modified parts within ensemble contexts
- Technology-assisted instruments (e.g., soundbeam, adaptive switches)
- Visual supports for structuring and sequencing performance
- Alternative notation systems (graphic scores, colour-coded systems)
- Multisensory approaches combining movement, visual, and auditory learning

Ockelford's (2000, 2008) research with pupils with profound and multiple learning difficulties demonstrates that musical performance remains accessible and meaningful across the ability spectrum when appropriately differentiated.

## Practice and Preparation

### Developing Effective Practice Habits

Hallam's research (1997, 2012) on practice strategies indicates that expert musicians develop metacognitive awareness of effective practice from early stages. Teachers should explicitly teach:

- Goal-setting for practice sessions
- Breaking pieces into manageable sections
- Slow, accurate repetition before speed development
- Identifying and targeting specific technical challenges
- Mental rehearsal and score study
- Recording and reviewing practice sessions

The 'little and often' principle (frequent, focused practice sessions) proves more effective than infrequent, lengthy sessions, particularly for younger learners.

### Performance Preparation and Anxiety

Mills and Smith (2003) highlight that performance anxiety affects pupils across age ranges. Strategies include:

- Regular, informal performance opportunities building gradual exposure
- Rehearsing performance conditions (performing to empty chairs, recording simulation)
- Developing pre-performance routines
- Breathing and relaxation techniques
- Reframing anxiety as excitement and readiness
- Post-performance reflection focusing on achievement alongside development areas

## Integration with Composing and Appraising

Swanwick's pedagogical model (the 'musical experience, musical learning' framework) emphasises that performing, composing, and appraising should interconnect rather than exist as discrete activities. Performing develops:

- **Aural awareness**: Through listening to one's own and others' sound production
- **Structural understanding**: Through experiencing form, phrase, and development from inside the music
- **Interpretative skills**: Through making decisions about expression and communication
- **Technical vocabulary**: Through embodied understanding of musical terminology

Green's pedagogical approach (2008) similarly advocates integration, showing that informal learning strategies blur boundaries between performing, improvising, and composing.

## Resources and Continuing Professional Development

The UK benefits from extensive support structures for performance teaching:

- **Music Education Hubs**: Provide instrumental teaching, ensembles, and progression routes
- **Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music (ABRSM)**, **Trinity College London**, **Rockschool**: Offer graded examination frameworks
- **National Youth Music Organisations**: National Youth Orchestra, National Youth Choir, and others provide aspiration and progression
- **Subject associations**: Music Mark, Incorporated Society of Musicians (ISM) provide CPD and resources

Teachers developing performance pedagogy should engage with:
- Specialist instrumental/vocal pedagogy courses
- Action research on performance teaching in their own contexts
- Regular performance practice maintenance
- Observation of specialist instrumental teachers
- Current research through journals such as *Music Education Research*, *British Journal of Music Education*

## Conclusion

Performing in music education serves purposes beyond skill acquisition: it develops discipline, creativity, collaboration, and cultural understanding. As Swanwick (2016) argued shortly before his death, music education's value lies in its capacity to develop musical thinking through engagement with music as performers, creators, and audience members. The interconnected development of singing and instrumental performance, grounded in inclusive practice and informed by research, remains central to delivering high-quality music education across all key stages in the UK curriculum.
