# Composing Across Key Stages

## Introduction

Composing is one of the three core strands of the National Curriculum for Music in England (alongside performing and listening/appraising) and represents a fundamental aspect of musical learning. The curriculum requires that all pupils "improvise and compose music for a range of purposes using the inter-related dimensions of music" (DfE, 2013). Martin Fautley, in his seminal work 'Assessing Musical Learning' (2010), argues that composing is not simply about creating products but involves complex cognitive processes including ideation, development, and evaluation. This knowledge file examines how composing develops progressively across key stages in UK schools.

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Swanwick's Developmental Spiral

Keith Swanwick's developmental model (Swanwick & Tillman, 1986) provides a crucial framework for understanding compositional progression. The spiral identifies eight developmental stages grouped into four main phases:

- **Materials** (0-4 years): Sensory and manipulative
- **Expression** (4-9 years): Personal expressiveness and vernacular
- **Form** (10-15 years): Speculative and idiomatic
- **Value** (15+ years): Symbolic and systematic

Swanwick's model emphasises that development is not strictly linear; students revisit earlier stages when encountering new musical materials or contexts. This has significant implications for curriculum planning, suggesting that even older students may need to explore the 'materials' of unfamiliar musical traditions before progressing to more sophisticated compositional work.

### Fautley's Compositional Process

Martin Fautley's research (2005, 2010) challenges traditional 'product-focused' approaches to composition. He identifies that student composers engage in iterative processes involving:

- **Generation** of musical ideas
- **Exploration** and experimentation
- **Organisation** of materials
- **Revision** and refinement

Fautley argues that assessment should focus on these processes rather than solely on finished products, and that teachers need to understand the 'compositional thinking' occurring during creative work.

### Green's Informal Learning Pedagogies

Lucy Green's work on informal learning (2002, 2008) has influenced approaches to composition, particularly at Key Stage 3. Her research demonstrates that allowing students to compose in friendship groups, work by ear, and choose their own music can lead to high levels of engagement and musical progression. Green's 'Musical Futures' pedagogy has been widely adopted in UK secondary schools, offering an alternative to traditional notation-based composition.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### National Curriculum Requirements

The Key Stage 1 Programme of Study requires pupils to:
- "Experiment with, create, select and combine sounds using the inter-related dimensions of music"

### Developmental Characteristics

At this stage, children are typically at Swanwick's 'Materials' and 'Expression' phases. Composing involves:

- **Sound exploration**: Investigating timbres through voices, body percussion, and classroom instruments
- **Improvisation**: Spontaneous music-making, often without clear product outcomes
- **Graphic notation**: Using pictures, symbols, and shapes to represent sounds
- **Pattern-making**: Creating simple rhythmic and melodic ostinati

### Practical Approaches

**Sound Stories**: Children compose soundscapes to accompany narratives, selecting appropriate timbres and dynamics. This integrates composing with cross-curricular literacy work.

**Question and Answer**: Working in pairs, children create musical 'conversations' using voices or instruments, developing understanding of phrase structure.

**Graphic Scores**: Children invent their own notations to record compositions, developing symbolic representation skills that precede staff notation (following Kodály's principle of 'sound before symbol').

### Assessment Considerations

Fautley emphasises that at this stage, assessment should focus on:
- Children's ability to make intentional choices about sounds
- Their developing musical vocabulary to describe their work
- Evidence of exploration and experimentation rather than polished products

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### National Curriculum Requirements

Pupils should:
- "Improvise and compose music for a range of purposes using the inter-related dimensions of music"
- "Listen with attention to detail and recall sounds with increasing aural memory"

### Developmental Characteristics

Students typically move through Swanwick's 'Expression' phase into 'Form', characterised by:

- **Structural awareness**: Understanding and using musical structures (ABA, rondo, verse-chorus)
- **Idiomatic composition**: Working within specific musical styles or genres
- **Staff notation**: Beginning to use conventional notation alongside graphic scores
- **Intentional expression**: Composing to convey specific moods, stories, or ideas

### Practical Approaches

**Melodic Composition**: Creating pentatonic melodies (avoiding semitones initially) using tuned percussion. This follows Kodály's pedagogy of introducing pentatonic scales before diatonic.

**Layered Ostinati**: Composing pieces using multiple repeating patterns at different pitch levels, developing understanding of texture and harmony.

**Structural Templates**: Using structures like rondo or theme and variations as frameworks for composition, helping students understand form.

**Film Music**: Composing music to accompany video clips, developing understanding of how music communicates meaning and mood.

### Inclusion and Differentiation

Gary Spruce (2015) argues for inclusive composition tasks that allow multiple entry points. At Key Stage 2, this might include:
- Providing both graphic and staff notation options
- Offering rhythmic or melodic starting points for those who need structure
- Using technology (iPads, Charanga, Soundtrap) to lower technical barriers
- Allowing varied ensemble sizes from solo to small group work

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### National Curriculum Requirements

Pupils should:
- "Improvise and compose; and extend and develop musical ideas by drawing on a range of musical structures, styles, genres and traditions"
- "Use staff and other relevant notations appropriately and accurately in a range of musical styles, genres and traditions"

### Developmental Characteristics

Students typically operate within Swanwick's 'Form' phase, demonstrating:

- **Genre awareness**: Understanding stylistic conventions across musical traditions
- **Technical competence**: Using instruments, technology, or voices with greater control
- **Critical evaluation**: Reflecting on and refining compositional work
- **Notation fluency**: Reading and writing in multiple notation systems

### Practical Approaches

**Genre-Based Projects**: Students compose in specific styles (blues, minimalism, reggae, Bollywood) developing understanding of idiom. This approach aligns with the National Curriculum's requirement to engage with diverse traditions.

**Remix and Arrangement**: Using DAW software (Logic, Cubase, Soundtrap, Bandlab), students manipulate existing materials, developing understanding of production techniques. Lucy Green's research supports this approach as validating students' existing musical interests.

**Song Writing**: Composing songs with lyrics, melody, harmony, and accompaniment. This integrates multiple musical skills and connects with students' informal musical practices.

**Chance and Aleatoric Techniques**: Exploring 20th-century compositional methods, including dice music and graphic scores, broadening students' understanding of what composition can be.

### Technology Integration

At Key Stage 3, technology becomes increasingly central:
- **DAW software**: GarageBand, Logic, Cubase, Soundtrap, Bandlab
- **Loop-based composition**: Using pre-recorded samples to create tracks
- **MIDI sequencing**: Programming performances using virtual instruments
- **Music notation software**: Sibelius, Finale, MuseScore, Dorico

Fautley (2014) notes that technology can democratise composition by removing technical barriers, but warns against compositions becoming 'cut and paste' exercises without genuine musical thinking.

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16): GCSE

### Examination Board Requirements

All GCSE specifications include composition, typically comprising 30% of the final mark:

**AQA Music (8271)**:
- Two compositions: one to a brief (30 marks) and one free composition (30 marks)
- Minimum combined duration: 3 minutes
- Compositions may be notated or recorded

**Edexcel Music (1MU0)**:
- Two compositions: one free composition and one to a brief linked to Areas of Study
- Minimum duration: 3 minutes combined
- Composition briefs assess understanding of musical devices and contexts

**OCR Music (J536)**:
- Two compositions: one free composition and one composition to a brief
- Total of 60 marks for composition component
- Briefs designed to assess application of musical elements

**WJEC Eduqas Music (C660)**:
- Two compositions totalling 4-6 minutes
- One composition must be in response to a brief linked to Areas of Study
- Emphasis on creative use of musical elements

### Developmental Focus

Students at this stage should demonstrate:

- **Stylistic authenticity**: Effective use of genre conventions when composing to briefs
- **Technical control**: Competent use of instruments, voices, or technology
- **Structural coherence**: Logical development and organisation of musical ideas
- **Expressive intent**: Clear communication of mood, character, or narrative

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Composition Portfolios**: Students maintain development logs showing ideation, experimentation, and refinement (Fautley's process model in action).

**Peer Feedback**: Structured peer evaluation develops critical listening and helps students articulate compositional decisions.

**Exemplar Analysis**: Studying successful GCSE compositions helps students understand assessment criteria and effective techniques.

**Iterative Drafting**: Multiple versions of compositions, with teacher feedback at key stages, supporting Fautley's emphasis on process.

### Common Challenges

Research by Fautley and Daubney (2019) identifies frequent issues:
- **Repetitive loops**: Compositions that lack development or variety
- **Style confusion**: Mixing incompatible genre elements without intentional fusion
- **Technical limitations**: Inability to realise musical ideas due to insufficient instrumental/software skills
- **Over-reliance on presets**: Using technology without creative transformation

## Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18): A-Level

### Examination Board Requirements

A-Level composition typically comprises 30-35% of final assessment:

**AQA Music (7272)**:
- Two compositions: one to a brief (35 marks) and one free composition (35 marks)
- Minimum combined duration: 4.5 minutes
- Briefs based on Western Classical Tradition or Popular Music/Jazz/Contemporary areas of study

**Edexcel Music (9MU0)**:
- One composition or three arrangements
- Minimum duration: 4-6 minutes
- Must be related to the techniques of one chosen area of study

**OCR Music (H543)**:
- Two compositions totalling 100 marks
- Composition 1 (40 marks): technique-focused, technical study of 2+ minutes
- Composition 2 (60 marks): free composition of 4+ minutes

**WJEC Eduqas Music (A670)**:
- Two compositions (one to a brief) totalling 6-8 minutes
- Requires written commentary explaining compositional processes and decisions

### Advanced Compositional Skills

Students at A-Level should demonstrate:

- **Sophisticated harmonic language**: Extended chords, modulation, chromaticism (in relevant styles)
- **Contrapuntal technique**: Effective use of independent melodic lines
- **Orchestration/instrumentation**: Idiomatic writing for varied ensembles
- **Formal sophistication**: Skilful use of extended structures (sonata form, through-composed, etc.)
- **Stylistic synthesis**: Ability to blend influences or subvert conventions creatively

### Swanwick's Value Phase

At this level, students may enter Swanwick's 'Value' phase, characterised by:
- **Symbolic thinking**: Using music to express complex abstract ideas
- **Systematic approach**: Applying theoretical knowledge consciously to compositional problems
- **Personal voice**: Developing individual compositional style whilst demonstrating technical mastery

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Analytical Composition**: Students compose 'in the style of' specific composers or pieces, deepening analytical understanding whilst developing technical skills.

**Portfolio Development**: Maintaining detailed composition logs showing research, sketches, drafts, and reflections (required by WJEC, beneficial for all specifications).

**Technology as Tool**: Using DAW, notation software, and virtual instruments as realisation tools rather than composition crutches. Students should understand that technology serves musical ideas, not vice versa.

**One-to-One Mentoring**: Individual tutorials focusing on each student's compositional voice and technical development, similar to conservatoire models.

### Assessment Criteria Across Boards

All A-Level specifications assess:
1. **Musical ideas**: Quality, originality, and development of material
2. **Musical techniques**: Skilful use of elements (melody, harmony, rhythm, texture, timbre)
3. **Structure**: Coherent organisation and form
4. **Stylistic understanding**: Appropriate and effective use of conventions (when composing to briefs)

## Cross-Cutting Pedagogical Principles

### The Importance of Listening

All composition pedagogy should integrate extensive listening. Swanwick's model emphasises that compositional development depends on rich aural experience. Students should:
- Analyse compositions in target styles before composing
- Listen to their own works-in-progress repeatedly and critically
- Experience live performances of peer compositions whenever possible

### Balancing Freedom and Structure

Fautley's research (2010) demonstrates that completely open-ended tasks can be paralysing, whilst overly prescriptive briefs limit creativity. Effective composition tasks provide:
- **Clear parameters** (instrumentation, duration, stylistic context)
- **Creative freedom** within those parameters
- **Scaffolding** appropriate to student developmental level

### The Role of Notation

Following Kodály's principle, sound should precede symbol. However, notation serves important functions:
- **Communication**: Sharing ideas with performers
- **Refinement**: Seeing ideas on paper enables reflection and revision
- **Analysis**: Understanding theoretical concepts through practical application

Teachers should accept multiple notation forms (staff notation, TAB, graphic scores, DAW piano rolls, chord charts) as appropriate to musical context.

### Inclusion and Access

Gary Spruce (2015) argues that composition teaching must not privilege Western classical approaches. Inclusive practice involves:
- **Diverse repertoire**: Studying and composing in varied traditions
- **Multiple pathways**: Accepting aural/oral composition alongside notated work
- **Technology**: Using assistive technologies and accessible software
- **Differentiated expectations**: Recognising that compositional sophistication can exist independently of notational literacy

### Formative Assessment

Fautley emphasises that effective composition teaching requires ongoing formative assessment:
- **Observation**: Noting students' working processes, not just products
- **Questioning**: Probing students' compositional thinking and intentions
- **Feedback**: Specific, actionable comments on drafts
- **Peer assessment**: Structured frameworks for students to evaluate each other's work
- **Self-assessment**: Developing students' capacity to evaluate their own compositions against criteria

## Progression Models

### Ofsted's Research Review

Ofsted's Research Review for Music (2021) identifies progression in composing as:
- Moving from exploring sounds to controlling musical elements intentionally
- Developing from simple patterns to complex structures
- Progressing from imitation to innovation
- Advancing from single-element focus to multi-element integration

### Subject Knowledge Requirements

Teachers require:
- **Theoretical knowledge**: Understanding harmony, form, orchestration appropriate to key stage
- **Stylistic knowledge**: Familiarity with diverse musical traditions students will encounter
- **Technical knowledge**: Competence with relevant instruments, notation, and technology
- **Pedagogical knowledge**: Understanding of compositional development and effective teaching strategies (Fautley, 2010)

## Contemporary Challenges and Debates

### Technology Dependence

Some researchers worry that technology-mediated composition may bypass important musical thinking. Savage (2005) argues for 'authentic' technology integration where digital tools genuinely extend creative possibilities rather than replacing fundamental skills.

### Time Constraints

Fautley and Daubney's research (2019) reveals that many secondary schools allocate insufficient time for sustained compositional work, resulting in superficial engagement. Quality composition requires:
- Extended projects rather than single-lesson tasks
- Time for iteration and refinement
- Opportunities for performance and feedback

### Equity Issues

Not all students have access to instruments or technology at home, creating equity challenges. Green's informal learning approach and school-based technology can partially address this, but systemic inequalities persist.

### Assessment Validity

Debates continue about whether composition can be reliably assessed using mark schemes. Fautley (2010) notes the tension between necessary standardisation for examination purposes and the inherently subjective nature of artistic evaluation.

## Conclusion

Effective composition pedagogy across key stages requires:
- Understanding developmental models (Swanwick) to pitch tasks appropriately
- Focusing on process as well as product (Fautley)
- Embracing diverse musical traditions and pedagogies (Green, Spruce)
- Integrating listening, performing, and composing (Kodály)
- Using technology purpos
