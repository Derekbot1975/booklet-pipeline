# A Level Music Specifications

## Overview of A Level Music in England and Wales

A Level Music represents the culmination of secondary music education in the UK, typically studied by students aged 16-18 in Years 12 and 13. The qualification is regulated by Ofqual in England and Qualifications Wales, with specifications designed by the four main awarding bodies: AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. These specifications were reformed in 2016 for first teaching in September 2016, following the broader A Level reforms that moved to linear assessment and removed AS Level contributions to final A Level grades.

Martin Fautley (2010) has argued that A Level Music must balance the development of specialist musical knowledge with the need to prepare students for diverse pathways, including university study, conservatoire training, and portfolio careers in music. The current specifications reflect this through their integration of performing, composing, and appraising.

## Common Framework Across Specifications

### Assessment Objectives

All four awarding bodies must adhere to common Assessment Objectives (AOs) as mandated by Ofqual. These are:

**AO1: Interpret musical ideas through performing** (25-35% of total marks)
- Demonstrate technical and expressive control
- Show understanding of musical elements and context
- Perform as a soloist or ensemble member

**AO2: Create and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherent use of musical elements** (25-35% of total marks)
- Compose integrated compositions
- Apply technical skills
- Develop musical ideas coherently

**AO3: Demonstrate and apply musical knowledge** (40% minimum of total marks)
- Analyse and evaluate music
- Make critical judgements
- Understand musical contexts and language

**AO4: Use appraising skills to make evaluative and critical judgements** (10% minimum of total marks)
- Connect musical elements, context, and language
- Make informed judgements

### Core Content Requirements

Ofqual stipulates that all specifications must include:
- Performance as a soloist and/or ensemble member
- Composition with technical control of musical elements
- Knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts, and language
- Development of aural perception and analysis skills

## Individual Awarding Body Specifications

### AQA Music (7272)

**Component 1: Appraising Music** (40%, 2 hours 30 minutes written exam)

AQA's distinctive approach focuses on six Areas of Study:
1. The Western Classical Tradition 1650-1910 (compulsory)
2. Pop Music
3. Music for Media
4. Music for Theatre
5. Jazz
6. Contemporary Traditional Music

Students must study the compulsory area plus one optional area. The specification requires detailed study of six set works, rotated on a two-year cycle. For 2024-2026, these include Mendelssohn's *Violin Concerto in E minor*, Shostakovich's *String Quartet No. 8*, and area-specific works.

**Component 2: Performance** (35%, internally assessed, externally moderated)
- Minimum 10 minutes of performance (can be solo, ensemble, or combination)
- Must include technical and expressive control

**Component 3: Composition** (25%, internally assessed, externally moderated)
- Two compositions totalling at least 4 minutes 30 seconds
- Free composition plus composition in response to a brief set by AQA
- Brief relates to Areas of Study 2-6

Keith Swanwick's influence is evident in AQA's emphasis on stylistic awareness and the integration of composition briefs that connect to studied repertoire.

### Edexcel Music (9MU0)

**Component 1: Performing** (30%, internally assessed)
- Minimum 8 minutes of performance
- At least Grade 5 standard (ABRSM or equivalent)
- Solo and/or ensemble performances

**Component 2: Composing** (30%, internally assessed)
- Two compositions totalling at least 6 minutes
- Free composition plus composition to a brief
- Briefs rotate across four Areas of Study

**Component 3: Appraising** (40%, 2 hours written exam)

Edexcel structures content around four Areas of Study:
1. Vocal Music
2. Instrumental Music
3. Music for Film
4. Popular Music and Jazz

The specification requires study of eight set works (two per area), with works changing every two years. Current set works (2024-2026) include Purcell's *Music for a While*, Reich's *New York Counterpoint*, and Schwartz's *Defying Gravity* from *Wicked*.

Lucy Green's work on informal learning and popular music pedagogy has informed Edexcel's approach to popular music study, encouraging analysis of recordings and recognition of diverse musical practices.

### OCR Music (H543)

**Component 1: Performing** (30%)
- Solo and/or ensemble performance (minimum 10 minutes)
- Performance commentary required

**Component 2: Composing** (30%)
- Two compositions totalling 7-10 minutes
- Free composition plus composition to brief
- Technical studies submission demonstrating compositional development

**Component 3: Listening and Appraising** (40%, 2 hours 15 minutes)

OCR's distinctive feature is its three Areas of Study with integrated set works:
1. The Western Classical Tradition 1650-1910
2. Popular Music 1960-present
3. Innovations in Music 1900-present

Six set works are prescribed, rotating every two years. OCR emphasises integrated study through connections between areas, reflecting Swanwick's spiral curriculum model where students revisit concepts at increasing depth.

**Component 4: Optional Extended Response (ERQ)**
- Allows students to demonstrate depth in one area
- Encourages independent research skills

### WJEC Eduqas Music (A680QS)

**Component 1: Performing** (35%, minimum 10-12 minutes)
- Solo and/or ensemble
- Performance log documenting development

**Component 2: Composing** (25%)
- Two compositions totalling minimum 4 minutes 30 seconds
- One free composition, one to set brief
- Composition portfolio including sketches and evaluation

**Component 3: Appraising** (40%, 2 hours 30 minutes)

WJEC Eduqas organises content into four Areas of Study:
1. The Western Classical Tradition
2. Rock and Pop
3. Musical Theatre
4. Jazz

Four set works are prescribed (one per area), rotating every two years. The Welsh specification reflects cultural context through inclusion of Welsh-language musical theatre and recognition of Celtic musical traditions.

## Pedagogical Considerations

### Integration of Components

Martin Fautley (2014) emphasises that effective A Level Music teaching integrates performing, composing, and listening rather than treating them as discrete activities. This reflects the interrelated nature of musical understanding articulated in Swanwick's CLASP model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition, Skill acquisition, Performance).

**Practical implications for teachers:**
- Use set works as models for compositional techniques
- Develop aural skills through active engagement with repertoire
- Encourage performance of set work excerpts to deepen understanding
- Link theoretical concepts to practical music-making

### Addressing Diverse Musical Backgrounds

Lucy Green's research (2008) on informal learning pathways highlights that A Level students arrive with varied musical experiences. Some have formal instrumental training; others are self-taught popular musicians or producers working with technology.

**Teaching strategies:**
- Differentiate compositional approaches to accommodate notation-based and technology-based composers
- Validate diverse performance practices (rock bands, DJs, electronic performers)
- Use peer learning and musical 'modelling' alongside formal instruction
- Recognise that musical understanding can be demonstrated through multiple means

### Developing Critical Listening Skills

All specifications require sophisticated analytical and evaluative skills. Gary Spruce (2013) argues that developing critical musicianship involves moving beyond descriptive commentary to interpretative and evaluative responses that recognise music's social and cultural dimensions.

**Effective approaches include:**
- Regular 'listening logs' encouraging personal response alongside technical analysis
- Comparative analysis linking set works to wider repertoire
- Focus on musical meaning and expressive intent, not just technical features
- Development of specialist vocabulary appropriate to different genres

### Technology Integration

Contemporary A Level Music necessarily involves music technology, whether for composition, performance, or appraising. This reflects the realities of professional musical practice.

**Key considerations:**
- DAW (Digital Audio Workstation) skills for recording, sequencing, and producing
- Notation software for score-based composition
- Audio analysis tools for detailed listening
- Recording equipment for capturing performances

### Addressing the Attainment Gap

Research by Fautley and Savage (2019) identifies persistent attainment gaps in A Level Music based on socioeconomic background, partially related to access to instrumental tuition and technology. Teachers must actively work to mitigate these inequalities.

**Strategies include:**
- Providing school-based access to instruments and technology
- Offering additional support for students with less formal training
- Valuing diverse musical starting points and genres
- Explicit teaching of musical concepts that privately educated students may have encountered earlier

## Assessment and Moderation

### Internal Assessment Standards

Performing and composing components are internally assessed by centres and externally moderated by awarding bodies. Teachers must:
- Apply mark schemes accurately and consistently
- Maintain assessment records and audio/video evidence
- Submit marks by specified deadlines (typically mid-May)
- Provide representative samples for moderation

**Common challenges:**
- Over-generous marking, particularly in performance
- Insufficient evidence of compositional process
- Inconsistent standards across different teachers in departments
- Technical issues with recordings

### Moderation Processes

Awarding bodies conduct moderation through:
- Sample-based moderation (reviewing submitted evidence)
- Adjustment of centre marks where necessary
- Feedback reports to centres
- Standardisation meetings for centre assessors

### External Examination

The appraising components are externally assessed through written examinations. Question types typically include:
- Analysis of unfamiliar music (aural excerpts)
- Essay questions on set works and Areas of Study
- Comparative questions requiring synthesis across repertoire
- Extended response questions demonstrating depth of understanding

## Links to Progression Routes

### University Music Degrees

A Level Music provides essential preparation for undergraduate music study. Russell Group universities typically require A Level Music for single honours degrees, alongside evidence of instrumental/vocal competence (often Grade 8 standard or equivalent).

Universities value:
- Analytical skills developed through appraising components
- Independence in composition
- Breadth of musical knowledge across periods and styles
- Critical thinking about music in cultural contexts

### Conservatoire Training

For students pursuing performance diplomas or degrees at conservatoires (Royal College of Music, Royal Academy of Music, Guildhall, etc.), A Level Music provides:
- Theoretical knowledge required for conservatoire studies
- Compositional skills for performers
- Understanding of musical contexts and repertoire
- Academic component alongside specialist performance training

Many conservatoire applicants combine A Level Music with Diploma-level performance qualifications (DipABRSM, ARSM).

### Music Industry and Portfolio Careers

A Level Music increasingly recognises diverse career pathways beyond traditional performance and teaching. The specifications' inclusion of music technology, popular music, and media music reflects industry realities.

Skills developed include:
- Multi-disciplinary musical competence
- Project management through composition portfolios
- Collaborative working through ensemble performance
- Critical evaluation of music in commercial contexts

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### Curriculum Balance

Ongoing debates concern the balance between Western classical music and other traditions. Gary Spruce (2017) has argued that A Level specifications still privilege Western art music, despite broadening content. Critics suggest that the 'Areas of Study' approach can compartmentalise rather than integrate diverse musics.

### Assessment Authenticity

Martin Fautley (2020) questions whether current assessment models authentically reflect musical practices, particularly for composers working primarily with technology or popular musicians whose music-making is inherently collaborative. The individual assessment model may not capture the reality of musical creativity.

### Digital Literacy

As music increasingly exists in digital forms, questions arise about whether A Level Music adequately prepares students for digital musical practices, including streaming culture, social media music communities, and AI-assisted composition.

### Inclusivity and Accessibility

Work by Deborah Blair and others highlights that A Level Music remains disproportionately accessed by students from privileged backgrounds. Addressing this requires:
- Curriculum content that reflects diverse musical cultures
- Recognition of multiple pathways to musical expertise
- Support structures for students without private tuition
- Technology provision to enable equitable access

## Practical Guidance for Curriculum Leaders

### Departmental Planning

Successful A Level Music provision requires:
- Two-year scheme of work integrating all components
- Regular performance opportunities (termly recitals)
- Scheduled composition workshops and tutorials
- Systematic coverage of set works and Areas of Study
- Technology resources and training

### Staffing and Expertise

Consider:
- Staff expertise across required musical styles and periods
- CPD for new set works and specification updates
- Visiting specialists for particular Areas of Study
- Links with local music services and organisations

### Resource Management

Essential resources include:
- Recordings of set works (multiple interpretations)
- Scores and analytical materials
- Technology suite with adequate hardware/software
- Practice rooms for independent work
- Professional recording equipment for NEA submissions

### Quality Assurance

Maintain standards through:
- Internal standardisation of marking
- Consultation with awarding body support materials
- Attendance at examination board training events
- Networking with other centres for moderation practice
- Regular review of student outcomes and department performance

## Conclusion

A Level Music specifications represent sophisticated qualifications that develop comprehensive musicianship through integrated performing, composing, and appraising. While the four awarding bodies share common principles, their distinctive approaches allow centres to select specifications matching their context, expertise, and student cohorts.

Effective delivery requires teachers who can bridge formal and informal musical practices, integrate technology authentically, and develop students' critical musicianship alongside technical competence. As Swanwick argued, music education at this level should develop students who can engage with music as 'symbolic forms' – understanding both technical craft and expressive meaning within cultural contexts.

The ongoing evolution of A Level Music must continue addressing questions of accessibility, diversity, and authenticity to ensure the qualification serves all students and reflects the realities of contemporary musical life.
