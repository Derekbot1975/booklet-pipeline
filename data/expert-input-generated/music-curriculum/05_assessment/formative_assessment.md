# Formative Assessment in Music

## Introduction

Formative assessment in music education represents an ongoing process of gathering, interpreting and acting upon evidence of student learning to inform teaching and enhance musical development. Unlike summative assessment, which measures achievement at a fixed point, formative assessment is embedded within the learning process itself, providing continuous feedback that shapes both teaching and learning.

Martin Fautley's seminal work *Assessment in Music Education* (2010, Oxford University Press) establishes formative assessment as fundamental to effective music pedagogy, arguing that assessment should be 'assessment for learning' rather than merely 'assessment of learning'. This distinction is crucial in music, where creative processes and aesthetic understanding cannot always be quantified through conventional testing methods.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Assessment for Learning in Music

The Assessment Reform Group's principles of Assessment for Learning (AfL), widely adopted across UK education since the 1990s, have particular resonance in music. Fautley and Savage's *Assessment for Learning and Teaching in Secondary Schools* (2008, Learning Matters) adapts these principles specifically for music education, emphasising that effective formative assessment:

- Is embedded in lesson planning and teaching
- Shares learning intentions and success criteria with pupils
- Helps pupils understand what quality looks like in musical contexts
- Provides feedback that moves learning forward
- Encourages self and peer assessment
- Uses questioning to elicit and explore musical understanding

Keith Swanwick's *Musical Knowledge: Intuition, Analysis and Music Education* (1994, Routledge) provides a theoretical framework through which formative assessment can be understood. His model of musical development and understanding informs how teachers might assess progression in materials, expression and form across different musical activities.

### Lucy Green's Informal Learning Approaches

Lucy Green's *Music, Informal Learning and the School: A New Classroom Pedagogy* (2008, Ashgate) has profoundly influenced formative assessment practices in UK music education. Her research into how popular musicians learn informally suggests that formative assessment should incorporate peer learning, self-directed exploration and authentic musical practices. Green advocates for assessment practices that recognise learning happening through copying recordings, improvisation and collaborative music-making, challenging traditional teacher-directed assessment models.

## National Curriculum Context

### Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Music in England (2013) establishes that pupils should be taught to:

- Perform, listen to, review and evaluate music across a range of historical periods, genres, styles and traditions
- Learn to sing and use their voices, create and compose music, and understand and explore how music is created

While the curriculum does not prescribe specific formative assessment methods, it requires schools to develop their own assessment frameworks that demonstrate progression. This creates both opportunity and challenge for music departments.

### Key Stage Expectations

**Key Stage 1 and 2**: Formative assessment focuses on developing confidence in performing, composing and listening. Teachers observe participation, musical gestures, rhythmic accuracy and pitch matching. Zoltán Kodály's principles of sequential musical development through singing inform many primary assessment practices, with teachers using hand signs, rhythm patterns and melodic fragments to assess understanding.

**Key Stage 3**: Assessment becomes more sophisticated, incorporating musical literacy, technical development on instruments, and critical understanding. The National Curriculum expects pupils to 'develop their creativity and ability to connect different dimensions of music', requiring formative assessment that captures both technical and aesthetic development.

**Key Stage 4 and 5**: GCSE and A-Level specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) include formal assessment criteria, but formative assessment remains essential for preparing students for final examinations, particularly in performance and composition portfolios.

## Practical Strategies for Formative Assessment

### Observation and Questioning

Fautley (2010) emphasises that much musical learning is ephemeral and occurs in performance. Therefore, systematic observation is crucial. Teachers should:

- Use focused observation schedules for rehearsals and group work
- Ask open-ended questions that probe musical understanding ('Why did you choose that tempo?', 'How does the harmony affect the mood?')
- Employ 'pose, pause, pounce, bounce' questioning techniques to develop musical reasoning
- Record observations using audio/video technology for later reflection

Gary Spruce, in *Teaching Music in Secondary Schools* (2001, RoutledgeFalmer), advocates for questioning that moves beyond technical accuracy to address musical intention, interpretation and aesthetic judgement.

### Self and Peer Assessment

Research by Lebler (2007) in *Assessment in Music Education* demonstrates that peer assessment develops critical listening skills and musical independence. Effective practices include:

- Providing clear success criteria derived from learning objectives
- Using structured peer feedback forms with prompts addressing specific musical elements
- Implementing 'two stars and a wish' frameworks adapted for musical contexts
- Facilitating peer mentoring where more experienced musicians support others
- Recording performances for self-evaluation against stated criteria

Lucy Green's Musical Futures programme exemplifies this approach, with students assessing their own and peers' work against self-selected goals and authentic musical standards.

### Feedback Strategies

Effective feedback in music requires immediacy and specificity. Fautley distinguishes between:

**Evaluative feedback**: Judgemental statements ('That was good/bad')
**Descriptive feedback**: Specific observations about what occurred
**Developmental feedback**: Information that suggests how to improve

Research by Hattie and Timperley (2007) shows feedback is most effective when it addresses:
- The task (accuracy, neatness, correctness)
- The process (strategies used to complete the task)
- Self-regulation (self-monitoring, effort, engagement)

In music, this translates to feedback on technical execution, musical decision-making processes and reflective practice. Teachers should:

- Provide verbal feedback during practical activities rather than written summaries afterwards
- Use comparative modelling (playing/singing correct and incorrect versions)
- Employ the 'feedback sandwich' cautiously, ensuring developmental points are specific and actionable
- Record verbal feedback using audio tools for students to revisit
- Balance positive reinforcement with challenging targets

### Learning Intentions and Success Criteria

Shirley Clarke's work on formative assessment, whilst not music-specific, has been widely adapted. In music education, learning intentions should:

- Distinguish between process objectives ('We are learning to compose using ternary form') and product objectives ('We will create a 32-bar composition')
- Use musical vocabulary appropriate to the key stage
- Connect to the National Curriculum attainment targets
- Reference specific musical elements (pitch, duration, dynamics, tempo, timbre, texture, structure)

Success criteria in music must account for interpretative and creative variation. Rather than prescribing single correct answers, criteria should establish parameters within which musical choices can be justified. For example: 'Your composition will include at least two contrasting sections' allows creative freedom whilst providing clear assessment focus.

### Assessment During Composing

Composing presents particular assessment challenges as the creative process is non-linear and often exploratory. Fautley's research into composing in the classroom (2005) reveals that students benefit from:

- Regular checkpoints during extended composing tasks
- Documentation of musical ideas through notation, recording or written annotation
- Opportunities to try multiple versions and receive feedback on drafts
- Discussion of compositional intentions before, during and after the process
- Use of composition portfolios showing development over time

Teachers should assess both the compositional product and the composing process, recognising that musical thinking develops through experimentation, revision and reflection.

### Assessment During Performing

Performance assessment should extend beyond technical accuracy to encompass musicianship, interpretation and ensemble skills. Formative strategies include:

- Regular low-stakes performances within lessons to reduce anxiety
- Section rehearsals where specific technical challenges are addressed
- Video recording practice sessions for student self-review
- Use of practice logs or diaries with reflective commentary
- Masterclass formats where students perform for peers and receive immediate feedback

Swanwick's C(L)A(S)P model (Composition, Literature studies, Audition [listening], Skill acquisition, Performance) suggests that performance should be assessed holistically within broader musical activity rather than in isolation.

### Assessment of Listening and Appraising

Listening assessments should evaluate the depth and sophistication of musical understanding. Formative approaches include:

- 'Think aloud' protocols where students verbalise their listening processes
- Comparative listening tasks with structured discussion prompts
- Musical mapping activities (graphical representations of structure, dynamics, texture)
- Use of musical vocabulary checklists appropriate to the key stage
- Listening journals documenting responses to music over time

The Model Music Curriculum (2021), whilst non-statutory, provides detailed progression frameworks for listening that can inform formative assessment practices across key stages.

## Technology-Enhanced Formative Assessment

### Digital Tools and Platforms

Contemporary music education increasingly employs technology for formative assessment:

- **Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs)**: Software like GarageBand, BandLab and Soundtrap allows teachers to provide time-stamped feedback on compositions
- **Learning Platforms**: Google Classroom, Microsoft Teams and specialist music platforms like Charanga enable submission and feedback on recordings
- **Assessment Apps**: Tools like Showbie, Seesaw and Class Charts facilitate photo/video/audio evidence collection
- **Notation Software**: Sibelius, Dorico and MuseScore allow teachers to annotate scores digitally

### Recording and Reflection

Regular audio/video recording serves multiple formative purposes:

- Creates evidence of progression over time
- Enables students to self-assess against earlier performances
- Facilitates parental engagement with musical learning
- Provides material for formative discussion in one-to-one tutorials
- Reduces reliance on memory when assessing ephemeral performances

However, Fautley cautions against technology becoming an end in itself rather than serving pedagogical purposes.

## Assessment at GCSE and A-Level

### Exam Board Requirements

While specifications include summative assessment, formative assessment prepares students for final submissions:

**AQA GCSE Music**: Requires portfolio of compositions and performances with teacher marking to defined criteria. Formative assessment tracks progress against specific mark schemes.

**Edexcel GCSE Music**: Similar structure but different assessment objectives. Teachers must familiarise students with what constitutes 'technical control', 'expressive qualities' and 'musical coherence'.

**OCR GCSE Music**: Integrated portfolio approach requires ongoing formative assessment across performing, composing and appraising.

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Music**: Distinctive assessment structure requiring formative tracking against Welsh-specific criteria.

At A-Level, all boards require extended composition portfolios and recitals where formative feedback throughout the course is essential for achieving higher grades.

### Moderation and Standardisation

Formative assessment at Key Stage 4 and 5 must align with examination criteria. Departments should:

- Conduct internal moderation sessions where teachers assess sample work against mark schemes
- Attend exam board training and standardisation events
- Use previous years' exemplar materials for calibrating judgements
- Implement 'mock' assessments mirroring examination conditions

## Progression and Tracking

### Defining Progression in Music

Progression in music is complex and non-linear. Swanwick and Tillman's developmental spiral (1986) suggests musical understanding develops through layers of mastery (materials), imitation (expression), imaginative play (form) and metacognition (value), cycling through these stages at increasing sophistication.

Fautley argues that progression frameworks should recognise:

- Technical development (instrumental/vocal skill)
- Aural acuity (discrimination and memory)
- Contextual understanding (historical, cultural, social)
- Musical creation (composing, improvising, arranging)
- Critical engagement (analysing, evaluating, responding)

### Assessment Without Levels

Following the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014, schools have developed diverse approaches to tracking progression. Effective models:

- Use musical descriptors rather than numerical grades for Key Stage 3
- Reference the National Curriculum attainment targets directly
- Employ 'working towards/working at/working beyond' age-related expectations
- Create department-specific progression matrices for performing, composing and listening
- Map progression of specific musical elements (e.g., rhythmic complexity, harmonic understanding)

The Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music (ABRSM) graded examination system, whilst external, provides a widely understood progression framework that many schools reference informally.

## Inclusion and Differentiation

### Accessible Assessment Practices

Formative assessment must be inclusive, recognising diverse musical strengths and learning needs:

- **SEND considerations**: Adapt success criteria for students with special educational needs; use multisensory approaches; allow extended time; provide visual and kinaesthetic supports
- **Cultural responsiveness**: Value diverse musical traditions equally; assess using musical examples familiar to students; recognise that musical 'quality' is culturally constructed
- **Differentiation by outcome**: Set open-ended tasks where students demonstrate understanding at their level rather than predetermined endpoints
- **Personalised targets**: Develop individual learning goals reflecting each student's musical journey

The Music Mark inclusion statement emphasises that all pupils should experience success in music regardless of background or ability.

## Challenges and Limitations

### Subjectivity in Musical Judgement

Assessing creative and performative work inevitably involves subjective judgement. Fautley acknowledges this challenge, arguing that:

- Transparency about assessment criteria reduces but does not eliminate subjectivity
- Musical value judgements are culturally and contextually situated
- Teachers must distinguish between personal taste and educational assessment
- Moderation and professional dialogue help calibrate subjective judgements

### Time Constraints

Practical music teaching involves managing multiple simultaneous activities, making systematic formative assessment challenging. Strategies include:

- Focused assessment: assessing specific students or groups each lesson rather than everyone
- Strategic recording: capturing key moments rather than entire lessons
- Student self-assessment: sharing assessment responsibility
- Efficient feedback mechanisms: verbal rather than extensive written comments

### Assessment Burden

The Department for Education's 2016 workload review highlighted assessment as a major burden. In music, this manifests as:

- Excessive recording keeping without pedagogical benefit
- Detailed written feedback when verbal would suffice
- Over-assessment reducing time for actual music-making

Fautley advocates for 'assessment-light' approaches that gather sufficient evidence without overwhelming teachers or disrupting musical learning.

## Professional Development and Departmental Practice

### Developing Assessment Literacy

Effective formative assessment requires teachers to develop expertise in:

- Understanding progression in musical learning
- Designing appropriate success criteria for diverse musical activities
- Providing feedback that balances encouragement with challenge
- Using assessment information to modify teaching
- Engaging students as assessment partners

Professional organisations including Music Mark, the Music Education Council and subject associations provide continuing professional development focused on assessment.

### Departmental Consistency

Music departments should establish:

- Shared understanding of progression through moderation activities
- Agreed assessment procedures and recording methods
- Common approaches to feedback
- Regular review of assessment effectiveness
- Alignment between Key Stage 3 and Key Stage 4 assessment practices

## Conclusion

Formative assessment in music education is fundamentally about improving learning through responsive teaching informed by evidence of student understanding. As Fautley argues, assessment should be integral to musical learning rather than an addition to it. When implemented effectively, formative assessment:

- Makes learning intentions transparent and achievable
- Provides feedback that students can act upon
- Develops musical independence and critical judgement
- Recognises the multifaceted nature of musical achievement
- Supports progression across diverse musical activities

The challenge for UK music educators is balancing the need for systematic evidence of progression with the creative, performative and aesthetic dimensions that make music education distinctive. By drawing on research and established practice whilst remaining responsive to individual contexts, teachers can develop formative assessment approaches that genuinely enhance musical learning.
