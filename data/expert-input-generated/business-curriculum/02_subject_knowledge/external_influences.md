# External Influences on Business

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

External influences on business represent a fundamental component of UK Business Studies education, appearing consistently across GCSE and A-level specifications. The topic examines how factors beyond a business's direct control affect strategic decision-making, operational efficiency, and long-term sustainability. As Ian Marcousé emphasises in his widely-used textbook *Business Studies* (Hodder Education), understanding external influences is essential for developing students' analytical skills and their appreciation of business operating within a dynamic, interconnected environment.

All major UK exam boards—AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas—incorporate external influences throughout their specifications, though terminology and emphasis vary. The 2015 reforms to both GCSE and A-level Business qualifications strengthened the requirement for students to analyse real-world business contexts, making external influences more prominent than in previous specifications.

## National Curriculum Framework

At Key Stage 4 (GCSE), external influences typically appear under themes such as "Influences on Business" or "The External Environment." Students aged 14-16 must understand how economic, legal, political, technological, social, and environmental factors affect business decisions. The Department for Education's subject content requirements (2015) specify that GCSE Business students should be able to "explain how different factors can impact on business success."

At Key Stage 5 (A-level), external influences become more sophisticated. Students aged 16-18 analyse these factors using formal frameworks such as PESTLE analysis, evaluate strategic responses, and assess long-term implications. A-level specifications require deeper understanding of topics including globalisation, emerging markets, and complex regulatory environments.

## The PESTLE Framework

### Political Influences

Political factors encompass government policies, political stability, taxation, trade restrictions, and regulatory frameworks. As Peter Stimpson and Cambridge International AS and A Level Business (Cambridge University Press) explains, political influences can fundamentally reshape business environments through legislative change.

**Key political influences include:**

- **Government economic policy**: Fiscal policy (taxation and government spending) directly affects consumer disposable income and business profitability. For example, changes to Corporation Tax rates (currently 25% for profits over £250,000 as of April 2023) impact retained profit available for reinvestment.

- **Trade policy and Brexit implications**: Since the UK's departure from the EU, businesses face new customs procedures, regulatory divergence, and changes to labour mobility. Mark Surridge and Andrew Gillespie's *AQA Business* textbook (Hodder Education) provides extensive coverage of Brexit's multifaceted impacts on UK businesses.

- **Political stability**: The degree of governmental stability affects business confidence and long-term investment decisions. Political uncertainty, such as during election periods or constitutional debates, typically correlates with reduced capital investment.

**Teaching implications**: Teachers should use contemporary political developments as case studies. Jim Riley's Tutor2u resources consistently demonstrate how current political events—from Budget announcements to trade negotiations—provide excellent teaching material. Students should track policy announcements using government publications such as HM Treasury Budget documents and departmental policy papers.

### Economic Influences

Economic factors represent perhaps the most immediately visible external influence on business operations. These factors affect both supply-side costs and demand-side consumer behaviour.

**Key economic influences include:**

- **Economic growth and the business cycle**: Measured by GDP (Gross Domestic Product), economic growth patterns significantly influence business performance. During periods of economic growth, consumer confidence rises, leading to increased discretionary spending. Conversely, recession (technically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth) reduces demand across most sectors. The 2008-2009 financial crisis and 2020 COVID-19 recession provide powerful teaching examples.

- **Inflation**: The rate at which prices rise affects both businesses' costs and consumers' purchasing power. The Bank of England targets 2% CPI (Consumer Price Index) inflation annually. When inflation exceeds this target significantly—as occurred in 2022-2023 when inflation reached 11.1%—businesses face pressure from both rising input costs and squeezed consumer real incomes. Ian Marcousé emphasises that students must understand the distinction between cost-push inflation (rising production costs) and demand-pull inflation (excessive demand).

- **Interest rates**: Set by the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee, the base rate influences borrowing costs throughout the economy. Higher interest rates increase the cost of servicing debt, discourage investment in capital projects (as the opportunity cost rises), and reduce consumer spending on credit-financed purchases. Nancy Wall's work on financial decision-making highlights how interest rate changes affect business investment appraisal.

- **Exchange rates**: The value of sterling against other currencies affects importers and exporters differently. A stronger pound makes imports cheaper but exports less competitive internationally; a weaker pound has the opposite effect. Approximately 45% of UK GDP derives from international trade (ONS data), making exchange rates particularly significant.

- **Unemployment**: Both an indicator of economic health and a factor affecting consumer spending power, unemployment rates influence aggregate demand. High unemployment reduces consumer confidence and spending whilst increasing the available labour pool.

**Teaching implications**: Teachers should incorporate live economic data from the Office for National Statistics (ONS), Bank of England Monetary Policy Reports, and HM Treasury forecasts. Economics and Business Studies departments should collaborate to ensure students understand fundamental economic concepts before applying them to business contexts. The Tutor2u Economics and Business Conference (annual event) provides excellent professional development on teaching economic influences effectively.

### Social and Cultural Influences

Social factors encompass demographic changes, cultural shifts, lifestyle trends, and evolving consumer attitudes. Peter Stimpson notes that social change often operates gradually but can fundamentally transform market landscapes.

**Key social influences include:**

- **Demographic change**: The UK's ageing population represents a significant demographic trend. According to ONS projections, by 2041, one in seven people will be aged over 75. This shift creates opportunities in healthcare, leisure, and financial services whilst challenging pension provision and social care funding.

- **Changing attitudes and values**: Consumer preferences increasingly favour ethical businesses. Research by Mintel and the Co-operative Bank's Ethical Consumer Markets Report demonstrates consistent growth in ethical consumerism, with UK ethical market sales exceeding £122 billion in 2021.

- **Lifestyle changes**: Remote working (accelerated by COVID-19), health consciousness, and environmental awareness reshape demand patterns across sectors. Businesses must adapt product offerings, marketing approaches, and operational practices accordingly.

- **Educational attainment**: Rising educational levels affect both labour market skills availability and consumer sophistication. More educated consumers typically demand higher quality, more ethical products and are more discerning in their purchasing decisions.

- **Cultural diversity**: The UK's multicultural society creates both opportunities (diverse market segments) and challenges (need for cultural sensitivity in marketing and product development).

**Teaching implications**: Social influences provide excellent opportunities for student research projects. Students can conduct primary research through surveys or interviews to identify changing social attitudes in their local area. Mark Surridge's emphasis on contemporary business examples is particularly relevant here—teachers should use current product launches, advertising campaigns, and business controversies to illustrate social influences in action.

### Technological Influences

Technology represents one of the most rapidly evolving external influences. Technological change drives creative destruction (Joseph Schumpeter's concept), whereby innovations simultaneously create new opportunities and render existing business models obsolete.

**Key technological influences include:**

- **E-commerce and digital platforms**: Online retail transformed the UK business landscape. According to ONS data, internet sales now account for approximately 27% of total retail sales. This digital transformation affects not only retailers but entire supply chains, logistics operations, and payment systems.

- **Automation and artificial intelligence**: Manufacturing automation, process automation, and increasingly AI-driven decision-making affect productivity, employment patterns, and competitive dynamics. The McKinsey Global Institute estimates that up to 30% of current work activities could be automated by 2030.

- **Communication technology**: High-speed internet, cloud computing, and mobile connectivity enable remote working, global collaboration, and data-driven decision-making. These technologies reduce barriers to entry in many sectors whilst increasing the pace of competitive response.

- **Social media and digital marketing**: Platforms such as Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube have fundamentally changed how businesses reach customers, manage brand reputation, and receive customer feedback. Jim Riley's resources extensively cover social media's business implications, particularly for marketing and customer service functions.

- **Data analytics**: Big data capabilities allow businesses to understand customer behaviour, optimise operations, and personalise offerings. However, this creates privacy concerns and regulatory challenges (see GDPR below).

**Teaching implications**: Technology moves rapidly, so teachers must regularly update examples and case studies. Practical activities might include analysing businesses' digital strategies, comparing online and traditional business models, or evaluating technology investment decisions. The annual Bett Show (education technology exhibition) often includes business education sessions on teaching technological influences.

### Legal Influences

Legal factors encompass all legislation affecting business operations. The UK legal framework is extensive, covering employment, consumer protection, competition, health and safety, and data protection.

**Key legal influences include:**

- **Employment law**: Legislation including the Employment Rights Act 1996, Equality Act 2010, National Minimum Wage Act 1998, and Working Time Regulations 1998 establishes minimum standards for employer-employee relationships. From April 2024, the National Living Wage for workers aged 21+ is £11.44 per hour. Changes to employment law directly affect labour costs and HR practices.

- **Consumer protection law**: The Consumer Rights Act 2015 consolidates previous consumer legislation, establishing that goods must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, and as described. The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) enforces consumer protection and competition law.

- **Health and safety legislation**: The Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and subsequent regulations (COSHH, Manual Handling, Display Screen Equipment, etc.) require employers to ensure workplace safety. The Health and Safety Executive (HSE) enforces these requirements through inspections and, where necessary, prosecutions.

- **Data protection**: The UK GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) and Data Protection Act 2018 govern how businesses collect, process, and store personal data. Maximum fines reach £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover, whichever is higher. The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) regulates data protection compliance.

- **Competition law**: The Competition Act 1998 and Enterprise Act 2002 prohibit anti-competitive practices including cartels, abuse of dominant position, and anti-competitive mergers. The CMA investigates potential breaches and can impose substantial fines.

- **Environmental legislation**: Including the Climate Change Act 2008, Environmental Protection Act 1990, and various regulations on emissions, waste management, and pollution control. These laws increasingly affect business operations and strategic planning.

**Teaching implications**: Legal influences provide opportunities for cross-curricular work with Law departments. Ian Marcousé recommends using real legal cases—employment tribunals, consumer disputes, or CMA investigations—as teaching resources. All major exam boards require students to understand key legislation, though depth varies between GCSE and A-level. Teachers should access government websites (legislation.gov.uk, gov.uk department pages) for authoritative information rather than relying solely on textbooks, which may not reflect recent legislative changes.

### Environmental Influences

Environmental factors have gained prominence in business education, reflecting growing societal concern about climate change, resource depletion, and environmental degradation.

**Key environmental influences include:**

- **Climate change and sustainability expectations**: Businesses face increasing pressure from consumers, investors, and regulators to reduce carbon emissions and adopt sustainable practices. The UK's commitment to reach net zero emissions by 2050 (Climate Change Act 2008, as amended) affects business strategy across all sectors.

- **Resource scarcity**: Finite natural resources, including fossil fuels, minerals, and water, affect input costs and operational feasibility. Businesses must consider resource efficiency and circular economy principles.

- **Extreme weather events**: Climate change increases the frequency and severity of floods, droughts, heatwaves, and storms, affecting supply chains, physical infrastructure, and operational continuity.

- **Environmental regulation**: Beyond legal requirements (covered above), voluntary frameworks such as ISO 14001 (environmental management systems) and carbon reporting requirements for large businesses shape environmental practices.

**Teaching implications**: Environmental influences connect strongly with students' personal concerns about climate change, making this topic engaging and relevant. Peter Stimpson emphasises the importance of evaluating business environmental claims critically—teachers should encourage students to distinguish between genuine sustainability initiatives and "greenwashing." Case studies might include businesses' carbon reduction strategies, renewable energy adoption, or sustainable product development.

## PESTLE Analysis: Application and Evaluation

PESTLE analysis (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) provides a systematic framework for analysing external influences. All A-level specifications reference this or similar frameworks (PEST, STEEPLE), though it may not appear explicitly at GCSE.

**Strengths of PESTLE analysis:**

- Provides comprehensive, structured approach to environmental scanning
- Encourages consideration of diverse external factors
- Useful for strategic planning and decision-making
- Facilitates comparison across different business contexts

**Limitations of PESTLE analysis:**

- Factors often interconnect and overlap (e.g., legal and political)
- Can become superficial without deep analysis of specific factors
- Snapshot in time—external environment constantly evolves
- Requires judgement about which factors are most significant
- Excessive time investment may not always yield actionable insights

Ian Marcousé cautions against mechanistic application of PESTLE, emphasising that effective analysis requires evaluation of which factors most significantly affect a particular business. Jim Riley's teaching resources suggest using PESTLE as a starting point for analysis before focusing detailed investigation on the most relevant factors.

## Globalisation and International Influences

Globalisation—the increasing interconnection of world economies—represents a meta-trend affecting all external influences. Mark Surridge identifies globalisation as a transformative force requiring dedicated attention in Business Studies education.

**Key aspects include:**

- **International trade expansion**: Reduced trade barriers, improved transportation, and digital communication enable businesses to source inputs globally and access international markets. UK businesses must navigate tariffs, trade agreements, and international regulations.

- **Multinational corporations (MNCs)**: Large businesses operating across multiple countries influence competition, employment, and supply chains globally. MNCs bring investment, employment, and knowledge transfer but may also dominate markets and relocate operations when conditions become less favourable.

- **Global supply chains**: Many products incorporate components from multiple countries. This increases efficiency through specialisation but creates vulnerability to international disruptions (as COVID-19 demonstrated).

- **Emerging markets**: Rapid economic growth in countries including China, India, Brazil, and Nigeria creates both opportunities (expanding markets) and challenges (increasing competition). Peter Stimpson's international perspective emphasises how businesses must understand diverse cultural, legal, and economic contexts when operating globally.

**Teaching implications**: Globalisation connects multiple specification areas including operations management (supply chains), marketing (international markets), and strategy (international expansion). Teachers should use multinational businesses as case studies and consider how global events (trade disputes, international crises, currency fluctuations) affect UK businesses. The British Chambers of Commerce provides accessible data on international trade affecting UK businesses.

## Competitive Environment

Whilst often treated separately, the competitive environment represents an external influence significantly affecting business decisions. Michael Porter's Five Forces framework (referenced by all major textbooks and A-level specifications) analyses competitive intensity:

1. **Threat of new entrants**: Barriers to entry including capital requirements, economies of scale, and regulatory approval affect how easily competitors can enter markets.

2. **Bargaining power of suppliers**: Supplier concentration, switching costs, and input uniqueness affect suppliers' ability to raise prices or reduce quality.

3. **Bargaining power of buyers**: Customer concentration, product differentiation, and switching costs affect buyers' ability to negotiate favourable terms.

4. **Threat of substitutes**: Alternative products or services satisfying similar needs limit pricing power and market potential.

5. **Competitive rivalry**: Number of competitors, market growth rate, product differentiation, and exit barriers determine intensity of competition.

Ian Marcousé emphasises that competitive analysis must be specific to particular industries—factors significant in one sector may be irrelevant in another.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Keeping Content Current

External influences change continuously, making currency a particular challenge. Teachers should:

- Subscribe to business news sources (BBC Business, Financial Times, The Economist)
- Use Jim Riley's Tutor2u platform, which provides regularly updated business news analysis
- Encourage students to follow business news and bring current examples to lessons
- Maintain a bank of recent case studies across different external factors
- Attend exam board training to understand assessment expectations
- Collaborate with Business Studies colleagues to share resources and updates

### Assessment and Examination Approach

All UK exam boards assess external influences through both knowledge demonstration and application/analysis.

**AQA GCSE Business (8132) and A-level (7131/7132)**: External influences appear throughout both specifications. AQA emphasises context-based assessment requiring students to apply understanding to unfamiliar business scenarios. External influences typically appear in longer-answer questions requiring analysis and evaluation.

**Edexcel (Pearson) GCSE Business (1BS0) and A-level (9BS0)**: Edexcel structures external influences thematically. The GCSE specification includes "Influences on Business" as Theme 2. A-level addresses external influences throughout all four themes. Edexcel assessments often require students to calculate effects of economic changes (inflation, exchange rates) before analysing broader implications.
