# Human Resource Management

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Human Resource Management (HRM) represents a fundamental component of UK Business Studies curricula across Key Stages 4 and 5. The subject examines how organisations manage their workforce to achieve strategic objectives whilst maintaining legal compliance and ethical standards. According to Ian Marcousé's "AQA Business" textbook series, HRM has evolved from personnel management to a strategic function integral to organisational success.

The study of HRM appears explicitly in:
- **GCSE Business (9-1)**: All major exam boards (AQA 8132, Edexcel 1BS0, OCR J204, WJEC Eduqas)
- **A-Level Business**: AQA 7132, Edexcel 9BS0, OCR H431/H432
- **BTEC Business**: Various units across Levels 2 and 3

Peter Stimpson and Mark Surridge's Cambridge International AS and A Level Business textbook emphasises that HRM encompasses "the strategic and coherent approach to the management of an organisation's most valued assets—the people working there who individually and collectively contribute to the achievement of its objectives."

## Key Theoretical Frameworks

### The Harvard Model vs Michigan Model

Two dominant frameworks underpin HRM teaching in UK Business Studies:

**The Michigan Model (Hard HRM)**: Emphasises strategic fit between HR policies and business strategy. People are viewed as resources to be managed rationally, similar to other organisational resources. This model aligns with Jim Riley's emphasis on quantitative workforce planning and efficiency metrics.

**The Harvard Model (Soft HRM)**: Focuses on employee development, commitment, and treating workers as valued assets. This approach emphasises stakeholder interests and long-term capability development.

Nancy Wall's work for Tutor2u highlights that most contemporary UK organisations adopt a blended approach, though the balance varies by sector, organisational culture, and competitive context.

### Strategic vs Operational HRM

Ian Marcousé distinguishes between:
- **Strategic HRM**: Long-term workforce planning aligned with corporate strategy, talent management, succession planning
- **Operational HRM**: Day-to-day management including recruitment, administration, grievance handling

Teachers should emphasise this distinction when preparing students for A-Level examinations, where questions frequently require analysis of strategic HRM decisions in context.

## Core Components of HRM

### Workforce Planning

Workforce planning involves forecasting future staffing needs and ensuring appropriate supply of labour. Key elements include:

**Labour turnover calculation**: (Number of staff leaving / Average number of staff) × 100

**Retention rates**: (Number of employees with 1+ years' service / Overall workforce) × 100

Jim Riley's teaching resources emphasise that students must understand both the calculation and interpretation of these metrics. High labour turnover may indicate poor management, inadequate pay, or limited career development, though contextual factors (e.g., seasonal businesses, retail sector norms) must be considered.

**Practical implications for teachers**: Students struggle to analyse workforce planning data contextually. Use real business case studies—Marcousé's textbooks feature companies like John Lewis Partnership and Timpson—to demonstrate sector-specific norms and strategic responses.

### Recruitment and Selection

This topic appears across all UK Business Studies specifications. The process comprises:

1. **Job analysis**: Determining role requirements
2. **Job description**: Outlining duties, responsibilities, reporting lines
3. **Person specification**: Identifying essential and desirable candidate characteristics
4. **Advertising**: Internal vs external recruitment
5. **Selection methods**: Applications, interviews, assessment centres, testing

**Internal recruitment** offers advantages including lower costs, faster appointment, and motivation of existing staff. However, it limits fresh perspectives and may create succession gaps.

**External recruitment** provides wider talent pools and new ideas but involves higher costs and longer induction periods.

Peter Stimpson emphasises that recruitment choices reflect organisational culture and strategic priorities. For example, companies pursuing innovation strategies often favour external recruitment for senior positions, whilst those prioritising stability may prefer internal development.

### Training and Development

The distinction between training and development is crucial:

**Training**: Improving skills for current roles (job-specific, often short-term)
**Development**: Preparing employees for future responsibilities (broader, career-focused)

Training methods include:

**On-the-job training**:
- Induction programmes
- Mentoring and coaching
- Job rotation
- Shadowing

**Off-the-job training**:
- External courses and qualifications
- In-house training centres
- E-learning platforms
- Conferences and seminars

Ian Marcousé notes that training represents an investment with measurable returns including improved productivity, reduced waste, enhanced customer service, and higher retention. However, costs include direct expenses, opportunity costs of time, and potential staff poaching by competitors.

**For exam boards**: AQA A-Level specifically requires evaluation of training methods in different contexts. OCR GCSE includes training as part of "Making the business effective" content.

### Motivation Theory and Practice

Motivation theory is extensively covered across UK curricula and represents approximately 15-20% of HRM examination content.

**Key theorists students must understand**:

**Frederick Taylor (Scientific Management)**: Workers motivated primarily by money; work should be scientifically designed with payment-by-results systems. Marcousé critiques Taylor's assumptions as overly simplistic but notes their continued relevance in some manufacturing and gig economy contexts.

**Abraham Maslow (Hierarchy of Needs)**: Five-tier pyramid from physiological needs through safety, social belonging, esteem, to self-actualisation. Nancy Wall emphasises teaching this progressively—workers must satisfy lower-level needs before higher ones become motivating.

**Frederick Herzberg (Two-Factor Theory)**: Distinguishes hygiene factors (preventing dissatisfaction: pay, conditions, security) from motivators (creating satisfaction: achievement, recognition, responsibility). Jim Riley notes this theory directly influences job enrichment and empowerment strategies examined in A-Level specifications.

**Elton Mayo (Human Relations School)**: Highlighted social factors and group dynamics following the Hawthorne Studies. Relevant when analysing teamworking and communication strategies.

**Douglas McGregor (Theory X and Theory Y)**: Theory X assumes workers are lazy and require close supervision; Theory Y assumes workers are self-motivated and seek responsibility. Links directly to management styles and organisational culture.

**Practical teaching approach**: Use contemporary case studies to apply theories. For example, Google's working environment illustrates Maslow's higher-level needs and Herzberg's motivators, whilst Amazon warehouse controversies raise Theory X concerns.

### Financial and Non-Financial Motivation

**Financial methods**:
- **Piecework/Commission**: Payment by output, common in manufacturing and sales
- **Performance-related pay (PRP)**: Bonuses linked to objectives/appraisals
- **Profit sharing**: Distributing company profits to employees
- **Fringe benefits**: Company cars, health insurance, pension contributions

**Non-financial methods**:
- **Job enrichment**: Increasing responsibility and variety (vertical loading)
- **Job rotation**: Moving between tasks (horizontal loading)
- **Empowerment**: Delegating decision-making authority
- **Teamworking**: Collaborative structures and team-based targets
- **Flexible working**: Remote work, flexitime, compressed hours

Mark Surridge argues that effective motivation strategies combine financial and non-financial elements, tailored to workforce characteristics and organisational context. A-Level students must evaluate the appropriateness of different approaches for specific scenarios.

### Performance Management and Appraisal

Performance appraisal systems assess employee contribution and inform development plans. Common approaches include:

- **360-degree feedback**: Input from managers, peers, subordinates, and self-assessment
- **Management by Objectives (MBO)**: Setting and reviewing specific targets
- **Continuous appraisal**: Ongoing feedback rather than annual reviews
- **Self-appraisal**: Employee-led assessment of achievement

Jim Riley emphasises that effective appraisal systems require clear criteria, trained appraisers, and integration with development planning. Poor implementation creates demotivation and legal risks.

**Examination focus**: Edexcel A-Level Theme 3 specifically examines performance management links to strategic objectives. Students must analyse how appraisal supports or hinders organisational goals.

## UK Employment Law and Compliance

Understanding employment law is mandatory across all UK Business Studies specifications, reflecting its practical importance and ethical dimensions.

### Key Legislation

**Equality Act 2010**: Consolidates discrimination law, protecting nine protected characteristics (age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy/maternity, race, religion/belief, sex, sexual orientation). Covers recruitment, promotion, pay, and dismissal.

**Employment Rights Act 1996**: Establishes rights including written contracts, itemised pay statements, minimum notice periods, protection against unfair dismissal (after two years' continuous service), and redundancy payments.

**National Minimum Wage Act 1998** (amended regularly): Sets minimum hourly rates by age category. As of 2024, the National Living Wage (21+) significantly exceeds the standard minimum wage. Ian Marcousé notes this creates cost pressures for labour-intensive businesses whilst reducing poverty.

**Working Time Regulations 1998**: Maximum 48-hour average working week (opt-out possible), rest breaks, annual leave entitlement (5.6 weeks).

**Health and Safety at Work Act 1974**: Employer duty of care to ensure safe working environments; employee responsibility to follow safety procedures.

**Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992**: Union recognition, collective bargaining rights, industrial action regulations.

**Practical teaching implications**: Students often memorise legislation without understanding application. Use case study scenarios requiring legal analysis—for example, examining whether dismissal for persistent lateness constitutes unfair dismissal, considering procedural fairness and ACAS Code of Practice requirements.

### Dismissal, Redundancy, and Disciplinary Procedures

**Fair dismissal** requires valid reasons (capability, conduct, redundancy, statutory restriction, or "some other substantial reason") and fair procedures following ACAS guidelines.

**Unfair dismissal** occurs when reasons are invalid or procedures inadequate. Tribunals award compensation based on actual loss, with statutory caps reviewed annually.

**Redundancy** arises when jobs genuinely disappear due to workplace closure, reduced staffing needs, or business sale/relocation. Statutory redundancy pay depends on age, service length, and weekly pay (capped).

**ACAS Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures** provides statutory guidance. Peter Stimpson emphasises that failure to follow ACAS procedures increases tribunal compensation by up to 25%.

### Employer-Employee Relations

The relationship between employers and employees exists on a spectrum:

**Collective bargaining**: Trade unions negotiate terms on behalf of members. More common in public sector and traditional industries.

**Individual bargaining**: Direct negotiation between employer and employee. Dominant in private sector, particularly professional and knowledge-based roles.

Nancy Wall identifies declining union membership in the UK (from 13.2 million in 1979 to approximately 6.4 million in 2023) as shifting power dynamics, though unions remain influential in education, health, and transport sectors.

**Works councils and employee forums**: Non-union consultation mechanisms, increasingly common in larger organisations complying with EU-derived information and consultation requirements.

**Examination focus**: A-Level questions frequently require evaluation of union effectiveness or analysis of changing employment relations. AQA and OCR specifications explicitly include industrial disputes and resolution methods.

## Contemporary HRM Issues

### The Gig Economy and Flexible Working

The rise of zero-hours contracts, self-employment, and platform-based work (Uber, Deliveroo) raises significant HRM challenges. Jim Riley notes that whilst employers gain flexibility and reduced employment costs, workers experience insecurity and limited rights.

The 2017 Taylor Review of Modern Working Practices and subsequent Employment Rights Bill proposals aim to strengthen protections whilst maintaining flexibility. This remains a dynamic policy area relevant to current specifications.

**For teachers**: The Supreme Court ruling in Uber BV v Aslam (2021), establishing drivers as "workers" entitled to minimum wage and holiday pay, provides excellent case study material for legal and ethical analysis.

### Diversity and Inclusion

Beyond legal compliance, progressive HRM emphasises diversity's business benefits: broader talent pools, enhanced innovation, improved decision-making, and stronger market understanding.

Mark Surridge identifies diversity challenges including unconscious bias in recruitment, glass ceiling effects limiting senior progression, and pay gap persistence despite equal pay legislation (dating to 1970).

The 2017 introduction of mandatory gender pay gap reporting for organisations with 250+ employees represents a significant development. Students should understand both the reporting requirements and strategic responses.

### Remote and Hybrid Working

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated remote working adoption, with lasting implications for HRM. Ian Marcousé's recent editions emphasise challenges including:

- Maintaining organisational culture and communication
- Managing performance without physical supervision
- Ensuring work-life balance and preventing isolation
- Adapting training and development programmes
- Addressing technology and home working cost issues

**For A-Level teaching**: Remote working provides excellent context for analysing motivation theory application, particularly Mayo's social factors and Herzberg's hygiene factors.

### Mental Health and Wellbeing

Increasing recognition of workplace mental health as an HRM priority reflects legal duties (Health and Safety at Work Act), productivity concerns (absence costs), and ethical responsibilities.

Progressive practices include:
- Mental Health First Aiders
- Employee Assistance Programmes (EAPs)
- Flexible working to support work-life balance
- Management training in mental health awareness
- Open communication and reduced stigma

**Examination relevance**: WJEC Eduqas A-Level specifically mentions employee wellbeing. Questions may require evaluation of wellbeing initiatives' impact on motivation and productivity.

### Skills Shortages and Brexit Implications

Post-Brexit immigration rule changes significantly affect UK labour markets and HRM strategy. Sectors including hospitality, agriculture, healthcare, and construction face acute shortages.

HRM responses include:
- Increased training investment to develop existing staff
- Enhanced retention strategies to reduce turnover
- Automation and technology adoption to reduce labour dependency
- Improved terms and conditions to attract applicants
- Lobbying for sector-specific visa arrangements

Teachers should incorporate current labour market data from sources including the Office for National Statistics and Chartered Institute of Personnel and Development (CIPD) to ensure contemporary relevance.

## Assessment and Examination Approaches

### Command Word Analysis

Understanding command words is crucial for examination success:

**Identify/State/Give**: Simple recall of knowledge (1-2 marks)
**Explain**: Demonstrating understanding through development and linkage (2-4 marks)
**Analyse**: Breaking down issues, showing cause and effect, using chains of reasoning (6-9 marks)
**Evaluate/Assess/To what extent**: Weighing up different factors, making supported judgements (10-20 marks)

Jim Riley emphasises that evaluation questions in HRM topics require consideration of:
- Organisational context (size, sector, culture, strategy)
- Stakeholder perspectives (employees, management, shareholders, customers)
- Short-term vs long-term implications
- Quantitative vs qualitative factors

### Common Student Misconceptions

**Teachers should address these misconceptions explicitly**:

1. **"Taylor's theory is completely outdated"**: Whilst limited, payment-by-results and scientific work design remain relevant in specific contexts. Students must analyse appropriateness rather than dismiss theories.

2. **"All employees want empowerment and responsibility"**: Individual differences matter. Some workers prefer structured roles with clear expectations (Theory X preferences are valid, not inherently wrong).

3. **"Higher pay always increases motivation"**: Herzberg demonstrates pay prevents dissatisfaction but doesn't create lasting motivation. Students must distinguish hygiene factors from motivators.

4. **"Unions always conflict with management"**: Partnership approaches and shared objectives are increasingly common. The relationship varies by context.

5. **"Employment law always favours employees"**: Legislation balances interests. Employers have protections, and procedures must be followed by both parties.

### Mark Scheme Expectations

Examination success in HRM topics requires:

**Knowledge**: Accurate terminology, appropriate theory citation, relevant legal framework
**Application**: Using case study/context information to tailor responses
**Analysis**: Developing points with chains of reasoning, showing consequences
**Evaluation**: Supported judgements considering multiple perspectives and contexts

Ian Marcousé's examination guides emphasise that Level 4 evaluation responses (highest marks) demonstrate:
- Contextualised judgements
- Weighing of alternative viewpoints
- Consideration of short and long-term implications
- Clear final judgement supported by preceding analysis

## Pedagogical Recommendations

### Effective Teaching Strategies

1. **Case study integration**: Use real business examples throughout. Marcousé's textbooks feature recurring cases (e.g., Jaguar Land Rover, Innocent Drinks) allowing cumulative understanding.

2. **Theory application practice**: Avoid teaching theories in isolation. Immediately apply Herzberg, Maslow, etc., to specific scenarios requiring students to select appropriate frameworks.

3. **Legal scenarios**: Create discipline, dismissal, and discrimination scenarios requiring students to apply legislation and ACAS guidance to reach supported conclusions.

4. **Current affairs integration**: Employment relations, pay disputes, and working practice changes feature regularly in news. Maintain a current examples bank for discussion and analysis.

5. **Data interpretation**: Use real workforce planning data, labour turnover statistics, and absence rates. Students must calculate, interpret, and recommend actions.
