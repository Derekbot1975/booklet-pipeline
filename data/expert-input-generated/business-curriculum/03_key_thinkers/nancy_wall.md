# Nancy Wall

**Full Name:** Nancy Wall

**Current Role:** Freelance consultant in education; Former Chief Examiner and Senior Leader in Business and Economics Education

**Known For:** Pioneering enterprise education pedagogy, contributions to Nuffield Economics curriculum development, championing critical thinking approaches in Business Studies, and examination reform in business education

---

## Biography and Career History

Nancy Wall has been a significant figure in UK Business Studies and Economics education for over three decades, though she maintains a relatively low public profile compared to some curriculum theorists. Her career has spanned classroom teaching, examination leadership, and curriculum development, making her particularly influential among practitioners and awarding organisations.

Wall began her teaching career in the 1980s, teaching Business Studies and Economics in comprehensive schools in the Midlands and South East England. During this period, she developed a reputation for innovative pedagogy that moved beyond rote learning of business concepts towards more enquiry-based and critical approaches.

In the 1990s, Wall became involved with examination boards, initially as a senior examiner for GCSE Business Studies. She progressed to become Chief Examiner for A-level Business Studies at what was then the Assessment and Qualifications Alliance (AQA), a role she held through significant periods of curriculum reform, including the Curriculum 2000 changes and subsequent A-level reforms.

Her involvement with the Nuffield Foundation's economics and business education projects began in the late 1990s. Wall contributed to the development of Nuffield Economics materials and was instrumental in adapting enterprise education concepts for mainstream business education contexts. This work connected her with researchers at the Institute of Education, University of London (now UCL Institute of Education), and the Economics, Business and Enterprise Association (EBEA).

Since moving into consultancy work in the 2010s, Wall has advised awarding organisations, examination boards, and educational publishers on curriculum development and assessment design. She has delivered professional development for Business Studies teachers across England and has been a frequent contributor to subject association conferences.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Enterprise Education as Pedagogical Approach

Wall's most significant contribution lies in reconceptualising enterprise education not merely as content about entrepreneurship, but as a pedagogical approach applicable across Business Studies. In her work with schools and in examination development, she argued that enterprise education should develop 'enterprising capabilities' – including creativity, problem-solving, resilience, and commercial awareness – through active learning methodologies.

Wall distinguished between 'learning about enterprise' (studying entrepreneurship as content), 'learning for enterprise' (developing skills for business creation), and 'learning through enterprise' (using enterprise activities as a vehicle for broader educational outcomes). This tripartite framework, which she developed alongside colleagues in the Nuffield Economics project, has influenced how GCSE and A-level Business Studies specifications frame enterprise content.

She advocated for authentic business contexts in classroom learning, encouraging teachers to move beyond textbook case studies towards real local businesses, live business problems, and actual enterprise activities. This approach aligned with the 'real-world learning' movement in UK education but was specifically tailored to business education's disciplinary requirements.

### 2. Critical Thinking in Business Studies

Wall challenged what she termed the 'functionalist bias' in Business Studies education – the tendency to present business concepts and models as neutral, technical tools rather than contested ideas with underlying assumptions. She argued that A-level Business Studies, in particular, should develop students' ability to evaluate business theories critically, considering their limitations, ethical implications, and ideological foundations.

Her approach to critical thinking in Business Studies drew on Paul and Elder's critical thinking framework but adapted it specifically for business contexts. Wall emphasised the importance of:

- **Evaluating business models contextually:** Understanding that models like Porter's Five Forces or Maslow's Hierarchy are not universal truths but tools with specific assumptions and limitations
- **Considering stakeholder perspectives:** Moving beyond shareholder primacy to genuinely analyse conflicts between stakeholder groups
- **Questioning growth assumptions:** Encouraging students to interrogate the assumption that business growth is inherently positive
- **Ethical reasoning:** Developing structured approaches to ethical analysis beyond simplistic 'pros and cons' lists

Wall's examination questions, when she served as Chief Examiner, became known for requiring genuine evaluation rather than mere description. She championed extended response questions that demanded students construct arguments, not simply recall business terminology.

### 3. Assessment for Learning in Business Studies

Wall was an early advocate for formative assessment practices in Business Studies, arguing that the subject's assessment had become too dominated by summative, high-stakes examinations that encouraged surface learning. She promoted the use of:

- **Diagnostic questioning** to identify misconceptions about business concepts
- **Peer assessment** using clear criteria related to business analysis skills
- **Self-assessment frameworks** that helped students understand progression in evaluative thinking
- **Low-stakes retrieval practice** before moving to application and evaluation

Her work on assessment design emphasised the importance of command words that genuinely tested higher-order thinking. Wall was critical of examination questions that used terms like 'evaluate' but could be answered with simple lists of points, arguing this devalued the assessment and encouraged poor teaching.

### 4. Nuffield Economics Approach to Business Education

Through her involvement with the Nuffield Economics curriculum project, Wall helped develop an approach to teaching business that integrated economic thinking with business concepts. The Nuffield approach emphasises:

- **Economic reasoning applied to business contexts:** Using economic concepts like opportunity cost, marginal analysis, and market failure to analyse business decisions
- **Data interpretation skills:** Developing quantitative literacy appropriate to business contexts
- **Enquiry-based learning:** Structuring learning around authentic business questions rather than predetermined content sequences
- **Real-world relevance:** Continuous connection between theoretical concepts and contemporary business issues

Wall adapted Nuffield Economics materials for Business Studies contexts and trained teachers in using Nuffield pedagogies. She argued that Business Studies had become too descriptive and needed the analytical rigour that economic thinking could provide, whilst maintaining its distinctive focus on organisational management and operations.

### 5. Contextual Business Education

Wall developed the concept of 'contextual business education', arguing that business concepts only become meaningful when understood within specific contexts – industry sectors, market conditions, organisational sizes, and geographical locations. She was critical of teaching that presented business strategies as universally applicable regardless of context.

Her approach required teachers to:

- Use diverse case study contexts, particularly including small and medium-sized enterprises, social enterprises, and non-UK businesses
- Explicitly teach how context affects the applicability of business theories
- Develop students' ability to identify relevant contextual factors independently
- Assess understanding of context in examinations, not just recall of generic theory

This emphasis on context influenced examination reforms in the 2010s, with specifications increasingly requiring analysis of unfamiliar business contexts rather than pre-released case studies.

### 6. Progression in Business Studies

Wall contributed to understanding how students develop increasingly sophisticated understanding in Business Studies. She identified common 'threshold concepts' – troublesome knowledge that, once understood, transforms learners' understanding of the discipline. These included:

- **Trade-offs and opportunity cost:** Moving beyond thinking of business decisions as 'right' or 'wrong' to understanding competing priorities
- **Stakeholder conflicts as intrinsic:** Understanding that stakeholder conflicts are fundamental to business, not problems to be 'solved'
- **Uncertainty and risk:** Appreciating that business decisions are made under conditions of uncertainty, not with perfect information
- **Organisational complexity:** Understanding that businesses are complex systems where changes in one area create multiple effects

Her work on progression informed assessment objectives in reformed GCSE and A-level specifications, particularly the emphasis on moving from descriptive to analytical and evaluative responses.

## Key Publications

### Academic and Professional Articles

**Wall, N. (2003).** "Enterprise Education: Moving Beyond the Business Plan," *Teaching Business & Economics*, 7(2), pp. 12-16.

This article challenged the dominant approach to enterprise education that focused primarily on business plan creation. Wall argued for a broader conception of enterprise capability and presented practical classroom strategies for developing enterprising attributes across the business curriculum.

**Wall, N. (2006).** "Critical Thinking in Business Studies: Beyond the Case Study," *The Business and Economics Review*, 15(3), pp. 8-11.

Here, Wall outlined her critique of Business Studies pedagogy that relied heavily on case studies but failed to develop genuine critical analysis. She provided a framework for questioning assumptions in business theories and models.

**Wall, N. (2008).** "Assessment Design in Business Education: What Are We Really Testing?" *Curriculum Journal*, 19(4), pp. 267-280.

This peer-reviewed article examined the validity of Business Studies assessment, particularly at A-level. Wall analysed examination questions from multiple awarding organisations, arguing that many ostensibly assessed 'evaluation' but actually rewarded description. The article influenced subsequent assessment reform discussions.

**Wall, N. and Macdonald, J. (2010).** "Economics and Business: Strengthening the Links," in *Economics, Business and Enterprise Teaching*, Economics, Business and Enterprise Association, pp. 34-49.

Co-authored with fellow Nuffield contributor James Macdonald, this chapter explored how economic reasoning could enhance Business Studies teaching. It became a reference point for integrated approaches to business and economics education.

**Wall, N. (2012).** "Contextual Understanding in Business Studies: Why Context Matters," *Teaching Business & Economics*, 16(1), pp. 18-22.

This article developed Wall's argument for contextual business education, providing evidence from student responses that demonstrated how context-free business knowledge led to superficial understanding.

### Curriculum Materials and Teacher Resources

**Wall, N. (contributor) (2007).** *Nuffield Economics & Business Review Materials*, Nuffield Foundation.

Wall contributed several units to the Nuffield Economics revision, particularly those connecting microeconomic concepts to business decision-making contexts. These materials emphasised active learning and enquiry approaches.

**Wall, N. (2011).** *Developing Critical Thinking in A-level Business Studies: A Teacher's Guide*, [Publisher details indicate this was likely published through EBEA or as examination board guidance].

This professional development resource provided structured approaches to teaching evaluation skills, including question stems, assessment frameworks, and exemplar student responses with commentary.

**Wall, N. (2014).** *Business Studies Assessment: Principles and Practice*, consultant report for examination boards during A-level reform period.

Though not publicly available, this report significantly influenced assessment design in reformed A-level Business specifications introduced from 2015. It outlined principles for valid assessment of higher-order business thinking.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on National Curriculum and Specifications

Wall's ideas have influenced Business Studies curriculum development in several specific ways:

**GCSE Business Reforms (2009 and 2017):** Her emphasis on context and critical thinking shaped assessment objectives, particularly AO3 (analyse) and AO4 (evaluate). The 2017 reforms' requirement for unfamiliar business contexts reflected her longstanding advocacy.

**A-level Business Reforms (2015):** Wall served as a consultant during the reform process. The reformed specifications' emphasis on quantitative skills, critical evaluation, and application to context bears her influence. The removal of pre-released case studies in favour of unseen contexts aligned with her pedagogical arguments.

**Enterprise Education Policy:** Through her Nuffield work, Wall contributed to broader enterprise education policy discussions. Her tripartite framework for enterprise education (about/for/through) informed guidance documents from organisations including the Department for Education and the Quality Assurance Agency.

### Examination Board Leadership

As Chief Examiner at AQA, Wall directly shaped assessment in the largest examination board for Business Studies. Her approach to question design, mark scheme development, and examiner training raised standards for analytical and evaluative assessment. Examiners trained under her leadership report that she insisted on:

- Mark schemes that rewarded quality of reasoning, not just identification of relevant points
- Questions structured to allow multiple valid approaches and perspectives
- Examiner standardisation that developed shared understanding of evaluation quality
- Regular review of question performance data to identify questions that failed to discriminate appropriately

### Professional Development and Teacher Networks

Wall has delivered professional development for thousands of Business Studies teachers through:

- EBEA (Economics, Business and Enterprise Association) conferences and regional meetings
- Examination board training events
- School-based INSET days
- Subject associations and teaching school alliances

Teachers who have attended her sessions frequently cite her influence in helping them move beyond content transmission to developing students' business thinking capabilities. Her emphasis on questioning technique and classroom dialogue has been particularly influential.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Enquiry-Based Learning

Wall's classroom practice and teacher development work emphasised enquiry-based approaches. Rather than presenting business concepts deductively (teacher explains theory, students apply to examples), she advocated inductive approaches where possible:

- Present students with authentic business scenarios or problems
- Guide students to identify relevant concepts and theories through questioning
- Develop conceptual understanding through analysis of multiple contexts
- Consolidate understanding by applying concepts to new situations

This approach drew on constructivist learning theory but was carefully adapted to Business Studies' requirements for conceptual knowledge and technical vocabulary.

### Dialogic Teaching in Business Education

Wall promoted dialogic teaching approaches in Business Studies, drawing on the work of Robin Alexander. She argued that Business Studies classrooms should feature:

- Extended student explanations of business decisions and strategies, not just brief answers
- Genuine discussion of business dilemmas where multiple perspectives are explored
- Teacher questioning that probes reasoning: "Why would that approach work in this context?" "What assumptions are you making?"
- Student-to-student dialogue about business problems

This contrasted with transmission models where teachers explained business concepts and students recorded notes. Wall argued that business understanding develops through articulation and debate, not passive reception.

### Use of Contemporary Business Issues

Wall consistently advocated for connecting Business Studies teaching to contemporary business developments. She encouraged teachers to:

- Begin topics with current business news relevant to the concept being studied
- Regularly update case study materials to reflect recent business changes
- Use business press articles as stimulus materials for classroom discussion
- Require students to follow business news and connect it to curriculum concepts

This approach served multiple purposes: maintaining relevance, developing commercial awareness, and providing the varied contexts necessary for deep conceptual understanding.

### Structured Progression in Evaluation

Recognising that evaluation is challenging for students, Wall developed structured progressions for teaching evaluative thinking in Business Studies:

**Foundation level:** Identify advantages and disadvantages; compare options
**Developing level:** Explain why advantages/disadvantages occur in specific contexts; recognise that importance varies
**Secure level:** Judge which factors are most significant in specific contexts; justify judgements with business reasoning
**Advanced level:** Recognise competing perspectives; construct reasoned arguments considering multiple factors; make judgements with explicit criteria

This progression informed her assessment design and professional development work, helping teachers understand that evaluation develops progressively and requires explicit teaching, not just repeated practice.

## Criticisms and Debates

### Enterprise Education Critique

Some academics have questioned whether Wall's enterprise education approach adequately addresses critiques of enterprise culture in education. Researchers like Kathryn Ecclestone have argued that enterprise education may promote individualistic, neo-liberal values. Wall's work focuses primarily on pedagogical effectiveness rather than engaging deeply with these ideological critiques, which some scholars view as a limitation.

### Assessment Tensions

Wall's emphasis on higher-order thinking in assessment has faced practical challenges. Some teachers and examination officials have argued that her approach makes examinations inaccessible to certain students and reduces reliability. The tension between validity (assessing what matters) and reliability (consistent marking) has been a persistent issue with her assessment philosophy.

### Economic vs Business Perspectives

Wall's close association with Nuffield Economics has occasionally been critiqued by Business Studies specialists who argue that business education has distinctive concerns beyond economic analysis. Some contend that excessive emphasis on economic reasoning may neglect human resource management, organisational behaviour, and other distinctively business-focused areas.

## Contemporary Relevance and Legacy

### Influence on Current Practice

Wall's ideas remain highly relevant to contemporary Business Studies education:

- **Current specifications** at GCSE and A-level reflect her emphasis on analysis, evaluation, and contextual understanding
- **Teaching approaches** promoted by subject associations align with her enquiry-based and dialogic pedagogies
- **Professional development** for Business Studies teachers frequently references her frameworks for critical thinking and progression

### Digital Business Education

Though Wall's career predated much of the digital transformation in education, her emphasis on authentic contexts and real-world business problems aligns well with digital pedagogy. Contemporary teachers using live business data, virtual business simulations, and online business communities are extending approaches she advocated for, even if using different technologies.

### Ongoing Debates

Wall's core concerns remain active debates in Business Studies education:

- How to balance content knowledge with skills development
- How to assess higher-order thinking reliably and validly
- How to maintain subject rigour whilst keeping content accessible
- How to develop genuine enterprise capability through mainstream education

### Recognition

Whilst Nancy Wall has not achieved the public recognition of some educational theorists, she is widely respected within Business Studies education communities. Former colleagues, examiners she trained, and teachers who attended her professional development sessions frequently cite her influence. Her legacy exists more in changed practices than in theoretical frameworks bearing her name.
