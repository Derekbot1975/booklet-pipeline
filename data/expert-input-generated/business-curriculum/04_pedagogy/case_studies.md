# Using Case Studies in Business

## Introduction

Case studies represent one of the most powerful pedagogical tools in Business Studies education, offering students authentic contexts in which to apply theoretical knowledge. Ian Marcousé, whose textbooks have shaped Business Studies teaching across the UK for over three decades, argues that "business education without real-world context is merely the memorisation of disconnected facts" (Marcousé, 2017). The case study method bridges the gap between abstract theory and practical application, developing the analytical and evaluative skills central to Business Studies at GCSE and A-level.

The use of case studies aligns explicitly with the National Curriculum's requirement that students "understand how businesses develop" and can "evaluate business decisions and their impact on stakeholders" (DfE, 2014). All major UK examination boards—AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas—embed case study material throughout their specifications, with some assessments, particularly at A-level, structured entirely around pre-released case studies.

## The Pedagogical Value of Case Studies

### Contextualised Learning

Peter Stimpson and Alain Anderton's research into Business Studies pedagogy emphasises that case studies provide the essential context that transforms business theory from abstract concepts into meaningful understanding. Stimpson notes that students who engage regularly with case studies demonstrate significantly stronger application skills in examinations (Stimpson & Farquharson, 2015).

Case studies enable students to:
- Apply theoretical frameworks to realistic scenarios
- Understand the complexity and interdependence of business functions
- Appreciate how external factors influence business decisions
- Develop commercial awareness essential for higher education and employment

### Development of Higher-Order Skills

Mark Surridge, a chief examiner for AQA Business, emphasises that case studies are particularly effective in developing analysis and evaluation—the skills that differentiate strong A-level candidates. His work on assessment objectives shows that students who practise with varied case studies consistently achieve higher marks in AO3 (Analysis) and AO4 (Evaluation) questions (Surridge & Gillespie, 2019).

Nancy Wall's research into Business Studies teaching practices found that case study work develops critical thinking by requiring students to:
- Identify relevant information from complex scenarios
- Make judgements based on incomplete data
- Consider multiple stakeholder perspectives
- Justify decisions with reasoned arguments

## Types of Case Studies in Business Education

### Historical Case Studies

Historical case studies examine businesses or decisions from the past, allowing students to analyse outcomes with the benefit of hindsight. Examples include the collapse of Woolworths (2008), the Cadbury-Kraft takeover (2010), or the BP Deepwater Horizon disaster (2010).

**Pedagogical advantages:**
- Clear outcomes enable evaluation of decision quality
- Rich secondary sources available for independent research
- Opportunities to explore how business environments change over time
- Links to economic history and wider societal changes

Ian Marcousé's textbooks frequently employ historical case studies, arguing they provide "teachable moments where cause and effect are clearly visible" (Marcousé, 2020).

### Contemporary Case Studies

These focus on current business situations, often drawn from recent news or developing scenarios. Jim Riley's Tutor2u resources exemplify this approach, with regular updates featuring businesses like Deliveroo, Boohoo, or Greggs responding to contemporary challenges.

**Pedagogical advantages:**
- High student engagement due to familiarity and relevance
- Opportunities for ongoing monitoring and updates
- Links to current affairs and digital literacy
- Reflects the dynamic nature of business environments

**Teaching consideration:** Contemporary cases require regular updating and teachers must verify information accuracy as situations evolve.

### Pre-Release Case Studies

Edexcel A-level Business and OCR Cambridge Technicals use substantial pre-release case studies distributed weeks before examinations. Students analyse fictional or anonymised businesses in depth before applying their knowledge under examination conditions.

**Pedagogical approach:**
- Structured classroom analysis sessions
- Independent research tasks
- Practice questions using the case material
- Group discussions exploring different interpretations

Peter Stimpson advocates dedicating 6-8 teaching hours to pre-release material, ensuring students develop deep familiarity whilst avoiding rote-learned responses (Stimpson, 2018).

### Local and SME Case Studies

These examine small businesses within students' communities or regions. This approach, championed by Nancy Wall, makes business education tangible and accessible, particularly for students from non-business backgrounds.

**Practical implementation:**
- Guest speakers from local enterprises
- Business visits and field studies
- Student-created case studies through interviews
- Analysis of local economic development

WJEC Eduqas specifications explicitly encourage the use of Welsh businesses, recognising the pedagogical value of local context.

## Key Stages and Progression

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

At GCSE level, case studies should be relatively straightforward, focusing on single issues or decisions. AQA GCSE Business (8132) typically presents 60-100 word scenarios requiring students to apply concepts like market research, break-even analysis, or organisational structures.

**Teaching approach:**
- Short, focused case studies examining one business function
- Clear links between case context and specification content
- Scaffolded questions progressing from identification to evaluation
- Regular practice with timing and command word interpretation

Mark Surridge recommends introducing case study work from Year 10, beginning with familiar businesses (Apple, Nike, local retailers) before progressing to unfamiliar contexts that test genuine application rather than recalled knowledge.

### Key Stage 5 (A-level)

A-level case studies are substantially more complex, often featuring multiple stakeholders, conflicting objectives, and requiring integration across different topic areas.

**AQA A-level Business (7132):** Paper 3 presents a detailed case study with financial and contextual information, requiring analysis of strategic options and evaluation of decisions.

**Edexcel A-level Business (9BS0):** Papers 2 and 3 both use extended case studies, with Paper 3's pre-release material forming the basis for all questions.

**OCR A-level Business (H431):** Paper 3 requires evaluation of a substantial case study relating to business strategy.

**Teaching approach for A-level:**
- Extended case studies (500-2000 words) explored over multiple lessons
- Integration of quantitative and qualitative data analysis
- Stakeholder analysis activities
- Strategic decision-making exercises
- Timed examination-style responses under authentic conditions

## Pedagogical Strategies for Effective Case Study Teaching

### The CUEGIS Framework

Adapted from IB Business Management but increasingly used in UK Business Studies teaching, this framework helps students systematically analyse case studies through the lenses of:
- **C**hange
- **C**ulture
- **E**thics
- **G**lobalisation
- **I**nnovation
- **S**trategy

This structured approach, endorsed by Jim Riley, ensures comprehensive analysis rather than superficial description.

### Layered Case Study Analysis

Ian Marcousé's "layering" technique involves revisiting the same case study at different points in a course, each time adding theoretical depth:

1. **Initial exposure:** Basic comprehension and identification of business functions
2. **Second layer:** Application of specific theories (e.g., motivation theory, market analysis)
3. **Third layer:** Evaluation of decisions considering constraints and trade-offs
4. **Final layer:** Strategic analysis integrating multiple topic areas

This approach builds confidence whilst demonstrating how different aspects of business studies interconnect.

### Stakeholder Perspective Work

Nancy Wall's research demonstrates that students develop stronger evaluation skills when they explicitly consider multiple stakeholder perspectives. Practical activities include:

- **Role-play exercises:** Students represent different stakeholders (shareholders, employees, customers, community) debating business decisions
- **Perspective writing:** Composing brief reports from different stakeholder viewpoints
- **Conflict analysis:** Identifying where stakeholder interests diverge and exploring resolutions

This approach directly addresses AO4 requirements to "make informed judgements" considering multiple perspectives.

### Data-Rich Case Studies

Peter Stimpson emphasises the importance of case studies incorporating financial and quantitative data, particularly at A-level. Effective practice includes:

- Ratio analysis of published accounts
- Break-even calculations based on cost structures
- Investment appraisal of strategic options
- Interpretation of market research data

Edexcel's pre-release materials typically include detailed financial appendices, requiring students to calculate and interpret ratios, profit margins, and return on capital employed.

## Assessment and Case Study Work

### Command Words and Case Study Responses

Mark Surridge's work with AQA emphasises that command words determine how case study material should be used:

- **State/Identify** (1-2 marks): Direct references to case material
- **Explain** (3-4 marks): Case-specific application of theory
- **Analyse** (6-9 marks): Developed chains of reasoning using case context
- **Evaluate/Justify** (12-20 marks): Judgements supported by case-specific analysis

**Teaching implication:** Explicit instruction in command word interpretation is essential, with exemplar responses demonstrating how case material should be integrated at each level.

### The Application Challenge

Jim Riley identifies "generic responses" as the most common weakness in case study answers. Students often write theoretically sound responses that could apply to any business, failing to reference specific case details.

**Effective teaching strategies:**
- **Highlighter technique:** Students highlight case-specific details in their responses
- **"Name-check" requirement:** Responses must reference the specific business by name multiple times
- **Context markers:** Identifying 3-4 case-specific points that must appear in each answer
- **Comparative exercises:** Evaluating paired responses (generic vs. contextual) to identify differences

### Mark Schemes and Levels-Based Marking

All major UK exam boards use levels-based marking for extended case study responses. Understanding this approach is crucial for effective teaching.

**AQA's levels descriptors** (typical for evaluation questions):
- **Level 4 (10-12 marks):** Sustained evaluation with effective judgement supported by case-specific reasoning
- **Level 3 (7-9 marks):** Analysis developed with some evaluation and case application
- **Level 2 (4-6 marks):** Application of knowledge with limited development
- **Level 1 (1-3 marks):** Generic knowledge demonstrated

**Teaching approach:** Students should practice self and peer-assessment using mark schemes, identifying which level responses achieve and what's required for progression.

Nancy Wall advocates for teachers creating bespoke case studies tailored to their students' interests and local context. Effective approaches include:

1. **Adapting news articles:** Business sections of The Guardian, Financial Times, or BBC News provide raw material
2. **Anonymising local businesses:** Creates relevance whilst avoiding commercial sensitivities
3. **Combining elements:** Merging aspects of multiple real businesses to create teaching-focused scenarios
4. **Student-generated cases:** Research projects where students interview and write cases about local enterprises

## Challenges and Solutions in Case Study Teaching

### Time Constraints

Case studies are time-intensive. Mark Surridge acknowledges that comprehensive case analysis can consume entire lessons, creating pressure to "cover content."

**Solutions:**
- **Flipped learning:** Students read cases independently; lesson time focuses on analysis and discussion
- **Staged analysis:** Breaking complex cases across multiple lessons with specific focus each time
- **Homework integration:** Case study analysis as structured homework tasks with classroom discussion following
- **Strategic selection:** Choosing cases that address multiple specification points simultaneously

### Student Difficulty with Unfamiliar Contexts

Students often struggle when case studies feature industries or business models outside their experience (B2B manufacturing, financial services, international trade).

**Teaching strategies:**
- **Pre-teaching context:** Brief introduction to industry/sector before case analysis
- **Visual resources:** Videos, factory tours, or websites building familiarity
- **Analogies:** Connecting unfamiliar contexts to known experiences
- **Vocabulary support:** Glossaries for sector-specific terminology

Peter Stimpson recommends deliberately exposing students to diverse business contexts throughout courses, building "commercial literacy" that enables engagement with any case study.

### Balancing Theory and Application

Ian Marcousé warns against case studies becoming "theory-free zones" where students discuss scenarios without deploying business concepts.

**Effective practice:**
- **Theory retrieval starters:** Quick tests on relevant concepts before case analysis
- **Explicit theory-to-case links:** Requiring students to name theories being applied
- **Dual coding:** Visual frameworks showing how theoretical models map onto case details
- **Mark scheme focus:** Emphasising that marks require theoretical terminology within contextual application

## Inclusion and Differentiation

### Supporting All Learners

Nancy Wall's research emphasises that case studies, properly scaffolded, provide accessible entry points for students across ability ranges.

**Differentiation strategies:**
- **Tiered questioning:** Multiple questions on the same case at different command word levels
- **Information highlighting:** Pre-highlighting key data for students requiring support
- **Writing frames:** Structured templates for analysis and evaluation paragraphs
- **Group work:** Mixed-ability groups with differentiated roles
- **Extended cases:** Additional complexity and data for high-achieving students

### Cultural Relevance and Representation

Effective case study selection reflects diverse businesses, entrepreneurs, and contexts. Consider:

- Gender representation in business leaders featured
- Ethnic diversity in entrepreneurship examples
- Geographical spread including different UK regions and global contexts
- Business sizes from micro-enterprises to multinationals
- Sector variety including creative, tech, manufacturing, and service businesses

WJEC specifications explicitly encourage Welsh business examples; similar principles apply across UK nations, ensuring students see their contexts reflected.

## Future Developments and Contemporary Relevance

### Digital Transformation Case Studies

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital transformation, providing rich contemporary case material. Jim Riley's analysis highlights businesses like Zoom, Peloton, and Amazon as exemplars of adaptation to changed environments, whilst high street retailers like Debenhams and Arcadia demonstrate consequences of insufficient digital strategy.

**Teaching opportunities:**
- E-commerce adoption and omnichannel retailing
- Remote working implications for organisational structure
- Digital marketing and social media strategies
- Cyber security and data protection considerations

### Sustainability and Ethics

Contemporary case studies increasingly feature environmental and ethical dimensions. Students require frameworks for analysing:

- Corporate social responsibility programmes (e.g., Patagonia, The Body Shop)
- Supply chain ethics (examining businesses like Boohoo or Primark)
- Sustainability strategies (Unilever's Sustainable Living Plan)
- Stakeholder conflicts between profit and purpose

Mark Surridge notes that evaluation questions increasingly require consideration of ethical dimensions, reflecting contemporary business priorities.

### Brexit and Economic Uncertainty

Post-Brexit case studies offer opportunities to explore:

- Supply chain reconfiguration
- Changing regulatory environments
- Market access and trade agreements
- Currency fluctuation impacts

These contexts develop students' understanding of external environmental factors whilst connecting Business Studies to contemporary political and economic debates.

## Conclusion

Case studies represent an indispensable pedagogical tool in Business Studies education, transforming abstract theory into meaningful, applicable knowledge. As Ian Marcousé argues, "the case study method doesn't just teach business content—it teaches students to think like business people" (Marcousé, 2020). Through systematic exposure to varied, well-structured case studies, students develop the analytical, evaluative, and decision-making capabilities that define successful Business Studies education.

Effective case study teaching requires careful selection, structured pedagogical approaches, and explicit assessment literacy. Whether employing historical analyses, contemporary scenarios, or pre-release examination materials, teachers must ensure students move beyond superficial description toward deep, contextualised analysis that integrates theoretical frameworks with specific business contexts.

The expertise of educational leaders like Marcousé, Stimpson, Surridge, Riley, and Wall provides evidence-based approaches that, properly implemented, enable all students to engage meaningfully with business education's real-world complexities. As business environments continue evolving, case study methodology ensures Business Studies remains relevant, engaging, and intellectually rigorous.
