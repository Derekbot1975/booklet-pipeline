# SEND in Business Studies

## Introduction

Special Educational Needs and Disability (SEND) provision within Business Studies is a critical dimension of inclusive education that enables all learners to access the rich conceptual landscape of business theory and practice. The Equality Act 2010 establishes the legal framework requiring reasonable adjustments for disabled students, whilst the SEND Code of Practice (DfE & DoH, 2015) provides statutory guidance for identifying and supporting learners with additional needs. Business Studies educators must reconcile subject-specific demands—including numerical analysis, extended writing, case study evaluation, and theoretical application—with differentiated approaches that ensure equitable access to learning.

## Legislative and Policy Framework

### UK Legal Requirements

The Equality Act 2010 defines disability as a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on a person's ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. Business Studies departments must make reasonable adjustments, including differentiated teaching materials, alternative assessment formats, and assistive technology provision. The Children and Families Act 2014 strengthened SEND provision through Education, Health and Care Plans (EHCPs), which replace statements and coordinate support across agencies for learners aged 0-25.

### SEND Code of Practice

The statutory guidance emphasises a graduated approach: Assess, Plan, Do, Review. Business Studies teachers should regularly assess SEND learners' specific barriers to accessing business concepts, plan targeted interventions, implement differentiated strategies, and review progress systematically. The Code identifies four broad areas of need: Communication and Interaction; Cognition and Learning; Social, Emotional and Mental Health; and Sensory and/or Physical Needs.

## SEND Categories and Business Studies Implications

### Communication and Interaction Needs

Students with Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC) or speech and language difficulties may struggle with Business Studies' collaborative elements, including group work on enterprise projects or role-play exercises for marketing campaigns. Jim Riley's teaching resources emphasise structured business scenarios with clear parameters, which particularly benefit ASC learners who require predictability and explicit success criteria.

**Practical strategies include:**
- Providing written instructions alongside verbal explanations for case study analysis
- Creating visual timetables for extended business project work
- Offering sentence stems for evaluation questions (e.g., "The stakeholder impact of this decision is significant because...")
- Using social stories to prepare students for business simulations or visiting speakers

### Cognition and Learning Needs

Learners with moderate learning difficulties (MLD), severe learning difficulties (SLD), or specific learning difficulties (SpLD) such as dyslexia face distinct challenges with Business Studies content. Ian Marcousé's emphasis on conceptual understanding over rote memorisation proves beneficial, but educators must scaffold access to dense theoretical material found in A-Level specifications.

**Subject-specific considerations:**

For **dyslexic learners**, Business Studies presents challenges with technical vocabulary (entrepreneur, diversification, liquidity ratios) and extended case studies. Exam boards including AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas permit 25% extra time and use of coloured overlays. Peter Stimpson and Mark Surridge's textbooks increasingly incorporate glossaries and key term boxes, which support vocabulary acquisition.

For **dyscalculic learners**, quantitative methods pose significant barriers. Break-even analysis, ratio interpretation, and investment appraisal require systematic scaffolding. Teachers should provide:
- Formulae sheets as standard (mirroring GCSE Maths Foundation Tier practice)
- Step-by-step calculation frameworks with worked examples
- Visual representations of financial data (bar charts, pie charts) alongside numerical tables
- Calculator proficiency development, including use of scientific functions

The Business Studies specifications at GCSE (particularly AQA 8132, Edexcel 1BS0, OCR J204) include substantial quantitative content (10% at GCSE, 10-15% at A-Level), making mathematical confidence essential.

### Social, Emotional and Mental Health Needs

Students with ADHD, attachment disorders, or anxiety conditions may find Business Studies' evaluative demands particularly challenging. The requirement to construct balanced arguments across examination questions—as Nancy Wall emphasises in her assessment guidance—demands emotional regulation and sustained concentration.

**Classroom adaptations:**

- Provide structured debate frameworks for analysing business decisions (e.g., should Amazon enter physical retail?)
- Offer movement breaks during case study comprehension
- Create low-stakes formative assessment opportunities before high-pressure examinations
- Develop portfolios showcasing incremental progress rather than relying solely on terminal examinations

Mark Surridge notes that Business Studies inherently addresses mental health through Units on human resource management, organisational culture, and leadership styles, providing opportunities for students to contextualise their experiences within academic frameworks.

### Sensory and Physical Needs

Students with visual impairment (VI) require enlarged texts, high-contrast resources, and tactile business models. Audio recordings of case studies prove invaluable. The prevalence of data response questions across all exam specifications (AQA's 'Context 1' and 'Context 2' papers, OCR's case study pre-release materials) necessitates accessible formatting.

Hearing impaired (HI) learners benefit from:
- Written transcripts for business documentary clips
- Preferential seating for teacher-led instruction
- Pre-teaching of technical vocabulary with British Sign Language (BSL) interpretations where available
- Visual organisers showing business concept hierarchies (e.g., stakeholder mapping diagrams)

Physical disabilities may limit participation in practical enterprise activities. Business Studies teachers should ensure virtual alternatives exist—online market research via Google Forms rather than street surveys, video pitches instead of live presentations.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Though Business Studies isn't statutory at KS3, many schools offer discrete courses or embed business concepts within PSHE. SEND learners benefit from concrete, experience-based introduction to business concepts:
- School enterprise projects with clearly defined roles
- Local business case studies with familiar contexts
- Simple financial literacy (budgeting, saving) linked to lived experience
- Visual representations of business structures (sole trader, partnership, company)

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16, GCSE)

GCSE Business Studies specifications demand analytical and evaluative skills coded in Assessment Objectives as AO2 (application) and AO3 (analysis/evaluation). SEND learners require explicit teaching of command words and assessment literacy.

**Exam board specifications:**

- **AQA 8132**: Two examination papers with structured contexts. SEND adaptations include access arrangements (readers, scribes, supervised rest breaks) and modified large print papers.
- **Edexcel 1BS0**: Theme 1 (Investigating small business) and Theme 2 (Building a business). The specification's emphasis on entrepreneurship allows SEND learners to engage with aspirational role models including disabled business owners.
- **OCR J204**: Includes pre-released case study (Component 2), permitting advance preparation particularly beneficial for processing speed difficulties.

Jim Riley advocates teaching to command words systematically—Explain (2 marks), Analyse (6 marks), Justify (9 marks), Evaluate (12 marks)—providing differentiated writing frames matched to cognitive load.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18, A-Level)

A-Level Business (AQA 7132, Edexcel 9BS0, OCR H431) presents intensified cognitive demands. Extended 20-mark essays requiring integration of multiple business theories challenge working memory and executive function.

**SEND-specific strategies:**

- Teach business theory incrementally: introduce Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs before applying to workforce motivation; establish Porter's Five Forces before analysing competitive environments
- Use graphic organisers (fishbone diagrams for cause-effect relationships in business failure)
- Provide anthology of pre-learned case study businesses (e.g., Innocent Drinks, Dyson, Greggs) reducing cognitive load during examination contexts
- Explicitly model written evaluation using Ian Marcousé's 'On one hand... However...' framework

Nancy Wall's research on Business Studies assessment emphasises 'context-rich' evaluation. SEND learners require scaffolding to select relevant business knowledge and apply it precisely to novel scenarios—a metacognitive skill requiring explicit teaching.

## Differentiation Strategies for Business Studies

### Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

UDL principles (CAST, 2018) advocate multiple means of representation, action/expression, and engagement—naturally aligned with Business Studies pedagogy:

**Representation**: Present marketing mix theory through video case studies (e.g., Apple product launches), infographics, textbook extracts, and podcast interviews with marketing directors, permitting SEND learners varied access points.

**Action/Expression**: Allow students to demonstrate understanding through presentations, written reports, annotated diagrams, video pitches, or audio commentaries on business decisions.

**Engagement**: Connect abstract concepts (economies of scale, market segmentation) to students' lives through relatable examples (Netflix pricing strategy, local restaurant competition).

### Scaffolding Techniques

Peter Stimpson's textbook approach models effective scaffolding: introducing concepts through simplified definitions, providing worked examples, then progressing to independent application. Business Studies teachers should:

- Use partially completed gap-fill exercises for weaker readers tackling dense case studies
- Provide writing frames with topic sentences for evaluation paragraphs
- Offer tiered activities within lessons (e.g., calculating gross profit for differentiated businesses of varying complexity)
- Pre-teach vocabulary before case study introduction

### Technology-Enhanced Learning

Assistive technologies transform SEND provision:
- **Text-to-speech software** (Natural Reader, Read&Write) supports dyslexic students accessing extensive case study materials
- **Mind-mapping tools** (MindMeister, Coggle) help organise stakeholder analysis or SWOT frameworks
- **Digital flashcards** (Quizlet, Anki) support technical vocabulary retention through spaced retrieval practice
- **Screen recording** (Loom, Screencastify) enables students to verbally explain business concepts rather than writing extended responses

The business education technology promoted by tutor2u (Jim Riley's platform) includes interactive quizzes, animated explainers, and case study digests—all inherently supportive of diverse learning needs.

## Assessment and Reasonable Adjustments

### Access Arrangements

JCQ (Joint Council for Qualifications) regulations permit various access arrangements following assessment by qualified specialists:

- **Extra time** (25% standard): Benefits processing speed difficulties, anxiety, or physical disabilities affecting writing speed
- **Supervised rest breaks**: Supports ADHD, chronic fatigue, or medical conditions
- **Reader** or **computer reader**: Assists dyslexic students or those with visual impairment
- **Scribe** or **speech recognition technology**: Enables students with dysgraphia or physical disabilities to demonstrate knowledge
- **Modified papers**: Enlarged text (A3, A4 font size 24) or braille for visually impaired learners
- **Separate invigilation**: Reduces anxiety or accommodates need for movement/verbal processing

Business Studies departments should liaise with SENCOs early in Key Stage 4 to establish appropriate arrangements validated through normal teaching practice.

### Assessment Design

Teachers can design SEND-inclusive assessments by:
- Providing scenario choice where possible (e.g., select from two case study businesses)
- Ensuring readability appropriate to specification level (Flesch-Kincaid score consideration)
- Using consistent terminology across question papers
- Incorporating visual data (graphs, charts) reducing text density
- Breaking multi-part questions into discrete sub-sections with clear mark allocations

Mark Surridge notes that business examinations' structured approach—with clearly signposted Assessment Objectives—supports SEND learners better than unstructured essay subjects might.

## Collaborative Working and Communication

### SENCO Liaison

Business Studies teachers should engage SENCOs regarding:
- Individual SEND profiles and recommended strategies
- EHCP outcomes relating to employability or vocational skills
- Monitoring arrangements for interventions
- Transition planning from Key Stage 3 to 4, or 4 to 5

### Parental Engagement

Parents/carers provide invaluable insights into successful strategies. For Business Studies specifically, discuss:
- Whether practical enterprise projects require additional preparation or simplified roles
- Home support for revision of technical vocabulary
- Career aspirations linking Business Studies content to student interests

### Multi-Agency Working

For SEND learners with complex needs, Business Studies teachers may coordinate with:
- Educational psychologists regarding cognitive processing recommendations
- Occupational therapists for physical access to practical activities
- Speech and language therapists for communication support in presentations

## Curriculum Content and SEND Relevance

### Inclusive Business Contexts

Business Studies naturally encompasses diversity and inclusion themes:
- **Employment law**: Equality Act 2010, disability discrimination, reasonable workplace adjustments
- **Corporate social responsibility**: Business responses to diversity, accessible product design (Microsoft's inclusive Xbox controllers)
- **Entrepreneurship**: Disabled entrepreneurs as case studies (Steven Sears' disability employment consultancy)
- **Organisational culture**: Creating inclusive workplace cultures, mental health support policies

Ian Marcousé emphasises authentic business contexts. SEND learners benefit from studying businesses with explicit accessibility missions—TalkTalk's apprenticeship schemes for young people with learning disabilities, Timpson's employment of ex-offenders and SEND individuals.

### Careers Education

Business Studies supports SEND learners' career development through:
- Understanding employment rights and workplace reasonable adjustments
- Developing transferable skills (communication, problem-solving, numeracy)
- Exploring diverse career pathways (apprenticeships, supported employment, self-employment)
- Building enterprise capabilities fostering independence

The Federation of Small Businesses (FSB) reports that self-employment rates among disabled people exceed national averages, making entrepreneurship content particularly relevant.

## Professional Development for Teachers

Business Studies teachers require ongoing CPD addressing:
- SEND awareness training (ASC understanding, dyslexia-friendly teaching)
- Assessment literacy regarding access arrangements
- Subject-specific differentiation techniques for quantitative content
- Assistive technology integration

The Business, Economics and Enterprise Association (BEEA) offers SEND-focused professional learning. Jim Riley's tutor2u platform provides webinars on inclusive Business Studies pedagogy. Exam boards increasingly offer guidance on supporting SEND learners within subject specifications.

## Conclusion

SEND provision in Business Studies represents both ethical imperative and pedagogical opportunity. By employing differentiated instruction, assistive technologies, and reasonable adjustments, teachers enable all learners to access rigorous business education. The subject's inherent relevance to employment, independence, and economic participation makes inclusive practice particularly significant. As Ian Marcousé observes, Business Studies should equip students with tools to understand and participate in the economic world—a goal achievable for learners of all abilities through thoughtful, evidence-informed teaching.
