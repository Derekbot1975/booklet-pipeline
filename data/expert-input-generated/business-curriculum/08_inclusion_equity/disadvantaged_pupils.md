# Disadvantaged Pupils and Business Education

## Introduction and Context

Disadvantaged pupils in UK Business Studies education represent a significant equity challenge that requires targeted pedagogical approaches and curriculum considerations. The Department for Education defines disadvantaged pupils primarily as those eligible for Pupil Premium funding, which includes students who have been eligible for free school meals (FSM) at any point in the last six years, children looked after by local authorities, and those adopted from care or who have left care.

Business Studies, as a discipline, presents unique opportunities and challenges for disadvantaged learners. The subject's practical, real-world applications can provide powerful engagement tools, yet the abstract nature of business concepts and the potential lack of family business experience among disadvantaged pupils can create barriers to achievement. According to the Education Endowment Foundation (EEF), the disadvantaged attainment gap at GCSE level persists across all subjects, with Business Studies seeing similar patterns to other humanities and social science subjects.

## National Curriculum and Statutory Framework

### Key Stage 3 and 4 Considerations

The National Curriculum for England does not mandate Business Studies at Key Stage 3, positioning it as an optional subject within the broader citizenship and economic wellbeing curriculum. However, many schools offer Business Studies from Year 9 onwards, with formal qualifications typically beginning at Key Stage 4 (GCSE level).

For disadvantaged pupils, this optional status creates an equity issue. Ian Marcousé, in his extensive work on Business Studies pedagogy, has argued that Business Studies should be more widely accessible at Key Stage 3, as it provides essential economic literacy and entrepreneurial thinking skills that can be transformative for pupils from non-business backgrounds. Research published in the *Journal of Vocational Education & Training* (2019) suggests that early exposure to business concepts correlates with improved financial literacy and career aspirations among disadvantaged groups.

### GCSE and A-Level Specifications

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas) structure their Business Studies specifications around core themes that require significant cultural capital:

- **AQA GCSE Business (8132)**: Focuses on business in the real world, requiring students to apply concepts to business contexts they may not have personal experience with
- **Edexcel GCSE Business (1BS0)**: Emphasises contemporary business issues and case studies, often featuring large corporations unfamiliar to disadvantaged pupils
- **OCR GCSE Business (J204)**: Includes significant focus on business finance and numerical concepts that may disadvantage pupils with weaker mathematical foundations
- **A-Level specifications** across all boards demand sophisticated understanding of economic theory, strategic thinking, and analytical writing skills that often correlate with socioeconomic advantage

## Barriers to Achievement for Disadvantaged Pupils

### Cultural Capital and Business Exposure

Pierre Bourdieu's concept of cultural capital is particularly relevant in Business Studies education. Nancy Wall, in her research on vocational education equity, identifies that disadvantaged pupils often lack the "business habitus" – the informal knowledge, vocabulary, and experiences that middle-class pupils acquire through family conversations, parental occupations, and exposure to business environments.

Specific barriers include:

- **Limited family business experience**: Pupils whose parents work in low-wage employment or are unemployed may lack dinner-table conversations about business strategy, workplace dynamics, or entrepreneurship
- **Restricted access to business news media**: The *Financial Times*, *The Economist*, and business-focused content often require subscriptions or are not part of disadvantaged households' media consumption
- **Fewer opportunities for work experience**: Pupils from disadvantaged backgrounds frequently have less access to quality work placements in professional business environments
- **Digital poverty**: Limited home internet access restricts engagement with online business resources, current affairs, and digital research skills

### Conceptual and Linguistic Challenges

Peter Stimpson and Mark Surridge, in their collaborative work on Business Studies pedagogy, emphasise that the subject contains significant tier 2 and tier 3 vocabulary that can disadvantage pupils with limited literacy exposure. Terms like "stakeholder," "liquidity," "economies of scale," and "market segmentation" require explicit teaching for disadvantaged learners who may not encounter such language outside the classroom.

Jim Riley, through his influential *tutor2u* platform, has documented how Business Studies assessment increasingly demands extended writing and evaluation skills. These "synoptic" requirements favour pupils with strong literacy backgrounds and extensive reading experience – areas where disadvantaged pupils statistically underperform.

### Mathematical and Financial Literacy

GCSE and A-Level Business Studies specifications include substantial quantitative content: ratio analysis, break-even calculations, cash flow forecasting, and investment appraisal. Research from the Nuffield Foundation (2017) demonstrates that pupils eligible for free school meals are, on average, 18 months behind their peers in mathematical attainment by the end of Key Stage 4. This attainment gap directly impacts Business Studies achievement, particularly in the financial and quantitative sections that typically constitute 30-40% of examination marks.

## Evidence-Based Strategies for Inclusion

### Building Cultural and Business Capital

**1. Structured exposure to business contexts**

Teachers should systematically build business awareness through:

- **Local business partnerships**: Inviting speakers from diverse business backgrounds, prioritising entrepreneurs from similar socioeconomic backgrounds to pupils
- **Business visits**: Organising trips to local enterprises, ensuring these include SMEs and social enterprises, not just large corporations
- **Virtual business experiences**: Utilising resources like the *Peter Jones Foundation's Tycoon in Schools* programme or *Young Enterprise* schemes that provide structured business simulation experiences
- **Current affairs integration**: Dedicating 10-15 minutes weekly to accessible business news discussion using resources like BBC Business News or simplified business journalism

Ian Marcousé recommends creating a "business vocabulary wall" that explicitly teaches and regularly revisits tier 3 business terminology, with contextual examples relevant to pupils' lived experiences.

**2. Scaffolding and knowledge organisers**

Mark Surridge advocates for comprehensive knowledge organisers that disadvantaged pupils can access at home, compensating for limited parental support with homework. These should include:

- Key definitions with contextualised examples
- Visual diagrams and models (e.g., Boston Matrix, Ansoff Matrix)
- Worked examples of calculations with step-by-step processes
- Real business case studies with guided questions

**3. Explicit teaching of examination skills**

Nancy Wall's research emphasises that disadvantaged pupils benefit significantly from explicit teaching of examination techniques, including:

- Command word analysis (evaluate, assess, analyse, discuss)
- Paragraph structure for extended responses using frameworks like PEEL (Point, Evidence, Explain, Link)
- Mark scheme deconstruction activities
- Regular low-stakes practice with immediate, specific feedback

### Addressing Financial and Mathematical Barriers

**1. Pre-teaching mathematical concepts**

Business Studies teachers should coordinate with mathematics departments to identify pupils requiring additional support with:

- Percentage calculations (particularly percentage change and reverse percentages)
- Ratio analysis
- Graph interpretation
- Data manipulation

Consider implementing "maths for Business Studies" intervention sessions, particularly in the lead-up to GCSE examinations.

**2. Contextualising calculations**

Jim Riley emphasises that disadvantaged pupils engage more successfully with quantitative content when calculations are presented in meaningful contexts rather than abstract exercises. For example, teaching break-even analysis using a student-designed business idea rather than generic textbook examples.

**3. Financial literacy foundations**

Many disadvantaged pupils lack basic financial literacy, which undermines their understanding of business finance topics. Integrate personal finance concepts (budgeting, saving, credit, debt) as bridges to business financial management, using resources from organisations like *Young Money* (formerly the Personal Finance Education Group).

### Pedagogical Approaches

**1. Dialogic teaching and oracy**

Research from the EEF's *Teaching and Learning Toolkit* shows that dialogic teaching approaches can have high impact for low cost, particularly benefiting disadvantaged pupils. In Business Studies, this means:

- Structured business debates on ethical issues (e.g., zero-hours contracts, environmental responsibility)
- Role-play activities representing different stakeholder perspectives
- Collaborative business planning exercises
- Think-pair-share routines for complex concepts

**2. Retrieval practice and spaced repetition**

Cognitive science research, popularised by organisations like *researchED*, demonstrates that retrieval practice benefits all pupils but particularly those without strong home learning environments. Implement:

- Low-stakes quizzes at lesson starts
- Cumulative testing across topics
- Interleaving of content (mixing topics rather than blocking)
- Homework that focuses on retrieval rather than new content creation

**3. Metacognitive strategies**

The EEF identifies metacognition and self-regulation as high-impact interventions. For Business Studies:

- Explicitly teach note-taking strategies for case study analysis
- Model "thinking aloud" when approaching complex business scenarios
- Use graphic organisers for planning extended writing
- Teach self-assessment using mark schemes and success criteria

## Assessment and Feedback Considerations

### Reducing Assessment Disadvantage

All four major exam boards' specifications include assessment objectives that can inadvertently disadvantage pupils lacking cultural capital:

- **AO2 (Application)**: Requires applying business knowledge to contexts that may be unfamiliar
- **AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation)**: Demands sophisticated written expression and reasoning

**Strategies for equitable assessment preparation:**

1. **Familiarisation with diverse business contexts**: Expose pupils to wide-ranging case studies including social enterprises, cooperatives, family businesses, and businesses from diverse cultural backgrounds, not just FTSE 100 companies

2. **Scaffolded case study analysis**: Provide structured frameworks for approaching unseen case studies in examinations, such as:
   - SWOT analysis templates
   - Stakeholder identification matrices
   - Decision-making trees

3. **Command word rehearsal**: Create a systematic programme ensuring pupils practice every command word (justify, recommend, assess, etc.) multiple times across different contexts before examinations

### Feedback That Closes Gaps

Research from Professor John Hattie indicates that feedback is among the highest-impact teaching strategies, but only when it is specific, timely, and actionable. For disadvantaged pupils in Business Studies:

- **Verbal feedback during lessons**: Disadvantaged pupils may struggle to act on written feedback at home; prioritise immediate, verbal feedback during lesson time
- **Model answers and exemplars**: Provide concrete examples of successful responses rather than abstract descriptors
- **Focused feedback**: Target one or two specific improvements rather than overwhelming pupils with multiple criticisms
- **Opportunity to respond**: Build lesson time for pupils to improve work based on feedback, ensuring the feedback loop is completed in school rather than relying on home completion

## Resource Equity

### Textbook and Material Considerations

Disadvantaged pupils are less likely to have textbooks at home and may lack funds for revision guides. Schools should consider:

- **Class sets maintained in school**: Ensuring all core texts remain in school with structured lesson-time reading
- **Digital access**: Providing logins for online platforms (e.g., *tutor2u*, *Seneca Learning*, *BBC Bitesize*) and facilitating access through school devices
- **Photocopied key resources**: Creating take-home resource packs for disadvantaged pupils covering essential content

Ian Marcousé's textbooks (published by Hodder Education for AQA and Edexcel specifications) are widely used but expensive. Schools should use Pupil Premium funding strategically to ensure disadvantaged pupils have equivalent access to revision materials.

### Technology and Digital Divide

The COVID-19 pandemic starkly revealed digital inequalities. For Business Studies specifically:

- **Remote access to business news**: Many quality business resources require subscriptions; schools should provide authenticated access through school libraries
- **Software for business planning**: Tools like basic spreadsheet software for financial calculations must be accessible
- **Device access**: Ensure disadvantaged pupils can access school devices for homework requiring digital literacy

## Pupil Premium Strategy for Business Departments

### Identifying and Tracking Disadvantaged Pupils

Business Studies departments should:

1. **Maintain awareness of Pupil Premium eligible students** in each class without stigmatisation
2. **Monitor progress data specifically for disadvantaged cohorts**, identifying gaps early
3. **Collaborate with senior leadership** to allocate Pupil Premium funding for Business Studies interventions

### Evidence-Based Interventions

According to EEF guidance, effective Pupil Premium spending focuses on:

**High-quality teaching as the primary intervention** (Peter Stimpson emphasises this in his teacher training work):
- Investment in Business Studies CPD focusing on pedagogy for disadvantaged pupils
- Collaborative lesson planning prioritising accessibility
- Regular lesson observations with equity focus

**Targeted academic support**:
- Small-group intervention for pupils falling behind in quantitative or literacy-heavy content
- One-to-one mentoring for disadvantaged pupils considering Business-related post-16 pathways
- Holiday revision programmes before GCSE examinations

**Wider strategies**:
- Subsidising *Young Enterprise* company programme fees
- Funding business visit transport costs
- Providing business attire for work experience placements

## Careers Education and Aspiration

Business Studies offers powerful career education opportunities for disadvantaged pupils, yet research from the Sutton Trust (2020) shows that disadvantaged pupils are less likely to access professional business careers despite studying the subject.

### Broadening Career Horizons

Nancy Wall's research emphasises the importance of exposing disadvantaged pupils to the full range of business careers beyond retail or self-employment:

- **Professional business services**: Accounting, management consultancy, business analysis
- **Corporate careers**: Marketing, human resources, operations management
- **Entrepreneurship**: Social enterprise, digital business, consultancy
- **Apprenticeship pathways**: Degree apprenticeships in business management, accounting, or chartered management

### Practical Career Integration

- **Guest speaker programme**: Invite business professionals from diverse backgrounds, including those who were first-generation university students or from disadvantaged backgrounds themselves
- **Alumni connections**: Connect current pupils with former pupils pursuing business careers
- **Work experience equity**: Actively support disadvantaged pupils in securing quality business work placements through school networks rather than relying on parental connections
- **Sixth form and university guidance**: Explicitly teach disadvantaged pupils about Russell Group business courses, apprenticeships, and application processes they may not access through family knowledge

## Pastoral and Wellbeing Considerations

Disadvantaged pupils often face additional pressures that affect their Business Studies engagement:

- **Part-time employment**: Some disadvantaged pupils work significant hours, impacting homework completion and revision time
- **Caring responsibilities**: Pupils with caring responsibilities may struggle with after-school enrichment
- **Stress and mental health**: Economic disadvantage correlates with higher stress levels that affect learning

**Department-level responses:**

1. **Flexible deadlines with dignity**: Allow additional time for coursework without requiring detailed personal explanations
2. **Lunchtime and before-school support**: Offer alternatives to after-school sessions
3. **Reduced homework burden**: Focus homework on high-impact activities (retrieval practice, reading) rather than extensive production tasks
4. **Trauma-informed approaches**: Recognise that some pupils' lack of engagement may reflect challenging home circumstances rather than low motivation

## Leadership and Whole-School Approaches

Curriculum leaders should advocate for Business Studies as a vehicle for social mobility by:

- **Promoting uptake among disadvantaged pupils**: Actively encouraging Pupil Premium pupils to select Business Studies at GCSE and A-Level
- **Challenging stereotypes**: Addressing misconceptions that Business Studies is only for those from business families
- **Data analysis**: Regularly analysing performance data by disadvantaged status and implementing responsive interventions
- **Resource allocation**: Bidding for Pupil Premium funds for Business Studies specific initiatives
- **Staff CPD**: Ensuring all Business Studies teachers receive training on teaching disadvantaged pupils effectively

## Conclusion and Key Principles

Supporting disadvantaged pupils in Business Studies requires a multifaceted approach combining high-quality teaching, explicit skill development, cultural capital building, and strategic resource allocation. The subject's real-world applicability and career potential make it particularly valuable for pupils from disadvantaged backgrounds, yet these same pupils face significant barriers to success without targeted support.

**Core principles for equity in Business Studies:**

1. **Explicit teaching** of vocabulary, concepts, and examination skills that advantaged pupils may acquire informally
2. **Cultural capital building** through structured exposure to business contexts, news, and experiences
3. **Pedagogical strategies** emphasising retrieval practice, dialogic teaching, and scaffolding
4. **Assessment preparation** that familiarises pupils with diverse business contexts and command words
5. **Resource equity** ensuring disadvantaged pupils have equivalent access to materials and technology
6. **Career education** that broadens aspirations and provides practical pathway information
7. **Monitoring and evaluation** of disadvantaged pupil outcomes with responsive intervention

As Jim Riley notes in his teacher guidance materials, Business Studies teachers have a unique opportunity to demystify the business world for pupils who might otherwise feel it is not "for them." By implementing evidence-based inclusive practices, teachers can ensure that Business Studies becomes a genuine vehicle for social mobility rather than a subject that reinforces existing inequalities.

The work of Ian Marcousé, Peter Stimpson, Mark Surridge, Jim Riley, and Nancy Wall collectively emphasises that equity in Business Studies education requires intentional, informed practice. Success depends on teachers and curriculum leaders recognising the specific barriers disadvantaged pupils face and implementing proven strategies to overcome them, ultimately ensuring that all pupils can access the knowledge, skills, and aspirations that Business Studies can provide.
