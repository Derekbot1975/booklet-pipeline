# Textiles at Key Stage 3

## National Curriculum Context

Key Stage 3 Textiles education forms part of the Design and Technology National Curriculum in England, covering Years 7-9 (ages 11-14). The current Design and Technology programmes of study (DfE, 2013) require students to develop 'creativity and innovation through designing and making products that solve real and relevant problems within a variety of contexts, considering their own and others' needs, wants and values.'

Within this framework, Textiles represents one of the material areas through which students develop design and technology capability. The subject is unique in its blend of technical knowledge, cultural context, and creative expression, as noted by Professor Stephanie Atkinson in her research on design and technology pedagogy at Sunderland University.

### Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum states that all pupils should:
- Develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently
- Build and apply a repertoire of knowledge, understanding and skills to design and make high-quality prototypes and products
- Evaluate and test their ideas and products and the work of others
- Understand and apply the principles of nutrition and learn how to cook

For Textiles specifically at KS3, students must learn about:
- New and emerging technologies
- Energy generation and storage
- Developments in new materials
- Systems approach to designing
- Mechanical devices
- Materials and their working properties

## Key Pedagogical Approaches

### Design Thinking in Textiles

Kay Stables' influential work on designerly thinking emphasises the importance of developing students' ability to think through materials. At Key Stage 3, this means moving beyond simply following instructions to developing independent design capability. Stables argues that effective Textiles education should enable students to 'think with their hands', using fabric manipulation and construction techniques as vehicles for creative problem-solving.

Practical implications for teachers include:
- Providing open-ended design briefs that require genuine decision-making
- Encouraging iterative development through sampling and experimentation
- Building in reflection time for students to evaluate material choices
- Creating opportunities for students to experience 'design fixation' and work through it

### Making Skills Development

At KS3, students should progressively develop technical competence across a range of textile techniques. A well-structured three-year programme typically builds skills hierarchically:

**Year 7 foundations:**
- Hand sewing techniques (running stitch, backstitch, blanket stitch, oversewing)
- Introduction to sewing machine operation and safety
- Basic pattern reading and cutting out
- Simple surface decoration (appliqué, basic embroidery, fabric printing)
- Understanding of fabric properties and fibre content

**Year 8 development:**
- More complex construction techniques (seams, hems, facings)
- Pattern adaptation and modification
- Extended surface decoration techniques (tie-dye, batik, screen printing)
- Introduction to CAD/CAM in textiles (computerised embroidery, digital printing)
- Textile science investigations

**Year 9 sophistication:**
- Independent construction of 3D textile products
- Integration of multiple techniques within single products
- Understanding of industrial/commercial processes
- Sustainable textiles and ethical considerations
- Portfolio development preparing for GCSE

## Core Knowledge and Understanding

### Textile Materials and Components

Students at KS3 must develop understanding of:

**Natural fibres:**
- Cotton (cellulose-based, absorbent, biodegradable)
- Wool (protein-based, elastic, insulating)
- Silk (protein filament, luxurious properties)
- Linen (cellulose from flax, strong, cool-wearing)

**Synthetic fibres:**
- Polyester (thermoplastic, hardwearing, non-absorbent)
- Nylon (strong, elastic recovery, quick-drying)
- Acrylic (wool-like, lightweight, retains heat)
- Elastane/Lycra (extreme stretch properties)

**Fabric construction:**
- Woven fabrics (plain, twill, satin weaves)
- Knitted fabrics (weft and warp knitting)
- Non-woven fabrics (felt, bonded fabrics)
- Smart and technical textiles (conductive thread, phase-change materials, microencapsulation)

### Tools and Equipment

Safe and competent use of:
- Cutting tools (shears, scissors, rotary cutters, seam rippers)
- Sewing machines (threading, tension adjustment, stitch selection)
- Pressing equipment (irons, pressing cloths, seam rolls)
- Specialist equipment (embroidery hoops, fabric paints, transfer materials)
- CAD/CAM equipment (digital cutters, computerised embroidery machines)

### Technical Knowledge

Building technical understanding through:
- Seam construction and seam allowances (typically 1.5cm in education)
- Pattern markings and their meanings (notches, grain lines, fold lines, darts)
- Fabric grain and bias (warp, weft, selvedge, bias stretch)
- Fastenings and their appropriate applications (zips, buttons, Velcro, press studs)
- Tension and stitch length for different fabrics and purposes

## Design Process in Textiles

### Research and Analysis

Stephanie Atkinson's research emphasises the importance of authentic design contexts. At KS3, effective textiles projects should:
- Begin with genuine user needs or clear contextual drivers
- Include primary and secondary research appropriate to the student's age
- Analyse existing textile products to understand design decisions
- Consider cultural, historical and contemporary textile traditions

Students should investigate:
- Target user/client requirements through interviews or questionnaires
- Mood boards exploring colour, texture, pattern and style
- Fabric samples and their properties for specific applications
- Sustainable and ethical considerations in textile production

### Design Development

Kay Stables identifies 'designerly behaviour' as central to effective practice. At KS3, this manifests through:

**Sampling and experimentation:**
Students should create physical samples before committing to final designs, exploring:
- Colour combinations and effects
- Fabric manipulation techniques (gathering, pleating, tucking, quilting)
- Surface decoration trials (printing, dyeing, embellishment)
- Construction method tests
- Scale and proportion studies

**Design communication:**
- Annotated sketches showing design thinking
- Use of appropriate textile-specific vocabulary
- Fabric swatches and material boards
- Working drawings with measurements and construction details
- CAD applications (Adobe Illustrator/Photoshop for textile design, pattern software)

### Making and Quality

Students should understand quality indicators in textile products:
- Neat and accurate seam construction
- Consistent stitch length appropriate to fabric
- Well-pressed seams and hems
- Secure fastenings properly positioned
- Finished edges (raw edges concealed or neatened)
- Accurate measuring and cutting
- Appropriate thread colour and weight selection

### Evaluation

Moving beyond simple likes/dislikes to analytical evaluation:
- Testing against design criteria and user requirements
- Identifying specific technical successes and areas for improvement
- Understanding fitness for purpose and target market appropriateness
- Considering environmental impact and sustainability
- Reflecting on process as well as product

## Cultural and Contextual Understanding

### Historical and Contemporary Context

Artist-practitioners Alice Kettle and Cas Holmes exemplify how contemporary textiles bridges art, craft and design. Their work should be introduced to KS3 students to demonstrate:
- Textiles as fine art medium (Kettle's large-scale embroidered works)
- Experimental and sustainable approaches (Holmes' use of found materials and stitch)
- Narrative and meaning in textile work
- Connection between traditional techniques and contemporary expression

Students should study:
- Historical textile traditions (British embroidery, Arts and Crafts movement, Bauhaus weaving)
- Cultural textiles from around the world (Indian block printing, Japanese shibori, African wax prints)
- Contemporary textile designers and their work
- Fashion designers and their influence on textile trends
- Ethical fashion movements and sustainable textile innovation

### Wider Context and Careers

Exposure to textile-related careers including:
- Fashion design and textile design
- Costume design for theatre and film
- Interior design and soft furnishings
- Technical textiles for medical, sport and industrial applications
- Textile conservation and museum curation
- Textile technology and sustainable materials innovation
- CAD/CAM specialists in textile industries

## Assessment at Key Stage 3

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Effective formative assessment in Textiles should:
- Use practical demonstrations to assess skill development
- Employ photographic evidence of process and progress
- Include peer assessment of samples and products
- Provide immediate feedback during practical sessions
- Use design portfolios/sketchbooks as evidence of design thinking

### Summative Assessment

End-of-project assessment should evaluate:

**Design capability (typically 40%):**
- Quality of research and analysis
- Development of ideas through sampling
- Communication of design intentions
- Consideration of user needs and context

**Making capability (typically 40%):**
- Technical skills and accuracy
- Quality of finish
- Appropriate tool and equipment use
- Independence and problem-solving during making

**Evaluation (typically 20%):**
- Analysis of product against criteria
- Understanding of improvements
- Reflection on process and learning

## Planning a Key Stage 3 Textiles Programme

### Three-Year Scheme Overview

**Year 7: Foundations**
Typical projects:
- Cushion or bag project (introducing machine sewing, seams, hems)
- Textile art piece (exploring surface decoration techniques)
- Simple garment (e.g., pyjama bottoms, apron) introducing pattern use

**Year 8: Development**
Typical projects:
- Fashion accessory with surface decoration (combining techniques)
- Product responding to specific design brief (user-centred design)
- Introduction to smart/technical textiles (conductive thread projects)

**Year 9: Sophistication**
Typical projects:
- Extended project with client/user focus
- Portfolio development preparing for GCSE
- Sustainable textiles investigation and product
- Integration of CAD/CAM technologies

### Cross-Curricular Links

Effective Textiles teaching connects to:

**Art and Design:**
- Colour theory and application
- Pattern and composition
- Artists working in textiles (Kettle, Holmes, Grayson Perry)
- Cultural and historical art movements

**Mathematics:**
- Measuring accurately
- Scale and proportion
- Calculating fabric quantities
- Pattern grading and sizing
- Tessellation in surface pattern

**Science:**
- Material properties and testing
- Fibre science and chemistry
- Dyes and colour fastness
- Sustainable materials and biodegradability

**Geography:**
- Global textile industry and trade
- Ethical production and fair trade
- Environmental impact of fast fashion

**History:**
- Industrial Revolution and textile manufacturing
- Social history through clothing and textiles

## Practical Workshop Management

### Health and Safety

Essential safety considerations for textiles rooms:
- Machine guards in place and functioning
- Safe positioning of electrical cables
- Hot equipment (irons) on stable surfaces with appropriate warning
- Cutting safety (always cutting away from body, storing sharp tools safely)
- Chemical safety for dyes and printing inks (COSHH assessments, ventilation)
- Fire safety (safe storage of fabrics, clear emergency exits)

Risk assessments should be regularly reviewed and age-appropriate safety briefings given before each new technique.

### Resource Management

Effective resource organisation includes:
- Clear labelling and storage of fabrics by type and colour
- Accessible thread storage (organised by colour)
- Equipment maintenance schedules (regular machine servicing)
- Sample collections for teaching fabric properties
- Component storage (buttons, zips, fastenings) in accessible systems
- Digital resource libraries of exemplar work and technical guides

## Differentiation and Inclusion

### Supporting All Learners

Strategies for inclusive Textiles teaching:

**For students requiring additional support:**
- Pre-cut pattern pieces or simplified patterns
- Visual step-by-step guides with photographs
- Working models/samples to follow
- Structured worksheets breaking down processes
- Additional adult support during complex construction

**For more able students:**
- Extension tasks requiring independent problem-solving
- Introduction to more complex techniques
- Opportunities to combine multiple processes
- Research into professional practice and industry standards
- Mentoring of less experienced students

**For SEND students:**
- Adaptive equipment (ergonomic scissors, needle threaders)
- Tactile learning opportunities emphasising sensory experiences
- Clear, uncluttered workspaces
- Consistent routines and procedures
- Accessible vocabulary and terminology lists

## Transition to Key Stage 4

### Preparing for GCSE

Students completing KS3 Textiles should have developed:
- Confidence across a range of hand and machine techniques
- Understanding of design process from research through to evaluation
- Technical knowledge of fibres, fabrics and their properties
- Experience of independent project work
- Portfolio skills and design communication abilities
- Awareness of contemporary textile practice and industry

### GCSE Pathways

Students may progress to:

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552):**
- Textiles as one material/technical area within broad D&T GCSE
- Non-Exam Assessment (NEA): 50% (contextual challenges)
- Written examination: 50% (core and specialist technical principles)

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310):**
- Iterative Design Challenge: 50%
- Principles of Design and Technology exam: 50%
- Textiles as specialist material area

**Eduqas/WJEC GCSE Design and Technology (C560):**
- Design and Make Tasks: 50%
- Written examination: 50%
- Textiles-focused pathway available

**AQA GCSE Art and Design: Textile Design (8204):**
- Portfolio: 60%
- Externally Set Assignment: 40%
- Fine art approach to textiles

## Current Challenges and Developments

### Sustainability and Ethical Production

Contemporary Textiles education must address:
- Fast fashion impacts and alternatives
- Circular economy principles in textiles
- Upcycling and zero-waste pattern cutting
- Organic and sustainable fibre sources
- Water usage and pollution in textile production
- Fair trade and ethical labour practices

Cas Holmes' work with found materials and sustainable stitch provides excellent exemplars for discussing these issues with KS3 students.

### Digital Technologies

Integration of:
- CAD software for pattern design and surface pattern
- Digital printing on fabric
- Laser cutting for precision textile cutting
- Computerised embroidery machines
- 3D body scanning for bespoke garment production
- Virtual sampling reducing physical waste

### Inclusive Representation

Ensuring curriculum content represents:
- Diverse cultural textile traditions
- Range of body types and adaptive clothing
- Male and female textile practitioners and designers
- Global perspectives on textile production and consumption
- Contemporary debates around cultural appropriation in textile design

## Professional Development for Teachers

### Subject Knowledge Enhancement

Teachers should engage with:
- Professional organisations (DATA - Design and Technology Association)
- Contemporary textile exhibitions and galleries
- Industry visits to textile manufacturers and designers
- Continuing professional development courses in new techniques
- Research publications from Atkinson, Stables and others in D&T education
- Networks of textile teachers sharing practice

### Keeping Current

Maintaining relevance through:
- Following contemporary textile artists and designers (Kettle, Holmes, others)
- Monitoring sustainable textile innovations
- Understanding fashion and interior trends
- Awareness of technological developments in smart textiles
- Engagement with examination board updates and requirements

## Conclusion

Key Stage 3 Textiles education provides essential foundation for both academic progression and life skills. When taught effectively, drawing on the pedagogical insights of researchers like Atkinson and Stables, and illustrated through contemporary practitioners like Kettle and Holmes, Textiles enables students to develop creativity, technical capability, and critical understanding of the material world. The subject's unique combination of cultural awareness, technical skill, and design thinking prepares students for informed citizenship in relation to consumption, sustainability, and creative expression.

Effective KS3 Textiles teaching balances skill development with conceptual understanding, practical making with critical evaluation, and individual creativity with collaborative learning, establishing foundations that serve students whether they progress to GCSE qualifications or simply become more informed, capable individuals.
