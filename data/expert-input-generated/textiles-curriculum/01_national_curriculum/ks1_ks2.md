# Textiles at Key Stages 1 and 2

## Introduction and National Curriculum Framework

Textiles education at Key Stages 1 and 2 forms a foundational component of Design and Technology (D&T) within the National Curriculum for England. The 2014 National Curriculum for Design and Technology explicitly requires pupils to work with a range of materials, including textiles, to develop their creative, technical, and practical expertise. At primary level, textiles work enables children to explore material properties, develop making skills, and engage with the design process in tangible, accessible ways.

Professor Stephanie Atkinson, a leading authority on primary D&T education, has emphasised that early textiles experiences are crucial for developing children's understanding of "smart" and modern materials, whilst also connecting pupils to cultural heritage and traditional craft practices. Her research at Sunderland University has highlighted how textiles uniquely combines technical precision with creative expression, making it an ideal medium for primary-aged learners.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Years 1-2)

The National Curriculum states that pupils should be taught to:

- Design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users based on design criteria
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through talking, drawing, templates, mock-ups and, where appropriate, information and communication technology
- Select from and use a range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks (such as cutting, shaping, joining and finishing)
- Select from and use a wide range of materials and components, including textiles, according to their characteristics
- Explore and evaluate a range of existing products
- Evaluate their ideas and products against design criteria

In textiles specifically, Key Stage 1 pupils should develop fundamental skills including basic joining techniques (gluing, stapling, simple stitching), understanding of fabric properties, and the ability to create simple textile products such as puppets, bookmarks, or decorated fabric items.

### Key Stage 2 (Years 3-6)

The National Curriculum builds upon Key Stage 1 foundations, requiring pupils to:

- Use research and develop design criteria to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products that are fit for purpose
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through discussion, annotated sketches, cross-sectional and exploded diagrams, prototypes, pattern pieces and computer-aided design
- Select from and use a wider range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks accurately
- Select from and use a wider range of materials and components, including textiles, according to their functional properties and aesthetic qualities
- Investigate and analyse a range of existing products
- Evaluate their ideas and products against their own design criteria and consider the views of others
- Understand how key events and individuals in design and technology have helped shape the world

Technical knowledge requirements include understanding how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures, and applying their understanding of materials to textiles projects.

## Progression in Textiles Skills

### Foundation Skills (Reception/Year 1)

Kay Stables, Professor of Design and Technology Education at Goldsmiths, University of London, has emphasised the importance of systematic skill progression in D&T education. For textiles, foundational skills include:

- **Material exploration**: Handling different fabrics, describing texture, appearance, and basic properties (soft, rough, shiny, stretchy)
- **Basic manipulation**: Tearing, cutting with scissors, folding, rolling fabric
- **Simple decoration**: Using fabric crayons, printing with objects, sticking on embellishments
- **Introduction to joining**: Using PVA glue, staplers, masking tape to join fabric pieces
- **Simple templates**: Using adult-made templates for cutting shapes

### Developing Skills (Years 2-4)

- **Running stitch**: The fundamental hand-sewing technique, typically introduced in Year 2
- **Overstitch**: For joining fabric edges and attaching appliqué
- **Cross stitch**: Often introduced in Years 3-4 using Binca or Aida fabric
- **Threading needles**: Developing fine motor control and independence
- **Measuring and marking**: Using rulers and fabric markers to create pattern pieces
- **Strengthening techniques**: Using interfacing, creating hems, understanding seam allowances
- **Fastenings**: Sewing on buttons, using hook-and-loop fasteners, poppers
- **Pinning**: Learning to use pins safely to hold fabric before sewing
- **Working from patterns**: Following simple instructions and templates

### Advanced Primary Skills (Years 5-6)

- **Backstitch**: A stronger stitch for more secure seams
- **Blanket stitch**: For decorative edges and appliqué work
- **Chain stitch**: Introducing decorative embroidery techniques
- **Introduction to machine sewing**: Where resources permit, basic straight-line machine stitching under supervision
- **Pattern adaptation**: Modifying existing templates to create variations
- **Quality finishing**: Understanding how to finish work to a high standard, including neat seam finishing
- **Fabric manipulation**: Gathering, pleating, basic smocking techniques
- **Resist dyeing**: Tie-dye, batik, or simple dye techniques
- **Weaving**: Using simple looms or card weaving frames
- **Textile art techniques**: Inspired by practitioners like Alice Kettle and Cas Holmes

## Design Process in Primary Textiles

### Investigating and Evaluating Existing Products (IEP)

Stephanie Atkinson's research has demonstrated that effective D&T teaching begins with pupils investigating existing products to understand design decisions and construction methods. For textiles projects, this might include:

- Examining bags, cushions, or clothing items to identify construction techniques
- Discussing how products are fit for purpose (e.g., why PE bags have drawstrings, why aprons have pockets)
- Deconstructing fabric samples to understand layering and joining methods
- Analysing decoration techniques in textile products from different cultures
- Comparing handmade and manufactured textile items

### Focused Practical Tasks (FPT)

Before embarking on design-and-make assignments, pupils should develop specific skills through focused tasks:

- Practising stitches on sample squares
- Experimenting with different joining techniques on scrap fabric
- Testing fabric stiffening methods
- Creating sample books of decoration techniques
- Learning to thread needles and secure starting and finishing threads

### Design and Make Assignments (DMA)

The full design-make-evaluate cycle in textiles projects should include:

1. **Design brief and research**: Understanding user needs and context
2. **Design criteria**: Establishing clear, measurable success criteria
3. **Design ideas**: Generating multiple solutions through drawing and annotation
4. **Pattern/template making**: Creating paper patterns before cutting fabric
5. **Prototyping**: Making samples or mock-ups where appropriate
6. **Making**: Following a logical sequence of construction, with teacher support for new techniques
7. **Evaluation**: Assessing against original criteria, gathering user feedback, identifying improvements

## Suitable Projects by Key Stage

### Key Stage 1 Projects

- **Hand puppets**: Practising simple stitching and decoration, designing characters
- **Bookmarks**: Working on a small scale, adding tassels or decoration
- **Felt finger puppets**: Using pre-cut templates or creating simple designs
- **Fabric bunting**: Decorating triangles, understanding joining methods
- **Simple drawstring bags**: Introducing functional products
- **Decorated fabric frames**: For photographs or artwork
- **Christmas decorations**: Seasonal context for textile work

### Key Stage 2 Projects

- **Book bags/PE bags**: Incorporating drawstrings, considering size and strength
- **Purses or pencil cases**: Using fastenings, considering compartments
- **Cushions**: Understanding envelope closures, stuffing, decoration
- **Aprons**: Practical product requiring measuring, pattern cutting, ties
- **Stuffed toys**: More complex 3D construction requiring multiple pieces
- **Wall hangings/banners**: Opportunities for textile art techniques
- **Phone or tablet cases**: Modern context, requiring padding and fastenings
- **Bunting or flags**: Cultural/historical contexts (e.g., celebrations, significant events)
- **Simple clothing items**: Waistcoats, capes, or simple skirts (upper Key Stage 2)

## The Role of Textile Art and Contemporary Practice

Alice Kettle, Professor of Textile Art at Manchester Metropolitan University, has been instrumental in raising the profile of textiles as an art form. Her large-scale stitched installations demonstrate how textile techniques can create powerful, expressive artwork. In primary education, incorporating textile art approaches alongside functional products enriches children's understanding of textiles as a medium for creative expression.

Cas Holmes, a renowned textile artist and author of books including "The Found Object in Textile Art" and "Connected Cloth", advocates for exploratory, experimental approaches to textiles. Her work with collage, stitch, and found materials provides excellent inspiration for primary-aged pupils, encouraging them to see everyday materials as potential textile components.

### Integrating Art and Design Approaches

Primary textiles teaching should balance functional products with artistic exploration:

- **Stitch sampling**: Creating abstract compositions exploring stitch as line and mark-making
- **Fabric collage**: Inspired by Cas Holmes's layering techniques
- **Textile portraits or landscapes**: Using appliqué, embroidery, and mixed media
- **Responding to themes**: Environmental issues, identity, community, seasons
- **Cultural textiles**: Exploring traditions like Kantha stitching, African wax prints, Mexican embroidery
- **Story textiles**: Creating narrative wall hangings or story bags

- Designing products for story characters (e.g., bags for Little Red Riding Hood)
- Creating textile story maps or book covers
- Writing instructions for making processes
- Descriptive writing about textures and materials
- Poetry inspired by textile techniques or products

- Measuring fabric accurately using rulers and tape measures
- Understanding perimeter when calculating fabric needed
- Symmetry in pattern cutting and decoration design
- Repeating patterns in printing and weaving
- Scale (e.g., making templates twice the size)
- Problem-solving: calculating amounts of materials needed

- Historical clothing and textiles (Victorian Britain, Ancient Egypt, Tudor times)
- World cultures and traditional textile techniques
- The Industrial Revolution and textile manufacturing

## Health and Safety Considerations

The DATA (Design and Technology Association) provides comprehensive health and safety guidance for primary D&T, including textiles work:

### Essential Safety Measures

- **Needles**: Use blunt-ended tapestry needles for younger pupils; count needles before and after sessions; provide needle cases
- **Scissors**: Use appropriate safety scissors; teach correct handling and passing techniques
- **Pins**: Use with close supervision; establish clear rules for use; ensure all pins are accounted for
- **Irons**: Teacher use only; maintain safe distance from pupils; ensure cool-down time
- **Dyes and fabric paints**: Check COSHH (Control of Substances Hazardous to Health) regulations; ensure adequate ventilation; use non-toxic products suitable for educational use
- **Sewing machines**: Years 5-6 only with individual adult supervision; guard fingers from needle area; secure fabric appropriately

### Risk Assessment

Teachers must conduct risk assessments for all D&T activities, considering the specific pupils involved, the environment, and the techniques being taught. The DATA provides model risk assessments that can be adapted for school contexts.

## Assessment in Primary Textiles

### Design and Technology Association (DATA) Guidance

Assessment should focus on:

- **Design skills**: Generating ideas, communicating intentions, responding to design criteria
- **Technical skills**: Safe and accurate use of tools and techniques
- **Making skills**: Following procedures, working independently, achieving quality outcomes
- **Evaluative skills**: Testing and modifying, making judgements about products
- **Technical knowledge**: Understanding materials, construction methods, and functional properties

### Formative Assessment Strategies

- Photographic evidence of work in progress
- Annotated sketchbooks or design sheets
- Skill development records (e.g., stitch sample books)
- Verbal feedback during making activities
- Peer assessment against design criteria
- Self-evaluation reflections

### Summative Assessment

- Finished products assessed against design criteria
- Design folios showing the complete design process
- Written or verbal evaluations
- Teacher assessment using school-specific or DATA frameworks

### Essential Resources for Primary Textiles

- **Basic tools**: Tapestry needles (various sizes), embroidery needles, safety scissors, fabric scissors, pins, tape measures, rulers, fabric markers/chalk
- **Fabrics**: Cotton, felt, calico, Binca/Aida, hessian, interfacing, wadding
- **Threads**: Embroidery thread (various colours), sewing thread, wool
- **Fastenings**: Buttons, hook-and-loop fasteners (Velcro), poppers, zips
- **Decoration materials**: Fabric crayons, fabric paints, sequins, beads, ribbons, bias binding
- **Equipment**: Embroidery hoops, simple card or wooden looms, fabric printing tools

### Professional Organisations

- **The Design and Technology Association (DATA)**: Provides subject-specific guidance, training, and resources; publishes "Primary D&T" magazine
- **National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD)**: Offers resources connecting textile art to primary education
- **Crafts Council**: Provides educational resources about craft practices including textiles
- **62 Group of Textile Artists**: Showcase contemporary textile art, many members engage in education work

### Continuing Professional Development

Many primary teachers lack confidence in teaching D&T, particularly textiles. Kay Stables's research has repeatedly highlighted this issue. Effective professional development should include:

- Hands-on workshops developing teachers' own textile skills
- Subject knowledge enhancement about materials and techniques
- Pedagogical approaches specific to D&T teaching
- School-based mentoring from D&T subject leaders
- Access to online resources and video tutorials
- Visits to textile studios or exhibitions

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge: Limited Time Allocation

**Solution**: Integrate textiles with other subjects where appropriate; use teaching assistants for preparation work; establish systems for efficient resource distribution and classroom organisation; plan focused practical tasks that develop skills without requiring full design-make-evaluate cycles every time.

### Challenge: Teacher Confidence and Skill

**Solution**: Start with simple projects and develop alongside pupils; team-teach with more confident colleagues; access online tutorials before teaching new techniques; invite parent volunteers with textile skills; utilise DATA resources and support.

### Challenge: Budget Constraints

**Solution**: Build textile resources gradually; seek donations of fabric remnants from local businesses or parents; use recycled materials where appropriate; prioritise versatile, durable resources; apply for grants from parent-teacher associations or local community funds.

### Challenge: Managing Mixed-Ability Classes

**Solution**: Differentiate through outcome rather than task where possible; provide additional adult support for pupils who need it; offer extension challenges for more capable pupils; create "expert" pupils who can support peers; ensure design briefs allow for personal interpretation.

### Challenge: Storage and Organisation

**Solution**: Establish clear systems for storing work in progress; use ziplock bags labelled with pupil names; create portable textile kits that can be distributed quickly; designate specific storage areas for textiles resources; involve pupils in resource management.

## Inclusion and Differentiation

### Supporting SEND Pupils

Textiles can be highly accessible for pupils with special educational needs and disabilities:

- **Physical disabilities**: Larger needles, textured fabrics for tactile feedback, frames or hoops to hold work steady, adapted scissors
- **Visual impairment**: High-contrast fabrics, textured materials, verbal descriptions of techniques
- **Fine motor difficulties**: Thicker threads, pre-punched holes in card or thick felt, alternative fastening methods
- **Autism spectrum**: Clear, structured instructions; visual sequences; predictable routines; sensory considerations for fabric choices
- **Working memory challenges**: Step-by-step visual guides; breaking tasks into smaller components; adult support for sequencing

### Challenging More Able Pupils

- Introduce more complex stitches or techniques
- Encourage independent pattern development and adaptation
- Set challenges involving mathematical calculation or problem-solving
- Explore textile art responses to themes
- Research historical or cultural textile traditions
- Develop multi-component projects requiring planning and time management
- Introduce basic machine sewing where appropriate
