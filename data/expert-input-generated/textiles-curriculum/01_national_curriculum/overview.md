# Textiles in the National Curriculum

## Overview and Statutory Position

Textiles education in England operates within the Design and Technology (D&T) National Curriculum framework, which became statutory in September 2014. The current curriculum positions textiles as one of several material areas through which pupils develop design and technology capability, alongside resistant materials, food, and systems and control. The programme of study mandates that all pupils receive a broad and balanced D&T education, though textiles is not explicitly named as a separate subject after Key Stage 3, creating both opportunities and challenges for specialist provision.

The Department for Education's (DfE) National Curriculum documentation specifies that D&T should enable pupils to "use creativity and imagination to design and make products that solve real and relevant problems within a variety of contexts." Textiles provides an exceptionally rich medium for achieving these aims, offering unique opportunities for creative expression, technical skill development, and cultural understanding.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Curriculum Requirements

At Key Stage 1, the National Curriculum requires pupils to design purposeful, functional, appealing products based on design criteria. Within textiles specifically, children should explore and use mechanisms including templates and joining techniques. The curriculum states pupils should "select from and use a range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks such as cutting, shaping, joining and finishing."

### Practical Implementation

At this foundational stage, textiles work typically involves:

- **Simple joining techniques**: Threading needles, running stitch, over-sewing, and gluing fabrics
- **Fabric manipulation**: Weaving with card looms and paper, basic plaiting, and simple tie-dyeing
- **Template use**: Cutting fabric using pre-made templates for simple products like bookmarks, puppets, or bags
- **Material exploration**: Investigating fabric properties including texture, colour, and basic structure

Research by Stephanie Atkinson (2002) emphasises the importance of developing making skills alongside designing capabilities from the earliest stages. Her work on primary D&T capability argues that young children benefit from "iterative cycles of making, evaluating, and improving," rather than linear design-then-make approaches. This has significant implications for textiles teaching, suggesting that KS1 pupils should engage in exploratory fabric manipulation before formal designing.

Kay Stables' research on designerly thinking in schools (1997, 2008) demonstrates that even young children can engage with authentic design problems when the context is meaningful to them. Textiles projects at KS1 might include designing a fabric bag for a specific purpose (carrying PE kit, organising toys) or creating textile artwork inspired by familiar stories.

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Curriculum Requirements

The Key Stage 2 programme of study builds on KS1 foundations, requiring pupils to "select from and use a wider range of materials and components, including textiles, according to their functional properties and aesthetic qualities." The curriculum emphasises:

- Generating, developing, modelling and communicating ideas through discussion, annotated sketches, cross-sectional and exploded diagrams, prototypes, pattern pieces and computer-aided design
- Selecting from and using a wider range of tools and equipment
- Applying finishing techniques including those from art and design

### Textile-Specific Progression

At Key Stage 2, textiles work expands significantly to include:

- **Construction techniques**: Hand sewing with increasing accuracy (back stitch, blanket stitch), introduction to machine sewing, understanding seam allowances
- **Surface decoration**: Appliqué, embroidery (including chain stitch and cross stitch), fabric printing (block printing, screen printing basics), batik
- **Pattern and structure**: Working from simple patterns, creating templates, understanding pattern pieces, basic 2D to 3D translation
- **Technical knowledge**: Understanding warp and weft, learning about different fabric types (woven, knitted, non-woven), exploring natural and synthetic fibres

Atkinson's research on capability in primary D&T (2000, 2006) highlights the importance of progressive skill development. Her studies demonstrate that when children receive structured teaching of textile techniques within meaningful design contexts, they develop both technical competence and design confidence. This challenges historical approaches where textiles was sometimes treated as purely decorative "craft" rather than rigorous D&T.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Primary teachers often lack specialist textiles knowledge, making subject leadership crucial. The DATA (Design and Technology Association, now merged into the Design and Technology Association) published extensive guidance on progression in textiles for primary settings. Effective KS2 textiles teaching requires:

- **Safe working practices**: Teaching proper use of needles, pins, scissors, and sewing machines with appropriate risk assessment
- **Skill progression planning**: Ensuring stitching and construction techniques build systematically across Years 3-6
- **Cross-curricular links**: Connecting textiles with history (historical garments, cultural textiles), geography (textile traditions globally), and art (artists working with textiles including Alice Kettle and Cas Holmes)

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### Curriculum Requirements

The Key Stage 3 National Curriculum represents the final statutory entitlement to D&T education for all pupils. The programme of study states that pupils should "develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world."

Within D&T at KS3, pupils must:

- Identify and solve their own design problems
- Apply iterative design processes
- Develop and communicate design ideas using annotated sketches, detailed plans, 3-D and mathematical modelling, oral and digital presentations and computer-based tools
- Select from and use specialist tools, techniques, processes, equipment and machinery precisely
- Analyse the work of past and present professionals and others to develop and broaden their understanding

### Textiles Provision at KS3

The 2014 National Curriculum's material-neutral language has created variability in textiles provision across schools. Some schools offer dedicated textiles modules or rotations; others integrate textiles within broader D&T rotations; some provide minimal or no textiles experience at KS3.

Research evidence suggests this variability impacts GCSE uptake and achievement. Stephanie Atkinson's work on progression in D&T (2013) emphasises that sustained engagement with materials and processes is essential for developing capability. When pupils receive only limited exposure to textiles at KS3, they lack the foundational skills and knowledge required for GCSE success.

### Textile Knowledge and Skills at KS3

A comprehensive KS3 textiles curriculum should develop:

**Technical Knowledge:**
- Fibre types and properties (natural: cotton, wool, silk, linen; synthetic: polyester, nylon, acrylic; regenerated: viscose)
- Fabric construction methods (weaving, knitting, felting, bonding)
- Properties and characteristics affecting fabric choice (absorbency, drape, durability, elasticity, texture)
- Components and fastenings (buttons, zips, poppers, Velcro, hooks and eyes)
- Understanding of industrial and commercial textiles practices

**Making Skills:**
- Advanced hand sewing techniques (French seams, herringbone stitch, hemming)
- Confident use of sewing machines (straight stitch, zigzag, adjusting tension)
- Pattern reading and adaptation
- Cutting accurately with seam allowances
- Pressing and finishing to professional standards
- Use of specialist equipment (overlockers, embroidery machines where available)

**Surface Design and Decoration:**
- Resist dyeing techniques (tie-dye, batik, flour paste resist)
- Printing methods (screen printing, block printing, mono-printing)
- Embellishment (machine embroidery, hand embroidery, beading, appliqué, reverse appliqué)
- Digital textile design (CAD software, digital printing possibilities)

### Design Process in Textiles

Kay Stables' influential research on designerly behaviour (1997, 2008, 2015) provides theoretical foundations for textiles design education. Her concept of "enabling strategies" – the cognitive tools designers use to navigate design problems – has particular relevance for textiles, where material properties significantly influence design decisions.

Stables argues that effective design education requires pupils to:

- **Engage with authentic design contexts**: Rather than teacher-prescribed outcomes, pupils should identify user needs and design opportunities
- **Work iteratively**: Testing ideas through sampling, prototyping, and refinement
- **Think through making**: Using material exploration as a form of design thinking
- **Evaluate critically**: Developing judgement about aesthetic and functional qualities

In textiles specifically, this manifests through:

- Creating sample boards exploring different techniques, colour combinations, and fabric combinations
- Toile-making (creating test versions in inexpensive fabric before final construction)
- Iterative refinement of surface pattern designs
- Critical analysis of existing textile products and designer work

### Contemporary Textile Artists and Designers

The National Curriculum requires analysis of "past and present professionals." Incorporating contemporary textile artists enriches pupils' understanding of textiles' expressive possibilities beyond functional products.

**Alice Kettle** is a renowned British textile artist whose work demonstrates embroidery's artistic potential. Her large-scale narrative embroideries, combining free machine stitching with hand embroidery, challenge perceptions of textiles as purely craft-based. Kettle's practice exemplifies how traditional textile techniques can express contemporary themes. Her work is held in public collections including the Victoria and Albert Museum. Teachers can use Kettle's work to demonstrate how textiles communicate meaning, emotion, and narrative, particularly when exploring embroidery and mixed-media textiles.

**Cas Holmes** is an established British textile artist specialising in stitch and mixed-media collage. Her approach combines found objects, vintage textiles, paper, and natural materials with hand stitch to create layered, evocative pieces. Holmes' work is particularly valuable in education because she emphasises process over product, encouraging experimentation and personal response to materials. Her publications, including "The Found Object in Textile Art" (2010) and "Connected Cloth" (2013), provide accessible resources for teachers. Holmes' approach aligns with Kay Stables' concept of "thinking through making," demonstrating how design ideas develop through material exploration.

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16): GCSE

### Qualification Structure

While textiles is not specified separately in the National Curriculum beyond KS3, it continues through GCSE qualifications offered by UK exam boards. GCSE options vary by board:

**AQA** offers:
- **GCSE Design and Technology (8552)**: Students can specialise in one material area, including "Textiles-based materials." The specification requires understanding of textile fibres, fabrics, construction, and finishing techniques alongside broader D&T content including new and emerging technologies, energy systems, and modern materials. Assessment comprises Non-Exam Assessment (NEA, 50%) and written examination (50%).
- **GCSE Art and Design: Textile Design (8204)**: This provides an art-focused route emphasising creative and personal response through textile media. Assessment is portfolio-based (60%) with externally set assignment (40%).

**Edexcel (Pearson)** provides:
- **GCSE Design and Technology (1DT0)**: Material area specialisms including textiles. Similar structure to AQA with 50% NEA and 50% examination. The specification emphasises technical understanding and commercial awareness.

**OCR** offers:
- **GCSE Design and Technology (J310)**: Includes textiles within iterative design challenge assessment. The specification emphasises user-centred design and iterative development.

**WJEC (Wales)** provides:
- **GCSE Design and Technology (C560)**: Used in Wales and some English schools, includes textile technology as a specialised area.

### Knowledge Requirements at GCSE

All GCSE D&T specifications require substantial technical knowledge about materials and processes. For textiles, this includes:

**Materials and their working properties:**
- Classification of natural, synthetic, and blended fibres
- Physical and mechanical properties affecting performance
- Environmental considerations in textile production
- Smart and modern textiles (thermochromic fabrics, microencapsulation, conductive threads, moisture-wicking fabrics)

**Manufacturing processes:**
- Industrial textile production (spinning, weaving, knitting on industrial scale)
- Commercial pattern cutting and lay planning
- Quality control in textile manufacturing
- One-off, batch, and mass production implications for textiles

**Surface treatments and finishes:**
- Commercial printing processes (rotary screen printing, heat transfer, digital printing)
- Finishing treatments (Scotchgard, flame retardant treatments, mercerisation)
- Aesthetic and functional embellishment techniques at various scales

### Design and Make Activities

The Non-Exam Assessment (NEA) requires students to:

1. Investigate and analyse an iterative design challenge
2. Produce a design brief and specification
3. Generate and develop design ideas
4. Realise design ideas through high-quality prototype/product
5. Analyse and evaluate design decisions and outcomes

In textiles, successful NEA projects demonstrate:

- **User-centred focus**: Clear identification of user needs through research and analysis
- **Iterative development**: Evidence of sampling, testing, and refinement
- **Technical competence**: High-quality making demonstrating appropriate techniques
- **Critical evaluation**: Thoughtful analysis of design decisions, materials choices, and outcomes

Stephanie Atkinson's research on assessment in D&T (2017) highlights the importance of formative assessment throughout the design and make process. Her work suggests that overemphasis on portfolio documentation can detract from the quality of made outcomes. For textiles specifically, this means teachers should balance recording requirements with ensuring students have sufficient time to develop making skills and produce refined textile products.

## Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18): A-Level

### A-Level Textiles Provision

At A-Level, textiles typically appears within Art and Design qualifications rather than Design and Technology routes, though practices vary.

**AQA A-Level Art and Design: Textile Design (7204)** is the most widely offered qualification. The specification requires:

- Personal investigation (60%): Sustained project evidencing journey from initial engagement to realised outcome(s)
- Externally set assignment (40%): Response to starting point set by AQA

Assessment focuses on:

- AO1: Developing ideas through contextual investigations
- AO2: Experimenting with resources, media, materials, techniques and processes
- AO3: Recording ideas, observations and insights
- AO4: Presenting personal and meaningful response through sustained work

**OCR A-Level Art and Design** includes textile options within its broader art framework.

### Distinction from GCSE D&T Approach

A-Level Textile Design through Art specifications emphasises personal creative development, experimentation, and individual artistic voice. This differs substantially from GCSE D&T's focus on user-centred design, technical knowledge, and fitness-for-purpose. Students transitioning from GCSE D&T Textiles to A-Level Art and Design Textiles require support navigating this philosophical shift.

### Contemporary Practice at A-Level

At A-Level, engagement with contemporary practice deepens. Alice Kettle and Cas Holmes remain relevant, but students should encounter broader international contemporary textile practice including:

- Conceptual textile art challenging traditional boundaries
- Socially-engaged textile practice
- Installation and site-specific textile work
- Intersection of textiles with other disciplines (sculpture, performance, architecture)

## Cross-Curricular Connections

### Art and Design

The relationship between textiles as D&T and textiles within Art is complex and often contentious. Historically, textiles straddled both areas, leading to ongoing debates about disciplinary identity.

Kay Stables' work on D&T capability (2008) distinguishes between designing products to meet specific needs (D&T focus) and creating work expressing personal responses (Art focus). However, contemporary textile practice often blurs these boundaries. Artist-makers like Kettle and Holmes demonstrate that textiles can simultaneously serve functional purposes and express artistic vision.

Practical implications:

- Schools should clarify whether textiles provision is primarily D&T-focused, Art-focused, or genuinely interdisciplinary
- Teachers should understand their qualification requirements and assessment criteria
- Cross-departmental collaboration can enrich textile education but requires clear communication about different epistemological approaches

### History and Cultural Understanding

Textiles education provides exceptional opportunities for cultural and historical learning. The National Curriculum's broader aims include helping pupils "understand how Britain has influenced and been influenced by the wider world" – textiles offers rich material for this.

Topics might include:

- Historical textile traditions (British wool trade, cotton industry, Arts and Crafts movement)
- Global textile practices (Kente cloth, ikat, Japanese shibori, Indian block printing)
- Social history through textiles (suffragette banners, AIDS Memorial Quilt, Gee's Bend quilts)
- Contemporary ethical issues (fast fashion, labour conditions, sustainability)

### STEM Connections

Modern textiles education increasingly connects with STEM disciplines:

- **Science**: Fibre chemistry, dyeing chemistry, material properties testing
- **Technology**: Digital design tools, CAD/CAM for textiles, automated manufacturing
- **Engineering**: Structural textiles, technical textiles for specific applications (medical textiles, geotextiles, protective clothing)
- **Mathematics**: Pattern cutting geometry, tessellation in textile design, scaling and ratio in pattern grading
