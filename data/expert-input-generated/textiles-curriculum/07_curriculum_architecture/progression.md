# Progression in Textiles Skills

## Overview of Skills Progression in UK Textiles Education

Progression in textiles skills forms the backbone of effective curriculum design from Key Stage 1 through to A-Level. The National Curriculum for Design and Technology (2014) requires that pupils develop 'a broad range of subject knowledge' and 'technical skills' that build systematically across key stages. In textiles specifically, this involves developing both making competencies and critical understanding of materials, processes and cultural contexts.

Professor Stephanie Atkinson's research at Sunderland University emphasises that progression is not simply about doing more complex tasks, but about developing deeper conceptual understanding alongside technical capability. Her work on 'cognitive apprenticeship' in D&T demonstrates that skilled textile practitioners develop mental models of how materials behave and processes interact, not merely procedural knowledge.

## Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1: Building Textile Literacy

### Core Skills Development (Ages 4-7)

At Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1, textiles work should focus on developing tactile awareness and basic manipulation skills. The Early Years Foundation Stipulates that children should 'safely use and explore a variety of materials, tools and techniques'.

**Key progression markers include:**

- **Handling and sorting**: Understanding fabric properties through touching, sorting by texture, weight and appearance
- **Basic cutting skills**: Developing scissor control with fabric (initially felt and calico which don't fray)
- **Simple joining**: Threading large needles, using running stitch, basic fabric glue application
- **Simple decoration**: Adding buttons, sequins and pre-cut shapes using glue

Alice Kettle, Professor of Textile Art at Manchester School of Art, advocates for 'playful experimentation' at this stage rather than product-focused outcomes. Her research emphasises that young children should explore mark-making on fabric using various media before formal stitch teaching begins.

### Teaching Implications

Teachers should provide regular opportunities for sensory exploration. Create 'fabric libraries' with swatches organised by properties. Use vocabulary development cards showing correct terminology: warp, weft, weave, felt, knit. Documentation through photographs builds pupils' awareness of their own progression.

## Key Stage 2: Consolidating Core Techniques

### Technical Skills Progression (Ages 7-11)

The National Curriculum requires KS2 pupils to 'apply a range of finishing techniques, including those from art and design' and to 'understand and use mechanical systems in their products'. For textiles, this translates into systematic skill development across three domains:

**Making Skills:**

- **Measuring and marking**: Using templates, pattern pieces, measuring accurately with rulers on fabric
- **Cutting**: Developing accuracy with fabric shears, understanding grain direction
- **Joining techniques**: Running stitch, back stitch, blanket stitch, oversewing, cross stitch
- **Construction**: Simple seam allowances (1-1.5cm typically at KS2), turning through, pressing
- **Fastening**: Sewing on buttons with appropriate thread, basic hook and eye application

**Decorative Skills:**

- **Appliqué**: Hand and bonded methods using Bondaweb or similar
- **Embroidery**: Building stitch vocabulary (chain stitch, satin stitch, French knots)
- **Printing**: Block printing, stencilling, potato printing progressing to lino printing
- **Dyeing**: Tie-dye, batik (cold water dyes in school context)
- **Embellishment**: Sequins, beads, buttons creating patterns and textures

**Pattern and Structure:**

- **Understanding repeat**: Creating simple repeating patterns for printing
- **Simple pattern adaptation**: Enlarging or reducing basic templates
- **3D form**: Creating simple stuffed items, understanding how 2D fabric becomes 3D objects

Kay Stables' influential work on 'designerly thinking' emphasises that progression includes developing the ability to evaluate work against criteria. By upper KS2, pupils should articulate why they selected particular stitches or fabrics for specific purposes, demonstrating the interplay between technical skill and design thinking.

### Assessment of Progression

At KS2, progression can be evidenced through:

- Increased stitch accuracy and consistency
- Growing vocabulary of techniques used appropriately
- Ability to unpick and correct errors independently
- Understanding of quality markers (straight seams, secure starts/finishes, appropriate tension)

## Key Stage 3: Expanding Technical Repertoire

### Systematic Skill Development (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum states that KS3 pupils should 'develop the technical knowledge needed to perform everyday tasks confidently' including 'a range of techniques to shape, fabricate and construct'. In textiles, this represents significant progression in both hand and machine skills.

**Machine Skills Introduction:**

Most schools introduce domestic sewing machines during Year 7 or 8. Progression should be scaffolded:

1. **Machine familiarisation**: Threading, bobbin winding, straight stitch practice on paper then fabric
2. **Basic machine sewing**: Straight seams on scrap fabric, understanding stitch length and tension
3. **Seam types**: Plain seam, French seam, flat-fell seam (understanding appropriate applications)
4. **Machine decorative techniques**: Zigzag stitch for appliqué and neatening, free-motion embroidery introduction

**Advanced Hand Skills:**

- **Complex stitches**: Herringbone, feather stitch, Cretan stitch, lazy daisy
- **Structural stitches**: Buttonhole stitch, slip stitch (for invisible hems and turning gaps)
- **Smocking and gathering**: Understanding fabric manipulation for decorative and functional purposes

**Pattern Development:**

Year 9 typically introduces pattern drafting basics:

- Reading commercial patterns (Simplicity, Burda, independent designers)
- Understanding pattern markings (grain lines, notches, darts, seam allowances)
- Basic pattern adaptation (lengthening/shortening, simple customisation)

Stephanie Atkinson's research on capability in D&T highlights that KS3 represents a critical transition where pupils move from 'recipe following' to 'adaptive expertise'. Teachers should design projects that require problem-solving within technical constraints rather than purely replicative tasks.

### Cultural and Historical Contextualisation

Progression also includes understanding textiles within cultural contexts. Cas Holmes, whose work explores 'stitch as drawing' and the narrative potential of textiles, emphasises that technical skills gain meaning through cultural understanding. KS3 schemes should integrate:

- Analysis of historical textile techniques (quilting traditions, embroidery heritage, industrial textile development)
- Contemporary textile art (examining artists like Freddie Robins, Michael Brennand-Wood)
- Global textile traditions (African wax prints, Japanese sashiko, Indian embroidery)

## Key Stage 4: Technical Refinement and Specialisation

### GCSE Requirements and Skill Expectations

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) assess progression in textiles through both portfolio development and practical outcomes. While specific requirements vary, common expectations include:

**AQA GCSE Art and Design (Textile Design) 8204:**

Requires demonstration of 'purposeful control' of techniques. Assessment Objective 2 specifically addresses 'refining work by exploring ideas, selecting and experimenting with appropriate media, materials, techniques and processes'. Progression evidenced through:

- Systematic experimentation and refinement of techniques
- Sophisticated use of mixed media on textiles
- Quality of final outcome demonstrating technical mastery

**Edexcel GCSE Design and Technology (Textiles) 1DT0:**

Emphasises 'demonstrating technical knowledge and skills' in both core and in-depth material areas. Progression markers include:

- Understanding of industrial/commercial textile processes
- CAD/CAM application (computerised embroidery, laser cutting)
- Quality of prototype development showing iterative refinement

**Technical Skill Expectations at GCSE:**

By end of Year 11, competent students demonstrate:

- **Precision machine work**: Even seam allowances (typically 1.5cm), professional finish quality, curved seam management
- **Complex construction**: Inserting zips (centred, lapped, concealed), adding linings, creating facings
- **Advanced manipulation**: Pleating, tucking, rouleau loops, piping, binding
- **Surface design mastery**: Controlled resist dyeing, screen printing, digital printing, heat transfer techniques
- **Mixed media integration**: Combining print, stitch, appliqué, found materials coherently

Kay Stables' work on authentic design activity emphasises that GCSE level progression involves pupils making 'informed decisions about technical solutions' rather than applying teacher-directed techniques. Coursework should demonstrate pupils selecting techniques purposefully based on design intent and user requirements.

### Assessment and Differentiation

Progression at KS4 becomes individualised. High attaining students might explore:

- Historical reconstruction techniques (blackwork, goldwork, canvas work)
- Digital textile design using industry-standard software (Adobe Photoshop, Procreate)
- Experimental techniques (soluble fabrics, laser cutting, heat manipulation of synthetics)

Teachers should use exemplar materials from exam boards to establish quality benchmarks. Edexcel and AQA provide annotated student work showing progression from basic to exceptional technical control.

## Key Stage 5: Advanced Technical Capability and Conceptual Integration

### A-Level Expectations

A-Level Textiles (available through Art & Design routes from AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) requires 'in-depth knowledge' and 'technical confidence'. Progression at this level involves:

**Autonomous Technical Development:**

Students should independently research and master new techniques relevant to their personal investigation. This might include:

- Specialist embroidery (stumpwork, whitework, goldwork)
- Felting (wet felting, needle felting, nuno felting)
- Complex weaving structures (tapestry, ikat, supplementary warp)
- Textile sculpture (wire armatures, form manipulation)
- Sustainable practices (natural dyeing, upcycling, zero-waste pattern cutting)

**Integration of Making and Thinking:**

Alice Kettle's research on contemporary textile practice emphasises that advanced practitioners don't separate 'thinking' and 'making'. A-Level students should demonstrate:

- Sampling as research methodology
- Technical choices informed by conceptual intent
- Reflective development showing how making generates ideas

**Professional Practice:**

Progression includes understanding professional contexts:

- Industry standard processes (digital printing, large-scale production)
- Exhibition and presentation standards
- Health and safety in professional textile studios (dye safety, ventilation, equipment maintenance)

### Specialist Pathways

Some centres offer BTEC qualifications in Fashion and Textiles (Pearson) or Art Practice (UAL). These emphasise vocational skill development with different progression markers:

- **BTEC Level 3 Fashion and Textiles**: Industry-focused skills including pattern grading, commercial sample development, portfolio presentation to industry standards
- **UAL Level 3 Extended Diploma**: Fine art/textile art focus with emphasis on experimental making and conceptual development

## Whole-School Progression Planning

### Mapping Skills Across Key Stages

Effective progression requires strategic curriculum mapping. The Design and Technology Association (DATA) provides frameworks for skills progression, but textiles departments should develop contextualised versions:

**Vertical Progression (across year groups):**

Create skills matrices showing when techniques are introduced, consolidated and mastered. For example:

- **Running stitch**: Introduced Y2, consolidated Y3-4, mastered (even tension, appropriate length) Y5
- **Screen printing**: Introduced Y8, consolidated Y9-10, mastered (registration, ink consistency) Y11
- **Pattern drafting**: Introduced Y9, consolidated Y10-11, mastered (complex adaptations) Y12-13

**Horizontal Progression (within projects):**

Projects should scaffold progression. Stephanie Atkinson's research demonstrates effective progression through 'focused practical tasks' followed by 'design and make assignments' where skills are applied in new contexts.

### Teacher Development Implications

Progression in pupils requires progression in teacher expertise. Professional development should include:

- **Technique workshops**: Regular updates on contemporary processes (e.g., digital embroidery software, laser cutting parameters for textiles)
- **Industry links**: Visits to textile manufacturers, designer-maker studios
- **Exhibitions**: Analysis of contemporary textile art (Ruthin Craft Centre, The Whitworth Manchester, Fashion and Textile Museum London)

The National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD) Textiles Forum provides subject-specific CPD aligned with progression requirements.

## Assessment of Progression in Textiles

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Progression requires visible assessment practices. Effective strategies include:

**Live demonstration assessment**: Teacher demonstrates technique, pupils attempt immediately, teacher circulates providing individual feedback on tension, hand position, accuracy.

**Peer assessment using quality exemplars**: Display work at different progression levels (basic competence, secure, advanced). Pupils identify features of quality and set targets.

**Photographic progression journals**: Pupils photograph work at key stages with annotations explaining technical decisions and improvements.

**Technique banks**: Individual or class records of techniques learned, with dates and confidence ratings, reviewed termly.

### Summative Assessment

End-of-key-stage expectations should reference both qualitative and quantitative markers:

**Quality indicators:**
- Neatness of seams (straight, even seam allowance)
- Security of stitching (appropriate starts and finishes)
- Suitability of technique selection for purpose
- Quality of finish (pressing, trimming, no visible marks or puckering)

**Breadth indicators:**
- Range of techniques attempted
- Combination of processes (e.g., print and stitch, construction and embellishment)
- Independence in problem-solving when techniques don't work as expected

## Barriers to Progression and Intervention Strategies

### Common Progression Obstacles

Research by Atkinson and Stables identifies typical barriers:

**Technical barriers:**
- Poor fine motor control affecting stitch quality
- Difficulty visualising 3D outcomes from 2D patterns
- Frustration with machine threading/tension leading to avoidance

**Cognitive barriers:**
- Limited material knowledge affecting decision-making
- Inability to evaluate quality objectively
- Difficulty transferring skills to new contexts

**Affective barriers:**
- Textiles perceived as 'feminine' discouraging some pupils
- Fear of machinery after initial difficulties
- Perfectionism preventing experimentation

### Differentiation Strategies

Progression isn't linear. Effective differentiation includes:

**Process differentiation**: 
- Providing pre-cut components for pupils with motor difficulties while others cut independently
- Offering 'technique cards' with photographs and instructions for pupils needing additional support
- Pre-threaded needles in early Key Stage 2

**Outcome differentiation**:
- Core outcome accessible to all (e.g., simple cushion cover)
- Extension opportunities (e.g., adding concealed zip, piping, envelope back)

**Support structures**:
- Peer mentoring with Year 10 supporting Year 7
- Textile technicians modelling techniques
- Video demonstrations available via VLE for home practice

Cas Holmes' inclusive practice emphasises that 'mistakes' are design opportunities. Creating studio culture where unpicking is normalised and experimentation valued enables progression for all pupils.

## Future Directions: Digital Technologies and Progression

### Integrating Digital Making

Contemporary progression includes digital literacy:

- **CAD software**: Adobe Illustrator for repeat pattern design (typically Y10+)
- **Digital embroidery**: Machines reading USB files (requires software literacy)
- **Laser cutting**: Understanding vector vs. raster for fabric cutting and engraving
- **3D printing**: Integration of hard components with textile elements

Schools increasingly access FabLab facilities. Progression involves understanding when digital fabrication enhances textile outcomes vs. traditional craft primacy.

### Sustainability Progression

Contemporary textile practice requires environmental awareness. Progression includes:

- KS2: Understanding natural vs. synthetic fibres
- KS3: Lifecycle analysis of textile products, introduction to upcycling
- KS4: Natural dyeing techniques, zero-waste pattern cutting
- KS5: Critical engagement with fast fashion, circular economy design principles

This reflects thinking from researchers like Kate Fletcher (Centre for Sustainable Fashion, UAL) whose work influences contemporary curriculum design.

## Conclusion

Progression in textiles skills requires careful curriculum architecture balancing technique acquisition, conceptual development and cultural understanding. From foundational sensory exploration in EYFS through to sophisticated technical mastery at A-Level, effective progression is visible, assessed and responsive to individual starting points. Research from Atkinson, Stables, Kettle and Holmes emphasises that technical skill divorced from thinking and meaning-making produces limited capability. The most effective textiles curricula develop both hand and mind, creating pupils who can think through materials and make their thinking visible through skilled textile practice.
