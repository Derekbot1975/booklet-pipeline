# Sequencing Textiles Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in Textiles

Sequencing in Textiles education refers to the carefully structured progression of knowledge, skills and conceptual understanding across Key Stages 1-5. This document provides authoritative guidance for curriculum leaders and teachers on how to build coherent, progressive learning pathways that enable students to develop sophisticated textile capabilities over time.

Professor Stephanie Atkinson's research into design and technology pedagogy emphasises that effective sequencing must balance the development of procedural knowledge (making skills) with propositional knowledge (understanding materials, processes and contexts). Kay Stables' work on designerly thinking further supports the principle that students need repeated, increasingly complex opportunities to engage with authentic textile problems across their educational journey.

## National Curriculum Framework

The **National Curriculum for Design and Technology (2014)** provides the statutory framework for Textiles teaching in maintained schools in England. Whilst 'Textiles' is not separately identified, it is explicitly included within the materials and technical knowledge statements at both Key Stage 2 and Key Stage 3.

At Key Stage 2, pupils should be taught to: "understand and use mechanical systems in their products [for example, gears, pulleys, cams, levers and linkages]" and "apply their understanding of how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures". This includes textile structures.

At Key Stage 3, the programme of study states pupils should "learn about...textiles and how to formulate specifications, work with technical expertise and evaluate outcomes" and "understand developments in design and technology, its impact on individuals, society and the environment, and the responsibilities of designers, engineers and technologists".

## Key Stage 1 and Early Years: Foundational Experiences

### Material Exploration and Basic Techniques

At Early Years and Key Stage 1 (ages 4-7), sequencing focuses on **sensory exploration** and **basic manipulation** of textile materials. Research by Alice Kettle on contemporary textile practice emphasises that even young children benefit from encountering diverse fabric qualities, textures and structures.

**Recommended progression:**
1. **Reception/Year 1**: Free exploration of fabrics (touching, sorting by texture, colour, weight); simple threading with large needles and binca; basic wrapping and tying
2. **Year 2**: Introduction to running stitch; simple fabric joining using staples or large stitches; fabric printing with found objects; basic weaving using card looms

**Key vocabulary development**: texture, fabric, thread, stitch, weave, pattern, soft, rough, smooth

## Key Stage 2: Building Technical Competence

### Years 3-6 (Ages 7-11)

Kay Stables' research into capability development in Design and Technology emphasises that Key Stage 2 is critical for establishing technical fluency that enables later design autonomy. Textiles sequencing must therefore prioritise systematic skill development alongside design application.

**Technical Skills Progression:**

**Year 3:**
- Running stitch refinement for functional joining
- Introduction to backstitch for strength
- Simple pattern making from templates
- Fabric selection for purpose (considering properties)
- Introduction to embroidery embellishment

**Year 4:**
- Combining stitches for different purposes
- Introduction to appliqué techniques
- Pattern adaptation (simple modifications)
- Fabric preparation (measuring, marking, cutting accurately)
- Introduction to fabric printing (block printing, simple screen printing)

**Year 5:**
- Introduction to blanket stitch for edge finishing
- Cross stitch and decorative techniques
- Understanding seam allowances
- Introduction to fastenings (buttons, hooks, Velcro)
- Exploration of fabric manipulation (gathering, pleating)

**Year 6:**
- Refinement of all previous stitches with emphasis on quality
- Introduction to simple pattern drafting
- Understanding of textile properties in design decisions
- Introduction to machine sewing (where available)
- Complex fabric decoration combining techniques

### Design Process Integration

Stephanie Atkinson's work on capability emphasises that technical skills must be situated within meaningful design contexts. Each year should include 2-3 substantial design-and-make projects where students apply developing skills to solve authentic problems:

- **Functional textile items**: bags, book covers, pencil cases, cushions
- **Worn textiles**: simple garments, hats, accessories
- **Decorative textiles**: wall hangings, samplers, decorative panels

## Key Stage 3: Deepening Understanding and Sophistication

### Years 7-9 (Ages 11-14)

The transition to Key Stage 3 should involve increased sophistication in **material understanding**, **technical precision** and **design thinking**. Cas Holmes' research into textile art processes and narrative emphasises the importance of students developing personal creative voices through textile media during this phase.

**Technical Skills Progression:**

**Year 7:**
- Machine sewing competence (straight seams, turning corners)
- Introduction to darts and shaping
- Understanding fabric grain and bias
- Textile science: fibre types, fabric construction basics
- Introduction to resist techniques (tie-dye, batik)
- Digital design exploration (CAD for textiles)

**Year 8:**
- Advanced machine techniques (zip insertion, hemming methods)
- Pattern drafting and modification
- Understanding of textile industries and manufacturing
- Surface design techniques (screen printing, discharge printing)
- Introduction to smart/technical textiles
- Historical and cultural textile contexts

**Year 9:**
- Complex construction techniques (linings, facings, interfacing)
- Fashion and textile design principles
- Textile testing and performance analysis
- Sustainable practices and circular economy concepts
- Portfolio development skills
- Contemporary practitioners study (linking to Alice Kettle, Cas Holmes and others)

### Conceptual Development

Stables' research into designerly capability indicates that Key Stage 3 must develop students' ability to **evaluate critically** and **iterate effectively**. Sequencing should include:

1. **Analysis before making**: studying existing products, understanding user needs, researching contexts
2. **Developmental making**: sampling, testing ideas, refining techniques
3. **Evaluative practice**: continuous assessment against criteria, modification based on testing
4. **Contextual awareness**: understanding textiles in cultural, historical and contemporary frameworks

## Key Stage 4: GCSE Specialisation

### Years 10-11 (Ages 14-16)

At GCSE, students following **Art and Design: Textile Design** specifications (offered by AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) engage with increasingly sophisticated textile practice. Sequencing at this level requires careful planning across the two-year course.

### AQA Art and Design: Textile Design (8204/8206)

AQA's specification requires students to develop skills in four areas: line, tone, colour, form and composition. For Textiles, sequencing typically follows:

**Year 10 (Component 1 - Portfolio, 60%):**
- **Autumn Term**: Structured skill-building projects revisiting and extending Key Stage 3 techniques; introduction to artist analysis methodologies
- **Spring Term**: First sustained project with personal investigation emphasis; developing sampling and testing protocols
- **Summer Term**: Second sustained project with increased independence; refined evaluation and annotation practices

**Year 11 (Component 2 - Externally Set Assignment, 40%):**
- **Autumn Term**: Completion of portfolio; refined technical capabilities
- **Spring Term**: Response to ESA stimulus; independent investigation and development
- **Exam period**: 10-hour sustained making period

### Edexcel/Pearson Art and Design: Textiles (1TE0)

Edexcel's structure similarly requires careful sequencing with emphasis on:
- Building technical fluency in chosen specialisms (fashion, constructed textiles, printed and dyed textiles, surface pattern)
- Developing visual language through continuous contextual study
- Refining personal creative intentions

### OCR Art and Design: Textile Design (J171/J176)

OCR specifications emphasise material exploration and experimentaton. Effective sequencing ensures:
- Progressive risk-taking in material combinations
- Systematic recording of processes
- Clear links between investigation, development and realisation

### WJEC Eduqas Art and Design: Textiles (C650)

WJEC specifications require 40% non-exam assessment and 60% externally set assessment. Sequencing must balance:
- Sustained project development with clear progression
- Rapid response capabilities for exam conditions

### Pedagogical Considerations for Key Stage 4

Atkinson's research emphasises that GCSE courses must maintain practical engagement whilst developing critical and contextual sophistication. Effective sequencing includes:

1. **Technical workshops**: Regular skill-building sessions addressing student-identified needs
2. **Artist-led investigations**: Study of practitioners from diverse backgrounds and approaches
3. **Material-led exploration**: Systematic sampling and testing to inform design decisions
4. **Reflective practice**: Continuous evaluation and modification
5. **Independent planning**: Gradual release of teacher control toward student autonomy

## Key Stage 5: A-Level and Level 3 Vocational

### Years 12-13 (Ages 16-18)

Advanced level Textiles education requires sophisticated sequencing that develops genuine expertise and personal creative voice. Alice Kettle's research into professional textile practice provides valuable insights into the authentic practices students should experience.

### A-Level Art and Design: Textiles

All major exam boards (AQA 7204/7206, Edexcel 9TE0, OCR H600/H606, WJEC Eduqas A650) structure A-Level as:
- **Component 1** (Personal Investigation, 60%): Extended project with 1000-3000 word related study
- **Component 2** (Externally Set Assignment, 40%): Response to stimulus with 15-hour exam

**Year 12 Sequencing:**
- **Autumn Term**: Re-establishing and extending technical repertoire; exploring specialist areas; developing critical frameworks
- **Spring Term**: Beginning personal investigation; identifying themes and directions; substantial contextual research
- **Summer Term**: Developing personal investigation; refined technical experimentation; establishing essay focus

**Year 13 Sequencing:**
- **Autumn Term**: Completing personal investigation work and essay; sophisticated resolution
- **Spring Term**: ESA response with high-level independence; refined realisation
- **Exam period**: 15-hour sustained making

### Level 3 Vocational (UAL, BTEC, CTEC)

Vocational qualifications like **UAL Level 3 Diploma in Fashion and Textiles** or **Pearson BTEC Level 3 National Extended Diploma in Art and Design (Textiles)** require different sequencing approaches:

- **Unit-based structure**: Each unit develops specific employability skills
- **Industry engagement**: Work placements, live briefs, professional collaborations
- **Portfolio development**: Emphasis on presentation for progression or employment
- **Technical specialism**: Deep skill development in chosen pathways (fashion design, textile crafts, surface pattern, costume, etc.)

### Advanced Level Pedagogical Principles

Stables' work on capability at advanced levels emphasises the development of **autonomy**, **criticality** and **technical authority**. Effective sequencing includes:

1. **Self-directed inquiry**: Students identify and pursue personal lines of investigation
2. **Sophisticated contextual understanding**: Critical engagement with historical, cultural and contemporary practice
3. **Material mastery**: Expert-level technical capability in chosen specialisms
4. **Professional practices**: Time management, project planning, presentation skills
5. **Reflective sophistication**: Articulate evaluation of process and outcomes

## Cross-Key Stage Progression: Core Principles

### Spiral Curriculum Model

Effective Textiles sequencing employs a **spiral curriculum** approach, where key concepts are revisited with increasing sophistication:

**Material Understanding** spirals from:
- KS1: Sensory properties → KS2: Functional properties → KS3: Scientific understanding of fibres and fabrics → KS4/5: Sophisticated material selection for aesthetic and conceptual purposes

**Design Process** spirals from:
- KS1: Simple choices → KS2: User-focused design → KS3: Iterative development → KS4/5: Complex investigation and realisation

**Technical Skills** spiral from:
- KS1: Basic manipulation → KS2: Controlled techniques → KS3: Complex processes → KS4/5: Expert execution and innovation

### Cognitive Load Considerations

Sequencing must manage **cognitive load** appropriately. Atkinson's research indicates that students struggle when technical demands overwhelm design thinking capacity. Effective sequencing:

1. **Automates technical skills** through practice before expecting sophisticated design application
2. **Scaffolds complexity** gradually increasing the number of variables students manage
3. **Provides frameworks** for decision-making until students develop independent strategies
4. **Balances familiarity and novelty** building confidence whilst maintaining challenge

### Cultural and Contextual Sequencing

Cas Holmes' work on narrative and cultural identity in textiles emphasises the importance of students encountering diverse textile traditions and contemporary practices throughout their education.

**Recommended progression:**
- **KS1-2**: Stories and functions of textiles in diverse cultures; celebrating cultural heritage
- **KS3**: Historical developments in textiles; understanding industry and society connections
- **KS4-5**: Critical engagement with contemporary practice; understanding textiles as cultural commentary and artistic expression

## Assessment and Progress Monitoring

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Effective sequencing requires continuous monitoring of student progress through:

1. **Skill audits**: Regular assessment against technical benchmarks
2. **Design portfolios**: Ongoing documentation of thinking and making
3. **Critique sessions**: Peer and teacher feedback on work-in-progress
4. **Reflective journals**: Student self-assessment and target-setting

### Summative Waypoints

Strategic summative assessment points help ensure sequencing is effective:

- **End of KS1**: Can use basic joining techniques; selects appropriate materials
- **End of KS2**: Competent in range of hand stitches; understands basic pattern use; makes functional textile items
- **End of KS3**: Confident with machine sewing; understands textile science; designs with user focus
- **End of KS4**: Sophisticated technical capability; personal creative voice; critical contextual understanding
- **End of KS5**: Expert-level specialism; autonomous practice; articulate critical perspective

## Practical Implementation Guidance

### Resource Sequencing

Material resources should be sequenced to match developing capabilities:

**KS1-2**: Natural and synthetic fabrics in various textures; large needles and threads; simple fastenings; accessible decoration materials

**KS3**: Wider fabric range including specialist materials; sewing machines; basic screen printing equipment; digital design tools

**KS4-5**: Professional-quality materials; advanced equipment (industrial machines, heat presses, sublimation printers); specialist materials for individual projects

### Staff Development

Curriculum leaders must sequence teacher development alongside student learning:

1. **Technical upskilling**: Regular workshops to maintain and extend teacher expertise
2. **Pedagogical development**: Engagement with research (Atkinson, Stables, etc.)
3. **Industry connections**: Links with textile professionals and contemporary makers
4. **Examination requirements**: Understanding of assessment objectives and expectations

### Curriculum Planning Documents

Effective sequencing requires clear documentation:

- **Skills progression grids**: Showing technical development across years
- **Knowledge organisers**: Key vocabulary, techniques, and concepts for each unit
- **Assessment criteria**: Clear expectations for each Key Stage
- **Exemplar work**: Annotated examples showing expected standards

## Challenges and Solutions

### Common Sequencing Issues

**Challenge 1: Technical skill regression**
Students forgetting techniques between projects.
**Solution**: Regular skill-maintenance sessions; spiral revisiting of techniques; clear skill documentation.

**Challenge 2: Disconnected projects**
Learning appears fragmented rather than progressive.
**Solution**: Thematic links across projects; explicit articulation of progression; portfolio structures showing development.

**Challenge 3: Limited transfer**
Students can't apply skills in new contexts.
**Solution**: Varied application contexts; explicit teaching of transfer strategies; problem-solving focus.

### Meeting Diverse Needs

Sequencing must accommodate diverse starting points and progression rates:

- **Differentiated pathways**: Multiple routes to similar outcomes
- **Flexible pacing**: Extension and support as needed
- **Inclusive practices**: Adaptive tools and techniques for SEND students
- **Cultural responsiveness**: Valuing diverse textile traditions and experiences

## Conclusion

Effective sequencing in Textiles education creates coherent learning journeys where students progressively develop technical mastery, design capability, and critical understanding. By drawing on the research of Atkinson, Stables, Kettle, Holmes and others, and by carefully planning progression across Key Stages, teachers can ensure that students develop both the practical expertise and conceptual sophistication that characterises genuine textile capability.

Curriculum leaders should regularly review their sequencing against progression evidence, student outcomes, and developing best practice, ensuring that their Textiles curriculum provides rigorous, inspiring and equitable learning opportunities for all students.
