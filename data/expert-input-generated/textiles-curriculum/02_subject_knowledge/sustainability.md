# Sustainability in Textiles

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Sustainability in textiles has become a fundamental aspect of contemporary textiles education in the UK, reflecting the urgent global environmental challenges facing the fashion and textile industries. The subject encompasses environmental, social, and economic considerations throughout the lifecycle of textile products, from fibre production to end-of-life disposal.

The **National Curriculum for Design and Technology** (2014) explicitly requires pupils to understand how "more advanced materials and technologies can be used intelligently and creatively to design and make products" and to "evaluate their ideas and products against their own design criteria and consider the views of others to improve their work." Whilst sustainability is not explicitly named at Key Stage 3, it is embedded within expectations that pupils should "understand developments in design and technology, its impact on individuals, society and the environment, and the responsibilities of designers, engineers and technologists."

At GCSE level, all major UK examination boards now incorporate sustainability as a core assessment objective. **AQA Design and Technology: Textiles** specifications require understanding of "environmental, social and economic challenges," whilst **OCR Design and Technology** includes "the six Rs" (Rethink, Refuse, Reduce, Reuse, Repair, Recycle) as explicit assessment criteria. **Edexcel** specifications emphasise life cycle analysis and the circular economy.

## Historical Context and Key Thinkers

**Professor Stephanie Atkinson**, former President of the Design and Technology Association, has consistently advocated for embedding sustainability within design education. In her research on design pedagogy, Atkinson argues that sustainability must move beyond "tokenistic recycling projects" to encompass systemic thinking about production and consumption patterns. She emphasises that textiles education provides unique opportunities to explore these issues tangibly through making and materials investigation.

**Professor Kay Stables** (Goldsmiths, University of London) has conducted extensive research into designerly thinking and authentic design contexts. Stables' work on "designing with values" is particularly relevant to sustainability education, encouraging teachers to position environmental and ethical considerations as central to the design process rather than afterthoughts. Her research demonstrates that when pupils engage with real-world sustainability challenges, their design thinking becomes more sophisticated and socially conscious.

Contemporary textile artists **Alice Kettle** and **Cas Holmes** exemplify sustainable practice through their work. Holmes, in particular, has developed a practice rooted in "found textiles" and environmental awareness, using naturally dyed papers and reclaimed fabrics. Her publication *The Found Object in Textile Art* (Batsford, 2010) and subsequent workshops have influenced UK textiles teachers to incorporate environmental consciousness into creative practice. Holmes' work demonstrates that sustainability and aesthetic excellence are not mutually exclusive but can enhance creative outcomes.

## Key Concepts and Subject Knowledge

### The Environmental Impact of Textiles

The textiles and fashion industry is the second-largest polluter globally, responsible for approximately 10% of global carbon emissions and 20% of industrial water pollution. Teachers must understand specific impacts:

**Cotton production** requires approximately 10,000-20,000 litres of water to produce 1kg of cotton (equivalent to one t-shirt and pair of jeans). Conventional cotton farming uses 16% of global insecticides and 7% of pesticides whilst occupying just 2.5% of agricultural land. The Aral Sea disaster in Central Asia serves as a powerful case study for Key Stage 4 and 5 pupils, demonstrating the catastrophic environmental consequences of intensive cotton irrigation.

**Synthetic fibres**, predominantly derived from petroleum, constitute approximately 69% of all textile fibres produced globally (Textile Exchange, 2023). Polyester production for textiles releases approximately 706 billion kg of greenhouse gases annually. Additionally, synthetic textiles shed microplastic fibres during washing, with a single garment releasing up to 700,000 fibres per wash cycle, contributing significantly to ocean plastic pollution.

**Dyeing and finishing** processes account for approximately 20% of global industrial water pollution. Conventional textile dyeing uses heavy metals (chromium, copper, zinc) and toxic chemicals including formaldehyde, chlorine, and AZO dyes. The Citarum River in Indonesia and waterways near Tiruppur, India, provide documented case studies of textile industry water pollution.

### Sustainable Fibres and Materials

Teachers should develop detailed knowledge of alternative fibres for practical application:

**Organic cotton** eliminates synthetic pesticides and fertilisers, reducing water pollution and soil degradation. However, yields are typically 20-30% lower than conventional cotton, raising questions about land use and food security. The **Global Organic Textile Standard (GOTS)** provides third-party certification ensuring organic status throughout supply chains.

**Hemp and linen** (bast fibres) require significantly less water than cotton—hemp needs approximately 500 litres per kilogram—and naturally resist pests, eliminating pesticide requirements. Both fibres are naturally biodegradable and suitable for UK cultivation, offering opportunities for discussing local production and food miles. Hemp's historical suppression due to association with cannabis provides an engaging cross-curricular connection to history and economics.

**Regenerated cellulosic fibres** present complex sustainability narratives. **Viscose/rayon** production has historically involved toxic chemicals (carbon disulphide) and contributed to deforestation. However, newer processes including **Tencel™/Lyocell** (manufactured by Lenzing) use closed-loop systems recovering 99% of solvents and source from certified sustainable forests. This exemplifies how technological innovation can address sustainability challenges.

**Recycled fibres** include mechanically and chemically recycled options. Mechanical recycling of polyester (from PET bottles) is widely understood but has limitations: fibres shorten with each recycling cycle, and mixed-fibre textiles (polycotton blends) are difficult to separate. Emerging chemical recycling technologies (e.g., **Worn Again Technologies**, **Renewcell's Circulose™**) can regenerate fibres to virgin quality but remain commercially limited. Teachers should critically examine whether recycling polyester perpetuates reliance on fossil fuel-derived materials.

**Innovative bio-based materials** offer engaging teaching content: **Piñatex** (pineapple leaf fibre leather alternative), **Mylo™** (mycelium-based leather), **Orange Fiber** (citrus byproduct textiles), and bacterial cellulose. Whilst largely unavailable for school workshops, these materials stimulate discussion about materials innovation and biomimicry principles.

### The Circular Economy in Textiles

The **Ellen MacArthur Foundation's Circular Economy principles** provide an essential framework for Key Stage 4 and 5 teaching. The Foundation's report *A New Textiles Economy* (2017) outlined a vision for textiles based on three principles:

1. **Eliminate waste and pollution** through design
2. **Keep products and materials in use** at their highest value
3. **Regenerate natural systems** rather than merely extracting resources

Teachers should contrast this with the prevailing **linear economy** model (take-make-dispose) that characterises fast fashion. The UK disposes of approximately 300,000 tonnes of clothing to landfill annually, with the average garment worn only 7-10 times before disposal—down from 50-100 wears in previous generations.

**Design for longevity** strategies include:
- **Physical durability**: selecting appropriate fibres, constructions, and finishes
- **Emotional durability**: creating meaningful, timeless designs (Jonathan Chapman's research on emotionally durable design is relevant here)
- **Adaptability**: designing for adjustment, repair, and transformation

**Design for disassembly** considers end-of-life from the design stage. Mixed-fibre fabrics (polycotton), composite materials, and permanent fastenings hinder recycling. Teachers can introduce concepts like mono-material design, removable fastenings, and avoiding embellishments that contaminate recycling streams.

### Life Cycle Analysis (LCA)

Life Cycle Analysis provides a systematic methodology for evaluating environmental impacts across five stages:

1. **Raw material extraction/cultivation**
2. **Manufacturing and processing**
3. **Distribution and retail**
4. **Use phase** (washing, drying, ironing, repair)
5. **End-of-life** (disposal, recycling, biodegradation)

Surprisingly, the **use phase** often generates the highest environmental impact for many garments, particularly through laundering. A cotton t-shirt's lifetime carbon footprint may be 50-60% attributable to washing and drying. This provides opportunities for teaching about consumer behaviour and sustainable consumption practices: washing at lower temperatures, air-drying, and appropriate garment care.

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology** specifications explicitly require understanding of LCA, stating pupils must be able to "critically analyse existing products considering...sustainability issues at each stage of the life cycle." **OCR** specifications similarly require evaluation of "the environmental, social and economic impact throughout a product's life cycle."

Teachers should facilitate practical LCA activities using resources like the **Higg Materials Sustainability Index** (Sustainable Apparel Coalition) or simplified frameworks. Comparing the lifecycle impacts of a conventional cotton t-shirt versus organic cotton, recycled polyester, or hemp alternatives provides tangible learning experiences.

### Social Sustainability and Ethical Production

Sustainability extends beyond environmental considerations to encompass workers' rights, fair wages, and safe working conditions. The **Rana Plaza disaster** (Bangladesh, 2013), which killed 1,134 garment workers, catalysed increased awareness of fashion industry labour exploitation.

**Fair Trade** and **Ethical Trading Initiative** certifications ensure minimum standards for workers. Teachers should explore:

- **Living wages** versus minimum wages (research by Labour Behind the Label demonstrates garment workers in Bangladesh earning approximately £90 monthly, far below living wage calculations of £240)
- **Working conditions** including excessive hours, unsafe buildings, and workplace harassment
- **Child labour** in cotton harvesting (particularly Uzbekistan's state-orchestrated forced labour until recent reforms) and garment production
- **Gender issues** (approximately 80% of garment workers globally are women, often facing discrimination and gender-based violence)

**Fashion Revolution's "Who Made My Clothes?"** campaign provides accessible teaching resources connecting consumers to producers. The campaign's annual **Fashion Revolution Week** (April, commemorating Rana Plaza) offers opportunities for cross-curricular projects and community engagement.

### The Six Rs Framework

UK examination boards, particularly **OCR**, emphasise the **Six Rs** as a framework for sustainable design:

1. **Rethink**: Questioning necessity and challenging assumptions (Do we need this product? Could needs be met differently?)
2. **Refuse**: Declining unnecessary consumption and problematic materials
3. **Reduce**: Minimising material use, waste, and environmental impact
4. **Reuse**: Extending product life through direct reuse or repurposing
5. **Repair**: Designing for and practicing repair and maintenance
6. **Recycle**: Processing waste materials into new products

This framework should be applied iteratively throughout the design process. For example, when designing a bag, pupils might:
- **Rethink**: Could we design a multi-functional product combining bag, backpack, and tote functions to reduce total items owned?
- **Refuse**: Avoid vinyl/PVC materials due to toxicity concerns
- **Reduce**: Optimise pattern pieces to minimise fabric waste
- **Reuse**: Incorporate reclaimed denim or upholstered fabrics
- **Repair**: Include accessible seams, removable straps, and reinforcement at stress points
- **Recycle**: Use mono-material construction facilitating end-of-life recycling

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At Key Stage 3, sustainability education should establish foundational understanding through practical engagement:

**Materials exploration**: Pupils should physically investigate sustainable materials, comparing properties of organic versus conventional cotton, recycled polyester versus virgin, natural versus synthetic fibres. Burn tests, strength tests, and absorbency investigations make abstract concepts tangible.

**Simple life cycle mapping**: Using visual frameworks, pupils can map textile product lifecycles from fibre to disposal, identifying environmental impacts at each stage. Starting with familiar items (school uniform, sports kit) makes learning relevant.

**Basic ecological awareness**: Understanding how textiles connect to broader environmental systems—water pollution from dyeing, pesticide impacts on biodiversity, microplastic pollution in oceans. Cross-curricular links with Science and Geography are valuable.

**Upcycling and creative reuse projects**: Practical projects transforming discarded textiles (old jeans into bags, shirts into cushion covers) develop skills whilst challenging throwaway culture. However, following Atkinson's caution, these should include critical reflection on systemic issues rather than implying individual recycling solves industry-wide problems.

**Local and traditional practices**: Investigating historical textile practices (visible mending, darning, patchwork) connects sustainability to cultural heritage. Cas Holmes' approach to celebrating "the beauty of the worn and weathered" can inspire aesthetic appreciation of repair and age.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16, GCSE)

GCSE specifications require substantially deeper engagement:

**Detailed fibre knowledge**: Comprehensive understanding of sustainable fibre options, production methods, environmental impacts, and performance characteristics. Pupils should articulate trade-offs between different sustainable choices (e.g., organic cotton's water requirements versus hemp's limited fabric hand).

**Critical evaluation of sustainability claims**: Developing media literacy around greenwashing, unsubstantiated claims, and misleading marketing. Analysing actual company reports (e.g., H&M Conscious Collection critiques, Patagonia's transparency) against advertising messaging.

**Life Cycle Analysis application**: Conducting detailed LCAs for design projects, using quantitative data where possible (water usage, carbon footprints, waste generation) and qualitative assessment where quantitative data is unavailable.

**Circular economy design strategies**: Applying design for longevity, disassembly, repair, and transformation principles to practical projects. This might include modular designs, zero-waste pattern cutting, or visible mending as aesthetic feature.

**Contextual understanding**: Exploring economic and social dimensions through case studies of specific companies, supply chains, or communities. Research projects investigating brands' sustainability credentials or local textile heritage develop research skills whilst deepening understanding.

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology: Textiles** assessment criteria explicitly reference: "consideration of sustainability and environmental issues," "consideration of social, moral, ethical issues," and "life cycle analysis." Pupils must demonstrate this understanding in both written examination (50%) and Non-Exam Assessment coursework (50%).

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology** includes specific exam questions requiring pupils to "explain how a product could be designed to be more sustainable using the 6Rs" and to "evaluate the environmental impact of materials and manufacturing processes."

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18, A-Level and Level 3 Qualifications)

Advanced level study demands sophisticated critical analysis and independent research:

**Theoretical frameworks**: Engaging with academic discourse on sustainability, including systems thinking, degrowth economics, post-consumer culture, and ecological design philosophy. Introducing thinkers like Victor Papanek (*Design for the Real World*), Kate Fletcher (fashion and sustainability researcher at Centre for Sustainable Fashion), or Tristram Stuart (food and resource waste).

**Supply chain complexity**: Detailed investigation of global textile supply chains, understanding the fragmentation and outsourcing that obscures environmental and social impacts. Analysing transparency initiatives like Fashion Revolution's **Transparency Index** or specific brands' supply chain mapping.

**Materials innovation research**: Independent investigation of emerging sustainable materials and processes, evaluating technical feasibility, scalability, and genuine sustainability benefits versus marketing hype. Researching academic papers and industry reports develops higher-order research skills.

**Comparative analysis**: Sophisticated comparison of sustainability approaches across companies, from luxury brands (often emphasising longevity and craft) to sports brands (focusing on recycled polyester innovation) to certified B-Corporations (prioritising systemic positive impact).

**Designing for change**: Advanced projects might explore how design can facilitate behaviour change, develop new systems for clothing rental or repair, or propose radical alternatives to current consumption patterns. Alice Kettle's large-scale narrative textile works demonstrate how textiles can communicate complex ideas and provoke social reflection.

**AQA A-Level Design and Technology: Fashion and Textiles** requires pupils to "consider issues relating to social, moral, ethical and environmental factors" and demonstrate "comprehensive knowledge and thorough understanding of...factors that influence design decisions including sustainability."

**WJEC Level 3 Art and Design: Textiles** emphasises "evidencing sustained investigation into historical, contemporary, social and cultural contexts" which should include critical engagement with sustainability as a defining contemporary issue.

## Practical Teaching Strategies

### Resource Management in the Classroom

Teachers should model sustainable practice through department management:

**Fabric procurement**: Build relationships with local upholstery suppliers, furniture restorers, and theatrical costumiers who may donate or sell offcuts and remnants. **Scrap stores** (e.g., Children's Scrapstore network across UK) provide low-cost reclaimed materials. However, ensure materials meet safety standards and are appropriate for projects.
