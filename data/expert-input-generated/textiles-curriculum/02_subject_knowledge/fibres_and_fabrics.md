# Fibres and Fabrics

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Fibres and fabrics form the foundational knowledge base for textiles education in the UK, underpinning both the National Curriculum Design and Technology programmes of study and GCSE/A-level specifications. Understanding the properties, characteristics, and applications of different fibres and fabrics is essential for informed design decision-making and practical textile work.

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology in England requires pupils to develop 'a thorough knowledge of which materials and components can be used and how they can be shaped' (DfE, 2013). In textiles, this translates to understanding fibre origins, fabric construction methods, and the relationship between material properties and end-use applications.

## Classification of Fibres

### Natural Fibres

Natural fibres derive from plant, animal, or mineral sources. Kay Stables' research emphasises that understanding material origins helps pupils make informed decisions about sustainability and functionality in textile design (Stables, 2008).

**Plant-based (Cellulosic) Fibres:**
- **Cotton**: Derived from cotton plant seed pods (bolls). Absorbent, comfortable, breathable, and washes well at high temperatures. Widely used for clothing, bedding, and towels.
- **Linen**: Made from flax plant stems. Stronger than cotton, highly absorbent, conducts heat well, but creases easily. Traditional uses include summer clothing and home furnishings.
- **Hemp**: From cannabis plant stems. Extremely durable and sustainable, becoming increasingly important in eco-conscious textile design.
- **Jute**: Coarse fibre used primarily for sacks, ropes, and backing fabrics.

**Animal (Protein) Fibres:**
- **Wool**: From sheep fleeces. Excellent insulation properties, naturally elastic, flame-resistant, and can absorb moisture without feeling damp. Different breeds produce varying qualities (Merino vs. Shetland).
- **Silk**: Produced by silkworm larvae (Bombyx mori). Strong, lustrous, drapes beautifully, but requires careful handling and cleaning. Alice Kettle's embroidered textile art often exploits silk's light-reflecting properties.
- **Cashmere**: From cashmere goat undercoat. Extremely soft and luxurious but expensive.
- **Mohair**: From Angora goats. Lustrous, resilient, and takes dye well.
- **Alpaca**: From alpaca fleeces. Warmer than wool, hypoallergenic, and increasingly popular.

**Mineral Fibres:**
- **Asbestos**: Historically used but now banned due to health hazards. Important for pupils to understand historical context and why material safety matters.

### Manufactured Fibres

**Regenerated Fibres:**
These use natural polymer sources (usually cellulose) but require chemical processing:
- **Viscose/Rayon**: Made from wood pulp cellulose. Drapes well, absorbent, but weaker when wet. Stephanie Atkinson's work on material literacy emphasises understanding these processing distinctions (Atkinson, 2013).
- **Modal**: A type of rayon with improved strength and durability.
- **Lyocell (Tencel)**: Produced using closed-loop processing, making it more environmentally friendly than traditional viscose.
- **Acetate**: Derived from cellulose acetate. Used in linings and occasionwear due to its silk-like appearance.

**Synthetic (Polymer) Fibres:**
Created entirely from petrochemicals through polymerisation:
- **Polyester**: Extremely versatile, strong, resists creasing, quick-drying, but can be uncomfortable in hot weather due to poor breathability. Most widely used synthetic fibre globally.
- **Nylon (Polyamide)**: First fully synthetic fibre (1938). Strong, elastic, abrasion-resistant. Used in hosiery, sportswear, and carpets.
- **Acrylic**: Wool-like properties but synthetic. Lightweight, warm, but can pill easily.
- **Elastane (Spandex/Lycra)**: Exceptionally elastic (can stretch 500-700%). Usually blended with other fibres for stretch fabrics in sportswear and fashion.
- **Polypropylene**: Lightweight, moisture-wicking, used in thermal underwear and outdoor fabrics.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 3
Pupils should develop knowledge of:
- Basic fibre classification (natural vs. manufactured)
- Properties and characteristics of common fibres
- How fibre properties influence fabric selection
- Simple fabric construction methods (weaving, knitting)
- Basic care and maintenance requirements

Practical implications: Use handling collections where pupils physically examine samples, perform simple burn tests (under strict supervision), absorbency tests, and draping experiments.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)
All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) require detailed knowledge of:
- Comprehensive fibre classification including regenerated and synthetic categories
- Scientific understanding of fibre properties and their relationship to end-use
- Environmental and ethical considerations in fibre production
- Fabric construction techniques and their impact on fabric properties
- Technical specifications and quality control

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552)**: Specifically requires understanding of 'papers and boards, natural and manufactured timber, ferrous and non-ferrous metals, thermoforming and thermosetting polymers, **textile-based materials**' with emphasis on material properties and working characteristics.

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310)**: Section 3.1 requires knowledge of 'materials and their working properties' including detailed understanding of textile fibres and fabrics.

### Key Stage 5 (A-level)
A-level specifications demand deeper analysis:
- Molecular structure of fibres and how this determines properties
- Advanced fabric construction and finishing techniques
- Life cycle analysis of textile materials
- Innovation in smart and technical textiles
- Critical evaluation of sustainability in textile production

## Fabric Construction Methods

### Woven Fabrics

Weaving interlaces two sets of yarns at right angles: warp (lengthwise) and weft (crosswise).

**Basic Weave Structures:**
- **Plain weave**: Simple over-under pattern. Strongest weave structure. Examples: calico, muslin, canvas.
- **Twill weave**: Diagonal ribbing created by offset pattern. More drape than plain weave. Examples: denim, gabardine, herringbone.
- **Satin weave**: Yarns float over multiple threads creating lustrous, smooth surface. Snags easily. Examples: satin, sateen (cotton variant).

Cas Holmes' mixed-media textile work often incorporates fragments of various woven structures, demonstrating how understanding weave construction informs artistic practice (Holmes, 2015).

### Knitted Fabrics

Knitting creates fabric through interlocking loops of yarn.

**Weft Knitting:**
- Yarn runs horizontally across fabric
- More stretch and flexibility than woven fabrics
- Examples: jersey, rib, interlock
- Used extensively in T-shirts, underwear, and casualwear

**Warp Knitting:**
- Each yarn forms loops vertically
- More stable than weft knitting
- Examples: tricot, raschel lace
- Used in lingerie, swimwear, and technical textiles

### Non-Woven Fabrics

Fabrics produced without weaving or knitting:
- **Felted**: Fibres matted together using heat, moisture, and pressure (traditional wool felt)
- **Bonded**: Fibres held together with adhesive or heat (interfacing, disposable cloths)
- **Needle-punched**: Barbed needles mechanically interlock fibres (carpet underlay, geotextiles)

### Other Construction Methods
- **Lace**: Openwork fabric created by twisting and knotting threads
- **Braid**: Narrow fabric created by interlacing three or more strands
- **Laminated**: Multiple layers bonded together (waterproof breathable fabrics)

## Fabric Properties and Characteristics

### Physical Properties

**Strength**: Ability to withstand tension and stress. Nylon and polyester exhibit high tensile strength; silk is surprisingly strong for a natural fibre.

**Elasticity**: Ability to stretch and return to original shape. Wool has natural elasticity; elastane provides exceptional stretch in blends.

**Absorbency**: Capacity to absorb moisture. Cotton and linen are highly absorbent; polyester resists moisture absorption, making it quick-drying.

**Thermal properties**: Wool provides excellent insulation; linen conducts heat away from the body effectively.

**Durability**: Resistance to wear and abrasion. Nylon exhibits exceptional durability; silk, despite strength, can degrade with sunlight exposure.

### Aesthetic Properties

- **Drape**: How fabric hangs and falls (silk drapes beautifully; canvas is stiff)
- **Handle/Hand**: How fabric feels (cashmere is soft; hessian is coarse)
- **Lustre**: Surface shine (satin has high lustre; calico is matt)
- **Texture**: Surface characteristics (bouclé is textured; jersey is smooth)

Stephanie Atkinson's research emphasises that understanding aesthetic properties is crucial for developing 'material literacy' – the ability to read and interpret material qualities and make appropriate design decisions (Atkinson, 2013).

## Fibre Blends and Mixtures

Blending different fibres combines advantageous properties while minimising disadvantages:
- **Polycotton** (polyester/cotton): Combines cotton's comfort with polyester's easy-care properties. Common ratio: 65% polyester/35% cotton.
- **Cotton/elastane**: Provides stretch in denim and casual wear (typically 95% cotton/5% elastane)
- **Wool/nylon**: Increases wool's durability in socks and knitwear
- **Viscose/linen**: Combines linen's coolness with viscose's drape and lower cost

UK labelling regulations (Textile Products (Labelling and Fibre Composition) Regulations 2012) require fibre content disclosure, helping consumers make informed choices.

## Fabric Finishes

Finishes alter fabric properties and performance:

**Mechanical Finishes:**
- Brushing/raising (creates soft surface on flannelette)
- Calendering (produces smooth, polished surface)
- Pleating (permanent pleats set using heat)

**Chemical Finishes:**
- Flame retardant (essential for children's nightwear under UK regulations)
- Water repellent/waterproofing (outdoor clothing)
- Stain resistance (upholstery fabrics)
- Anti-microbial (medical textiles, sportswear)
- Easy-care/crease resistance (formalwear, schoolwear)

## Sustainability and Ethical Considerations

Contemporary textiles education must address environmental and ethical issues:

### Environmental Impact
- Cotton production: Heavy pesticide use and water consumption
- Synthetic fibres: Derived from non-renewable petrochemicals; microplastic pollution from washing
- Viscose production: Deforestation concerns and chemical pollution
- Textile waste: UK sends 300,000 tonnes of clothing to landfill annually

### Sustainable Alternatives
- **Organic cotton**: Grown without synthetic pesticides
- **Recycled polyester**: Made from PET bottles or textile waste
- **Lyocell/Tencel**: Closed-loop production minimises environmental impact
- **Hemp and linen**: Require fewer pesticides and less water than cotton
- **Recycled wool**: Shoddy production has historical roots in West Yorkshire textile industry

Kay Stables' work on sustainable design education emphasises the importance of pupils understanding the 'bigger picture' of material choices beyond immediate functional requirements (Stables & Kimbell, 2007).

### Social and Ethical Issues
- Fair trade cotton and certification schemes
- Living wages in garment production
- The Rana Plaza disaster (2013) and its impact on supply chain transparency
- Modern slavery in textile supply chains (UK Modern Slavery Act 2015)

## Technical and Smart Textiles

Emerging textile technologies relevant to contemporary curricula:

**Performance Textiles:**
- Moisture-wicking fabrics (coolmax, dri-fit)
- Breathable waterproofs (Gore-Tex, eVent)
- UV-protective fabrics
- Compression garments

**Smart Textiles:**
- Phase-change materials (Outlast) – regulate temperature
- Conductive fibres – enable integration with electronics
- Photochromic textiles – change colour with light exposure
- Shape-memory materials

A-level specifications increasingly reference these innovations. WJEC A-level Design and Technology: Fashion and Textiles explicitly requires understanding of 'smart materials and modern materials' within textile contexts.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Resources and Equipment
- Comprehensive fibre and fabric sample collections (ensure diverse range including technical textiles)
- Digital microscope for examining fibre and fabric structure
- Testing equipment: fabric weights, magnifying glasses, burn test equipment (used safely)
- Swatches for handling and sensory exploration

### Pedagogical Approaches

**Material-led investigations**: Cas Holmes advocates beginning with materials themselves, allowing exploration to inform understanding rather than starting with abstract knowledge (Holmes, 2015).

**Comparative testing**: Pupils perform systematic tests (absorbency, strength, thermal properties) and record findings scientifically, linking to STEM skills.

**Contextual case studies**: Examine real-world applications (mountaineering gear, medical textiles, historical costumes) to understand how material properties determine functionality.

**Disassembly activities**: Take apart garments or textile products to identify fibres, construction methods, and finishes – developing material literacy through reverse engineering.

### Assessment Considerations

GCSE controlled assessment/NEA tasks require pupils to:
- Justify material and component selection with reference to specific properties
- Consider environmental and social impacts of material choices
- Demonstrate understanding of how materials can be manipulated and combined

Teachers should ensure pupils develop precise technical vocabulary and can articulate the relationship between material properties and design requirements.

## Conclusion

Deep understanding of fibres and fabrics enables pupils to make informed, creative, and responsible design decisions. As Alice Kettle demonstrates through her large-scale embroidered installations, knowledge of material properties – how silk catches light differently to cotton, how woven substrates accept stitch differently to knits – directly informs artistic and functional outcomes.

Effective textiles education develops material literacy progressively across key stages, combining hands-on exploration with scientific understanding, historical context, and contemporary ethical awareness. Teachers should facilitate experiences where pupils develop intuitive understanding through handling and testing whilst building precise technical knowledge required for examination success and future practice in textile design, fashion, or textile engineering careers.
