# The Design Process in Textiles

## Introduction

The design process in textiles education represents a fundamental pedagogical framework that enables learners to develop creative, technical and evaluative capabilities. Within the UK curriculum context, the design process is not a linear sequence but rather an iterative, cyclical approach that mirrors authentic industry practice whilst developing critical thinking and practical making skills. This knowledge file examines established design process models, their application in textiles education, and the pedagogical underpinning that makes them effective teaching tools.

## The Design Process in the National Curriculum

### Key Stage 3 Requirements

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology in England (2013) establishes clear expectations for the design process across all material areas, including textiles. At Key Stage 3, pupils should be taught to:

- Use research and exploration to identify and understand user needs
- Identify and solve their own design problems
- Develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional products
- Generate, develop, model and communicate ideas through discussion, annotated sketches, cross-sectional drawings, exploded diagrams, prototypes and pattern pieces
- Select from and use specialist tools, techniques, processes, equipment and machinery precisely
- Test, evaluate and refine their ideas and products against a specification, taking into account the views of intended users

Professor Stephanie Atkinson's research emphasises that effective design education requires students to engage with 'authentic problem-solving' rather than simply following prescriptive tasks. Her work highlights the importance of student autonomy within structured frameworks.

### Key Stage 4 and GCSE Requirements

At GCSE level, examination boards require evidence of iterative design development. All major UK exam boards structure their coursework portfolios around clear design process stages:

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology (Textiles)** requires:
- Investigation of design possibilities (20% of NEA)
- Production of a detailed design brief and specification
- Generation and development of design ideas
- Manufacture of a prototype/final product
- Testing and evaluation

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology** emphasises iterative design through:
- Identifying and investigating design possibilities
- Producing a design brief and specification
- Generating design ideas and developing a chosen design
- Making the product
- Analysing and evaluating

**Eduqas (WJEC) GCSE Design and Technology** structures the NEA around:
- Design brief and research
- Design specification
- Design ideas
- Development and manufacture
- Testing and evaluation

### A-Level Textiles Requirements

At A-Level, the design process becomes more sophisticated and self-directed. Examination boards expect students to demonstrate:

- Independent identification of design needs and opportunities
- Substantial research incorporating primary and secondary sources
- Creative exploration using a range of media and techniques
- Iterative development showing refinement based on testing and analysis
- Critical evaluation throughout the design journey

## Theoretical Models of the Design Process

### Linear vs Cyclical Models

Early design education often presented linear models progressing from brief → research → design → make → evaluate. However, contemporary understanding recognises this as pedagogically limited. **Kay Stables**' research at Goldsmiths, University of London has been instrumental in establishing that expert designers work iteratively, constantly moving between conception, development, making and evaluation.

Stables identifies that students need to understand:
- **Procedural knowledge** (knowing how to progress through design stages)
- **Strategic knowledge** (knowing when to move between stages)
- **Metacognitive awareness** (understanding their own design thinking)

### The Iterative Design Model

The iterative model, now dominant in UK textiles education, recognises that:
- Designing and making are interconnected activities
- Evaluation occurs throughout, not just at the end
- Ideas are refined through cycles of testing and development
- Failures and modifications are valuable learning opportunities

This approach aligns with **Alice Kettle's** perspective as a practising textile artist who emphasises the importance of 'thinking through making' – the idea that textile processes themselves generate design ideas and solutions that cannot be predicted through drawing alone.

### Design Thinking in Textiles

Contemporary design thinking frameworks (Stanford d.school model) have influenced UK textiles pedagogy, particularly:
1. **Empathise** – understanding user needs
2. **Define** – articulating the design challenge
3. **Ideate** – generating multiple solutions
4. **Prototype** – creating textile samples and mock-ups
5. **Test** – gathering feedback and refining

This human-centred approach encourages textiles students to consider functionality, aesthetics, cultural context and user experience simultaneously.

## Pedagogical Approaches to Teaching the Design Process

### Scaffolding Student Independence

Effective textiles teaching requires careful scaffolding that gradually releases responsibility to students. **Stephanie Atkinson's** research identifies three levels of teacher intervention:

1. **High support** (KS3 early): Structured design briefs, predetermined contexts, guided research tasks
2. **Medium support** (KS3/4 transition): Choice within parameters, scaffolded independent research, guided evaluation prompts
3. **Low support** (KS4/5): Self-identified problems, independent research methodologies, critical self-evaluation

Teachers should explicitly teach design strategies rather than assuming students will develop them through exposure alone.

### Making as Research

**Cas Holmes**, textile artist and educator, advocates for 'making as research' – the principle that physical exploration with materials, techniques and processes is itself a form of research and idea generation. This challenges traditional textiles teaching that rigidly separates 'design work' (drawing/annotation) from 'practical work' (making).

Effective pedagogy integrates:
- **Sampling** as exploratory design thinking
- **Material manipulation** as idea generation
- **Process experimentation** as problem-solving
- **Technical development** as design refinement

Teachers should allocate curriculum time for exploratory making without predetermined outcomes, recognising this as valuable design process activity.

### Documentation and Reflection

All UK exam boards require documented evidence of the design process. Effective practice includes:

**Continuous documentation**: Students photograph, annotate and reflect throughout making, not retrospectively. This develops metacognitive awareness and provides authentic assessment evidence.

**Visual literacy**: Teaching students to communicate through annotated sketches, technical diagrams, mood boards, and digital photography. Different documentation methods suit different design stages.

**Critical reflection**: Moving beyond description ('I did this') to analysis ('this worked because') and evaluation ('I would modify this by'). Question prompts scaffold higher-order thinking.

## Key Stages of the Textiles Design Process

### 1. Context and Brief Development

**Purpose**: Establishing a clear design challenge grounded in authentic need.

**Pedagogical considerations**:
- At KS3, teachers typically provide structured contexts (design for a specific user, response to a theme)
- At KS4/5, students should identify their own design opportunities through observation and research
- Effective briefs are specific enough to provide direction but open enough to allow creative interpretation

**Research from Kay Stables** demonstrates that students who engage deeply with context analysis produce more innovative and appropriate design solutions than those who rush to idea generation.

**Practical teaching strategies**:
- User interviews and surveys
- Market research of existing textile products
- Analysis of cultural, historical or contemporary textile movements
- Identification of gaps in existing provision

### 2. Research and Analysis

**Purpose**: Building knowledge foundations that inform design decisions.

**Primary research activities**:
- Handling and analysing existing textile products
- Photography of relevant textile contexts
- User questionnaires and interviews
- Visits to exhibitions, galleries or retail environments

**Secondary research activities**:
- Artist/designer case studies (examining techniques, concepts, cultural context)
- Historical textile research
- Technical investigation of materials and processes
- Cultural and social context research

**Critical insight from Stephanie Atkinson**: Research must be purposeful and selective. Students often collect excessive information without synthesis. Teachers should emphasise research questions that directly relate to the design brief.

**Analysis requirements**: Students must analyse research, identifying:
- Design features they might adopt or adapt
- Techniques they wish to explore
- User preferences and requirements
- Technical constraints and opportunities

### 3. Design Specification

**Purpose**: Establishing measurable criteria against which designs will be evaluated.

Specifications should be **SMART** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) and address:
- **Function**: What must the textile product do?
- **User requirements**: Who is it for? What are their needs/preferences?
- **Aesthetics**: What visual qualities are desired?
- **Size/scale**: Specific dimensions
- **Materials**: Appropriate fabric types, properties required
- **Techniques**: Construction and decoration methods
- **Cost**: Budget parameters
- **Safety/sustainability**: Ethical and practical considerations

**Common pedagogical error**: Students writing vague specifications ('it must look nice'). Teachers should model specific, testable criteria ('it must use minimum 3 complementary colours based on user colour preference research').

### 4. Initial Idea Generation

**Purpose**: Creating multiple potential solutions without premature judgement.

**Divergent thinking strategies**:
- Mind mapping and word association
- Mood boards combining imagery, colour, texture, pattern
- Quick exploratory sketches (quantity over quality at this stage)
- Material and texture collages
- SCAMPER technique (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse)

**Alice Kettle's approach**: Begin with materials exploration – handle fabrics, experiment with basic techniques, allow the materials to suggest possibilities. This challenges the assumption that all design begins with drawing.

**Cas Holmes' perspective**: Embrace 'happy accidents' during initial sampling. Unintended results often generate the most innovative directions.

**Teacher facilitation**: Encourage risk-taking and experimental approaches. Avoid premature critique that stifles divergent thinking. Set quantity targets (generate minimum 6 distinctly different concepts) to prevent fixation on first idea.

### 5. Design Development and Refinement

**Purpose**: Iteratively developing and improving selected design ideas.

This is typically the most substantial phase and should demonstrate:

**Progressive refinement**:
- Selecting most promising ideas (justify selection against specification)
- Creating detailed design variations
- Developing colour schemes through sampling
- Experimenting with pattern, scale, composition
- Technical sampling of proposed techniques
- Testing material combinations

**Iterative cycles**: Kay Stables' research emphasises that development should show multiple cycles of:
1. Propose modification
2. Test through sampling/drawing
3. Evaluate against specification
4. Refine further

**Common assessment criteria** (across exam boards):
- Evidence of iterative development (not single linear progression)
- Use of annotation explaining design decisions
- Testing of technical approaches
- Reference to specification criteria
- Integration of research influences
- Demonstration of creativity and originality

**Practical teaching strategies**:
- Regular mini-critiques where students present development work
- Peer feedback sessions structured by specification criteria
- Teacher intervention through questioning rather than direction
- Physical sampling integrated throughout (not separate from design sheets)

### 6. Final Design Realisation

**Purpose**: Communicating the resolved design solution clearly.

Requirements typically include:
- **Working drawings**: Technical flats showing construction details
- **Colour specification**: Precise colour palette with reference codes/swatches
- **Technical specifications**: Fabric types, quantities, dimensions
- **Construction sequence**: Step-by-step manufacturing plan
- **Pattern pieces**: Where applicable, full-size pattern templates
- **Surface decoration details**: Placement diagrams for embroidery, print, embellishment

**Quality expectations increase through key stages**:
- KS3: Clear sketches with basic annotation and colour indication
- KS4: Detailed technical drawings with measurements, fabric specifications, construction notes
- KS5: Industry-standard technical packs including specifications, quality standards, manufacturing considerations

### 7. Making and Manufacturing

**Purpose**: Realising the design through skilled practical application.

While this file focuses on design process pedagogy, making is inseparable from designing in textiles. Alice Kettle argues that making reveals design problems and possibilities that cannot be anticipated through drawing alone.

**Pedagogical integration**:
- Encourage students to document making decisions and modifications
- Photograph work-in-progress showing technical processes
- Record problems encountered and solutions developed
- Allow flexibility to modify designs based on making experience

**Quality of manufacture** is assessed across all exam boards considering:
- Technical accuracy and precision
- Appropriate tool and equipment use
- Surface decoration quality
- Finishing standards
- Complexity of techniques attempted

### 8. Testing and Evaluation

**Purpose**: Measuring success against specification criteria and identifying improvements.

**Formative evaluation** occurs throughout:
- After research: "What have I learned that will inform my design?"
- During development: "How well does this sample meet my specification?"
- Throughout making: "Is this technique achieving the intended effect?"

**Summative evaluation** addresses:
- Specification criteria: systematic review of each criterion with evidence
- User feedback: gathering opinions from intended user/audience
- Strengths and weaknesses: honest appraisal of successes and limitations
- Modifications: specific, justified improvements for future iterations

**Common weaknesses** (identified by Stephanie Atkinson's research):
- Superficial description rather than analytical evaluation
- Exclusively positive self-assessment lacking critical reflection
- Evaluation conducted after completion rather than throughout
- Failure to link evaluation to specification criteria

**Effective teaching strategies**:
- Provide evaluation question prompts throughout the project
- Model critical evaluation using exemplar work
- Require photographic evidence to support evaluation claims
- Structure peer assessment against specification criteria

## Differentiation Within the Design Process

### Supporting Lower-Attaining Students

**Scaffolding strategies**:
- Provide structured design brief contexts rather than requiring independent problem identification
- Offer research direction sheets with specific questions to answer
- Supply templates for mood boards, specification writing, evaluation
- Break design development into smaller, sequenced tasks with clear success criteria
- Provide technical skill instruction before expecting independent application
- Use visual prompts and exemplars extensively

**Kay Stables' research** emphasises that scaffolding should support access whilst maintaining cognitive challenge. Students need appropriately challenging design problems, not simplified tasks.

### Extending Higher-Attaining Students

**Challenge strategies**:
- Require self-identification of design problems through observation and research
- Expect sophisticated primary research methodologies
- Demand complex technical skill combinations
- Require critical analysis of cultural/contextual issues
- Expect independent problem-solving when technical challenges arise
- Set higher expectations for aesthetic sophistication and originality

**At A-Level**, students should demonstrate autonomous design thinking, with teacher as facilitator rather than director.

## Assessment of the Design Process

### Assessment Frameworks Across Exam Boards

All major UK exam boards assess design process through Non-Examined Assessment (coursework portfolio), typically weighted:
- **AQA**: 50% of GCSE (100 marks)
- **OCR**: 50% of GCSE (100 marks)  
- **Eduqas**: 60% of GCSE (120 marks)

**Common assessment criteria include**:
1. **Investigation and research** (15-20%): Quality, relevance and analysis of research
2. **Design thinking** (25-30%): Idea generation, development, creativity, specification use
3. **Making skills** (25-30%): Technical accuracy, appropriate technique selection, finish quality
4. **Analysis and evaluation** (15-20%): Critical reflection, testing, user feedback, specification review

### Formative Assessment Strategies

**Effective practice includes**:
- Regular design reviews (mini-presentations to class/teacher)
- Checkpoint assessments at each design stage
- Peer assessment structured by specific criteria
- Self-assessment against specification and exam board criteria
- Teacher-student dialogue through annotation and questioning
- Progress tracking against project timelines

**Stephanie Atkinson's research** identifies that students benefit from seeing assessment criteria demystified through exemplar work at different grade boundaries, enabling them to understand quality standards.

## Common Pedagogical Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: Students Rush to Final Ideas

**Problem**: Students skip research and development, producing premature solutions.

**Solutions**:
- Impose milestone deadlines for each design stage
- Require minimum quantities (e.g., 'minimum 8 initial ideas', '6 development samples')
- Withhold materials until development is reviewed
- Teach explicitly why iterative development improves outcomes
- Share work of designers showing extensive development processes

### Challenge 2: Design Work Becomes Separate from Making

**Problem**: Students complete 'design folder' then make product, with no integration.

**Solutions** (drawing on Cas Holmes and Alice Kettle's approaches):
- Require ongoing documentation during making
- Set sampling tasks before design sheet work
- Allow students to modify designs based on making discoveries
- Photograph making processes for inclusion in portfolio
- Value material experimentation as design thinking

### Challenge 3: Superficial Research Without Analysis

**Problem**: Students collect images without understanding or application.

**Solutions**:
- Require written analysis for each research example
- Provide analytical question prompts
- Model annotation that identifies specific design features, techniques, compositional strategies
- Require students to take inspiration from research into their own samples
- Limit quantity of research to encourage depth over breadth
