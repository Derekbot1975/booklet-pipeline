# Stephanie Atkinson

**Full Name:** Stephanie Atkinson  
**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Design and Technology Education, University of Sunderland  
**Previously:** Professor of Design and Technology Education, University of Sunderland (retired)  
**Known For:** Pioneering research in textiles education pedagogy, health and safety in D&T, risk-benefit analysis in design and technology education, and developing frameworks for creative and technical textiles teaching

---

## Biography and Career History

Professor Stephanie Atkinson established herself as one of the United Kingdom's foremost authorities on Design and Technology education, with particular specialism in textiles education. Her academic career was primarily based at the University of Sunderland, where she served as Professor of Design and Technology Education and led teacher education programmes for both primary and secondary D&T specialists.

Atkinson's work at Sunderland positioned the university as a significant centre for D&T education research in the North East of England. She was instrumental in developing Initial Teacher Education (ITE) programmes that prepared teachers to deliver high-quality textiles and D&T education across key stages. Her influence extended beyond higher education through extensive partnerships with schools, local authorities, and national educational bodies.

Throughout her career, Atkinson has been an active member of the Design and Technology Association (DATA, now the Design and Technology Association or D&T Association), contributing to policy documents, conference presentations, and professional development initiatives. She has examined doctoral students, supervised EdD and PhD candidates in D&T education, and served on editorial boards for subject-specific journals.

Her research career spans approximately three decades, during which she has published extensively on textiles pedagogy, health and safety management in practical subjects, assessment in D&T, and the development of creativity through making activities. Atkinson's retirement has not diminished her influence; she continues to be cited in contemporary D&T education literature and her frameworks remain relevant to current practice.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Health and Safety in D&T: Risk-Benefit Analysis

Atkinson is perhaps best known for her pioneering work on reconceptualising health and safety in Design and Technology education. Moving beyond traditional risk-averse approaches, she advocated for **risk-benefit analysis** as a more educationally sound framework. This approach acknowledges that learning inherently involves some risk, and that completely eliminating risk also eliminates valuable learning opportunities.

Her work challenged the increasingly restrictive health and safety culture in UK schools during the 2000s and 2010s, which had led many departments to abandon certain practical activities, particularly in textiles (such as batik, tie-dye, and machinery use). Atkinson argued that teachers should conduct proper risk-benefit assessments that weigh potential hazards against educational value, enabling rather than preventing valuable hands-on experiences.

This framework considers:
- The developmental benefits of engaging with tools, materials and processes
- Age-appropriate skill progression
- Proper instruction and supervision as risk management strategies
- Building pupils' own risk awareness and management capabilities
- The educational cost of over-protective practices

Her research in this area has been cited in policy documents from the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) and CLEAPSS, and influenced the DATA's guidance on safe practice in D&T.

### 2. Textiles Education Pedagogy

Atkinson developed sophisticated pedagogical frameworks specifically for textiles education, recognising it as a distinct discipline within D&T with its own knowledge base, skills progression, and creative possibilities. She identified textiles as suffering from particular challenges: historical associations with 'feminine' domestic crafts, lower status compared to resistant materials, and inadequate teacher subject knowledge.

Her pedagogical approach emphasises:
- **Technical knowledge**: Understanding fibres, fabrics, construction methods, and finishing techniques as foundational knowledge
- **Creative exploration**: Using textiles as a medium for creative expression and design thinking
- **Cultural contexts**: Exploring textiles traditions from diverse cultures and historical periods
- **Contemporary applications**: Connecting textiles education to fashion, interior design, technical textiles, and emerging technologies (smart textiles, sustainability)
- **Progression frameworks**: Clear skill development from Key Stage 3 through Key Stage 4 and beyond

Atkinson argued that effective textiles teaching requires teachers with genuine subject expertise, not generalist D&T teachers with minimal textiles background—a position that challenged some models of D&T teacher education.

### 3. Creative and Critical Capabilities in Making

Drawing on research into creativity within D&T contexts, Atkinson explored how textiles education specifically develops creative and critical capabilities. She identified textiles work as offering unique opportunities for creative development through:
- **Sensory engagement**: The tactile, visual and aesthetic qualities of textiles materials
- **Iterative experimentation**: Sampling and testing as creative exploration
- **Personal expression**: Creating items with individual meaning and style
- **Problem-solving**: Addressing design challenges through material manipulation

Her research examined how teachers can structure textiles projects to maximise creative development whilst maintaining technical rigour. This balanced approach resists both overly prescriptive "recipe-following" teaching and completely unstructured "free expression" that lacks skill development.

### 4. Assessment in Textiles and D&T

Atkinson contributed significantly to understanding assessment challenges in practical subjects. She explored tensions between:
- Assessing creative process versus finished products
- Technical skill demonstration versus design thinking
- Individual capability versus collaborative work
- Objective criteria versus subjective aesthetic judgement

Her work on assessment literacy for D&T teachers emphasised helping teachers develop robust, defendable assessment judgements that recognise the multifaceted nature of D&T capability. This included research into moderation practices, portfolio assessment, and criterion-referenced assessment specific to textiles work.

She was particularly concerned with assessment equity issues in textiles, where factors such as access to resources outside school, prior experience with sewing and craft, and gendered expectations could disadvantage certain pupils.

### 5. Primary D&T and Foundation for Secondary Learning

Atkinson researched the crucial role of primary D&T education in establishing foundations for secondary textiles learning. She identified significant variability in primary D&T provision, with textiles often being the weakest area due to primary teachers' lack of confidence and subject knowledge.

Her research advocated for:
- Comprehensive primary ITE programmes including substantial textiles content
- Clear progression from EYFS through Key Stage 2 in textiles-related making skills
- Age-appropriate introduction to tools such as needles, fabric scissors, and simple sewing machines
- Building confidence with fabric manipulation, joining techniques, and decoration methods
- Establishing design-and-make vocabulary and processes

She argued that pupils arriving at secondary school with minimal textiles experience face significant disadvantages, and that secondary teachers must often spend Key Stage 3 establishing skills that should have been developed earlier.

### 6. Gender and Textiles Education

Throughout her career, Atkinson engaged with persistent gender issues in D&T and textiles education. Her research examined:
- Historical legacy of gendered subject divisions (needlework/woodwork)
- Continuing patterns of subject choice, with textiles remaining predominantly female-chosen at GCSE
- Strategies for making textiles appealing across genders
- The problematic status of textiles as 'feminine' within D&T departments
- Male under-representation in textiles teaching

She advocated for reframing textiles within contemporary contexts (fashion technology, technical textiles, product design) to challenge gendered perceptions whilst valuing traditional textile crafts. Her position rejected both the dismissal of traditional techniques as outdated and the abandonment of textiles' distinctive character in attempts to make it seem more "technological."

### 7. Subject Knowledge for Teaching Textiles

Atkinson made significant contributions to understanding the subject knowledge requirements for effective textiles teaching. She distinguished between:
- **Subject content knowledge**: Understanding of fibres, fabrics, processes, equipment, and technical terminology
- **Pedagogical content knowledge**: Knowing how to teach textiles effectively, including common pupil misconceptions, effective demonstrations, and skill progression
- **Curriculum knowledge**: Understanding National Curriculum requirements, examination specifications, and progression frameworks

Her research revealed that many D&T teachers had inadequate textiles subject knowledge, having specialised in other material areas. She advocated for specialist textiles teachers rather than the generalist model promoted in some quarters, particularly for Key Stage 4 teaching.

This work informed ITE curriculum design at Sunderland and influenced national discussions about D&T teacher education and continuing professional development needs.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Atkinson's work influenced understanding of progression in textiles within the National Curriculum for Design and Technology. During various iterations of the curriculum (particularly the 2007/2008 and 2014 revisions), her research informed discussions about:
- The place of textiles within the broader D&T curriculum
- Appropriate skill progression across key stages
- Balance between traditional craft techniques and contemporary applications
- Technical vocabulary and knowledge requirements

Her advocacy for maintaining textiles as a distinct strand within D&T helped resist proposals to subsume it entirely within generic "materials" categories.

### Examination Board Specifications

Atkinson's expertise informed GCSE and A-level examination specifications in D&T: Textiles Technology. Examination boards including AQA, Edexcel, and OCR consulted her research regarding:
- Appropriate content for different qualification levels
- Assessment criteria that capture both technical and creative capabilities
- Practical assessment methodologies
- Health and safety requirements in controlled assessment conditions

### Initial Teacher Education Standards

Through her work at Sunderland and contributions to national ITE discussions, Atkinson influenced expectations for D&T teacher preparation. Her research supported arguments for:
- Mandatory textiles content in all secondary D&T ITE programmes
- Subject knowledge audit requirements for trainees
- Practical competencies in textile techniques as part of qualifying standards
- Understanding of health and safety specific to textiles equipment and processes

### Health and Safety Policy

Atkinson's risk-benefit analysis framework influenced policy documents from:
- **CLEAPSS** (Consortium of Local Education Authorities for the Provision of Science Services): Her research informed their guidance on managing risks in practical subjects
- **Health and Safety Executive (HSE)**: Contributed to more balanced approaches to risk in educational contexts
- **D&T Association**: Central to their position statements on health and safety in D&T

Her work provided evidence-based arguments against excessive risk-aversion that was limiting practical learning opportunities in schools.

### Professional Development Frameworks

Atkinson's research informed continuing professional development (CPD) programmes for D&T teachers nationally. Her frameworks for subject knowledge development, particularly in textiles, shaped CPD offerings from:
- D&T Association regional networks
- Local authority CPD programmes
- National Science Learning Centre D&T programmes
- University-based CPD provision

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructivist Foundations

Atkinson's pedagogical approach draws on constructivist learning theory, emphasising that pupils build understanding through active engagement with materials, tools and processes. In textiles contexts, this means:
- Learning through doing: Practical experimentation with fabrics and techniques
- Building on prior knowledge: Connecting new learning to existing craft and making experiences
- Social construction: Learning through peer interaction, critique, and collaborative projects
- Reflective practice: Encouraging pupils to articulate and evaluate their design decisions

### Iterative Design Process

Central to Atkinson's pedagogy is the iterative design process specific to textiles:
1. **Investigation**: Exploring existing textiles products, techniques, and contexts
2. **Design development**: Sketching, mood boards, fabric sampling
3. **Experimentation**: Testing materials, techniques, and construction methods
4. **Making**: Progressive skill development through increasingly complex projects
5. **Evaluation**: Critical reflection on outcomes, processes, and learning
6. **Refinement**: Revisiting and improving designs based on evaluation

This process is not linear but cyclical, with pupils moving back and forth between stages as their understanding develops.

### Demonstration and Modelling

Atkinson emphasised the critical importance of high-quality teacher demonstration in textiles education. Effective demonstrations:
- Break complex skills into manageable steps
- Make tacit knowledge explicit through teacher talk
- Allow pupils to see the process, not just the outcome
- Anticipate and address common errors
- Build progressively on established skills

She advocated for teachers maintaining their own making practice to keep demonstration skills current and credible.

### Differentiation Through Material and Technique

Atkinson's approach to differentiation recognises that textiles work allows for natural differentiation through:
- **Material selection**: Different fabric types offer varying levels of challenge
- **Technique complexity**: From basic running stitch to advanced embroidery or pattern cutting
- **Scale and ambition**: Scope of projects can be adjusted
- **Support frameworks**: Templates, guides, and structures can be added or removed
- **Creative interpretation**: Open-ended design briefs allow varied responses

This approach maintains high expectations for all whilst providing appropriate challenge levels.

### Assessment for Learning

Drawing on assessment for learning (AfL) principles, Atkinson advocated for:
- Clear learning objectives shared with pupils
- Success criteria co-constructed with classes
- Regular formative feedback during making processes
- Peer assessment of design work and outcomes
- Self-assessment and reflection embedded in projects
- Modelling exemplar work to establish standards
- Critique sessions that build evaluative capability

This formative approach helps pupils develop both practical skills and critical understanding of quality in textiles work.

### Cultural and Contemporary Contexts

Atkinson argued for situating textiles education within rich cultural and contemporary contexts:
- **Historical traditions**: Exploring textiles heritage from diverse cultures
- **Contemporary practice**: Linking to fashion, interior design, sustainable textiles
- **Career pathways**: Making explicit connections to creative industries
- **Technological developments**: Smart textiles, digital printing, laser cutting
- **Social and environmental issues**: Sustainability, ethical production, fast fashion critique

This contextualisation makes textiles education more relevant and engaging whilst developing cultural literacy.
