# Cas Holmes

**Full Name:** Cas Holmes

**Current Roles:** Senior Lecturer in Textiles at University for the Creative Arts (UCA); Practising textile artist; Author; Workshop leader

**Known For:** Mixed media textiles; sustainable practice using found and reclaimed materials; stitch as drawing; site-responsive textile art; environmental consciousness in contemporary textile practice; influential pedagogy in UK art and design education

---

## Biography and Career History

Cas Holmes is a leading figure in contemporary UK textile art and education, whose practice and teaching have fundamentally shaped approaches to sustainable and experimental textile work in British art schools. Based at the University for the Creative Arts (UCA) in Farnham, Surrey, Holmes has developed a distinctive pedagogical approach that places environmental awareness, material reuse, and creative experimentation at the heart of textile education.

Holmes trained initially in fine art before specialising in textiles, a cross-disciplinary foundation that remains evident in her approach to the medium. Her career spans over three decades of professional practice, teaching, and publication. She has exhibited widely throughout the UK and internationally, with work held in private and public collections. Her practice is characterised by an engagement with landscape, memory, and the material traces of everyday life.

At UCA, Holmes has been instrumental in developing curricula that challenge traditional distinctions between fine art and textiles, encouraging students to view textile processes as fundamental tools for visual thinking rather than merely decorative craft. Her teaching philosophy emphasises direct material engagement, sketchbook development, and the creative potential of constraint—particularly the creative opportunities presented by working with found, reclaimed, and 'worthless' materials.

Holmes is widely recognised as a pioneering voice in sustainable textile practice, though she approaches sustainability not as a limiting ethical imperative but as a creative catalyst. Her work has influenced thousands of practitioners through workshops delivered at venues including the Whitworth Art Gallery, West Dean College, and numerous regional and international institutions.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Stitch as Mark-Making and Drawing

Holmes fundamentally positions stitch as a form of drawing rather than exclusively as a method of construction or decoration. This conceptual shift, central to her teaching and writing, encourages practitioners to view needle and thread as equivalent to pencil or charcoal—tools for visual thinking and mark-making. In this framework, stitch becomes a medium for recording observation, expressing gesture, and creating tonal variation. This approach liberates students from anxiety about 'correct' stitching techniques, instead emphasising personal expression and visual communication. Holmes demonstrates how running stitch, cross stitch, and simple embroidery techniques can create line quality, texture, and rhythm comparable to drawn marks. This pedagogical approach has been particularly influential in breaking down hierarchies between fine art and textile practice within UK curricula.

### 2. Found Materials and Creative Constraint

A cornerstone of Holmes's practice and teaching is the use of found, salvaged, and reclaimed materials. Rather than viewing these materials as second-best substitutes for purchased supplies, Holmes positions them as sources of inherent meaning, history, and aesthetic richness. Tea-stained papers, packaging materials, old textiles, natural detritus, and industrial offcuts all feature prominently in her work and teaching. This approach serves multiple purposes: it challenges consumerist models of art-making; it democratises access to materials; it encourages close observation and creative problem-solving; and it embeds environmental consciousness into the making process. Holmes teaches students to 'read' found materials for their visual qualities, histories, and potential meanings, developing a form of material literacy that has become increasingly relevant in contemporary sustainable design discourse.

### 3. Layering and Palimpsest

Holmes's work is characterised by complex layering of materials, marks, and processes, creating what she describes as visual 'palimpsests'—surfaces that reveal traces of multiple interventions and histories. This approach mirrors both natural processes (geological layering, weathering, growth) and human patterns of use and reuse. In pedagogical terms, the layering process allows for experimentation, revision, and development without the pressure of 'getting it right first time'. Students learn to build complexity gradually, responding to emerging compositions rather than following predetermined plans. Holmes demonstrates how layers can be partially revealed or concealed through cutting, tearing, and selective stitching, creating depth and visual interest. This methodology has influenced approaches to textile sampling and development work across UK art school curricula.

### 4. Site-Responsive Practice and Environmental Engagement

Holmes's practice is deeply engaged with place, landscape, and environmental observation. She teaches methods for responding to specific sites through textile work, encouraging students to collect material evidence (in the form of found objects, rubbings, photographs) and to use textile processes to explore and express responses to place. This approach connects textile practice to broader contemporary art concerns with site-specificity, ecology, and environmental change. Holmes's site-responsive methodology includes techniques such as contact printing with natural materials, creating portable 'field kits' for outdoor working, and developing notation systems using stitch and collage. This aspect of her work has been particularly influential in connecting textile education to environmental studies and has supported curriculum developments linking art and design to sustainability agendas.

### 5. Sketchbook as Central Tool

Holmes champions the sketchbook as the primary site for textile thinking and development. Her approach to sketchbook practice integrates drawing, collage, stitch sampling, writing, and material collection in ways that challenge traditional boundaries between 'research' and 'making'. She teaches that sketchbooks should be working documents—sites of experimentation, failure, and discovery—rather than polished presentation objects. This philosophy encourages risk-taking and authentic creative development. Holmes's sketchbook methods include 'fast and slow' making (quick intuitive responses alongside sustained development), integrated use of stitch within page-based work, and the creation of 'portable studios' that allow students to work responsively in varied contexts. Her sketchbook pedagogy has influenced assessment criteria and teaching approaches across UK art and design courses.

### 6. Slow Making and Contemplative Practice

In an educational context increasingly concerned with employability, digital skills, and rapid production, Holmes maintains a commitment to slow, contemplative making processes. Hand stitch, in particular, is positioned not as an inefficient throwback but as a valuable mode of thinking, a meditative practice, and a form of resistance to accelerated contemporary life. Holmes teaches students to value the knowledge that emerges through sustained material engagement and to recognise the cognitive and emotional dimensions of making. This philosophical stance connects textile practice to broader movements around slow fashion, mindfulness, and critiques of consumer culture. Her teaching validates time-intensive processes and supports students in articulating the value of hand-making in contemporary contexts.

### 7. Democratised Creativity

Holmes's pedagogy is fundamentally inclusive, rejecting notions of special talent or prerequisite skills. Her workshops and teaching emphasise that everyone can engage meaningfully with textile processes using simple materials and techniques. This democratic approach is partly facilitated by her focus on found materials (removing financial barriers) and basic stitches (removing technical barriers). Holmes demonstrates that creative textile practice requires not expensive equipment or advanced skills but rather careful observation, willingness to experiment, and responsive engagement with materials. This philosophy has made her workshops accessible to diverse audiences including community groups, teachers, and amateur practitioners, extending the influence of her ideas beyond formal art education.

### 8. Colour and Restraint

Holmes's aesthetic is characterised by muted, sophisticated colour palettes often derived from natural and found materials. She teaches approaches to colour that emphasise observation of natural and urban environments, restricted palettes, and the subtle variations within apparently monochromatic ranges. This approach to colour challenges the bright, commercial palettes often prevalent in textile education and encourages students to develop more nuanced visual sensitivity. Holmes demonstrates how material choice itself contributes to colour—the greys of newsprint, the ochres of rust-stained paper, the varied neutrals of salvaged fabrics. Her teaching on colour is integrated with material exploration rather than treated as a separate design element.

## Key Publications

### *The Found Object in Textile Art* (2010, Batsford)

This influential publication established Holmes's reputation as a leading voice in sustainable textile practice. The book provides both philosophical justification and practical methodology for working with found and reclaimed materials. It includes detailed case studies of Holmes's own work alongside contributions from other practitioners, situational exercises for finding and selecting materials, and techniques for integrating diverse found elements into cohesive textile work. The publication has been widely adopted as a recommended text on UK textile degree programmes and has influenced curriculum development toward more sustainable practices.

### *Connected Cloth: Creating Collaborative Textile Projects* (2013, Batsford)

This publication explores collaborative textile practice, documenting Holmes's experience leading community projects and group workshops. It addresses pedagogical challenges of facilitating collaborative making while maintaining individual creative voices, and provides frameworks for designing and managing textile projects involving multiple participants. The book has been particularly influential in community arts contexts and in informing approaches to group projects within formal education settings. It includes documentation of specific projects with diverse groups, practical advice on materials and logistics, and reflection on the social dimensions of textile making.

### *Stitch Stories: Personal Places, Spaces and Traces* (2016, Batsford)

Perhaps Holmes's most pedagogically influential publication, this book articulates her approach to narrative, memory, and place in textile work. It details methods for using stitch and mixed media to explore personal and collective histories, to respond to landscape and environment, and to create meaningful textile narratives. The book includes extensive visual documentation of process, from initial site visits and material collection through to finished pieces. Technical instruction is embedded within broader creative frameworks, making the book valuable both for specific techniques and for overall approach. This publication has been widely adopted in UK textile programmes and has influenced project brief development, particularly in final year degree work where students develop sustained personal investigations.

### *Textile Landscape: Painting with Cloth in Mixed Media* (2019, Batsford)

This later publication synthesises Holmes's mature practice and teaching approach, focusing specifically on landscape as subject and on painterly approaches to textile composition. The book demonstrates how textile techniques can be used to explore landscape in ways analogous to but distinct from painting, emphasising the particular qualities of cloth, stitch, and layering. It includes detailed studies of specific locations and seasonal changes, providing models for sustained site-based investigation. The publication is notable for its integration of environmental awareness with aesthetic practice, addressing climate change and habitat loss through creative work rather than solely through didactic means.

### Journal Articles and Book Chapters

Holmes has contributed chapters to several edited collections on contemporary textile practice, including contributions to *Textile Thinking* and other academic compilations. Her writing appears in *Selvedge*, *Embroidery*, and other specialist publications. These shorter pieces often address specific technical or conceptual concerns and extend the reach of her ideas to broader audiences including amateur makers and school-level educators.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Higher Education Textile Programmes

Holmes's influence on UK textile education is substantial, particularly within the university sector. Her pedagogical approaches have informed curriculum development at UCA and, through visiting lectures and workshops, at institutions including Goldsmiths, Royal College of Art, Manchester School of Art, and others. Key curricular impacts include:

**Integration of Sustainability:** Holmes's work has provided practical models for embedding sustainability into textile curricula without compromising creative ambition or aesthetic sophistication. Her demonstration that environmental consciousness can drive rather than constrain creativity has influenced curriculum revisions across the sector, particularly following increased institutional focus on Education for Sustainable Development (ESD).

**Repositioning of Process:** Her emphasis on process, experimentation, and iterative development has reinforced pedagogical shifts away from outcomes-focused teaching toward valuing research, development, and critical reflection. This aligns with Quality Assurance Agency (QAA) expectations for creative learning at higher education levels.

**Breaking Down Disciplinary Boundaries:** Holmes's fine art-inflected approach to textiles has supported curriculum developments that position textiles within broader contemporary art contexts rather than isolating it as craft or design specialism. This has been particularly relevant as many institutions have moved toward multidisciplinary art and design programmes.

### Assessment Criteria and Learning Outcomes

Holmes's pedagogical philosophy has influenced how textile work is assessed in UK higher education. Her emphasis on sketchbooks, process documentation, and material experimentation has supported assessment criteria that value research and development alongside finished outcomes. This approach aligns with Creative Skillset standards and Subject Benchmark Statements for Art and Design, which emphasise critical reflection and process.

### Continuing Professional Development (CPD)

Holmes's extensive workshop programme has functioned as informal CPD for teachers across sectors. Art and design teachers from further education colleges and secondary schools regularly attend her workshops, taking methods and approaches back to their own teaching contexts. Her publications serve similar functions, providing accessible models that can be adapted to different educational levels.

### Community Arts and Lifelong Learning

Beyond formal education, Holmes's work has influenced community arts practice and adult education provision. Her inclusive, materials-accessible approach has provided frameworks for socially engaged textile projects and has demonstrated textiles' potential for community cohesion, memory work, and therapeutic making. This has informed Arts Council England-funded projects and third-sector arts provision.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Teaching Philosophy

Holmes's pedagogy is fundamentally constructivist, positioning students as active makers of knowledge through material engagement rather than passive recipients of instruction. She creates conditions for discovery rather than prescribing outcomes, a approach consistent with contemporary creative education theory as articulated by researchers including Anna Craft and Penny Hay.

Her teaching emphasises:

**Material Immersion:** Extended direct engagement with materials, tools, and processes as the primary mode of learning. Holmes typically begins workshops with extensive material exploration before any discussion of 'project' or 'outcome'.

**Low-Threshold Entry:** Use of accessible materials and simple techniques ensures students can engage immediately without prerequisite skills, building confidence and allowing focus on creative decision-making rather than technical anxiety.

**Observation and Responsiveness:** Development of visual sensitivity through close observation of materials, environments, and emerging compositions. Students are taught to 'read' and respond to their work in progress rather than impose predetermined plans.

**Embracing Accident and Error:** Mistakes, material behaviours, and unexpected outcomes are positioned as valuable rather than problematic. This approach reduces anxiety and encourages genuine experimentation.

**Integration of Theory and Practice:** Holmes's teaching avoids false separation between 'making' and 'thinking'. Theoretical concepts emerge from and through material engagement rather than being imposed externally.

### Workshop Structure

Holmes's typical workshop structure reflects her pedagogical values:

1. **Material exploration** without predetermined outcome (30-45 minutes)
2. **Demonstration** of specific techniques or approaches in response to emerging student needs
3. **Extended making time** with individual tutorial support
4. **Group reflection** and sharing of work, emphasising diverse responses to same starting points
5. **Discussion** of broader contexts, connecting practical work to professional practice and theoretical concepts

This structure allows for both individual discovery and collective knowledge-building, balancing autonomy with support.

### Tutorial Approach

Holmes's one-to-one teaching is characterised by probing questions rather than prescriptive advice. She uses the Socratic method to help students articulate their own intentions and to develop critical frameworks for evaluating their work. Typical tutorial questions include:

- "What interests you about this material/area/mark?"
- "What happens if you...?"
- "Where do you see potential for development?"
- "What are you trying to communicate/explore?"

This questioning approach develops student autonomy and critical thinking skills, key graduate attributes identified in QAA Subject Benchmark Statements.

### Assessment and Feedback

Holmes's approach to assessment emphasises formative feedback throughout development processes. She values:

- **Process documentation** (sketchbooks, samples, photographic records) equally with finished work
- **Risk-taking and experimentation** even when outcomes are unsuccessful
- **Personal voice and authentic engagement** with materials and concepts
- **Critical reflection** demonstrated through written and visual means

This assessment philosophy aligns with contemporary understanding of assessment for learning as articulated by researchers including Royce Sadler and David Nicol.

## Impact on Practice Beyond Education

While primarily known as an educator, Holmes's influence extends to professional textile practice in the UK and internationally. Her work has:

- **Legitimised found materials** as appropriate for 'serious' textile art, challenging material hierarchies
- **Modelled sustainable practice** without compromise to aesthetic ambition
- **Demonstrated viability** of stitch and textile processes within contemporary art contexts
- **Provided vocabulary and conceptual frameworks** for articulating textile practice in critical discourse

Practitioners across career stages cite Holmes's work as influential, particularly her demonstration that sustainable practice can be aesthetically sophisticated rather than worthy but dull.

## Recognition and Professional Activity

Holmes has served as external examiner for textile programmes across the UK, in which capacity she has influenced curriculum development and quality assurance processes. She regularly delivers keynote presentations at conferences including the Textile Society conference and the Contemporary Textiles conference series.

She has been featured in numerous exhibitions including at the Whitworth Art Gallery, Manchester; Bankfield Museum, Halifax; and the Knitting & Stitching Show. Her work has been acquired for permanent collections including at the Harris Museum, Preston.

Holmes maintains an active professional practice alongside her teaching, modelling the integration of practice and pedagogy that characterises effective creative education.

Holmes's work has gained increased relevance in recent years as sustainability has moved to the centre of design discourse. Her decades-long commitment to reclaimed materials and slow making positions her as a pioneer whose practice anticipated urgent contemporary concerns.

- **Increased institutional focus** on sustainable practice following declarations of climate emergency
- **Growing interest** in repair, mending, and material reuse within textile culture
