# Alice Kettle

## Overview

**Full Name:** Alice Kettle

**Current Role:** Professor of Textile Arts, Manchester School of Art, Manchester Metropolitan University

**Known For:** Large-scale narrative embroidery, contemporary stitch practice, pioneering approaches to textile art education, and advocacy for stitch as a contemporary art form

---

## Biography and Career History

### Early Career and Education

Alice Kettle studied Fine Art Embroidery at Goldsmiths, University of London, completing her degree in 1987. She subsequently undertook postgraduate study at the Royal College of Art, graduating in 1990 with an MA in Embroidery. This rigorous training established the foundation for her distinctive approach to stitch as a drawing medium and her commitment to pushing the boundaries of textile practice beyond craft traditions.

### Professional Development

Following her RCA studies, Kettle established herself as a practising artist whilst simultaneously developing her educational practice. Her work has been exhibited internationally, including at the Victoria and Albert Museum, Whitworth Art Gallery, and numerous solo exhibitions across Europe. She became recognised for her large-scale embroidered installations that address themes of human experience, migration, conflict, and cultural identity.

### Academic Positions

Kettle joined Manchester School of Art at Manchester Metropolitan University, where she has been instrumental in shaping contemporary textile education. She was appointed Professor of Textile Arts, a position from which she has influenced both practice-based research and pedagogical approaches to textiles across the UK. Her academic leadership has positioned Manchester School of Art as a significant centre for contemporary textile practice and research.

### Recognition and Awards

Kettle was elected as a Royal Designer for Industry (RDI) in 2019, one of the highest accolades for designers in the UK, awarded by the Royal Society of Arts. This recognition acknowledged both her artistic contribution and her influence on textile education. She is also a Fellow of the Royal Society of Arts and has received numerous commissions for public and ecclesiastical spaces.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Stitch as Drawing

Kettle fundamentally reconceptualises embroidery as a drawing medium rather than a decorative craft. She employs the sewing machine as a drawing tool, creating gestural, expressive marks that parallel traditional drawing practices. This approach liberates stitch from its historical associations with domesticity and women's work, repositioning it within contemporary art discourse. Her teaching emphasises the immediacy and spontaneity possible with machine embroidery, encouraging students to think of the needle as equivalent to a pencil or brush. This conceptual shift has been particularly influential in enabling students to approach textiles with the same conceptual rigour applied to fine art practices.

### 2. Scale and Monumentality in Textile Practice

Kettle's commitment to large-scale work challenges conventional perceptions of embroidery as intimate and small-scale. Her major installations, such as "Thread Bearing Witness" (2016-2018), measure several metres in length and height, demanding engagement with textiles as architectural and spatial interventions. This approach to scale has pedagogical implications, requiring students to consider fabrication methods, structural integrity, and spatial relationships beyond traditional textile formats. She advocates for textiles occupying gallery spaces as equivalent to painting or sculpture, thereby shifting the hierarchical positioning of textile art within fine art education.

### 3. Narrative and Social Engagement

Throughout her practice and teaching, Kettle emphasises textiles as a medium for storytelling and social commentary. Her "Thread Bearing Witness" project, created in collaboration with individuals from refugee and migrant communities, demonstrates how textile practice can facilitate dialogue about displacement, identity, and human rights. This socially engaged approach informs her pedagogical philosophy, encouraging students to consider the communicative and political potential of textiles. She teaches that textile practice can address contemporary social issues with the same critical engagement as other art forms, positioning stitch as a legitimate medium for political and social commentary.

### 4. Collaborative Making and Participatory Practice

Kettle has pioneered collaborative approaches to large-scale textile work, particularly through her "Thread Bearing Witness" project, which involved workshops with over 240 participants across multiple countries. This methodology demonstrates how textile practice can build communities and facilitate cross-cultural dialogue. Her pedagogical approach incorporates collaborative projects that develop students' skills in workshop facilitation, community engagement, and participatory art practices. This emphasis on collaboration challenges the individualistic model often dominant in fine art education, instead celebrating textile practice's capacity to bring diverse voices together.

### 5. Materiality and Technical Innovation

Whilst advocating for conceptual rigour, Kettle maintains that deep material knowledge and technical skill remain fundamental to contemporary textile practice. She works primarily with domestic sewing machines, demonstrating how accessible technology can produce sophisticated artistic outcomes. Her teaching emphasises understanding thread behaviour, fabric substrates, and machine capabilities, whilst encouraging experimental approaches that push technical boundaries. This balance between technical mastery and conceptual innovation characterises her pedagogical philosophy, rejecting false dichotomies between craft skill and artistic thinking.

### 6. The Professional Artist-Educator Model

Kettle exemplifies the artist-educator model, maintaining an active exhibition schedule and studio practice alongside her teaching responsibilities. She advocates for educators remaining engaged with professional practice, arguing that this currency informs teaching with contemporary relevance. Her approach demonstrates how academic positions can support rather than compromise artistic practice, and she encourages emerging educators to maintain this dual identity. This model has influenced how textile programmes recruit and support staff, emphasising the importance of practising artists within education.

### 7. Research Through Making

Kettle's practice-based research methodology positions making as a form of knowledge production equivalent to traditional academic research. Her PhD by publication at Manchester Metropolitan University (awarded 2014) established a framework for how textile practice generates unique insights into human experience, cultural identity, and material culture. This research-through-practice model has been influential in validating textile practice within the research economy of UK higher education, supporting colleagues seeking to articulate the scholarly value of making.

### 8. Institutional Positioning of Textiles

Throughout her career, Kettle has worked to position textiles within art school structures as equivalent to painting, sculpture, and other fine art disciplines. She advocates for textiles being taught within interdisciplinary fine art contexts rather than isolated craft departments, whilst maintaining specialised technical facilities and expertise. This positioning reflects broader debates within UK art education about disciplinary identities and has influenced curriculum structures at Manchester School of Art and beyond.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Manchester School of Art Curriculum Development

As Professor of Textile Arts at Manchester School of Art, Kettle has significantly influenced curriculum design for textile programmes. The MA Textiles programme under her influence emphasises conceptual development alongside technical skills, positioning textile practice within contemporary art discourse. The curriculum incorporates socially engaged practice, participatory methodologies, and large-scale installation, reflecting Kettle's own practice priorities.

The undergraduate curriculum similarly reflects her pedagogical philosophy, with students encouraged to explore stitch as drawing from early stages, work across scales, and engage with narrative and social themes. Technical workshops are integrated with critical studies, ensuring students develop sophisticated understanding of textiles' cultural and historical contexts alongside making skills.

### Influence on National Textile Education

Kettle's advocacy work extends beyond Manchester to influence textile education nationally. Through conference presentations, external examining roles, and advisory positions, she has promoted contemporary approaches to textile education that challenge craft/art divisions. Her Royal Designer for Industry status provides authority for these interventions, and her work is regularly cited in validations and revalidations of textile programmes across UK universities.

### Research Excellence Framework (REF) Submissions

Kettle's practice-based research has featured in Manchester Metropolitan University's Research Excellence Framework submissions, helping establish precedents for how textile practice contributes to the research economy. Her work demonstrates impact beyond academia through exhibitions, public engagement, and social outcomes, providing models for colleagues articulating the value of practice research.

### Advocacy for Textile Arts

Kettle regularly speaks at policy forums about the importance of maintaining textile education within art schools, particularly amidst sector pressures towards STEM subjects and concerns about workshop costs. She articulates textiles' unique contributions to creative education, including tactile learning, material thinking, and connections to cultural heritage. Her arguments have influenced institutional decisions about resource allocation and programme development.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Studio Teaching Philosophy

Kettle's studio teaching emphasises direct engagement with materials and processes. She encourages students to work intuitively, making decisions through the making process rather than extensive pre-planning. This approach develops confidence and material fluency, enabling students to discover possibilities through experimentation. She models this approach in her own practice, often beginning work without predetermined outcomes, allowing narratives and forms to emerge through the stitching process.

### Technical Demonstration and Skill Development

Whilst emphasising conceptual development, Kettle maintains that technical proficiency is fundamental. She provides rigorous technical instruction in machine embroidery, encouraging students to master their equipment thoroughly. Her demonstrations reveal how technical understanding enables artistic freedom rather than constraining it. She teaches troubleshooting and problem-solving, developing students' independence and resilience when working with unpredictable textile materials.

### Critical Context and Theoretical Frameworks

Kettle integrates critical and historical studies throughout studio teaching, ensuring students understand their practice within broader cultural contexts. She introduces students to historical embroidery traditions, contemporary textile artists, and relevant fine art practices. Critical discussions address issues including gendered perceptions of textiles, craft/art hierarchies, and textiles' roles in different cultures. This contextual knowledge enables students to position their work within contemporary debates and articulate their practice conceptually.

### Individual Tutorial Approach

In individual tutorials, Kettle employs a questioning methodology, prompting students to articulate their intentions and reflect on their making decisions. She encourages students to identify what interests them most about emerging work, helping them recognise and develop their distinctive concerns. This reflective practice develops students' critical awareness and ability to evaluate their own work, essential skills for professional practice.

### Collaborative and Participatory Projects

Kettle incorporates collaborative projects within teaching, reflecting her professional practice methodology. Students might work collectively on large-scale pieces or develop participatory projects with external communities. These experiences develop skills in negotiation, workshop facilitation, and working with diverse participants. They also reveal how textile practice can facilitate dialogue and build relationships, extending students' understanding of their potential professional roles.

### Professional Practice Integration

Kettle ensures students understand professional contexts for textile practice, including exhibition opportunities, commissioning processes, funding applications, and portfolio presentation. She facilitates visits to galleries, studios, and workshops, helping students understand the infrastructure supporting professional textile practice. Guest practitioners share insights into sustaining practices, diversifying income, and navigating the contemporary art world.

### Research Skills Development

Drawing on her own practice-based research experience, Kettle teaches students to document their practice systematically, reflect on making processes, and articulate the knowledge generated through making. She introduces methodologies including visual research, material investigations, and reflective writing. These skills prove valuable whether students progress to postgraduate study or professional practice, enabling them to evaluate and communicate their work effectively.

## Challenges and Debates

### Craft/Art Distinctions

Kettle's practice and pedagogy engage directly with ongoing debates about craft and art distinctions. Whilst some argue for embracing textile practice's craft identity, Kettle advocates for textiles' positioning within contemporary art, arguing this provides greater opportunities and institutional support. This position has generated productive debates within textile communities about identity, hierarchies, and professional contexts.

### Accessibility and Specialisation

Kettle's emphasis on technical skill and specialised equipment raises questions about accessibility in textile education. Whilst advocating for maintaining well-resourced textile facilities, she also demonstrates how domestic equipment can produce sophisticated work, attempting to balance specialisation with accessibility. This tension remains ongoing in textile education, particularly amidst funding pressures.

### Traditional vs. Contemporary Approaches

Kettle's contemporary approach sometimes encounters resistance from those valuing traditional embroidery techniques and aesthetics. She navigates this by articulating respect for historical practices whilst arguing for textiles' evolution to address contemporary concerns. Her work demonstrates continuity and rupture, maintaining technical connections to traditions whilst transforming their aesthetic and conceptual frameworks.

## Contemporary Relevance and Future Directions

### Sustainability and Textile Practice

Increasingly, Kettle addresses sustainability within her teaching, reflecting broader concerns about textile industries' environmental impacts. She encourages students to consider material sources, waste reduction, and the slow making inherent to hand and machine stitching as counter-narratives to fast fashion. This environmental consciousness positions textile education as contributing to urgent contemporary debates.

### Digital Technologies

Whilst primarily working with conventional sewing machines, Kettle engages with digital embroidery technologies, encouraging students to understand relationships between hand, domestic, and industrial processes. She advocates for digital technologies expanding rather than replacing traditional skills, maintaining that conceptual concerns should drive technical choices.

### Interdisciplinary Practice

Kettle increasingly emphasises interdisciplinary approaches, encouraging collaborations with artists working in other media, scientists, and community organisations. This interdisciplinarity reflects contemporary art school structures and prepares students for collaborative professional contexts. Her "Thread Bearing Witness" project exemplifies this interdisciplinary model, incorporating oral history, social research, and community development alongside textile making.

### Global Perspectives

Through international exhibitions and collaborative projects, Kettle brings global perspectives into UK textile education. She introduces students to textile traditions from diverse cultures, encouraging respectful engagement with international practices. This global awareness prepares students for internationalised creative industries and challenges Eurocentric art historical narratives.
