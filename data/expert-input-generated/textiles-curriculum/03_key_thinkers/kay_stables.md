# Kay Stables

**Full Name:** Kay Stables
**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Design and Technology Education, Goldsmiths, University of London
**Known For:** Pioneering research in design cognition, assessment in Design and Technology, creativity in textiles education, and conceptual frameworks for understanding designerly thinking

---

## Biography and Career History

Kay Stables established herself as one of the most influential figures in Design and Technology education in the UK, with particular impact on textiles education and pedagogy. Her academic career was primarily based at Goldsmiths, University of London, where she served as Professor of Design and Technology Education and subsequently as Emeritus Professor.

Stables began her academic work during a crucial period in the development of the National Curriculum for Design and Technology, which brought together previously separate craft subjects including textiles, resistant materials, food technology, and graphic products. Her research emerged at a time when textiles education was transitioning from predominantly skills-based 'needlework' to a design-led, technologically informed discipline.

Throughout her career at Goldsmiths, Stables supervised numerous doctoral students and developed influential research programmes funded by bodies including the Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council (EPSRC), the Design and Technology Association (DATA), and the Technology Education Research Unit (TERU). Her work has been particularly significant in establishing research-based foundations for teaching practice in textiles and broader Design and Technology education.

Stables has worked extensively with curriculum and assessment bodies, contributing to policy development and teacher education. Her influence extends beyond the UK, with international recognition for her research methodologies and conceptual frameworks.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. Design-and-Make Activities and Cognitive Processes

Stables developed comprehensive frameworks for understanding how learners engage cognitively with design-and-make activities in textiles. Her research identified that textiles design involves distinctive cognitive processes compared to other design disciplines, including haptic reasoning (thinking through touch and material manipulation), aesthetic decision-making integrated with technical problem-solving, and iterative development through making.

Her work demonstrated that in textiles, the material itself serves as a cognitive tool, with students thinking *through* fabric manipulation rather than merely applying pre-formed ideas to materials. This challenged traditional models of design education that separated planning from making phases.

### 2. The Designerly Thinking Framework

Building on Nigel Cross's concept of 'designerly ways of knowing', Stables articulated how designerly thinking manifests specifically in textiles education. She identified key characteristics including: solution-focused thinking, iterative problem-solving, the integration of aesthetic and functional considerations, and the use of modelling (through sampling, toiles, and prototyping) as a thinking tool.

Her research demonstrated that textiles learners develop sophisticated design cognition through practical engagement, challenging hierarchies that positioned theoretical knowledge above practical skill. This work was particularly important in validating textiles as an intellectually rigorous discipline within Design and Technology.

### 3. The Capability-Competence Framework

Stables is particularly renowned for her work on the distinction between capability and competence in Design and Technology education. She argued that competence refers to the ability to perform specific, defined tasks with established procedures, whilst capability encompasses the ability to operate in uncertain situations, to innovate, and to apply knowledge flexibly in novel contexts.

This framework had profound implications for textiles education, arguing that curricula should develop capability rather than merely training competence in specific techniques. It influenced how teachers approached project work, emphasising open-ended problems and authentic design contexts rather than prescriptive 'making by numbers' approaches.

### 4. Assessment and Design Capability

Stables conducted extensive research on assessment practices in Design and Technology, with specific focus on textiles. She critiqued assessment systems that privileged easily measurable competences over holistic design capability, arguing that textiles assessment needed to recognise the integrated nature of designing and making.

Her research identified problems with atomised assessment criteria that fragmented the design process and led to 'teaching to the specification' rather than developing genuine design capability. She advocated for portfolio-based assessment that captured iterative development and for assessment literacy among teachers to make holistic judgements about design quality.

### 5. The Role of Modelling and Prototyping

Stables researched extensively on how modelling and prototyping function in textiles design education. She identified that sampling in textiles serves multiple purposes: testing technical feasibility, exploring aesthetic possibilities, and serving as an external representation that advances thinking. Her work demonstrated that students who engaged in extensive sampling developed more sophisticated design outcomes and demonstrated deeper understanding.

This research challenged efficiency-driven approaches to textiles education that minimised sampling time in favour of 'final piece' production, showing that time spent on exploration and testing was pedagogically crucial rather than merely preparatory.

### 6. Creativity in Constrained Contexts

Stables researched how creativity operates within the constraints typical of school textiles projects, including limited time, resources, and student skill levels. Her work demonstrated that constraints, when appropriately structured, can stimulate rather than inhibit creativity, providing frameworks within which students can innovate.

She developed guidance for teachers on creating 'enabling constraints' - limitations that provide sufficient structure to support learning whilst allowing space for individual creative responses. This work was particularly influential in helping teachers balance examination requirements with creative pedagogical aims.

### 7. Gender and Design and Technology Education

Stables contributed to research examining gender participation and achievement patterns in Design and Technology, with particular attention to textiles. Her work examined how the subject's evolution from gender-specific craft subjects to a unified Design and Technology curriculum affected both boys' and girls' engagement and achievement.

She researched how textiles could be positioned to appeal to diverse student interests beyond traditional gender stereotypes, whilst recognising that historical associations between textiles and femininity continued to influence subject choices and perceptions.

### 8. Technology in Textiles Education

Stables researched the integration of new technologies in textiles education, including CAD/CAM systems, digital textile printing, and computerised embroidery. Her work examined how these technologies transformed both processes and possibilities in textiles design, whilst also considering how traditional hand skills remained relevant.

She argued for a both/and rather than either/or approach, where digital technologies extended rather than replaced traditional making skills, and where students developed understanding of how different technologies afforded different design possibilities.

### 9. Authentic Design Contexts and Client-Based Projects

Stables researched the impact of authentic design contexts in textiles education, including real client projects and design-for-manufacture scenarios. Her work demonstrated that authenticity increased student motivation and led to more sophisticated design thinking, as students engaged with genuine constraints and user needs rather than artificial school-based scenarios.

She developed frameworks for teachers to create appropriately authentic contexts within school limitations, balancing genuine challenge with achievable outcomes for learners at different stages.

### 10. Teacher Professional Development and Subject Knowledge

Stables conducted research on teacher subject knowledge in Design and Technology, examining what teachers need to know to teach effectively across the subject's breadth. Her work on the 'subject knowledge audit' provided frameworks for trainee and practising teachers to identify development needs.

She researched the particular challenges for textiles specialists teaching broader Design and Technology, and for non-specialists teaching textiles, contributing to professional development programmes that recognised the distinctive pedagogical content knowledge required for effective textiles teaching.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Stables influenced multiple iterations of the National Curriculum for Design and Technology in England. Her research on design cognition and capability informed curriculum aims that emphasised developing design thinking rather than merely acquiring making skills. The 2000 and 2007 National Curriculum iterations particularly reflected her emphasis on iterative design processes and evaluation.

Her work helped establish the legitimacy of textiles within the unified Design and Technology curriculum, arguing that textiles involved equally sophisticated design thinking to other material areas, challenging perceptions of textiles as a 'soft' or less rigorous option.

### Assessment and Qualifications

Stables contributed to examination board consultations and Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA, later Ofqual) reviews of GCSE and A-level Design and Technology. Her research critiquing atomised mark schemes influenced moves toward more holistic assessment approaches in textiles qualifications.

Her work on portfolio assessment helped establish coursework/controlled assessment as the dominant assessment mode in Design and Technology, arguing that design processes could not be adequately assessed through written examinations alone. This had particular significance for textiles, where physical outcomes and making quality were integral to design capability.

### Teacher Education and Professional Standards

Stables' research informed Initial Teacher Education (ITE) requirements for Design and Technology teachers. Her subject knowledge audit frameworks were adopted by many teacher education programmes to structure trainee development. Her work influenced the Teacher Training Agency's (TTA, later Training and Development Agency for Schools) specifications for Design and Technology teacher training.

Her emphasis on pedagogical content knowledge - understanding not just textiles techniques but how to teach them effectively - shaped the structure of PGCE courses in Design and Technology with textiles specialism.

### Policy Reports and Consultations

Stables contributed to various policy consultations including:
- Reviews of 14-19 Design and Technology pathways
- Consultations on assessment without levels following National Curriculum reforms
- Reviews of practical education and vocational qualifications
- Reports to the Department for Education on standards and curriculum breadth

Her evidence-based approach, grounded in classroom research, gave credibility to arguments for maintaining practical design and make activities in an increasingly knowledge-focused curriculum environment.

## Pedagogical Approach and Teaching Philosophy

### Design-Centred Pedagogy

Stables advocated for pedagogy that placed design activity at the centre of textiles education, rather than technical skill development. She argued that techniques should be learned in context, when needed for design purposes, rather than through decontextualised skills exercises.

This approach positioned the teacher as a facilitator of design learning, supporting students in developing their own solutions rather than demonstrating 'correct' techniques to be replicated. It required teachers to have strong subject knowledge to respond flexibly to diverse student needs as they emerged during project work.

### Iterative and Reflective Practice

Stables emphasised the importance of iteration in design learning, arguing that students needed opportunities to test, evaluate, and refine ideas through multiple cycles. Her pedagogy encouraged extensive use of sampling, experimentation, and prototyping rather than linear progression from brief to final outcome.

She advocated for building reflection points into projects, where students paused making to evaluate progress, consider alternatives, and make conscious decisions about development directions. This reflective practice developed metacognitive awareness of design processes.

### Authentic Contexts and User-Centred Design

Stables promoted project briefs based on authentic design contexts with real users and genuine constraints. She argued that authenticity increased engagement and developed transferable design capability, as students learned to consider user needs, market contexts, and realistic production constraints.

Her approach involved collaborations with external clients where feasible, and careful simulation of authentic conditions where external links were not possible. This required teachers to develop networks beyond school and to structure projects that balanced authenticity with educational accessibility.

### Dialogic Teaching and Design Critique

Stables emphasised the importance of dialogue in design learning, both teacher-student dialogue and peer critique. She researched how questioning could scaffold design thinking, identifying productive question types that opened up possibilities rather than closing down thinking toward teacher-approved solutions.

Her approach to design critique involved establishing classroom cultures where critical discussion was supportive and constructive, and where students learned to articulate design decisions and evaluate quality using appropriate technical and aesthetic vocabulary.

### Inclusive Practice

Stables advocated for inclusive approaches that recognised diverse strengths and learning preferences in textiles education. Her research on capability challenged deficit models that positioned students as 'unable', instead focusing on what students could achieve with appropriate support and suitable contexts.

She argued for differentiation by outcome within open-ended projects rather than differentiated tasks, allowing all students access to complex design challenges whilst producing outcomes appropriate to their current capability.

## Legacy and Continuing Influence

Kay Stables' work established textiles as a rigorous, intellectually demanding discipline within Design and Technology education. Her research provided evidence-based foundations for pedagogical approaches that remain influential in current practice.

The capability-competence framework continues to inform curriculum planning and assessment design in textiles education. Teachers and curriculum leaders regularly reference this distinction when critiquing overly prescriptive schemes of work or assessment criteria that fragment design processes.

Her research on design cognition influenced subsequent researchers including those examining situated cognition, embodied learning, and material thinking in textiles. Her methodologies for studying design processes, including think-aloud protocols and portfolio analysis, established standards for design education research.

Stables' work on assessment particularly remains relevant as the sector continues to debate appropriate ways to measure design capability. Her critiques of atomised mark schemes and advocacy for holistic judgement inform ongoing discussions about assessment reform.

Her influence extends through former doctoral students and colleagues who hold positions in universities and professional organisations, continuing to develop ideas she pioneered. The Technology Education Research Unit at Goldsmiths, with which she was closely associated, continues as a centre for design education research.
