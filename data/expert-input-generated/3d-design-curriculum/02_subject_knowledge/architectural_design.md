# Architectural and Environmental Design

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Architectural and Environmental Design represents a crucial strand within UK 3D Design education, bridging the gap between creative expression, technical understanding, and spatial problem-solving. This area encompasses the design of built environments, interior spaces, landscape features, and sustainable structures that respond to human needs and environmental contexts.

Within the National Curriculum for Design and Technology (Key Stages 3 and 4), architectural and environmental design addresses requirements for pupils to "develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world" (DfE, 2013). Richard Kimbell's seminal work on design capability emphasises the importance of "modelling ideas in the mind and in reality" (Kimbell et al., 1991), which is particularly relevant to architectural education where spatial reasoning and three-dimensional visualisation are paramount.

David Barlex has argued consistently for the importance of authentic design contexts, noting that architectural problems provide "rich, real-world challenges that require pupils to consider multiple stakeholders and complex constraints" (Barlex, 2007). This positions architectural and environmental design as an ideal vehicle for developing higher-order thinking skills within 3D Design curricula.

## Key Stage 3: Foundation Knowledge and Skills

### Spatial Understanding and Scale

At Key Stage 3, pupils should develop fundamental understanding of scale, proportion, and spatial relationships. Teachers should introduce the concept of orthographic projection (plans, elevations, and sections) as the universal language of architectural communication. Practical activities might include measuring existing spaces, creating scale models at 1:50 or 1:100, and using simple CAD software such as SketchUp to model room layouts.

Nigel Cross's research into "designerly ways of knowing" (Cross, 2006) emphasises that designers think through making. In architectural education, this translates to encouraging pupils to test spatial concepts through physical card modelling, foam-board construction, and digital modelling iteratively.

### Materials and Construction Principles

Pupils should explore how materials behave structurally, examining concepts such as compression, tension, and stability through practical investigations. Activities might include building paper structures that support weight, investigating how corrugation increases strength, or exploring how triangulation provides rigidity in frameworks.

The study of vernacular architecture—buildings constructed using local materials and traditional methods—provides excellent opportunities to understand material properties contextually. Case studies might include dry stone walls in the Yorkshire Dales, timber-framed buildings in the Weald of Kent, or slate-roofed cottages in Wales.

### Environmental Awareness

Key Stage 3 establishes foundational understanding of how buildings interact with their environment. Pupils should investigate passive solar design principles, natural ventilation, thermal mass, and orientation. Simple experiments using data loggers to measure temperature in different locations or creating solar path diagrams for specific sites help develop this understanding practically.

## Key Stage 4: Development and Application

### GCSE Specifications and Assessment

All major UK exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC) include architectural and environmental design within their 3D Design GCSE specifications. The typical assessment structure comprises 50% coursework (Non-Exam Assessment or NEA) and 50% written examination.

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552)** requires students to demonstrate "in-depth knowledge of a range of materials" and "how products can be designed and made to meet the needs of users and clients." The specification explicitly references sustainable design and environmental considerations.

**Edexcel GCSE Design and Technology (1DT0)** organises content around core and specialist technical principles, with architectural contexts appearing throughout. Students must understand "structural integrity and how to reinforce and strengthen a 3D form" and "contemporary and potential future design issues."

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310)** emphasises iterative design and the importance of prototyping. The specification states students should be able to "design and make prototypes that are fit for purpose" and "analyse the work of past and present professionals."

**WJEC GCSE Design and Technology (C880QS)** includes specific content on architectural design, requiring knowledge of "structures, frameworks, and reinforcement" and "the impact of forces and stresses on products and structures."

### Design Process and Methodology

At GCSE level, architectural projects should follow a structured design methodology that Kimbell describes as "interaction between mind and hand" (Kimbell, 2000). This typically includes:

1. **Contextual research and site analysis**: Understanding user needs, site constraints, orientation, access, existing buildings, and planning regulations
2. **Design development**: Generating multiple concept solutions through sketching, mood boards, and precedent studies
3. **Modelling and testing**: Creating developmental models at appropriate scales to test spatial quality, structural integrity, and environmental performance
4. **Technical refinement**: Producing detailed drawings, specifications, and construction details
5. **Final realisation**: Creating presentation models or scaled prototypes with appropriate finish quality

David Barlex's research emphasises that pupils benefit from "structured frameworks that scaffold their design thinking without constraining creativity" (Barlex & Trebell, 2008). In architectural contexts, this might involve providing site surveys, client briefs, and building regulations guidance whilst allowing freedom in design response.

## Key Stage 5: Advanced Study and Specialisation

### A-Level Specifications

A-Level Design and Technology (3D Design) provides opportunities for sustained architectural investigation. Students typically complete an extended design project (approximately 40-60 hours) alongside examined components assessing technical knowledge and design analysis.

**AQA A-Level Design and Technology: Product Design (7552)** requires understanding of "factors that influence design decisions" including "social, cultural, economic, and environmental factors." Students must demonstrate sophisticated understanding of materials, manufacturing processes, and structural principles.

**Edexcel A-Level Design and Technology: Product Design (9DT0)** expects students to "investigate the work of others" including architects and environmental designers. The specification includes content on "principles of design science" relevant to architectural applications.

**OCR A-Level Design and Technology (H406)** offers optional specialist content in architectural design, including "how designers develop products to meet specific criteria" and "anthropometric and ergonomic principles."

**WJEC A-Level Design and Technology (A660QS)** requires "critical analysis of design work by others" and expects students to demonstrate "sophisticated making skills" in their project work.

### Architectural Theory and Critical Analysis

A-Level students should engage with architectural theory and develop critical vocabulary. Key concepts include:

**Functionalism**: The principle that form should follow function, exemplified in the work of modernist architects. Case studies might include Le Corbusier's Villa Savoye or the Bauhaus school building by Walter Gropius.

**Contextual design**: How buildings respond to their physical and cultural context. Contemporary UK examples include the Stirling Prize-winning buildings such as Haworth Tompkins' Everyman Theatre in Liverpool or Zaha Hadid's London Aquatics Centre.

**Sustainable architecture**: Integration of environmental principles including passive design, renewable energy, and life-cycle thinking. Examples include BedZED (Beddington Zero Energy Development) by Bill Dunster Architects or the Hockerton Housing Project in Nottinghamshire.

**Social responsibility**: How architecture serves communities and addresses social needs. Projects such as Alison Brooks Architects' Newhall Be development in Harlow demonstrate social housing excellence.

Nigel Cross's work on "design thinking" emphasises that expert designers work simultaneously across multiple levels of abstraction (Cross, 2004). A-Level architectural students should develop this capability, working from conceptual diagrams through to construction details seamlessly.

### Technical Knowledge and Building Science

Advanced students require sophisticated understanding of:

**Structural systems**: Portal frames, shell structures, tension structures, and geodesic domes. Practical investigations might involve testing model structures to failure or using finite element analysis software.

**Environmental control**: Detailed knowledge of insulation values (U-values), thermal bridging, air-tightness, and Building Regulations Part L (Conservation of Fuel and Power). Calculations using SAP (Standard Assessment Procedure) methodologies provide mathematical rigour.

**Construction technology**: Understanding of foundation types, wall construction (cavity walls, timber frame, SIPs panels), roof structures, and weatherproofing. Site visits to construction projects provide invaluable real-world learning.

**Building regulations and standards**: Awareness of UK Building Regulations, particularly Parts A (Structure), B (Fire Safety), F (Ventilation), K (Protection from Falling), and M (Access to and Use of Buildings).

## Research Evidence and Pedagogical Approaches

### Design Capability and Spatial Reasoning

Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research established that design capability comprises interconnected "domains of interaction between the conceptual and the practical" (Kimbell et al., 1991). In architectural education, this manifests as iterative movement between drawing, modelling, and evaluation.

Research by Kay Stables and Kimbell (1998) demonstrated that pupils learn most effectively when engaging with "holistic design tasks that require integration of knowledge from multiple domains." Architectural projects naturally provide such integration, combining mathematics (scale, proportion, calculation), science (structures, materials, environmental physics), art (aesthetics, composition), and humanities (social context, cultural meaning).

### Making and Modelling Pedagogy

David Barlex's research emphasises the importance of "modelling as a tool for thinking" (Barlex, 2005). In architectural contexts, this includes:

**Sketch models**: Quick, low-fidelity models using paper, card, or foam to explore massing and spatial arrangement
**Development models**: More refined models testing specific aspects such as structural system or environmental strategy
**Presentation models**: High-quality models communicating design intent to clients or examiners
**Working drawings**: Technical drawings specifying dimensions, materials, and construction methods

Teachers should provide access to appropriate modelling materials including mounting board, foam board, balsa wood, acrylic, and modelling clay. Digital tools such as SketchUp, Revit, or AutoCAD extend modelling capabilities but should complement rather than replace physical making.

### Assessment for Learning in Design

Kimbell's later work on e-assessment (Kimbell, 2012) demonstrated that design portfolios should capture the "journey of development" rather than simply presenting finished outcomes. In architectural projects, this means documenting:

- Initial site analysis and research
- Multiple concept explorations
- Testing and evaluation of options
- Iterative refinement based on feedback
- Technical development and detailing
- Final presentation with critical reflection

Assessment criteria typically address: innovation and creativity, fitness for purpose, technical understanding, quality of realisation, and communication clarity.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Workshop Resources and Safety

Architectural modelling requires specific workshop resources:

**Hand tools**: Metal rulers, cutting mats, scalpels (safety knives), compasses, set squares, and circle cutters
**Power tools**: Hot wire foam cutters, laser cutters (where available), and belt sanders for shaping materials
**Drawing equipment**: Drawing boards, parallel motion equipment (or adjustable set squares), scale rules, and templates
**Digital resources**: Computers with CAD software, 3D printers for model components, and vinyl cutters for detail work

Safety considerations include proper training in knife use (always cut away from the body, use appropriate pressure), supervision of hot wire cutters (fumes, burns), and laser cutter operation (must be supervised, appropriate extraction required). Risk assessments must follow CLEAPSS guidance for design technology facilities.

### Developing Spatial Literacy

Many pupils struggle initially with spatial visualisation and orthographic projection. Effective teaching strategies include:

**Physical manipulation**: Using card nets that fold into 3D forms, or examining real objects alongside their technical drawings
**Isometric sketching**: Teaching systematic approaches to drawing 3D forms on isometric grids before progressing to freehand sketching
**Digital tools**: Using SketchUp's "Scenes" feature to generate plan, elevation, and perspective views of the same model
**Drawing from observation**: Regular practice sketching existing buildings, developing understanding of perspective and proportion

David Barlex notes that pupils require "explicit teaching of technical conventions rather than assuming they will acquire these through osmosis" (Barlex & Steeg, 2017). This means dedicating specific lesson sequences to drawing techniques, not merely expecting pupils to produce technical drawings within projects.

### Structuring Architectural Projects

Effective architectural projects balance freedom with structure. Typical project frameworks might include:

**Year 9 (KS3)**: Small-scale architecture such as bus shelters, garden studios, or beach huts (single space, manageable complexity, clear function)
**Year 10 (GCSE)**: Residential extensions or small pavilion buildings (multiple spaces, structural considerations, user requirements)
**Year 11 (GCSE)**: Complete small buildings such as community facilities, small retail units, or visitor centres (complex programme, site constraints, multiple stakeholders)
**Year 12-13 (A-Level)**: Substantial buildings with social purpose—schools, community centres, arts venues, or affordable housing (architectural ambition, theoretical position, technical sophistication)

Each project should include real or realistic constraints: actual sites (pupils could measure local sites), genuine building regulations, realistic budgets (even if notional), and authentic user needs determined through research.

### Cross-Curricular Opportunities

Architectural and environmental design provides natural connections across the curriculum:

**Mathematics**: Scale calculations, area and volume, ratio and proportion, trigonometry for roof pitches, and structural calculations
**Science**: Material properties, forces and structures, thermal physics, and environmental science
**Geography**: Climate analysis, site topography, sustainable development, and urbanisation
**History**: Evolution of architectural styles, vernacular traditions, and technological development
**Art**: Composition, form and space, drawing techniques, and aesthetic theory

Nigel Cross's concept of "designerly ways of knowing" (Cross, 2006) suggests that design represents a distinct intellectual domain alongside scientific and humanistic ways of knowing. Architectural projects exemplify this, requiring synthetic thinking that integrates knowledge from multiple disciplines into coherent proposals.

## Contemporary Issues and Future Directions

### Sustainability and Climate Emergency

The climate emergency makes environmental performance central to contemporary architectural education. Pupils should understand:

**Embodied carbon**: The carbon emissions associated with manufacturing and transporting building materials
**Operational carbon**: Emissions from heating, cooling, and powering buildings during use
**Whole-life carbon**: Total carbon impact including construction, operation, and eventual demolition
**Circular economy principles**: Designing for disassembly, material reuse, and adaptability

The UK Green Building Council provides educational resources and case studies demonstrating net-zero carbon buildings. Projects such as the Enterprise Centre at the University of East Anglia (Architype architects) provide exemplars of low-energy design appropriate for student analysis.

### Digital Design Technologies

Building Information Modelling (BIM) represents industry standard practice in UK architecture. While full BIM workflows exceed GCSE/A-Level requirements, introducing parametric thinking and digital modelling principles prepares students for further study. Free educational licences for software such as Revit, ArchiCAD, or SketchUp Pro enable exploration of digital design processes.

However, David Barlex cautions against "technology for technology's sake," arguing that digital tools should enhance rather than replace fundamental design thinking (Barlex, 2003). Physical modelling remains essential for developing spatial intuition and material understanding.

### Social and Community Architecture

Contemporary practice increasingly emphasises participatory design and community engagement. Student projects might incorporate user consultation, community needs analysis, or participatory design workshops. This addresses the National Curriculum requirement to understand "the wider influences on design and technology" including social and cultural contexts.

Organisations such as Architecture 00, muf architecture/art, and Assemble demonstrate socially engaged practice suitable for case study analysis. The Scottish Government's "Place Standard" tool provides frameworks for community-led design thinking applicable to student projects.

## Conclusion and Further Development

Architectural and environmental design within UK 3D Design education provides unparalleled opportunities for developing spatial intelligence, technical knowledge, creative thinking, and social awareness. Richard Kimbell's vision of design capability as "the interaction of mind and hand in purposeful activity" (Kimbell, 2000) finds full expression in architectural projects where conceptual ambition meets material reality.

Teachers should develop progression pathways that build sophistication gradually: from understanding basic spatial relationships at KS3, through structured problem-solving at GCSE, to theoretically informed design research at A-Level. David Barlex's emphasis on authentic contexts and real constraints ensures projects remain meaningful and engaging.

Nigel Cross reminds us that "everyone can—and does—design" (Cross, 2011), but that excellent design requires developed capability. Architectural and environmental design provides the rich, challenging contexts through which pupils develop this capability, preparing them not only for potential careers in the built environment professions but for informed participation in shaping the sustainable, socially responsive architecture our future requires.
