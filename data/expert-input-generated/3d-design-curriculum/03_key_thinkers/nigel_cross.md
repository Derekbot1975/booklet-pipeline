# Nigel Cross

**Full Name:** Nigel Cross

**Current Role:** Emeritus Professor of Design Studies, The Open University

**Known For:** Pioneer of design research methodology, design cognition studies, "Designerly Ways of Knowing" concept, establishing design as a legitimate academic discipline, protocol analysis in design research

---

## Biography and Career History

Nigel Cross is one of the most influential figures in design research and education globally, with particular significance for UK 3D Design curriculum development. He joined The Open University in 1970 as one of the founding members of the Design Discipline, where he spent his entire academic career until retirement, becoming Professor of Design Studies and eventually Emeritus Professor.

Cross's academic background spans mechanical engineering and industrial design, providing him with both technical rigour and creative insight. His career at The Open University coincided with a critical period in British design education when design was transitioning from craft-based apprenticeship models to academic discipline status. He was instrumental in developing The Open University's pioneering distance-learning courses in design, including the groundbreaking course T263 'Design: Principles and Practice' (later updated as T211), which influenced thousands of design students and teachers across the UK.

Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Cross worked to establish design research as a rigorous academic field, distinguishing it from both scientific and humanities-based research traditions. He served as editor of the influential journal *Design Studies* from 1979 to 2020, shaping the discourse of design research internationally for over four decades. His editorial leadership transformed Design Studies into the premier academic journal in the field.

Cross was a founding member and Fellow of the Design Research Society (DRS), established in 1966, and has been central to its development as the key professional body for design researchers in the UK and beyond. He has supervised numerous doctoral students who have themselves become influential design educators and researchers, extending his impact across generations.

His work has been recognised internationally with honorary doctorates and awards, including the Design Research Society's Lifetime Achievement Award. His influence extends beyond the UK, with his publications translated into multiple languages and his methodological approaches adopted in design schools worldwide.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Designerly Ways of Knowing

Cross's most significant contribution to design education is the concept of "Designerly Ways of Knowing," first articulated in his seminal 1982 article "Designerly Ways of Knowing" in *Design Studies*. This concept establishes design as a distinct intellectual culture alongside the sciences and humanities, arguing that design has its own legitimate forms of knowledge, thinking, and practice.

Cross identified three fundamental cultures of human knowledge: sciences (concerned with what is), humanities (concerned with human experience and meaning), and design (concerned with what ought to be). He argued that design represents a third culture of knowledge that cannot be reduced to either scientific or humanities-based thinking. This framework provided crucial intellectual justification for design's place in academic institutions and curriculum frameworks.

The concept encompasses several key characteristics of design thinking: problem-focused rather than subject-focused approaches; solution-led rather than problem-led strategies; constructive rather than analytical methods; and the use of codes and non-verbal modelling. These characteristics distinguish design cognition from scientific or literary thinking and have profound implications for how 3D Design is taught in schools.

For UK 3D Design education, this concept legitimised the teaching of design as a distinct subject with its own epistemology, not merely applied art or simplified engineering. It provided theoretical grounding for design's inclusion in the National Curriculum and for GCSE and A-Level specifications that assess design-specific capabilities.

### 2. Design as a Discipline

Building on "Designerly Ways of Knowing," Cross developed a comprehensive argument for design as an academic discipline in its own right. In his 2001 article "Designerly Ways of Knowing: Design Discipline Versus Design Science" and his 2006 book *Designerly Ways of Knowing*, he distinguished between design discipline (the practices, knowledge, and culture of designers) and design science (the systematic study of design).

Cross argued that design discipline encompasses: design epistemology (what designers know), design praxiology (what designers do), and design phenomenology (what designers experience). This tripartite structure provides a framework for curriculum design, ensuring that 3D Design education addresses knowledge, skills, and reflective practice.

He emphasised that design has its own "things to know, ways of knowing them, and ways of finding out about them." This includes material culture knowledge, tacit knowledge developed through making, and designerly ways of problem-solving. For 3D Design teachers, this framework validates the importance of workshop practice, iterative making, and material experimentation as legitimate forms of knowledge generation, not merely skill acquisition.

### 3. Design Cognition and Protocol Analysis

Cross pioneered the use of protocol analysis—a research method borrowed from cognitive psychology—to study how designers think and work. His empirical studies, beginning in the 1980s and continuing through his career, involved observing and recording designers at work, analysing their verbal protocols (think-aloud processes), and identifying patterns in design cognition.

His major studies, documented in publications including *Analysing Design Activity* (1996, with colleagues Christiaans and Dorst) and *Design Thinking: Understanding How Designers Think and Work* (2011), revealed that expert designers employ distinctive cognitive strategies. These include: solution-focused thinking (proposing solutions early to frame problems), primary generators (key concepts that drive design development), satisficing rather than optimising (finding satisfactory solutions within constraints), and co-evolution of problem and solution spaces.

These findings have direct pedagogical implications. Cross demonstrated that design expertise involves domain-specific cognitive strategies that can be taught. His work showed that novice designers tend to get stuck in analysis or pursue perfect solutions, while experts work through iterative cycles of proposing, testing, and refining. This evidence base supports teaching approaches that emphasise rapid ideation, prototyping, and iterative development—core principles in contemporary GCSE and A-Level 3D Design specifications.

Cross's protocol studies also revealed the importance of sketching, modelling, and external representation in design thinking. His research showed that designers think through drawing and making, not just before it. This validates pedagogical approaches that integrate practical work with conceptual development rather than treating making as merely executing pre-formed ideas.

### 4. The Reflective Practitioner in Design

Drawing on Donald Schön's concept of the "reflective practitioner," Cross developed this framework specifically for design contexts. In his analysis, design involves "reflection-in-action"—thinking while doing—and "reflection-on-action"—analysing completed work. However, Cross extended this by identifying design-specific forms of reflection.

He distinguished between reflection through action (using making and modelling as thinking tools) and conversation with materials (the dialogue between designer intentions and material possibilities). This conceptualisation helps explain why practical work is epistemologically central to design education, not supplementary.

For 3D Design education, this framework supports assessment criteria that value process documentation, iterative development, and reflective analysis. It justifies portfolio-based assessment that captures the evolution of design thinking, not just finished products. Cross's work provides theoretical grounding for the AQA, Pearson, and OCR specifications' emphasis on design journals, annotation, and evaluative reflection.

### 5. Design Methods and Systematic Design

While Cross critiqued overly rigid design methodology movements of the 1960s-70s, he recognised the value of structured approaches for novice designers and complex problems. His work on design methods, documented in *Engineering Design Methods* (1989, multiple editions through 2008) and numerous articles, identified useful frameworks without prescribing inflexible processes.

Cross examined methods including morphological analysis, systematic search, quality function deployment, and value engineering, evaluating their appropriate applications. He distinguished between descriptive models (how designers actually work) and prescriptive models (structured approaches for specific contexts), helping educators understand when to teach systematic methods and when to encourage intuitive exploration.

His analysis showed that expert designers flexibly apply and adapt methods rather than following them rigidly. This supports teaching approaches that introduce methods as tools in a designer's repertoire rather than mandatory procedures, encouraging students to develop metacognitive awareness about selecting appropriate strategies.

### 6. Design Ability and Expertise

Cross's research into design expertise, synthesised in *Design Thinking* (2011) and numerous articles, identified specific capabilities that characterise skilled designers. These include: the ability to resolve ill-defined problems, adopting solution-focused cognitive strategies, employing abductive reasoning (inference to best explanation), and using non-verbal thinking through sketching and modelling.

He demonstrated that design expertise is domain-specific, not transferable general problem-solving ability. Expert industrial designers think differently from expert architects or graphic designers, though they share some designerly characteristics. This finding has implications for 3D Design curriculum, suggesting the value of depth within specific contexts (product design, furniture, ceramics, etc.) rather than superficial coverage of many areas.

Cross's work also addressed the development of design ability, showing it requires extensive practice with feedback, exposure to multiple design contexts, and development of domain knowledge. His research supports curriculum structures that provide sustained engagement with design projects, not fragmented exercises, and that build progressively more complex challenges.

### 7. Design Pedagogy and Education Research

Though primarily a design researcher rather than education researcher, Cross made significant contributions to design pedagogy through his Open University course development and his writings on design education. His 1984 paper "Developments in Design Methodology" (in *Developments in Design Methodology*, edited by Cross) and later reflections on design teaching demonstrated how research findings should inform practice.

Cross advocated for studio-based learning where students engage in authentic design projects with critique and iteration. He emphasised the importance of: tacit knowledge transmission through demonstration and practice, peer learning through shared studio culture, expert critique providing formative feedback, and real-world constraints creating authentic learning contexts.

His Open University courses demonstrated how design education could be adapted for distance learning through carefully structured projects, excellent visual materials, and supported independent learning. These courses influenced thousands of UK teachers who studied part-time through the OU while teaching, disseminating Cross's ideas widely through the education system.

## Key Publications

### Books

**Designerly Ways of Knowing** (2006, Springer; reprinted 2007, Board of International Research in Design). Cross's most influential book, synthesising decades of research into a comprehensive account of design as a discipline. Essential reading for understanding design's epistemological foundations. The book examines design ability, design expertise, design knowledge, design process, and design practice through empirical research and theoretical analysis.

**Design Thinking: Understanding How Designers Think and Work** (2011, Berg Publishers; Bloomsbury). A highly accessible synthesis of Cross's research into design cognition, intended for students, practitioners, and educators. Contains detailed case studies of designers at work, analysis of design strategies, and practical implications. Widely used in UK design teacher education and curriculum planning.

**Engineering Design Methods: Strategies for Product Design** (1989, John Wiley & Sons; 4th edition 2008). Though focused on engineering, this book's systematic treatment of design methods has influenced 3D Design teaching, particularly in product design contexts. Provides practical frameworks while acknowledging their limitations.

**Design in General Education** (1983, The Design Council, co-edited with Robin Roy). Proceedings of a seminar held at The Royal College of Art examining design's role in broader education. Influential in arguments for design in the National Curriculum during the 1980s.

### Seminal Journal Articles

**"Designerly Ways of Knowing"** (1982, *Design Studies*, 3(4), pp. 221-227). The foundational paper establishing design as a distinct intellectual culture. This brief, clearly argued paper has been cited thousands of times and remains required reading in design programmes worldwide. It articulated the theoretical basis for design as an academic subject.

**"Design Discipline Versus Design Science"** (2001, *Design Issues*, 17(3), pp. 49-55). Clarifies the distinction between studying design (design science) and the knowledge embedded in design practice (design discipline). Important for understanding design research's relationship to design education.

**"Natural Intelligence in Design"** (1999, *Design Studies*, 20(1), pp. 25-39). Analysis of expertise in design, contrasting natural human design ability with attempts at computational design. Demonstrates the sophistication of human design cognition and its resistance to algorithmic reproduction.

**"Expertise in Design: An Overview"** (2004, *Design Studies*, 25(5), pp. 427-441). Synthesises research on design expertise, identifying key characteristics of expert designers and implications for design education. Provides evidence base for teaching strategies that develop designerly thinking.

**"Forty Years of Design Research"** (2007, *Design Studies*, 28(1), pp. 1-4). Reflective editorial tracing the development of design research from the 1960s Design Methods Movement through contemporary design science. Provides historical context for current design education approaches.

**"The Nature and Nurture of Design Ability"** (1990, *Design Studies*, 11(3), pp. 127-140). Examines whether design ability is innate talent or can be taught, concluding that while some aptitude helps, design thinking can be systematically developed. Important for democratising design education and justifying design in general education.

### Edited Collections

**Analysing Design Activity** (1996, John Wiley & Sons, co-edited with Henri Christiaans and Kees Dorst). Collection of protocol studies examining design cognition across different domains. Establishes methodological standards for design research and provides empirical evidence about design thinking.

**Research in Design Thinking** (1992, Delft University Press, co-edited with Kees Dorst and Norbert Roozenburg). Proceedings from an international workshop examining design cognition. Brought together leading researchers and established design thinking as a legitimate research field.

### Open University Course Materials

**T263/T211 Design: Principles and Practice** (various dates, Open University). Though not published commercially, these comprehensive course materials influenced design education across the UK. They presented design as systematic yet creative practice, integrated theory with practice, and demonstrated how design could be taught rigorously through distance learning.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Cross's work provided crucial intellectual foundations during the development of the National Curriculum Design and Technology from the late 1980s onwards. His articulation of "Designerly Ways of Knowing" gave policymakers and curriculum developers a theoretical framework for justifying design's inclusion as a foundation subject alongside traditional academic disciplines.

The original Technology in the National Curriculum (1990) and subsequent iterations reflected Cross's emphasis on: design as a distinct form of cognition and knowledge; the importance of practical capability alongside theoretical understanding; iterative design processes of investigating, designing, making, and evaluating; and design's capacity to address ill-defined, open-ended problems.

Cross served on various advisory committees and contributed to policy discussions about design education. His 1983 work with Robin Roy, *Design in General Education*, influenced the Cockroft Report (1982) and subsequent arguments that design develops cognitive capabilities valuable across the curriculum, not just for future designers.

### GCSE and A-Level Specifications

Contemporary GCSE and A-Level 3D Design specifications from AQA, Pearson (Edexcel), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas reflect Cross's research findings, even when not explicitly cited. Key influences include:

**Assessment of design thinking**: Specifications require evidence of iterative development, exploration of multiple solutions, and refinement based on testing. This reflects Cross's research showing expert designers work through propose-test-refine cycles rather than linear processes.

**Portfolio and process documentation**: The emphasis on design journals, annotated sketches, and development work reflects Cross's findings about reflection-in-action and the importance of externalised thinking through drawing and modelling.

**Contextual understanding**: Requirements for investigating existing products, design movements, and designers reflect Cross's emphasis on design knowledge as encompassing the material culture and traditions of design practice.

**Making as thinking**: Practical work constitutes substantial proportions of assessment (typically 50% for non-exam assessment), validating Cross's evidence that designers think through making, not just before it.

### Design and Technology Association (DATA)

The Design and Technology Association (now DATA, previously Design and Technology Education Association) has drawn extensively on Cross's work in its guidance documents, training programmes, and publications. *DESIGN & TECHNOLOGY Education: An International Journal*, published by DATA, has featured Cross's work and applications of his ideas to classroom practice.

DATA's curriculum guidance and resources for teachers incorporate Cross's frameworks, particularly in explaining the distinctiveness of design thinking and providing rationale for studio-based learning approaches.

### Teacher Education

Cross's publications are standard reading in UK design teacher education programmes (PGCE and School Direct routes). His clear explanations of design thinking and expertise provide trainee teachers with theoretical foundations for their practice. Universities including Loughborough, Goldsmiths, Sheffield Hallam, and others incorporate Cross's frameworks into their design education courses.

The Open University's own education courses for teachers have disseminated Cross's ideas widely, reaching thousands of practising teachers pursuing further qualifications while teaching.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Studio-Based Learning

Cross advocated for studio-based design education where students work on authentic design projects in a shared space with peer interaction, expert guidance, and access to tools and materials. Though his own teaching was primarily through distance learning at the OU, his research validated the effectiveness of studio pedagogy.

His protocol studies demonstrated that designers develop expertise through repeated practice with feedback, exposure to multiple contexts, and observation of more experienced designers. This supports pedagogical approaches emphasising sustained project work over fragmented exercises, formative assessment through critique, and peer learning.
