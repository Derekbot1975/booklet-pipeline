# David Barlex

**Full Name:** David Barlex

**Current/Recent Roles:** Independent Consultant in Design and Technology Education; Former Director of the Nuffield Design & Technology Project; Visiting Professor at Brunel University London

**Known For:** Leading development of the Nuffield Design & Technology curriculum; pioneering 'design without make' approaches; developing conceptual frameworks for capability in design and technology; advancing pedagogical thinking in 3D Design education through evidence-based research and curriculum innovation

---

## Biography and Career History

David Barlex has been one of the most influential figures in UK Design and Technology education for over three decades. His career spans classroom teaching, curriculum development, research, and policy advisory work, establishing him as a leading authority on how young people learn design capability.

Barlex began his career as a secondary school teacher before moving into curriculum development and research roles. His most significant institutional contribution came through his directorship of the Nuffield Design & Technology Project from 1995 to 2007, where he led the development of curriculum materials that fundamentally reshaped D&T teaching across England and Wales. This twelve-year period saw the creation of some of the most widely adopted and researched D&T resources in UK educational history.

Following his work with Nuffield, Barlex continued as an independent consultant, working with examination boards, educational charities, government departments, and international organisations. He has held visiting professorships at several universities, including Brunel University London, where he has supervised doctoral research and contributed to teacher education programmes. His work has extended beyond the UK, with consultancy projects in countries including Israel, Singapore, and various European nations.

Throughout his career, Barlex has maintained a commitment to evidence-based practice, conducting and commissioning research that examines how design thinking develops in young people. He has been a regular presenter at international conferences, particularly the Design and Technology Association's annual conferences and the International Conference on Design and Technology Educational Research (IDATER/PATT conferences).

His contributions have been recognised through various honours within the D&T education community, and his publications remain core reading for PGCE students, practising teachers, and curriculum leaders in 3D Design and D&T subjects.

## Key Ideas and Contributions

### 1. Design Without Make

One of Barlex's most significant conceptual contributions to 3D Design education is the 'design without make' approach, developed extensively through the Nuffield curriculum materials. This pedagogical strategy addresses a persistent challenge in D&T education: enabling students to engage with ambitious, authentic design problems without being constrained by the time, resources, and manufacturing skills required for full realisation.

The design without make approach allows students to develop design capability through activities that focus on:
- Design thinking and conceptual development
- Communication through drawing, CAD modelling, and multimedia presentation
- Evaluation using criteria drawn from authentic contexts
- Engagement with complex, real-world problems that would be impractical to manufacture in school

Barlex argued this approach was essential for maintaining curriculum breadth and relevance. In his work, he distinguished between 'design and make assignments' (DMAs) and 'design activities', proposing that a balanced curriculum requires both. Design without make activities enable students to tackle industrial-scale projects, architectural challenges, or complex systems that would be impossible to physically construct within school constraints.

This concept has been particularly influential in 3D Design qualifications at GCSE and A-level, where examination boards incorporate design portfolio work that doesn't require manufactured outcomes. Barlex's framework provided the pedagogical justification for these assessment structures.

### 2. The Nuffield Design & Technology Curriculum Framework

As Project Director, Barlex led the development of the Nuffield Design & Technology curriculum, which introduced a structured, capability-focused approach to D&T education. The framework was built around several core principles:

**Resource Tasks and Capability Tasks:** The Nuffield approach distinguished between activities that develop specific skills and knowledge (resource tasks) and those that require students to apply capability in addressing design problems (capability tasks). This structure provided teachers with a clear pedagogical framework for curriculum planning.

**The IDEAS Pack:** Barlex oversaw development of 'Ideas and Evidence: Analysing' (IDEAS), a resource that taught students systematic approaches to product analysis and investigation. This became foundational for how 3D Design students learn to research existing products, understand user needs, and identify design opportunities.

**Values in Design:** The Nuffield curriculum explicitly addressed values, ethics, and sustainability in design decision-making, anticipating contemporary concerns about responsible design practice. Barlex championed the inclusion of environmental, social, and economic considerations as integral to design capability rather than bolt-on additions.

### 3. Informed Design and Designing

Barlex developed the concept of 'informed design' to describe the knowledge base necessary for effective designing. He argued that design capability requires students to draw upon:
- Technical knowledge (materials, processes, systems)
- Contextual knowledge (users, markets, cultural factors)
- Procedural knowledge (design strategies, evaluation methods)
- Conceptual knowledge (design principles, aesthetic understanding)

This framework helped teachers understand that effective 3D Design education requires building knowledge alongside developing design process skills. Barlex was critical of approaches that treated designing as a generic, content-free process, arguing instead for domain-specific knowledge development within material areas such as resistant materials, textiles, or graphics.

His work on informed design influenced how examination specifications articulated knowledge requirements and how teachers structured schemes of work to build knowledge progressively.

### 4. Designerly Thinking and Pedagogy

Drawing on research from design cognition studies, Barlex explored how 'designerly thinking' differs from scientific or mathematical thinking. He emphasised characteristics including:
- Working with ill-defined problems
- Generating and evaluating multiple solutions
- Using iterative development processes
- Employing drawing and modelling as thinking tools
- Balancing creativity with constraint

His pedagogical recommendations included teaching strategies such as:
- **Focused practical tasks** to develop specific making skills
- **Design activities** to apply knowledge in shorter contexts
- **Product disassembly and analysis** to build technical understanding
- **Client/user engagement** to develop empathy and contextual awareness

These strategies remain embedded in 3D Design pedagogy across UK secondary education.

### 5. Assessment and Progression in Design Capability

Barlex conducted extensive research into how design capability develops and how it can be reliably assessed. He critiqued assessment approaches that fragmented design into discrete, atomised skills, arguing instead for holistic assessment of capability.

His work influenced the development of assessment frameworks that recognise:
- The interconnected nature of designing activities
- The role of knowledge in enabling capability
- The importance of context in determining quality
- The challenges of reliability in creative assessment

For 3D Design qualifications, Barlex's thinking supported portfolio-based assessment approaches that allow students to demonstrate capability across extended projects rather than through decontextualised tests.

### 6. Realistic Contexts and Authentic Problems

Barlex consistently advocated for design education grounded in realistic contexts drawn from professional practice, commercial environments, and social challenges. He argued that artificial, school-contrived problems undermined the development of authentic design capability.

His curriculum materials featured case studies from industry, collaborations with manufacturing companies, and design briefs based on genuine market opportunities. This approach influenced how 3D Design courses structure project briefs and how teachers develop design contexts.

He emphasised that authenticity doesn't require industrial partnerships for every project but does require teachers to research real design challenges and present them with sufficient contextual richness to enable informed designing.

### 7. The Role of Making in Design Education

Despite his advocacy for design without make approaches, Barlex maintained that making remains essential to design education. He argued for a balanced curriculum where:
- Making develops tacit knowledge about materials and processes
- Physical prototyping enables design development through iteration
- Craft skills provide a language for communicating with manufacturers
- Material engagement stimulates creative thinking

His position was that making should serve designing rather than dominate curriculum time. He critiqued D&T curricula where making skills became ends in themselves rather than means to develop design capability.

This balanced view has influenced 3D Design qualifications that require some physical outcome work alongside portfolio development, rather than purely digital or presentation-based assessment.

### 8. Collaboration Between D&T and Other Subjects

Barlex explored opportunities for collaborative curriculum development between D&T and subjects including science, mathematics, art and design, and computing. He developed teaching materials that demonstrated how design projects could address learning objectives across subject boundaries.

His work on 'designing for a purpose' showed how 3D Design projects could incorporate:
- Scientific understanding of materials properties and structures
- Mathematical modelling and calculation
- Aesthetic and cultural analysis from art and design
- Digital technologies for design development and presentation

These cross-curricular frameworks anticipated integrated STEM approaches while maintaining the distinct contribution of designerly thinking.

### 9. Continuing Professional Development for Teachers

Throughout his career, Barlex developed and delivered extensive CPD for D&T and 3D Design teachers. His approach to professional learning emphasised:
- Subject knowledge development alongside pedagogical strategies
- Practical engagement with materials and processes
- Critical examination of curriculum materials
- Reflection on teaching approaches through evidence

His CPD programmes, delivered through organisations including the Design and Technology Association, regional subject networks, and examination boards, influenced generations of teachers. His materials often included detailed teacher notes explaining pedagogical rationales, enabling teachers to adapt approaches for their own contexts.

### 10. Technology Education in the 21st Century

In recent years, Barlex has contributed to debates about the future of technology education in an era of digital manufacturing, artificial intelligence, and sustainability imperatives. He has argued that:
- Design education must engage with contemporary technologies including CAD/CAM, 3D printing, and digital fabrication
- Understanding sustainable design is essential, not optional
- Design capability includes systems thinking and understanding interconnections
- Technology education should prepare students for technological citizenship, not just technical employment

His position has been that 3D Design education must evolve to address contemporary challenges while maintaining core design capabilities of problem identification, creative solution generation, and iterative development.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### Impact on National Curriculum Development

Barlex's work significantly influenced iterations of the National Curriculum for Design and Technology in England. Although the Nuffield curriculum operated as an alternative to National Curriculum programmes of study, its widespread adoption (used in approximately 30% of English secondary schools at peak implementation) meant that Nuffield approaches influenced practice across the system.

The 2008 National Curriculum revision for D&T drew explicitly on Nuffield principles, including:
- Recognition of both making and design without make activities
- Emphasis on realistic contexts and authentic problems
- Integration of values, sustainability, and ethical considerations
- Structured progression through focused learning and design activities

Barlex contributed to consultation processes for this revision through the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA) and the Department for Children, Schools and Families (DCSF).

### Examination Specifications

The pedagogical frameworks developed by Barlex have substantially influenced GCSE and A-level specifications in Design and Technology and 3D Design subjects. Examination boards including AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas have incorporated:

- **Portfolio assessment structures** that recognise design without make approaches
- **Contextual challenge requirements** reflecting Barlex's emphasis on authentic problems
- **Knowledge requirements** that align with informed design principles
- **Assessment objectives** that value integrated capability over fragmented skills

Barlex has worked directly with awarding organisations as a consultant and advisor, reviewing specifications and assessment materials. His critique of assessment that fragments design activity has pushed examination boards toward more holistic assessment approaches.

### 3D Design Qualifications Specifically

For GCSE and A-level 3D Design (Product Design, Three-Dimensional Design), Barlex's influence is evident in:

- The balance between practical outcome and design portfolio work
- Requirements for technical knowledge about materials, processes, and systems
- Assessment of iterative design development rather than just final proposals
- Recognition of CAD, digital modelling, and presentation as legitimate design tools
- Integration of user-centred design approaches and contextual analysis

The current specifications (2017 onwards) for Design and Technology subjects reflect Barlex's long-standing advocacy for knowledge-rich, capability-focused curriculum models.

### Policy Advisory Work

Barlex has contributed to numerous policy reviews and inquiries into design and technology education, including:

- The **Royal Academy of Engineering's education programmes**, where he has advised on engineering education and its relationship with D&T
- **The Design Commission** (now Policy Connect's All-Party Parliamentary Design and Innovation Group), contributing to reports on design education
- **Department for Education consultations** on curriculum reform and subject content
- **International curriculum development projects** in countries seeking to develop or reform technology education

His evidence to parliamentary inquiries and policy submissions has consistently argued for:
- The distinctive educational value of design and technology as a subject
- Adequate resourcing for practical subjects including specialist facilities and materials budgets
- High-quality initial teacher education and continuing professional development
- Curriculum time sufficient to develop design capability effectively
