# Richard Kimbell

**Full Name:** Richard Kimbell
**Current Roles:** Emeritus Professor of Design and Technology Education, Goldsmiths, University of London
**Known For:** Pioneering work in design capability, performance-based assessment in Design and Technology, directing the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) design and technology surveys, developing the e-scape digital assessment system, and fundamentally shaping Design and Technology curriculum and pedagogy in the UK

## Biography

### Early Career and Academic Foundation

Richard Kimbell established himself as one of the most influential figures in Design and Technology education in the United Kingdom during the late 20th and early 21st centuries. His academic base at Goldsmiths, University of London, provided the intellectual foundation for much of his groundbreaking work in design education research and assessment.

### Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) Work

Kimbell's most significant early contribution came through his role as Director of the Design and Technology component of the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) from 1985 to 1991. This national monitoring programme, funded by the Department of Education and Science, sought to assess standards of performance in schools. The APU design and technology surveys represented the first large-scale systematic investigation of pupils' design capability in England and Wales.

The APU work was revolutionary because it moved beyond traditional knowledge-based testing to assess how pupils actually performed design tasks. Kimbell directed extensive research involving thousands of pupils aged 11, 13, and 15, examining their capability to engage in authentic design and technology activities. This research fundamentally challenged existing assumptions about assessment in practical subjects.

### Goldsmiths and the Technology Education Research Unit (TERU)

At Goldsmiths, Kimbell established and directed the Technology Education Research Unit (TERU), which became internationally recognised as a leading centre for research in design and technology education. Under his leadership, TERU conducted numerous influential research projects that shaped curriculum development and assessment practices. The unit's work combined rigorous academic research with practical application, ensuring that findings had direct relevance to classroom practice.

### Later Career and e-scape Development

In the 2000s, Kimbell led the development of e-scape (electronic solutions for creative assessment in portfolio environments), an innovative digital assessment system that addressed long-standing challenges in assessing design capability. This work attracted significant funding from the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA) and represented a technological leap forward in capturing and assessing creative design processes.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Design Capability

Kimbell's central theoretical contribution is the concept of 'design capability', which he articulated most fully in his APU work and subsequent publications. Design capability represents the holistic integration of thinking and doing in design activity. Kimbell argued that design and technology education should develop pupils' capability to operate in the 'procedural domain' – the active space where designing and making occur – rather than focusing narrowly on knowledge acquisition or separate skill development.

In *Assessing Technology* (1991), Kimbell described capability as involving interaction between 'the mind' (thoughts, ideas, conceptualisation) and 'the hand' (action, modelling, making). He rejected models that separated mental and physical activity, arguing instead for a dynamic, iterative relationship where thought and action continuously inform each other. This conceptualisation challenged traditional divisions between 'theory' and 'practice' in design education.

Design capability, in Kimbell's framework, encompasses the ability to:
- Identify and clarify design tasks and opportunities
- Generate, develop and communicate ideas
- Work with materials, tools and equipment to create outcomes
- Evaluate processes and products
- Manage design projects through to completion

### 2. The Iterative Nature of Design Activity

Through detailed observation and analysis in the APU studies, Kimbell documented how successful design activity involves constant iteration between thinking and doing, rather than linear progression through discrete stages. He criticised design process models that presented designing as a sequential journey from 'brief' through 'research', 'design', and 'making' to 'evaluation', arguing these failed to capture the reality of design behaviour.

Kimbell's research showed that effective designers move fluidly between different modes of activity, using making and modelling to generate ideas, not merely to realise pre-formed concepts. This insight had profound implications for curriculum design and pedagogy, suggesting that pupils needed opportunities for continuous interaction between cognitive and practical activity throughout projects.

### 3. Task-Based Assessment and Authentic Performance

Kimbell pioneered task-based assessment approaches that required pupils to engage in authentic design activity rather than answering written questions about design. The APU surveys used extended tasks lasting several hours where pupils worked individually on design challenges, with researchers documenting their processes and outcomes.

This work established principles that assessment in Design and Technology should:
- Evaluate performance in realistic design contexts
- Capture process as well as product
- Allow pupils to demonstrate capability across different modes (ideation, development, realisation)
- Use structured observation and holistic judgement
- Recognise the emergent and unpredictable nature of creative activity

Kimbell argued that traditional examination formats were fundamentally unsuited to assessing design capability because they fragmented holistic activity and privileged written communication over other forms of design expression.

### 4. 'Modelling' as Central to Design Activity

A key finding from Kimbell's research was the centrality of modelling in design thinking. He used 'modelling' broadly to encompass sketching, drawing, making prototypes, computer modelling, and other forms of externalising ideas. Kimbell demonstrated that modelling serves not just to communicate or test ideas, but is fundamental to generating and developing them.

This emphasis on modelling challenged pedagogical approaches that treated drawing and making primarily as recording or production activities. Kimbell advocated for classroom environments where pupils had immediate access to materials and tools to model ideas continuously throughout projects, arguing this was essential for developing design capability.

### 5. The 'Thought and Action' Model

In *The Assessment of Performance in Design and Technology* (1991), Kimbell and colleagues presented their 'thought and action' model of design activity. This visual representation showed designing as involving continuous oscillation between two domains: the mind (imaging, speculating, reasoning) and the hand (confronting reality through action, making, modelling).

The model depicted design activity as a dynamic spiral where ideas are proposed, tested through modelling, reflected upon, and refined through further action. This conceptualisation influenced how curriculum materials represented the design process and how teachers structured learning activities.

### 6. Quality in Design Outcomes

Kimbell developed sophisticated frameworks for judging quality in design outcomes that moved beyond simplistic criteria checklists. He argued that quality emerges from the interaction between multiple factors including fitness for purpose, technical accomplishment, aesthetic resolution, and innovation. Importantly, he emphasised that quality must be judged in relation to the context and constraints of the task, the resources available, and the designer's intentions.

This approach recognised that identical criteria applied mechanistically across different contexts could not capture genuine quality. Instead, Kimbell advocated for holistic judgement informed by expertise, supported by structured frameworks that ensured consistency and reliability.

### 7. e-scape and Digital Assessment

The e-scape system, developed from 2004 onwards, represented Kimbell's response to persistent challenges in assessing design capability reliably and manageably. The system allowed pupils to record their design activity using digital photography and annotation, creating portfolios that captured their thinking and making processes.

Crucially, e-scape incorporated an innovative approach to marking. Rather than atomised mark schemes, assessors viewed entire portfolios and made holistic judgements, with pairs of assessors discussing and agreeing marks. The system then used algorithms to standardise judgements across different assessor pairs, achieving high reliability whilst preserving holistic assessment principles.

e-scape demonstrated that technology could address the tension between validity (assessing what matters) and reliability (consistent judgements), a tension that had plagued Design and Technology assessment for decades.

### 8. The Nature of Technological Activity

Kimbell consistently argued for understanding Design and Technology as a distinct form of activity, different from scientific investigation or artistic expression, though drawing on both. In *Understanding Practice in Design and Technology* (1996), he characterised technological activity as purposeful intervention to improve situations, involving:
- Identifying human needs and wants
- Working within constraints and optimising solutions
- Applying and developing technical knowledge
- Making value judgements about outcomes

This conceptualisation helped establish Design and Technology's intellectual legitimacy and educational value, countering perceptions that it was merely vocational training or craft activity.

### 9. Critique of Knowledge-Led Approaches

Throughout his career, Kimbell critiqued curriculum approaches that prioritised knowledge transmission over capability development. He argued against fragmenting Design and Technology into separate knowledge domains (materials, systems, electronics, etc.) taught discretely, instead advocating for integrated project-based learning where knowledge was encountered in context and developed through application.

This stance positioned him in ongoing debates about curriculum balance between 'knowing that' and 'knowing how', with Kimbell consistently championing the primacy of procedural capability whilst recognising the essential role of conceptual knowledge.

### 10. Teacher Expertise and Professional Judgement

Kimbell's work on assessment emphasised the importance of teacher expertise and professional judgement. He rejected mechanistic assessment systems that reduced judgement to box-ticking, arguing instead that valid assessment of complex capability required expert holistic evaluation. His e-scape work demonstrated that, properly supported and moderated, such judgements could achieve both validity and reliability.

This respect for professional expertise extended to his pedagogical approach, where he advocated for teachers having flexibility to respond to pupils' emergent ideas and interests rather than following rigid schemes of work.

## Key Publications

### Books and Major Reports

**Assessing Technology: International Trends in Curriculum and Assessment** (1991, edited with Richard Kimbell). This international comparison examined different approaches to technology education assessment across multiple countries, providing context for UK developments.

**The Assessment of Performance in Design and Technology: The Final Report of the APU Design and Technology Project 1985-1991** (1991, with Kay Stables, Richard Green, and others). This substantial report presented findings from six years of national monitoring, establishing foundations for understanding design capability and how to assess it. The report documented research involving thousands of pupils and provided detailed analysis of task performance, presenting the 'thought and action' model and extensive evidence about how pupils engage with design activities.

**Understanding Practice in Design and Technology** (1996, with Kay Stables and Richard Green). This book synthesised research about the nature of design and technology activity, exploring how pupils develop capability and what characterises quality in design work. It examined different types of design tasks and their educational value, providing theoretical frameworks and practical guidance for teachers and curriculum developers.

**Assessing Design Innovation: A Guide to Assessment in Design and Technology** (2004, with Kay Stables and others). This publication focused specifically on assessment challenges and approaches in Design and Technology, building on earlier APU work and addressing contemporary curriculum contexts. It explored how innovation and creativity could be assessed validly and reliably.

**e-scape Portfolio Assessment (e-solutions for Creative Assessment in Portfolio Environments): Final Report** (2007, with Kay Stables and David Perry). This report documented the development and validation of the e-scape system, presenting evidence of its reliability and validity, and discussing implications for qualification assessment. It demonstrated how digital technology could address long-standing assessment challenges whilst preserving authentic task-based approaches.

### Journal Articles and Book Chapters

Kimbell published extensively in academic journals including *International Journal of Technology and Design Education*, *Design and Technology Education: An International Journal*, and *The Curriculum Journal*. His articles addressed topics including assessment methodology, curriculum design, international comparisons, and theoretical frameworks for understanding design activity.

Notable articles include:
- 'Reflections on the Nature of Design and Technology' exploring epistemological questions about the subject
- Papers on assessment reliability and validity in creative subjects
- Analyses of curriculum policy developments and their implications
- Research findings about pupil capability development

### Research Reports and Policy Papers

Throughout his career, Kimbell authored numerous research reports for government agencies, examination boards, and educational organisations. These included evaluation studies of curriculum initiatives, feasibility studies for new qualifications, and evidence submissions to policy reviews.

His work for the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority (QCA), Design and Technology Association (DATA), and examination boards directly influenced qualification design and assessment practices.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Kimbell's research fundamentally influenced the development of Design and Technology in the National Curriculum from its inception in 1990. The APU findings, published just as the first National Curriculum orders were implemented, provided evidence-based guidance about what pupils could do and what progression looked like. The emphasis on capability, iterative processes, and integration of thinking and doing shaped curriculum language and structure.

Subsequent National Curriculum revisions continued to draw on Kimbell's frameworks. The 1995 revision, in particular, reflected APU findings about the importance of focused practical tasks alongside longer design-and-make assignments. The attainment target structure, describing progression in design capability, incorporated concepts developed through his research.

### GCSE and A-Level Assessment

Kimbell's assessment principles profoundly influenced GCSE and A-Level Design and Technology qualifications. His work established the coursework portfolio as central to assessment, demonstrating that extended project work could be assessed reliably through structured holistic judgement. Examination boards adopted frameworks influenced by his research for designing tasks and marking schemes.

The e-scape system was piloted for potential use in national qualifications, though full implementation faced obstacles relating to infrastructure and cost. Nevertheless, the system demonstrated alternative possibilities and influenced thinking about how qualifications might evolve.

### International Influence

Kimbell's work achieved international recognition, influencing technology education curriculum development in countries including Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, and various European nations. His publications were widely cited internationally, and he contributed to international conferences and collaborative research projects.

The APU assessment approaches and frameworks were particularly influential, with several countries adapting Kimbell's methods for their own curriculum monitoring programmes. His conceptualisation of design capability provided language and theory that transcended national boundaries.

### DATA and Professional Organisations

Kimbell worked closely with the Design and Technology Association (DATA, now known as the Design and Technology Association), contributing to professional development materials, policy positions, and advocacy for the subject. His research provided evidence that DATA used in arguing for Design and Technology's place in the curriculum and appropriate resourcing.

### Policy Reviews and Consultations

Kimbell regularly contributed to government policy reviews affecting Design and Technology, including responses to curriculum consultations and reviews of qualification systems. His evidence submissions combined rigorous research findings with practical understanding of implementation challenges, making them influential with policy makers.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Principles for Teaching Design and Technology

Kimbell's research implied clear pedagogical principles, though he generally avoided prescriptive teaching methods. Key principles included:

**Immersion in Practice**: Pupils should spend substantial time engaged in actual design activity, not just learning about design. Lessons should prioritise 'doing' over 'talking about'.

**Access to Resources**: Immediate access to modelling materials and tools is essential so pupils can externalise and test ideas continuously. Classroom organisation should facilitate fluid movement between thinking and making.

**Authentic Challenges**: Tasks should present genuine design challenges with scope for individual interpretation and multiple valid solutions, rather than closed problems with predetermined answers.

**Iteration and Development**: Learning activities should encourage iteration, allowing pupils to generate multiple ideas, test them, evaluate outcomes, and refine solutions through successive cycles.

**Integration Rather Than Separation**: Kimbell argued against teaching 'theory' lessons separate from practical work. Conceptual knowledge should be introduced contextually when needed to advance design projects.

### Teacher as Facilitator

Kimbell's model positioned teachers as facilitators of design activity rather than transmitters of knowledge. The teacher's role involved:
- Structuring challenging but manageable design tasks
- Providing timely interventions to support progress
- Asking questions that stimulate thinking rather than giving answers
- Modelling design behaviour and thinking
- Creating environments conducive to creative risk-taking

This approach required significant professional skill and judgement, recognising teaching Design and Technology as intellectually demanding work.

### Formative Assessment Embedded in Practice

Kimbell advocated for formative assessment integrated into design activity rather than bolted-on separately. This meant:
- Teachers observing and questioning during practical work
- Pupils reviewing and evaluating their own developing work
- Peer critique and collaborative evaluation
- Using interim outcomes (sketches, models, prototypes) as assessment opportunities

This approach recognised that assessment serves learning when it informs immediate next steps in design development.

### Differentiation Through Open Tasks

Rather than differentiation through different tasks or extensive teacher intervention, Kimbell's approach relied on open-ended tasks that allowed pupils of different capabilities to respond at appropriate levels. Well-designed tasks would be 'low floor, high ceiling', accessible to all but allowing scope for sophisticated responses.

## Pedagogical Research Contributions

### Observational Studies

Much of Kimbell's research involved detailed observation of pupils working on design tasks, using video recording, think-aloud protocols, and analysis of work produced. This observational approach generated rich data about actual design behaviour rather than relying on self-report or theoretical assumption.

These methods became widely adopted in design education research, establishing observational analysis of authentic activity as a rigorous research approach.

### Task Design Methodology

The APU work developed sophisticated approaches to task design for assessment, including:
- Clear but open briefs allowing individual interpretation
- Appropriate scaffolding providing structure without constraining creativity
- Realistic contexts that pupils could relate to
- Manageable scope given time constraints
- Potential for evidence across different aspects of capability

These principles informed task design in teaching as well as assessment contexts.
