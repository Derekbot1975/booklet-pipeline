# 3D Design in the Curriculum Overview

## Introduction to 3D Design within UK Education

3D Design occupies a distinctive position within the UK curriculum, bridging art, design, technology, and problem-solving disciplines. Unlike many other subjects, 3D Design explicitly develops spatial awareness, material understanding, and practical making skills alongside conceptual thinking. Within the National Curriculum framework, 3D Design primarily sits within **Design and Technology (D&T)** at Key Stages 1-3, whilst becoming a discrete specialist subject at Key Stage 4 and beyond through GCSE and A Level qualifications.

Professor Richard Kimbell, whose seminal work *Assessing Technology* (1997) fundamentally shaped UK D&T education, emphasises that design capability emerges through the interplay of 'imaging' and 'modelling' - the mental conceptualisation of three-dimensional forms and their physical realisation. This bidirectional process remains central to 3D Design pedagogy across all key stages.

## National Curriculum Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 1 and 2: Design and Technology

The **National Curriculum in England: Design and Technology Programmes of Study** (DfE, 2013) establishes the foundation for 3D thinking from primary education. Pupils are required to:

**At Key Stage 1:**
- Design purposeful, functional, appealing products for themselves and other users based on design criteria
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through talking, drawing, templates, mock-ups and, where appropriate, information and communication technology
- Select from and use a range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks
- Explore and use mechanisms (levers, sliders, wheels and axles) in their products

**At Key Stage 2:**
- Use research and develop design criteria to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products that are fit for purpose, aimed at particular individuals or groups
- Generate, develop, model and communicate their ideas through discussion, annotated sketches, cross-sectional and exploded diagrams, prototypes, pattern pieces and computer-aided design
- Select from and use a wider range of materials and components, including construction materials, textiles and ingredients, according to their functional properties and aesthetic qualities

David Barlex, through his extensive work with the Nuffield Design & Technology Project, highlights that even at primary level, pupils should engage with 'authentic design contexts' where material choices and structural considerations matter. His research demonstrates that children as young as seven can grasp sophisticated concepts about rigidity, stability, and material behaviour when appropriately scaffolded.

### Key Stage 3: Developing Design Capability

At Key Stage 3, the National Curriculum (DfE, 2013) requires pupils to develop deeper understanding of 3D design principles:

- Apply their understanding of how to strengthen, stiffen and reinforce more complex structures
- Understand developments in design and technology, its impact on individuals, society and the environment, and the responsibilities of designers, engineers and technologists
- Identify and solve their own design problems and understand how to reformulate problems given to them
- Develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional, appealing products that respond to needs in a variety of situations
- Use a variety of approaches to explore form, including digital and physical modelling

The KS3 programme emphasises working with a broader range of materials including woods, metals, polymers, textiles, and modern materials. Professor Nigel Cross, in his influential book *Designerly Ways of Knowing* (2006), argues that this stage is critical for developing what he terms 'design intelligence' - the ability to think simultaneously about aesthetics, function, manufacture, and user needs in three-dimensional space.

## Key Stage 4: GCSE 3D Design

At Key Stage 4, 3D Design typically becomes available as a discrete GCSE qualification, separate from broader D&T pathways. All major examination boards offer specifications:

### AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552)

AQA's specification (introduced 2017) requires students to study core technical and designing and making principles, with opportunities to specialise in 3D design contexts. The **Non-Exam Assessment (NEA)** component (50% of qualification) demands a substantial 3D design project addressing contextualised design challenges. Students must demonstrate:
- Investigation of user needs and wants
- Development of design solutions through iterative processes
- Application of technical knowledge about materials, processes and systems
- Production of high-quality prototypes or products

### Edexcel GCSE Design and Technology (1DT0)

Edexcel's specification similarly balances theoretical knowledge with practical application. Their assessment criteria explicitly value the development of 3D spatial solutions, material experimentation, and manufacturing competence.

### OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310)

OCR's iterative design challenge approach aligns closely with professional design practice. Their **Iterative Design Challenge** (50%) requires students to work through substantial 3D design problems, demonstrating understanding of anthropometrics, ergonomics, and user-centred design - concepts Kimbell identifies as essential for authentic design capability.

### WJEC GCSE Design and Technology (C560QS)

WJEC offers specific endorsement pathways, with '3D Design' as a recognised area of focus alongside fashion and textiles, engineering, and product design. Their approach emphasises Welsh industrial heritage and contemporary making cultures.

## Key Stage 5: A Level 3D Design

### AQA A Level Design and Technology: Product Design (7552)

At A Level, 3D Design achieves full specialist status. AQA's specification requires deep engagement with:
- Commercial manufacture and enterprise
- Design theory and contemporary influences
- Material science and testing
- Digital design technologies including CAD/CAM
- Sustainable design practices

The **NEA (Practical Application of Technical Principles)** comprises 50% of the qualification, demanding a substantial 3D design project demonstrating design thinking at an advanced level. Students typically work to professional standards, producing portfolio work equivalent to foundation diploma expectations.

### Edexcel A Level Design and Technology: Product Design (9DT0)

Edexcel's A Level places particular emphasis on industrial contexts, requiring students to understand manufacturing systems, quality control, and design for manufacture and assembly (DFMA). This reflects Cross's argument that design education must connect to authentic industrial practice.

### OCR A Level Design and Technology (H406)

OCR's specification includes dedicated content on 'Designing for the User' and 'Design Theory', requiring students to engage with design movements, theorists, and contemporary practitioners. The iterative design project (50%) must demonstrate sophisticated problem-solving through 3D design solutions.

### WJEC A Level Design and Technology (A660QS)

WJEC offers clear 3D design pathways, with assessment criteria rewarding innovation in form, sophisticated material manipulation, and manufacturing excellence.

## Pedagogical Approaches to 3D Design

### The Design Process Model

Kimbell's research challenges linear models of design education. His 'interaction of mind and hand' concept, developed through Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research in the 1980s-90s, demonstrates that effective 3D design education requires constant oscillation between thinking and making. Teachers should facilitate:

1. **Investigative activities** - exploring existing products, user needs, and contexts
2. **Focused practical tasks** - developing specific technical skills and material knowledge
3. **Design-and-make assignments** - applying knowledge through substantial projects
4. **Evaluative activities** - critical reflection on processes and outcomes

### Barlex's 'Nuffield Approach'

David Barlex's work emphasises 'capability tasks' that authentically mirror professional design practice. His framework suggests teachers should:

- Present genuine design problems with real constraints and stakeholders
- Encourage extensive primary research including user interviews and product analysis
- Facilitate rapid prototyping and iterative development
- Require rigorous testing against objective criteria
- Connect classroom work to industry standards and practices

His publication *Assessing Capability in Design and Technology* (2007) provides detailed guidance on formative assessment strategies specific to 3D design work.

### Cross's 'Designerly Thinking'

Nigel Cross's research into design cognition reveals that expert designers employ specific thinking patterns:

- **Solution-focused thinking** - proposing potential solutions early and refining them
- **Abductive reasoning** - making informed leaps from incomplete information
- **Visual-spatial reasoning** - thinking directly in three dimensions rather than translating from two dimensions

Cross argues in *Design Thinking: Understanding How Designers Think and Work* (2011) that these capabilities must be explicitly taught through structured activities rather than assumed to develop naturally.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Workshop Organisation and Safety

Effective 3D Design education requires well-organised workshop environments complying with CLEAPSS guidelines (Consortium of Local Education Authorities for the Provision of Science Services). Teachers must:

- Conduct regular risk assessments for all making activities
- Ensure appropriate supervision ratios, particularly for machinery use
- Maintain equipment to professional standards
- Model exemplary practice in tool use and material handling
- Teach explicit safety protocols before any practical work

The **CLEAPSS Secondary Science and Technology Handbook** provides definitive guidance for UK schools.

### Material Literacy Development

Kimbell emphasises that material knowledge develops through sustained handling and manipulation. Teachers should:

- Provide extensive hands-on experience with diverse materials (woods, metals, polymers, ceramics, composites)
- Facilitate systematic testing of material properties
- Create opportunities for material experimentation and failure
- Build progressively complex skills in material shaping and joining
- Connect material choices to environmental and ethical considerations

### Digital Technologies Integration

Contemporary 3D design education must embrace digital tools whilst maintaining material engagement:

- **CAD (Computer-Aided Design)** software including Fusion 360, SolidWorks, Rhino, and OnShape for parametric modelling
- **CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacture)** technologies including laser cutters, 3D printers, and CNC routers
- **Digital rendering and visualisation** tools for communication
- **Digital portfolio development** using platforms like Adobe Creative Suite or Affinity Designer

However, Barlex warns against 'technology for its own sake', arguing that digital tools must enhance rather than replace direct material engagement and hand skills.

### Assessment Practice

Effective assessment in 3D Design differs significantly from text-based subjects. Teachers should:

**Formative Assessment:**
- Conduct regular design reviews modelled on professional critique culture
- Use photographic documentation to track iterative development
- Facilitate peer assessment against clear success criteria
- Provide verbal feedback during making processes
- Employ structured reflection protocols

**Summative Assessment:**
- Apply examination board criteria consistently
- Assess design thinking processes as well as final outcomes
- Value innovation, risk-taking, and sophisticated problem-solving
- Consider technical competence in making alongside conceptual development
- Document student work comprehensively through photography

Kimbell's concept of 'authentic assessment' requires evaluating students' capability to operate as designers, not merely reproduce taught techniques.

## Cross-Curricular Connections

3D Design naturally connects to multiple curriculum areas:

**Mathematics:**
- Geometric principles and spatial reasoning
- Scale, proportion, and ratio in design development
- Measurement and accuracy in making
- Tessellation and surface development

**Science:**
- Material properties and scientific testing
- Forces, structures, and stability
- Energy transfer in mechanical systems
- Sustainability and lifecycle analysis

**Art:**
- Form, composition, and aesthetic judgement
- Colour theory and surface decoration
- Art movements and design heritage
- Contemporary practitioners and critical studies

**Computing:**
- CAD/CAM technologies and digital manufacture
- Parametric design and algorithmic thinking
- Digital portfolio development and communication

The National Curriculum explicitly encourages such connections, stating that D&T should "draw on disciplines such as mathematics, science, engineering, computing and art."

## Current Challenges and Opportunities

### Curriculum Time Pressures

The Educational Policy Institute (EPI) reports significant decline in D&T uptake since 2010, with 'creative subjects' generally receiving reduced curriculum time. School leaders often deprioritise 3D Design in favour of EBacc subjects, despite its value in developing practical capabilities and design thinking.

### Teacher Supply and Expertise

The Design and Technology Association (DATA, now simply 'The Association') reports critical shortages of specialist D&T teachers, particularly those with expertise in 3D Design. Many existing teachers lack training in contemporary digital manufacture technologies, requiring sustained professional development.

### Facilitating Excellence

Despite challenges, opportunities exist:

**Industry Partnerships:**
- Collaborations with design studios, manufacturers, and engineers
- Apprenticeship awareness and career pathway development
- Access to industry-standard equipment and expertise
- Real-world design briefs and authentic contexts

**Design Competitions and Enrichment:**
- **Big Bang Fair** regional and national competitions
- **Arkwright Scholarships** for aspiring engineers and designers
- **RSA Student Design Awards** providing authentic briefs
- **CREST Awards** linking design to STEM enrichment

**Digital Innovation:**
- Increased access to affordable CAD/CAM technologies
- Online learning platforms and tutorial resources
- Virtual collaboration tools enabling distributed design teams
- Digital portfolio platforms showcasing student excellence

## Conclusion: The Value of 3D Design

Cross argues that design capability represents "a third culture" alongside sciences and humanities, with distinctive ways of knowing and doing. 3D Design education develops:

- Spatial and material intelligence
- Creative problem-solving capabilities
- Practical making skills and craftsmanship
- Critical evaluation and refinement abilities
- User-empathy and human-centred thinking
- Project management and independent working

As the UK advances its industrial strategy and emphasises advanced manufacturing, 3D Design education provides essential foundations for future designers, engineers, architects, and makers. Kimbell's vision of 'authentic capability' - the ability to conceptualise, develop, realise, and evaluate three-dimensional design solutions - remains as relevant today as when first articulated.

Teachers of 3D Design carry responsibility for nurturing the next generation of design thinkers, ensuring young people develop both the technical competence and creative confidence to shape the material world around them.
