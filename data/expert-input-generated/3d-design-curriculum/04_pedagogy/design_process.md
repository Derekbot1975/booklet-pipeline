# Teaching the 3D Design Process

## Introduction

The 3D Design process lies at the heart of effective design and technology education in the UK, representing the iterative, creative and problem-solving journey that students undertake from initial brief to final outcome. This knowledge file provides comprehensive guidance on teaching the design process within UK 3D Design education, drawing on established curriculum frameworks, research evidence and pedagogical best practice.

## Historical Context and Key Thinkers

### Richard Kimbell's Interaction Model

Professor Richard Kimbell's seminal work, particularly through the Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research in the 1980s and 1990s, fundamentally shaped how we understand and teach design process. Kimbell's 'interaction of mind and hand' model emphasises that designing is not a linear progression through discrete stages, but rather an iterative dialogue between thinking (mind) and making (hand). His research demonstrated that effective designing involves constant oscillation between conceptual development and practical exploration through modelling and prototyping.

Kimbell argued against the 'procedural' approach to design that dominated earlier curricula, where students mechanically progressed through rigid stages. His 1997 publication 'Assessing Technology' (with Kay Stables and Richard Green) provided robust evidence that quality designing emerges when students are encouraged to work fluidly across thinking and doing activities.

### David Barlex's Pedagogical Framework

David Barlex, through his extensive work with the Nuffield Design & Technology Project and DATA (Design and Technology Association), has developed practical pedagogical strategies for teaching design process. His 'Informed Design' approach emphasises the importance of students understanding existing products, manufacturing processes and user needs before embarking on their own design work. Barlex's framework, detailed in publications such as 'Nuffield Design & Technology' (2003), advocates for:

- Front-loading of technical knowledge through focused practical tasks
- Use of design briefs that emerge from real contexts
- Structured opportunities for iterative development
- Integration of research, analysis and practical exploration

### Nigel Cross's Design Thinking Research

Nigel Cross's research into 'designerly ways of knowing', particularly his work 'Designerly Ways of Knowing' (2006), has informed understanding of how designers actually think and work. Cross identified that expert designers employ solution-focused rather than problem-focused strategies, working iteratively between problem and solution spaces. This research supports teaching approaches that encourage early practical exploration rather than excessive analysis before making.

## National Curriculum Framework

### Key Stage 3 Requirements

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology in England (2014) states that students should be taught to:

- "Use research and exploration to identify and understand user needs"
- "Identify and solve design problems and understand how to reformulate problems"
- "Develop specifications to inform the design of innovative, functional products"
- "Generate, develop, model and communicate ideas"

At Key Stage 3, the design process should be introduced progressively, with students developing confidence in each aspect whilst understanding the holistic, iterative nature of designing. Teachers should ensure students experience multiple design cycles within each year, building sophistication through repeated practice.

### Key Stage 4 and GCSE Expectations

GCSE Design and Technology specifications (introduced 2017) require students to demonstrate understanding of:

- Iterative design processes
- User-centred design approaches
- The work of past and present designers
- How to generate, develop, model and test design ideas

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure their coursework assessment (Non-Exam Assessment/Controlled Assessment) around the design process, typically allocating 50% of the qualification to practical design work.

### A-Level Expectations

A-Level Design and Technology specifications demand sophisticated application of design process, including:

- Critical analysis of existing products and design movements
- User-centred design methodologies
- Extensive iterative development through prototyping
- Industrial and commercial awareness
- Application of technical principles to design decisions

## The Design Process Model for 3D Design Education

### A Non-Linear Framework

Rather than teaching design as a rigid sequence, effective 3D Design pedagogy presents the process as a flexible framework with interconnected phases:

**1. Investigation and Research**
- User and market research
- Analysis of existing products
- Materials and technical research
- Contextual study

**2. Design Development**
- Idea generation
- Concept exploration through sketching, modelling and CAD
- Testing and refinement
- Specification development

**3. Manufacture**
- Planning and preparation
- Making with quality and precision
- Problem-solving during production
- Quality control

**4. Testing and Evaluation**
- User testing and feedback
- Performance against specification
- Critical reflection
- Proposed modifications

Students should understand that designers move fluidly between these phases, returning to research when development reveals new questions, or testing early prototypes to inform further development.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Front-Loading Technical Knowledge

David Barlex's research demonstrates that students design more successfully when they possess relevant technical knowledge before beginning open-ended design work. Effective teaching sequences include:

- **Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs)**: Short, teacher-directed activities that develop specific skills or understanding
- **Materials exploration**: Hands-on investigation of properties, working characteristics and joining methods
- **Product analysis**: Systematic examination of existing 3D products to understand construction, materials choices and design features
- **Technical demonstrations**: Teacher modelling of processes, tools and techniques

This front-loading should occur throughout a project, not just at the beginning, with new knowledge introduced as students' designs require it.

### Facilitating Iterative Development

Richard Kimbell's research shows that quality outcomes emerge from iterative cycles of thinking and making. Teachers should:

**Create expectations for iteration:**
- Require multiple design concepts (minimum of three substantially different approaches)
- Demand development work showing refinement of selected concept
- Structure assessment to reward developmental work, not just final outcomes

**Provide tools and materials for rapid prototyping:**
- Low-fidelity modelling materials (card, foam board, wire, clay)
- Quick-access CAD facilities
- Rapid prototyping technologies (3D printing, laser cutting)
- Workshop access for exploratory making

**Use formative assessment strategically:**
- Regular review sessions where students present development work
- Peer critique protocols
- Design reviews that identify specific development needs

### Teaching Sketching and Visual Communication

Three-dimensional thinking requires effective visual communication. Students should be taught:

**Sketching techniques specific to 3D Design:**
- Isometric and perspective drawing conventions
- Annotation practices that explain construction, materials and dimensions
- Rendering techniques to communicate materials and finish
- Exploded and sectional views to show internal detail

**Integration with CAD:**
- When hand sketching is more appropriate than CAD (early concepts, rapid exploration)
- How CAD can test technical feasibility of sketched concepts
- Using CAD renders alongside hand-drawn development work

E.I. Seery and J. Gordon (2016) in their research on graphicacy in design education emphasise that sketching remains fundamental even in digital contexts, as it enables rapid thinking and communication that CAD cannot replicate during initial concept development.

### User-Centred Design Approaches

Contemporary design practice emphasises understanding user needs. Teaching should include:

**Research methodologies:**
- User interviews and questionnaires
- Observation studies
- Persona development
- User journey mapping

**Involving users throughout the process:**
- Early concept testing with target users
- Prototype evaluation sessions
- Iterative refinement based on feedback

**Critical reflection on inclusive design:**
- Considering diverse user needs
- Accessibility requirements
- Cultural sensitivity in design

GCSE and A-Level specifications from all exam boards now explicitly require evidence of user-centred approaches in coursework.

## Key Stage Specific Guidance

### Key Stage 3: Building Foundations

**Year 7:**
- Introduction to the design process through structured projects with clear constraints
- Development of basic sketching and modelling skills
- Simple user research (e.g., surveying classmates)
- Projects lasting 6-8 weeks allowing complete design cycles

**Year 8:**
- Increased complexity in briefs with less prescription
- Introduction to specification writing
- More sophisticated prototype development
- Understanding that designing is iterative not linear

**Year 9:**
- Students managing their own design process with teacher guidance
- Complex, open-ended briefs
- Extended projects (10-12 weeks) allowing multiple iterations
- Critical analysis of their own and others' design process

### Key Stage 4: GCSE Application

All GCSE specifications require students to produce coursework (NEA - Non-Exam Assessment) demonstrating the full design process. Typical requirements:

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552):**
- 30-35 hours coursework
- Investigation, design, make and evaluate
- Must show iterative design development

**Edexcel GCSE Design and Technology (1DT0):**
- Approximately 30 hours coursework
- Investigation of needs, design, making, evaluation
- Evidence of prototyping and testing

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310):**
- Iterative Design Challenge (30 hours)
- Research, development, realisation, evaluation
- Marks awarded specifically for iteration and refinement

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Design and Technology (C660):**
- Design and Make Task (40 hours)
- Investigation, design, making, analysis and evaluation
- Clear requirement for ongoing evaluation throughout

Teachers should structure Year 10 to develop process understanding through shorter projects before the extended Year 11 NEA.

### Key Stage 5: A-Level Sophistication

A-Level Design and Technology requires demonstration of sophisticated design thinking:

**AQA A-Level (7552):**
- NEA coursework (50% of A-Level, 100 hours)
- Substantial project investigating and responding to needs
- Extensive iterative development required
- Commercial and industrial awareness integrated

**Edexcel A-Level (9DT0):**
- NEA project (50% of A-Level)
- Independently investigated and developed
- Must demonstrate understanding of user-centred design
- Iterative development through prototyping essential

**OCR A-Level (H404/H405):**
- Iterative Design Project (50% of A-Level)
- Substantial investigation and research phase
- Commercial viability consideration
- Multiple iterations evidenced

**WJEC A-Level:**
- Design and Technology Project (50% of A-Level, 120 hours)
- In-depth investigation of user needs
- Sophisticated iterative development
- Industrial and commercial contexts considered

## Practical Teaching Strategies

### Structuring Project Briefs

Effective design briefs balance openness with appropriate constraint:

**Well-structured briefs include:**
- Clear context explaining why the design is needed
- Identification of target user/client
- Design opportunity rather than prescribed solution
- Constraints that focus creativity (e.g., maximum size, specific material, manufacturing process)

**Avoid:**
- Overly prescriptive briefs that eliminate design decisions
- Contexts unfamiliar to students without adequate front-loading
- Unrealistic scale (too ambitious or too simple)

David Barlex emphasises that briefs should emerge from authentic contexts that students can research and understand, rather than artificial scenarios.

### Managing Time and Progress

**Establish clear phases with deadlines:**
- Research phase (with minimum requirements)
- Initial concepts (quantity requirement)
- Development phase (iteration evidence)
- Manufacturing planning
- Making time
- Testing and evaluation

**Use interim assessment strategically:**
- Design reviews at phase completion
- Self and peer assessment opportunities
- Written feedback identifying specific development needs
- Verbal feedback during practical lessons

**Avoid:**
- Allowing students to stagnate in research phase
- Permitting late starts to manufacturing
- Accepting superficial development without iteration

### Classroom Environment and Resources

**Physical space organisation:**
- Display areas showing exemplar work demonstrating process
- Access to research materials (books, product collections, material samples)
- Easy access to modelling materials
- Dedicated spaces for different activities (drawing, CAD, making)

**Resource provision:**
- Range of modelling materials always accessible
- Visual guides to drawing techniques
- Process reminder posters
- Examples of professional designers' process work

### Using Exemplar Material

Effective teachers use exemplar material strategically:

**Historical and contemporary designer case studies:**
- Showing real designers' sketchbooks and development work
- Discussing how professionals iterate and refine concepts
- Analysing design movements and influential products

**Student work from previous years:**
- Annotated exemplars showing what successful process looks like
- Work at different grade boundaries with commentary
- Examples showing specific techniques or approaches

**Exam board materials:**
- Specimen coursework with examiner comments
- Published mark schemes with detailed descriptors
- Principal examiner reports highlighting common strengths and weaknesses

## Assessment of Design Process

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Richard Kimbell's research on assessment emphasises that effective formative assessment supports learning rather than merely measuring it. Strategies include:

**Design reviews:**
- Regular (weekly or fortnightly) review sessions
- Students present current work
- Teacher and peers ask questions and make suggestions
- Focus on identifying next development steps

**Interim deadlines:**
- Checkpoint submissions for teacher feedback
- Written feedback identifying strengths and specific development needs
- Opportunity to redraft before final submission

**Self-assessment:**
- Students evaluate their own progress against success criteria
- Reflection on what's working and what needs development
- Planning next steps independently

### Summative Assessment Approaches

All GCSE and A-Level specifications assess design process through coursework NEA. Assessment typically considers:

**Investigation and research:**
- Relevance and depth of research
- Analysis of existing products
- Understanding of user needs
- Technical research appropriate to project

**Design and development:**
- Quantity and quality of initial concepts
- Iterative refinement of selected concept
- Use of modelling and prototyping
- Decision-making and justification

**Manufacture:**
- Planning and organisation
- Quality of making
- Problem-solving during production
- Accuracy and finish

**Testing and evaluation:**
- User testing evidence
- Performance against specification
- Critical reflection
- Proposed modifications

Mark schemes reward evidence of iterative development throughout, not just final outcomes.

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge: Students Over-Analysing Without Making

Students sometimes become trapped in research and analysis phases without progressing to practical design work.

**Solutions:**
- Impose time limits on research phases
- Require minimum number of sketch concepts by specific date
- Introduce practical modelling tasks early
- Value exploratory making as part of research

### Challenge: Linear, Superficial Design Process

Students sometimes produce work that appears to follow stages mechanically without genuine iteration.

**Solutions:**
- Explicitly teach what iteration looks like
- Show exemplars demonstrating real development
- Require evidence of testing and refinement
- Structure reviews that identify development needs
- Allocate marks specifically for iteration and refinement

### Challenge: Students Producing Initial Concept as Final Outcome

Without structured development, students sometimes proceed directly from initial idea to manufacture.

**Solutions:**
- Require minimum number of development pages
- Introduce interim deadlines for development work
- Model the development process through teacher demonstrations
- Use case studies showing professional designers' development processes
- Assess development work separately from final outcome

### Challenge: Weak Links Between Research and Design Decisions

Students sometimes conduct research but fail to apply findings to their design work.

**Solutions:**
- Require explicit justifications linking research to design decisions
- Use annotation to explain design choices
- Regular questioning: "How did your research inform this decision?"
- Teach specification writing that translates research into design criteria

## Integration with Technical Knowledge

Nigel Cross's research demonstrates that design thinking is fundamentally connected to domain-specific technical knowledge. Effective teaching integrates technical learning with design process:

**Materials and processes knowledge:**
- Just-in-time teaching of relevant techniques
- Focused practical tasks developing specific skills
- Material exploration through hands-on investigation

**Manufacturing understanding:**
- Teaching about industrial processes informs feasible designs
- Understanding batch and mass production influences design decisions
- Knowledge of CAD/CAM capabilities shapes design possibilities

**Technical principles:**
- Structural principles inform 3D form development
- Mechanisms knowledge enables function
- Electronics understanding expands design possibilities

This integration should occur throughout the design process, not isolated in separate lessons.

## Conclusion

Teaching the 3D Design process effectively requires understanding that design is neither a linear progression through fixed stages nor an entirely free-form creative activity. Drawing on the research of Richard Kimbell, David Barlex and Nigel Cross, effective pedagogy:

- Presents design as an iterative interaction between thinking and making
- Front-loads relevant technical knowledge whilst maintaining creative openness
- Structures projects with appropriate constraints and expectations
- Uses formative assessment to guide development
- Values the process of designing as much as final outcomes
- Integrates user-centred approaches throughout

By developing students' capabilities across research, development, making and evaluation—whilst emphasising the fluid movement between these activities—teachers enable students to think and work as designers, preparing them for both examination success and genuine design capability beyond the classroom.
