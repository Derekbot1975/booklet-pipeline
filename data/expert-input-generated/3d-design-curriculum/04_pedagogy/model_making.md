# Model Making and Prototyping

## Introduction and Pedagogical Context

Model making and prototyping represent fundamental pedagogical strategies within UK 3D Design education, serving as critical vehicles for developing students' design thinking, material understanding, and iterative problem-solving capabilities. As Richard Kimbell articulated in his seminal work 'Assessing Technology' (1997), the physical act of making enables students to engage in what he terms "thought in action" – the cognitive process whereby conceptual understanding emerges through practical manipulation of materials and forms.

David Barlex, in his extensive work with the Nuffield Design & Technology Project, emphasises that prototyping serves dual purposes: it functions both as a learning tool and as a design methodology. The physical model becomes what Barlex describes as a "conversation piece" – an artefact that mediates dialogue between designer, client, and manufacturing constraints whilst simultaneously developing the designer's understanding.

Nigel Cross, in 'Designerly Ways of Knowing' (2006), positions model making as a fundamental aspect of design cognition, arguing that designers think through making. This constructionist approach aligns with the UK National Curriculum's emphasis on "iterative design processes" and the expectation that students develop competence in "using a range of tools and equipment to perform practical tasks accurately."

## National Curriculum Framework and Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 3 Requirements

The National Curriculum in England for Design and Technology (2013) specifies that students should "develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world." Within 3D Design specifically, this manifests through requirements that students:

- Develop and communicate design ideas using annotated sketches, detailed plans, 3D and mathematical modelling
- Select from and use specialist tools, techniques, processes, equipment and machinery precisely
- Test, evaluate and refine their ideas and products against a specification, taking into account the views of intended users

Model making serves as the primary vehicle for achieving these outcomes, bridging conceptual thinking with material reality.

### Key Stage 4 Progression

At GCSE level, all major examination boards mandate substantial engagement with prototyping and model making. AQA's Design and Technology: 3D Design specification (8552) requires students to demonstrate "the use of models, prototypes and samples to test aspects of design ideas." The Non-Exam Assessment (NEA) explicitly assesses students' capacity to "manufacture prototypes that are fit for purpose" worth 50% of the final qualification.

Edexcel's Design and Technology specification (1DT0) similarly emphasises "modelling design ideas physically and digitally" within both the written examination (50%) and the NEA component. OCR's Design and Technology (J310) specification identifies "iterative design" as a core principle, with prototyping positioned as essential methodology for realising this principle in practice.

WJEC Eduqas Design and Technology (601/8180/2) specifically requires evidence of "concept modelling, prototype development, and user testing" within the Iterative Design Project component.

## Theoretical Foundations and Research Evidence

### Constructionist Learning Theory

The pedagogical power of model making derives substantially from constructionist learning theory, as developed by Seymour Papert. Richard Kimbell's application of this theory to design and technology education demonstrates that physical modelling creates what he terms "modelling behaviours" – patterns of thinking that enable students to manipulate variables, test hypotheses, and develop procedural knowledge through direct material engagement.

Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research (1991) provided empirical evidence that students who engage extensively with modelling demonstrate significantly enhanced problem-solving capabilities compared to those working primarily through drawing or computer-aided design alone. His research identified that modelling enables students to encounter and resolve "authentic problems" – challenges that emerge from material properties, structural requirements, and manufacturing constraints rather than being artificially imposed by teachers.

### Tacit Knowledge Development

David Barlex's work emphasises how prototyping develops what Michael Polanyi termed "tacit knowledge" – understanding that cannot be fully articulated verbally but resides in skilled practice. In 'Assessing Capability in Design and Technology' (Barlex & Rutland, 2008), the authors demonstrate that model making develops haptic intelligence: knowledge held in the hands and accessed through material manipulation.

This research has significant implications for assessment practices. Barlex argues that written examinations cannot adequately capture the sophisticated understanding students develop through prototyping, supporting the retention of substantial practical assessment components in GCSE and A-Level specifications.

### Design Fixation and Iteration

Nigel Cross's research into design cognition highlights how premature commitment to a single solution – "design fixation" – represents a significant barrier to innovative outcomes. Model making, when structured appropriately, provides a methodology for avoiding fixation through rapid, low-commitment exploration of multiple alternatives.

Cross's observational studies of professional designers revealed that successful practitioners create numerous quick models early in the design process, using these physical investigations to identify promising directions before committing to detailed development. This "divergent exploration followed by convergent refinement" pattern should inform pedagogical approaches in classroom contexts.

## Typology of Models in Educational Contexts

### Conceptual Models (Proof of Principle)

These early-stage models test fundamental concepts, spatial relationships, or formal qualities without concern for material accuracy or manufacturing feasibility. Richard Kimbell advocates for what he terms "quick and dirty modelling" at this stage – rapid construction using card, foam, clay, or found materials that enables students to externalise mental concepts for evaluation and discussion.

**Practical implications for teachers:**
- Establish "modelling workshops" distinct from final manufacturing sessions
- Provide accessible materials that can be manipulated quickly without specialist equipment
- Explicitly teach that these models are disposable thinking tools rather than assessed outcomes
- Use time constraints (e.g., "15-minute model challenge") to prevent over-refinement at conceptual stages

### Aesthetic Models (Appearance Prototypes)

These models simulate the final appearance, surface qualities, and visual characteristics of a proposed design without necessarily incorporating functional mechanisms or appropriate structural materials. In 3D Design education, aesthetic models prove particularly valuable for testing form, proportion, colour relationships, and visual impact.

**Key Stage 4 application:**
AQA's specification explicitly requires students to "model aesthetics to inform design decisions." Teachers should guide students in creating appearance prototypes using materials that enable high-quality surface finishes – including modelling clay, high-density foam suitable for shaping and finishing, or 3D-printed forms that can be post-processed.

### Functional Prototypes (Proof of Performance)

These models prioritise operational performance, testing whether mechanisms work, structures support required loads, or ergonomic requirements are satisfied. David Barlex's research emphasises that functional prototypes create what he terms "performance conversations" – dialogues between designer and design that reveal constraints and possibilities invisible in drawings or computer models.

**Practical implications for teachers:**
- Integrate user testing protocols where prototypes are evaluated by intended users
- Teach systematic approaches to isolating variables (testing one function at a time)
- Develop students' capacity to accept and learn from prototype "failures"
- Document performance through photography, video, and annotated analysis

### Scale Models

Scale modelling enables students to explore designs that would be impractical to prototype at full size – architectural spaces, large furniture, public installations. The Educational Technology and Society research journal (2015) published findings demonstrating that scale modelling develops sophisticated proportional reasoning and spatial cognition when integrated with mathematical calculation and CAD visualisation.

**Teaching considerations:**
- Explicitly teach scale conventions (1:10, 1:20, 1:50) aligned with professional practice
- Connect scale modelling to mathematical curriculum through calculation of dimensions
- Address the limitations of scale models (material behaviour changes at different scales)
- Use scale models as communication tools in client presentations

### Systems Prototypes

Particularly relevant in product design contexts, systems prototypes test how multiple components interact, how assembly sequences function, or how modular elements combine. OCR's specification specifically references "systems thinking" as a core competency, with physical prototyping enabling students to understand emergent properties that arise from component interactions.

## Materials and Techniques for Educational Prototyping

### Resistant Materials Prototyping

Traditional resistant materials – woods, metals, plastics – remain central to 3D Design education, providing students with understanding of material properties, manufacturing processes, and structural principles that transfer to professional contexts.

**Woods:**
Balsa and jelutong serve as excellent prototyping timbers, offering ease of cutting and shaping whilst developing students' understanding of timber's directional properties. MDF provides dimensional stability for jig-making and former construction. Barlex's "Design & Technology for the Next Generation" (2007) advocates teaching students to select prototyping materials that exhibit similar properties to intended final materials whilst being more forgiving to work.

**Metals:**
Aluminium serves as an accessible prototyping metal for secondary education, being soft enough to cut and form with standard workshop equipment whilst introducing students to metalworking principles. Mild steel wire enables exploration of linear structures and mechanisms without requiring welding capabilities.

**Plastics:**
Acrylic (polymethyl methacrylate) enables prototyping of transparent or translucent components, teaching students about thermoplastic forming processes. High-impact polystyrene (HIPS) offers ease of cutting, joining, and vacuum forming for shell constructions. Expanded polystyrene enables rapid subtractive prototyping of complex forms.

### Compliant Materials for Rapid Prototyping

Card and paperboard provide the most accessible rapid prototyping materials, enabling quick exploration of forms, structures, and mechanisms. The Design and Technology Association's "DATA Project Guides" recommend specific techniques:

**Net development and pop-up engineering** teach students about geometric development of three-dimensional forms from two-dimensional materials – a principle applicable to sheet metal work and packaging design. David Barlex's research demonstrates that students who develop competence in card modelling transfer this understanding effectively to more resistant materials.

**Corrugated card** introduces students to composite material structures and the principle of structural depth, applicable to understanding I-beams, sandwich panels, and stressed-skin constructions.

### Mouldable Materials

Modelling clay (non-hardening) enables rapid exploration of organic forms and ergonomic qualities. Polymer clays (e.g., Fimo, Sculpey) provide a progression route, offering permanence once heat-cured and enabling students to evaluate finished forms over time.

Plaster and casting resins enable students to understand reproduction processes and mould-making principles. Richard Kimbell's APU research identified mould-making as particularly valuable for developing understanding of manufacturing constraints and the relationship between positive and negative forms.

### Digital Fabrication Technologies

Computer Numerical Control (CNC) milling, laser cutting, and 3D printing increasingly feature in UK 3D Design education. The Design and Technology Association's "Guidance on 3D Printing in Schools" (2019) emphasises that digital fabrication should complement rather than replace traditional making skills.

**Pedagogical considerations for 3D printing:**
Research published in the International Journal of Technology and Design Education (2018) found that students who prototype exclusively through 3D printing develop weaker material understanding than those who combine digital and traditional making. The study recommends integrating 3D printing within broader prototyping sequences rather than as isolated activities.

Teachers should explicitly address 3D printing's limitations: build times can inhibit iterative refinement, material properties differ substantially from conventional manufacturing materials, and students may become detached from understanding how forms are constructed.

**Laser cutting applications:**
Laser cutting enables precision in sheet material prototyping, teaching students about kerf compensation, joint design, and nesting for material efficiency. AQA's NEA moderator reports consistently identify well-executed laser-cut prototypes as evidence of higher-order designing when students demonstrate understanding of tolerances, assembly sequences, and material properties.

## Pedagogical Strategies and Classroom Organisation

### Structured Iteration Frameworks

Richard Kimbell's research advocates teaching prototyping as a structured iterative process rather than a linear progression from concept to final outcome. His "Interactive Approach" model positions prototyping as cyclical: Make → Test → Evaluate → Modify → Make.

**Classroom implementation:**
- Explicitly teach iteration as a design methodology, not as remedial action following failure
- Require students to photograph and document each prototype iteration
- Assess the quality of iteration (what was learned and how it informed modification) not just final outcomes
- Display exemplar materials showing multiple iterations leading to refined solutions

### Scaffolded Risk-Taking

David Barlex emphasises that prototyping's pedagogical value depends on students feeling safe to experiment and potentially "fail." His research identifies that assessment structures focusing exclusively on final outcomes inhibit the risk-taking necessary for innovative design.

**Teaching strategies:**
- Implement formative assessment that credits experimentation and learning from unsuccessful prototypes
- Use "prototype portfolios" documenting process rather than only final pieces
- Teach explicit protocols for analysing prototype failures: What was tested? What occurred? Why? What does this reveal? How will this inform the next iteration?
- Share case studies of professional designers whose innovations emerged from prototype failures (e.g., Dyson's 5,127 cyclone prototypes)

### Resource Management and Workshop Organisation

Effective prototyping pedagogy requires careful organisation of materials, equipment, and space. The Design and Technology Association's "Timesaving and Safety in the Workshop" guidance provides frameworks for efficient resource management.

**Recommended approaches:**
- Establish dedicated "prototyping material" storage separate from "final outcome materials"
- Create mobile prototyping kits containing card, adhesives, cutting tools, and basic fixings
- Designate specific workshop zones for prototyping activities versus final manufacturing
- Implement booking systems for specialist equipment ensuring equitable access

### Integration with Computer-Aided Design

Nigel Cross's research demonstrates that professional designers move fluidly between physical and digital modelling, using each approach where it offers specific advantages. This "hybrid prototyping" approach should inform pedagogical practice.

**Practical integration:**
- Teach students to create physical models, digitise them through photography or 3D scanning, refine digitally in CAD software, then output refined versions through digital fabrication
- Use physical models as referents for CAD modelling, developing dimensional accuracy skills through measurement and transfer
- Create digital prototypes (renders, animations) for client presentations, then physical prototypes for ergonomic and functional testing

## Assessment of Prototyping and Model Making

### GCSE Assessment Frameworks

All major examination boards assess prototyping within both examined and NEA components, though emphases vary:

**AQA (8552):**
Written examination assesses students' knowledge of "modelling and testing in the development of products" (15% of paper content). NEA assessment explicitly rewards "modelling design ideas as they are developed" within the Design and Make strand.

**Edexcel (1DT0):**
The Principles component includes questions requiring students to explain how different modelling techniques support design development. The Practical Investigation and Making Project specifies that students "produce and test models, prototypes and/or samples to inform design decisions."

**OCR (J310):**
Assessment criterion "Prototype Development" (15 marks) specifically assesses iterative refinement through modelling. Moderator reports emphasise that high-achieving students demonstrate "substantial evidence of prototype iteration with clear development visible between successive models."

**WJEC Eduqas (601/8180/2):**
The Iterative Design Project mark scheme allocates 20 marks (of 100 total) to "Modelling and prototype development," requiring evidence of "testing materials, construction techniques and/or design solutions through physical and/or digital modelling."

### A-Level Assessment Considerations

AQA's A-Level Design and Technology: Product Design (7552) requires more sophisticated prototyping demonstrating understanding of industrial contexts. The specification requires students to "make iterative improvements to prototypes based on feedback and testing" and to "demonstrate technical competence in using materials, components and equipment appropriately and skilfully to manufacture high-quality prototypes."

Assessment at A-Level distinguishes between student capability levels through the sophistication of prototyping methodologies employed rather than only the quality of final outcomes.

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Richard Kimbell's "e-scape" project developed digital assessment approaches specifically designed to capture the iterative nature of design activity. Whilst e-scape itself has not been widely adopted, its principles inform current best practice:

- Portfolio assessment documenting process through annotated photographs
- "Critical friend" review protocols where students present prototypes to peers for structured feedback
- Video documentation of functional prototype testing
- Reflective annotations explaining what each prototype tested and what was learned

## Common Pedagogical Challenges and Solutions

### Time Management and Curriculum Pressure

Teachers frequently report insufficient curriculum time for extensive prototyping activities. Research by Barlex and Steeg (2017) in "Pedagogy in Design & Technology Education" identifies this as the most commonly cited barrier to effective design teaching.

**Recommended strategies:**
- Distinguish between quick conceptual modelling (15-30 minutes) and detailed prototyping (multiple lessons)
- Use homework time for low-tech modelling activities requiring minimal equipment
- Focus prototyping on critical design decisions rather than attempting to model entire designs
- Employ digital photography and annotation rather than time-consuming rendered drawings to document models
