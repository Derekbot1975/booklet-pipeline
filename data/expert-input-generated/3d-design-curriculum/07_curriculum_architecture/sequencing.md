# Sequencing 3D Design Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Curriculum Sequencing in 3D Design

Effective sequencing of 3D Design across Key Stages 3, 4, and 5 requires careful attention to the progressive development of design thinking, technical capabilities, and critical evaluation skills. As Richard Kimbell's seminal work "Assessing Technology" (1997) established, design education must move beyond simple linear progression to embrace the iterative, reflective nature of authentic design practice. The challenge for curriculum architects is to create a coherent learning journey that builds conceptual understanding whilst maintaining the creative, problem-solving essence that David Barlex identifies as central to design education.

## Theoretical Frameworks for Sequencing

### Design Thinking Development

Nigel Cross's research in "Designerly Ways of Knowing" (2006) provides essential insights into how design cognition develops. Cross argues that design thinking is a distinct form of intelligence requiring specific pedagogical approaches. In 3D Design education, this manifests through:

- **Solution-focused problem framing** (KS3 foundation)
- **Abductive reasoning** (developing through KS4)
- **Constructive problem-solving** (sophisticated application at KS5)

The sequencing must therefore progress from teacher-structured design challenges at KS3 towards increasingly open-ended, student-identified problems at KS5, reflecting Cross's observation that expert designers work differently from novices in their problem-construction strategies.

### Kimbell's Task-Action-Capability Framework

Richard Kimbell's "Task-Action-Capability" (TAC) framework, developed through the Assessment of Performance Unit work in the 1980s-90s, remains highly relevant for sequencing decisions. Kimbell demonstrated that design capability emerges through the interaction between:

- **Tasks** of increasing complexity and authenticity
- **Actions** requiring progressively sophisticated thinking and making skills
- **Capability** developing through cumulative experience

This framework suggests that curriculum sequencing should deliberately increase task complexity, demand more sophisticated actions, and scaffold developing capability through carefully structured progression.

## Key Stage 3 Foundation (Ages 11-14)

### National Curriculum Requirements

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology (2014) states that pupils should "develop the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world." At KS3, 3D Design education must establish foundational concepts whilst maintaining engagement and creativity.

### Sequencing Principles for KS3

**Year 7: Fundamental Skills and Concepts**

Begin with projects that develop:
- Basic workshop safety and material manipulation (woods, plastics, metals)
- Understanding of 3D form through simple geometric constructions
- Introduction to iterative design through structured sketch modelling
- Basic technical drawing skills (orthographic projection introduction)
- Simple CAD skills using accessible software (Tinkercad, OnShape)

David Barlex's "Nuffield Design & Technology" project emphasises that early KS3 experiences should prioritise "designerly thinking" over technical perfection. Students should experience quick design-make-evaluate cycles with forgiving materials.

**Year 8: Developing Complexity**

Progress towards:
- More complex jointing techniques and material combinations
- Understanding anthropometrics and ergonomics through practical application
- Introduction to manufacturing processes and batch production concepts
- Development of CAD skills including constraints and parametric thinking
- Analysis of existing products using ACCESS FM or similar frameworks

The sequencing here moves from "making to learn" towards "learning to make," as students begin to understand why certain processes are selected.

**Year 9: Pre-GCSE Consolidation**

Establish sophistication through:
- Extended projects requiring sustained design development
- Introduction to iterative prototyping with increasing independence
- Understanding of materials science (properties, selection, sustainability)
- More complex CAD/CAM integration including laser cutting and 3D printing
- Critical analysis of design movements and influential designers

Projects should increasingly mirror GCSE expectations, with students maintaining design portfolios that demonstrate development thinking.

## Key Stage 4 Transition and Development (Ages 14-16)

### Exam Board Specifications and Sequencing

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) structure GCSE 3D Design around similar components, but with subtle differences that affect sequencing decisions:

**AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552)**
- 50% non-exam assessment (NEA): substantial design and make project
- 50% written examination: core technical principles and specialist technical principles

**Eduqas (WJEC) GCSE Design and Technology (603/0400/0)**
- Similar 50/50 split with emphasis on "designing and making principles"

**OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310)**
- Iterative Design Challenge (50%) and Principles of Design and Technology (50%)

### Sequencing Across Years 10-11

**Year 10: Building GCSE Capability**

The transition from KS3 to KS4 represents a significant cognitive leap. Following Kimbell's research on "progressive focusing," Year 10 sequencing should:

- **Term 1**: Introduce exam board requirements through focused practical tasks (FPTs)
- Develop understanding of core technical principles through material-focused mini-projects
- Establish portfolio documentation expectations with explicit success criteria
- Practice iterative design through multiple rapid cycles before extended projects

**Focused Practical Tasks (FPTs) Approach:**
Each 4-6 week FPT should target specific technical principles:
- Task 1: Workshop skills consolidation (timber or metal)
- Task 2: Modern/smart materials application
- Task 3: CAD/CAM integration
- Task 4: Systems thinking (mechanical or electronic)

This approach, advocated by Barlex in "Authentic Design for Learning," allows students to build component skills before the extended NEA.

**Year 10 (Later) into Year 11: NEA Project**

The Non-Exam Assessment must be strategically sequenced:

1. **Contextual understanding** (investigating user needs and existing solutions)
2. **Design development** (generating, developing, and refining ideas)
3. **Realising design ideas** (making with increasing skill and accuracy)
4. **Analysing and evaluating** (testing, refining, and critical reflection)

Research by Kimbell and Perry in "Design and Technology in the Knowledge Economy" (2001) demonstrates that successful students engage in "cognitive oscillation" between designing and making. The sequencing must therefore avoid rigid stage-gates, instead encouraging iterative movement between phases.

### Theoretical Knowledge Sequencing

The written examination component requires systematic knowledge building. Following cognitive load theory, sequence technical principles from concrete to abstract:

**Year 10 Coverage:**
- Material properties and selection (tangible, testable)
- Manufacturing processes (observable, practical)
- Anthropometrics and ergonomics (measurable, applicable)
- Basic structural engineering principles (demonstrable)

**Year 11 Coverage:**
- Energy and power systems (more abstract)
- Modern/smart materials (conceptual understanding)
- Sustainability and lifecycle analysis (systems thinking)
- Commercial manufacturing and industry (contextual understanding)

## Key Stage 5 Sophistication (Ages 16-18)

### A-Level Specifications and Advanced Sequencing

A-Level 3D Design (available through AQA 7552 and other boards) demands significantly more sophisticated design thinking and technical execution. Nigel Cross's research indicates that advanced design capability requires extensive domain-specific knowledge combined with metacognitive awareness of design processes.

### Year 12: Establishing Advanced Practice

**Personal Investigation Component (typically 60% of A-Level)**

Sequencing in Year 12 should emphasise:

- **Term 1**: Experimental making and material exploration without predetermined outcomes
- Development of personal design interests and specialisms
- Advanced CAD skills including generative design approaches
- Engagement with design theory and critical perspectives
- Independent research methodologies

David Barlex's concept of "authentic design for learning" becomes crucial here. Students must move beyond teacher-set briefs towards identifying authentic problems within areas of personal interest.

**Theoretical Knowledge Development:**

Year 12 should introduce:
- Design history and critical theory (Modernism, Postmodernism, Contemporary practice)
- Advanced materials science (composite materials, nanotechnology applications)
- Sustainable design strategies (Cradle to Cradle, Circular Economy principles)
- Professional design practices and industry contexts

### Year 13: Refinement and Sophistication

**Extended Personal Investigation:**

Year 13 sequencing focuses on:
- Sustained personal project work over 3-6 months
- Integration of advanced manufacturing techniques (CNC, multi-axis machining, advanced 3D printing)
- Sophisticated design thinking evidencing iterative development
- Professional-standard presentation and communication
- Critical evaluation demonstrating metacognitive awareness

**Externally Set Assignment (typically 40% of A-Level):**

The ESA, usually conducted over 10-15 hours in examination conditions, requires:
- Rapid design thinking under time constraints
- Immediate application of technical knowledge
- Confident decision-making based on accumulated expertise

Sequencing must therefore include regular timed design challenges throughout Year 13 to develop this capability.

## Vertical Progression: Critical Concepts Across Key Stages

### Design Thinking Progression

Following Cross's research, design thinking capability should progress as follows:

**KS3:** 
- Teacher-structured problems with clear parameters
- Divergent thinking encouraged within boundaries
- Simple iterative cycles (design-make-evaluate)
- Emphasis on generating multiple ideas

**KS4:**
- Student-negotiated design contexts
- Balanced convergent and divergent thinking
- Complex iterative development with sophisticated refinement
- Evidence of progressive focusing on optimal solutions

**KS5:**
- Student-identified authentic problems
- Metacognitive awareness of design processes
- Extended iterative development with advanced prototyping
- Design decisions justified through theoretical understanding

### Technical Capability Progression

**Materials and Processes:**

- **KS3:** Fundamental manipulation of resistant materials (sawing, filing, drilling, basic joining)
- **KS4:** Advanced techniques with precision (complex joints, advanced finishing, CAD/CAM integration)
- **KS5:** Expert-level application (bespoke jigs and fixtures, advanced surface treatments, professional finishing standards)

**CAD/CAM Development:**

- **KS3:** Basic 3D modelling (primitive shapes, simple assemblies)
- **KS4:** Parametric design, constraint-based modelling, integration with manufacture
- **KS5:** Generative design, complex assemblies, simulation and analysis, professional software competency

### Critical Evaluation Progression

Richard Kimbell's Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) work established that evaluation capability develops through increasingly sophisticated criteria:

**KS3:** Simple fit-for-purpose testing against given criteria
**KS4:** Student-generated success criteria with systematic testing and user feedback
**KS5:** Sophisticated critical analysis using theoretical frameworks, comparative evaluation, and reflective practice

## Horizontal Coherence: Integration With Related Subjects

### STEM Integration

3D Design should not exist in isolation. David Barlex's work on "interdisciplinary design & technology" emphasises connections with:

- **Mathematics:** Geometric understanding, trigonometry in design, parametric relationships, data analysis in testing
- **Science:** Material properties (Chemistry), forces and structures (Physics), human factors (Biology)
- **Computing:** Algorithmic thinking in CAD, computational design approaches

Curriculum architects should identify explicit connection points in schemes of learning.

### Art and Design Coherence

3D Design shares territory with:
- Fine Art (sculptural practice)
- Graphics (communication and presentation)
- Textiles (soft product design)

Sequencing should acknowledge these connections whilst establishing 3D Design's distinct focus on functional problem-solving and technical realisation.

## Pedagogical Implications for Teachers

### Assessment for Learning in Sequenced Curricula

Formative assessment must align with curriculum sequencing. Following Kimbell's TAC framework:

- **Diagnostic assessment** at transition points (end of KS3, start of Year 12) identifies gaps in capability
- **Formative feedback** during iterative cycles focuses on design thinking processes, not just outcomes
- **Summative assessment** should measure cumulative capability development

### Adaptive Teaching and Differentiation

Sequencing must accommodate varied starting points. David Barlex advocates for "designing with students of diverse abilities," suggesting:

- **Common projects with tiered outcomes** (all students engage with core concepts, depth varies)
- **Process differentiation** (varied levels of independence and teacher support)
- **Product differentiation** (complexity of technical realisation matched to capability)

### Workshop Management and Safety Progression

Practical implications include progressive risk management:

**KS3:** Tightly supervised basic tool use, restricted machine access
**KS4:** Independent safe working with advanced tools, supervised machine use
**KS5:** Autonomous workshop practice with minimal supervision, advanced machine competency

This requires careful sequencing of technical instruction and competency assessment.

## Common Sequencing Pitfalls and Solutions

### Premature Complexity

**Problem:** Introducing advanced techniques before foundational understanding is secure.

**Solution:** Follow Kimbell's "progressive mastery" model—ensure students can perform basic operations confidently before advancing. For example, master hand-tool joinery before introducing biscuit joints or dominos.

### Disconnected Knowledge

**Problem:** Teaching technical principles separately from practical application.

**Solution:** Adopt Barlex's "just-in-time" knowledge approach where theoretical understanding is taught immediately before practical application in projects.

### Assessment Tail Wagging Curriculum Dog

**Problem:** Sequencing driven entirely by examination requirements, losing authentic design experiences.

**Solution:** Balance exam preparation with genuine design challenges. Use early KS4 for building capability through authentic projects before focusing on exam-specific requirements.

### Neglecting Foundational Skills

**Problem:** Assuming KS3 skills transfer automatically to KS4/5.

**Solution:** Build "retrieval practice" into sequencing through regular skills refreshers and progressive complexity in familiar techniques.

## Contemporary Considerations in Sequencing

### Digital Technologies Integration

Modern 3D Design requires thoughtful integration of:
- **Traditional craft skills** (hand-tool proficiency, material understanding through direct manipulation)
- **Digital design tools** (CAD, generative design, parametric thinking)
- **Hybrid making** (combining traditional and digital processes)

Sequencing should introduce digital tools to enhance, not replace, fundamental making capabilities. David Barlex warns against "premature digitalisation" where students use CAD before understanding 3D form through hand-sketching and physical modelling.

### Sustainability and Circular Economy

Contemporary 3D Design education must sequence understanding of:

**KS3:** Basic concepts of waste reduction and material selection
**KS4:** Lifecycle analysis, design for disassembly, sustainable materials
**KS5:** Circular economy principles, systems thinking, Cradle to Cradle design strategies

This progression aligns with the National Curriculum's emphasis on designing "sustainable and enterprising products."

### Inclusive Design Principles

Sequencing should progressively develop understanding of:
- Universal design principles (KS3 introduction)
- User-centred design methodologies (KS4 application)
- Inclusive design philosophy and practice (KS5 sophisticated application)

## Practical Implementation Framework

### Scheme of Learning Development

When constructing sequenced schemes of learning, curriculum leaders should:

1. **Map conceptual progression** across key stages using knowledge organisers
2. **Identify critical threshold concepts** that unlock subsequent understanding (e.g., understanding constraints in design, material properties determining process selection)
3. **Plan retrieval practice** spacing to strengthen long-term retention
4. **Build in deliberate practice** opportunities for key technical skills
5. **Ensure iterative revisiting** of core concepts at increasing sophistication

### Staff Development Implications

Effective sequencing requires teachers to understand:
- The complete KS3-5 journey (not just their specific year groups)
- Prerequisite knowledge for their teaching
- How their teaching enables future learning
- Assessment literacy around design capability development

Regular departmental moderation and vertical planning sessions support coherent sequencing.

## Conclusion: Principles for Effective Sequencing

Drawing on the research of Kimbell, Barlex, and Cross, effective sequencing of 3D Design education across key stages should:

1. **Progress from structured to open** design challenges, developing student autonomy
2. **Build technical capability systematically** whilst maintaining creative engagement
3. **Deepen theoretical understanding** in parallel with practical application
4. **Develop metacognitive awareness** of design processes and personal capability
5. **Maintain authentic design experiences** alongside examination requirements
6. **Integrate traditional craft with contemporary technologies** appropriately
7. **Embed sustainable and inclusive design thinking** progressively
8. **Enable cumulative knowledge building** through spaced retrieval and deliberate practice

As Richard Kimbell observed, "design capability is not simply acquired; it is developed through sustained engagement with increasingly complex design challenges." Effective curriculum sequencing creates the conditions for this development to flourish across the critical years of secondary education.
