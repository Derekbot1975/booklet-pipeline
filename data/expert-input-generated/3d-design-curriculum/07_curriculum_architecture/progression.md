# Progression in 3D Design Skills

## Introduction to Progression in 3D Design

Progression in 3D Design education represents the systematic development of knowledge, skills, and understanding as students advance through their educational journey. In the UK context, this progression spans from early Key Stage 3 through to A-level and beyond, encompassing both making skills and conceptual understanding. Richard Kimbell's seminal work on design and technology capability emphasises that progression is not merely about technical proficiency but involves the development of 'designerly ways of knowing' – a concept further explored by Nigel Cross in his research on design cognition.

The National Curriculum for Design and Technology (2014) establishes that students should develop 'the creative, technical and practical expertise needed to perform everyday tasks confidently and to participate successfully in an increasingly technological world'. For 3D Design specifically, progression must account for material knowledge, technical skills, design thinking, and critical evaluation capabilities.

## Theoretical Frameworks for Progression

### Kimbell's Model of Design and Technology Capability

Richard Kimbell's influential Assessment of Performance Unit (APU) research (1991) identified progression in design and technology through the interaction between 'mind' and 'hand' – the cognitive and practical aspects of design work. His model suggests that progression occurs when students develop increasingly sophisticated interaction between:

- **Conceptualising**: generating, developing and communicating ideas
- **Realising**: making, constructing and evaluating outcomes

Progression is evident when the dialogue between these domains becomes more complex, reflexive and autonomous. For 3D Design, this means moving from simple making tasks to sophisticated iterative design processes where students fluidly move between ideation, making, and refinement.

### Barlex's Taxonomy of Design Skills

David Barlex has contributed significantly to understanding progression through his work with the Nuffield Design & Technology Project. His taxonomy identifies distinct skill domains:

- **Procedural skills**: following instructions and standard techniques
- **Declarative knowledge**: understanding materials, processes and contexts
- **Strategic knowledge**: knowing when and how to apply skills appropriately
- **Reflective practice**: evaluating and improving both process and outcome

Progression occurs as students move from teacher-dependent procedural skills towards independent strategic application and reflective practice.

## Key Stage 3 Progression (Ages 11-14)

### Foundation Skills and Knowledge

At Key Stage 3, the National Curriculum requires students to develop technical knowledge including '3D and CAD modelling' and work with 'a variety of materials including resistant materials'. Progression at this stage typically follows this trajectory:

**Year 7 (Age 11-12)**:
- Basic health and safety understanding with hand tools and equipment
- Fundamental mark-making skills: measuring with rulers, use of templates
- Introduction to resistant materials: woods (softwood, hardwood, manufactured boards)
- Simple joining techniques: PVA adhesive, mechanical fixings (screws, nails)
- Basic surface finishing: sanding, painting
- Understanding of orthographic drawing conventions
- Introduction to design thinking: what problems are, who users are
- Use of simple making equipment: hand drills, coping saws, bench hooks

**Year 8 (Age 12-13)**:
- Increased precision in measuring and marking (to 1mm accuracy)
- Introduction to machinery: pillar drill, disc sander, belt sander (with close supervision)
- Expanded material knowledge: plastics (acrylic, HIPS), metals (aluminium)
- More sophisticated joining: dowel joints, housing joints, plastic bending and forming
- CAD introduction: basic 2D Design software or TinkerCAD
- Design development through sketching and annotation
- Understanding user needs through simple research
- Introduction to batch production concepts

**Year 9 (Age 13-14)**:
- Independent and accurate use of measuring and marking equipment
- Confident operation of standard workshop machinery with appropriate risk assessment
- Complex joint making: mortise and tenon, box joints, laminating
- Introduction to CAM processes: laser cutting, potentially 3D printing
- Iterative design development with multiple concepts
- Detailed technical drawing including sections and exploded diagrams
- Understanding material properties and selection criteria
- Evaluation against design criteria with suggested improvements

### Cognitive Development at KS3

Nigel Cross's research into 'designerly ways of knowing' is particularly relevant here. At KS3, students should progress from concrete, solution-focused thinking towards more abstract problem analysis. This involves:

- Moving from single right-answer thinking to appreciating multiple valid solutions
- Developing pattern recognition in design problems
- Beginning to understand design precedents and influences
- Starting to articulate design decisions with reasoning

## Key Stage 4 Progression (Ages 14-16, GCSE)

### GCSE Specifications and Progression

All major UK exam boards (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure GCSE Design and Technology with similar progression expectations. Students must demonstrate:

**Technical Skills Progression**:
- Autonomous selection and use of tools and equipment
- High accuracy and precision in making (tolerances of ±0.5mm)
- Complex forming techniques: steam bending, press forming, casting
- Advanced joining: welding (where facilities permit), complex adhesives, sophisticated mechanical joints
- Surface treatments and finishes appropriate to function and aesthetic intent
- Confident use of CAD/CAM including 3D modelling (Fusion 360, SolidWorks)
- Quality control procedures and testing protocols

**Design Thinking Progression**:
- Sophisticated user-centred research methodologies
- Analysis of existing products using ACCESS FM or similar frameworks
- Development of detailed design specifications with measurable criteria
- Extended iterative development with modelling and prototyping
- Understanding of environmental, social, and ethical considerations
- Application of anthropometric and ergonomic data
- Contextual understanding including designers, movements, and technological developments

### AQA GCSE Design and Technology (8552)

AQA's specification emphasises making skills worth 50% (NEA) with explicit assessment criteria requiring:
- **15 marks** for selecting and using tools with precision
- **20 marks** for technical competence in making
- **15 marks** for quality of outcome

Progression is demonstrated through increasingly independent working, reducing errors, and achieving outcomes that are 'fit for purpose and meet the design brief'.

### Edexcel GCSE Design and Technology (1DT0)

Edexcel structures progression through three 'core areas': understanding materials and techniques, understanding user needs, and designing and making principles. Their NEA (50%) assesses progression in:
- **Complexity of techniques**: moving beyond simple processes
- **Independence**: reducing teacher intervention
- **Accuracy and precision**: measurable improvements in tolerances
- **Finish quality**: professional standards appropriate to material and context

### OCR GCSE Design and Technology (J310)

OCR emphasises the 'iterative design process' and progression through making skills assessed via:
- Selection of appropriate materials, components, tools, and equipment
- Competence in cutting, shaping, forming, joining, and finishing
- Accuracy of manufacture
- Use of appropriate techniques to test and check quality

## Key Stage 5 Progression (Ages 16-18, A-level)

### A-level Expectations

A-level 3D Design requires significant progression beyond GCSE, with all major exam boards expecting:

**Advanced Technical Capability**:
- Mastery of complex processes requiring sustained development
- Sophisticated CAD work including parametric modelling, rendering, and simulation
- Advanced prototyping techniques potentially including CNC routing, advanced 3D printing technologies
- Professional-standard finishing and presentation
- Portfolio-quality photography and documentation
- Understanding of industrial and commercial manufacturing processes

**Design Thinking at A-level**:
- Original, creative responses to challenging briefs
- Sophisticated visual communication including technical illustration
- Critical analysis of design contexts, movements, and practitioners
- Understanding of sustainable design principles and circular economy
- Application of Human Factors research
- Professional presentation standards

### AQA A-level Design and Technology: Product Design (7552)

AQA's A-level places 50% weighting on the NEA (Personal Investigation), requiring:
- **20 marks** for investigating and analysing the work of others
- **36 marks** for exploring and creating: experimental working and iterative development
- **24 marks** for recording ideas, observations and insights
- **20 marks** for presenting a refined personal outcome

Progression from GCSE is evident through greater autonomy, sophistication of investigation, and depth of technical exploration.

### Edexcel A-level Design and Technology: Product Design (9DT0)

Edexcel structures A-level progression through:
- **NEA (50%)**: substantial design and make project requiring sustained practical ability
- Expectation of workshop independence and problem-solving
- Integration of modern and industrial practices
- Understanding of technological developments and their impact

### OCR A-level Design and Technology: Product Design (H406)

OCR's A-level emphasises the 'Iterative Design Process' with NEA (50%) requiring:
- Evidence of sustained practical ability through complex technical processes
- 45-hour minimum making time expectation
- Integration of digital and traditional techniques
- Professional standards of realisation

### WJEC Eduqas A-level Design and Technology (Product Design) (A680QS)

WJEC structures progression through three interconnected components:
- Component 1: Design Opportunities (written exam, 20%)
- Component 2: Design and Technology in Practice (written exam, 30%)
- Component 3: Design and Make Task (NEA, 50%)

Their specification explicitly requires progression in 'designing and making skills to create products and systems that demonstrate the realisation of ideas through physical form'.

## Domains of Progression in 3D Design

### 1. Material Knowledge Progression

**Foundational (KS3)**:
- Recognition of common materials (pine, MDF, acrylic, aluminium)
- Basic properties (hardness, flexibility, appearance)
- Simple working characteristics (easy to cut, requires sharp tools)

**Intermediate (KS4)**:
- Detailed material properties (mechanical, physical, aesthetic)
- Understanding stock forms and commercial sizes
- Environmental impact and sustainability considerations
- Appropriate selection based on design requirements
- Costing and feasibility understanding

**Advanced (KS5)**:
- Comparative analysis of materials with justification
- Understanding of material science principles
- Knowledge of emerging and smart materials
- Life cycle analysis considerations
- Specification writing for commercial production

### 2. Technical Skills Progression

Richard Kimbell's work emphasises that technical skills should not be taught in isolation but within meaningful design contexts. Progression involves:

**Hand Skills**:
- Measuring and marking: from ±2mm tolerance (KS3) to ±0.5mm (GCSE) to ±0.2mm (A-level)
- Cutting: from simple straight cuts to complex curves and accurate joinery
- Joining: from basic adhesives to sophisticated permanent and temporary fixings
- Finishing: from basic sanding to multi-stage finishing processes

**Machinery Skills**:
- From close supervision to independent risk assessment and operation
- From standard processes to modified and adapted techniques
- From single-material work to composite and mixed-material making

**Digital Skills**:
- From basic 2D templates to complex parametric 3D modelling
- From simple laser cutting to integrated CAD/CAM workflows
- From decorative application to functional integration of digital technologies

### 3. Design Thinking Progression

Nigel Cross's research identifies that expert designers think differently from novices. Progression in 3D Design should develop these 'designerly' capabilities:

**Problem Identification**:
- **KS3**: Teacher-provided problems, clear parameters
- **KS4**: Student-identified problems within broad contexts, some ambiguity
- **KS5**: Original problem identification requiring research and justification, embracing complexity

**Solution Generation**:
- **KS3**: Single concept development, linear progression
- **KS4**: Multiple concepts with comparative evaluation, some iteration
- **KS5**: Extensive iterative development, sophisticated synthesis of influences

**Evaluation Capability**:
- **KS3**: Simple comparison to brief, identification of obvious issues
- **KS4**: Systematic evaluation against specification, user testing, suggested improvements
- **KS5**: Critical evaluation including third-party feedback, professional standards comparison, commercial viability consideration

### 4. Visual Communication Progression

David Barlex emphasises that communication is integral to design capability. Progression includes:

**Drawing and Sketching**:
- From copying and simple representation to original visualisation
- From 2D elevations to confident 3D sketching (isometric, perspective)
- From basic annotation to technical communication with appropriate terminology
- From pencil sketches to mixed media presentation drawings

**Technical Drawing**:
- From simple dimensioned drawings to complex multi-view orthographic projection
- From basic assemblies to exploded diagrams with parts lists
- From manual drawing to CAD-generated technical illustration
- Understanding of BS8888 standards and technical communication conventions

**Portfolio and Presentation**:
- From chronological documentation to curated design narratives
- From basic photography to professional product photography with context
- From descriptive text to analytical and evaluative writing
- Integration of digital and physical presentation techniques

## Assessment of Progression

### Formative Assessment Approaches

David Barlex's work on assessment in design and technology emphasises the importance of formative assessment in supporting progression. Effective approaches include:

**Skill-specific Assessment**:
- Practical competency checklists with increasing complexity
- Video documentation of technique development
- Time-bound making challenges showing efficiency gains
- Tolerance and accuracy measurements

**Process Assessment**:
- Design review presentations at key stages
- Peer critique and feedback sessions (developing evaluative skills)
- Learning journals documenting problem-solving and decision-making
- Photographic evidence of iterative development

**Holistic Assessment**:
- Portfolio reviews using GCSE/A-level criteria at appropriate stages
- Self-assessment against exemplar materials
- Target-setting linked to specific progression indicators

### Summative Assessment Criteria

All major exam boards provide mark schemes that implicitly describe progression:

**Making Skills Bands** (adapted from exam board criteria):
- **Basic (GCSE Band 1)**: Simple techniques, teacher support required, obvious errors
- **Competent (GCSE Band 2)**: Appropriate techniques, some independence, generally accurate
- **Proficient (GCSE Band 3)**: Effective techniques, independent working, accurate with minor errors
- **Advanced (A-level expectation)**: Complex techniques, complete independence, professional standards

## Barriers to Progression and Intervention Strategies

### Common Barriers

Research by Kimbell and others identifies typical barriers to progression:

**Technical Barriers**:
- Fear of machinery or tools limiting skill development
- Poor foundational skills (especially measuring/marking) creating cumulative errors
- Lack of practice opportunities limiting muscle memory and confidence
- Limited access to digital technologies creating capability gaps

**Cognitive Barriers**:
- Fixed mindset about creative ability ('I can't draw')
- Difficulty visualising in three dimensions
- Concrete thinking limiting ability to handle ambiguity in design problems
- Lack of design vocabulary limiting communication and thought development

**Pedagogical Barriers**:
- Over-scaffolding preventing independent problem-solving development
- Task-focused teaching rather than capability-focused teaching
- Insufficient time for iterative development
- Assessment practices that reward completion over quality or learning

### Intervention Strategies

**For Technical Skills**:
- Structured 'skills passports' documenting competencies with clear next steps
- Dedicated practice time separate from project work
- Video tutorials allowing repeated viewing at student pace
- Peer mentoring with more advanced students supporting skills development

**For Design Thinking**:
- Explicit teaching of design strategies (Nigel Cross's work suggests experts use 'solution-focused' strategies that can be taught)
- Structured design review protocols building evaluative language
- Analysis of exemplar work identifying quality indicators
- Design challenges with increasing ambiguity and complexity

**For Visual Communication**:
- Drawing workshops focusing on observational skills and construction techniques
- Templates and frameworks gradually removed as confidence grows
- Regular 'quick sketch' activities building fluency
- Use of physical modelling reducing reliance on drawing ability alone

## Differentiation and Personalised Progression

### High Prior Attainers

Students demonstrating rapid progression require:
- **Extended challenges**: open briefs with complex requirements
- **Advanced techniques**: introduction to professional-level processes
- **Independent research**: exploration of specialist areas (e.g. biomimicry, parametric design)
- **Mentoring opportunities**: supporting others to consolidate and extend understanding

### Students Requiring Additional Support

Strategies aligned with Kimbell's inclusive approach to design capability:
- **Structured decomposition**: breaking complex processes into manageable steps
- **Enhanced scaffolding**: templates, jigs, and guides supporting accuracy
- **Alternative communication**: physical modelling where drawing is challenging
- **Success criteria clarity**: transparent expectations with exemplars
- **Extended time**: allowing mastery before progression
