# Photographic Genres (Portrait, Landscape, Documentary, etc.)

## Overview and Pedagogical Context

Photographic genres represent distinct categories of image-making, each with specific conventions, historical trajectories, and aesthetic considerations. Understanding genres is fundamental to UK Photography education, providing students with frameworks for analysis, contextual understanding, and practical production. As John Ingledew articulates in *Photography* (2005, Laurence King), genres function as 'visual languages' that photographers both work within and against, creating a dynamic relationship between convention and innovation.

The study of genres aligns with the **National Curriculum Art & Design** requirements at Key Stages 3 and 4, particularly the expectation that students should 'analyse and evaluate their own work, and that of others, in order to strengthen the visual impact or applications of their work' and 'know about great artists, craft makers and designers, and understand the historical and cultural development of their art forms'.

## Core Genres in UK Photography Education

### Portrait Photography

Portrait photography centres on the representation of individuals or groups, exploring identity, character, and social relationships. Charlotte Cotton, in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (2004, Thames & Hudson), identifies portraiture as 'the most direct means by which photography engages with questions of identity and selfhood'.

**Historical Development:**
- Early studio portraiture (Hill & Adamson, Julia Margaret Cameron)
- Documentary portraiture (August Sander's typological approach)
- Fashion and commercial portraiture (Cecil Beaton, Norman Parkinson)
- Contemporary conceptual portraiture (Gillian Wearing, Rineke Dijkstra)

**Key Characteristics:**
- Focus on facial features, expression, and body language
- Consideration of lighting, particularly Rembrandt, butterfly, and split lighting
- Relationship between subject and photographer
- Context and environmental elements
- Formal vs. informal approaches

**Practical Implications for Teachers:**

At **Key Stage 3**, introduce students to basic portrait conventions through practical studio sessions, examining lighting ratios and background choices. Reference accessible practitioners such as Martin Parr's observational portraits or Nadav Kander's environmental portraits.

At **Key Stage 4** (GCSE), students should engage with **AQA Photography** specifications requiring 'knowledge and understanding of art, craft and design in historical and contemporary contexts, societies and cultures'. Edexcel's Unit 1 similarly demands contextual understanding. Teachers should facilitate analysis of portraiture from diverse cultural contexts, including Zanele Muholi's self-portraits addressing LGBTQ+ identity in South Africa, or Yousuf Karsh's iconic character studies.

For **A Level**, OCR's Component 1 and AQA's Component 1 (Personal Investigation) provide opportunities for extended portrait projects. David Bate's *Photography: The Key Concepts* (2009, Berg) offers valuable theoretical frameworks, particularly regarding the 'portrait as social document' and psychoanalytic readings of facial representation.

### Landscape Photography

Landscape photography encompasses the representation of natural and built environments, raising questions about perception, environmental politics, and the sublime.

**Historical Development:**
- Picturesque and Romantic traditions (pre-photographic influence)
- Victorian topographical photography (Francis Frith)
- American West (Ansel Adams, Edward Weston)
- New Topographics movement (Robert Adams, Lewis Baltz)
- Contemporary expanded landscape (Fay Godwin, John Davies, Jem Southam)

**Key Characteristics:**
- Relationship between foreground, middle ground, and background
- Quality of light and atmospheric conditions
- Technical considerations: depth of field, exposure, filtration
- Conceptual frameworks: land as territory, property, heritage
- Environmental and political dimensions

**Practical Implications for Teachers:**

The **WJEC Photography** specification explicitly references landscape as a thematic possibility within Component 1. Teachers should encourage students to move beyond purely aesthetic appreciation toward critical engagement with landscape as ideology. David Bate argues that 'landscape is never "natural" but always already cultural', a concept accessible at Key Stage 4 through comparison of Romantic landscape painting with documentary approaches.

Practical work should address:
- Technical competence with wide-angle lenses and tripod-based working
- Understanding of the 'decisive moment' in landscape (light conditions, weather)
- Critical analysis of British landscape traditions (Fay Godwin's *Land*, 1985; John Davies' industrial landscapes)
- Contemporary practices addressing climate change and environmental degradation

Reference the **Rural England** project by Jem Southam or Chris Killip's *In Flagrante* (1988) for specifically British contextualisation.

### Documentary Photography

Documentary photography claims evidential status, representing social conditions, communities, and events with claims toward truthfulness and social engagement.

**Historical Development:**
- Social reform photography (Jacob Riis, Lewis Hine)
- Farm Security Administration (Dorothea Lange, Walker Evans)
- British documentary tradition (Bill Brandt, Humphrey Spender, Mass Observation)
- Concerned photography (Don McCullin, Philip Jones Griffiths)
- Contemporary subjective documentary (Martin Parr, Anna Fox)

**Key Characteristics:**
- Claims to authenticity and evidential status
- Social purpose and engagement
- Series or essay format rather than single images
- Relationship between photographer and subject
- Ethical considerations regarding representation

Charlotte Cotton identifies 'deadpan' documentary as a significant contemporary mode, characterised by 'apparently neutral description' that actually involves careful formal construction.

**Practical Implications for Teachers:**

Documentary photography offers rich opportunities for cross-curricular links with **Citizenship** and **PSHE** curricula. At Key Stage 3, introduce concepts of visual evidence and photographic truth through accessible projects documenting school or local community.

**AQA A Level Photography** Component 1 (60% of qualification) explicitly supports 'personal investigation' which aligns naturally with documentary approaches. Teachers should reference:

- Stuart Hall's theories of representation (encoding/decoding)
- John Tagg's *The Burden of Representation* (1988) on photography and evidence
- Martha Rosler's critique in *In, Around and Afterthoughts on Documentary Photography* (1981)

Practical considerations include:
- Ethics of consent and representation
- Power relationships between photographer and subject
- Captioning and contextualisation
- Sequencing and narrative construction
- Distribution and audience

British exemplars include Homer Sykes' *Once a Year: Some Traditional British Customs* (1977), Daniel Meadows' *National Portraits* (1978-79), and contemporary work by Juno Calypso or Laura El-Tantawy.

### Street Photography

Street photography captures candid moments in public spaces, emphasising spontaneity, observation, and the 'decisive moment' as theorised by Henri Cartier-Bresson.

**Key Characteristics:**
- Unposed, spontaneous capture
- Public spaces as sites of observation
- Often 35mm rangefinder aesthetic
- Formal interest in geometry, light, and chance
- Legal and ethical considerations specific to UK context

**UK Legal Context:**

Teachers must address UK-specific considerations regarding public photography. Under the **Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988**, photographers retain copyright, but the **Data Protection Act 2018** and **UK GDPR** create complexities regarding identifiable individuals. The **Street Photographers' Rights** guidance (available through UK Copyright Service) clarifies that photography in public spaces remains legal, but teachers should emphasise ethical considerations beyond legal minimums.

**Practical Implications:**

At **Key Stage 4**, street photography projects develop observational skills and spontaneous decision-making. Reference British practitioners including Tony Ray-Jones (*A Day Off*, 1974) and contemporary work by Dougie Wallace or Niall McDiarmid.

Technical skills include:
- Zone focusing and hyperfocal distance
- Exposure anticipation in changing conditions
- Compositional awareness and visual geometry
- Editing and sequencing for narrative impact

### Fine Art Photography

Fine art photography positions the medium within gallery contexts, emphasising conceptual depth, aesthetic sophistication, and critical engagement with photographic history and theory.

**Key Characteristics:**
- Self-reflexive engagement with the medium
- Conceptual frameworks derived from contemporary art discourse
- Often produced as limited edition prints for gallery exhibition
- Challenges to photographic conventions and expectations
- Interdisciplinary approaches incorporating installation, text, and appropriation

John Ingledew notes that fine art photography 'foregrounds ideas and concepts' above technical proficiency or documentary claims, positioning the photographer as 'artist-author'.

**Practical Implications:**

Fine art photography aligns particularly with **A Level specifications**. OCR's Component 1 (Personal Investigation) explicitly requires 'knowledge and understanding of how images relate to relevant critical/contextual material' (Assessment Objective 1).

Reference British practitioners including:
- Cindy Sherman (constructed identity)
- Victor Burgin (text and image)
- Susan Hiller (conceptual approaches)
- John Stezaker (appropriation and collage)
- Clare Strand (challenging photographic authority)

David Bate's chapter 'Art Photography' in *Photography: The Key Concepts* provides theoretical grounding, examining photography's contested status within art institutions and the influence of postmodern theory.

### Fashion and Commercial Photography

Fashion photography combines commercial imperatives with aesthetic innovation, creating aspirational imagery for publication and advertising contexts.

**Key Practitioners:**
- Norman Parkinson (British elegance and outdoor settings)
- David Bailey (1960s youth culture and energy)
- Tim Walker (theatrical, surreal narratives)
- Solve Sundsbo (digital manipulation and abstraction)
- Nadine Ijewere (diversity and contemporary representation)

**Practical Implications:**

Teachers should address the ideological dimensions of fashion imagery, including representation of bodies, gender construction, and consumer capitalism. This aligns with **Media Studies** crossover and critical media literacy objectives.

Technical considerations include:
- Studio lighting techniques (beauty lighting, fashion lighting)
- Art direction and styling
- Post-production and retouching
- Editorial vs. advertising approaches
- Publication formats and contexts

### Architectural Photography

Architectural photography documents buildings and structures, balancing technical precision with interpretive possibilities.

**Key Considerations:**
- Perspective control and shift lenses
- Relationship between building and environment
- Light quality and time of day
- Interior vs. exterior approaches
- Critical engagement with built environment

British architectural photographers include John Donat, Richard Bryant, and Hélène Binet. At A Level, students might explore the relationship between architectural photography and documentary, particularly in representing urban regeneration or social housing.

## Genre Hybridity and Contemporary Practices

Contemporary photography increasingly challenges genre boundaries. Charlotte Cotton identifies this tendency in her updated editions of *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (latest edition 2014), noting that many photographers 'resist easy categorisation'.

**Examples of Hybrid Practices:**
- Tom Hunter's staged documentary (combining tableau construction with social documentation)
- Paul Graham's *A Shimmer of Possibility* (blurring street, documentary, and fine art)
- Alec Soth's narrative portraiture within landscape contexts
- Rinko Kawauchi's intimate documentary-as-poetry

**Practical Implications:**

At **A Level**, encourage students to develop personal visual languages that may transcend single genres. **AQA A Level Photography** Assessment Objective 2 requires students to 'explore and select appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes'. This naturally supports hybrid experimentation.

Teachers should facilitate critical reflection on genre conventions as enabling constraints rather than limitations. John Ingledew suggests students ask: 'What are the conventions of this genre, and how might I work productively within or against them?'

## Assessment and Genre

UK exam specifications reference genre implicitly rather than explicitly, focusing instead on thematic or conceptual approaches:

- **AQA Photography A Level**: Component 1 (Personal Investigation, 60%) and Component 2 (Externally Set Assignment, 40%) allow genre-based projects if conceptually justified
- **Edexcel Photography A Level**: Component 1 (Personal Experimental Studies, 60%) supports genre exploration within personal thematic development
- **OCR Photography A Level**: Component 1 (Personal Investigation, 60%) explicitly requires 'evidence of personal engagement' which may utilise genre frameworks
- **WJEC Photography A Level**: Component 1 (Personal Creative Enquiry, 60%) emphasises conceptual coherence over genre adherence

All specifications assess against four Assessment Objectives focusing on contextual understanding (AO1), creative exploration (AO2), recording and observation (AO3), and personal presentation (AO4). Genre knowledge primarily supports AO1.

## Teaching Strategies and Resources

**Key Stage 3:**
- Introduction to genre through visual sorting activities
- Genre-specific technical workshops (portrait lighting, landscape composition)
- Analysis of accessible contemporary practitioners
- Local documentary projects

**Key Stage 4 (GCSE):**
- Extended genre studies with historical context
- Comparison across genres using common themes (identity, place, memory)
- Critical writing developing genre-specific vocabulary
- Portfolio development demonstrating genre understanding

**Key Stage 5 (A Level):**
- Theoretical engagement with genre as construction
- Independent research into genre histories
- Hybrid experimental work challenging genre boundaries
- Critical essay writing engaging with Cotton, Bate, and Ingledew

**Essential Resources:**
- *Photography* by John Ingledew (2005, Laurence King)
- *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* by Charlotte Cotton (2004/2014, Thames & Hudson)
- *Photography: The Key Concepts* by David Bate (2009, Berg)
- *Photography: A Cultural History* by Mary Warner Marien (2010, Laurence King)
- Online resources: Tate Photography, V&A Photography Collection, British Journal of Photography

## Conclusion

Genre provides essential frameworks for understanding photographic practice, history, and theory within UK Photography education. However, as David Bate argues, genres 'are never static categories' but rather 'dynamic cultural formations' that evolve through technological change, theoretical development, and artistic innovation. Effective teaching acknowledges genre conventions whilst encouraging critical engagement and creative experimentation that pushes beyond established boundaries.

Teachers should position genre knowledge as enabling rather than restrictive, providing students with vocabularies for analysis, historical contexts for understanding, and practical frameworks for production whilst maintaining space for individual voice and contemporary innovation.
