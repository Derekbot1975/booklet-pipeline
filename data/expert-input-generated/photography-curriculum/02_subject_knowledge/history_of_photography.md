# History of Photography

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

The history of photography forms a fundamental component of Photography education in the UK, providing essential contextual knowledge that underpins practical work and critical analysis. The subject is explicitly referenced in GCSE and A-Level specifications across all major UK exam boards, requiring students to demonstrate understanding of historical developments, key practitioners, and the evolution of photographic practice.

According to David Bate in *Art Photography* (2016), historical knowledge enables students to "understand photography as a cultural practice with its own history, technologies, and social functions." This historical framework is essential for developing critical and contextual understanding, as mandated by Ofqual's assessment objectives for Art and Design qualifications.

## Curriculum Requirements and Assessment Objectives

### National Curriculum and Examination Specifications

The National Curriculum for Art and Design at Key Stage 3 requires students to "know about great artists, craft makers and designers, and understand the historical and cultural development of their art forms." For Photography, this explicitly includes understanding the medium's evolution from its inception to contemporary practice.

**AQA GCSE Photography (8206) and A-Level (7206)** require students to demonstrate "knowledge and understanding of art, craft, design and photography, including social, historical and cultural contexts" (Assessment Objective 3). The specification explicitly mentions the requirement to study historical photographers from the 19th century onwards.

**Pearson Edexcel GCSE Art and Design: Photography (1PY0)** and A-Level (9PY0) similarly emphasise "understanding of historical and contemporary art practice and critical/contextual influences" in Assessment Objective 3.

**OCR GCSE Art and Design: Photography (J174)** and A-Level (H603) require knowledge of "historical and contemporary photography, including cultural contexts."

**WJEC/Eduqas GCSE Photography** and A-Level specifications mandate study of "different periods, styles and genres" within photographic practice.

## The Birth of Photography (1826-1850s)

### Early Photographic Processes

Photography's invention is attributed to Joseph Nicéphore Niépce, who created the first permanent photograph, *View from the Window at Le Gras*, in 1826 or 1827 using a process he called heliography. This required approximately eight hours of exposure time and represents the birth of the medium.

Louis Daguerre refined Niépce's process to create the daguerreotype, announced to the world in 1839. This process produced highly detailed, unique images on silvered copper plates and became commercially successful. William Henry Fox Talbot simultaneously developed the calotype process in England, which introduced the negative-positive principle that would dominate photography until digital technology. As John Ingledew notes in *Photography* (2013), Talbot's process was "revolutionary because it allowed multiple prints from a single negative," establishing photography's reproducibility as a defining characteristic.

### Pedagogical Implications for Key Stage 3

At Key Stage 3, students should understand:
- The basic principles of early photographic processes
- The chemical and optical foundations of image-making
- The relationship between technological development and artistic possibility

**Practical activities** might include creating photograms or cyanotypes to understand pre-digital processes, connecting historical techniques to contemporary practice.

## Victorian Photography and Documentation (1850s-1890s)

### Documentary and Social Photography

The Victorian period saw photography establish itself as a documentary medium. Roger Fenton's Crimean War photographs (1855) represent early photojournalism, though their staged nature raises questions about photographic truth that remain relevant today. Julia Margaret Cameron's portrait work (1860s-1870s) demonstrated photography's artistic potential through soft-focus, dramatically lit images that challenged the medium's documentary associations.

In social documentary, photographers like John Thomson collaborated with writer Adolphe Smith on *Street Life in London* (1877-1878), combining images with text to document poverty. This established precedents for socially engaged photography that continue through contemporary practice.

### Scientific and Colonial Photography

Victorian photography served colonial, scientific, and classificatory purposes. Medical photography, anthropological documentation, and expedition photography expanded the medium's applications whilst embedding problematic power relationships. Charlotte Cotton, in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (2020 edition), notes how "understanding photography's historical complicity in systems of power and classification" is essential for contemporary practitioners and students.

### Teaching Approach for Key Stage 4

GCSE students should examine:
- How Victorian photographers balanced documentary and aesthetic ambitions
- The relationship between photography and social reform
- Critical questions about photographic objectivity and representation
- Technical constraints and their aesthetic consequences

## Pictorialism and Art Photography (1885-1920s)

### The Pictorialist Movement

Pictorialism emerged as photographers sought recognition as artists rather than mere technicians. Practitioners like Peter Henry Emerson in Britain advocated for "naturalistic photography," whilst the Linked Ring Brotherhood (founded 1892) promoted photography as fine art. Pictorialists employed soft focus, manipulated printing processes, and chose subjects reminiscent of painting.

Alfred Stieglitz in America and the Photo-Secession movement (1902) advanced similar aims. As David Bate explains, Pictorialism represented "photography's attempt to claim cultural legitimacy through imitating established art forms," a strategy that would later be rejected by Modernist photographers.

### Educational Significance

Students studying Pictorialism should understand:
- The social and cultural forces driving photography's art world legitimacy
- Technical methods including gum bichromate, platinum printing, and soft-focus techniques
- The movement's eventual rejection in favour of "straight photography"

## Modernist Photography (1920s-1940s)

### Straight Photography and Form

The Modernist period saw photographers embrace the medium's specific characteristics rather than imitating painting. The f/64 group in America, including Ansel Adams, Edward Weston, and Imogen Cunningham, championed sharp focus, precise exposure, and celebration of photographic clarity.

European Modernism, influenced by Bauhaus principles, explored abstraction, photograms (Man Ray's "rayographs," László Moholy-Nagy's experimental work), and new perspectives. Alexander Rodchenko's dramatic angles and compositions reflected revolutionary politics and artistic innovation.

John Ingledew emphasises that Modernist photography "established vocabularies of form—line, shape, texture, tone—that remain central to photographic education." These formal elements appear explicitly in GCSE and A-Level marking criteria.

### Documentary Modernism

Simultaneously, documentary photography developed sophisticated approaches to social subjects. August Sander's systematic portrait project *People of the Twentieth Century* combined documentary impulse with Modernist classification systems. Walker Evans and the American Farm Security Administration photographers created influential documentary work during the Great Depression.

### Key Stage 4 and 5 Applications

A-Level students should critically engage with:
- Modernist formal strategies and their theoretical foundations
- The relationship between photographic form and political/social content
- How Modernist principles influence contemporary photography education
- Technical mastery and visualisation (Ansel Adams' Zone System)

## Post-War Photography (1945-1970s)

### Photojournalism and the "Decisive Moment"

Henri Cartier-Bresson's concept of the "decisive moment" and the Magnum Photos agency (founded 1947) established influential approaches to documentary and photojournalism. British photographers like Don McCullin documented conflict and social conditions, raising ethical questions about representation and witnessing that remain pedagogically valuable.

### Street Photography and Humanism

Post-war humanist photography celebrated everyday life whilst often reinforcing particular worldviews. Robert Frank's *The Americans* (1958) offered a more critical, subjective documentary approach. British photographers including Roger Mayne documented urban life, particularly youth culture, establishing vernacular photography's importance.

### Consumer Culture and Colour

The 1960s-70s saw artistic engagement with colour photography, previously considered commercial rather than artistic. William Eggleston's work legitimised colour in art contexts. British photographer Martin Parr later used colour to examine consumer culture and social class with critical irony.

Charlotte Cotton notes that this period "problematised photography's truth claims whilst expanding its expressive possibilities," a tension central to contemporary photographic education.

## Contemporary Photography and Current Practice (1980s-Present)

### Postmodern Approaches

Contemporary photography embraces staged, constructed, and conceptual approaches. Cindy Sherman's self-portraits question representation and identity. Jeff Wall's large-scale, constructed images reference art history. British photographers including Tom Hunter reinterpret classical paintings through contemporary documentary subjects, demonstrating photography's dialogic relationship with art history.

### Documentary Debates

Contemporary documentary practice questions traditional approaches. Martha Rosler's critique in "In, Around, and Afterthoughts on Documentary Photography" (1981) remains pedagogically relevant. British photographers like Richard Billingham (*Ray's a Laugh*, 1996) blur distinctions between documentary and autobiography.

### Digital Revolution and Contemporary Issues

Digital technology fundamentally altered photographic practice, education, and distribution. Mobile phone cameras, social media, and image manipulation software democratised image-making whilst raising questions about authenticity, authorship, and value.

David Bate argues that "digital technology requires reconsidering what we teach as photography's essential characteristics." Contemporary curricula must address digital manipulation, online distribution, copyright in digital contexts, and photography's relationship to lens-based media including video.

### Contemporary UK Photography Education Context

Current GCSE and A-Level specifications require students to engage with contemporary practice. All exam boards explicitly mention digital processes and contemporary practitioners. Students must understand:

- How historical practices inform contemporary work
- Digital technology's impact on photographic meaning and distribution
- Contemporary debates about photographic ethics, representation, and truth
- Photography's relationship to social media and networked image culture

## Specific Historical Themes for Pedagogical Emphasis

### Representation and Power

Throughout photography's history, questions of who photographs whom, and with what authority, remain central. Colonial photography, ethnographic documentation, fashion photography, and photojournalism all embed power relationships. Teaching historical photography must address:

- Whose perspectives are represented and whose are excluded
- How photographic subjects are positioned and described
- Photography's role in constructing social categories and hierarchies
- Contemporary practitioners addressing these historical legacies

### Technical Change and Aesthetic Possibility

John Ingledew emphasises that "technological change continually reshapes what photographs can mean and do." Understanding historical technical developments helps students recognise that:

- Aesthetic choices are often technically determined
- New technologies enable new visual languages
- Technical knowledge empowers creative decision-making
- Digital and analogue processes offer different possibilities

### Genre Development

Photography developed distinct genres—portrait, landscape, still life, documentary, fashion, scientific—each with historical trajectories that students should understand. Charlotte Cotton's genre-based analysis in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* provides useful frameworks for curriculum organisation.

## Practical Teaching Strategies and Resources

### Chronological vs Thematic Approaches

Teachers might organise historical content chronologically (as above) or thematically (by genre, concept, or technique). Most exam board specifications allow flexibility. **Thematic organisation** often better supports students' personal project development by connecting historical and contemporary practitioners addressing similar concerns.

### Primary Source Engagement

Effective history of photography teaching includes:
- Examining original prints when possible (museum/gallery visits)
- Analysing high-quality reproductions
- Reading photographers' own writings (Ansel Adams, László Moholy-Nagy, Susan Sontag)
- Contextualising images within broader social/cultural history

### Integrating Theory and Practice

Historical study should inform practical work. As David Bate notes, "historical knowledge becomes meaningful when students apply it to their own practice." Strategies include:

- Responding to historical photographers through practical projects
- Researching historical practitioners relevant to personal projects
- Understanding technical processes through historical examples
- Critically examining historical precedents for contemporary concerns

### Assessment and Historical Knowledge

All UK exam boards assess historical/contextual knowledge through Assessment Objective 3, worth 25% at GCSE and A-Level. Students demonstrate this through:

- Written annotation in portfolios/sketchbooks
- Personal Study/Essay (A-Level, 1000-3000 words depending on exam board)
- Contextual references supporting practical work
- Critical analysis of own work in relation to others

**Marking criteria** reward:
- Specific, relevant references to named practitioners
- Understanding of contextual factors influencing photographic practice
- Meaningful connections between historical and contemporary practice
- Critical rather than merely descriptive analysis

## Progression and Sequencing

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

- Introduction to photography's invention and early processes
- Basic chronological framework (Victorian to contemporary)
- Key inventors and practitioners (Daguerre, Talbot, Cameron)
- Technical foundations (camera obscura to digital cameras)
- Practical connection through accessible processes (photograms, pinhole cameras)

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16, GCSE)

- Detailed study of 3-4 historical periods or movements
- Understanding genre development
- Technical evolution and aesthetic implications
- Named practitioners with detailed image analysis
- Connection between historical and contemporary practice
- Critical engagement with representation and ethics

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18, A-Level)

- Sophisticated theoretical frameworks
- Extended critical analysis through Personal Study
- Contemporary debates informed by historical understanding
- Independent research and source evaluation
- Complex relationships between technical, aesthetic, social, and political dimensions
- Photography within broader art historical and cultural contexts

## Conclusion: Why History Matters

Understanding photography's history enables students to:

1. **Contextualise their practice** within ongoing artistic and cultural conversations
2. **Make informed creative decisions** based on historical precedents and developments
3. **Think critically** about photography's claims, functions, and effects
4. **Meet assessment requirements** across all UK exam board specifications
5. **Develop visual literacy** essential for navigating contemporary image-saturated culture

As Charlotte Cotton argues, "historical knowledge transforms students from image-makers into critically aware practitioners who understand their medium's capacities and complications." This transformation represents the central aim of photography education in UK secondary schools and colleges.

David Bate similarly emphasises that "teaching photography's history isn't about memorising dates and names, but understanding how photographic meaning is produced, circulated, and transformed across time." This sophisticated historical consciousness distinguishes accomplished student work and supports progression to higher education and professional practice.

Teachers should continually update their historical knowledge, engage with contemporary scholarship, attend exhibitions, and model critical historical thinking for students. History of photography remains not a fixed canon but a developing field, with ongoing debates about inclusion, interpretation, and relevance that enrich photography education.
