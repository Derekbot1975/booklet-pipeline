# Photography in the Curriculum Overview

## Introduction to Photography in UK Education

Photography occupies a distinctive position within the UK curriculum, sitting at the intersection of art, technology, and visual literacy. As John Ingledew articulates in *Photography* (2013, Laurence King Publishing), photography is both a "technical craft and a critical language" that enables students to understand and participate in an increasingly visual culture. In UK schools, photography is primarily delivered through Art and Design programmes at Key Stages 3 and 4, extending into specialist qualifications at Key Stage 5.

The subject has evolved considerably since the widespread adoption of digital technologies in the early 2000s, shifting from a predominantly technical discipline to one that emphasises conceptual thinking, critical analysis, and visual communication alongside practical skills.

## Statutory Framework and National Curriculum Position

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Photography is embedded within the Art and Design National Curriculum at Key Stage 3, though not explicitly named as a separate discipline. The National Curriculum for Art and Design (DfE, 2013) states that pupils should be taught to:

- "Create sketch books to record their observations and use them to review and revisit ideas"
- "Improve their mastery of art and design techniques, including drawing, painting, sculpture and other art, craft and design techniques"
- "Know about great artists, craft makers and designers, and understand the historical and cultural development of their art forms"

Photography naturally fulfils these requirements through the development of visual portfolios, mastery of technical and compositional techniques, and engagement with photographic practitioners from diverse historical periods and cultural contexts.

David Bate, in *Photography: The Key Concepts* (2016, Bloomsbury), emphasises that photography education must balance "technological competency with conceptual literacy," a principle that aligns with the National Curriculum's dual emphasis on practical skills and critical understanding.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

At Key Stage 4, photography becomes available as a discrete GCSE subject, separate from broader Art and Design qualifications, though students may also incorporate photography within general Art and Design GCSEs.

**GCSE Photography specifications are offered by:**

- **AQA** (GCSE Photography 8206) – the most widely adopted specification
- **Pearson Edexcel** (GCSE Photography 1PH0)
- **OCR** (GCSE Art and Design: Photography J174/04)
- **WJEC Eduqas** (GCSE Photography C660QS)

All specifications share common assessment objectives (AOs) mandated by Ofqual:

- **AO1**: Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and cultural understanding (25%)
- **AO2**: Refine ideas through experimenting and selecting appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes (25%)
- **AO3**: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions in visual and/or other forms (25%)
- **AO4**: Present a personal, informed and meaningful response demonstrating analytical and critical understanding, realising intentions and, where appropriate, making connections between visual, written, oral or other elements (25%)

Charlotte Cotton, in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (2014, Thames & Hudson), argues that photography education should enable students to "position their work within broader aesthetic and conceptual conversations," which these assessment objectives facilitate through their emphasis on contextual understanding and critical analysis.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

At A Level, photography is available as a distinct subject or as a pathway within Art and Design qualifications. A Level specifications include:

- **AQA A Level Photography** (7206)
- **Pearson Edexcel A Level Photography** (9PH0)
- **OCR A Level Art and Design: Photography** (H602/04)
- **WJEC Eduqas A Level Photography** (A660QS)

A Level assessment objectives mirror those at GCSE but require greater depth, independence, and sophistication in practical and critical work. The typical structure includes:

- **Component 1**: Personal Investigation (60%) – including a practical portfolio and a related study of 1,000-3,000 words
- **Component 2**: Externally Set Assignment (40%) – responding to an exam board brief under supervised conditions

## Curriculum Models and Pedagogical Approaches

### Technical and Visual Literacy Development

Effective photography curricula balance technical instruction with conceptual development. John Ingledew's *The A-Z of Visual Ideas* (2011, Laurence King Publishing) provides a framework widely adopted in UK photography departments, emphasising that technical skills serve creative vision rather than existing as ends in themselves.

**Progressive technical curriculum typically includes:**

**Year 7-8:**
- Camera handling and basic operation (exposure, focus, composition)
- Understanding light and how cameras capture it
- Introduction to formal elements (line, shape, form, texture, colour, tone, pattern)
- Basic digital workflow and editing principles

**Year 9:**
- Manual exposure control (aperture, shutter speed, ISO)
- Depth of field and motion representation
- Studio lighting fundamentals
- More sophisticated editing techniques and software proficiency

**Year 10-11 (GCSE):**
- Advanced lighting techniques (natural, artificial, mixed)
- Genre-specific technical demands (portrait, landscape, documentary, etc.)
- Analogue processes (where facilities permit)
- Professional workflow and presentation standards

**Year 12-13 (A Level):**
- Specialist techniques aligned with personal direction
- Large format, alternative processes, or advanced digital methods
- Professional-standard post-production
- Technical innovation in service of concept

### Critical and Contextual Studies

David Bate argues in *Art Photography* (2015, Tate Publishing) that photography education must develop "critical literacy" alongside practical skills, enabling students to "decode the complex visual grammar of photographic images." This contextual dimension is legally required through AO1 and significantly influences the depth of students' practical work.

**Effective contextual curriculum integration includes:**

- Study of historical movements (Pictorialism, Straight Photography, Documentary, Postmodernism)
- Contemporary practitioners and current photographic discourse
- Cultural and social contexts of image-making and consumption
- Critical frameworks (semiotics, representation, authorship, truth claims)
- Gallery and museum visits to encounter original photographic prints

Charlotte Cotton emphasises in her writing that students should understand photography's "multiple contexts" – including fine art, commercial, documentary, and vernacular traditions – recognising that these boundaries are increasingly fluid in contemporary practice.

### Project-Based Learning

UK photography curricula typically employ extended project structures that allow deep investigation of themes, techniques, and personal interests. This approach aligns with research on effective creative education, particularly the findings of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD).

**Typical project structures include:**

- **Introduction phase**: Encountering stimuli, research, and initial responses (2-3 weeks)
- **Development phase**: Experimentation with techniques, materials, and ideas (4-6 weeks)
- **Refinement phase**: Editing, selecting, and improving outcomes (2-3 weeks)
- **Presentation phase**: Final realisation and evaluation (1-2 weeks)

Projects should balance teacher-directed learning with student autonomy, progressively increasing independence from Key Stage 3 through to Key Stage 5.

## Assessment Practices and Standards

### Formative Assessment

Effective photography teaching employs continuous formative assessment through:

- **Regular portfolio reviews**: One-to-one discussions examining work in progress
- **Peer critique sessions**: Structured feedback using subject-specific vocabulary
- **Photographic exercises**: Skills-focused tasks with immediate feedback
- **Visual journals**: Ongoing documentation of thinking, research, and experimentation

John Ingledew advocates for assessment language that emphasises "visual problem-solving" rather than merely technical execution, encouraging students to articulate the relationship between intention, method, and outcome.

### Summative Assessment

At GCSE and A Level, photography is assessed through portfolio work and externally set assignments. Exam boards provide standardised criteria, with moderation ensuring national consistency.

**Key assessment considerations include:**

- **Quality over quantity**: Emphasis on depth of investigation rather than volume of work
- **Coherence**: Clear connections between research, experimentation, and outcomes
- **Personal response**: Evidence of individual interpretation and creative voice
- **Technical competence**: Appropriate skill level for the key stage
- **Critical understanding**: Meaningful engagement with contexts and practitioners

Moderation typically occurs in May-June, with centres submitting marks and sample portfolios for examination by external moderators. Teachers must ensure robust internal standardisation across groups.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Resource Requirements

**Essential resources:**
- DSLR or mirrorless cameras (ideally one per two students)
- Computing facilities with image editing software (Adobe Photoshop/Lightroom)
- Basic studio lighting equipment
- Display facilities for work-in-progress and final outcomes
- Reference library including monographs, journals, and exhibition catalogues

**Desirable resources:**
- Darkroom facilities for analogue processes
- Professional-grade lighting equipment
- Large format or medium format cameras
- Specialist printing capabilities (inkjet, darkroom)
- Mobile devices for experimental image-making

### Scheme of Work Development

Effective schemes of work for photography should:

1. **Map progression clearly** across key stages, with explicit skill and knowledge development
2. **Balance breadth and depth**, exposing students to diverse practices while allowing specialisation
3. **Integrate making and contextual study**, ensuring they inform each other
4. **Build technical competency systematically**, avoiding gaps in foundational skills
5. **Allow for differentiation**, accommodating varying abilities and interests
6. **Include regular opportunities for reflection**, developing metacognitive awareness
7. **Connect to contemporary practice**, ensuring relevance and engagement

### Inclusive Practice

Photography can be highly inclusive when appropriately scaffolded:

- **Visual nature suits diverse learners**, including those with literacy difficulties
- **Technical support can be adjusted** to maintain challenge at appropriate levels
- **Subject matter can be personalised**, allowing culturally responsive pedagogy
- **Collaboration opportunities** support social learning
- **Multiple pathways to success** (technical, conceptual, aesthetic) accommodate different strengths

However, teachers must remain alert to potential barriers, including the cost of materials, access to equipment outside lessons, and cultural assumptions embedded in photographic conventions.

### Professional Development

Photography teachers benefit from:

- **Subject association membership** (NSEAD offers photography-specific resources)
- **Gallery and museum education programmes** (Tate, V&A, National Portrait Gallery, The Photographers' Gallery)
- **Exam board training** and standardisation events
- **Engagement with contemporary practice** through exhibitions, publications, and online platforms
- **Technical skills updating** as technology evolves
- **Pedagogical research** in creative and visual education

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### Digital Technology and Changing Practice

The ubiquity of smartphone cameras and image-sharing platforms has fundamentally altered photography's cultural position. David Bate notes that "everyone is now a photographer," which paradoxically makes critical photographic education more important. Students must understand not just how to make images, but how images function in contemporary culture.

### Diversifying the Canon

There is growing recognition that photography education has historically privileged Western, predominantly male practitioners. Contemporary curricula increasingly incorporate:

- Women photographers historically marginalised (Vivian Maier, Dorothea Lange, Diane Arbus, Rineke Dijkstra)
- Non-Western photographic traditions and practitioners
- BAME photographers addressing representation and identity
- Contemporary practitioners challenging photographic conventions

Charlotte Cotton's curatorial work and writing has been influential in expanding the boundaries of what constitutes "serious" photography, legitimising vernacular, amateur, and digitally-native practices.

### Interdisciplinary Connections

Photography increasingly connects with other curriculum areas:

- **English and Media Studies**: Visual literacy, narrative, representation
- **History**: Documentary evidence, historical research methods
- **Science**: Optics, chemistry (for analogue processes), digital technology
- **Geography**: Landscape, environment, place
- **Citizenship and PSHE**: Identity, representation, ethics

Schools are experimenting with cross-curricular projects that leverage photography's broad relevance.

### Environmental and Ethical Considerations

Contemporary photography education must address:

- **Sustainability**: Digital versus analogue environmental impacts, responsible consumption
- **Ethics**: Consent, representation, appropriation, manipulation
- **Truth claims**: Understanding photography's relationship to reality in the digital age
- **Social responsibility**: Photography's power to shape perception and drive change

## Conclusion

Photography in the UK curriculum offers students powerful tools for creative expression, critical thinking, and visual communication. Effective photography education balances technical skill development with conceptual and contextual understanding, preparing students not just to make photographs, but to think photographically about themselves and their world.

As John Ingledew concludes in *Photography* (2013): "To teach photography well is to teach students to see with intention and to communicate with precision, using the unique properties of the photographic medium." This remains the core purpose of photography education across all UK key stages, delivered through progressively sophisticated engagement with practical, critical, and contextual dimensions of the discipline.

Teachers must navigate evolving technologies, expanding critical frameworks, and increasingly diverse student bodies while maintaining rigorous standards and fostering genuine creative development. When delivered effectively, photography education develops transferable skills in visual literacy, critical analysis, and creative problem-solving that serve students well beyond their formal education.
