# Photography at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview and Curriculum Context

Photography at Key Stage 5 represents the pinnacle of secondary school arts education in the UK, typically delivered through A Level qualifications for students aged 16-18. Photography A Level sits within the broader Art and Design suite of qualifications, governed by specifications from AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. Unlike Key Stages 3 and 4, there is no statutory National Curriculum at KS5; instead, specifications are regulated by Ofqual and must meet specific assessment objectives and subject content criteria.

The Photography A Level is classified under GCSE/GCE Art and Design Subject Content (2016) documentation, which mandates that students must "actively engage in the creative process of art, craft and design in order to develop as effective and independent learners, and as critical and reflective thinkers with enquiring minds."

## Examination Board Specifications

### AQA Photography A Level (7206)

AQA's specification is the most widely adopted, comprising two components: Component 1 (Personal Investigation, 60%) and Component 2 (Externally Set Assignment, 40%). The Personal Investigation includes a practical portfolio (minimum 80% of marks) and a related study of 1000-3000 words (maximum 20% of marks). David Bate's *Art Photography* (2016, Tate Publishing) is frequently cited in exemplar materials for its rigorous theoretical framework connecting photographic practice to contemporary art discourse.

### Edexcel/Pearson Photography A Level (9PH0)

Edexcel structures their qualification similarly but emphasises the "creative journey" from initial engagement to realised outcomes. Their specification explicitly references the importance of contextual understanding, where John Ingledew's *Photography* (2nd edition, Laurence King, 2011) provides valuable pedagogical support through its systematic approach to photographic thinking and making.

### OCR Photography A Level (H601)

OCR's approach distinguishes itself through explicit emphasis on "personal response" and "connections with the work of others." Their assessment criteria particularly value students who demonstrate understanding of photography's relationship to broader visual culture, an area where Charlotte Cotton's *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (4th edition, Thames & Hudson, 2020) offers essential critical vocabulary and historical frameworks.

### WJEC Eduqas Photography A Level

The Welsh board's specification maintains alignment with other UK boards whilst emphasising sustained investigative work. Their mark schemes particularly reward evidence of photographic literacy and technical proficiency alongside conceptual development.

## Assessment Objectives

All UK examination boards assess against four standardised Assessment Objectives (AOs):

**AO1: Develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and critical understanding** (25%)

This requires students to research photographers, movements, and theoretical positions. Teachers should integrate critical reading from theorists like Bate, who argues in *Photography: The Key Concepts* (2009, Berg) that photography education must move beyond technical instruction to engage with semiotics, ideology, and the photograph's social functions.

**AO2: Explore and select appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes, reviewing and refining ideas as work develops** (25%)

This encompasses both traditional darkroom and digital processes. Practical implications include maintaining well-equipped facilities with industry-standard software (Adobe Creative Cloud) and ensuring students experiment with analogue processes, alternative photographic methods, and moving image where relevant.

**AO3: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions, reflecting critically on work and progress** (25%)

Students must maintain working journals/sketchbooks documenting their creative process. John Ingledew's emphasis on "visual thinking" (2011) is particularly relevant here—students should use photography as a research methodology, not merely an output medium.

**AO4: Present a personal and meaningful response that realises intentions and, where appropriate, makes connections between visual and other elements** (25%)

Final outcomes must demonstrate technical competence, conceptual coherence, and personal voice. Charlotte Cotton's analysis of contemporary photographic practices provides students with models of how photographers "make meaning" through sequencing, presentation formats, and contextual framing (2020).

## Component 1: Personal Investigation

### The Practical Portfolio

Students produce a body of practical work developed over approximately 14 months, demonstrating all four assessment objectives. The portfolio should evidence:

- **Contextual research**: Analysis of historical and contemporary photographers, movements, and theories
- **Experimentation**: Testing different approaches, techniques, processes, and technologies
- **Development**: Evolution of ideas through iterative refinement
- **Realisation**: Polished final outcomes with considered presentation

David Bate emphasises in *The Key to Photography* (2012, AVA Publishing) that successful A Level photography requires understanding photography's "expanded field"—students should explore documentary, constructed narrative, conceptual, experimental, and lens-based moving image practices.

### The Related Study (1000-3000 words)

This written component requires sustained critical analysis connecting practical work to contextual understanding. It is not a separate essay but an integrated element supporting the practical portfolio. Teachers should guide students to:

- Identify clear research questions emerging from practical work
- Analyse primary sources (photographers' works) using appropriate critical frameworks
- Reference theoretical texts and critical writing appropriately
- Demonstrate personal voice and critical independence

Charlotte Cotton's methodological approach in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* models how to construct meaningful taxonomies of photographic practice—students might analyse their work within Cotton's categories (deadpan, something and nothing, intimate life, moments in history, revived and remade, physical and material, etc.).

## Component 2: Externally Set Assignment

Released in February for May/June assessment, this 15-hour supervised component (typically conducted over 3 days) tests students' ability to respond to an external brief under examination conditions. The preparatory period (approximately 12 weeks) allows for research, experimentation, and planning, with the supervised time dedicated to final production and presentation.

Successful preparation requires:

- **Analysis of the theme**: Deconstructing the brief's possibilities and limitations
- **Focused research**: Investigating relevant photographers and approaches
- **Experimental development**: Testing materials, techniques, and conceptual directions
- **Planning for supervision**: Clear intentions for what will be produced during the examination period
- **Resource preparation**: Ensuring all necessary materials, equipment, and digital assets are ready

John Ingledew advocates for "strategic thinking" in responding to briefs (2011)—students should identify unique angles rather than literal interpretations, demonstrating conceptual sophistication alongside technical capability.

## Technical Requirements and Facilities

### Essential Equipment and Facilities

A Level Photography requires substantial technical infrastructure:

**Digital capabilities**:
- Professional or prosumer cameras (full-frame or APS-C sensors)
- Tripods, lighting equipment (continuous and flash)
- Computers with calibrated monitors
- Industry-standard editing software (Adobe Lightroom, Photoshop)
- Large-format printing facilities (minimum A3, preferably A2)

**Analogue facilities**:
- Darkroom with enlargers, developing equipment
- Film cameras (35mm, medium format where possible)
- Chemistry and materials for black and white processing

**Studio resources**:
- Backdrop systems (paper and fabric)
- Studio lighting (minimum 2-3 light setup)
- Light modifiers (softboxes, umbrellas, reflectors)

David Bate argues in *Photography: The Key Concepts* that maintaining analogue processes remains pedagogically vital, as darkroom work provides "haptic understanding" of photographic principles that digital workflows can obscure (2009).

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Project-Based Learning

A Level Photography is delivered through extended projects allowing deep investigation. Effective projects typically:

- Begin with broad themes requiring personal interpretation
- Allow 8-12 weeks for development
- Include structured check-in points for formative assessment
- Require both research and practical outputs at each stage
- Culminate in finished bodies of work with accompanying critical reflection

### Critical Studies Integration

Rather than separating "practical" and "theory," exemplary teaching integrates contextual understanding throughout. John Ingledew's *Photography* provides a pedagogical model structured around key concepts (truth, beauty, communication, etc.) rather than chronological history, enabling students to make conceptual connections across periods and practices (2011).

### Workshop Model

Many successful A Level Photography programmes adopt a workshop approach where:

- Technical skills are taught through focused mini-projects
- Students work independently on personal investigations
- Teachers provide individual tutorials addressing specific developmental needs
- Peer critique sessions develop critical literacy
- Professional photographers visit to share practice-based insights

Charlotte Cotton emphasises the importance of students understanding "how photographers think" (2020)—not merely technical execution but the conceptual frameworks, research methodologies, and critical positions informing contemporary practice.

## Developing Photographic Literacy

### Visual Analysis Skills

Students must develop sophisticated ability to "read" photographs, understanding:

- **Formal qualities**: Composition, light, tone, colour, focus, depth of field
- **Semiotic content**: What is depicted, symbolic meanings, cultural codes
- **Contextual factors**: Where/when/why produced, intended audience, circulation
- **Conceptual frameworks**: Theoretical positions, critical debates, ideological dimensions

David Bate's *Art Photography* provides essential frameworks for this analysis, particularly his discussions of "art discourse" and how photography functions within gallery contexts versus vernacular uses (2016).

### Critical Writing Development

The Related Study requires academic writing skills often unfamiliar to arts students. Effective teaching strategies include:

- Modelling critical analysis through teacher examples
- Scaffolding research through structured questions
- Teaching referencing conventions (typically Harvard system)
- Providing feedback on draft sections
- Sharing examiner reports to clarify expectations

## Contemporary Practices and Relevance

### Digital Culture and Photography

A Level specifications increasingly acknowledge photography's expanded role in digital culture. Teachers should address:

- **Social media and image circulation**: Instagram, TikTok, and vernacular digital photography
- **Post-photography debates**: Computational photography, AI-generated imagery, deepfakes
- **Networked practices**: Collaborative projects, online communities, digital archives
- **Hybrid forms**: Relationships between still and moving image, multimedia installations

Charlotte Cotton's recent editions address these developments, noting how photography's "contemporary art" manifestations increasingly engage with digital mediation, appropriation, and the image's social life beyond the print (2020).

### Decolonising the Curriculum

There is increasing emphasis on diversifying the photographers and perspectives students encounter. Examination board exemplar materials now feature more diverse practitioners, and teachers should actively:

- Challenge Eurocentric photography histories
- Include photographers from diverse cultural backgrounds
- Address photography's colonial histories and contemporary legacies
- Examine whose stories are told and whose are marginalised
- Consider photography's role in identity construction and representation politics

David Bate's theoretical frameworks enable critical examination of photography's ideological functions, supporting teachers in developing students' critical consciousness (2009).

## Assessment and Moderation

### Internal Assessment

Teachers must assess student work against the four AOs, applying mark schemes provided by examination boards. Effective internal assessment involves:

- Regular formative feedback during development
- Clear success criteria aligned with assessment objectives
- Exemplar materials showing different grade boundaries
- Mock assessments using previous years' briefs
- Annotation of work highlighting where AOs are met

### Moderation Process

External moderators sample centres' marking to ensure national standards. Teachers should:

- Provide clear evidence of students meeting all AOs
- Annotate portfolios indicating significant work
- Maintain detailed records of the investigative process
- Photograph or document final presentations
- Prepare comprehensive authentication statements

## Progression Routes

A Level Photography provides pathways to:

### Higher Education
- Photography BA/BFA programmes
- Fine Art degrees (many students use photography within broader practice)
- Film, media, and visual culture degrees
- Graphics, illustration, advertising courses
- Art history and critical studies programmes

### Professional Practice
- Commercial photography (fashion, product, editorial)
- Photojournalism and documentary
- Technical roles (retouching, digital imaging, post-production)
- Gallery, curatorial, and arts administration
- Teaching and education

John Ingledew notes that contemporary photography education prepares students for "plural careers" combining creative practice with critical thinking applicable across creative industries (2011).

## Resources and Professional Development

### Key Texts for Teachers

- **David Bate**: *Photography: The Key Concepts* (2009), *Art Photography* (2016)—essential theoretical frameworks
- **Charlotte Cotton**: *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (4th ed., 2020)—contemporary practices and critical approaches
- **John Ingledew**: *Photography* (2011)—pedagogically structured introduction to photographic thinking

### Professional Networks

- **National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD)**: Subject association offering CPD, conferences, resources
- **Examination board training**: Regular standardisation and moderation training events
- **Regional networks**: Local authority and gallery-led photography education networks
- **University partnerships**: Links with HE institutions for portfolio preparation and progression guidance

## Conclusion

Photography at Key Stage 5 represents a sophisticated qualification requiring integration of technical craft, critical thinking, and creative independence. Successful teaching balances practical skill development with conceptual and contextual understanding, preparing students for both immediate examination success and longer-term creative and critical capabilities. The theoretical frameworks provided by thinkers like Bate, Cotton, and Ingledew are essential for moving beyond technical instruction toward photography education that engages with the medium's complex cultural, social, and artistic dimensions.

Teachers must maintain currency with examination board requirements whilst fostering genuine photographic literacy and creative voice. The challenge and opportunity of A Level Photography lies in its openness—students can explore diverse practices from documentary to conceptual, analogue to digital, representational to abstract—whilst developing the critical frameworks to understand their work within broader photographic and visual culture.
