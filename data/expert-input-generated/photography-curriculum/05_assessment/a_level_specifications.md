# A Level Photography Specifications

## Overview of A Level Photography in the UK

A Level Photography represents the culmination of secondary photography education in England, Wales and Northern Ireland, typically studied by students aged 16-18. Unlike GCSE level, A Level Photography operates as a specialist qualification under the broader umbrella of Art and Design, requiring substantial independent study, sophisticated conceptual thinking, and mastery of both technical and critical skills. As David Bate argues in *Art Photography* (2016), advanced photography education must move beyond technical competency to engage with photography as "a complex cultural practice" embedded in art history, visual theory, and contemporary discourse.

The qualification is offered by four main examination boards: AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Eduqas. Whilst each board maintains distinct specifications, all operate within parameters established by Ofqual, ensuring comparability of standards and rigour across awarding organisations.

## Regulatory Framework and Assessment Objectives

### Ofqual Requirements

All A Level Photography specifications must comply with Ofqual's *GCE Subject Level Conditions and Requirements for Art and Design* (revised 2015, updated 2023). These conditions mandate that photography qualifications must:

- Enable students to develop intellectual, imaginative, creative and intuitive capabilities
- Develop competence, critical understanding and knowledge of art, craft and design
- Develop and refine ideas and proposals, personal outcomes and solutions
- Acquire and develop technical skills through working with tools, equipment, materials and processes

### Standard Assessment Objectives (AOs)

All UK A Level Photography specifications assess four identical Assessment Objectives, weighted consistently across exam boards:

**AO1: Develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations** (25%)
Students must demonstrate informed connections with the work of others, showing critical understanding of contextual and cultural sources. John Ingledew, in *Photography* (2nd edition, 2013), emphasises that this requires "active research that goes beyond passive observation" – students must position their work within broader photographic traditions and contemporary practices.

**AO2: Explore and select appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes** (25%)
This objective assesses technical experimentation and practical refinement. Students must demonstrate increasing sophistication in working methods, from camera operation and lighting to post-production and presentation strategies.

**AO3: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions** (25%)
Charlotte Cotton describes in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (2014) how photographic thinking operates through "accumulation and edit." This AO values the generative process of visual research, demanding comprehensive documentation through annotated contact sheets, test shoots, and reflective journals.

**AO4: Present a personal and meaningful response** (25%)
The final objective assesses coherence, realisation and communication of intentions. Students must produce resolved outcomes that demonstrate sophisticated understanding of context, audience and purpose.

## Examination Board Specifications

### AQA A Level Photography (7206)

AQA's specification, the most widely adopted in England, divides assessment into two components:

**Component 1: Personal Investigation** (60% of A Level, 96 marks)
- A practical portfolio (72 marks) comprising 3,000-5,000 words of integrated critical and contextual material
- Extended written study (24 marks): 1,000-3,000 words of continuous prose

The Personal Investigation requires 15-month development, typically initiated in Year 12. AQA's *Teacher Resource Pack* (2023) emphasises that successful investigations demonstrate "clear developmental progression from initial engagement with ideas through to fully resolved intentions."

**Component 2: Externally Set Assignment (ESA)** (40% of A Level, 96 marks)
- Preparatory period (approximately 12 weeks) plus 15 hours of supervised time
- Theme released 1st February for May/June examination series

AQA provides broad thematic starting points (e.g., "Identity," "Journeys," "Transitions") requiring personal interpretation. David Bate's concept of "thematic photography" – working within subject categories whilst developing unique perspectives – proves particularly relevant here.

### Pearson Edexcel A Level Photography (9PY0)

Edexcel structures their specification around four available pathways:
- Lens-based (predominantly photography)
- Light-based (including experimental processes)
- New technologies (digital and hybrid)
- Mixed media (photography combined with other disciplines)

**Component 1: Personal Investigation** (60%, marked out of 120)
Requires integrated practical work and written material (minimum 1,000 words). Edexcel permits greater flexibility in presentation format, explicitly encouraging photobooks, installations, and multimedia outcomes.

**Component 2: Externally Set Assignment** (40%, marked out of 120)
Similar structure to AQA but with 15-hour supervised component preceded by sustained preparatory investigation.

Edexcel's specification particularly emphasises *technical competence* in their assessment guidance, requiring demonstrated "control of techniques, materials and processes appropriate to lens-based and light-based image making."

### OCR A Level Photography (H601)

OCR offers distinctive approaches within their Art and Design suite:

**Component 1: Personal Investigation** (60%)
- Portfolio of practical work
- 1,000-3,000-word contextual study (marked separately)

OCR's specification explicitly requires students to work "from direct observation, experience or constructed settings," ensuring photography engages with phenomenological reality rather than existing imagery.

**Component 2: Externally Set Task** (40%)
Eight-hour supervised examination preceded by preparatory period. OCR provides multiple questions per paper, offering students choice.

### WJEC Eduqas A Level Photography (C690QS)

The Welsh specification emphasises cultural context and Welsh-medium provision:

**Component 1: Personal Creative Inquiry** (60%)
- Practical portfolio (48 marks)
- Personal Study (1,000-3,000 words, 24 marks)

WJEC distinctively encourages engagement with Welsh cultural identity, landscape and heritage, though international contexts remain equally valid.

**Component 2: Externally Set Assignment** (40%)
Structured similarly to English specifications with preparatory period plus 15 supervised hours.

## Content Requirements Across Specifications

### Mandatory Knowledge and Skills

All specifications require students to develop understanding across:

**Photographic Processes**
- Traditional film-based processes (developing, printing, analogue manipulation)
- Digital capture and post-production (RAW workflow, Adobe suite proficiency)
- Alternative processes (cyanotype, photogram, pinhole, chemical manipulation)
- Lens-based moving image (not primary focus but permissible)

**Contextual Studies**
Students must demonstrate knowledge of historical and contemporary practitioners. Whilst boards don't prescribe specific artists, successful candidates typically engage with:
- Documentary traditions (Henri Cartier-Bresson, Dorothea Lange, Don McCullin, contemporary photojournalism)
- Conceptual photography (Cindy Sherman, Jeff Wall, Andreas Gursky, Wolfgang Tillmans)
- British photography (Martin Parr, Paul Graham, Hannah Starkey, Clare Strand)
- Contemporary practice (Rinko Kawauchi, Viviane Sassen, Alec Soth, Taryn Simon)

Charlotte Cotton's categorisations in *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* – including documentary, tableau, deadpan, and intimacy – provide useful frameworks for contextual analysis.

### Technical Competencies

John Ingledew's *Photography* outlines essential technical knowledge for advanced study:

- **Camera operation**: Manual exposure control, understanding ISO/aperture/shutter speed relationships, depth of field manipulation
- **Lighting**: Natural light control, studio lighting setups, location lighting, understanding colour temperature
- **Composition**: Advanced rule-breaking, spatial relationships, sequencing and editing
- **Post-production**: Non-destructive workflow, colour management, sophisticated manipulation, output preparation
- **Presentation**: Mounting, framing, photobook construction, exhibition design, digital presentation

## Teaching Approaches and Practical Implications

### Structuring the Two-Year Programme

Most centres adopt this structure:

**Year 12 (Lower Sixth)**
- September-December: Skills development through focused projects exploring techniques and genres
- January-July: Initiation of Personal Investigation, experimental workshops, contextual research

**Year 13 (Upper Sixth)**
- September-January: Intensive Personal Investigation development, written component completion
- February-May: ESA preparatory period and supervised examination
- Post-examination: Portfolio refinement and moderation preparation

### The Personal Investigation Challenge

David Bate emphasises that advanced photography education must develop "authorship" – the capacity to generate personally meaningful work situated within broader cultural conversations. The Personal Investigation represents the vehicle for this development.

Effective investigations demonstrate:
1. **Genuine personal engagement** with subject matter
2. **Sophisticated contextual positioning** showing understanding of photographic traditions
3. **Rigorous technical exploration** pushing beyond comfortable competencies
4. **Reflective development** documented through comprehensive annotation
5. **Coherent resolution** with clear sense of audience and purpose

### Supporting Written Components

The 1,000-3,000-word requirement challenges many visually-oriented students. Effective approaches include:

- **Early integration**: Embedding writing throughout the investigative process rather than retrospective additions
- **Visual-textual synthesis**: Encouraging integrated page layouts where text illuminates visual development
- **Incremental development**: Setting regular word-count targets preventing last-minute composition
- **Academic conventions**: Teaching proper citation (Harvard or footnote systems), bibliography construction, and analytical writing structures

John Ingledew advocates for writing that demonstrates "critical distance" – students must analyse their own practice with the same rigour applied to established photographers.

## Assessment Practices and Standardisation

### Internal Assessment and Moderation

Teachers mark both components using board-specific mark schemes, typically structured as level-based descriptors across AO strands. All work undergoes external moderation:

- **Sample moderation**: Boards request representative samples spanning achievement levels
- **Moderator visits**: Some specifications permit/require centre visits
- **Standardisation**: Teachers must attend board-provided training events

### Common Assessment Pitfalls

Examination reports consistently identify weaknesses:

1. **Superficial contextual analysis**: Name-dropping photographers without genuine critical engagement
2. **Technical limitation**: Over-reliance on single approaches (e.g., only digital manipulation)
3. **Weak investigation structure**: Absence of clear developmental trajectory
4. **Derivative outcomes**: Work that imitates rather than responds to influences
5. **Inadequate annotation**: Descriptive rather than analytical reflection

### Excellence Indicators

High-achieving work demonstrates:

- **Sophisticated visual literacy**: Understanding photography's grammatical conventions and deliberate subversion/deployment
- **Conceptual depth**: Ideas that transcend aesthetic surface, engaging with meaningful cultural questions
- **Technical ambition**: Experimental approaches evidencing substantial refinement
- **Authentic voice**: Genuinely personal perspectives rather than pastiche
- **Professional presentation**: Gallery-standard mounting, sequencing, and contextualisation

## Practical Resource Requirements

### Essential Equipment and Facilities

Delivering A Level Photography requires substantial resources:

**Core Requirements**
- DSLR or mirrorless cameras (ideally 1:2 student ratio minimum)
- Studio space with continuous/flash lighting systems
- Digital darkroom: networked computers with Adobe Creative Suite
- Large-format printers (A3 minimum, A2 preferred)
- Mounting and finishing facilities

**Desirable Additional Resources**
- Traditional darkroom facilities for film development and printing
- Medium/large format cameras
- Lighting modifiers (softboxes, reflectors, gels)
- Specialist lenses (macro, telephoto, tilt-shift)
- Exhibition spaces for final presentation

### Textbooks and Critical Resources

Essential reading includes:

- Ingledew, J. (2013) *Photography* (2nd edition), Laurence King Publishing – comprehensive technical and conceptual foundation
- Cotton, C. (2014) *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (4th edition), Thames & Hudson – essential categorisation of contemporary approaches
- Bate, D. (2016) *Art Photography*, Tate Publishing – theoretical sophistication for advanced students
- Wells, L. (2015) *Photography: A Critical Introduction* (5th edition), Routledge – academic rigour for extended writing

## Current Developments and Future Directions

### Specification Reviews and Updates

Ofqual conducts periodic reviews ensuring specifications remain current. The 2023-2024 review considered:

- **Digital practice**: Ensuring parity between traditional and contemporary methodologies
- **Moving image**: Clarifying boundaries between still and time-based work
- **AI and computational photography**: Addressing emerging technologies whilst maintaining photographic authenticity
- **Decolonisation**: Broadening contextual canons beyond Euro-American traditions

### Pedagogical Evolution

Contemporary A Level Photography teaching increasingly emphasises:

**Research-led practice**: Following David Bate's model where "theory enables sophisticated practice rather than retrospective justification"

**Authentic audiences**: Creating exhibition opportunities, publication contexts, and community engagement rather than purely academic exercises

**Cross-disciplinary connections**: Charlotte Cotton advocates for photography education that recognises "medium fluidity" – contemporary photographers increasingly work across disciplines

**Critical making**: John Ingledew's concept where technical experimentation serves conceptual investigation rather than skill acquisition for its own sake

## Progression Routes

A Level Photography prepares students for:

**Higher Education**
- BA Photography degrees (typically requiring portfolio interview)
- Related disciplines: Fine Art, Graphic Design, Fashion, Documentary, Photojournalism
- Academic programmes: Visual Culture, Art History, Cultural Studies

**Professional Pathways**
- Commercial photography (advertising, fashion, product)
- Editorial and documentary practice
- Gallery and museum sectors
- Creative industries (design, media production, digital content)

University admissions tutors prioritise conceptual sophistication and authentic creative voice over technical virtuosity alone – qualities fostered through rigorous engagement with A Level specifications.

## Conclusion

UK A Level Photography specifications provide robust frameworks for developing sophisticated photographic practitioners capable of critical thinking, technical mastery, and meaningful cultural contribution. Whilst specifications vary in structure, all share commitment to sustained investigation, contextual awareness, experimental practice, and personal voice development. Teachers must balance technical instruction with theoretical sophistication, ensuring students understand photography not merely as mechanical image-making but as complex cultural practice engaging with identity, society, politics, and aesthetics. Success requires substantial resource investment, pedagogical expertise, and commitment to developing students' authentic artistic identities within broader photographic traditions.
