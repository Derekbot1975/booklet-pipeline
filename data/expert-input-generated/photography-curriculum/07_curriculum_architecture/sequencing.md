# Sequencing Photography Projects

## Introduction to Project Sequencing in Photography Education

Sequencing photography projects represents a fundamental aspect of curriculum architecture, determining how learning progresses from foundational skills to sophisticated creative and critical practice. In UK photography education, effective sequencing must balance technical competency development with conceptual understanding, whilst responding to the requirements of examination specifications and the broader aims of visual arts education.

John Ingledew, in his seminal text *Photography* (2005, Laurence King Publishing), emphasises that photography education should move students from technical dependency towards conceptual independence. This progression principle underpins effective sequencing across all key stages. Charlotte Cotton's *The Photograph as Contemporary Art* (2004, Thames & Hudson) provides a framework for understanding photography's diverse practices, which informs how teachers might sequence exposure to different genres and approaches.

## Pedagogical Foundations of Sequencing

### Vertical Coherence

Effective sequencing requires vertical coherence across key stages, ensuring that each learning phase builds logically upon previous knowledge whilst preparing students for subsequent challenges. The National Curriculum for Art and Design (2013) requires that students should "produce creative work, exploring their ideas and recording their experiences," which necessitates a carefully structured progression from observation and recording towards personal interpretation and conceptual development.

David Bate's *Art Photography* (2016, Tate Publishing) provides crucial insight into how photographic literacy develops. Bate argues that students must first understand photography's representational conventions before they can effectively subvert or critique them. This suggests that sequencing should introduce documentary and observational photography before abstract or conceptual approaches.

### Horizontal Coherence

Horizontal coherence addresses how photography connects with other curriculum areas and how simultaneous learning experiences reinforce each other. Photography naturally intersects with English (narrative and textual analysis), History (documentary evidence), Science (optics and chemistry), and Digital Technology (image processing). Teachers should sequence projects to exploit these connections meaningfully.

## Key Stage 3: Foundational Sequencing

### Initial Projects (Years 7-8)

The foundational sequence should establish technical competency alongside visual literacy. A typical sequence might progress:

**Project 1: Camera as Tool** - Introduction to camera operations, exposure triangle, and composition fundamentals. Students explore formal elements through structured assignments focused on line, shape, pattern, and texture. This aligns with NC Art & Design requirements to "use a range of techniques to record their observations."

**Project 2: Seeing and Framing** - Development of observational skills and compositional awareness. Students examine how viewpoint, distance, and framing affect meaning. Reference to photographers like Fay Godwin (landscape) and Martin Parr (documentary) introduces genre awareness.

**Project 3: Light and Time** - Investigation of how lighting conditions and temporal factors affect photographic outcomes. Students experiment with natural and artificial light, time of day, and exposure duration. This builds technical understanding whilst introducing atmospheric and expressive possibilities.

### Progressive Projects (Year 9)

Year 9 sequencing should transition towards thematic exploration:

**Project 4: Place and Identity** - Students investigate their local environment, connecting personal experience with documentary approaches. This introduces research methodology and artist reference work, examining photographers like Anna Fox or Keith Arnatt.

**Project 5: Portraiture and Representation** - Exploration of how identity is constructed through photographic portraiture. Students examine historical and contemporary portrait conventions, working practically with natural light and environmental portraiture before progressing to more controlled studio techniques.

## Key Stage 4: GCSE Sequencing (Years 10-11)

### AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and WJEC Alignment

All major examination boards require evidence of developing, refining, recording, and presenting ideas. Sequencing at KS4 must demonstrate clear progression whilst accommodating the portfolio and controlled assessment structure.

### Year 10 Sequencing

**Autumn Term - Skills Consolidation and Extension**: Projects should revisit and deepen technical competencies whilst introducing darkroom practice (where available) or advanced digital workflows. Students should explore both digital and analogue processes, understanding their different aesthetic and conceptual implications. Charlotte Cotton's categorisation of photographic practices (documentary, tableau, deadpan, snapshot aesthetic) provides a useful framework for exposing students to diverse approaches.

**Spring Term - Thematic Development**: Students undertake extended projects requiring sustained investigation. Themes might include Constructed Realities, Urban Landscape, or Personal Documentation. These projects develop research skills, artist analysis, and personal visual language. Assessment Objective 1 (AO1: Develop ideas through investigations) receives particular emphasis.

**Summer Term - Technical Specialism**: Introduction to specialised techniques such as long exposure, multiple exposure, photomontage, or studio lighting. This supports AO2 (Refine work through experimentation) whilst preparing students for independent project work.

### Year 10-11 Transition

Summer holiday bridging projects establish independence and research methodology. Students begin preliminary investigations for coursework, identifying personal interests and potential directions. This transition period is critical for developing the self-directed practice required at KS5.

### Year 11 Sequencing

**Autumn/Spring - Portfolio Development**: Sustained personal investigation (60% of GCSE). Sequencing should support iterative development, with regular review points embedded. Students refine their conceptual focus, technical approach, and presentation strategies. John Ingledew emphasises that students should be "thinking through photography" rather than merely making photographs - this conceptual depth develops through structured reflection and refinement.

**Spring - Externally Set Assignment (ESA)**: The 10-hour controlled test (40% of GCSE) requires efficient workflow and confident technical execution. Prior sequencing should have established these capacities through progressively independent project work.

## Key Stage 5: A-Level Sequencing (Years 12-13)

### Year 12: Personal Investigation Foundation

Advanced Level photography requires sophisticated conceptual thinking alongside technical excellence. All boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) assess through Personal Investigation (60%) and Externally Set Assignment (40%).

**Autumn Term - Contextual Positioning**: Students explore historical and contemporary photography in depth, positioning their practice within wider discourse. David Bate's framework of photographic modes (documentary, portraiture, reportage, staging, appropriation) helps students understand their relationship to photographic traditions.

**Spring Term - Methodological Development**: Students develop sustained working methods, experimentation protocols, and critical reflection practices. Projects should explore multiple outcomes, testing different approaches to similar concerns. This period develops AO2 and AO3 (Record ideas and observations; make connections between sources).

**Summer Term - Independent Practice**: Extended personal project requiring minimal teacher direction. Students identify research questions, develop methodologies, and produce substantial bodies of work. The 1,000-3,000 word essay component is introduced, requiring students to articulate their practice in relation to critical and contextual material.

### Year 13: Realisation and Refinement

**Autumn Term - Personal Investigation Completion**: Students finalise their investigation, ensuring coherence between practical work, written component, and presentation. Charlotte Cotton's emphasis on photography's relationship to broader contemporary art discourse becomes particularly relevant - students should position their work within current debates and practices.

**Spring Term - ESA Preparation and Completion**: The 15-hour controlled test requires sophisticated time management and technical confidence. Prior sequencing should have established robust working methods and contingency planning capabilities.

## Micro-Sequencing Within Projects

### Lesson-Level Architecture

Individual projects require internal sequencing that models professional photographic practice:

1. **Research and Inspiration** (15% of project time): Contextual investigation, artist analysis, and initial idea generation
2. **Experimentation and Exploration** (40% of project time): Practical testing, technical trials, and iterative development
3. **Refinement and Development** (30% of project time): Focused development of successful directions
4. **Realisation and Presentation** (15% of project time): Final outcomes and presentation decisions

John Ingledew identifies that photography students often rush to final outcomes without sufficient experimentation. Embedding experimentation time within project sequencing addresses this tendency.

### Assessment Point Integration

Formative assessment should be integrated throughout project sequences, not relegated to endpoints. Mini-deadlines for research, contact sheets, test prints, and development work create accountability whilst supporting progressive development. This approach aligns with Assessment Objective 4 (Present a personal and meaningful response) by ensuring students develop considered, refined outcomes rather than rushed submissions.

## Cross-Project Coherence

### Technical Skill Progression

Sequencing should systematically develop technical competencies:

- **KS3**: Exposure control, composition basics, observational photography, basic editing
- **KS4**: Manual control mastery, lighting techniques, advanced composition, workflow efficiency, presentation strategies
- **KS5**: Specialised techniques, large format (where available), complex post-production, professional presentation standards

### Conceptual Development

Parallel to technical progression, conceptual sophistication should develop:

- **KS3**: Personal expression, thematic exploration, basic symbolism
- **KS4**: Sustained thematic investigation, contextual awareness, artist influence integration
- **KS5**: Original research questions, theoretical positioning, sophisticated critical dialogue

David Bate emphasises that photographic education must develop critical literacy - the capacity to read photographs as cultural texts. Sequencing should progressively develop this analytical capability alongside production skills.

## Responsive Sequencing

### Differentiation Through Sequencing

Effective sequencing accommodates diverse learner needs whilst maintaining high expectations. Projects can offer:

- **Multiple entry points**: Technical focus, conceptual emphasis, or research-led approaches
- **Tiered outcomes**: Core technical requirements with extension opportunities for advanced students
- **Flexible pathways**: Students pursue similar themes through different photographic approaches

### Cultural Responsiveness

Charlotte Cotton's examination of global photographic practices reminds us that photography education must represent diverse voices and traditions. Sequencing should introduce students to photographers from varied cultural backgrounds, working across different contexts and addressing diverse concerns. This enriches students' visual vocabulary whilst promoting inclusive practice.

## Practical Implementation for Teachers

### Planning Tools

**Curriculum Mapping**: Create visual maps showing how projects connect, which skills each develops, and how assessment objectives are addressed across sequences. This supports both teacher planning and student understanding of progression.

**Exemplar Archives**: Maintain annotated examples of student work at different levels across project sequences. This clarifies expectations and demonstrates progressive achievement.

**Threshold Documents**: Define clear criteria for progression between project phases. These might specify technical competencies, research depth, or conceptual sophistication required before advancing.

### Common Sequencing Pitfalls

1. **Front-loading technique**: Over-emphasising technical skills before students understand their creative application
2. **Premature conceptual demands**: Expecting sophisticated theoretical understanding before students have sufficient visual literacy
3. **Insufficient consolidation**: Moving to new projects before skills from previous work are secure
4. **Genre isolation**: Teaching photographic approaches separately without exploring their interrelationships

John Ingledew warns against "technique for technique's sake," emphasising that technical teaching should always connect to meaningful image-making purposes.

## Assessment and Sequencing Alignment

### Formative Assessment Integration

Sequencing should build formative assessment opportunities throughout:

- **Contact sheet reviews**: Regular examination of work-in-progress develops editing skills and critical judgement
- **Peer critique sessions**: Structured group reviews develop visual literacy and articulation skills
- **Tutorial checkpoints**: One-to-one discussions at defined project stages ensure individual progression

### Summative Assessment Preparation

Project sequences should gradually develop the skills required for summative assessment:

- **Portfolio construction**: Early projects should introduce selection and presentation principles
- **Annotation practices**: Progressive development from simple technical notes to sophisticated critical reflection
- **Time management**: Controlled conditions should be rehearsed through progressively independent projects

## Digital and Darkroom Integration

Where schools maintain darkroom facilities, sequencing should integrate analogue and digital processes meaningfully. Rather than treating these as separate domains, effective sequencing might:

- Introduce both processes in parallel, exploring their different aesthetic characteristics
- Use darkroom work to develop understanding of digital editing (burning, dodging, cropping)
- Examine how contemporary photographers integrate both approaches

David Bate notes that understanding analogue processes deepens appreciation of photography's materiality and indexical relationship to reality - concepts crucial for sophisticated photographic practice.

## Conclusion

Effective sequencing in photography education requires careful attention to technical progression, conceptual development, and assessment requirements. By drawing on frameworks provided by Ingledew, Cotton, Bate, and others, whilst responding to National Curriculum and examination board specifications, teachers can construct coherent learning pathways that develop confident, critically-aware photographic practitioners. The key is maintaining flexibility within structure - providing clear progression whilst accommodating individual creative development and diverse learning needs.
