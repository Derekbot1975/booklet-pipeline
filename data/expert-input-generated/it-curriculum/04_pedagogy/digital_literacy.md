# Teaching Digital Literacy

## Introduction

Digital literacy has become a cornerstone of modern education, representing far more than basic computer skills. As Miles Berry (formerly Principal Lecturer at the University of Roehampton) articulates, digital literacy encompasses the ability to "use, understand and create with technology." This knowledge file provides comprehensive guidance for teaching digital literacy within the UK Information Technology curriculum context.

The National Curriculum for Computing (2013) implicitly embeds digital literacy throughout its three strands: computer science, information technology, and digital literacy itself. Unlike its predecessor ICT curriculum, the current framework emphasises critical engagement with technology rather than mere functional skills.

## Theoretical Frameworks and Key Thinkers

### Miles Berry's Approach to Digital Literacy

Miles Berry has been instrumental in shaping UK computing education discourse. His work emphasises that digital literacy must extend beyond operational competence to include understanding "what's going on beneath the surface." Berry advocates for teaching pupils to be critical consumers and creative producers of digital content, not merely passive users. His analysis of the 2013 curriculum reform highlighted how digital literacy should be woven through all subject areas, not isolated to computing lessons.

### Neil Selwyn's Critical Perspective

Neil Selwyn (Professor at Monash University, though extensively published on UK education) brings a sociological lens to digital literacy. His seminal works, including "Education and Technology: Key Issues and Debates" (2017), challenge techno-deterministic assumptions. Selwyn argues that digital literacy teaching must address power structures, data privacy, and the commercial interests shaping digital environments. His research emphasises teaching pupils to question technology's role rather than accepting it uncritically.

### Bob Harrison and Mark Anderson's Practical Pedagogies

Bob Harrison (educator and consultant) and Mark Anderson (ICT Evangelist) have contributed significantly to practical digital literacy pedagogy. Anderson's "#PrimaryICT" movement has championed accessible approaches to teaching digital literacy in primary settings, emphasising creativity and problem-solving. Harrison's work focuses on embedding digital literacy across the curriculum whilst maintaining rigorous academic standards.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum states pupils should:
- Use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content
- Recognise common uses of information technology beyond school
- Use technology safely and respectfully

**Practical implications**: Teachers should focus on foundational skills such as using simple devices, understanding basic online safety rules, and beginning to evaluate digital content. Activities might include creating simple digital art, taking and editing photographs, or using age-appropriate search engines with guided support.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Pupils should:
- Use search technologies effectively
- Appreciate how results are selected and ranked
- Select, use and combine software on a range of digital devices
- Use technology safely, respectfully and responsibly

**Practical implications**: Digital literacy at this stage becomes more sophisticated. Teachers should introduce concepts of algorithms affecting search results, digital footprints, and critical evaluation of online sources. Miles Berry particularly emphasises teaching pupils to understand that search engines use algorithms with built-in biases and commercial interests.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Pupils should:
- Understand a range of ways to use technology safely, respectfully, responsibly and securely
- Recognise acceptable/unacceptable behaviour
- Identify a range of ways to report concerns about content and contact

**Practical implications**: Teaching should address social media literacy, cyberbullying, online identity management, and understanding terms of service. Neil Selwyn's research suggests incorporating critical discussions about data collection, surveillance capitalism, and digital rights.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

Whilst GCSE specifications focus primarily on computer science and IT, digital literacy remains essential:

**AQA GCSE Computer Science (8525)**: Paper 2 includes questions on ethical, legal and environmental impacts of digital technology.

**OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277)**: Component 01 addresses ethical, legal and environmental concerns, including data protection and computer misuse acts.

**Edexcel GCSE Computer Science (1CP2)**: Topic 6 covers ethical, legal, cultural and environmental concerns.

**WJEC GCSE Computer Science**: Unit 1 includes understanding the ethical and legal implications of digital technology.

## Core Components of Digital Literacy

### Information Literacy

Information literacy involves finding, evaluating, and using digital information effectively. According to Berry, this requires teaching pupils to:

- Understand how search algorithms work and their limitations
- Evaluate source credibility and identify bias
- Distinguish between factual content, opinion, and misinformation
- Understand Creative Commons and copyright
- Synthesise information from multiple digital sources

**Teaching strategy**: Implement "lateral reading" techniques advocated by digital literacy researchers, where pupils open multiple browser tabs to verify information across sources before engaging deeply with content.

### Media Literacy

Media literacy encompasses understanding how digital media shapes communication and culture. Mark Anderson emphasises practical creation activities that help pupils understand media construction.

**Key skills include**:
- Understanding how images, video and audio can be manipulated
- Recognising persuasive techniques in online advertising
- Creating digital media whilst considering audience and purpose
- Understanding how algorithms curate social media feeds
- Recognising echo chambers and filter bubbles

### Technical Literacy

Technical literacy bridges computer science and digital literacy, requiring users to understand enough about how technology works to use it effectively and safely.

**Essential knowledge**:
- Understanding networks and how the internet functions
- Managing files and folder structures logically
- Understanding cloud storage and synchronisation
- Troubleshooting common technical problems
- Understanding the implications of different software licences

### Social and Ethical Literacy

Neil Selwyn's research strongly emphasises this dimension. Pupils need to navigate complex social and ethical dimensions of digital participation.

**Teaching must address**:
- Digital citizenship and appropriate online behaviour
- Understanding and managing digital identity
- Privacy settings and data protection (including GDPR implications)
- Recognising and responding to cyberbullying
- Understanding the addictive design of social media and apps
- Environmental impact of digital technology consumption

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Critical Digital Pedagogy

Drawing on Selwyn's work, critical digital pedagogy encourages pupils to question technology rather than accepting it uncritically. This approach involves:

- **Deconstructing interfaces**: Analysing why apps and websites are designed as they are, identifying persuasive design patterns
- **Examining ownership**: Understanding which companies own popular platforms and their business models
- **Discussing alternatives**: Exploring open-source software and privacy-respecting alternatives to mainstream platforms
- **Analysing bias**: Investigating how algorithms can perpetuate discrimination

**Practical example**: When teaching about social media, rather than focusing solely on safety rules, facilitate discussions about why platforms are free to use, how targeted advertising works, and what data companies collect.

### Constructionist Approaches

Miles Berry advocates for constructionist learning where pupils build understanding through creating digital artefacts. This aligns with Seymour Papert's constructionism theory.

**Implementation strategies**:
- Pupils create digital content (videos, podcasts, blogs) to demonstrate understanding of digital literacy concepts
- Project-based learning where pupils research and create campaigns about online safety issues
- Pupils design their own social media platform or app interface, considering ethical implications
- Creating tutorials or guides for younger pupils about digital literacy topics

### Scaffolded Inquiry

Bob Harrison's work emphasises structured approaches that gradually develop independence:

1. **Modelled exploration**: Teacher demonstrates evaluating a website for credibility
2. **Guided practice**: Pupils work in groups with teacher support to analyse sources
3. **Independent application**: Pupils independently research topics and evaluate sources
4. **Metacognitive reflection**: Pupils explain their evaluation processes

### Cross-Curricular Integration

Digital literacy should not be isolated to computing lessons. The National Curriculum recognises that "all teachers have a responsibility to ensure that pupils are able to use technology effectively."

**Subject-specific applications**:
- **English**: Evaluating online sources for research, understanding how digital texts differ from print
- **History**: Assessing reliability of online historical sources, understanding digital archives
- **Science**: Using data-logging software, accessing scientific databases, evaluating scientific claims online
- **PSHE**: Online relationships, cyberbullying, managing screen time
- **Citizenship**: Digital democracy, online activism, data rights

## Age-Appropriate Progression

### Foundation Stage and Key Stage 1

Focus on basic operational skills and fundamental safety concepts:
- Using approved websites and apps with teacher guidance
- Understanding that online resources are created by real people
- Knowing when to ask for adult help online
- Beginning to recognise that not all online information is accurate

### Lower Key Stage 2 (Years 3-4)

Developing independence with continued support:
- Using search engines with increasing independence
- Beginning to evaluate website credibility using simple criteria
- Understanding concepts like usernames and passwords
- Recognising that online actions have consequences

### Upper Key Stage 2 (Years 5-6)

Increasing sophistication and critical thinking:
- Understanding how search results are ordered
- Comparing information across multiple sources
- Creating strong passwords and understanding why this matters
- Beginning to understand how social media works
- Recognising online advertising and persuasive techniques

### Key Stage 3

Critical engagement and ethical awareness:
- Deep understanding of how algorithms shape online experiences
- Critical analysis of online information sources
- Understanding data protection principles (particularly relevant post-GDPR)
- Examining digital divides and inequalities
- Understanding business models of major technology companies

### Key Stage 4

Sophisticated analysis and responsible practice:
- Understanding legal frameworks (Data Protection Act 2018, Computer Misuse Act 1990)
- Analysing ethical dilemmas in technology use
- Understanding implications of artificial intelligence and automation
- Considering environmental impacts of digital technology
- Developing personal digital well-being strategies

## Assessment Approaches

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Digital literacy presents unique assessment challenges as it encompasses knowledge, skills, and dispositions. Berry emphasises that assessment should focus on understanding and critical thinking rather than mere technical competence.

**Effective formative approaches**:
- **Digital literacy portfolios**: Pupils curate examples of their critical engagement with digital content
- **Think-alouds**: Pupils verbalise their thinking whilst evaluating online sources
- **Peer evaluation**: Pupils assess each other's digital creations using agreed criteria
- **Reflective journals**: Pupils document their digital practices and reflect on improvements

### Summative Assessment

At GCSE level, digital literacy is assessed indirectly through:
- Written examinations testing knowledge of ethical, legal and environmental issues
- Practical projects requiring appropriate tool selection and safe working practices
- Understanding of data protection and privacy in NEA (Non-Examined Assessment) contexts

**OCR Cambridge Nationals in IT (J808)**: More explicitly assesses digital literacy through:
- R012: Understanding tools, safety and security
- R018: Design and create a digital animation requiring appropriate tool selection

## Addressing Current Challenges

### Misinformation and "Fake News"

Neil Selwyn's research acknowledges that combating misinformation requires more than fact-checking skills. Teachers must help pupils understand:
- Economic incentives for creating misleading content
- Confirmation bias and motivated reasoning
- How misinformation spreads through social networks
- Techniques for lateral reading and source verification

**Practical strategy**: Use real examples from recent events, analysing how misleading information spread and how it could be identified. Resources like BBC Bitesize's Reality Check and Full Fact provide educational materials.

### Social Media and Well-being

Mark Anderson and other practitioners emphasise balanced approaches that acknowledge both benefits and risks of social media:
- Teaching about persuasive design and "attention economy"
- Discussing parasocial relationships and online comparison
- Strategies for managing screen time and digital well-being
- Understanding FOMO (fear of missing out) and other psychological effects

### Privacy and Data Protection

Post-GDPR, understanding data protection is crucial. Teaching should cover:
- What personal data means and why it matters
- Rights under Data Protection Act 2018 (including right to access, rectification, erasure)
- How to read privacy policies effectively
- Understanding cookies and tracking technologies
- Implications of "free" services that monetise user data

### AI and Emerging Technologies

As artificial intelligence becomes ubiquitous, digital literacy must evolve. Miles Berry suggests teaching pupils to:
- Understand basic AI principles (machine learning, training data)
- Recognise AI systems in everyday tools (recommendation algorithms, voice assistants)
- Consider ethical implications of AI (bias in algorithms, automation of jobs)
- Understand limitations of AI and importance of human oversight

## Conclusion and Future Directions

Teaching digital literacy is not a static endeavour. As Miles Berry notes, "digital literacy is contextual and constantly evolving." Teachers must remain current with technological developments whilst grounding teaching in enduring principles of critical thinking, ethical awareness, and creative expression.

The most effective digital literacy education, as advocated by all key thinkers discussed, moves beyond skills training to develop thoughtful, critical, and responsible digital citizens. This requires sustained commitment across all key stages, integration across the curriculum, and recognition that all teachers share responsibility for developing pupils' digital literacy.

Future developments will likely emphasise AI literacy, data literacy, and sophisticated understanding of algorithmic systems. However, the fundamental principles articulated by Berry, Selwyn, Harrison, and Anderson—critical engagement, creative production, ethical awareness, and practical competence—will remain central to effective digital literacy education.
