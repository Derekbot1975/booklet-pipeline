# IT at Key Stage 3

## Introduction and National Curriculum Context

Information Technology at Key Stage 3 (ages 11-14) represents a critical phase in developing students' digital competencies within the broader Computing curriculum. Following the 2014 National Curriculum reforms, IT is no longer a standalone subject but forms one of three interconnected strands alongside Computer Science and Digital Literacy. As Miles Berry (2014) emphasised in his work for the Computing at School organisation, IT focuses on students becoming "digitally competent" – able to create, manipulate, and communicate digital content effectively across multiple platforms and contexts.

The National Curriculum for Computing at Key Stage 3 states that pupils should be taught to "undertake creative projects that involve selecting, using, and combining multiple applications, preferably across a range of devices, to achieve challenging goals, including collecting and analysing data and meeting the needs of known users" (DfE, 2013). This emphasis on purposeful, project-based work distinguishes modern IT education from earlier iterations focused primarily on software proficiency.

## Defining Information Technology within Computing

### The Three-Strand Model

Mark Anderson, through his ICT Evangelist blog and contributions to Computing at School, has consistently articulated the relationship between the three Computing strands. IT specifically encompasses:

- **Digital content creation**: producing multimedia artefacts including documents, presentations, video, audio, and graphics
- **Data handling and analysis**: collecting, organising, interpreting, and presenting data using appropriate tools
- **Digital communication**: using technology to collaborate, share, and communicate effectively
- **Systems and applications**: selecting and combining appropriate software and hardware to solve problems

Bob Harrison, former President of NAACE (now the EdTech Consortium), emphasises that IT should not be viewed in isolation but rather as the "application layer" that makes computational thinking tangible and relevant to students' lived experiences.

## Key Stage 3 Curriculum Requirements

### Statutory Programme of Study

The National Curriculum for Computing (DfE, 2013) requires that all Key Stage 3 pupils:

1. **Create, reuse, revise and repurpose digital artefacts for a given audience, with attention to trustworthiness, design and usability**
2. **Understand a range of ways to use technology safely, respectfully, responsibly and securely, including protecting their online identity and privacy**
3. **Recognise inappropriate content, contact and conduct, and know how to report concerns**

The IT-specific expectations centre on the creative project requirement: students must "undertake creative projects that involve selecting, using, and combining multiple applications, preferably across a range of devices, to achieve challenging goals."

### Progression Across Key Stage 3

#### Year 7 (Ages 11-12)

Students typically transition from primary Computing with variable prior knowledge. Miles Berry's research (2016) highlights the "patchy" nature of primary IT provision, necessitating careful baseline assessment. Year 7 IT focus areas include:

- **Document production**: word processing with emphasis on structure, formatting, and templates
- **Presentation skills**: creating effective multimedia presentations understanding design principles
- **Basic data handling**: introduction to spreadsheets, simple formulae, and charts
- **Digital media basics**: capturing and editing images, understanding file formats and compression
- **E-safety foundation**: establishing safe, responsible online behaviour and digital citizenship

#### Year 8 (Ages 12-13)

Building upon Year 7 foundations, students engage with more sophisticated applications:

- **Advanced data manipulation**: spreadsheet modelling, complex formulae (IF, VLOOKUP), data validation
- **Video and audio production**: editing software, understanding codec, resolution, and export settings
- **Web technologies**: HTML/CSS fundamentals, understanding client-server architecture, creating structured websites
- **Vector graphics**: introduction to object-based drawing software, understanding raster vs vector
- **Database concepts**: relational databases, queries, forms, and reports (often using Microsoft Access or similar)

#### Year 9 (Ages 13-14)

Year 9 represents a culmination of Key Stage 3 IT, preparing students for GCSE options:

- **Integrated projects**: combining multiple applications (e.g., database-driven websites, data journalism projects)
- **Data analysis and visualisation**: advanced spreadsheet techniques, pivot tables, statistical functions
- **Digital media production**: professional-standard outputs in video, audio, or interactive media
- **Project management**: understanding project lifecycles, testing, user feedback, and iterative development
- **Automation**: introduction to macros, mail merge, and basic scripting to enhance productivity

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Project-Based Learning

Neil Selwyn's critical perspective on educational technology (2016, 2019) cautions against "solutionist" approaches where technology is applied without pedagogical grounding. Effective IT teaching at Key Stage 3 should employ authentic, meaningful projects that:

- **Address real problems**: work with genuine users and stakeholders where possible
- **Require design thinking**: emphasise planning, prototyping, testing, and refinement
- **Integrate multiple applications**: mirror professional practice by combining tools strategically
- **Develop transferable skills**: focus on concepts and principles rather than specific software

### Flipped Learning and Blended Approaches

Mark Anderson advocates for flipped learning models in IT, where students access instructional content (particularly technical tutorials) independently, freeing lesson time for creative application, problem-solving, and teacher intervention with struggling learners. This approach aligns well with IT's practical nature and supports differentiated learning pathways.

### Critical Digital Literacy

Miles Berry emphasises that IT education must incorporate critical perspectives on technology. Students should question:

- **Design decisions**: understanding interface design choices and their impact on users
- **Algorithmic bias**: recognising how data collection and processing can embed discrimination
- **Digital divides**: acknowledging unequal access to technology and digital skills
- **Surveillance capitalism**: understanding business models based on data harvesting
- **Environmental impact**: considering energy consumption and e-waste in technology decisions

## Core IT Topics and Skills

### Document Production and Desktop Publishing

Beyond basic word processing, Key Stage 3 students should understand:

- **Information architecture**: structuring documents logically using styles, headings, and navigation
- **Typography**: selecting appropriate fonts, understanding readability principles
- **Templates and automation**: creating reusable structures, using mail merge for personalisation
- **Collaborative editing**: version control, track changes, commenting, and cloud-based collaboration
- **Accessibility**: ensuring documents are usable by people with diverse needs (WCAG principles)

Practical activities include producing multi-page reports, newsletters, instruction manuals, and collaborative research documents.

### Spreadsheets and Data Modelling

Bob Harrison identifies spreadsheet competency as perhaps the most transferable IT skill for future employment. Key Stage 3 coverage should include:

- **Worksheet design**: creating clear, well-structured models with appropriate use of cell references
- **Formula construction**: arithmetic operations, cell referencing (relative, absolute, mixed)
- **Functions**: SUM, AVERAGE, COUNT, MAX, MIN, IF, COUNTIF, SUMIF, VLOOKUP
- **Data validation**: controlling input to prevent errors
- **Charts and visualisation**: selecting appropriate chart types, understanding data-to-ink ratios
- **What-if analysis**: using spreadsheets for financial modelling, scenario planning

Authentic contexts include personal budgeting, analysing sports statistics, scientific data analysis, and business modelling.

### Database Systems

Understanding relational databases provides foundational knowledge for data-driven applications:

- **Entity-relationship modelling**: identifying entities, attributes, and relationships
- **Normalisation**: understanding why data should be structured to avoid redundancy
- **Queries**: selecting, filtering, and sorting data using query-by-example or SQL basics
- **Forms**: creating user-friendly interfaces for data entry
- **Reports**: presenting data professionally for specific audiences

Many schools use Microsoft Access, though MySQL with simple front-ends or tools like BaseX are alternatives. The focus should be on concepts transferable to any database system.

### Digital Media Production

#### Image Creation and Manipulation

Students should distinguish between raster and vector graphics and understand appropriate applications:

- **Raster editing** (Photoshop, GIMP, Photopea): layers, selections, filters, colour adjustment
- **Vector graphics** (Illustrator, Inkscape): paths, anchor points, fills, strokes
- **Photography principles**: composition, lighting, resolution, file formats (JPEG, PNG, RAW)
- **Copyright and licensing**: Creative Commons, fair use, attribution requirements

#### Video Production

Mark Anderson's EdTech guides emphasise authentic video projects that develop transferable skills:

- **Pre-production**: storyboarding, shot planning, scripting
- **Production**: camera techniques, lighting, audio recording
- **Post-production**: timeline editing, transitions, titles, colour grading (iMovie, DaVinci Resolve, Premiere Pro)
- **Technical understanding**: resolution, frame rates, codecs, compression, export settings

Projects might include documentary films, educational content, promotional videos, or narrative storytelling.

#### Audio Production

Often overlooked, audio production develops understanding of:

- **Recording techniques**: microphone types, recording levels, noise reduction
- **Multi-track editing**: using Audacity, GarageBand, or Reaper
- **Audio effects**: equalisation, compression, reverb
- **Podcasting**: planning, recording, editing, and publishing audio content

### Web Technologies

While web development overlaps with Computer Science, the IT perspective emphasises:

- **HTML structure**: semantic markup, accessibility considerations
- **CSS styling**: separating content from presentation, responsive design principles
- **Content management**: using platforms like WordPress, understanding templates and plugins
- **Web standards**: W3C guidelines, validation, cross-browser compatibility
- **Publishing and hosting**: understanding domains, hosting, FTP/file management

Miles Berry (2017) suggests authentic web projects where students create sites for real purposes – school clubs, community organisations, or awareness campaigns.

### Presentation and Communication

Moving beyond bullet-pointed slides, effective presentation teaching includes:

- **Design principles**: white space, alignment, contrast, proximity
- **Cognitive load theory**: avoiding death by PowerPoint, using visual hierarchy
- **Alternative tools**: Prezi, Canva, Google Slides, interactive presentations
- **Delivery skills**: presenting confidently, engaging audiences, storytelling techniques
- **Accessibility**: ensuring presentations work with screen readers, providing transcripts

## Cross-Curricular Opportunities

### IT Across the Curriculum

The Computing at School community, including contributions from Bob Harrison and Miles Berry, consistently advocates for IT as an enabler across all subjects:

- **Science**: data logging, spreadsheet analysis, digital lab books, creating explanatory animations
- **Mathematics**: dynamic geometry software, statistical analysis, mathematical modelling
- **English**: digital storytelling, collaborative writing, multimedia book reviews, podcasting
- **History**: creating digital archives, timeline software, historical documentary production
- **Geography**: GIS systems, mapping tools, presenting geographical data
- **Art and Design**: digital photography, graphic design, animation
- **Music**: digital audio workstations, composition software, notation programs
- **MFL**: creating audio recordings, collaborative translation projects, digital pen-pals
- **PE**: video analysis, performance tracking, fitness data analysis

### Integrating Computer Science Concepts

While IT focuses on application, it should connect with computational thinking:

- **Algorithms**: understanding how spell-checkers, auto-correct, and search functions work
- **Data representation**: how images, sound, and video are digitally encoded
- **Networks**: client-server architecture, cloud computing, internet protocols
- **Abstraction**: recognising patterns in different software applications
- **Decomposition**: breaking complex projects into manageable components

## Assessment in Key Stage 3 IT

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Without GCSE-style examinations, Key Stage 3 IT assessment should be predominantly formative:

- **Peer assessment**: students evaluating each other's work against criteria
- **Self-reflection**: encouraging metacognition about learning and skill development
- **Digital portfolios**: curating best work with reflective commentary (using tools like Seesaw or Google Sites)
- **Practical observations**: teacher assessment during project work
- **Screencasting**: students recording themselves explaining their process and decisions

### Summative Assessment

Where schools require summative assessment, approaches include:

- **Project-based assessment**: major pieces of work evaluated against clear criteria
- **Skills audits**: testing specific technical competencies in controlled conditions
- **Written examinations**: testing understanding of concepts, terminology, and theory
- **Presentations**: students explaining their work, demonstrating understanding

Mark Anderson emphasises that assessment should value creativity, problem-solving, and independent learning rather than simply measuring software proficiency.

### Assessment Without Levels

Following the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014, schools have developed diverse approaches. Computing at School's progression pathways (with IT strand) provide one framework, whilst some schools adopt:

- **Skill-based competency frameworks**: students achieve badges or certificates for specific skills
- **Progress descriptors**: bespoke statements describing developing IT capability
- **GCSE criteria**: back-mapping from Key Stage 4 assessment objectives
- **Bloom's taxonomy**: assessing from basic application through evaluation and creation

## Resources and Software

### Core Applications

Whilst avoiding vendor-dependence, most Key Stage 3 IT curricula include:

- **Office suites**: Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, LibreOffice
- **Image editing**: GIMP, Photopea, Paint.NET, Photoshop (if licensed)
- **Video editing**: iMovie, DaVinci Resolve, Shotcut, OpenShot
- **Audio editing**: Audacity, GarageBand, Reaper
- **Web authoring**: Notepad++, VS Code, WordPress, Google Sites
- **Databases**: Microsoft Access, LibreOffice Base, MySQL with phpMyAdmin
- **Presentation**: PowerPoint, Google Slides, Prezi, Canva

### Platform Considerations

Schools operate diverse technological ecosystems:

- **Windows-based networks**: typically Microsoft-centric with Active Directory management
- **Apple environments**: MacOS/iPad ecosystems with emphasis on creative applications
- **Chromebooks**: cloud-first approach using primarily web-based tools
- **Linux**: increasingly popular for cost-effectiveness and FOSS philosophy
- **BYOD environments**: mixed devices requiring platform-independent solutions

Neil Selwyn's research warns against deterministic assumptions about technology's educational impact, reminding educators that pedagogy always trumps platform.

## Professional Development for IT Teachers

### Subject Knowledge Enhancement

Many Computing teachers come from Computer Science backgrounds and require IT-specific professional development:

- **Creative software proficiency**: training in media production tools
- **Project management skills**: understanding authentic project methodologies
- **Industry awareness**: connecting with professional designers, data analysts, and digital creators
- **Assessment literacy**: developing appropriate IT assessment approaches

### Professional Organisations and Networks

- **Computing At School (CAS)**: subject association providing resources, networks, and professional development
- **BCS, The Chartered Institute for IT**: professional body offering qualifications and CPD
- **Mirandanet**: community of practice supporting IT educators
- **NAACE/EdTech Consortium**: advocating for effective technology use in education
- **Regional Computing Hubs**: funded networks providing local support and training

Miles Berry's extensive work with CAS includes numerous resources and guidance documents specifically addressing IT within Computing.

## Transition and Progression

### From Key Stage 2

The 2014 curriculum introduced Computing in primary schools, but Miles Berry's research (2016) identified significant variability in IT provision. Key Stage 3 teachers should:

- **Conduct baseline assessment**: determine students' actual capabilities rather than assuming progression
- **Build foundational skills**: not all students will have primary IT experience
- **Recognise informal learning**: students' self-taught skills from personal device use
- **Address misconceptions**: students may have developed inefficient or problematic practices

### To Key Stage 4

Students progressing to GCSE should have developed:

- **Technical proficiency**: confident use of multiple applications
- **Conceptual understanding**: knowing when and why to apply different tools
- **Problem-solving approaches**: ability to decompose complex tasks
- **Design thinking**: understanding user needs, prototyping, and iteration
- **Digital literacy**: critical awareness of technology's social implications

#### GCSE Connections

Whilst GCSE Computer Science focuses heavily on programming, IT skills remain relevant:

- **AQA GCSE Computer Science**: practical project requiring digital artefact creation
- **OCR GCSE Computer Science**: computational thinking and practical problem-solving elements
- **Cambridge Nationals in IT** (OCR): vocational qualification with explicit IT focus including data manipulation and digital communication
- **Pearson BTEC Tech Award in Digital Information Technology**: practice-based assessment emphasising IT skills application

## Contemporary Issues and Challenges

### Software Licensing and Costs

Schools face significant challenges in providing appropriate software:

- **Subscription models**: shift from perpetual licensing to recurring costs (Adobe Creative Cloud, Microsoft 365)
- **Free and open-source alternatives**: GIMP, LibreOffice, Audacity reducing costs but requiring retraining
- **Educational licensing**: understanding volume licensing, Charity Commission status benefits
- **Cloud vs local**: balancing accessibility with data sovereignty and internet dependency

### Device Access and Digital Divide

Neil Selwyn's research (2016, 2019) consistently highlights that technology access remains inequitable:

- **School provision**: adequate devices, reliable networks, technical support
- **Home access**: homework expectations must acknowledge varied student circumstances
