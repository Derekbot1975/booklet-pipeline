# IT in the National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction

Information Technology (IT) in the UK National Curriculum represents a critical yet often misunderstood component of digital education. Following the 2013 curriculum reforms, the subject area formally known as ICT was replaced with 'Computing', which encompasses three distinct but interrelated strands: Computer Science, Information Technology, and Digital Literacy. This structural change, heavily influenced by organisations like Computing At School (CAS) and thought leaders such as Miles Berry, fundamentally reshaped how digital competencies are taught across English schools.

## Defining IT within the Computing Curriculum

### The Three-Strand Model

Miles Berry, formerly Principal Lecturer at the University of Roehampton and a prominent voice in UK computing education, has consistently articulated the distinction between the three strands of Computing:

**Computer Science** focuses on understanding how digital systems work, programming, and computational thinking—the scientific and theoretical foundations.

**Information Technology** centres on using digital systems to solve real-world problems, create products, and manipulate data—the application and creative use of technology.

**Digital Literacy** addresses safe, responsible, and effective use of technology—the social and ethical dimensions.

As Berry (2013) emphasised in his writing for the National Centre for Computing Education, IT sits at the intersection of creativity and functionality, requiring students to select appropriate technologies, understand their capabilities, and apply them purposefully to authentic tasks.

## National Curriculum Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The 2014 National Curriculum for Computing states that pupils should be taught to:

- Use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate, and retrieve digital content
- Recognise common uses of information technology beyond school

**IT implications**: At this foundational stage, IT focuses on basic productivity applications. Children learn to use simple word processors, paint programs, and digital cameras. Mark Anderson, the ICT Evangelist and popular educational technology blogger, emphasises that even at KS1, the focus should be on purpose—children should understand *why* they're using particular tools, not just *how*.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Pupils should be taught to:

- Use search technologies effectively
- Select, use, and combine a variety of software (including internet services) on a range of digital devices to design and create a range of programs, systems, and content that accomplish given goals
- Use technology safely, respectfully, and responsibly

**IT implications**: Bob Harrison, formerly of Edexcel and a long-standing advocate for practical computing education, has highlighted that KS2 represents the crucial developmental stage where IT capabilities mature from basic use to considered selection and combination. Students might create multimedia presentations, use spreadsheets for data analysis, edit video or audio, or design websites. The emphasis shifts toward evaluating which tools best serve specific purposes.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The programme of study states that pupils should be taught to:

- Undertake creative projects that involve selecting, using, and combining multiple applications, preferably across a range of devices, to achieve challenging goals, including collecting and analysing data and meeting the needs of known users
- Create, reuse, revise, and repurpose digital artefacts for a given audience, with attention to trustworthiness, design, and usability

**IT implications**: At KS3, IT becomes significantly more sophisticated. Neil Selwyn, Professor at Monash University and author of "Education and Technology: Key Issues and Debates" (2017), argues that this is where students should develop critical awareness of how digital tools shape their work and thinking. Practical projects might include developing apps using visual programming environments, creating digital portfolios, designing databases, or producing sophisticated multimedia products.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

Whilst the statutory National Curriculum for Computing ends at KS3, most students continue to engage with IT through:

- GCSE Computer Science (predominantly programming-focused)
- Creative iMedia qualifications (OCR)
- Digital Technology qualifications (AQA, WJEC)
- Enterprise and vocational pathways

## Examination Board Provision

### OCR Creative iMedia

OCR's Cambridge National in Creative iMedia (Level 1/2) represents the most established IT-focused qualification at KS4. The specification includes:

- **R081**: Pre-production skills (mandatory)
- **R082**: Creating digital graphics
- **R085**: Creating a multipage website
- **R087**: Creating interactive multimedia products

This qualification explicitly addresses the IT strand, focusing on selecting and using tools to create products for specified audiences and purposes.

### AQA Level 1/2 Technical Award in Digital Technology

Introduced to address the perceived gap left by the removal of ICT GCSE, AQA's Digital Technology qualification covers:

- Fitness for purpose of digital technology solutions
- Designing, creating, testing, and evaluating solutions
- Ethical, legal, and security implications

### WJEC Digital Technology

WJEC offers a similar Level 1/2 qualification focusing on practical application of technology to solve real-world problems, maintaining the spirit of the IT strand within a formal assessment structure.

### Edexcel/Pearson

While Edexcel focuses primarily on GCSE Computer Science, their legacy in ICT assessment (pre-2013) continues to influence how centres structure IT provision, particularly through BTEC Digital Information Technology qualifications at KS4 and beyond.

## Practical Implications for Teachers

### Curriculum Planning

Miles Berry's 2015 work "A Curriculum for Digital Literacy" emphasises that effective IT teaching requires:

**Project-based learning**: IT skills develop most effectively through purposeful, extended projects rather than isolated skill exercises. Teachers should design scenarios requiring students to solve authentic problems for real or realistic audiences.

**Iterative development**: Following design thinking principles, students should plan, create, test, evaluate, and refine their digital products, mirroring professional practice.

**Tool-agnostic teaching**: While schools inevitably use specific software, effective IT education focuses on transferable concepts—understanding how databases work matters more than memorising Microsoft Access commands.

### Assessment Challenges

Bob Harrison has consistently highlighted the assessment difficulties inherent in IT education. Unlike Computer Science, where programs either function correctly or don't, IT products are assessed on subjective criteria including:

- Fitness for purpose
- User experience
- Design quality
- Technical sophistication
- Creativity and originality

Teachers must develop clear success criteria and use exemplar materials to support consistent judgement-making.

### Resource Considerations

Mark Anderson's "The ICT Handbook" series and accompanying blog (ICT Evangelist) provide extensive practical guidance for IT provision, emphasising that effective IT teaching requires:

**Diverse software access**: Students need exposure to various tools—word processors, spreadsheets, databases, presentation software, image editors, video editors, web design tools, and emerging technologies.

**Cross-platform experience**: With students using Windows, macOS, iOS, Android, and ChromeOS devices in different contexts, IT education must develop platform-independent understanding.

**Industry-standard tools**: Where feasible, introducing professional-grade software (Adobe Creative Suite, industry databases, content management systems) prepares students for post-school contexts.

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Digital Capability

Neil Selwyn's work on digital capability provides crucial theoretical grounding for IT education. In "Education and Technology: Key Issues and Debates" (2017), Selwyn argues against simplistic notions of "digital natives," emphasising that meaningful technology use requires explicit teaching. Students don't automatically develop sophisticated IT capabilities through mere exposure; they require structured opportunities to:

- Analyse problems and identify technological solutions
- Evaluate tool capabilities and limitations
- Make informed software and platform selections
- Understand how technologies mediate their creative work
- Recognise the social and cultural implications of design choices

### Computational Thinking in IT

Miles Berry has extensively explored how computational thinking principles apply beyond programming to IT practice:

**Decomposition**: Breaking complex projects into manageable components
**Pattern recognition**: Identifying common design patterns and structures across applications
**Abstraction**: Understanding underlying principles rather than surface-level tool features
**Algorithm design**: Planning systematic approaches to achieving digital goals

This framework helps bridge Computer Science and IT, showing their complementary rather than competing relationship.

## The IT/Computer Science Tension

### Historical Context

The 2013 curriculum reforms created ongoing debate about the relative status of IT versus Computer Science. The Royal Society's 2012 report "Shut Down or Restart?" criticised the previous ICT curriculum as insufficiently rigorous, advocating for computational and programming education. However, critics including Neil Selwyn argue this swing toward Computer Science has marginalised essential IT capabilities.

### Current Concerns

Bob Harrison and others have expressed concern that the pendulum has swung too far toward programming at the expense of IT. Schools facing timetable pressures and assessment accountability often prioritise GCSE Computer Science, potentially leaving students without essential practical digital skills for employment and higher education.

Mark Anderson's 2018 blog series "The Problem with Computing" highlighted survey evidence suggesting many students, particularly girls, feel alienated by programming-heavy curricula and would prefer more balanced provision including substantial IT components.

## Digital Literacy Interconnections

Miles Berry's work emphasises that IT and Digital Literacy are deeply interconnected. When creating digital products, students must consider:

- **E-safety**: Understanding how their creations might affect users
- **Privacy**: Making informed decisions about data collection and use
- **Accessibility**: Designing inclusive products for diverse users
- **Digital footprints**: Recognising that published work contributes to online identity
- **Intellectual property**: Respecting copyright whilst understanding open licensing

Effective IT teaching integrates these considerations throughout project work rather than treating them as separate lessons.

## International Perspectives

The UK's approach to distinguishing IT from Computer Science is relatively unusual. Neil Selwyn's comparative research shows that many education systems maintain integrated digital literacy/capability curricula. The ISTE Standards (USA) and DigiComp framework (EU) take more holistic approaches, suggesting the UK's firm boundaries between strands may be administratively convenient but pedagogically problematic.

## Future Directions

### Emerging Technologies

As artificial intelligence, machine learning tools, augmented reality, and other emerging technologies become accessible, IT education faces new challenges. Mark Anderson's recent work explores how students should learn to use AI-powered tools ethically and effectively—a quintessentially IT concern about selecting and applying appropriate technologies.

### Skills for Employment

Industry voices consistently emphasise demand for IT capabilities—proficiency with business applications, data analysis tools, project management software, collaborative platforms, and digital creation tools. The CBI's annual education surveys regularly identify these practical IT skills as priorities, supporting arguments for maintaining robust IT provision alongside Computer Science.

### Assessment Evolution

Miles Berry and others advocate for more sophisticated IT assessment approaches, possibly drawing on portfolio models used in Art and Design. The challenge remains creating valid, reliable assessment frameworks that capture the complexity and creativity of IT work whilst remaining manageable for schools.

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Cross-Curricular IT

Effective IT development often occurs through meaningful application in other subjects:

- **Geography**: GIS software, data visualisation, mapping tools
- **History**: Digital archives, multimedia presentations, timeline software
- **Science**: Data logging, spreadsheet analysis, scientific report production
- **English**: Desktop publishing, blogging, multimedia storytelling
- **Music and Art**: Digital audio workstations, graphics editing, animation tools

Teachers should coordinate to ensure students encounter progressive IT challenges across the curriculum, not just in Computing lessons.

### Hardware Considerations

IT education requires appropriate devices. Whilst Raspberry Pi computers excellently serve Computer Science, IT work often demands higher-specification machines capable of running professional software. Schools must balance budgets against pedagogical needs, potentially adopting blended approaches with different device types serving different purposes.

## Conclusion

Information Technology within the National Curriculum represents essential education for digital participation in contemporary society. Whilst the 2013 reforms appropriately elevated Computer Science, the pendulum must not swing so far as to neglect IT capabilities. As Miles Berry, Bob Harrison, Mark Anderson, and Neil Selwyn variously argue, balanced Computing education requires all three strands—Computer Science, Information Technology, and Digital Literacy—working in concert to develop capable, creative, and critical digital citizens.

Teachers and curriculum leaders must advocate for adequate IT provision, resist false dichotomies between "vocational" IT and "academic" Computer Science, and ensure students develop sophisticated capabilities to select, use, combine, and evaluate technologies for purposeful ends. Only through such balanced provision can schools fulfil the genuine promise of Computing education.
