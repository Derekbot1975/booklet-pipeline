# Bob Harrison

## Overview

**Full Name:** Robert (Bob) Harrison

**Current Role:** Formerly Professor of Education and Toshiba Chair in Community Education at Durham University

**Known For:** Leading research and practice in educational technology, digital strategy in schools, and community-based learning; significant contributions to UK ICT curriculum development and teacher training in technology integration.

## Biography

### Early Career and Academic Foundation

Bob Harrison established himself as a prominent figure in UK education technology during the 1990s and early 2000s, a critical period when Information and Communication Technology (ICT) was becoming embedded in the National Curriculum. His work centred on understanding how digital technologies could transform teaching and learning, particularly in secondary education contexts.

Harrison held the prestigious Toshiba Chair in Community Education at Durham University, a position that reflected his dual commitment to educational technology and community engagement. This endowed chair enabled him to conduct extensive research into how technology could bridge formal education with broader community learning contexts.

### Academic Leadership

At Durham University's School of Education, Harrison developed a significant research profile examining the implementation of ICT in schools. His work during the late 1990s and early 2000s coincided with the UK government's National Grid for Learning initiative and the substantial investment in school ICT infrastructure. This timing positioned him to influence policy debates about effective technology integration.

Harrison's leadership extended beyond research into teacher education, where he contributed to initial teacher training programmes and continuing professional development for serving teachers. He understood that successful technology integration depended fundamentally on teacher capability and confidence.

### Community Education Focus

The community education dimension of Harrison's chair was distinctive. He explored how schools could use technology to engage with parents, local communities, and lifelong learners. This work pre-dated many current discussions about digital inclusion and community engagement, making his contributions particularly prescient.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Technology as Catalyst for Pedagogical Change

Harrison consistently argued that educational technology should not simply automate existing teaching practices but should catalyse fundamental pedagogical transformation. He emphasised that ICT's value lay in enabling new forms of teaching and learning rather than merely making traditional approaches more efficient.

His research demonstrated that effective technology integration required teachers to reconsider their pedagogical assumptions. Harrison documented cases where technology enabled more student-centred, inquiry-based approaches, but only when teachers received appropriate support and professional development.

This perspective influenced thinking about ICT teacher training, shifting focus from technical skills to pedagogical transformation. Harrison advocated for CPD programmes that helped teachers reimagine curriculum delivery rather than simply learning software applications.

### 2. Digital Strategy and Whole-School Approaches

Harrison was instrumental in developing frameworks for whole-school digital strategies. He argued that effective technology integration required coordinated planning across curriculum, infrastructure, professional development, and leadership dimensions.

His research identified common pitfalls in school technology implementation: purchasing hardware without pedagogical planning, insufficient teacher training, lack of technical support, and failure to align technology use with school improvement priorities. Harrison's strategic frameworks addressed these challenges systematically.

He emphasised the importance of distributed leadership for technology, arguing that successful schools developed technology champions across departments rather than isolating expertise in ICT departments. This approach ensured technology became embedded in subject teaching rather than remaining separate.

Harrison's work on digital strategy influenced local authority planning and school improvement programmes. His frameworks were adopted by several regional education authorities during the 2000s expansion of school ICT provision.

### 3. Community Access and Digital Inclusion

Through his community education focus, Harrison explored how schools could serve as community technology hubs. He researched initiatives where school ICT facilities were made available to parents and community members, providing access to digital resources and training.

This work addressed digital inclusion before it became a mainstream policy concern. Harrison documented how community access programmes helped address the 'digital divide', particularly in areas of socio-economic disadvantage. His research showed that school-based community ICT provision could increase parental engagement with children's learning whilst providing valuable skills training.

Harrison's community education research examined sustainability challenges: funding models, staffing arrangements, safeguarding considerations, and balancing school and community needs. His findings informed thinking about extended schools and community facility provision.

### 4. Evidence-Based Technology Integration

Harrison advocated strongly for evidence-based approaches to educational technology. He was critical of technology adoption driven by commercial pressures or fashionable trends rather than pedagogical rationale and research evidence.

His research methodology combined quantitative measures of attainment impact with qualitative examination of classroom practice. This mixed-methods approach provided nuanced understanding of how, when, and why technology enhanced learning. Harrison documented that technology's impact was highly variable, depending on implementation quality, subject context, and teacher capability.

This evidence-based stance positioned Harrison as a thoughtful voice during periods of educational technology hype. He supported technology integration whilst maintaining critical perspective about inflated claims and inadequate implementation.

### 5. Subject-Specific Technology Pedagogy

Harrison recognised that effective technology use varied across curriculum subjects. His research examined subject-specific applications: data-logging in science, computer-aided design in technology, digital composition in music, and simulation in geography.

This subject-specific perspective contrasted with generic ICT skills approaches. Harrison argued that technology's educational value emerged through integration with subject content and pedagogy rather than as separate skills instruction.

His work influenced thinking about ICT across the curriculum, supporting arguments that technology should support subject learning rather than constitute a separate curriculum area. This perspective aligned with subsequent curriculum developments that embedded technology across subjects rather than maintaining discrete ICT programmes of study.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Constructivist Foundations

Harrison's pedagogical approach was grounded in constructivist learning theory. He advocated for technology uses that supported active, student-centred learning through inquiry, collaboration, and authentic problem-solving.

His research documented effective practice examples where technology enabled students to construct knowledge through investigation and creation rather than passive consumption. Harrison emphasised technology's potential to support higher-order thinking and genuine understanding rather than mere information transfer.

### Context-Sensitive Implementation

Harrison recognised that effective technology integration required context-sensitive approaches rather than prescriptive solutions. His research documented how successful practice varied across school contexts, subject areas, and student populations.

This context-sensitivity informed Harrison's resistance to simplistic technology adoption models. He advocated for thoughtful adaptation of technology practices to local circumstances rather than uncritical replication of examples from different contexts.

### Teacher Professional Development

Harrison viewed teacher professional development as central to effective technology integration. His CPD models emphasised sustained engagement, classroom-based experimentation, peer collaboration, and reflective practice rather than one-off training sessions.

His research documented effective CPD characteristics: subject-specific focus, opportunities for practice and feedback, connection to teachers' immediate needs, and ongoing support. Harrison's CPD frameworks influenced professional development programme design across the UK.

### Critical Technology Perspective

Whilst advocating for educational technology, Harrison maintained critical perspective about implementation challenges, equity implications, and sustainability issues. His research acknowledged technology's limitations and documented failed implementations alongside successes.

This balanced, critical stance gave Harrison credibility with practitioners experiencing the messy reality of technology integration. His willingness to acknowledge challenges whilst identifying pathways to improvement made his work practically valuable.

## Legacy and Continuing Relevance

### Foundational Concepts

Harrison's key concepts remain relevant despite technological change since his most active period. His emphasis on pedagogical transformation rather than technical adoption continues to inform discussions about educational technology effectiveness.

The strategic frameworks Harrison developed for whole-school technology planning remain applicable to current digital strategy challenges. His attention to leadership, professional development, infrastructure, and curriculum alignment addresses enduring implementation requirements.

Harrison's community education work pre-dated current concerns about digital poverty and educational inequality exacerbated by remote learning requirements. His research on community technology access and digital inclusion established principles that inform current policy debates.

The COVID-19 pandemic's exposure of digital inequalities vindicated Harrison's earlier attention to access issues. His frameworks for community engagement and technology provision offer models for addressing persistent digital divides.

Harrison's influence on teacher education continues through educators he trained and conceptual frameworks he established. His emphasis on pedagogical capability rather than mere technical skill remains embedded in teacher training approaches to educational technology.

The integration of technology across subject methods teaching, rather than as separate ICT modules, reflects Harrison's advocacy for subject-specific technology pedagogy. Current teacher training programmes embody principles Harrison articulated during formative curriculum development periods.
