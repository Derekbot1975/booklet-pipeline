# Miles Berry

**Full Name:** Miles Berry

**Current Role:** Principal Lecturer in Computing Education at the University of Roehampton, London

**Known For:** Significant contributions to the development of the 2014 Computing National Curriculum in England, advocacy for open source software in education, pioneering work in digital literacy and computing pedagogy, and extensive writing on computing education through his influential blog and academic publications.

---

## Biography and Career History

Miles Berry has been a central figure in the transformation of Information Technology education in England since the early 2000s. His career trajectory uniquely combines classroom teaching experience, academic leadership, and policy influence, making him one of the most respected voices in UK computing education.

Berry began his career as a primary school teacher before moving into educational technology leadership. He served as a deputy head teacher and subsequently became headteacher of St Ives School in Haslemere, Surrey, where he implemented innovative approaches to ICT teaching that would later inform his broader educational philosophy. His early work focused on integrating technology meaningfully into primary education, moving beyond simple skills-based instruction towards deeper conceptual understanding.

In 2009, Berry joined the University of Roehampton as a lecturer, eventually becoming Principal Lecturer in Computing Education. At Roehampton, he has been instrumental in developing teacher training programmes for computing, including both initial teacher education (ITE) and continuing professional development (CPD). His work at Roehampton has focused on preparing teachers to deliver the challenging new computing curriculum introduced in 2014, which replaced the previous ICT National Curriculum.

Berry played a crucial advisory role during the 2012-2014 period when the Department for Education completely revised the ICT curriculum. He worked closely with Computing at School (CAS), the subject association that campaigned successfully for the introduction of computer science into the school curriculum. His expertise in primary computing pedagogy was particularly valuable during this transition, as many primary teachers felt underprepared for teaching programming and computational thinking.

Throughout his career, Berry has been a prolific writer and speaker. His blog, 'Open Thinking' (milesberry.net), has been widely read by teachers, curriculum leaders, and policymakers since 2004. He has presented at numerous national and international conferences, including BETT, the British Computer Society, and various Computing at School events.

Berry has also been involved with numerous professional organisations and initiatives. He served on the Mirandanet Fellowship, an international community of practice exploring innovative uses of technology in education. He has been a long-standing advocate for Creative Commons licensing and open educational resources, believing that educational materials should be freely accessible to teachers.

His academic work at Roehampton has included supervising doctoral students, conducting research into computing pedagogy, and contributing to national discussions about digital literacy and computational thinking. He has been particularly interested in how young children develop understanding of programming concepts and how teachers can be supported in delivering high-quality computing education.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Computational Thinking as a Fundamental Literacy

Berry has consistently argued that computational thinking represents a fundamental literacy for the 21st century, comparable to reading, writing, and mathematics. Drawing on Jeannette Wing's seminal 2006 paper, Berry has extended this concept to primary education, arguing that even young children can develop sophisticated understanding of abstraction, decomposition, pattern recognition, and algorithmic thinking.

In his work, Berry emphasises that computational thinking isn't solely about programming computers but about developing problem-solving approaches applicable across the curriculum. He has written extensively about how computational thinking can be integrated into subjects like mathematics, science, and even humanities, providing examples of decomposition in historical analysis and pattern recognition in literary studies.

Berry's conception of computational thinking goes beyond Wing's original formulation to explicitly consider the social and ethical dimensions of computation. He argues that understanding how algorithms shape society—from search engine results to social media feeds—is essential for active citizenship in a digital age.

### 2. The Pedagogy of Programming in Primary Education

Berry has developed detailed pedagogical frameworks for teaching programming to young children, drawing on constructivist learning theory and the work of Seymour Papert. He advocates for an approach that emphasises creativity, exploration, and debugging as learning opportunities rather than focusing narrowly on syntax and error-free code.

His work with Scratch, the visual programming environment developed at MIT, has been particularly influential. Berry has written extensively about how Scratch's design—with its emphasis on tinkering, remixing, and sharing—aligns with effective primary pedagogy. He has developed progression frameworks showing how children can move from simple sequence and repetition structures through to more complex concepts including variables, conditionals, and procedures.

Berry emphasises the importance of 'unplugged' computing activities—teaching computational concepts without computers. He has documented numerous activities involving physical games, card sorting, and role-play that help children understand algorithms, binary representation, and networks before encountering screen-based programming.

He has also been vocal about the importance of debugging as a metacognitive skill. Berry argues that debugging helps children develop resilience, systematic thinking, and understanding of program execution, making it central to computing pedagogy rather than merely a practical necessity.

### 3. Digital Literacy Beyond Technical Skills

Berry has articulated a sophisticated understanding of digital literacy that extends far beyond operational skills with software applications. Drawing on the work of Gillen and Barton, he conceptualises digital literacy as encompassing critical evaluation of digital content, understanding of how digital systems work, creative production of digital artefacts, and ethical awareness of digital citizenship.

In contrast to the functional ICT curriculum that preceded the 2014 Computing curriculum, Berry has argued for an approach that develops genuine understanding of digital technologies rather than superficial familiarity with particular software packages. He contends that understanding foundational principles—how data is represented, how networks function, how software is created—provides more durable and transferable knowledge than learning to use specific applications.

Berry has written extensively about e-safety and digital citizenship, arguing that these should be integrated throughout the computing curriculum rather than treated as separate topics. He emphasises the importance of developing children's agency and judgement in digital environments rather than simply imposing restrictions or teaching rules.

### 4. Open Source Philosophy in Education

Berry has been a prominent advocate for open source software and open educational resources throughout his career. His advocacy is grounded in both pragmatic and principled arguments. Pragmatically, he notes that open source software like Linux, Scratch, and Python provides schools with powerful, free tools that can be used across different devices and platforms.

More fundamentally, Berry argues that open source aligns with educational values of transparency, collaboration, and shared improvement. He contends that proprietary software represents a 'black box' that discourages genuine understanding, whilst open source allows students and teachers to examine, modify, and learn from the code itself.

Berry has written extensively about the pedagogical benefits of open source communities as models of collaborative learning. The practices of sharing code, peer review, version control, and iterative improvement that characterise successful open source projects mirror effective learning communities. He has encouraged teachers to adopt similar approaches in their classrooms, with students sharing their projects, learning from each other's code, and building on existing work.

His advocacy has influenced school purchasing decisions and DfE guidance, with increasing recognition that schools need not be locked into expensive proprietary software when free, high-quality alternatives exist.

### 5. Assessment in Computing Education

Berry has contributed important thinking about assessment in computing, addressing one of the most challenging aspects of the new curriculum. He distinguishes between assessing computational thinking (problem-solving processes), programming skills (technical implementation), and digital literacy (critical understanding and citizenship).

He has argued against over-reliance on summative testing, particularly for younger children, instead advocating for assessment approaches that capture the creative and iterative nature of computing work. Berry has promoted portfolio-based assessment where students demonstrate progression through projects, debugging logs, and reflective commentary on their work.

Drawing on work in formative assessment by Dylan Wiliam and Paul Black, Berry emphasises the importance of peer assessment in computing. He argues that reviewing others' code helps students develop critical understanding of programming concepts and good practice, whilst articulating feedback strengthens their own metacognitive awareness.

Berry has also contributed to debates about GCSE and A-level Computer Science assessment, arguing for appropriate balance between theoretical understanding and practical programming skills, and for assessment tasks that allow creativity within structured parameters.

### 6. Teacher Professional Development in Computing

Berry's work at Roehampton has centred significantly on preparing and supporting teachers to deliver high-quality computing education. He has recognised that the shift from ICT to Computing represented an enormous challenge for many teachers, particularly in primary schools where generalist teachers suddenly needed to teach programming and computer science concepts.

He has developed and delivered numerous CPD programmes, often working with Computing at School hubs and regional networks. His approach to CPD emphasises subject knowledge development alongside pedagogical understanding, recognising that teachers need both to teach effectively.

Berry advocates for professional learning communities where teachers can share resources, discuss challenges, and learn from each other's practice. He has championed online networks, particularly through social media, as valuable professional development resources, whilst also recognising the importance of face-to-face collaboration and mentoring.

He has written extensively about teacher confidence in computing, arguing that the subject's iterative, exploratory nature—where failure and debugging are integral to learning—actually aligns well with growth mindset approaches to teaching. Berry encourages teachers to be open about their own learning journey in computing rather than positioning themselves as omniscient experts.

### 7. The Continuum from Primary to Secondary Computing

Berry has given particular attention to progression and continuity in computing education from early years through to Key Stage 3 and beyond. He has critiqued educational systems that treat primary and secondary computing as disconnected, arguing for coherent progression in both conceptual understanding and practical skills.

His work has documented how foundational computational thinking concepts introduced in Key Stage 1 can develop through increasingly sophisticated applications. For example, he traces how understanding of algorithms might progress from simple instructions for physical movement, through block-based programming, to text-based coding, and eventually to algorithm analysis and design in secondary school.

Berry has been particularly concerned about the 'transition dip' where students moving from primary to secondary school often repeat content or experience discontinuity in their computing education. He has worked with schools to develop transition frameworks that ensure secondary schools build appropriately on primary foundations.

### 8. Inclusion and Equity in Computing Education

Throughout his work, Berry has addressed issues of inclusion and equity in computing education. He has written about the gender gap in computing, noting that whilst programming is often stereotyped as a male domain, primary computing education—before such stereotypes become entrenched—provides opportunities to engage all students.

Berry advocates for inclusive pedagogical approaches that recognise different learning styles and entry points into computing. He has promoted pair programming and collaborative projects as ways to make computing more accessible and social rather than individualistic.

He has also addressed questions of digital divide, arguing that schools have a responsibility to ensure all students gain meaningful computing education regardless of access to technology at home. This has informed his views on curriculum design, arguing for approaches that don't assume students have constant access to devices outside school.

This chapter provided practical insights into implementing the new computing curriculum based on his Roehampton work with trainee and practising teachers.

**'The Roehampton Annual Computing Education Report'** series (2015-2019), which Berry edited and contributed to extensively. These reports provided annual surveys of computing education in England, tracking implementation of the computing curriculum, teacher confidence, and resource availability. These became important reference documents for policymakers and school leaders.

### Academic Journal Articles

**'A Computational Thinking Approach to Primary Computing'** published in *Primary Teacher Update* (2014). This article provided accessible guidance for primary teachers transitioning to the new curriculum, offering practical activities and progression frameworks.

**'The Computing Curriculum: Challenges and Approaches for Primary Schools'** in *Impact: Journal of the Chartered College of Teaching* (2015). This peer-reviewed article analysed early implementation of the computing curriculum and identified key challenges around teacher confidence, assessment, and resource availability.

**'Inclusion and Computer Science Education: Recent Developments in the United Kingdom'** published in *Computer Science Education Research* (2017). This article examined equity issues in computing education with particular focus on gender participation.

Berry has written extensively for professional publications including *Teach Primary*, *Primary Teacher Update*, and the Computing at School newsletter *SwitchedOn*. ### Conference Presentations and Keynotes

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Berry's influence on computing education policy in England has been substantial, particularly during the crucial 2012-2014 period when the ICT National Curriculum was replaced by the Computing Programme of Study.

### The 2014 Computing National Curriculum

Berry served as an advisor during the development of the 2014 Computing National Curriculum, working alongside the expert panel led by the British Computer Society (BCS) and Computing at School (CAS). His particular contribution focused on ensuring the primary curriculum was developmentally appropriate and achievable for generalist primary teachers whilst maintaining rigour and ambition.

The final Programme of Study reflects several of Berry's key ideas, particularly in its emphasis on computational thinking, the balance between computer science, information technology, and digital literacy, and the progression from concrete activities in Key Stage 1 through to more abstract concepts in Key Stage 2.

Berry was particularly influential in the decision to begin computing education in Year 1 (age 5-6), arguing that young children are capable of understanding fundamental concepts like algorithms and debugging when approached through developmentally appropriate pedagogy. This contrasted with some voices suggesting computer science should only begin in secondary school.

### Computing at School (CAS) and Teacher Networks

Berry has been closely associated with Computing at School (CAS), the grassroots organisation that successfully campaigned for the introduction of computer science into the school curriculum. He has contributed to CAS publications, presented at CAS conferences, and worked with CAS regional hubs to deliver teacher training.

His contribution to the **CAS Primary Curriculum** guidance document (2014) was particularly significant. This document, which Berry co-authored, provided detailed exemplification of the Programme of Study with progression frameworks and lesson ideas. It became a widely-used resource for schools implementing the new curriculum.

Berry has also contributed to **CAS community resources** including the 'Barefoot Computing' project, which provides free resources for primary teachers. His pedagogical approach—emphasising unplugged activities, creativity, and debugging—is evident throughout these materials.

### Ofsted and Inspection Framework

Berry has engaged with Ofsted (the schools inspectorate in England) regarding computing inspection, contributing to discussions about what high-quality computing education looks like and how it should be evaluated. His writing has influenced inspector training and Ofsted's subject reports on computing.

His emphasis on progression, computational thinking, and digital literacy—rather than narrow focus on programming syntax—has shaped how computing is evaluated in schools. Ofsted's 2019 research report on computing education cited concerns about curriculum breadth that align with Berry's longstanding advocacy for balanced computing education.

### Initial Teacher Education Framework

At Roehampton, Berry has shaped how primary computing is taught in initial teacher education. The approaches developed at Roehampton—emphasising subject knowledge development, practical pedagogy, and confidence-building—have influenced practice at other ITE providers through his writing, presentations, and external examining work.

Berry contributed to the development of the Computing ITT curriculum framework consulted on by the Department for Education in 2021. His expertise ensured that the framework appropriately specified what trainee teachers should know and be able to do regarding computing education.

### International Influence

Berry's work has influenced computing education policy internationally, particularly in Commonwealth countries with similar educational systems. He has presented at international conferences in Australia, New Zealand, and various European countries, sharing England's experience of curriculum reform and lessons learned from implementation.

His blog readership extends internationally, with teachers and policymakers from numerous countries drawing on his resources and ideas. The English experience of moving from skills-based ICT to conceptual computing education has been studied by many countries considering similar reforms.
