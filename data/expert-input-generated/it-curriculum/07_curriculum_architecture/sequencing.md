# Sequencing IT Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Information Technology Sequencing

Sequencing Information Technology (IT) across Key Stages requires a coherent approach that builds pupils' practical capabilities alongside their conceptual understanding of digital systems. Unlike Computing Science, which emphasises algorithmic thinking and programming, IT focuses on the purposeful use of digital tools, systems and data to create, manipulate and communicate information effectively.

Miles Berry (2014) emphasises that effective curriculum sequencing in technology education must balance progression in technical skills with developing pupils' critical understanding of how digital systems shape society. The sequencing challenge lies in ensuring pupils develop both operational fluency and conceptual sophistication as they progress through the key stages.

## National Curriculum Context

The 2014 National Curriculum for Computing in England integrates Computing Science, IT and Digital Literacy into a single subject. Within this framework, IT is primarily addressed through the requirement that pupils should "use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content" (DfE, 2013). However, the curriculum's brevity regarding IT specifics places significant responsibility on schools to develop appropriate sequencing.

Mark Anderson (2015) argues that the Computing curriculum's focus on programming has sometimes marginalised IT, yet IT remains essential for pupils' future employability and digital participation. Schools must therefore construct their own sequencing frameworks that ensure systematic IT progression.

## Foundation Stage and Early Years

While not statutory, early exposure to IT concepts establishes crucial foundations. The Early Years Foundation Statement (EYFS) framework includes Understanding the World objectives where children explore technology in their environment.

### Pre-KS1 Progression Principles

Appropriate sequencing begins with:
- Recognition of technology in everyday contexts (toys, household items, classroom equipment)
- Supervised interaction with simple input devices (touchscreens, switches)
- Cause-and-effect understanding through controlled technology use
- Beginning to describe what technology does

Bob Harrison's work on primary computing emphasises that early IT experiences should be playful and exploratory, establishing positive dispositions towards technology rather than formal skill acquisition.

## Key Stage 1 Sequencing (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum states that KS1 pupils should "use technology purposefully to create, organise, store, manipulate and retrieve digital content" and "recognise common uses of information technology beyond school."

### Recommended Sequence for KS1

**Year 1 Focus:**
- Basic input device proficiency (mouse control, keyboard awareness)
- Simple content creation (paint programmes, word processing with images)
- Taking and viewing digital photographs
- Understanding that technology stores information
- Recognising technology in homes and schools

**Year 2 Development:**
- Combining text and images purposefully
- Basic file management (saving, retrieving named work)
- Simple data collection and pictogram creation
- Exploring different software for different purposes
- Understanding technology beyond school (shops, libraries, transport)

Miles Berry (2016) emphasises that KS1 sequencing should establish the principle that technology serves human purposes, not vice versa. Tasks should be contextualised within meaningful activities rather than isolated skill drills.

## Key Stage 2 Sequencing (Ages 7-11)

KS2 requirements expand: pupils should "select, use and combine a variety of software (including internet services) on a range of digital devices to design and create a range of programs, systems and content that accomplish given goals."

### Lower KS2 (Years 3-4)

**Year 3 Priorities:**
- Systematic keyboard skills development (touch-typing introduction)
- Purposeful word processing (formatting, editing, saving appropriately)
- Structured data collection and simple spreadsheet use
- Digital content creation (audio recording, basic video capture)
- Introduction to presentation software
- Understanding file organisation (folders, naming conventions)

**Year 4 Advancement:**
- Advanced formatting (styles, templates, document structure)
- Spreadsheet formulae and simple modelling
- Multimedia integration (combining text, image, sound, video)
- Basic image manipulation and editing
- Understanding appropriate software selection for tasks
- Collaborative working with shared documents

### Upper KS2 (Years 5-6)

**Year 5 Development:**
- Advanced spreadsheet use (multiple sheets, charts, complex formulae)
- Database concepts (sorting, filtering, querying)
- Sophisticated presentation techniques (animation, hyperlinks)
- Video editing and production
- Understanding of file formats and conversion
- Cloud services and synchronisation concepts

**Year 6 Consolidation:**
- Integrated project work combining multiple IT skills
- Advanced data analysis and visualisation
- Website creation and publishing
- Digital publishing and desktop publishing
- Evaluation of digital solutions for appropriateness
- Understanding of accessibility and inclusive design

Bob Harrison (2017) emphasises that upper KS2 represents a critical transition point where pupils should move from teacher-directed tool use to autonomous selection and combination of appropriate technologies for self-defined purposes.

## Key Stage 3 Sequencing (Ages 11-14)

KS3 continues the progression: pupils should "undertake creative projects that involve selecting, using, and combining multiple applications, preferably across a range of devices, to achieve challenging goals."

### Year 7 Foundation

Sequencing at KS3 must acknowledge varied prior attainment from different primary schools:

- Auditing and consolidating KS2 IT capabilities
- Advanced word processing (mail merge, advanced formatting, style sheets)
- Complex spreadsheet modelling (what-if scenarios, validation, conditional formatting)
- Relational database concepts and implementation
- Advanced presentation techniques for specific audiences
- Understanding of software licensing and intellectual property
- Digital media production standards (video, audio, graphics)

### Year 8 Development

- Project management using digital tools (Gantt charts, collaboration platforms)
- Advanced data manipulation and analysis
- Web development (HTML, CSS foundations)
- Graphics editing and vector/bitmap understanding
- Client-focused design briefs and evaluation
- Understanding of data protection and GDPR principles
- Automation and macros introduction

### Year 9 Specialisation

- Extended project work requiring IT solution design
- Advanced database querying (SQL introduction where appropriate)
- Responsive web design principles
- Digital content creation for specific purposes and audiences
- Understanding emerging technologies and their applications
- Critical evaluation of IT systems and their societal impact
- Portfolio development demonstrating IT capability

Mark Anderson (2018) argues that KS3 IT sequencing should increasingly emphasise transferable digital literacy skills rather than specific software mastery, given rapid technological change. Pupils should develop strategic thinking about tool selection and application.

## Key Stage 4 Sequencing (Ages 14-16)

At KS4, IT becomes optional through qualifications offered by exam boards. Sequencing depends on chosen pathways:

### GCSE Computer Science IT Components

OCR GCSE Computer Science (J277) includes significant IT elements in "Computational thinking, algorithms and programming" and "Data and data representation."

### Vocational IT Qualifications

**OCR Cambridge Nationals in IT (J836):**
- Level 1/2 progression through R050 (IT in the digital world), R060 (Data manipulation using spreadsheets), R070 (Using augmented reality), R080 (Website development)
- Sequenced to build from foundational knowledge to creative application

**Pearson BTEC Tech Award in Digital Information Technology:**
- Component 1: Exploring User Interface Design Principles
- Component 2: Collecting, Presenting and Interpreting Data
- Component 3: Effective Digital Working Practices
- Deliberately sequenced from analysis through creation to evaluation

**WJEC Level 1/2 Vocational Award in IT:**
- Progressive units from understanding IT to creating IT solutions
- Emphasis on applied learning in authentic contexts

Neil Selwyn (2019) cautions against purely instrumental approaches to KS4 IT, arguing that critical digital literacy should remain central even in vocational programmes, questioning how technology shapes work, society and individual agency.

## Key Stage 5 Sequencing (Ages 16-18)

Post-16 IT pathways typically separate from Computer Science, offering specialised vocational routes:

### Level 3 Qualifications

**OCR Cambridge Technical in IT (05838):**
- Year 12: Fundamentals of IT (mandatory), Global information, Cyber security
- Year 13: Social media and digital marketing, Data modelling, Project management
- Sequenced from foundational understanding through application to independent project work

**Pearson BTEC National Extended Certificate in IT:**
- Progressive units from Information Technology Systems through Social Media and Digital Marketing to IT Project Management
- Designed to support progression to higher education or employment

**AQA Level 3 Certificate in IT (7517):**
- Structured progression through IT systems, digital communication, data management
- Emphasis on contemporary IT practices and emerging technologies

### Curriculum Principles for KS5

Miles Berry (2020) identifies key principles for effective post-16 IT sequencing:
- Integration of technical capability with critical understanding
- Authentic project-based learning
- Engagement with contemporary IT practices and emerging technologies
- Development of professional behaviours and practices
- Preparation for higher education or employment through portfolio development

## Vertical Progression Across Key Stages

Effective IT sequencing requires careful mapping of progression in several dimensions:

### Technical Capability Progression

**Data Handling:**
- KS1: Simple pictograms
- KS2: Spreadsheets, databases, charts
- KS3: Complex data analysis, relational databases
- KS4/5: Data modelling, SQL, advanced analytics

**Content Creation:**
- KS1: Simple text and images
- KS2: Multimedia integration
- KS3: Professional-standard digital media
- KS4/5: Industry-standard production and design

**Digital Systems Understanding:**
- KS1: Recognition of technology
- KS2: Understanding software purposes
- KS3: System architecture and networking
- KS4/5: Enterprise systems, cloud infrastructure

### Conceptual Progression

Bob Harrison (2019) emphasises that technical skills must develop alongside conceptual understanding:

- **Representation**: From understanding that computers store information (KS1) through file formats (KS2/3) to data structures and encoding (KS4/5)
- **Abstraction**: From recognising technology categories (KS1) through appropriate tool selection (KS2/3) to system design and modelling (KS4/5)
- **Evaluation**: From describing what technology does (KS1) through fitness-for-purpose assessment (KS2/3) to critical analysis of digital systems' societal impact (KS4/5)

## Horizontal Coherence: IT Across the Curriculum

Mark Anderson (2019) argues that IT sequencing should not exist in isolation but connect meaningfully with other subjects:

- **English**: Digital literacy, multimedia storytelling, publishing
- **Mathematics**: Data handling, modelling, computational thinking
- **Science**: Data logging, simulation, scientific communication
- **Humanities**: Research skills, geographic information systems, historical sources
- **Arts**: Digital media creation, graphic design, music technology

Effective sequencing ensures IT capabilities develop through authentic application across the curriculum, not just within designated Computing lessons.

## Assessment and Progression Tracking

Sequencing requires clear assessment frameworks to ensure pupils progress appropriately:

### Formative Assessment Strategies

- Systematic observation of practical capability development
- Portfolio evidence demonstrating progression in technical and conceptual understanding
- Self and peer assessment against clear success criteria
- Regular retrieval practice of foundational concepts

### Summative Assessment Approaches

- End-of-key-stage capability audits
- Project work demonstrating integrated IT skills
- Exam board assessments at KS4/5
- Digital portfolios evidencing progressive capability

Neil Selwyn (2016) cautions against assessment approaches that privilege technical compliance over critical engagement, arguing that assessment should value pupils' ability to question and evaluate technology, not merely use it.

## Practical Implications for Schools

### Curriculum Planning Considerations

**Resource Requirements:**
- Ensure hardware and software provision supports planned progression
- Plan for technology refresh cycles aligned with curriculum requirements
- Provide access to industry-standard tools at appropriate key stages

**Staff Development:**
- Ongoing professional development to maintain currency with IT developments
- Pedagogical development in teaching IT alongside Computing Science
- Collaboration with other subjects to support cross-curricular IT application

**Scheme of Work Development:**
- Clear learning objectives mapping progression across years and key stages
- Authentic contexts and purposes for IT skill development
- Regular review and updating to reflect technological change

### Transition Management

Effective sequencing requires careful transition planning:

**Primary-Secondary Transition:**
- Information sharing about pupils' IT capabilities from KS2
- Bridging units that consolidate and extend prior learning
- Auditing processes to identify gaps and differentiate appropriately

**Key Stage 4 Options:**
- Clear guidance on different IT pathways (GCSE Computer Science vs vocational IT)
- Sequencing that prepares pupils for chosen routes
- Ensuring breadth of IT capability for those not continuing with IT qualifications

**Post-16 Progression:**
- Portfolio development supporting applications for further study or employment
- Engagement with industry standards and practices
- Links with higher education and employment to inform curriculum sequencing

## Challenges and Considerations

### The Pace of Technological Change

Miles Berry (2021) identifies the challenge of sequencing a curriculum in a rapidly changing technological landscape. Schools must balance teaching current technologies with developing transferable understanding that remains relevant as technologies evolve.

**Sequencing Strategies:**
- Focus on enduring concepts over specific software versions
- Develop pupils' ability to learn new technologies independently
- Regular curriculum review and updating
- Engagement with emerging technologies while maintaining progression coherence

### Digital Divide Considerations

Neil Selwyn (2020) emphasises that sequencing assumptions about prior experience may not hold for all pupils:

- Home technology access varies significantly
- Assumptions about "digital natives" often prove unfounded
- Cultural and socioeconomic factors affect technology engagement
- Sequencing must include explicit teaching of assumed "baseline" skills

### Balancing Breadth and Depth

Curriculum time constraints require careful sequencing decisions:

- Which IT capabilities are essential at each key stage?
- How is depth of understanding balanced against breadth of capability?
- When should specialisation occur?
- How are diverse pathways and progression routes accommodated?

Bob Harrison (2020) suggests that effective sequencing prioritises transferable digital literacy over comprehensive coverage of every possible IT application, accepting that some breadth is sacrificed for deeper understanding of core concepts.

## Future Directions

### Emerging Technologies in Sequencing

As technologies evolve, sequencing frameworks must adapt:

- **Artificial Intelligence**: When and how should AI tools enter the curriculum?
- **Cloud Computing**: How does ubiquitous cloud access change progression in file management and collaboration?
- **Mobile Technology**: How does mobile-first design thinking integrate into sequencing?
- **Immersive Technologies**: Where do virtual and augmented reality fit in progression frameworks?

### Research and Development Needs

Current understanding of effective IT sequencing would benefit from:

- Longitudinal studies tracking IT capability development across key stages
- Comparative research on different sequencing approaches
- Evidence about transfer of IT skills across contexts
- Investigation of appropriate points for introducing specific technologies or concepts

## Conclusion

Sequencing IT across key stages requires balancing systematic skill development with conceptual progression, ensuring pupils develop both practical capability and critical understanding. Effective sequencing acknowledges the integrated nature of the Computing curriculum while maintaining IT's distinct character and purposes.

As Mark Anderson (2020) observes, the ultimate goal of IT sequencing is developing digitally capable, critically literate citizens who can select, use and evaluate technology purposefully throughout their lives. This requires curriculum architecture that is simultaneously structured and flexible, progressive yet responsive to technological change, skills-focused yet conceptually grounded.

Schools must develop their own sequencing frameworks appropriate to their contexts while ensuring alignment with National Curriculum requirements and, where relevant, exam board specifications. The expertise of teachers, supported by continuing professional development and engagement with research and practice, remains central to translating sequencing principles into effective learning experiences for all pupils.
