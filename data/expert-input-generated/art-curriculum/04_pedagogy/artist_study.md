# Studying Artists, Craftspeople and Designers

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

The study of artists, craftspeople and designers constitutes one of the four key areas of study within the National Curriculum for Art and Design in England (DfE, 2013). This requirement appears across all key stages and underpins the development of visual literacy, critical thinking and contextual understanding that characterises high-quality art education.

John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently argued that studying the work of others is not merely an 'add-on' to practical work but an integral component of artistic development. As Steers notes in *Revaluing Drawing* (2009), engagement with the work of established practitioners provides students with a visual vocabulary and conceptual framework that informs and enriches their own creative practice.

The National Curriculum states that pupils should be taught "about great artists, architects and designers in history" (Key Stage 1), expanding at Key Stage 2 to "about great artists, craftspeople and designers, and understand the historical and cultural development of their art forms." At Key Stage 3, students should "analyse and evaluate their own work, and that of others, in order to strengthen the visual impact or applications of their work" and "know about great artists, craftspeople and designers."

## Pedagogical Approaches and Models

### The Critical Studies Approach

Rachel Mason's work on critical studies pedagogy, particularly in her seminal text *Art Education and Multiculturalism* (1988), established frameworks for integrating the study of artists into practical art education. Mason advocated for approaches that move beyond biographical facts to engage with works of art through structured questioning, contextual analysis and comparative study.

Leslie Cunliffe's research into effective critical studies teaching identified several key principles:

- **Integration not isolation**: Artist studies should connect directly to students' practical work, not exist as separate 'theory lessons'
- **Active engagement**: Students should respond practically to artists' work through workshops, experiments and creative responses
- **Cultural breadth**: Selection should reflect diversity of practice, culture, gender and historical period
- **Depth over breadth**: Sustained engagement with fewer artists yields better understanding than superficial surveys

### Enquiry-Based Learning

Eileen Adams' work on environmental art education and critical pedagogy emphasises the importance of students as active researchers. In *The Practice of Art & Design Teaching* (co-authored, 1982), Adams argues that studying artists should develop students' capacity to ask questions, investigate visual sources and construct meaning independently.

Effective enquiry-based approaches include:

- Posing open questions about artworks before providing information
- Encouraging students to research artists' contexts, influences and intentions
- Facilitating peer discussion and collaborative interpretation
- Supporting students in identifying connections to their own practice

### The Four-Domain Model

Building on disciplines-based art education (DBAE) approaches developed in the United States but adapted for UK contexts, effective artist study typically encompasses four domains:

1. **Making** (practical engagement)
2. **Appreciating** (aesthetic response and enjoyment)
3. **Understanding** (contextual and historical knowledge)
4. **Evaluating** (critical analysis and judgement)

Jeff Adams, in his research on digital visual culture and art education, has expanded this model to include understanding of how artists work with contemporary technologies, media and participatory practices.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 and 2

At primary level, the emphasis is on introducing children to artists working in different media and styles. NSEAD guidance suggests:

**Key Stage 1** should focus on:
- Direct, sensory engagement with artworks (reproductions, museum visits)
- Simple comparisons between artists ("How is this different from...?")
- Practical responses to artists' techniques
- Artists working in different media (painting, sculpture, textiles, ceramics)

**Key Stage 2** development includes:
- Understanding artists' lives and cultural contexts
- Comparing artists from different periods and cultures
- Beginning to use subject-specific vocabulary
- Research skills using books, galleries and digital resources
- Making connections between artists' work and pupils' own themes

John Steers has emphasised the importance of first-hand experience at primary level, advocating for museum and gallery visits where possible, and use of high-quality reproductions and online resources such as Google Arts & Culture and Tate Kids.

### Key Stage 3

At secondary level, artist study becomes more analytical and research-focused. The National Curriculum requirement to "know about great artists, craftspeople and designers" implies substantive knowledge development.

Effective KS3 practice includes:

- **Sustained projects**: Extended engagement with 2-3 artists rather than superficial surveys
- **Comparative analysis**: Examining how different practitioners approach similar themes, media or concepts
- **Contextual understanding**: Exploring movements, manifestations, cultural contexts and artistic influences
- **Primary source analysis**: Working directly from artworks, artists' statements and critical writings
- **Practical workshops**: Learning through making, using artists' techniques and processes
- **Personal response**: Developing individual interpretations and critical positions

Rachel Mason's research demonstrates that students develop deeper understanding when artist studies are structured around specific analytical frameworks, such as formal analysis, iconographic interpretation or socio-cultural contextualisation.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

All major exam boards (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) require substantive artist, craftsperson and designer studies as part of both coursework portfolios and externally set assignments.

**AQA GCSE Art and Design** Assessment Objective 1 includes "investigate and analyse the work of others to inform and develop their ideas." The specification requires students to show "understanding of the social, cultural, historical and contemporary context of art and design."

**Pearson Edexcel GCSE Art and Design** includes similar requirements under Assessment Objective 1: "Develop ideas through investigations, demonstrating critical understanding of sources."

**OCR GCSE Art and Design** explicitly requires "critical and contextual understanding demonstrated through sustained work on a theme, project or topic."

**WJEC Eduqas GCSE Art and Design** Assessment Objective 3 specifically addresses "record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions, reflecting critically on work and progress" which includes critical analysis of artists' work.

Effective GCSE practice includes:

- **Annotation**: Detailed written analysis accompanying visual research
- **Artist comparison**: Examining multiple practitioners in relation to student themes
- **Technical analysis**: Understanding materials, processes and formal elements
- **Influence and application**: Demonstrating how artist study informs practical development
- **First-hand sources**: Primary research through gallery visits where possible
- **Critical vocabulary**: Using subject-specific terminology accurately and confidently

### Key Stage 5 (A Level)

At A Level, all specifications require sophisticated critical and contextual understanding. The expectation shifts from teacher-directed study to independent research.

**AQA A Level Art and Design** requires students to "develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and critical understanding" (AO1). The Personal Study component (1000-3000 words) requires extended writing about artists in relation to the student's practical work.

**Pearson Edexcel A Level Art and Design** includes similar requirements, with Assessment Objective 1 weighted at 25% of total marks for coursework.

**OCR A Level Art and Design** requires a Written Personal Study of 1500-3000 words demonstrating "sustained critical and contextual investigation."

**WJEC Eduqas A Level Art and Design** Component 1 includes a Personal Investigation with supporting study of 1000-3000 words.

John Steers and Leslie Cunliffe's research on A Level Art emphasises the importance of genuine research skills rather than derivative copying or superficial illustration-style presentation. Effective A Level practice includes:

- Independent identification of relevant practitioners
- Sustained critical engagement with complex artworks
- Understanding of theoretical frameworks and critical perspectives
- Synthesis of multiple sources (primary artworks, critical writing, artist interviews)
- Sophisticated comparative analysis
- Clear articulation of influence on personal practice
- Academic writing skills including referencing and bibliography

## Selection of Artists, Craftspeople and Designers

### Diversity and Representation

Eileen Adams and John Steers have both written extensively about the importance of inclusive, diverse representation in artist selection. Key principles include:

**Cultural diversity**: Representing non-Western traditions, global contemporary practice, and diverse cultural perspectives. This counters the historical Eurocentrism of much art education.

**Gender representation**: Ensuring female artists, craftspeople and designers receive equal prominence. Research by Adams (1999) demonstrated systemic under-representation of women in curriculum materials.

**Historical range**: Including contemporary practitioners alongside historical masters. Jeff Adams' work emphasises the importance of students encountering living artists working with current concerns and technologies.

**Media diversity**: Representing craft practices (ceramics, textiles, metalwork), design disciplines (graphic, product, fashion, architecture), as well as fine art.

**Challenging canons**: Rachel Mason's work on multiculturalism advocates questioning traditional art historical hierarchies and broadening definitions of artistic value.

### Relevance and Connection

Leslie Cunliffe's research demonstrates that students engage more deeply with artists whose work:

- Relates to accessible themes and experiences
- Uses media and processes available in schools
- Demonstrates visible creative processes
- Addresses contemporary issues and concerns
- Offers multiple entry points for interpretation

Effective selection balances challenge (encountering unfamiliar perspectives and practices) with accessibility (finding personal connections and relevance).

## Practical Strategies for Teachers

### Resource Development

**Digital resources**: Platforms such as Tate Digital, National Gallery Learning, V&A Resources, and artist websites provide high-quality images and educational materials.

**Physical resources**: Building departmental collections of exhibition catalogues, artist monographs, reproduction packs and materials from galleries.

**Collaboration**: NSEAD regional networks facilitate resource sharing between departments.

**Student-created resources**: A Level and GCSE students can create artist study presentations for younger students, embedding learning through teaching.

### Classroom Activities

**Gallery workshops**: Practical sessions where students experiment with techniques inspired by artists' processes (e.g., Matisse cut-outs, Hockney iPad drawing, Riley optical experiments).

**Comparative analysis tasks**: Structured comparison of two artists' treatment of same subject or use of same medium.

**Sequencing activities**: Students arrange reproduction cards chronologically or thematically, developing understanding of progression and influence.

**Mystery object**: Present artwork without attribution; students investigate through visual analysis and research.

**Contemporary response**: Students create work 'in dialogue with' artist studied, not copying but responding conceptually.

**Artist statement writing**: Students write statements in the voice of artist studied, requiring deep understanding of intentions and context.

### Assessment and Progression

Progression in artist study encompasses:

**Descriptive → Analytical**: Moving from "what I can see" to "how meaning is constructed"

**Personal → Contextual**: Developing from subjective response to informed understanding of cultural/historical factors

**Teacher-directed → Independent**: Shifting from provided artists to self-directed research

**Single medium → Interdisciplinary**: Broadening from painting/drawing to installation, performance, socially-engaged practice

**Formal analysis → Theoretical frameworks**: Advancing from elements/principles to critical theory, postcolonial perspectives, feminist readings

## Common Pitfalls and Solutions

### The 'Copy and Paste' Syndrome

John Steers has frequently critiqued portfolio work that consists of internet images with minimal analysis or connection to practice. Solutions include:

- Requiring annotation that explains relevance and influence
- Emphasising quality over quantity of artists studied
- Structured analytical frameworks (formal, contextual, personal response)
- Practical workshops that engage with techniques rather than styles
- Making connections explicit through comparative layouts

### Superficial Biography

Rachel Mason's research demonstrates that focusing on artists' lives without engaging with artworks produces limited learning. Effective practice foregrounds visual analysis with biographical information providing context when relevant.

### Cultural Tourism

Eileen Adams warns against tokenistic inclusion of non-Western art or treating diverse practices as exotic 'other'. Solutions include:

- Presenting all work with equal scholarly rigour
- Avoiding essentialising statements about cultures
- Including contemporary global practitioners as equal participants in ongoing discourse
- Examining cross-cultural influences and hybridity

### Separation from Practice

Leslie Cunliffe's research conclusively demonstrates that isolated 'theory lessons' produce minimal impact on students' practical development. Artist study must be integrated through:

- Timing study to precede relevant practical units
- Explicit practical tasks responding to artists studied
- Workshopping techniques and processes
- Sketchbook investigations that synthesise research and making
- Exhibition-style presentation showing clear developmental journey

## Impact and Evidence

Research consistently demonstrates that well-structured artist study enhances:

- **Technical repertoire**: Students acquire broader range of materials knowledge and processes
- **Conceptual development**: Exposure to diverse approaches to theme/subject enriches idea generation
- **Critical thinking**: Analysis of others' work develops evaluation of own practice
- - **Cultural literacy**: Understanding art's role in society and history
- **Aspiration**: Encountering professional practice raises expectations and ambitions

Jeff Adams' research into digital visual culture suggests that contemporary approaches must also develop critical literacy around image-making in digital contexts, social media, and participatory culture.

## Conclusion

The study of artists, craftspeople and designers, when effectively integrated into art and design pedagogy, provides the contextual knowledge, visual literacy and critical understanding essential for students' development as makers and informed audiences. As John Steers argues, this is not optional enrichment but fundamental to art education's capacity to develop cultural understanding and creative confidence.

Effective practice requires careful selection ensuring diversity, structured pedagogy that promotes genuine engagement over superficial coverage, integration with practical work, and progression from supported exploration to independent critical research. The key thinkers discussed here provide robust frameworks for achieving these aims within UK curriculum contexts.
