# Assessment in Art & Design

## Introduction

Assessment in Art & Design presents unique challenges within the UK education system, requiring approaches that recognise both process and product, acknowledge subjective interpretation, and value creative risk-taking. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), argues, assessment in art must be "fit for purpose" and appropriate to the subject's distinctive nature, rather than attempting to force artistic learning into frameworks designed for other disciplines (Steers, 2009).

The National Curriculum for Art & Design in England (2013) requires that pupils "evaluate and analyse creative works using the language of art, craft and design," establishing assessment as integral to learning rather than merely summative judgement. This principle underpins effective assessment practice across all key stages.

## Theoretical Foundations

### The Challenge of Subjectivity

Eileen Adams has consistently argued that assessment in Art & Design must acknowledge and work with, rather than against, its inherently subjective nature. In her work on evaluation and assessment, Adams (1989) contends that quality in art education cannot be reduced to measurable outcomes alone, but must encompass the development of visual literacy, aesthetic understanding, and creative thinking. This positions assessment as interpretive practice requiring professional expertise and contextual understanding.

Rachel Mason's research on assessment validity in art education emphasises that effective assessment must be "authentic" – measuring what we genuinely value in art learning rather than what is merely measurable (Mason, 1995). She argues for assessment approaches that recognise the complex interplay between conceptual understanding, technical skill, creative thinking, and cultural knowledge.

### Process Versus Product

Leslie Cunliffe's work highlights the tension between assessing artistic process and finished outcomes. Cunliffe (1999) argues that over-emphasis on final products can discourage experimentation and risk-taking, essential components of creative development. Effective assessment must therefore value "working artistically" – the research, experimentation, refinement, and reflection that constitutes genuine artistic practice.

Jeff Adams extends this argument through his concept of "distributed cognition" in art education, suggesting that creative thinking is embodied in sketchbooks, preparatory work, and developmental processes, not only in finished pieces (Adams, 2007). This has significant implications for assessment design and weighting.

## Assessment Purposes and Types

### Formative Assessment (Assessment for Learning)

Formative assessment in Art & Design serves to guide learning and teaching decisions during the creative process. The Assessment Reform Group (1999) established principles that remain foundational: effective formative assessment should provide feedback that helps learners understand where they are, where they need to be, and how to bridge that gap.

**Practical strategies include:**

- **Visual dialogues**: Teacher-pupil conversations conducted directly over work in progress, using visual language and demonstration
- **Working walls and critique**: Displaying developmental work for peer and self-assessment against learning intentions
- **Sketchbook conferences**: Regular one-to-one reviews of research and development work
- **Digital annotation**: Using tablets or visualisers to provide immediate visual feedback during lessons
- **Success criteria co-construction**: Developing assessment criteria with pupils using exemplar materials

At Key Stage 1 and 2, formative assessment should emphasise growth in confidence, willingness to experiment, and developing technical control. Teachers might use observation notes, photographic records of processes, and conversations to track pupil development against National Curriculum attainment targets.

At Key Stage 3, formative assessment becomes more sophisticated, incorporating subject-specific terminology and explicit connections to artists' practices. Pupils should engage in self-assessment using discipline-specific criteria, developing their critical vocabulary.

### Summative Assessment (Assessment of Learning)

Summative assessment provides judgements about achievement at specific points. In Art & Design, this becomes particularly significant at Key Stages 4 and 5, where formal qualifications require standardised assessment.

**GCSE specifications** (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) typically assess through:
- **Component 1: Portfolio** (60% weighting) – sustained project(s) demonstrating the creative journey
- **Component 2: Externally Set Assignment** (40% weighting) – time-constrained response to stimuli

Assessment Objectives (AOs) are consistent across exam boards:
- **AO1**: Develop ideas through sustained investigation informed by contextual sources
- **AO2**: Refine work through experimentation and analysis
- **AO3**: Record ideas and observations in visual and/or other forms
- **AO4**: Present personal response showing understanding of visual language

**A-Level specifications** maintain these AOs with increased weighting toward personal outcomes and conceptual sophistication. Component 1 typically constitutes 60% (internally assessed, externally moderated) and Component 2 40% (15-hour supervised time component).

John Steers critiques the heavy weighting toward coursework, arguing it can encourage "portfolio culture" where work is endlessly refined rather than demonstrating genuine creative fluency (Steers, 2014). He advocates for assessment structures that better balance sustained investigation with responsive creative thinking.

### Ipsative Assessment

Ipsative assessment – measuring pupil progress against their own previous performance – is particularly valuable in Art & Design, where pupils begin with vastly different prior experiences and aptitudes. Rachel Mason advocates this approach for recognising individual growth and encouraging intrinsic motivation (Mason, 1988).

**Practical implementation:**
- Baseline assessments at project beginnings, revisited at conclusion
- Pupil-maintained progress portfolios showing development over time
- Reflective statements comparing current with previous work
- Target-setting based on individual development areas rather than cohort standards

## Assessment Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to "use a range of materials creatively," "use drawing, painting and sculpture," and "develop techniques." Assessment at this stage should be primarily observational and formative, focusing on:

- Engagement and enjoyment
- Willingness to explore materials
- Development of fine motor control
- Beginning to describe their work and intentions
- Response to visual stimuli

Teachers should maintain evidence through photographs, brief observational notes, and examples of work showing progression. Assessment judgements should reference "emerging," "expected," and "exceeding" year group expectations, though these must be interpreted generously given the subject's developmental nature.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Assessment becomes more structured while remaining primarily formative. The National Curriculum requires pupils to "create sketchbooks to record observations," "improve mastery of techniques," and "learn about great artists, architects and designers."

**Assessment should address:**
- Quality of research and observation (sketchbook use)
- Technical development in chosen media
- Understanding of artistic concepts and vocabulary
- Ability to discuss own and others' work
- Creative problem-solving and refinement

Eileen Adams emphasises that assessment criteria should be shared with pupils in age-appropriate language, enabling them to become active participants in their learning journey (Adams, 2003). Visual success criteria and exemplar materials support this approach.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum for KS3 requires increased sophistication: pupils should "use a range of techniques and media, including painting," "increase proficiency in handling different materials," "analyse and evaluate their own work and that of others," and "know about great artists, craft makers and designers."

Assessment frameworks should distinguish between:
- **Developing**: Basic control of materials, simple connections to artists, descriptive evaluation
- **Secure**: Confident material handling, informed research, analytical evaluation using subject terminology
- **Advanced**: Sophisticated technique, perceptive contextual understanding, critical evaluation demonstrating insight

Leslie Cunliffe warns against overly prescriptive assessment at KS3, arguing that this is the stage where creative confidence is most fragile and assessment should support risk-taking rather than risk-aversion (Cunliffe, 2005).

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

GCSE assessment is criterion-referenced against the four Assessment Objectives. While exam boards provide grade descriptors, effective assessment requires:

**Regular assessment points:**
- Project commencement: AO1 focus (contextual research and initial ideas)
- Mid-project: AO2 and AO3 (experimentation and recording)
- Project conclusion: AO4 (personal response)
- Holistic review: ensuring balanced coverage across all AOs

**Moderation considerations:**
- Internal standardisation within departments using exam board exemplars
- Rank ordering to ensure consistency across the cohort
- Annotation explaining how work meets assessment criteria
- Evidence of teacher feedback throughout the creative process

OCR's "Preparing for Assessment" guidance (2020) emphasises that annotation should be evaluative rather than descriptive, demonstrating why work meets specific criteria at specific levels.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-Level assessment maintains the same AO structure but with higher expectations for:
- Conceptual sophistication and theoretical understanding
- Technical expertise and material fluency
- Critical and contextual depth
- Coherence and intentionality in personal responses

AQA's Chief Examiner reports consistently identify that high-achieving candidates demonstrate "purposeful and sustained development" rather than scattered experimentation, alongside "perceptive critical understanding" of contextual sources (AQA, 2022).

**Assessment at A-Level requires:**
- Clear differentiation between routine competence and exceptional insight
- Recognition of individual creative voice and artistic authenticity
- Evidence of independent thought and self-directed research
- Sophisticated visual and written communication

## The Role of Sketchbooks in Assessment

Sketchbooks occupy a unique position in Art & Design assessment as repositories of process, research, and creative thinking. Jeff Adams argues that sketchbooks should be understood as "thinking spaces" where ideas develop through visual means, not merely preparation for final outcomes (Adams, 2012).

**Effective sketchbook assessment considers:**
- **Research quality**: depth and breadth of visual and contextual investigation
- **Experimentation**: willingness to test ideas, materials, and approaches
- **Annotation**: reflective thought demonstrating artistic reasoning
- **Connectivity**: relationships between research, experimentation, and outcomes
- **Personal voice**: individual approach and creative identity

John Steers cautions against overly prescriptive sketchbook requirements, arguing that excessive teacher direction can stifle the personal investigation that sketchbooks should facilitate (Steers, 2003). Balance is required between structure that supports learning and freedom that enables genuine creative inquiry.

## Marking and Feedback

### Effective Feedback Practices

Research by Hattie and Timperley (2007), though not Art & Design-specific, establishes feedback effectiveness principles applicable to the subject:

**Feedback should address:**
1. **Feed up**: Where am I going? (clarity about learning intentions)
2. **Feed back**: How am I going? (information about current progress)
3. **Feed forward**: Where to next? (guidance for improvement)

In Art & Design contexts, this translates to:
- **Visual demonstration**: showing rather than merely describing improvements
- **Comparative feedback**: referencing artists' work or exemplars
- **Question-based prompts**: encouraging problem-solving rather than providing solutions
- **Prioritised targets**: focusing on 2-3 key development areas
- **Dialogue not monologue**: interactive feedback conversations

Rachel Mason argues that effective feedback in art education must be "educative" – developing pupils' capacity to self-assess and improve independently, rather than creating dependency on teacher judgement (Mason, 2008).

### Written Commentary

While Art & Design is fundamentally visual, written feedback serves important functions:
- Recording assessment decisions and rationale
- Providing specific, actionable guidance
- Supporting moderation and accountability
- Developing pupils' critical literacy

**Effective written feedback:**
- Uses subject-specific terminology appropriately
- References specific aspects of work visually or by location
- Balances affirmation with challenge
- Connects to Assessment Objectives at KS4/5
- Sets clear, achievable targets

Eileen Adams warns against feedback that is generic or could apply to any pupil's work, emphasising the need for responses that demonstrate genuine engagement with individual creative thinking (Adams, 2010).

## Moderation and Standardisation

### Internal Standardisation

Within departments, maintaining consistent standards requires:
- **Regular moderation meetings**: collective review of work against criteria
- **Blind double-marking**: assessing work samples without prior knowledge of provisional marks
- **Exemplar libraries**: building departmental collections showing standards at different levels
- **Shared annotation**: discussing and agreeing upon assessment rationales
- **Cross-key-stage dialogue**: ensuring progression in expectations

Leslie Cunliffe emphasises that standardisation should not mean homogenisation – agreement on standards can coexist with diverse creative approaches and outcomes (Cunliffe, 2015).

### External Moderation

At GCSE and A-Level, exam board moderation ensures national standards:
- **Sample selection**: representing the full range of centre marks
- **Annotation requirements**: clear evidence of how criteria were applied
- **Rank ordering**: demonstrating relative achievement within cohorts
- **Centre Authentication**: confirming work authenticity and appropriate supervision

Edexcel's "Understanding Moderation" guidance (2021) emphasises that moderators assess whether centres have correctly applied assessment criteria, not whether they agree with creative choices or aesthetic preferences.

## Assessing Diverse Learners

### Inclusive Assessment

Art & Design can provide accessible success routes for pupils who struggle in text-heavy subjects. Assessment must recognise diverse strengths:

**For pupils with SEND:**
- Emphasis on practical achievement where appropriate
- Alternative recording methods (photographic, audio, video)
- Success criteria differentiated to individual targets
- Recognition of effort and persistence alongside outcomes

**For EAL learners:**
- Visual communication as strength rather than barrier
- Vocabulary support embedded in teaching and assessment
- Exemplar materials reducing language dependency
- Collaborative assessment conversations with additional time

**For disadvantaged learners:**
- Ensuring material access doesn't limit creative ambition
- Valuing resourcefulness and creative problem-solving
- Providing structured opportunities for enrichment activities
- Recognition that cultural capital varies

John Steers argues that Art & Design assessment should celebrate "visual intelligence" in its own right, not as compensation for limitations elsewhere (Steers, 2009).

## Assessment and Creative Risk-Taking

A persistent tension exists between assessment requirements and creative experimentation. Eileen Adams identifies how "playing safe" strategies can emerge when pupils perceive assessment as threatening, leading to formulaic responses rather than genuine creative thinking (Adams, 2008).

**Strategies to maintain creative risk-taking:**
- **Formative emphasis**: high-frequency, low-stakes feedback during development
- **Explicit value of process**: assessment weighting toward experimentation
- **Failure as learning**: celebrating and assessing learning from unsuccessful experiments
- **Student choice**: autonomy in project directions and material selection
- **Diverse exemplars**: showing multiple successful approaches to similar briefs

Jeff Adams argues for "negotiated assessment" in post-16 contexts, where pupils and teachers collaboratively determine assessment criteria for individual projects, promoting ownership and authentic creative inquiry (Adams, 2014).

## Digital Portfolios and Assessment Technologies

Contemporary practice increasingly employs digital tools:

**Benefits:**
- Comprehensive documentation of process through photography and video
- Easy annotation and feedback delivery
- Accessibility for moderation and parents
- Development of digital literacy skills
- Environmental sustainability

**Considerations:**
- Ensuring photographic evidence fairly represents physical work
- Authentication concerns with digital manipulation
- Equity of access to technology
- Screen-based limitations for assessing material qualities
- Teacher workload in managing digital systems

Exam boards now accept digital portfolios under specified conditions, though John Steers cautions against assuming digital presentation inherently improves assessment validity (Steers, 2019).

## Research-Informed Assessment Development

Current research directions include:

**Comparative judgement approaches**: Research by Pollitt (2012) and trialled in Art & Design suggests that teachers may make more reliable assessment decisions through paired comparisons than absolute marking schemes. The Daisy Christodoulou (2017) work at No More Marking has explored this in creative subjects, suggesting potential for reducing workload while improving reliability.

**Assessment literacy**: Rachel Mason's recent work emphasises developing both teacher and pupil "assessment literacy" – sophisticated understanding of quality and standards – as essential for valid assessment (Mason, 2019).

**Decolonising assessment**: Current discourse questions whose artistic values assessment criteria privilege. Jeff Adams calls for assessment frameworks that recognise diverse cultural aesthetics and creative traditions (Adams, 2020).

## Practical Recommendations for Teachers

1. **Make assessment integral to learning**: embed peer and self-assessment routinely, not as separate activities
2. **Develop shared visual literacy**: use exemplars extensively to build understanding of standards
3. **Balance structure with freedom**: provide clear frameworks while enabling individual creative agency
4. **Prioritise formative over summative**: frequent, low-stakes feedback supporting development
5. **Assess the whole creative journey**: value research, experimentation, and reflection alongside outcomes
6. **Use subject-specific language**: develop precise critical vocabulary in feedback
7. **Engage pupils as assessment partners**: co-construct criteria and success indicators
8. **Document process systematically**: ensure evidence exists for the creative journey
9. **Moderate collaboratively**: regular departmental dialogue on standards
10. **Reflect on bias**: consciously consider whose artistic values assessment privileges
