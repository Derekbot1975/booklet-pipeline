# Teaching the Creative Process

## Introduction

The creative process sits at the heart of Art & Design education in the UK, underpinning both National Curriculum requirements and examination specifications. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently argued, art education should develop students' capacity to think and work as artists, craftspeople and designers, rather than simply producing prescribed outcomes. The creative process encompasses ideation, experimentation, refinement and realisation, and requires explicit pedagogical attention to develop students' creative capabilities effectively.

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2014) states that pupils should be taught to "develop their creativity and ideas" and to "increase their proficiency in the execution of their ideas", positioning process as fundamental to learning rather than ancillary to product.

## Understanding the Creative Process

### Theoretical Frameworks

Eileen Adams, in her seminal work on environmental art education and community-engaged practice, emphasises that creativity is not a mystical gift but a learnable set of behaviours and dispositions. She advocates for process-based learning that values exploration, risk-taking and iterative development. Adams argues that students need structured opportunities to develop creative thinking alongside technical skills.

Rachel Mason's research into art curriculum theory identifies the creative process as encompassing several distinct but interconnected phases: investigation, experimentation, development and refinement. Her work with teachers internationally demonstrates that when these phases are made explicit and deliberately taught, students develop greater metacognitive awareness of their own creative decision-making.

Leslie Cunliffe's contributions to understanding progression in art education highlight that the creative process becomes increasingly sophisticated as students mature. At Key Stage 2, process may be relatively linear and teacher-scaffolded; by Key Stage 4, students should demonstrate independent, cyclical working with deliberate iteration and critical self-reflection.

### The Creative Cycle in Practice

Jeff Adams, in his writings on digital art and contemporary practice, extends traditional notions of the creative process to encompass contemporary multi-modal approaches. He argues that the creative process is fundamentally cyclical rather than linear, involving constant movement between doing and reflecting, making and evaluating.

The typical creative cycle in Art & Design includes:
- **Research and investigation**: gathering primary and secondary sources
- **Experimentation**: exploring materials, techniques and visual language
- **Development**: refining ideas through iterative making
- **Realisation**: producing resolved outcomes
- **Evaluation**: critical reflection on process and product

This cycle reflects requirements across all major exam boards and key stages.

## Key Stage Considerations

### Key Stage 2

At primary level, the National Curriculum requires pupils to "develop their techniques" and "to improve their mastery of art and design techniques". The creative process at this stage should emphasise:

- **Playful exploration**: allowing extended periods for material investigation without pressure for immediate outcomes
- **Process documentation**: introducing simple sketchbooks or visual journals where experimentation is valued
- **Modelling thinking**: teachers demonstrating their own creative decision-making through think-aloud protocols
- **Making thinking visible**: using display to celebrate process as well as finished work

Teachers should avoid overemphasis on neat, completed products at the expense of exploratory making. As Eileen Adams notes in her work on primary art education, young children's creative confidence can be undermined when process is devalued in favour of prescribed outcomes.

### Key Stage 3

The transition to secondary education represents an opportunity to make the creative process more explicit and structured. Students should develop:

- **Independent investigation skills**: researching artists, movements and contexts relevant to their work
- **Systematic experimentation**: testing materials and techniques with increasing rigour
- **Analytical annotation**: articulating their thinking, intentions and evaluations in written form
- **Iterative development**: understanding that ideas evolve through multiple iterations

John Steers emphasises that Key Stage 3 is crucial for establishing robust creative habits that will serve students through GCSE and beyond. The process should become increasingly personalised while maintaining structure and rigour.

### Key Stage 4: GCSE Requirements

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure their GCSE Art & Design specifications around Assessment Objective 1 (AO1) through AO4, which explicitly assess the creative process:

**AO1**: Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual and other sources (25%)
**AO2**: Refine work by exploring ideas, selecting and experimenting with appropriate media, materials, techniques and processes (25%)
**AO3**: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions (25%)
**AO4**: Present a personal and meaningful response (25%)

The portfolio component (typically 60% of the qualification) exists specifically to evidence the creative process. Teachers must ensure students understand that sustained engagement with process is not merely preparatory for final outcomes but is itself the primary object of assessment.

Rachel Mason's research indicates that many students struggle to understand this fundamental shift from product-focused to process-focused assessment. Explicit teaching of portfolio development as a record of creative thinking is therefore essential.

### Key Stage 5: A Level Requirements

At A Level, the creative process becomes more sophisticated and personally directed. Specifications from all boards maintain the AO1-AO4 structure but expect greater independence, criticality and coherence.

OCR's emphasis on the "personal investigation" (worth 60% at A Level) requires students to develop "a sustained and focused project evidencing the journey from initial engagement to the realisation of intentions". This journey is the creative process made manifest.

AQA's specification similarly requires evidence of how "ideas, feelings and meanings are effectively conveyed through purposeful and considered selection, interpretation and application of visual language, materials, techniques and processes".

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Sketchbooks and Visual Journals

The sketchbook is the primary vehicle for teaching and evidencing creative process in UK Art & Design education. However, as John Steers has observed, sketchbooks can become formulaic and restrictive if approached prescriptively.

**Effective sketchbook pedagogy includes:**

- **Freedom within structure**: providing frameworks (e.g., "explore three approaches to mark-making") while allowing personal interpretation
- **Regular formative assessment**: frequent, low-stakes review conversations that focus on thinking rather than neatness
- **Diversified documentation**: incorporating photography, ephemera, written reflection, digital experimentation
- **Modelling practice**: teachers maintaining their own sketchbooks and sharing their process with students

Leslie Cunliffe's work on assessment emphasises that sketchbooks should evidence thinking-in-progress rather than retrospective presentation. Messy exploration and apparent "failures" should be valued as authentic evidence of risk-taking and problem-solving.

### Explicit Strategy Instruction

Jeff Adams advocates for explicit teaching of creative strategies rather than assuming students will develop them independently. This includes:

**Divergent thinking techniques**: brainstorming, mind-mapping, SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse)

**Research strategies**: artist studies, contextual investigation, primary observation, photographic recording

**Experimental protocols**: systematic material testing, controlled variation of techniques, comparative sampling

**Reflective practices**: annotation, evaluation frameworks, peer critique structures

### Structured Experimentation

Rachel Mason's research demonstrates that students benefit from structured frameworks for experimentation, particularly at Key Stage 3 and early Key Stage 4. This might include:

- **Material matrices**: testing a consistent visual element (e.g., a motif) across different media
- **Technical progressions**: building complexity systematically (e.g., monochrome to limited palette to full colour)
- **Comparative studies**: working from the same source in multiple styles or approaches
- **Time-bounded challenges**: short, focused experiments that reduce anxiety about "wasting" work

The key is providing sufficient structure to scaffold productive experimentation while avoiding prescription that eliminates creative decision-making.

### Making Time for Process

A persistent challenge in UK Art & Design education is curriculum time. Eileen Adams has been particularly vocal about the erosion of curriculum time for art at Key Stage 4, which has been reduced in many schools to three hours per fortnight or less.

**Teachers must therefore:**

- **Prioritise depth over breadth**: fewer, more sustained projects rather than multiple shallow activities
- **Integrate homework strategically**: research, gallery visits, photographic recording conducted outside lessons
- **Use starter activities purposefully**: quick experiments that build technical vocabulary without dominating lesson time
- **Plan for sustained engagement**: double or triple periods where possible to allow immersive making

John Steers argues that the creative process cannot be meaningfully engaged with in isolated single lessons; students need extended time to enter a state of creative flow.

## Assessment of Creative Process

### Formative Assessment Strategies

Assessment of process requires fundamentally different approaches than assessment of products. Leslie Cunliffe's research into progression in art emphasises the importance of formative assessment conversations that:

- **Focus on decision-making**: "Why did you choose this approach?" rather than "Is this finished?"
- **Encourage metacognition**: "What did you learn from this experiment?"
- **Identify next steps**: "What could you try next to develop this further?"
- **Value risk and experimentation**: celebrating productive failures as learning

**Practical strategies include:**

- **Process portfolios**: distinct from exhibition portfolios, these document working methods
- **Learning logs**: structured reflection templates completed during project work
- **Peer critique protocols**: structured frameworks for students to discuss process (e.g., Critical Response Protocol, Tuning Protocol)
- **Video documentation**: time-lapse or reflective video journals capturing making-in-progress

### Summative Assessment and Exam Board Requirements

All GCSE and A Level specifications assess creative process through portfolio work, but approaches vary:

**AQA** requires portfolios to show "evidence of drawing activity and creative development" with clear links between investigation, experimentation and outcomes.

**Edexcel/Pearson** emphasises "developmental evidence" showing how ideas have been "explored, evaluated and refined".

**OCR** specifies that portfolios should evidence "a journey of development" with "evidence of review and refinement".

**WJEC Eduqas** requires "evidence of the creative journey" with "connections between contextual study and practical work".

Teachers must ensure students understand these requirements and structure portfolios accordingly, typically through:

- **Regular portfolio reviews**: whole-class sessions examining exemplar portfolios and discussing quality of process evidence
- **Annotation workshops**: teaching students to articulate their thinking effectively
- **Mock assessments**: applying mark schemes to work-in-progress to identify gaps in process documentation

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### The "Final Piece" Fixation

Rachel Mason identifies a persistent tendency among students (and some teachers) to view the entire creative process as merely preparation for a "final piece". This fundamentally misunderstands the nature of art education assessment.

**Solutions include:**

- **Reframing language**: discussing "outcomes" or "resolutions" rather than "final pieces"
- **Multiple outcomes**: requiring several resolved responses rather than a single culminating work
- **Process-based display**: exhibiting sketchbook work alongside outcomes to demonstrate parity of value
- **Mark scheme analysis**: showing students that 75% of assessment focuses on process (AO1-AO3)

### Performative Process

John Steers warns against "performative" process work where students retrospectively create the appearance of experimentation to satisfy assessment requirements rather than genuinely engaging with creative exploration.

**Indicators of performative process include:**
- Over-neat, uniform sketchbook pages lacking authentic working
- Annotation that describes rather than reflects
- Absence of risk-taking, failure or revision
- Evidence created after outcomes are complete

**Solutions involve:**
- **Regular progress checks**: monitoring work-in-progress rather than only reviewing completed portfolios
- **Authentic documentation**: encouraging photographs of messy workspaces, test pieces, abandoned directions
- **Valuing uncertainty**: celebrating "I don't know where this is going yet" as legitimate creative practice

### Differentiation in Process Work

Leslie Cunliffe's research highlights that students enter secondary art education with vastly different experiences of creative process. Some arrive with extensive primary school experience of exploratory making; others have encountered only prescriptive "craft" activities.

**Differentiation strategies include:**

- **Tiered scaffolding**: providing structured frameworks for those who need them while allowing independence for confident experimenters
- **Exemplar analysis**: examining a range of process work at different levels to help students identify appropriate challenge
- **Individual target-setting**: personalised next steps based on current process strengths and development areas
- **Flexible timeframes**: allowing some students extended investigation periods while others move more quickly to resolution

## Contemporary Developments

### Digital Technologies and Creative Process

Jeff Adams's work on digital art education emphasises that digital technologies fundamentally alter creative process rather than simply providing new tools. Digital workflows enable:

- **Non-linear development**: easy movement between stages through layers, versions and files
- **Iterative experimentation**: rapid testing of variations through filters, effects, digital manipulation
- **Hybrid approaches**: integration of analogue and digital making within unified processes
- **Documentation affordances**: screenshots, screen recordings and digital portfolios capturing process automatically

However, Adams warns against assuming digital natives intuitively understand creative digital process; explicit pedagogy remains essential. Students need teaching about:

- **Versioning strategies**: maintaining evidence of development through saved iterations
- **Digital sketchbooks**: using tools like Procreate, Photoshop or Illustrator for genuine exploration rather than only final production
- **Reflective annotation**: embedding commentary within digital portfolios

### Sustainable and Socially-Engaged Practice

Eileen Adams's later work emphasises creative process within community-engaged and socially-responsive art practice. This extends traditional studio-based process to include:

- **Collaborative development**: co-creation with communities or peers
- **Responsive iteration**: adapting work based on audience engagement or site-specific factors
- **Ethical reflection**: considering social and environmental implications within the creative cycle

This aligns with contemporary professional practice and prepares students for art and design careers involving participatory and sustainable approaches.

## Practical Implications for Curriculum Planning

### Scheme of Work Design

John Steers and Rachel Mason both emphasise that creative process must be embedded systematically across schemes of work rather than addressed sporadically. Effective curriculum planning includes:

**Project structures that:**
- Begin with extended research and investigation phases (typically 2-3 weeks at GCSE)
- Include explicit experimentation stages with technical skill-building
- Allow multiple development cycles before finalisation
- Build in review and reflection points

**Progressive skill development:**
- Key Stage 3: foundations of investigation, recording and experimentation
- Year 10: increasing independence and personal direction within structured projects
- Year 11: largely self-directed process with teacher as critical friend
- Year 12: individual investigation routes with sustained focus
- Year 13: sophisticated, personally-meaningful creative inquiry

### Resource Implications

Teaching creative process effectively requires:

**Material resources:**
- Diverse media for experimentation (not restricted to "precious" materials)
- Adequate sketchbooks/journals for all students
- Access to cameras/devices for documentation
- Digital tools for process recording where possible

**Time resources:**
- Extended lesson blocks where timetabling permits
- Dedicated reflection/review time within lessons
- Gallery/site visit opportunities for primary investigation

**Professional development:**
- Teachers engaging with their own creative practice
- Sharing process-focused pedagogy across departments
- Examining exemplar student work from exam boards

## Conclusion

Teaching the creative process effectively requires moving beyond romantic notions of creativity as spontaneous inspiration towards understanding it as a structured, learnable set of practices. As the key thinkers in UK Art & Design education consistently argue—from Eileen Adams's emphasis on exploratory learning to John Steers's advocacy for rigorous creative education to Rachel Mason's frameworks for process pedagogy—students benefit from explicit instruction, structured scaffolding and genuine valuing of experimentation and development.

The National Curriculum and all major exam board specifications position creative process as central to Art & Design education. Teachers must therefore ensure that process is not merely gestured towards but is systematically taught, authentically practiced and appropriately assessed. This requires curriculum time, appropriate resources, and pedagogical commitment to valuing the journey as much as the destination.

When creative process is taught well, students develop not only improved artistic capabilities but also transferable skills in problem-solving, critical thinking, resilience and self-directed learning—capabilities valuable far beyond the art room.
