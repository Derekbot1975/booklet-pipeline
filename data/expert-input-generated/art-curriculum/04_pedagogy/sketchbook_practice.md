# Sketchbook Practice and Process

## Introduction and Curriculum Context

Sketchbook practice forms a cornerstone of UK Art & Design education, serving as a continuous visual diary that documents the creative journey from initial concept to final outcome. The National Curriculum for Art and Design (2014) explicitly requires that pupils be taught to 'record their observations and use them to review and revisit ideas', positioning the sketchbook as an essential tool for developing visual literacy and creative thinking.

John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently advocated for sketchbooks as spaces for experimentation rather than merely presentational portfolios. He argues that the sketchbook should function as 'a thinking tool, not just a recording device' (Steers, 2009), emphasising the importance of process over product in authentic creative development.

## Theoretical Foundations

### The Sketchbook as Reflective Practice

Eileen Adams' research into visual learning emphasises the sketchbook as a site for developing 'visual literacy' and critical thinking skills. Her work in publications such as *Power of Art* (2003) and her contributions to NSEAD's International Journal of Art & Design Education demonstrate how sketchbook practice enables pupils to develop metacognitive awareness of their creative processes.

Rachel Mason's research into international art education practices highlights the cultural specificity of sketchbook use. In her comparative studies, Mason (2005) notes that UK examination systems place particular emphasis on documented process work, contrasting this with approaches in other countries where the final artwork may be valued independently. This cultural context shapes how UK teachers approach sketchbook pedagogy.

### Process-Based Learning

Leslie Cunliffe's work on assessment in art education underscores the significance of visible working processes. In her contributions to debates around GCSE and A-Level assessment, Cunliffe argues that sketchbooks provide essential evidence of Assessment Objective 1 (AO1): 'Develop ideas through investigations, demonstrating critical understanding of sources'. The sketchbook becomes the primary vehicle for demonstrating this developmental journey.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Ages 5-11)

At primary level, sketchbooks should be introduced as personal, exploratory spaces where mistakes are valued. The National Curriculum requires pupils to 'use a range of materials creatively to design and make products' and to 'use drawing, painting and sculpture to develop and share their ideas, experiences and imagination'.

**Practical approaches:**
- Introduce sketchbooks as 'visual journals' or 'idea books' rather than finished portfolios
- Establish a culture where experimental marks and 'failures' are celebrated
- Encourage annotation through talk, recorded observations, and emergent writing
- Model sketchbook use regularly through teacher demonstration
- Provide dedicated sketchbook time rather than homework-only use
- Use diverse paper types and sizes to maintain interest and accommodate different media

Jeff Adams' research into digital media in art education (Adams, 2005) suggests that even at primary level, sketchbooks can incorporate digital elements such as printed photographs, QR codes linking to digital work, or collaged screen captures, preparing pupils for multimodal creative practice.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

At KS3, sketchbook practice becomes increasingly sophisticated. The curriculum requires pupils to 'increase their proficiency in the handling of different materials' and to 'analyse and evaluate their own work, and that of others'. Sketchbooks provide the ideal vehicle for both objectives.

**Developmental focus:**
- Introduce formal recording techniques (observational drawing, tonal studies, colour mixing)
- Develop annotation skills: analytical writing about artists, evaluation of experiments
- Establish connections between primary sources, artist research, and personal development
- Introduce comparative analysis within sketchbook pages
- Encourage risk-taking and experimentation with unfamiliar materials
- Build independence in self-directed study and research

John Steers emphasises that KS3 is crucial for establishing what he terms 'visual thinking habits'—the routine practices of observation, recording, experimentation, and reflection that underpin successful creative work at GCSE and beyond.

### Key Stage 4 and 5 (Ages 14-18)

GCSE and A-Level specifications across all UK exam boards place substantial weight on sketchbook evidence, typically comprising 50% or more of the overall grade through coursework portfolios.

#### GCSE Requirements (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC)

All GCSE specifications assess four objectives, with AO1-AO3 predominantly evidenced through sketchbook and development work:
- **AO1:** Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual and other sources (25%)
- **AO2:** Refine work by exploring ideas, selecting and experimenting with appropriate media, materials, techniques and processes (25%)
- **AO3:** Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions (25%)
- **AO4:** Present a personal and meaningful response (25%)

**Critical practice:**
- Sustained investigations showing clear development from initial stimuli
- Primary source material (observational studies, photographs, collected objects)
- Analytical artist research with clear visual connections to own work
- Systematic experimentation with techniques, media, and processes
- Evaluative annotation demonstrating critical thinking
- Evidence of refinement: revisiting, improving, and modifying ideas

#### A-Level Requirements

A-Level specifications maintain the same assessment objectives but demand greater depth, sophistication, and independence. OCR's specification explicitly states that sketchbooks should demonstrate 'sustained engagement' with ideas, while AQA emphasises 'coherent, in-depth development'.

Eileen Adams' concept of 'visual argumentation' becomes particularly relevant at A-Level, where students must construct persuasive visual narratives that demonstrate intellectual engagement with concepts, contexts, and materials.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Establishing Effective Practice

Rachel Mason's research emphasises the importance of explicit teaching about sketchbook purposes and processes. Teachers should not assume students intuitively understand what constitutes effective sketchbook work; rather, they must actively teach these practices.

**Strategies for developing sketchbook culture:**

1. **Regular, dedicated time:** Timetable specific lessons for sketchbook development rather than relegating it to homework
2. **Teacher modelling:** Maintain and share your own sketchbook practice, demonstrating thinking processes
3. **Exemplar analysis:** Examine and discuss high-quality examples, deconstructing what makes them effective
4. **Structured freedom:** Provide frameworks (layout templates, question prompts) while encouraging creative interpretation
5. **Formative feedback:** Implement regular sketchbook reviews with specific, actionable feedback
6. **Peer dialogue:** Create opportunities for students to share and discuss work-in-progress

### Beyond the Traditional: Evolving Sketchbook Practice

Jeff Adams' work on digital technologies challenges traditional conceptions of the bound paper sketchbook. His research suggests that contemporary sketchbook practice might include:

- **Digital sketchbooks:** Using iPads, tablets, or software like Photoshop, Procreate, or Adobe Sketch
- **Hybrid formats:** Combining physical and digital elements through printing, augmented reality, or QR codes
- **Online platforms:** Instagram, blogs, or e-portfolios as living sketchbooks
- **Film and video:** Recording performance, installation, or process-based work
- **3D documentation:** Photographing sculptural experiments, model-making processes

However, Adams cautions against technology for its own sake, emphasising that pedagogical purpose must drive format choices. The sketchbook, in whatever form, must facilitate thinking, experimentation, and reflection.

### Assessment and Feedback

Leslie Cunliffe's research into art assessment practices highlights the complexity of evaluating process-based work. She argues that assessment criteria must value genuine experimentation, including productive failures, rather than incentivising 'safe' or overly-polished presentations.

**Effective assessment practice:**

- **Formative emphasis:** Use sketchbooks primarily for ongoing feedback rather than summative grading
- **Clear success criteria:** Co-construct rubrics with students, making expectations transparent
- **Holistic evaluation:** Assess the overall journey and thinking rather than individual page quality
- **Dialogue-based:** Conduct one-to-one discussions about sketchbook work, enabling students to articulate their thinking
- **Developmental commentary:** Focus feedback on next steps and possibilities rather than deficits

The Assessment and Qualifications Alliance (AQA) provides detailed guidance stating that 'annotation should be evaluative rather than descriptive' and that examiners look for evidence of 'meaningful investigation' rather than quantity of pages.

## Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: The 'Neat Sketchbook' Syndrome

Many students, particularly high-achievers, treat sketchbooks as display portfolios, inhibiting genuine experimentation. John Steers describes this as prioritising 'presentation over exploration', fundamentally undermining the sketchbook's pedagogical purpose.

**Solutions:**
- Explicitly teach that experimental pages are valued equally or more highly than 'finished' ones
- Display examples of working pages from professional artists' sketchbooks
- Introduce 'messy Monday' or similar initiatives celebrating experimentation
- Separate presentation portfolios from working sketchbooks where appropriate

### Challenge 2: Equity of Access

Eileen Adams' work on inclusive practice highlights potential barriers in sketchbook requirements, including cost of materials, space to work at home, and cultural capital regarding what constitutes 'art practice'.

**Solutions:**
- Provide all materials in school, ensuring equity
- Dedicate curriculum time for sketchbook development
- Teach explicitly about creative processes, not assuming prior knowledge
- Recognise diverse cultural approaches to visual documentation
- Offer flexible formats accommodating different working preferences and needs

### Challenge 3: Digital Disconnect

Students increasingly create visual work digitally but may struggle to integrate this into physical sketchbooks, or teachers may undervalue digital development work.

**Solutions:**
- Teach integration techniques (printing, screen-capture, photographic documentation)
- Accept purely digital sketchbooks where appropriate (check with exam boards)
- Value digital experimentation equally with traditional media
- Teach critical evaluation of digital processes, not just outcomes

## Research Evidence and Impact

Research by Dorn and Sabol (2006), while American, has influenced UK practice by demonstrating that regular sketchbook use correlates with improved observational skills, creative confidence, and academic achievement in art. Their longitudinal study showed students maintaining consistent sketchbook practice demonstrated significantly greater progress in technical skills and creative problem-solving.

UK-specific research by the National Foundation for Educational Research (NFER) into GCSE Art & Design outcomes consistently shows that students who engage deeply with process work achieve higher grades. The 2019 Ofqual report on GCSE Art & Design noted that 'the strongest portfolios demonstrated extensive, organic development through sketchbooks, with clear evidence of visual thinking'.

Rachel Mason's international comparative research suggests that the UK's emphasis on documented process work, while sometimes critiqued as bureaucratic, actually develops valuable transferable skills in research, analysis, self-evaluation, and project management that benefit students beyond art education.

## Professional Development Implications

For effective sketchbook pedagogy, teachers should:

1. **Maintain personal practice:** Regularly use sketchbooks yourself, sharing your creative process with students
2. **Study exam board requirements:** Thoroughly understand assessment criteria and exemplification materials
3. **Engage with current artists:** Follow contemporary artists on social media to see diverse sketchbook practices
4. **Collaborate departmentally:** Establish consistent approaches across year groups while allowing individual teacher creativity
5. **Reflect critically:** Question whether your sketchbook requirements genuinely serve learning or become performative box-ticking

NSEAD provides extensive continuing professional development resources on sketchbook practice, and regional subject associations offer network meetings where teachers share effective approaches.

## Conclusion

Sketchbook practice, when approached thoughtfully, represents far more than an examination requirement. As John Steers articulates, it embodies a way of thinking visually, of engaging with the world through observation and making, and of developing personal creative voice. The challenge for UK Art & Design teachers is to maintain the integrity of this exploratory practice within an assessment-driven system, ensuring that sketchbooks remain genuine sites of creative risk-taking and intellectual engagement rather than becoming formulaic documentation exercises.

Eileen Adams reminds us that 'the quality of looking precedes the quality of making'—sketchbooks are where this looking, questioning, and understanding develops. By establishing robust sketchbook practices from Key Stage 1 through to A-Level, teachers enable students to develop visual literacy, creative confidence, and critical thinking that extends well beyond the art room.
