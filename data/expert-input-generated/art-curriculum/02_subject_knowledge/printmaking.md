# Printmaking Across Key Stages

## Introduction to Printmaking in UK Art & Design Education

Printmaking holds a distinctive position within the UK Art & Design curriculum as a discipline that bridges historical craft traditions with contemporary digital practices. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has emphasised, printmaking offers unique opportunities for understanding processes, multiples, and the relationship between intention and outcome that are fundamental to art and design education.

The National Curriculum for Art and Design in England (2013) explicitly requires pupils to develop competence in a range of art techniques, including printing, whilst also encouraging engagement with great artists, craft makers and designers. Printmaking addresses both requirements effectively, providing access to historical traditions whilst remaining highly relevant to contemporary visual culture through digital reproduction and screen-based imagery.

## Theoretical Frameworks and Key Thinkers

### Educational Philosophy of Printmaking

Rachel Mason's research on craft education emphasises the importance of 'intelligent making', where the process of creation involves continuous problem-solving and decision-making. Printmaking exemplifies this approach, requiring pupils to think ahead, work in stages, and understand causality between action and result. The discipline demands what Mason terms 'procedural knowledge' – understanding not just what to do but why particular sequences matter.

Eileen Adams, through her extensive work on environmental and creative learning, advocates for printmaking as a means of engaging with the immediate environment. Her research demonstrates how printmaking techniques enable pupils to collect, record and transform observations from their surroundings, creating what she terms 'visual mapping' that deepens environmental literacy.

Leslie Cunliffe's work on assessment in art education highlights printmaking's value in making thinking visible. The staged nature of print processes creates natural documentation points that support both formative assessment and pupils' metacognitive development. Unlike drawing or painting, where work can be continuously modified, printmaking's commitment points encourage planning and reflection.

## Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

### Curriculum Requirements and Context

At Key Stage 1, the National Curriculum requires pupils to 'use a range of materials creatively to design and make products' and 'develop a wide range of art and design techniques in using colour, pattern, texture, line, shape, form and space'. Printmaking provides an accessible entry point for these requirements whilst introducing foundational concepts of repetition, pattern and process.

### Appropriate Techniques and Approaches

**Found object printing** serves as an ideal introduction, enabling pupils to discover that any surface with texture or relief can transfer marks. This approach connects directly to Eileen Adams' emphasis on environmental engagement – collecting leaves, fabrics, corrugated card, and manufactured objects to explore their printing potential.

**Potato and vegetable printing** remains pedagogically valuable despite potential perceptions of being 'simplistic'. This technique introduces the crucial concept of reversed imagery and enables pupils to grasp cause-and-effect relationships between cutting and printing.

**Monoprinting** using ink rolled onto flat surfaces (such as perspex sheets, old CD cases, or laminated card) allows immediate experimentation with mark-making, drawing, and colour mixing. Pupils can draw into the ink or create subtractive marks, transferring unique images to paper through burnishing.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Teachers should establish basic printmaking vocabulary from Key Stage 1: 'relief', 'transfer', 'impression', 'print', 'reverse'. The physical action of printing – applying pressure to transfer – helps develop fine motor skills and hand-eye coordination whilst introducing scientific concepts of force and transfer.

Safety considerations at this stage are minimal but important: water-based inks should be used exclusively, and teachers should model safe use of rollers (brayers) to prevent trapping fingers. Printing stations work well in KS1, with different tables offering various found objects, stamp-making materials, or monoprinting opportunities.

## Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

### Curriculum Requirements and Development

The National Curriculum at Key Stage 2 requires pupils to 'improve their mastery of art and design techniques, including... printing'. This stage should see progression from exploratory printing toward controlled, multi-stage processes and introduction to specific printmaking traditions.

### Progressive Techniques

**Collagraph printing** enables pupils to build printing plates from card, textured materials, and found objects, creating relief surfaces that print through inked rollers or rubbing. This technique develops spatial planning and understanding of positive/negative space.

**Polystyrene/foam printing** provides an accessible form of relief printing where pupils incise designs into polystyrene sheets (such as clean foam food trays or purchased printing foam). This introduces the concept of editioning – making multiple consistent prints from a single matrix.

**Simple lino printing** can be introduced in upper Key Stage 2 using safety lino-cutting tools with guarded blades. Jeff Adams' research on risk and creativity in art education supports age-appropriate tool use that develops physical skills whilst requiring careful planning and technical thinking.

**Stencil printing** using cut paper or card templates introduces the concept of positive and negative space, and enables pupils to build up layered, multi-coloured images. This connects naturally to street art traditions and contemporary graphic design.

### Artist Engagement

Key Stage 2 is appropriate for studying William Morris's textile and wallpaper designs, which demonstrate repeat patterns and the relationship between natural observation and stylisation. Andy Warhol's screenprints introduce concepts of popular culture, repetition with variation, and the multiple as artwork. Henri Matisse's linocuts demonstrate how established artists adapted printmaking for distinctive aesthetic purposes.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Teachers should introduce proper printmaking terminology: 'matrix', 'edition', 'registration', 'relief', 'incise', 'impression'. Understanding that printmaking has specialised vocabulary develops pupils' cultural capital and enables specific discussions about technique and intention.

Workspace organisation becomes crucial at this stage. Designated 'wet' areas for inking, 'dry' areas for paper preparation, and 'drying' systems (washing lines, drying racks, or flat storage) prevent smudging and enable systematic working. Colour-mixing with printing inks introduces different properties compared to paint – the viscosity, transparency/opacity, and layering possibilities require experimentation and develop pupils' material knowledge.

Assessment should focus on planning (through sketchbooks showing design development), technical competence (quality of cutting, inking and printing), and evaluation (comparing outcomes with intentions). Leslie Cunliffe's assessment frameworks emphasise that pupils should articulate their technical choices and problem-solving strategies.

## Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

### Curriculum Requirements and Depth

At Key Stage 3, pupils should 'use a range of techniques and media, including painting' – with printing representing a major technique requiring extended study. This stage should introduce historical and contemporary contexts, technical refinement, and conceptual approaches to printmaking.

### Advanced Techniques and Processes

**Reduction lino printing** introduces multi-colour printing from a single block, requiring careful planning and understanding of layering sequence. This technique develops sophisticated colour theory understanding and requires pupils to visualise the accumulating image through successive printing stages.

**Screen printing** using simple paper stencils or drawn stencils with screen filler enables high-quality, professional-looking results. John Steers advocates for techniques that connect school practice with professional art and design contexts, and screen printing achieves this whilst remaining manageable in school settings.

**Drypoint and collagraph intaglio printing** can be introduced using acetate sheets or card plates, with printing achieved through hand-burnishing rather than requiring printing presses. These techniques introduce surface incision rather than relief, expanding pupils' understanding of printmaking principles.

**Photographic printmaking processes** including cyanotype (sun printing) and photogram techniques connect historical processes with contemporary photography. These light-sensitive processes demonstrate chemical reactions and documentation methods whilst creating distinctive aesthetic qualities.

### Conceptual Development

Rachel Mason's research on artistic thinking emphasises that Key Stage 3 should move beyond technical acquisition toward conceptual engagement. Printmaking supports this through exploration of:

- **Multiples and reproduction**: investigating how meaning changes through repetition, seriality, and mass production
- **Appropriation and remix culture**: using found imagery, photocopying, transfers, and collage within print processes
- **Identity and representation**: using portraiture, self-representation, and cultural symbols through print media
- **Text and image relationships**: incorporating typography, concrete poetry, and visual-verbal communication

### Artist and Cultural Contexts

Pupils should study diverse printmaking traditions: Japanese woodblock prints (ukiyo-e), examining the cultural context and collaborative workshop practices; contemporary British printmakers including Grayson Perry's etchings, Chris Ofili's prints, and Anish Kapoor's screenprints; political and protest printmaking from Käthe Kollwitz to contemporary activist graphics; Pop Art screenprints analysing consumerism and popular culture.

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Equipment investment at Key Stage 3 should prioritise safety cutters, adequate rollers (brayers) of various sizes, screen printing frames (which can be constructed using embroidery hoops or simple wooden frames with mesh fabric), and proper printing inks. While traditional printing presses enhance outcomes, hand-burnishing with baren, wooden spoons, or printing barens remains effective and develops understanding of pressure and transfer.

Sketchbook work should document the printmaking process through photographs, test prints, colour trials, and reflective annotation. This develops pupils' understanding of process as integral to artistic practice, not merely preparation for a final outcome.

Cross-curricular connections enhance contextual understanding: history curriculum links through propaganda posters, wartime printing, and historical documentation; English through illustrated texts, book arts, and poetry printing; science through understanding of chemical processes in photographic printing and material properties.

## Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16): GCSE

### Examination Board Requirements

All major UK examination boards include printmaking within their GCSE Art & Design specifications:

**AQA Art & Design (8201/8206)** requires students to work in areas of study including fine art, graphic communication, textile design, three-dimensional design, or photography, with printmaking applicable across all areas. Assessment Objective 2 specifically requires students to 'explore and select appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes'.

**Edexcel Art & Design (1AD0)** similarly positions printmaking as a potential specialism within Component 1 (Personal Portfolio, 60%) and Component 2 (Externally Set Assignment, 40%). The specification emphasises 'skilful and appropriate use of techniques and processes'.

**OCR Art & Design (J170)** includes printmaking within 'Fine Art' or 'Graphic Communication' pathways, requiring students to demonstrate 'assured, appropriate and skilful control of a wide range of skills, techniques and processes'.

**WJEC Art & Design (C670QS)** requires exploration of 'formal elements, techniques, materials and processes' with printmaking representing a valid focus for sustained investigation.

### Technical Expectations and Sophistication

GCSE-level printmaking should demonstrate technical control, sustained investigation, and integration with other media. Students typically focus on one or two printmaking techniques in depth rather than superficial coverage of multiple processes.

**Relief printing** at this level includes sophisticated reduction printing with multiple colours, experimental approaches combining relief with collage or mixed media, and integration with digital design through photopolymer plates or laser-cut matrices.

**Screen printing** extends to photographic stencils using light-sensitive emulsions, multi-layer prints with careful registration, and integration with digital imagery. Students should demonstrate understanding of colour transparency, layering sequences, and editioning consistency.

**Intaglio processes** including etching (where facilities permit) or sophisticated drypoint and collagraph work demonstrate understanding of line quality, tonal range, and printing variables. Even without acid etching facilities, students can create complex intaglio prints through card plates, textured surfaces, and careful inking techniques.

**Digital printmaking** appropriately appears at GCSE as students integrate photography, digital manipulation, and traditional processes. Laser printing, inkjet printing, or photocopying become deliberate artistic choices when students articulate aesthetic intentions and integrate these with hand-worked elements.

### Artist References and Critical Understanding

John Steers emphasises that practical work must connect with critical and contextual understanding. GCSE students should analyse printmakers' techniques, cultural contexts, and conceptual intentions. Relevant artists span historical and contemporary practice:

- **Albrecht Dürer's** woodcuts and engravings for understanding technical precision and religious/symbolic content
- **Kara Walker's** silkscreened silhouettes addressing race, history, and representation
- **David Hockney's** recent iPad printouts challenging definitions of printmaking
- **Paula Rego's** etchings exploring narrative and psychological complexity

### Practical Implications for Teachers

Teachers must balance technical teaching with conceptual development. Jeff Adams' research on creativity warns against over-emphasis on technical compliance at the expense of personal expression. GCSE printmaking should emerge from genuine investigation rather than imposed technique exercises.

Portfolio development requires photographic documentation of all stages: matrix development, test prints, colour trials, editioning decisions, and final outcomes. This documentation serves assessment purposes whilst developing students' metacognitive understanding.

Mock examinations for Component 2 should replicate actual exam conditions, with students accessing pre-prepared materials (pre-cut lino, pre-coated screens, prepared papers and inks) to simulate the 10-hour examination period constraints.

## Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18): A Level

### Advanced Study Requirements

A Level specifications position printmaking as a sophisticated medium for personal investigation and artistic development:

**AQA A Level Art & Design (7201/7206)** requires sustained investigation in Component 1 (Personal Investigation, 60%) and Component 2 (Externally Set Assignment, 40%), with printmaking supporting conceptual exploration and technical refinement.

**Edexcel A Level Art & Design (9AD0)** emphasises 'technical competence, creativity and expression' with printmaking enabling sophisticated responses across fine art, graphic communication, or mixed media pathways.

**OCR A Level Art & Design (H600)** requires 'highly developed and sophisticated technical skills' with printmaking demonstrating 'fluent and creative experimentation'.

**WJEC A Level Art & Design (A650QS)** explicitly values 'refining, developing and extending expertise in selected materials, processes and techniques' with printmaking offering substantial scope for advanced practice.

### Advanced Technical Possibilities

A Level students should achieve professional-level technical control whilst developing distinctive personal approaches. Advanced techniques include:

**Multi-plate etching and aquatint** (where facilities exist), demonstrating understanding of tonal range, colour separation, and printing registration. Safety considerations with acid etching require proper ventilation, equipment, and supervision.

**Large-scale screen printing** with photographic stencils, exploring layering, transparency, and precise registration across extended editions. Integration with digital photography and manipulation extends creative possibilities.

**Japanese woodblock (moku hanga) techniques** using water-based pigments and multiple blocks, demonstrating engagement with non-Western traditions and achieving subtle colour gradations.

**Experimental and hybrid processes** combining traditional printmaking with digital technologies, installation contexts, or performative elements. Contemporary printmaking often challenges traditional definitions, as Rachel Mason's research on boundary-crossing in art practice demonstrates.

### Conceptual Sophistication and Critical Context

A Level students must articulate sophisticated understanding of printmaking's cultural significance. The Personal Investigation essay (1000-3000 words for AQA) requires sustained critical analysis connecting personal practice with broader contexts.

Contemporary debates particularly relevant to A Level printmaking study include:

- **Authenticity and reproduction**: Walter Benjamin's concepts of mechanical reproduction and aura remain relevant to understanding printmaking's cultural position
- **Democratic art and accessibility**: printmaking's historical role in political communication, propaganda, and popular culture
- **Craft and digital technologies**: tensions and syntheses between traditional hand processes and digital reproduction
- **Sustainability and materials**: contemporary interest in non-toxic printmaking processes, plant-based dyes, and environmental responsibility

Relevant contemporary printmakers for A Level study include:

- **Kiki Smith** for exploration of body, nature, and feminist perspectives through multiple print media
- **Kehinde Wiley** for appropriation, identity politics, and decorative traditions
- **El Anatsui** whose print-related exploration of pattern, recycling, and cultural hybridity connects to post-colonial discourse
- **Julie Mehretu** for architectural scale, layering, and mapping concepts in printmaking

### Practical Implications for Teachers

A Level teaching requires sophisticated workshop management, including proper ventilation for solvent-based processes, adequate drying and storage systems, and quality materials enabling professional results. Leslie Cunliffe's research on assessment emphasises that A Level requires 'consistent and purposeful refinement' visible through systematic exploration and editioning.

Teachers should facilitate independent research through gallery visits, artist lectures, and professional printmaking workshops where accessible. Organisations like the Royal Academy, Tate galleries, and regional printmaking studios offer education programmes supporting A Level study.

Technical troubleshooting becomes crucial: understanding why prints fail (poor registration, inadequate pressure, incorrect ink consistency, paper moisture) and systematically addressing these issues develops sophisticated problem-solving. Sketchbooks should document technical experiments with analytical annotation explaining variables tested and outcomes achieved.

Printmaking progression across key stages should reflect increasing technical control, conceptual sophistication, and cultural understanding. Eileen Adams' research on progression in art education identifies key indicators:
