# Drawing Skills and Progression

## Introduction

Drawing forms the bedrock of Art & Design education in the UK, serving as both a discrete skill and a fundamental mode of visual thinking and communication. The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2013) emphasises that pupils should be taught to "develop their techniques, including their control and their use of materials, with creativity, experimentation and an increasing awareness of different kinds of art, craft and design." Drawing sits at the heart of this progression, providing the primary means through which pupils record observations, explore ideas, and develop visual literacy.

Eileen Adams has long argued that drawing is not merely a technical skill but "a form of thinking made visible," a perspective that has profoundly influenced contemporary approaches to art education in the UK. This understanding positions drawing as both a perceptual and conceptual activity, requiring careful pedagogical scaffolding throughout a pupil's educational journey.

## The Nature and Purpose of Drawing

### Drawing as Visual Research

John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), emphasises that drawing encompasses a broad spectrum of activities: observational drawing, imaginative drawing, analytical drawing, and drawing as a means of recording and communicating ideas. This multiplicity of purposes must inform how we teach drawing across all key stages.

Rachel Mason's research into international art education practices highlights that successful drawing curricula balance skill acquisition with personal expression, avoiding overly prescriptive approaches that can stifle creativity whilst ensuring pupils develop genuine competence and confidence.

### The Role of Sketchbooks

The development of sustained drawing practice through sketchbooks represents a critical aspect of progression. From Key Stage 2 onwards, sketchbooks serve as:

- Visual diaries documenting artistic development
- Spaces for experimentation without fear of failure
- Records of personal investigation and research
- Evidence of progression for assessment purposes

Leslie Cunliffe's work on sketchbook practice emphasises the importance of teaching pupils to view their sketchbooks as active thinking tools rather than repositories for finished work.

## Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At this foundation stage, drawing focuses on developing mark-making confidence and basic observational skills. The National Curriculum states that pupils should "use drawing, painting and sculpture to develop and share their ideas, experiences and imagination."

**Expected progression includes:**

- Controlling a variety of drawing implements (pencils, crayons, charcoal, pastels)
- Making marks with increasing control and deliberation
- Drawing from observation, memory and imagination
- Recognising and describing simple visual elements (lines, shapes, patterns)
- Developing hand-eye coordination and fine motor control
- Beginning to look carefully at objects, people and the environment

**Practical implications for teachers:**

Provide diverse drawing materials and surfaces to develop tactile awareness. Encourage exploratory mark-making without premature emphasis on "correctness." Create meaningful contexts for drawing linked to topics and first-hand experiences. Value process over product at this stage.

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Drawing skills become more refined and intentional. Pupils develop observational accuracy whilst maintaining creative freedom. The National Curriculum requires pupils to "improve their mastery of art and design techniques, including drawing, painting and sculpture with a range of materials."

**Expected progression includes:**

- Developing observational drawing skills with increasing accuracy
- Understanding and applying basic principles of proportion and scale
- Exploring tone through shading, hatching and cross-hatching
- Using viewfinders and frames to compose drawings
- Drawing from primary sources (objects, people, places)
- Understanding foreground, middle ground and background
- Making preparatory sketches and studies
- Responding to the work of artists, discussing drawing techniques observed
- Beginning to evaluate and refine their own drawing work

**Practical implications for teachers:**

Introduce structured observational drawing exercises whilst maintaining opportunities for imaginative work. Teach specific techniques explicitly (e.g., demonstrating how to create tonal gradation). Use high-quality reproductions of artists' drawings to analyse technique. Develop sustained projects that require multiple drawing approaches.

Eileen Adams advocates for drawing experiences rooted in the local environment, arguing that drawing from direct observation of familiar places develops both technical skills and deeper connection to one's surroundings.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Drawing becomes increasingly sophisticated, analytical and purposeful. Pupils develop personal drawing styles whilst expanding their technical repertoire. The National Curriculum expects pupils to "use a range of techniques and media, including painting" and to "increase their proficiency in the handling of different materials."

**Expected progression includes:**

- Demonstrating confident control of line, tone and mark-making
- Understanding and applying perspective (one-point and two-point)
- Exploring scale, making drawings larger and smaller than observed subjects
- Working from diverse sources including photographs, digital images and imagination
- Combining media (mixed media drawing)
- Developing sustained observational studies
- Using drawing for different purposes (recording, exploring, planning, presenting)
- Analysing artists' drawing approaches and applying insights to own practice
- Demonstrating understanding of figure proportions and gesture
- Creating drawings that communicate ideas and emotions
- Beginning to develop personal visual language and style

**Practical implications for teachers:**

Provide structured technical teaching alongside open-ended creative projects. Introduce life drawing with appropriate sensitivity. Use digital drawing tools alongside traditional media. Encourage critical reflection through annotation and evaluation. Connect drawing skills to wider design and communication contexts.

Jeff Adams' research on digital technologies in art education reminds us that contemporary drawing practice must encompass digital drawing tools such as graphics tablets and drawing applications, which offer distinct but complementary approaches to traditional media.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16 - GCSE)

At GCSE level, drawing competencies are assessed across all major exam boards as part of Assessment Objective 3: "Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions as work progresses."

**AQA, Edexcel, OCR and WJEC specifications all require:**

- Skilful use of drawing to record observations from primary and secondary sources
- Refinement of ideas through drawing
- Experimental and exploratory drawing approaches
- Technical competence in selected media
- Purposeful drawing directly relevant to personal intentions
- Evidence of sustained investigation through drawing

**Expected progression includes:**

- Sophisticated observational accuracy when required
- Purposeful selection of drawing media and techniques
- Drawing as integral to personal creative process
- Analytical drawing that investigates visual properties
- Experimental mark-making and media exploration
- Drawing that communicates complex ideas and concepts
- Understanding drawing within historical and contemporary contexts
- Development of distinctive personal approach

**Practical implications for teachers:**

Drawing must be taught as purposeful activity supporting personal investigations rather than as isolated skill exercises. Encourage diverse drawing approaches reflecting individual intentions. Model professional artists' research processes. Provide regular whole-class technical skill-building alongside individual project work. Ensure pupils understand how drawing connects to Assessment Objectives.

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18 - A Level)

A Level specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) place drawing within sophisticated, contextually informed practice. Drawing is assessed as part of AO3: "Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions, reflecting critically on work and progress."

**Expected progression includes:**

- Drawing as critical investigation tool
- Sophisticated integration of drawing with contextual understanding
- Drawing that challenges conventions and explores boundaries
- Technically accomplished and conceptually driven drawing
- Drawing as thinking process made visible
- Critical reflection on own drawing development
- Drawing practice informed by contemporary and historical precedents

**Practical implications for teachers:**

Support pupils in developing truly personal approaches to drawing. Encourage risk-taking and experimental practice. Connect drawing to critical studies work. Facilitate independent research into artists' drawing methodologies. Challenge pupils to question what constitutes drawing. Provide extension opportunities through workshops with practising artists.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Direct Teaching of Techniques

John Steers argues for explicit technical instruction, rejecting the notion that drawing skills emerge naturally without intervention. Effective teaching includes:

- Demonstration and modelling of techniques
- Structured skill-building exercises
- Clear success criteria and exemplars
- Progressive introduction of materials and approaches
- Regular whole-class technical sessions

### Responsive Teaching

Drawing development requires formative assessment and responsive intervention. Effective practitioners:

- Observe pupils working and provide in-the-moment guidance
- Ask questions that prompt closer observation
- Demonstrate techniques individually or in small groups as needs arise
- Provide differentiated support based on pupils' developmental stage
- Balance encouragement with constructive technical guidance

### Creating a Drawing Culture

Leslie Cunliffe emphasises the importance of establishing classroom cultures where drawing is valued and practised regularly. This includes:

- Daily drawing activities and warm-ups
- High-quality displays of pupils' drawing work
- Access to diverse drawing materials
- Regular sketchbook reviews and sharing
- Celebration of diverse drawing approaches and styles

## Assessment of Drawing Progression

### Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment should focus on:

- Observational accuracy (where appropriate to task)
- Control and confidence in mark-making
- Appropriate selection of media and techniques
- Willingness to experiment and take risks
- Development of ideas through drawing
- Evidence of closer looking and deeper investigation
- Application of taught techniques
- Progression visible in sketchbooks and portfolios

### Summative Assessment

At GCSE and A Level, drawing contributes to assessment primarily through AO3, though drawing competence supports all assessment objectives. Exam boards provide guidance on assessment, typically looking for:

- Technical competence appropriate to intentions
- Purposeful recording that supports development
- Evidence of investigation and refinement
- Quality and depth of engagement
- Critical reflection on drawing practice

## Common Challenges and Misconceptions

### "I Can't Draw" Syndrome

Many pupils develop fixed mindsets about drawing ability. Rachel Mason's research emphasises the importance of teaching drawing as learnable skill rather than innate talent. Effective strategies include:

- Teaching explicit techniques that demonstrate improvement
- Showing diversity of drawing styles and approaches
- Celebrating effort and progress over natural ability
- Providing successful experiences for all pupils
- Using appropriate scaffolding and differentiation

### Overemphasis on Accuracy

Eileen Adams warns against narrow conceptions of drawing focused solely on realistic representation. Teachers should:

- Value diverse drawing purposes (expressive, analytical, communicative, exploratory)
- Celebrate personal style and interpretation
- Teach that accuracy is one possible intention among many
- Introduce abstract and expressionistic drawing approaches
- Connect drawing to conceptual thinking and idea development

### Neglect of Drawing in Secondary Settings

John Steers has expressed concern that drawing sometimes receives insufficient attention in secondary art education, with pupils rushing to final pieces without adequate preparatory drawing work. Teachers should:

- Allocate dedicated time for drawing skill development
- Require substantial sketchbook investigation
- Resist pressure to produce finished pieces prematurely
- Model drawing as ongoing practice not just preparation
- Assess and value drawing work distinctly within projects

## Resources and Materials Progression

### Primary Phase

Begin with accessible materials that build confidence: wax crayons, oil pastels, soft pencils (4B-6B), felt tips, charcoal, chalk pastels. Progress to finer implements requiring greater control: graphite pencils (range of grades), coloured pencils, fine-liner pens, brush pens.

### Secondary Phase

Expand media range to include: compressed charcoal, willow charcoal, graphite sticks, conté crayons, ink (Indian ink, drawing inks), technical pens, water-soluble pencils and crayons, digital drawing tools and tablets, mixed media combinations.

Introduce varied surfaces: different paper weights and textures, toned and coloured papers, cartridge paper, sugar paper, newsprint, specialist papers for different media.

## Contemporary Developments

### Digital Drawing

Jeff Adams' research highlights the necessity of incorporating digital drawing into contemporary curricula. This includes:

- Drawing applications on tablets and computers
- Graphics tablets and styluses
- Digital manipulation of hand-drawn work
- Understanding relationships between traditional and digital approaches
- Exploring unique possibilities of digital mark-making

### Cross-Curricular Drawing

Eileen Adams advocates for drawing across the curriculum, recognising its value in science (observational drawing of specimens), geography (field sketches), design technology (technical drawing, design development), and mathematics (geometric drawing, visualisation).

### Inclusive Practice

Contemporary best practice ensures drawing opportunities are accessible to all pupils, including those with SEND. Adaptations might include:

- Alternative drawing implements (grip aids, chunky materials)
- Digital tools with accessibility features
- Varied scale of working
- Collaborative drawing activities
- Voice-activated or eye-tracking drawing technologies
- Multi-sensory approaches to observational work

## Conclusion

Drawing skills develop progressively when teaching is informed by clear understanding of developmental stages, purposeful pedagogy, and broad conceptions of what drawing encompasses. As John Steers articulates, drawing remains "the most direct route from the mind to the page," deserving sustained, thoughtful attention throughout pupils' art education. By combining explicit technical teaching with opportunities for personal exploration and expression, teachers can ensure all pupils develop both competence and confidence in this fundamental aspect of visual literacy.
