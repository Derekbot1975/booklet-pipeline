# A Level Art & Design Specifications

## Overview of A Level Art & Design in the UK

A Level Art & Design qualifications represent the culmination of secondary art education in England, Wales and Northern Ireland, typically studied by students aged 16-18. These specifications were substantially reformed in 2015-2016 to align with government reforms emphasising greater rigour, linear assessment and increased focus on critical written engagement. As John Steers (2014) argued in his critique of the reforms published in the *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, whilst the changes aimed to strengthen analytical thinking, concerns remained about the reduction in coursework weightings and potential narrowing of practical experimentation.

The current A Level specifications are offered by four main examination boards: AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR and WJEC Eduqas. All must comply with Ofqual's GCE Subject Level Conditions and Requirements for Art & Design (2016), ensuring consistency in assessment objectives, content requirements and grading standards across England.

## Specification Structure and Assessment Components

### Common Framework Across Exam Boards

All A Level Art & Design specifications consist of two components, assessed entirely through controlled assessment and moderated portfolios:

**Component 1: Personal Investigation** (60% of total A Level)
- Coursework portfolio demonstrating sustained project development
- Practical work supported by a written element (1,000-3,000 words)
- Internally marked, externally moderated
- No time restrictions, developed over approximately 15-18 months

**Component 2: Externally Set Assignment** (40% of total A Level)
- Response to exam board-set stimulus paper
- Preparatory studies followed by 15-hour supervised examination
- Internally marked, externally moderated

This structure reflects what Rachel Mason (2018) described as the "authentic art practice model" where sustained investigation and development mirror professional creative processes, though the written requirement represents a significant shift from pre-2015 specifications.

### Assessment Objectives (AOs)

The four Assessment Objectives are standardised across all exam boards by Ofqual and apply equally to both components:

**AO1: Develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations** informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and critical understanding (25%)

**AO2: Explore and select appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes**, reviewing and refining ideas as work develops (25%)

**AO3: Record ideas, observations and insights** relevant to intentions, reflecting critically on work and progress (25%)

**AO4: Present a personal and meaningful response** that realises intentions and, where appropriate, makes connections between visual and other elements (25%)

As Leslie Cunliffe (2017) noted in *Teaching Secondary Art*, the equal weighting across AOs represents a significant philosophical shift, requiring teachers to ensure balanced curriculum coverage rather than privileging making skills over critical understanding or vice versa.

## Subject Endorsements (Titles)

The Ofqual framework specifies seven distinct endorsements within Art & Design, each with specific content requirements. Students must study one endorsement at A Level:

### 1. Art, Craft and Design

The most popular endorsement (approximately 65% of entries), encompassing broad exploration across:
- Fine Art (drawing, painting, printmaking, sculpture)
- Graphic Communication
- Three-dimensional Design
- Textile Design
- Photography and lens-based media

Students must demonstrate engagement with both historical and contemporary practice, though not necessarily restricted to a single discipline. Eileen Adams (2003) championed this breadth in *Power of Art*, arguing that artificial boundaries between fine art and applied design limit creative thinking.

### 2. Fine Art

Focus on drawing, painting, printmaking, sculpture and installation, with emphasis on:
- Traditional and contemporary fine art practices
- Personal visual language development
- Conceptual and aesthetic engagement with materials
- Critical understanding of art movements and practitioners

### 3. Graphic Communication

Encompasses:
- Typography and layout
- Illustration
- Advertising and branding
- Packaging design
- Digital and print-based graphics

AQA and Pearson particularly emphasise industry-relevant contexts and contemporary professional practice.

### 4. Textile Design

Includes:
- Constructed textiles
- Printed and dyed textiles
- Fashion design and illustration
- Surface pattern and decoration
- Textile art and installation

### 5. Three-Dimensional Design

Covers:
- Product design
- Architectural design and models
- Sculpture and installation
- Theatre, film and television design
- Environmental/landscape design

### 6. Photography

Specific focus on lens-based and light-based media:
- Portraiture and documentary
- Photojournalism
- Experimental imagery
- Installation and moving image
- Digital and analogue processes

OCR's Photography endorsement notably requires engagement with historical photographic processes, reflecting debates about digital versus traditional practice highlighted by Jeff Adams (2008).

### 7. Textile Art

Differentiated from Textile Design through emphasis on:
- Expressive and experimental textile manipulation
- Textile installation and sculpture
- Mixed-media approaches
- Conceptual textile practice

WJEC Eduqas particularly distinguishes between design-oriented and art-oriented textile work within their specification structure.

## The Personal Investigation Component

### Practical Portfolio Requirements

The Personal Investigation allows students to develop a self-directed project demonstrating sustained creative development. Typical expectations include:

- **Primary source engagement**: Direct observation, photography, experimental studies
- **Contextual studies**: Analysis of artists, craftspeople, designers informing personal direction
- **Process documentation**: Development of techniques, materials exploration, refinement
- **Final outcome(s)**: Resolved piece(s) demonstrating realisation of intentions

John Steers (2021) emphasises in NSEAD guidance that portfolios should evidence "iterative development" rather than linear progression, reflecting authentic creative practice where ideas are tested, rejected and refined cyclically.

### The Written Component

The 1,000-3,000 word written element represents the most significant change from pre-2015 specifications. Requirements include:

- **Continuous prose** (not annotations or extended captions)
- **Critical analysis** of own work and contextual sources
- **Bibliography** with Harvard or equivalent referencing
- **Integration** with practical work, not standalone essay

Exam boards differ in guidance:
- **AQA** recommends 1,000-1,500 words, emphasising personal voice and critical reflection
- **Pearson Edexcel** suggests 2,000-3,000 words with more academic structure
- **OCR** requires minimum 1,000 words with emphasis on analysis over description
- **WJEC Eduqas** specifies 1,000-3,000 words with Welsh language option

Rachel Mason (2019) critiques this requirement in *Art Education*, arguing that whilst developing academic literacy is valuable, the word count parameters potentially disadvantage students with specific learning differences and may privilege articulate writing over visual intelligence. Many departments now provide structured scaffolding and explicit teaching of critical writing, moving away from assumptions that students will naturally develop these skills through practice alone.

### Assessment Approaches

Internal marking requires holistic professional judgement across all four AOs. Moderation processes involve:
- Centre-assessed marks submitted by specified deadline (typically early May)
- Sample selection by exam board moderators
- Potential adjustment of centre marks to align with national standards
- Final marks and grades released in August

Best practice, as outlined by Cunliffe (2018), involves:
- Regular internal standardisation meetings
- Mock moderation exercises with other centres
- Comparative judgement approaches using exemplar materials
- Clear assessment documentation and justifications

## The Externally Set Assignment (ESA)

### Stimulus Paper Release and Preparation Period

The ESA paper is released on 1st February each year, providing students with:
- Multiple starting points (typically 5-10 options per endorsement)
- Open-ended themes encouraging diverse interpretations
- Images, questions or statements as stimuli

The preparatory period (February to late May/early June) allows approximately 12-14 weeks for:
- Research and contextual investigation
- Materials testing and technique development
- Compositional planning and preparatory studies
- Refinement of final piece intentions

Students may generate substantial preparatory portfolios, though assessment focuses on demonstration of all four AOs across preparation and supervised time combined.

### The 15-Hour Examination

The supervised examination requires:
- **Continuous 15 hours** (typically three 5-hour sessions over consecutive days)
- **Production of final outcome(s)** under examination conditions
- **Access to preparatory work** for reference during examination
- **No written component** required during supervised time

Eileen Adams (2016) noted this compressed timeframe contrasts with professional creative practice, questioning whether authentic assessment can occur under such constraints. However, examination boards justify it as demonstrating working discipline and decision-making under pressure.

### Centre Administration

Centres must ensure:
- Appropriate supervision and security of examination materials
- Controlled access to facilities and resources
- Accurate timing and break allowances following JCQ regulations
- Authentication of student work through appropriate supervision

## Exam Board Specification Variations

### AQA Art & Design (7201, 7202)

AQA holds approximately 50% market share. Distinctive features include:

- **Specification code 7201**: covers all seven endorsements
- **Specification code 7202**: separate code for Fine Art endorsement
- **Supportive materials**: extensive exemplar work with examiner commentaries
- **"Ideas and intentions" approach**: emphasis on conceptual clarity in assessment guidance
- **Teacher community**: active online forums and regional network meetings

AQA's approach, influenced by former Chief Examiner input, emphasises accessible language and supportive interpretation of requirements, though Steers (2017) suggests this occasionally leads to inconsistent standards between centres.

### Pearson Edexcel Art & Design (9AD0)

Pearson's specification is characterised by:

- **More structured guidance**: detailed content descriptors for each endorsement
- **Professional practice emphasis**: particularly strong in Graphic Communication and 3D Design
- **Extended written requirement**: recommendations towards upper word count limit
- **Digital portfolio option**: acceptance of digitally presented work with careful authentication

Approximately 30% market share, Edexcel specifications appeal to centres prioritising academic rigour and professional vocational contexts.

### OCR Art & Design (H600, H601, H602, H603, H604, H605, H606)

OCR provides separate specification codes for each endorsement:

- **H600**: Art, Craft and Design
- **H601**: Fine Art
- **H602**: Graphic Communication
- **H603**: Photography
- **H604**: Textile Design
- **H605**: Three-dimensional Design
- **H606**: Critical and Contextual Studies (withdrawn 2018)

OCR's distinctive features include:
- **Separate specifications**: clearer demarcation between endorsements
- **Technical terminology**: more subject-specific language in assessment guidance
- **Historical process emphasis**: particularly in Photography endorsement
- **Detailed grade descriptors**: explicit performance characteristics at each grade boundary

Market share approximately 15%, with particular strength in Photography.

### WJEC Eduqas Art & Design (A670QS)

The Welsh examination board offers:

- **Bilingual provision**: Welsh and English language assessment options
- **Textile Art distinction**: clearest differentiation from Textile Design
- **Cultural context flexibility**: accommodation of Welsh artistic traditions
- **Supportive specification**: accessible guidance with practical examples

Market share approximately 5% (primarily in Wales), though increasingly chosen by English centres for clarity of guidance and perceived fair moderation standards.

## Teaching the Personal Investigation

### Planning and Pacing

Effective Personal Investigation delivery requires careful curriculum mapping. Best practice models suggested by Leslie Cunliffe (2020) include:

**Year 12 (Lower Sixth):**
- September-December: Skills consolidation and contextual investigations
- January-March: Initial project proposals and exploratory studies
- April-July: Focused development of chosen theme/direction

**Year 13 (Upper Sixth):**
- September-November: Intensive practical development and written drafting
- December-January: Final outcome realisation and written completion
- February-May: ESA preparation and completion

This timeline allows approximately 18 months for Component 1, though Jeff Adams (2010) warns against over-extending projects, which may lead to creative exhaustion and diminishing returns.

### Developing the Written Component

Teaching academic writing in Art & Design requires explicit instruction, challenging historical assumptions about tacit knowledge development. Effective pedagogical strategies include:

**Structured Writing Frameworks:**
- **Comparative analysis paragraphs**: comparing own work with contextual sources
- **Reflective practice models**: Schön's "reflection-in-action" applied to creative development
- **Critical theory introduction**: accessible engagement with concepts like semiotics, postmodernism, identity

**Differentiated Support:**
- Sentence starters and paragraph templates for developing writers
- Annotated exemplars demonstrating effective critical voice
- Peer assessment using assessment objectives as success criteria
- Regular drafting and feedback cycles

Rachel Mason (2020) emphasises that written work should emerge from practical exploration rather than preceding it, maintaining primacy of visual investigation whilst developing complementary analytical skills.

### Contextual Studies Integration

Effective contextual investigation moves beyond superficial artist research toward genuine influence on practical development. John Steers (2019) advocates for:

- **Primary source engagement**: gallery visits, practitioner workshops, archive research
- **Active analysis**: formal analysis, comparative studies, semiotic investigation
- **Creative response**: experiments in styles/techniques of studied practitioners
- **Critical positioning**: articulation of how own work relates to/diverges from contextual sources

This approach contrasts with outdated "artist reference page" models, instead treating contextual understanding as genuine dialogue informing creative direction.

## Assessment Challenges and Standardisation

### Inter-Centre Reliability

Standardisation remains a significant challenge in A Level Art & Design assessment. Research by Ofqual (2018) identified:

- **Greater mark variation** between centres in Art & Design than most other subjects
- **Particular inconsistency** in AO1 (development of ideas) assessment
- **Written component uncertainty**: varying standards in what constitutes 1,000-3,000 "words"
- **Final outcome emphasis**: tendency to over-weight presentation at expense of process

Exam boards have responded with:
- **Enhanced exemplar materials**: annotated work samples at multiple grade levels
- **Standardisation recordings**: video explanations of moderator decision-making
- **Network meetings**: opportunities for comparative judgement with other centres
- **Pre-moderation advice services**: optional submission for feedback before final assessment

### Addressing Assessment Bias

Eileen Adams (2017) highlights potential assessment biases requiring conscious mitigation:

**Medium Bias:** Traditional painting/drawing skills may be unconsciously privileged over digital, installation or time-based work. Moderators now receive specific training in assessing diverse contemporary practices.

**Presentation Bias:** Highly resolved, gallery-standard presentation may influence judgement disproportionately. Assessment criteria emphasise process evidence alongside final outcomes.

**Cultural Bias:** Western fine art traditions historically dominated exemplar materials. Recent specifications explicitly require engagement with diverse cultural contexts and non-European artistic traditions.

**Accessibility:** Students with disabilities must receive appropriate access arrangements whilst maintaining assessment standards. Practical accommodations might include assistive technology, additional time, or alternative presentation formats.

## Progression Routes and Destination Data

A Level Art & Design facilitates diverse progression pathways:

### Higher Education

- **Art Foundation Diploma**: selective entry to specialist art schools (UAL, RCA, GSA, etc.)
- **Direct degree entry**: BA Fine Art, Graphic Design, Fashion, Architecture
- **Related disciplines**: Design Engineering, Art History, Museum Studies
- **Portfolio requirements**: universities typically require substantial practical portfolios regardless of A Level results

### Employment and Apprenticeships

- Creative industries (estimated 2.1 million jobs, DCMS 2021)
- Advertising and marketing
- Film, television and digital media production
- Heritage and cultural sectors
- Design consultancies and in-house creative teams

### Transferable Skills

John Steers and Jeff Adams (2021) emphasise that A Level Art & Design develops capabilities valued across sectors:

- **Creative problem-solving**: lateral thinking and innovative approaches
- **Self-directed learning**: independent research and project management
- **Visual literacy**: critical reading of images and designed environments
- **Technical versatility**: adaptability to new tools and processes
- **Critical analysis**: evaluative thinking and reflective practice

Data from UCAS (2022) indicates approximately 23,000 students achieve A Level Art & Design annually, with average A*-B rates around 55%, though substantial variation exists between centres and endorsements.

## Current Debates and Future Directions

### Digital Technologies Integration

The specifications remain largely medium-agnostic, allowing digital work but not requiring it. Jeff Adams (2019) argues this risks producing graduates unfamiliar with industry-standard software and contemporary digital practices. Counter-arguments emphasise foundational thinking skills transferable across technologies.
