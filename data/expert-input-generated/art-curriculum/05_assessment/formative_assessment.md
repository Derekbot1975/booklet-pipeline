# Formative Assessment in Art

## Introduction

Formative assessment in art and design represents a fundamental shift from traditional, predominantly summative approaches towards assessment as an integral part of learning. Within the UK context, formative assessment—often termed 'assessment for learning'—enables teachers to gather evidence of students' artistic development, inform pedagogical decisions, and support learners in understanding their creative progress. As John Steers has consistently argued, effective assessment in art education must recognise the discipline's distinctive epistemology, where knowledge is constructed through making, visual thinking, and iterative processes rather than solely through written or verbal responses.

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2014) emphasises that pupils should develop their creativity and ideas, become proficient in drawing, painting, sculpture and other art, craft and design techniques, and evaluate and analyse creative works using the language of art. Formative assessment provides the mechanism through which teachers can monitor and support progress in these interconnected domains.

## Theoretical Foundations

### The Nature of Formative Assessment in Art

Eileen Adams has been instrumental in articulating how formative assessment in art differs from other curriculum areas. Her work emphasises that artistic learning is non-linear, often tacit, and emerges through embodied practice. Adams argues that formative assessment must capture the thinking that occurs through making—the dialogue between artist, materials, and ideas that characterises creative practice.

Rachel Mason's research highlights that formative assessment in art education serves multiple purposes: diagnostic (identifying what students know and can do), educational (advancing learning through feedback), and developmental (supporting artistic identity formation). Mason distinguishes between assessment *of* learning (summative), assessment *for* learning (formative), and assessment *as* learning (where students self-assess and regulate their own creative development).

Leslie Cunliffe's work on progression in art education provides frameworks for understanding how formative assessment can identify development in visual language, technical skills, conceptual understanding, and critical engagement. Cunliffe emphasises that progression in art is domain-specific and often manifests differently across various aspects of artistic practice.

## Key Principles for Formative Assessment in Art

### 1. Learning Intentions and Success Criteria

Effective formative assessment begins with clear learning intentions that are shared with students. In art education, these must balance specificity with creative openness. For example, a Key Stage 3 project might have the learning intention: "Students will explore how artists use tone to create form and atmosphere." Success criteria should be co-constructed with students where appropriate, recognising that in art, multiple routes to success exist.

**Practical implications:**
- Display learning intentions visually, incorporating exemplar images
- Differentiate between technical objectives (e.g., demonstrating tonal gradation) and conceptual objectives (e.g., expressing mood through tone)
- Avoid overly prescriptive criteria that constrain creative exploration
- Revisit and refine success criteria as projects develop

### 2. Questioning and Dialogue

John Steers emphasises the central role of dialogue in formative assessment in art. Questioning should provoke artistic thinking, encourage reflection, and make visible the often-tacit knowledge embedded in creative practice. Effective questions include:

- **Process questions:** "What are you trying to achieve here?" "Why did you choose these colours?"
- **Comparative questions:** "How does your composition differ from the artist we studied?"
- **Evaluative questions:** "Which section works best? Why?"
- **Speculative questions:** "What would happen if you...?"

Jeff Adams' research on digital art education highlights how formative dialogue must adapt to contemporary practices, including peer critique in virtual learning environments and collaborative feedback on digital portfolios.

### 3. Feedback that Advances Learning

Research by Black and Wiliam (1998), widely applied in UK education, demonstrates that feedback improves learning when it:
- Focuses on the task, process, and self-regulation rather than personal attributes
- Provides specific guidance on improvement
- Is timely and actionable
- Encourages student response and revision

In art education, effective feedback addresses multiple dimensions:

**Technical dimension:** "Your observational drawing shows good proportion. To develop further, notice how the tone transitions gradually on the curved surfaces."

**Conceptual dimension:** "Your composition effectively conveys isolation through negative space. Consider how scale might further emphasise this theme."

**Contextual dimension:** "Research how Käthe Kollwitz used expressive line to convey emotion—this connects to what you're exploring."

Eileen Adams advocates for feedback that respects the student's creative agency whilst providing clear direction. Feedback should prompt artistic decision-making rather than prescribe solutions.

## Formative Assessment Strategies Across Key Stages

### Key Stage 1 and 2 (Ages 5-11)

At primary level, formative assessment should cultivate confidence, curiosity, and foundational skills. The National Curriculum requires pupils to develop a wide range of art and design techniques, learn about great artists, and produce creative work.

**Effective strategies include:**

- **Visual journals:** Regular sketchbook work that captures experimentation, with teacher annotations highlighting successful elements
- **Learning walls:** Display work-in-progress with annotations showing development
- **Verbal feedback during making:** Real-time guidance whilst children are engaged in creative activity
- **Peer discussion:** Structured opportunities for children to share work and respond positively to each other's ideas
- **Digital documentation:** Photographs of 3D work, processes, and collaborative projects that enable review and reflection

**Key Stage 2 progression:** Formative assessment should track development in control and experimentation with materials, understanding of artistic techniques, ability to discuss and evaluate art, and growing independence in creative decision-making.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum for Key Stage 3 requires students to develop their creativity and ideas through increasing fluency in visual language, become proficient in techniques, and analyse art from different periods and cultures.

**Formative assessment strategies:**

- **Developmental sketchbooks:** Students maintain sketchbooks that show research, experimentation, refinement, and reflection. Teachers provide written and verbal feedback that identifies strengths and specific next steps
- **Critique sessions:** Structured peer and teacher critique using protocols that ensure constructive, specific feedback
- **Progress reviews:** Regular one-to-one or small group discussions reviewing development against learning objectives
- **Self-assessment frameworks:** Students use criteria to evaluate their own work, identifying areas for development
- **Interim deadlines:** Breaking projects into phases with formative checkpoints enables intervention and redirection

Rachel Mason's research emphasises that at this stage, formative assessment should develop students' metacognitive awareness—their understanding of themselves as creative learners.

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE, Ages 14-16)

GCSE Art & Design specifications from AQA, Edexcel (Pearson), OCR, and WJEC Eduqas all incorporate formative assessment within their structures, though summative portfolio assessment ultimately determines grades.

**Assessment Objectives across exam boards:**
- AO1: Develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations
- AO2: Refine work through experimentation and analysis
- AO3: Record ideas and observations in visual and written forms
- AO4: Present a personal and meaningful response

**Formative assessment approaches:**

- **Portfolio development tracking:** Regular review of portfolio against assessment objectives, identifying strengths and areas requiring development
- **Mock assessments:** Practice examinations with detailed feedback enable students to understand requirements and refine approaches
- **Annotation workshops:** Teaching students to critically annotate their own work, with formative feedback on the quality of analysis
- **Target-setting:** Specific, actionable targets related to assessment objectives (e.g., "Develop AO2 by creating three experimental variations exploring different colour palettes")
- **Comparative analysis:** Students compare their work against exemplar materials provided by exam boards, identifying differences and improvement strategies

John Steers has critiqued the potential tension between formative approaches and GCSE assessment structures, arguing that excessive focus on assessment objectives can constrain genuine creative development. Effective formative assessment at GCSE maintains focus on artistic learning whilst building assessment literacy.

### Key Stage 5 (A-Level, Ages 16-18)

A-Level specifications maintain similar assessment objectives to GCSE but require greater independence, conceptual sophistication, and sustained investigation. OCR, AQA, Edexcel, and WJEC specifications all emphasise the personal study component alongside practical work.

**Formative assessment strategies:**

- **Tutorial model:** One-to-one discussions that probe conceptual thinking, contextual understanding, and creative direction
- **Presentation and critique:** Students present work-in-progress to peers and teachers, defending choices and receiving critical feedback
- **Written component feedback:** Formative assessment of personal study/essay development, addressing analysis, critical understanding, and connections to practical work
- **Professional practices:** Exposure to artist critique models and professional standards
- **Action research:** Students document how feedback influences their creative development, evidencing responsiveness

Leslie Cunliffe's work emphasises that at this level, formative assessment should develop genuine artistic thinking—the capacity to work with uncertainty, make informed creative decisions, and articulate artistic intentions.

## Practical Tools and Approaches

### Sketchbooks and Visual Journals

Sketchbooks represent the primary site for formative assessment in art education. Rather than finished products, they should be working documents that capture thinking-through-making. Effective formative use of sketchbooks includes:

- **Developmental annotations:** Teacher comments that acknowledge effective elements and pose questions or suggestions for development
- **Student reflection prompts:** Questions that encourage metacognitive thinking ("What surprised you during this experiment?" "What will you try next?")
- **Visual dialogues:** Teachers respond with sketches, visual references, or technical demonstrations directly in sketchbooks
- **Formative grading alternatives:** Using coded symbols, stamps, or stickers rather than marks to indicate achievement and areas for development

Eileen Adams emphasises that sketchbooks should be personal spaces where risk-taking is encouraged. Formative feedback should reinforce experimentation and development rather than polished presentation.

### Assessment Conversations

Structured conversations during making are amongst the most powerful formative assessment tools. Jeff Adams' research demonstrates that when teachers engage students in dialogue about their creative decisions, artistic thinking becomes visible and can be guided.

**Effective conversation frameworks:**
1. **Observe:** Watch the student working before interrupting
2. **Open question:** "Tell me about what you're working on here"
3. **Listen:** Allow student to articulate their thinking
4. **Probe:** Ask specific questions about decisions, intentions, problems
5. **Suggest:** Offer specific strategies, techniques, or references
6. **Agree next steps:** Establish what the student will do next

Documentation of these conversations (brief notes, voice recordings) provides evidence of progress and informs future planning.

### Peer Assessment

When structured effectively, peer assessment develops critical literacy and self-regulation. Rachel Mason's work identifies that peer assessment in art must balance critical rigour with supportive community. Effective approaches include:

- **Critique protocols:** Structured formats ensuring descriptive, interpretive, evaluative, and suggestive feedback
- **Comparative analysis:** Students identify specific techniques or approaches in peers' work that succeed
- **Gallery walks:** Work displayed anonymously or openly, with structured feedback tasks
- **Peer mentoring:** Pairing students to provide ongoing mutual support and feedback

Teachers must model constructive critique and establish ground rules emphasising respect, specificity, and developmental intent.

### Digital Formative Assessment

Contemporary practice increasingly incorporates digital tools for formative assessment:

- **Digital portfolios:** Platforms like Google Classroom, Microsoft Teams, or specialist art portfolio systems enable ongoing feedback and documentation
- **Process documentation:** Students photograph work-in-progress, creating visual narratives of development that can be annotated and discussed
- **Screen capture for digital work:** Recording processes in digital art, photography, or graphic design enables analysis of creative decision-making
- **Collaborative annotation:** Tools enabling multiple participants to comment on work digitally

Jeff Adams' research on digital art education emphasises that formative assessment must adapt to the distinctive characteristics of digital media, including version control, non-destructive editing, and collaborative creation.

## Challenges and Considerations

### Subjectivity and Consistency

Art assessment inevitably involves interpretation and judgement. John Steers acknowledges this whilst arguing for rigorous professional dialogue to develop shared understanding. Strategies for managing subjectivity include:

- **Moderation:** Regular departmental discussion of student work against criteria
- **External reference:** Exam board exemplar materials, specialist subject association resources (NSEAD)
- **Explicit criteria:** Clear frameworks whilst acknowledging multiple routes to success
- **Professional development:** Ongoing engagement with assessment theory and practice

### Balancing Formative and Summative Functions

Leslie Cunliffe's research identifies tension between formative assessment's developmental purpose and accountability demands for grading and reporting. Teachers must:

- Separate feedback conversations from grading decisions where possible
- Use formative evidence to inform summative judgements rather than conflating the two
- Communicate clearly with students about when assessment is formative versus summative
- Resist pressure to grade all work, protecting space for risk-taking and experimentation

### Time and Workload

Effective formative assessment is time-intensive. Practical strategies for managing workload include:

- **Focus on quality over quantity:** Detailed feedback on selected pieces rather than superficial comments on everything
- **Real-time assessment:** Formative feedback during lessons reduces marking burden
- **Student self-assessment:** Developing students' capacity to evaluate their own work reduces teacher assessment load
- **Strategic intervention:** Targeting formative assessment at critical points in projects rather than continuously

### Cultural and Individual Differences

Rachel Mason's work emphasises that formative assessment must be culturally responsive and recognise diverse artistic traditions and values. Teachers should:

- Acknowledge that artistic quality is culturally constructed
- Value diverse aesthetic traditions and creative approaches
- Recognise that students' cultural backgrounds influence how they interpret and respond to feedback
- Avoid imposing narrow definitions of artistic success

## Links to the National Curriculum and Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2014) states that teaching should ensure pupils "develop their creativity and ideas" and "evaluate and analyse creative works using the language of art, craft and design." Formative assessment directly enables these outcomes by providing opportunities for reflection, refinement, and critical engagement.

While the National Curriculum is not prescriptive about assessment methods, it establishes that schools must provide information on pupil achievement at the end of Key Stages. Effective formative assessment provides the evidence base for summative reporting whilst prioritising learning development.

## Conclusion

Formative assessment in art education represents professional practice that honours the distinctive nature of artistic learning whilst providing rigorous support for development. As Eileen Adams argues, when done well, formative assessment makes visible the often-hidden processes of creative thinking, enabling teachers to guide students towards genuine artistic development rather than mere task completion.

The research of John Steers, Rachel Mason, Leslie Cunliffe, and Jeff Adams collectively demonstrates that formative assessment in art must be dialogic, situated in making, responsive to individual creative trajectories, and grounded in disciplinary understanding. It requires teachers to be simultaneously rigorous and flexible, critically engaged and supportive, technically knowledgeable and creatively open.

For UK art educators working across Key Stages and within examination frameworks, formative assessment provides the means to develop students' creative confidence, technical capability, critical understanding, and artistic identity—the authentic purposes of art education that transcend grades and specifications.
