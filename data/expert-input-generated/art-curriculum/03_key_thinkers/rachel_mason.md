# Rachel Mason

**Full Name:** Rachel Mason

**Current/Former Roles:** Professor Emeritus of Art Education; formerly Reader in Art and Design Education at Roehampton University, London

**Known For:** Pioneering work in multicultural art education, community arts practice, cross-cultural art pedagogy, and international perspectives in art education; extensive research on art education systems across different cultural contexts, particularly in East Asia and South Asia

---

## Biography and Career History

Rachel Mason established herself as one of the most internationally-minded art educators in the United Kingdom during the late 20th and early 21st centuries. Her academic career was characterised by a sustained commitment to understanding how cultural context shapes art education practice and theory.

Mason held the position of Reader in Art and Design Education at Roehampton University (formerly Roehampton Institute London), where she worked for several decades developing research programmes focused on comparative and international art education. Her work bridged theory and practice, consistently emphasising the importance of understanding diverse cultural perspectives in art teaching.

Throughout her career, Mason conducted extensive fieldwork in various countries, particularly in Asia. She developed significant research partnerships with institutions in Hong Kong, Taiwan, India, and other nations, examining how different cultural traditions influence approaches to art teaching and learning. This comparative methodology became a hallmark of her scholarship.

Mason's academic trajectory reflected the growing recognition within UK art education of the need for more inclusive, culturally responsive pedagogies. During the 1990s and 2000s, as the UK's student population became increasingly diverse, her work provided crucial theoretical frameworks for teachers seeking to develop more equitable and culturally relevant practice.

She served on numerous international committees and editorial boards, contributing to journals such as the *International Journal of Art & Design Education* and *Studies in Art Education*. Her influence extended well beyond the UK, with her work being widely cited in art education scholarship globally.

Mason's career also encompassed significant work in community arts, examining how participatory art practices could serve educational and social purposes. She researched community-based art projects across different cultural contexts, identifying principles that could inform formal educational settings.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Cross-Cultural Art Education

Mason's most significant contribution lies in her development of frameworks for understanding art education across cultural boundaries. In her seminal work, she argued that Western models of art education—particularly those emphasising individual creative expression and formalist aesthetics—cannot be universally applied without consideration of local cultural values and traditions.

Mason identified fundamental differences in how cultures conceptualise the purpose of art education. For instance, she contrasted Western emphasis on originality and self-expression with East Asian traditions that value technical mastery, respect for tradition, and the cultivation of character through artistic practice. She documented how these different philosophical foundations manifest in classroom practice, teacher training, and curriculum design.

Her cross-cultural research methodology involved extended periods of observation and collaboration with teachers and students in different countries. She advocated for what she termed 'cultural translation' in art education—the process of understanding educational practices within their own cultural logic rather than judging them against Western standards.

### 2. Multicultural Art Education in the UK Context

Mason made substantial contributions to debates about multicultural education in the UK. She distinguished between superficial multiculturalism (what she termed the 'saris, samosas and steel bands' approach) and more substantive engagement with diverse cultural perspectives on art and aesthetics.

Drawing on her international research, Mason argued that effective multicultural art education requires teachers to develop genuine understanding of different artistic traditions, their philosophical foundations, and their social functions. She critiqued tokenistic approaches that reduced diverse cultures to exotic objects of study rather than alternative frameworks for understanding art itself.

Mason emphasised that multicultural art education should not be conceived merely as teaching *about* art from different cultures, but rather as examining how different cultures conceptualise what art is, what it is for, and how it should be learned. This more radical approach challenged teachers to question their own cultural assumptions about creativity, aesthetics, and the purpose of art education.

She documented various models for implementing multicultural approaches in UK schools, identifying both successes and limitations. Her research highlighted the importance of teacher education in developing the cultural knowledge and pedagogical skills necessary for authentic multicultural practice.

### 3. Community Arts and Social Practice

Mason's research on community arts examined how participatory art practices function in educational and social contexts. She studied community art projects in diverse settings, from urban regeneration initiatives to rural development programmes, analysing their educational dimensions and social impacts.

She developed frameworks for understanding the distinctive pedagogical characteristics of community arts practice, contrasting them with formal art education. Community arts, in Mason's analysis, typically emphasise collaboration over individual expression, process over product, and social relevance over aesthetic autonomy. She explored how these principles might inform practice in formal educational settings.

Mason was particularly interested in how community arts projects in different cultural contexts reflected local values and priorities. Her comparative research revealed how community arts in various countries served different social functions—from preserving traditional culture to challenging social inequalities—and how these purposes shaped pedagogical approaches.

She argued for greater dialogue between formal art education and community arts practice, suggesting that each could learn from the other. Her work documented examples of successful integration, whilst also acknowledging the tensions between different institutional logics and value systems.

### 4. Art Education and Cultural Values

Throughout her scholarship, Mason examined the relationship between cultural values and art education practice. She argued that every art education system embodies particular cultural assumptions about childhood, learning, creativity, and the social function of art.

Mason identified how values shape apparently technical aspects of art teaching, from classroom organisation to assessment methods. For example, she documented how individualistic cultures tend to organise art classrooms to maximise personal workspace and expression, whilst more collectivist cultures might emphasise collaborative work and shared resources.

Her research explored how cultural values influence what is considered 'good' art teaching. Western progressive traditions, she noted, often value student autonomy, experimentation, and making 'mistakes' as part of learning. By contrast, some Asian traditions emphasise respectful observation of teacher demonstrations, careful copying of models, and developing technical proficiency before attempting innovation.

Mason advocated for cultural reflexivity in art education—the capacity to recognise one's own cultural assumptions and understand alternative perspectives. She developed activities and approaches for teacher education that fostered this awareness.

### 5. Visual Culture and Art Education

Mason contributed to debates about visual culture approaches in art education, bringing international perspectives to predominantly Western discussions. She examined how visual culture operates differently across cultural contexts and what implications this holds for curriculum and pedagogy.

She critiqued Western-centric assumptions in visual culture education, noting that concepts like 'critical media literacy' often presume particular relationships between images, power, and meaning that may not translate across cultural contexts. Her research documented how different cultures develop distinct visual literacies and pedagogical approaches for teaching about images.

Mason argued for a more globally-informed visual culture curriculum that acknowledged diverse traditions of image-making and interpretation. She provided examples of how teachers might incorporate non-Western perspectives on visual culture without falling into tokenism or cultural appropriation.

### 6. Teacher Education for Cultural Diversity

A significant strand of Mason's work addressed how teacher education might better prepare art teachers for culturally diverse classrooms. She argued that conventional UK teacher education often failed to adequately address the cultural dimensions of art teaching.

Mason advocated for teacher education that included substantial engagement with different cultural traditions of art and art education. She recommended that trainee teachers study art education systems in other countries, examine their own cultural assumptions, and develop skills for cultural translation and adaptation.

She developed specific approaches for teacher education, including comparative analysis of teaching episodes from different cultural contexts, collaborative work with teachers from diverse backgrounds, and sustained study of particular artistic traditions. Her research documented the challenges teachers faced in developing culturally responsive practice and identified factors that supported or hindered this development.

## Key Publications

### Books and Edited Volumes

**Mason, R. (1988).** *Art Education and Multiculturalism*. London: Croom Helm.

This early work established Mason's reputation in the field. The book examined theoretical and practical dimensions of multicultural art education in the UK context, critiquing superficial approaches and proposing more substantive engagement with cultural diversity. It became a standard reference in UK art education discourse during the 1990s.

**Mason, R. (Ed.). (1999).** *Sharing Practice: A Guide to Self-Evaluation for Artists, Arts Organisations and Funders Involved in Projects in the Criminal Justice System*. London: Unit for the Arts and Offenders.

This publication reflected Mason's interest in community arts and participatory practice. It provided practical frameworks for evaluating arts projects in challenging contexts, embodying her commitment to bridging theory and practice.

**Mason, R., & Holm, L. (Eds.). (2001).** *Art Education and Ethnicity*. Special issue of *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, 20(1).

This influential special issue brought together international scholars examining relationships between ethnicity, cultural identity, and art education. Mason's editorial contributions and her own chapter provided frameworks that shaped subsequent research in the field.

### Journal Articles and Book Chapters

**Mason, R. (1995).** "Art Education and Multiculturalism in a Global Context." In R. Prentice (Ed.), *Teaching Art and Design: Addressing Issues and Identifying Directions* (pp. 141-156). London: Cassell.

This chapter situated UK debates about multicultural art education within broader international contexts, drawing on Mason's comparative research. It challenged parochial assumptions and advocated for more globally-informed perspectives.

**Mason, R. (2000).** "The Art of Translation: A Prerequisite for Effective Arts Education Policies in the Developing World." In J. Baldacchino (Ed.), *Revisioning Arts Education* (pp. 85-98). Hong Kong: Hong Kong Arts Development Council.

Mason developed her concept of 'cultural translation' in this chapter, examining challenges of adapting educational policies across cultural contexts. The work drew on her research in Asia and had significant influence on international educational development programmes.

**Mason, R., & Steers, J. (2006).** "The Impact of Formal Assessment Procedures on Teaching and Learning in Art and Design in Secondary Schools." *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, 25(2), 119-133.

This empirical study examined how assessment systems shape art teaching practice, comparing approaches across different contexts. It provided evidence for policy debates about assessment in UK art education.

**Mason, R. (2007).** "Towards Cultural Democracy in Art Education: What Can We Learn from Taiwan and Elsewhere?" *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, 26(2), 177-187.

This article examined art education reforms in Taiwan, extracting lessons for UK practice. Mason analysed how democratic ideals manifest differently across cultural contexts and what this means for curriculum development.

**Mason, R., & Iwano, M. (2004).** "Art Education in Cultural Context: A Japanese Model." *Research in Art Education*, 5(1), 1-20.

This detailed case study explored Japanese approaches to art education, examining philosophical foundations and classroom practices. Mason's analysis revealed alternative conceptualisations of creativity, learning, and the purposes of art education.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Mason's work influenced curriculum development and policy discourse in several significant ways, though her impact was more evident in academic circles and teacher education than in direct policy formation.

Her frameworks for understanding multicultural art education informed National Curriculum reviews during the 1990s and early 2000s. Whilst the curriculum documents did not directly cite her work, her ideas circulated through curriculum working groups and consultation processes. Her critique of tokenistic multiculturalism contributed to more sophisticated approaches in curriculum guidance.

Mason's international research provided evidence for debates about cultural diversity in the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD). She contributed to NSEAD publications and conferences, influencing how the professional community understood issues of cultural difference and equity.

Her work on assessment, particularly the comparative research examining how different systems approach evaluation of student work, informed discussions about assessment reform. Mason's research revealed how cultural values shape what is considered 'good' work and how achievement is measured, challenging assumptions in UK assessment practice.

In teacher education, Mason's influence was more direct. Her publications became standard reading on Initial Teacher Education (ITE) programmes across the UK. Many ITE providers incorporated her frameworks for understanding cultural diversity into their curricula. Her emphasis on cultural reflexivity influenced how art education tutors approached issues of diversity with trainees.

Mason's international work contributed to the UK's soft power and educational influence. She represented UK art education in international forums, including UNESCO consultations and international research collaborations. Her work demonstrated UK engagement with global perspectives, countering perceptions of insularity.

Through her editorial work with the *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, Mason shaped what issues gained attention in art education scholarship. She championed international voices and cross-cultural research, broadening the field's geographical and cultural scope.

## Pedagogical Approach

Mason's pedagogical approach, as evidenced in her teaching and her recommendations for art education practice, embodied several distinctive principles.

**Comparative and Critical Analysis:** Mason consistently employed comparative methodologies, encouraging students and teachers to examine practices from multiple cultural contexts. She believed that understanding differences helped reveal the cultural assumptions underlying familiar practices. Her teaching involved analysing teaching episodes, curriculum documents, and student work from diverse contexts to develop this comparative consciousness.

**Experiential Learning from Cultural Traditions:** Rather than merely reading about different cultural approaches to art education, Mason advocated direct engagement with artistic practices from various traditions. This might involve learning traditional techniques, working with artists from different backgrounds, or participating in community arts projects. She believed embodied experience was essential for genuine understanding.

**Reflexive Practice:** Mason emphasised the importance of teachers examining their own cultural assumptions and values. Her pedagogical approach included activities that made familiar practices strange, helping participants recognise that Western progressive traditions in art education are not universal truths but cultural constructions. This reflexivity was considered prerequisite for culturally responsive teaching.

**Dialogue Across Difference:** Mason fostered dialogue between people from different cultural backgrounds and educational traditions. She structured learning experiences that brought together diverse perspectives, facilitating respectful exchange rather than attempting to synthesise differences into a single 'correct' approach. This dialogical method respected plurality whilst enabling mutual learning.

**Connecting Theory and Practice:** Throughout her work, Mason insisted on grounding theoretical discussions in concrete practice. She used detailed case studies, teaching episodes, and examples from actual classrooms to illustrate abstract concepts. Conversely, she encouraged practitioners to theorise their own practice, recognising teachers as knowledge producers.

**Long-term, Sustained Engagement:** Mason's research methodology—involving extended periods of immersion in different cultural contexts—implied a pedagogical principle. She believed genuine understanding of cultural difference required sustained engagement rather than brief encounters. This principle challenged conventional approaches to multicultural education that involved short units on different cultures.

**Critical Multiculturalism:** Mason's pedagogical approach embodied what she termed critical multiculturalism. Rather than celebrating diversity uncritically, she encouraged examination of power relationships, the politics of representation, and questions of cultural appropriation. Her teaching addressed difficult questions about who has the right to teach what cultural content and how to do so respectfully.

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

Rachel Mason's contributions remain highly relevant to contemporary art education in the UK and internationally. As classrooms become increasingly diverse and digital technologies facilitate global connections, her frameworks for understanding cultural difference and practising cultural translation have enduring value.

Her critique of superficial multiculturalism resonates with current concerns about decolonising the curriculum. Mason's insistence on substantive engagement with diverse perspectives rather than tokenistic inclusion anticipates contemporary decolonial approaches. Her work provides historical context for current debates and offers methodologies for addressing them.

The comparative dimension of Mason's work offers valuable perspectives on current policy debates. As educational policymakers look internationally for evidence and inspiration, her careful analyses of how cultural context shapes what educational practices mean and how they function provides essential cautionary guidance.

Mason's emphasis on community arts and participatory practice connects with contemporary interest in socially engaged art and relational aesthetics. Her research on how community arts function pedagogically remains relevant for teachers seeking to incorporate such approaches in formal educational settings.

For teacher education, Mason's frameworks for developing cultural reflexivity and competence address pressing challenges. As ITE providers work to prepare teachers for diverse classrooms, her pedagogical approaches and her research on teacher learning offer valuable resources.

Mason's international scholarship contributes to ongoing efforts to globalise art education discourse. Her work demonstrated that productive international dialogue requires more than simply including voices from different countries; it demands developing capacities for cultural translation and genuine engagement with alternative frameworks.
