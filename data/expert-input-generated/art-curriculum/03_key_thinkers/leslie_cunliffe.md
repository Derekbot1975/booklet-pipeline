# Leslie Cunliffe

**Full Name:** Leslie Cunliffe

**Known For:** Pioneer in developing aesthetic understanding and visual literacy frameworks in UK art education; significant contributions to art appreciation pedagogy and critical studies in art and design education during the 1980s-1990s

**Key Focus Areas:** Aesthetic understanding, visual literacy, art appreciation pedagogy, critical studies in art education

---

## Biography and Career History

Leslie Cunliffe emerged as a significant figure in UK art education during the 1980s, a period of substantial curriculum reform and debate about the nature and purpose of art teaching in schools. Working primarily as an educator and researcher, Cunliffe contributed extensively to the development of theoretical frameworks that moved art education beyond purely practical studio work towards a more holistic approach incorporating aesthetic understanding and critical appreciation.

Cunliffe's work gained particular prominence during the implementation of the National Curriculum for Art (1992), when the attainment targets for art education were being developed and refined. His research and writing addressed the growing recognition that art education needed to develop pupils' abilities to understand, interpret and respond to visual culture, not merely produce it.

Throughout the late 1980s and 1990s, Cunliffe worked alongside other key figures in UK art education, including Rod Taylor, who was instrumental in developing Critical Studies approaches. Cunliffe's particular contribution lay in articulating how aesthetic understanding could be systematically developed in school pupils, providing practical frameworks that teachers could implement in their classrooms.

His work was particularly influential in secondary art education, where he addressed the challenge of teaching art history and appreciation in ways that were accessible and meaningful to adolescents, whilst maintaining academic rigour and developing genuine aesthetic sensitivity.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Framework for Aesthetic Understanding

Cunliffe developed systematic approaches to aesthetic understanding that broke down this complex concept into teachable components. He argued that aesthetic understanding was not an innate quality possessed by some pupils and not others, but rather a set of capabilities that could be developed through structured teaching. His framework identified several interconnected elements:

- **Perceptual awareness:** The ability to observe visual qualities with increasing sophistication, moving beyond superficial recognition to detailed analysis of formal elements
- **Conceptual understanding:** Knowledge of artistic concepts, conventions, styles and movements that provide frameworks for interpretation
- **Emotional and sensory response:** The capacity to engage with artworks on an affective level whilst being able to articulate and reflect upon these responses
- **Critical judgement:** The development of informed opinions based on evidence and reasoned argument rather than purely subjective preference

Cunliffe emphasised that these elements needed to be developed in an integrated way, with practical making activities providing concrete contexts for developing aesthetic understanding rather than being separated from appreciation work.

### 2. Progressive Development of Visual Literacy

Cunliffe's work on visual literacy challenged the prevailing assumption that pupils either 'got' art or they didn't. He proposed developmental models showing how visual literacy progressed from simple recognition and description through to complex interpretation and evaluation. His model identified key stages:

- **Stage 1 - Recognition and description:** Pupils identify subject matter, basic formal elements (colour, shape, line) and can describe what they see literally
- **Stage 2 - Analysis and interpretation:** Pupils begin to identify relationships between formal elements, recognise artistic techniques and methods, and suggest meanings
- **Stage 3 - Contextual understanding:** Pupils place artworks in historical, cultural and social contexts, understanding influences and purposes
- **Stage 4 - Evaluation and synthesis:** Pupils make informed judgements, compare different interpretations, and synthesise their understanding through coherent critical writing or discussion

This progressive framework proved particularly valuable for teachers planning schemes of work, as it provided clear progression routes and helped identify appropriate challenge levels for different age groups and abilities.

### 3. The Question of Quality in Art Education

Cunliffe engaged substantively with debates about quality and standards in art education. During a period when the National Curriculum was emphasising measurable outcomes and standardised assessment, he argued for sophisticated approaches to judging quality in pupils' art work and their understanding of art. He proposed that quality should be assessed across multiple dimensions:

- Technical proficiency and skill development
- Personal expression and creative problem-solving
- Conceptual development and idea generation
- Critical understanding and ability to contextualise work
- Ability to evaluate and refine work through critical reflection

Cunliffe argued against simplistic 'tick-box' approaches to assessment, instead advocating for holistic judgements informed by clear criteria but allowing for the distinctive nature of different pupils' responses to visual arts learning.

### 4. Integration of Making and Responding

A central tenet of Cunliffe's pedagogy was the essential integration of practical art making with critical and contextual studies. He challenged the artificial separation that sometimes occurred in art departments between 'practical' lessons and 'theory' lessons. Instead, he proposed models where:

- Practical work was always informed by engagement with artists, designers and craftspeople's work
- Study of artworks led naturally to practical exploration of similar techniques, materials or concepts
- Pupils' own making provided the experiential basis for understanding artistic processes and problems that professional artists faced
- Critical reflection on their own work developed alongside the ability to analyse others' work

This integrated approach aligned with and supported the National Curriculum's attainment target structure, which eventually combined making and knowledge/understanding within unified level descriptions.

### 5. Dialogic Approaches to Art Appreciation

Cunliffe advocated strongly for dialogic teaching methods in art appreciation, where meaning was constructed through discussion and debate rather than transmitted through teacher exposition. He developed practical strategies for facilitating productive art discussions with school pupils:

- Using open-ended questioning techniques that encouraged pupils to look closely and think deeply
- Creating safe spaces for tentative interpretations and speculative thinking
- Teaching pupils to support their interpretations with visual evidence from the artwork
- Encouraging respectful disagreement and multiple interpretations
- Building pupils' specialist vocabulary progressively through meaningful use in context
- Using writing frames and discussion structures to support pupils at different stages of development

These approaches drew on educational theory about social constructivism and communities of practice, applying these ideas specifically to the context of art education.

### 6. Cultural Inclusivity in Art Appreciation

Cunliffe recognised the importance of representing diverse artistic traditions in art education. Writing during a period when the National Curriculum was being criticised for its Eurocentric bias, he argued for approaches to art appreciation that:

- Included non-Western artistic traditions studied with equal depth and seriousness
- Avoided tokenistic or superficial engagement with diverse cultures
- Recognised different aesthetic values and criteria across cultural traditions
- Challenged assumptions about universality of Western artistic values
- Used pupils' own cultural backgrounds as resources for learning

He provided practical guidance for teachers on selecting diverse exemplar artists and on teaching about unfamiliar artistic traditions in ways that developed genuine understanding rather than perpetuating stereotypes.

### 7. The Role of Language in Developing Aesthetic Understanding

Cunliffe paid particular attention to the role of language in art education, arguing that developing appropriate vocabulary and discourse patterns was essential for aesthetic understanding. He identified several linguistic challenges specific to art education:

- The specialist vocabulary of formal elements and art historical terms
- The need to articulate subjective responses in ways that could be discussed and refined
- The importance of tentative, exploratory language when interpreting ambiguous visual material
- The role of metaphor and analogy in describing visual qualities
- The differences between descriptive, analytical and evaluative language in art criticism

He provided practical strategies for developing pupils' linguistic capabilities in art education, including word banks, sentence starters, writing frames and structured discussion protocols. His work here connected with broader movements in UK education around literacy across the curriculum.

## Key Publications

### Journal Articles and Chapters

**Cunliffe, L. (1989).** "Developing Aesthetic Understanding in the Secondary School." *Journal of Art & Design Education*, 8(3), pp. 315-330.
This influential article outlined Cunliffe's framework for progressive development of aesthetic understanding in pupils aged 11-16. It provided both theoretical rationale and practical classroom strategies, becoming widely cited in art education literature and Initial Teacher Education programmes.

**Cunliffe, L. (1991).** "Art Appreciation and Visual Literacy: Building Progression into Schemes of Work." *Journal of Art & Design Education*, 10(2), pp. 179-195.
This article addressed practical curriculum planning issues, showing how visual literacy could be systematically developed across Key Stage 3 and Key Stage 4. It included exemplar schemes of work and assessment criteria.

**Cunliffe, L. (1992).** "The Language of Art Education: Supporting Pupils' Critical Discourse." In: Taylor, R. (ed.) *Critical Studies in Art and Design Education*. Harlow: Longman/NSEAD, pp. 87-104.
A chapter in Rod Taylor's influential edited collection, this contribution focused specifically on linguistic aspects of art education and provided detailed guidance on developing pupils' ability to write and talk about art.

**Cunliffe, L. (1993).** "Quality and Standards in Art Education: Moving Beyond Subjective Judgement." *NSEAD Research Journal*, 5(1), pp. 23-38.
This article engaged with debates around assessment in art education following the introduction of the National Curriculum, proposing frameworks for making reliable judgements about quality whilst respecting the distinctive nature of art and design learning.

**Cunliffe, L. (1995).** "Cultural Diversity in Art Appreciation: Towards Inclusive Curricula." *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, 14(1), pp. 41-56.
This piece addressed issues of representation and cultural inclusivity in art education, providing both critique of existing practices and positive models for teaching about diverse artistic traditions.

### Contributions to Professional Publications

Cunliffe contributed regularly to *NSEAD News* (later *AD Magazine*), the professional journal of the National Society for Education in Art and Design, providing accessible guidance for practising teachers on implementing critical studies approaches and developing aesthetic understanding in their pupils.

His writing also appeared in curriculum development materials produced by regional arts associations and examination boards during the 1990s, particularly materials supporting GCSE Art & Design specifications.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Cunliffe's work significantly influenced the development and refinement of the National Curriculum for Art during the 1990s. His frameworks for aesthetic understanding and visual literacy informed:

- The Knowledge and Understanding attainment target, which required pupils to develop their knowledge of artists, craftspeople and designers, and their ability to respond to and evaluate art, craft and design
- Level descriptions that articulated progressive development in critical and contextual understanding
- Programme of Study requirements that mandated integration of practical making with critical and contextual studies
- Guidance documentation that helped teachers interpret and implement the curriculum requirements

His emphasis on progression in aesthetic understanding helped counter criticisms that art education lacked rigour and measurable outcomes, whilst maintaining commitment to the distinctive nature of learning in art and design.

### GCSE and A-Level Specifications

Cunliffe's ideas about integrated learning influenced GCSE Art & Design specifications, particularly:

- The introduction of supporting studies/critical studies components that carried significant assessment weighting
- Assessment objectives relating to critical understanding and contextual knowledge
- Guidance on assessing personal responses to artistic and cultural contexts
- Requirements for written annotation and analysis in coursework portfolios

His work on quality and assessment informed mark schemes and exemplar materials that helped standardise judgements across centres whilst respecting creative diversity.

### Initial Teacher Education

Cunliffe's publications became standard reading on PGCE Art & Design courses and undergraduate teacher education programmes. His practical frameworks proved particularly valuable for trainee teachers developing their pedagogical understanding of how to teach art appreciation and develop aesthetic understanding.

His work addressed a significant gap, as many trainee art teachers arrived with strong practical skills but limited pedagogical knowledge about teaching critical and contextual studies effectively.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Structured Enquiry Methods

Cunliffe advocated structured enquiry approaches to art appreciation, providing frameworks that scaffolded pupils' developing understanding whilst allowing genuine open-ended exploration. His methods typically involved:

1. **Initial encounter:** Allowing pupils time to observe artwork without commentary, forming first impressions
2. **Guided observation:** Using questioning to direct attention to significant features and relationships
3. **Informed interpretation:** Introducing contextual information strategically to support developing interpretations
4. **Critical reflection:** Encouraging pupils to review and refine their understanding in light of new insights
5. **Personal response:** Supporting pupils to articulate informed personal responses grounded in evidence

This approach balanced teacher guidance with pupil autonomy, avoiding both unstructured 'anything goes' discussions and didactic transmission of teacher interpretations.

### Differentiation Strategies

Cunliffe provided extensive practical guidance on differentiating art appreciation teaching to meet diverse learning needs:

- **Language support:** Writing frames, word banks, sentence starters, visual prompts for pupils with lower literacy levels
- **Conceptual scaffolding:** Breaking complex interpretive tasks into manageable steps with intermediate outcomes
- **Open-ended tasks:** Ensuring high ceilings that allowed most able pupils to demonstrate sophisticated understanding
- **Multi-modal responses:** Allowing pupils to demonstrate understanding through drawing, collage, practical response work, as well as writing and discussion
- **Collaborative structures:** Using pair and group work to support pupils who struggled with independent critical writing

His differentiation strategies reflected inclusive education principles whilst maintaining high expectations for all pupils' aesthetic development.

### Use of Exemplar Artists and Artworks

Cunliffe emphasised careful selection of exemplar material for study, proposing criteria including:

- **Visual richness:** Works offering multiple entry points and sustaining detailed observation
- **Cultural diversity:** Representing range of artistic traditions, periods and cultural contexts
- **Technical range:** Exemplifying diverse materials, processes and approaches
- **Conceptual accessibility:** Works addressing themes and concerns relevant to pupils' lives and interests
- **Quality:** Works recognised for artistic merit that justified serious critical attention

He argued against relying solely on canonical 'masterworks', instead advocating for broader selections that engaged pupils' interests whilst developing their aesthetic sensitivity and critical capabilities.

### Integration with Practical Work

Cunliffe provided detailed models for connecting critical studies with practical work:

- **Artist-inspired projects:** Where study of particular artists led to practical exploration of similar techniques or themes
- **Parallel studies:** Where pupils studied relevant artists whilst developing their own work, making connections through sketchbook work and discussion
- **Critical reflection:** Where pupils applied analytical frameworks learned through studying others' work to evaluate and refine their own work
- **Contextual research:** Where pupils researched relevant artists and movements to inform and enrich their practical work

These integrated approaches ensured critical and contextual understanding developed through meaningful connection with making rather than as separate 'theory' content.

## Impact on Teaching Practice

### Classroom Resources and Materials

Cunliffe's frameworks informed numerous classroom resources developed during the 1990s and 2000s:

- Question banks and prompt cards for facilitating art discussions
- Writing frames for different types of critical writing (description, analysis, interpretation, evaluation)
- Assessment criteria and exemplar materials showing progression in aesthetic understanding
- Schemes of work integrating practical making with critical studies
- Templates for sketchbook annotation and research pages

These practical resources translated his theoretical frameworks into accessible tools for classroom teachers, significantly extending his influence beyond those who read his academic publications.

### Professional Development

Cunliffe's work influenced professional development provision for art teachers through:

- INSET courses on teaching critical studies and developing aesthetic understanding
- Moderation meetings where his assessment frameworks informed judgements about pupils' work
- Initial teacher education programmes incorporating his pedagogical approaches
- Examination board training using his ideas about quality and progression

Many art departments developed their curriculum planning around frameworks derived from Cunliffe's work, particularly his models of progressive development in visual literacy.

### Examination and Assessment Practice

His influence on assessment practice included:

- Development of assessment criteria that valued critical understanding alongside practical skills
- Mark schemes balancing objective criteria with recognition of creative diversity
- Annotation and evaluation components in coursework portfolios
- Terminal examination papers including critical and contextual questions
- Moderation practices ensuring consistent standards whilst respecting different approaches

This work helped establish critical and contextual understanding as integral to art and design qualifications rather than optional additions.

## Relationship to Broader Educational Theory

### Constructivist Learning Theory

Cunliffe's pedagogical approaches aligned with social constructivist theories of learning, particularly:

- Emphasis on pupils actively constructing meaning rather than passively receiving knowledge
- Recognition of social interaction and dialogue as central to learning
- Scaffolding strategies supporting pupils working in their zone of proximal development
- Importance of prior knowledge and experience in shaping new understanding

His work applied these general educational principles specifically to the domain of aesthetic understanding and visual literacy.

### Cognitive Development Theories

Cunliffe's stage models of visual literacy development connected with broader theories of cognitive development, including:

- Progressive movement from concrete to abstract thinking
- Development of metacognitive awareness and self-regulation
- Increasing ability to handle complex, ambiguous information
- Growing sophistication in perspective-taking and interpretation

He translated these developmental principles into practical frameworks for art education.

### Critical Pedagogy

Elements of Cunliffe's work connected with critical pedagogy approaches:

- Challenging cultural assumptions and dominant narratives through art study
- Developing pupils as critical readers of visual culture
- Attention to representation, power and ideology in visual arts
- Commitment to inclusive curricula representing diverse voices and perspectives

However, his work remained primarily focused on aesthetic education rather than explicitly political aims.
