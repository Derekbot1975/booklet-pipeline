# John Steers

**Full Name:** John Steers

**Current and Former Roles:** Former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), Editor of the *International Journal of Art & Design Education* (iJADE), Visiting Professor at Roehampton University

**Known For:** Advocacy for art and design education, international art education research, curriculum policy analysis, defending the creative subjects in UK schools, critiquing government education policy, and championing teacher autonomy

---

## Biography and Career History

John Steers is one of the most influential figures in UK art and design education over the past three decades. His career has been characterised by unwavering advocacy for the subject, rigorous policy analysis, and international scholarship that has shaped discourse around creativity in schools.

Steers served as General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD) from 1981 to 2011, a period of thirty years during which he transformed the organisation into a powerful voice for art and design teachers. Under his leadership, NSEAD became the principal professional association for art educators in the UK, representing teachers across all phases of education and providing crucial professional development, resources, and policy guidance.

Throughout his tenure at NSEAD, Steers was a vocal critic of successive government policies that marginalised arts subjects. He consistently challenged accountability measures such as the English Baccalaureate (EBacc), which omitted creative subjects and led to dramatic declines in arts teaching time and examination entries. His evidence-based critiques appeared regularly in professional journals, parliamentary submissions, and media commentary.

Since 1985, Steers has served as General Editor of the *International Journal of Art & Design Education* (iJADE), published by Wiley-Blackwell in association with NSEAD. Under his editorship, iJADE has become the leading international peer-reviewed journal in the field, publishing research from scholars worldwide and maintaining rigorous academic standards whilst remaining accessible to practitioners.

Following his retirement from NSEAD in 2011, Steers continued his work as a Visiting Professor at Roehampton University, where he has contributed to teacher education programmes and research in art and design pedagogy. He remains an active researcher, writer, and speaker, regularly contributing to debates about curriculum reform, assessment, and the place of creativity in education.

Steers has also been involved in international art education through organisations such as the International Society for Education through Art (InSEA), contributing to global conversations about visual literacy, cultural education, and the role of the arts in developing creative and critical thinking.

## Key Ideas

### 1. The Marginalisation of Creative Subjects Through Accountability Measures

Steers has extensively documented and critiqued how performance measures and accountability frameworks have systematically marginalised art and design in English schools. His analysis of the English Baccalaureate (EBacc), introduced in 2010, demonstrated how this performance measure—which excluded all creative subjects—led to dramatic reductions in arts teaching. Steers argued that by creating a hierarchy of subjects through accountability, the government fundamentally undermined the comprehensive curriculum it claimed to support.

His research showed that between 2010 and 2017, GCSE entries in art and design subjects fell significantly, particularly in schools serving disadvantaged communities. Steers documented how headteachers, under pressure to improve EBacc performance, reduced curriculum time for creative subjects, closed departments, and made art teachers redundant. He characterised this as a form of "policy by stealth," where government claimed not to be prescribing curriculum whilst creating conditions that forced schools to narrow their offerings.

Steers' critique extended beyond the EBacc to encompass Progress 8, Attainment 8, and other performance measures that effectively devalue subjects outside the core academic disciplines. He argued that these measures reflect an impoverished view of education, privileging easily measurable outcomes over the complex cognitive and affective learning that occurs in creative subjects.

### 2. The Importance of Teacher Autonomy and Professional Judgement

Throughout his career, Steers has championed teacher autonomy and professional judgement against increasing prescription and centralised control. He has argued that effective art and design teaching requires teachers to exercise professional discretion in responding to students' needs, interests, and contexts. This principle informed his critique of overly prescriptive curricula and assessment frameworks that attempted to standardise creative practice.

Steers has written extensively about the tension between accountability and autonomy, arguing that whilst teachers must be accountable for their students' learning, effective accountability should trust teachers' professional judgement rather than attempting to control every aspect of practice through detailed specification. He has pointed to international evidence, particularly from high-performing education systems in Finland and Singapore, that demonstrates the effectiveness of trusting teachers as skilled professionals.

In his work with NSEAD, Steers promoted continuing professional development (CPD) as essential for maintaining and developing teacher expertise. He argued that subject-specific CPD, led by experienced practitioners and informed by research, enables teachers to make better professional judgements and respond more effectively to policy changes and curriculum innovations.

### 3. Art and Design as Distinct Forms of Knowledge and Understanding

Steers has consistently argued that art and design constitute unique forms of knowledge and understanding that cannot be reduced to other curriculum areas or regarded merely as "skills" transferable across subjects. Drawing on the work of Elliot Eisner, Arthur Efland, and other art education theorists, he has articulated how visual and material thinking represents a distinctive mode of cognition essential to human development.

He has emphasised that art and design involve complex cognitive processes including visual perception, spatial reasoning, material understanding, and symbolic representation. Students learn to generate and communicate meaning through visual and tactile means, developing what Eisner termed "multiple literacies." Steers has argued that these capabilities are increasingly important in contemporary society, where visual communication pervades digital media, advertising, architecture, and everyday experience.

Steers has also defended the intrinsic value of art and design education against instrumentalist arguments that justify creative subjects solely through their contribution to economic competitiveness or their supposed benefits for learning in other subjects. Whilst acknowledging the economic importance of the creative industries, he has insisted that art and design deserve curriculum time because they develop essential human capabilities and contribute to cultural understanding, not merely because they serve economic purposes.

### 4. The Relationship Between Making and Critical Understanding

A consistent theme in Steers' work is the importance of integrating practical making with critical and contextual understanding. He has argued against false dichotomies that separate "doing" from "thinking" or "practical skills" from "theoretical knowledge," insisting that effective art and design education involves a constant interplay between these dimensions.

Steers has emphasised that making is itself a form of thinking—what Donald Schön termed "reflection-in-action." When students engage with materials, processes, and visual problems, they develop understanding through practice. Simultaneously, encountering the work of artists, designers, and craftspeople from diverse cultures and periods enriches students' practical work, providing exemplars, provocation, and context.

This principle informed NSEAD's advocacy during the development of the National Curriculum. Steers argued against assessment frameworks that separated practical skills from contextual knowledge, maintaining that this artificial division misrepresented the nature of learning in art and design. He championed approaches that assess students' ability to integrate making, critical analysis, and contextual understanding in sustained project work.

### 5. International Perspectives on Art Education

As editor of iJADE and through his involvement with InSEA, Steers has promoted international perspectives on art education and encouraged comparative research. He has argued that English art educators can learn from practices, pedagogies, and policies in other countries, challenging insularity and ethnocentrism in curriculum thinking.

Steers has published comparative analyses of curriculum frameworks, assessment systems, and policy approaches across different national contexts. This work has highlighted how England's assessment-driven, accountability-focused approach contrasts with more holistic, child-centred approaches in some other countries. He has been particularly interested in how different education systems balance standardisation with teacher autonomy, and how they value creative subjects relative to academic disciplines.

Through iJADE, Steers has facilitated global conversations about visual literacy, cultural education, digital technologies, and the role of art education in addressing contemporary challenges including sustainability, social justice, and intercultural understanding. He has encouraged research that examines how art education can respond to globalisation whilst respecting local cultures and traditions.

### 6. Critique of Narrowing Curriculum and Assessment

Steers has been a persistent critic of the narrowing of curriculum and assessment in English schools, arguing that this trend undermines comprehensive education and disproportionately affects disadvantaged students. His analysis has shown how policy emphasis on "basics" and "core" subjects has reduced access to creative education, particularly in primary schools and in secondary schools serving deprived communities.

He has documented how curriculum narrowing begins in early primary years, where pressures to achieve targets in literacy and numeracy lead schools to reduce time for art, music, drama, and design technology. This foundation of creative deprivation, Steers argues, has consequences throughout students' education, affecting their confidence, engagement, and breadth of capability.

Steers has also critiqued assessment practices that prioritise easily measured outcomes over more complex learning. He has challenged the dominance of written assessment in subjects like art and design, arguing that students should be assessed through the forms of representation appropriate to the discipline—primarily visual and material rather than verbal. His critique extends to the increasing use of tick-box criteria and mechanistic mark schemes that reduce teacher judgement to administration.

### 7. The Economic and Cultural Importance of Creative Education

Whilst resisting purely instrumentalist arguments, Steers has nevertheless articulated the economic and cultural importance of art and design education. He has highlighted the contribution of the creative industries to the UK economy and the role of art and design education in developing the creative workforce. His work has drawn on reports from organisations including the Creative Industries Federation, Arts Council England, and the Design Council to demonstrate the sector's economic significance.

Steers has been careful, however, to argue that education should not be reduced to workforce preparation. He has maintained that art and design education contributes to students' cultural development, helping them understand and participate in visual culture, appreciate diverse cultural traditions, and engage critically with the designed environment. This cultural dimension, he argues, is essential for informed citizenship in contemporary society.

He has also challenged narrow definitions of "employability skills" or "transferable skills" that ignore the specific expertise developed in creative subjects. Steers has argued that employers value the particular capabilities that art and design education develops, including creative problem-solving, visual communication, material understanding, and the ability to work through ambiguity and complexity.

### 8. Relationships Between Theory and Practice in Art Education Research

As editor of iJADE, Steers has promoted research that bridges theory and practice, addressing the sometimes problematic relationship between academic scholarship and classroom teaching. He has encouraged research that informs practice whilst maintaining rigorous standards, and has championed practitioner research that allows teachers to investigate their own practice systematically.

Steers has argued that art education research should engage with broader educational theory and research methods whilst respecting the distinctive characteristics of the subject. He has been open to diverse methodologies, from quantitative studies of policy impacts to qualitative investigations of learning processes, from philosophical analysis to action research in classrooms.

Through iJADE's editorial approach, Steers has promoted international dialogue between researchers, ensuring that UK art educators engage with scholarship from North America, Europe, Australia, Asia, and beyond. He has maintained that this international perspective enriches understanding and challenges assumptions that might otherwise remain unexamined.

## Key Publications

### Articles and Chapters

**"Taking the initiative: the role of national society in the development of art and design education"** (1986), published in *Journal of Art & Design Education*, Vol. 5, No. 3. This early article examined NSEAD's role in shaping policy and practice during a period of significant curriculum reform.

**"Practical ideology: The development of 'A' level art"** (1987), published in *Journal of Art & Design Education*, Vol. 6, No. 3. Steers analysed the evolution of A-level Art examination, exploring how assessment frameworks embody particular ideological assumptions about the nature of art education.

**"Art and design: compulsory for whom?"** (1995), published in *Journal of Art & Design Education*, Vol. 14, No. 2. This article examined debates about whether art should be compulsory in Key Stage 4, analysing arguments about entitlement, choice, and subject status.

**"Curriculum change and continuity"** (2003), in *Issues in Art and Design Teaching*, edited by Nicholas Addison and Lesley Burgess, published by RoutledgeFalmer. This chapter provided historical perspective on curriculum development, examining how art and design education has evolved in response to educational and social change.

**"The English Baccalaureate: an arts perspective"** (2011), published in *The Curriculum Journal*, Vol. 22, No. 1. Steers offered a comprehensive critique of the EBacc, documenting its impact on arts subjects and challenging the government's justification for the measure.

**"Reforming the school curriculum in England: a critique"** (2014), published in *Reimagining Schools: New Visions for Educational Practice*, edited by Steve Higgins and Peter Rubie-Davies. Steers analysed the Coalition government's curriculum reforms, examining their implications for creative subjects.

**"Assessment in art and design education: a necessary evil?"** (2015), published in *Debates in Art and Design Education*, edited by Nicholas Addison and Lesley Burgess, published by Routledge. This chapter examined tensions between assessment and creative practice, exploring possibilities for assessment that supports rather than constrains learning.

**"Curriculum and the arts"** (2016), chapter in *The Curriculum: Gallimaufry to Coherence?*, edited by Mark Priestley and Gert Biesta, published by Bloomsbury. Steers analysed how accountability measures had marginalised creative subjects and argued for more coherent curriculum thinking.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

Steers' influence on UK art and design curriculum and policy has been substantial and sustained over three decades. Through NSEAD, he participated in every major curriculum review from the introduction of the National Curriculum in 1988 through subsequent iterations in 1995, 2000, 2007, and 2014.

During the development of the original National Curriculum, Steers and NSEAD advocated successfully for art to be included as a foundation subject, ensuring statutory entitlement from ages 5-14. He contributed to the Art Working Group's deliberations, arguing for a curriculum that balanced making, investigating, and critical studies rather than separating these elements.

In the 1990s, Steers influenced the development of GCSE and A-level specifications, working with examination boards to ensure that assessment frameworks reflected contemporary art and design practice rather than outdated academic traditions. His advocacy helped establish project-based assessment as the norm, allowing students to develop sustained bodies of work rather than performing decontextualised exercises.

Steers was instrumental in NSEAD's campaign against the English Baccalaureate, contributing evidence that influenced parliamentary debates and media coverage. Whilst the EBacc remained in place, his work helped generate political awareness of its damaging effects and contributed to modest policy adjustments, including the government's eventual acknowledgement of the importance of creative subjects.

His analysis of Performance 8 and its impact on arts subjects contributed to wider debates about accountability measures, influencing organisations including the Education Policy Institute, the Royal Society of Arts, and the Cultural Learning Alliance. Steers' evidence-based approach and willingness to engage with data gave credibility to arguments that might otherwise have been dismissed as special pleading.

More broadly, Steers influenced professional discourse about art and design education through his writing, speaking, and editorial work. Generations of art teachers, teacher educators, and researchers have encountered his ideas through iJADE articles, conference presentations, and NSEAD publications. His insistence on maintaining high standards, respecting evidence, and engaging seriously with policy has helped establish norms for professional advocacy in the field.

Whilst Steers' primary contributions have been in policy analysis and professional advocacy rather than developing specific pedagogical methods, his work reflects clear pedagogical principles that have influenced art and design teaching in UK schools.
