# Eileen Adams

**Full Name:** Eileen Adams

**Current Roles:** Independent consultant in art, design and environmental education; formerly Senior Research Fellow, Institute of Education, University of London

**Known For:** Pioneer in built environment education, development of 'Power Drawing' pedagogical approach, promoting design education through place-based learning, advocating for children's engagement with their local environment through art and design

---

## Biography and Career History

Eileen Adams established herself as one of the most influential figures in UK art and design education from the 1970s onwards, with a particular focus on how children learn through engagement with the built environment. Her career has spanned teaching, research, curriculum development, and advocacy work that has fundamentally shaped approaches to environmental and design education in British schools.

Adams began her career as an art teacher before moving into research and curriculum development roles. She worked extensively with the School Curriculum Development Committee and later the Arts Council of England, developing frameworks and resources that connected art and design education with children's understanding of place, space, and the built environment.

Her work at the Institute of Education, University of London, positioned her as a leading researcher in the field of environmental education through art and design. During this period, she developed the theoretical frameworks and practical approaches that would influence national curriculum development and teacher training programmes across the UK.

Adams founded and directed several significant initiatives, including the Learning Through Landscapes Trust's educational programmes, which transformed how schools approached their grounds as learning environments. She also worked internationally, contributing to UNESCO programmes on environmental education and advising on curriculum development in various countries.

Throughout her career, Adams has maintained that art and design education should not be confined to the studio or classroom but should engage children actively with their surroundings, enabling them to become critical observers, creative thinkers, and active participants in shaping their environment.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Built Environment Education

Adams pioneered the integration of built environment studies into art and design education. She argued that children's understanding of architecture, urban planning, landscape design, and spatial relationships should be central to their artistic and design education. This approach positioned art and design as tools for critical engagement with the everyday environment rather than solely as studio-based practices.

Her framework for built environment education encompasses four key dimensions: **perceptual** (how we experience places through our senses), **cognitive** (how we understand and remember places), **affective** (how places make us feel), and **evaluative** (how we judge and critique the quality of places). This comprehensive approach ensures that children develop sophisticated ways of reading and responding to their environment.

Adams advocated for fieldwork and site-based learning as essential components of art and design education. She developed methodologies for taking children out of the classroom to observe, record, analyse, and respond to real places, from streetscapes to parks, from shopping centres to heritage sites.

### 2. Power Drawing

'Power Drawing' represents Adams's most distinctive pedagogical contribution to art and design education. This approach positions drawing not merely as representation but as a tool for thinking, investigating, and understanding the world. The term 'Power Drawing' emphasises drawing as an active, investigative process that gives learners agency and enables them to develop visual literacy.

Power Drawing involves several interconnected practices:

- **Observational drawing** as active looking and inquiry rather than passive copying
- **Analytical drawing** that explores structure, pattern, relationships, and systems
- **Speculative drawing** that imagines possibilities and tests ideas
- **Communicative drawing** that shares understanding and proposes change
- **Reflective drawing** that documents learning processes and develops metacognition

Adams distinguishes Power Drawing from conventional approaches that emphasise technical skill or finished products. Instead, she foregrounds drawing as a democratic skill accessible to all learners, a cognitive tool that supports learning across the curriculum, and a means of empowerment that enables children to participate in discussions about their environment.

### 3. Place-Based Learning

Adams developed sophisticated approaches to place-based learning that position the local environment as a primary curriculum resource. She argued that children's engagement with their immediate surroundings provides authentic contexts for learning in art, design, geography, history, science, and citizenship.

Her place-based pedagogy involves several stages:

- **Familiarisation** – developing sensory awareness and personal connections to places
- **Investigation** – systematic observation, recording, and analysis of environmental features
- **Interpretation** – understanding the social, historical, and cultural meanings embedded in places
- **Evaluation** – developing critical judgements about environmental quality and design
- **Action** – participating in environmental improvement and design processes

This approach recognises that places are not neutral backdrops but are socially constructed, culturally significant, and constantly changing. Adams encouraged teachers to help children understand that environments are designed and can be redesigned, fostering a sense of agency and responsibility.

### 4. Design Literacy

Adams conceptualised 'design literacy' as a fundamental educational outcome, comparable in importance to textual and numerical literacy. Design literacy encompasses the ability to 'read' designed objects and environments critically, understanding how design decisions affect functionality, aesthetics, meaning, and user experience.

She identified key components of design literacy:

- Understanding that everything designed reflects choices, values, and priorities
- Recognising the relationship between form and function
- Appreciating how materials, colours, textures, and spatial arrangements communicate meaning
- Evaluating design quality and appropriateness to purpose and context
- Understanding the design process and being able to participate in design activities

Adams argued that developing design literacy empowers children as consumers, citizens, and potential creators, enabling them to engage critically with the designed world and participate in discussions about environmental quality and change.

### 5. Children's Participation in Environmental Design

A consistent theme in Adams's work is advocacy for children's meaningful participation in decisions about their environment. Drawing on Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, she developed frameworks for involving children in real environmental design projects, from school grounds improvements to community planning initiatives.

Her participatory approaches include:

- Using art and design methods to elicit children's views and ideas
- Developing children's skills in environmental observation and analysis
- Teaching design processes that enable children to develop and communicate proposals
- Facilitating genuine dialogue between children and adult decision-makers
- Evaluating the impact of children's contributions on actual environmental outcomes

Adams demonstrated that children's participation in environmental design produces better outcomes – environments more suited to children's needs – whilst simultaneously providing powerful learning experiences in art, design, citizenship, and democratic participation.

### 6. Cross-Curricular Integration

Adams championed approaches to art and design education that connect with other curriculum areas. She demonstrated how engagement with the built environment through art and design naturally integrates learning in geography (place and spatial understanding), history (heritage and change over time), mathematics (proportion, measurement, pattern), science (materials and structures), and literacy (descriptive and persuasive writing about places).

This integration was not about diluting art and design but about demonstrating its distinctive contributions to broader educational goals. Adams showed how visual and spatial thinking, aesthetic judgement, and creative problem-solving – core capabilities in art and design – enhance learning across the curriculum.

### 7. Teacher Development

Adams recognised that her innovative approaches required substantial teacher development. She designed numerous courses, workshops, and resources to support teachers in developing confidence and competence in built environment education and place-based learning.

Her teacher development work emphasised:

- Developing teachers' own observational and drawing skills
- Enhancing teachers' understanding of architectural and design concepts
- Building teachers' confidence to work outside the classroom
- Providing practical strategies and lesson frameworks
- Encouraging teachers to adopt inquiry-based, child-centred pedagogies
- Supporting teachers to develop partnerships with architects, planners, and design professionals

## Key Publications

### Books

**"Art and Design in the Primary School" (with Colin Ward), 1982**  
This influential book challenged conventional approaches to primary art education, advocating for engagement with the real environment rather than relying on secondary sources. It provided practical guidance for teachers on taking children into the local environment for observational work and environmental studies.

**"Learning Through Landscapes: A Report on the Use, Design, Management and Development of School Grounds" (contributor), 1990**  
Adams contributed significantly to this landmark report that established the Learning Through Landscapes movement. The publication demonstrated the educational potential of school grounds and influenced thousands of schools to improve their outdoor environments.

**"Power Drawing: An Approach to Drawing and Design with Children", published in various formats and contexts from the 1990s onwards**  
Though not published as a single comprehensive book, Adams's Power Drawing framework has been disseminated through numerous articles, chapters, and educational resources. The concept has been elaborated in conference presentations, teacher training materials, and project publications.

**"A Place to Learn: Improving the Quality of School Grounds and External Environment", 2006**  
This publication synthesised decades of Adams's research and practice, providing comprehensive guidance on transforming school grounds into rich learning environments. It included case studies, design principles, and curriculum links.

### Journal Articles and Chapters

**"The Aesthetic Dimension of the Environment" in *The Built Environment: A Creative Resource for the School Curriculum*, 1987**  
This chapter established theoretical foundations for built environment education, arguing for aesthetic understanding as central to environmental education.

**"Back to Basics: Aesthetic Experience" in *Journal of Art and Design Education*, Vol. 12, No. 3, 1993**  
Adams critiqued narrow interpretations of 'back to basics' in education, arguing that aesthetic experience and engagement with the environment represent fundamental educational basics rather than optional extras.

**"Towards Creative Learning Spaces: Re-thinking the Architecture of Post-Compulsory Education" in *Educational and Child Psychology*, 2008**  
This article extended Adams's thinking beyond primary education, exploring how post-compulsory educational environments could better support creative learning.

**"Bringing Art and Design to the School through the Built Environment" in various publications**  
Adams contributed chapters to numerous edited collections on art education, consistently advocating for environmental engagement as central to art and design curriculum.

### Reports and Resources

**"Art and the Built Environment: A Teacher's Approach" (for the Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation), 1982**  
This resource pack provided practical guidance for teachers on using the built environment as a curriculum resource, including lesson plans, activity suggestions, and theoretical rationale.

**"School Grounds: A KIDS' Guide to School Ground Design" (with others), 1990**  
An innovative publication that provided guidance for children themselves on how to participate in designing and improving their school grounds, exemplifying Adams's commitment to children's participation.

**"Shaping Places: Young People's Participation in Environmental Design" (contributor to various editions)**  
Adams contributed to resources supporting the inclusion of young people's voices in environmental planning and design processes.

## Influence on Curriculum and Policy

### National Curriculum Development

Adams's ideas significantly influenced the development of the National Curriculum in England, particularly in Art and Design, Geography, and Design and Technology. Her advocacy for environmental education through art and design can be traced in various iterations of these curriculum documents.

The **Art and Design National Curriculum** has included references to recording observations from the environment, exploring environmental spaces, and engaging with architecture and design, all reflecting Adams's influence. The emphasis on investigating and responding to the environment in Key Stage 1 and 2 programmes of study directly echoes her pedagogical approaches.

The **Design and Technology curriculum** similarly shows Adams's influence in its emphasis on evaluating existing products and environments, understanding how design solutions respond to needs, and considering aesthetic as well as functional qualities.

### Policy Documents and Frameworks

Adams contributed to or influenced numerous policy documents:

**Commission for Architecture and the Built Environment (CABE) Educational Work**  
Adams worked closely with CABE (now absorbed into the Design Council) on educational initiatives. CABE's publications promoting built environment education in schools, including resources for teachers and guidance on school design, reflected her thinking and often featured her contributions.

**Department for Education and Skills (DfES) Guidance on School Grounds**  
Various DfES publications on developing school grounds as learning environments drew on Adams's research and recommendations, particularly regarding the educational value of outdoor spaces.

**QCA Schemes of Work**  
The Qualifications and Curriculum Authority's schemes of work for Art and Design in primary schools included units focusing on environmental observation and recording that reflected Adams's pedagogical approaches.

### Learning Through Landscapes Movement

Adams was instrumental in establishing and developing Learning Through Landscapes as a major force in UK education. This organisation has influenced thousands of schools to transform their grounds into rich learning environments, with direct impact on children's experiences of outdoor learning and environmental education.

The organisation's frameworks for using school grounds across the curriculum, guidance on design and development processes involving children, and emphasis on aesthetic quality all reflect Adams's foundational contributions.

### Professional Organisations

Adams's work influenced the policies and publications of organisations including:

- **National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD)** – particularly its advocacy for environmental education and place-based learning
- **Architectural Education Trust** (now incorporated into other organisations) – which promoted architectural awareness in schools
- **Royal Institute of British Architects (RIBA) Education Programme** – particularly initiatives connecting schools with architects and promoting design literacy

## Pedagogical Approach

### Theoretical Foundations

Adams's pedagogy draws on several educational traditions:

**Constructivism**: Children actively construct understanding through direct experience and inquiry rather than passive reception of information. Engagement with the built environment provides authentic contexts for active learning.

**Progressive Education**: Following John Dewey's emphasis on experience and environmental education traditions, Adams positioned learning within real-world contexts that connect to children's lives.

**Critical Pedagogy**: Adams's work encourages children to question and critique their environment, understanding that designed spaces reflect power relations and can be challenged and changed.

**Situated Learning**: Knowledge and skills are developed through participation in authentic activities within specific contexts, rather than as abstract or decontextualised exercises.

### Characteristic Teaching Strategies

Adams advocated specific teaching approaches:

**Fieldwork**: Regular, systematic engagement with places outside the classroom, involving observation, recording, discussion, and response. Fieldwork should be carefully structured with clear learning objectives but remain responsive to children's interests and discoveries.

**Drawing as Investigation**: Using drawing not as an end product but as a thinking tool, with emphasis on looking carefully, noticing details, exploring relationships, and developing visual understanding. Multiple quick sketches, annotated drawings, and comparative studies feature prominently.

**Questioning and Discussion**: Encouraging children to ask questions about their environment – Why is this here? Who designed this? How does it work? How could it be different? – develops critical thinking and design literacy.

**Making and Modeling**: Translating observations and ideas into three-dimensional form through model-making, enabling children to explore spatial relationships and design possibilities.

**Collaborative Learning**: Many activities involve paired or group work, reflecting how architects and designers work collaboratively. Children learn to share observations, discuss interpretations, and develop collective responses.

**Real-World Connections**: Whenever possible, connecting learning to real design processes, involving professional designers, or contributing to actual environmental improvement projects.

### Assessment Approaches

Adams advocated assessment that:

- Values process as well as product, with sketchbooks and working drawings recognised as important outcomes
- Recognises diverse forms of response including drawings, models, written descriptions, photographs, and presentations
- Assesses development in observation skills, visual literacy, design thinking, and critical evaluation
- Involves children in self-assessment and reflection on their learning
- Documents progression in environmental awareness and design literacy over time

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

Eileen Adams's influence continues to resonate in contemporary art and design education despite changes in curriculum policy and educational priorities. Her core ideas remain highly relevant to current educational concerns:

**Environmental Education and Sustainability**: As climate change and sustainability become increasingly urgent educational priorities, Adams's frameworks for helping children understand and engage with their environment have renewed significance. Her emphasis on critical evaluation of environmental quality and children's participation in environmental decision-making aligns with education for sustainable development.

**Cultural Capital and Equity**: Adams's commitment to using the local environment as a primary curriculum resource addresses issues of cultural capital and educational equity. By valuing children's own environments as worthy of study, her approaches validate all children's experiences rather than privileging those with access to museums, galleries, or cultural institutions.

**Wellbeing and Outdoor Learning**: Current emphasis on children's wellbeing and outdoor learning echoes Adams's long-standing advocacy for learning beyond the classroom. Research demonstrating benefits of outdoor learning for physical and mental health reinforces the importance of her place-based pedagogies.

**Design Thinking**: Contemporary emphasis on 'design thinking' as a transferable skill for problem-solving across contexts reflects Adams's earlier arguments about design literacy and design processes as fundamental educational outcomes.

**Pupil Voice and Participation**: Current policy emphasis on pupil voice and participation in school development provides opportunities to implement Adams's frameworks for children's involvement in environmental design and decision-making.

### Influence on Current Practitioners

Many current art and design educators, particularly those specialising in environmental education or community engagement, acknowledge Adams's influence on their practice. Her approaches continue to be taught in initial teacher education and continuing professional development programmes.

Organisations such as the Royal Institute of British Architects' education programme, the Sorrell Foundation's young designers initiative, and various architecture centres across the UK continue work that builds on foundations Adams established.

## Critical Perspectives

While Adams's work has been widely influential and generally positively received, some critical perspectives are worth noting:

**Resource Implications**: Her approaches require time for fieldwork, access to diverse environments, and often smaller group sizes or additional adult support. In contexts of limited resources and timetable pressures, full implementation can be challenging.

**Teacher Confidence**: Effectiveness depends significantly on teacher confidence in working outdoors, managing fieldwork, and facilitating open-ended investigations. Not all teachers feel equally prepared for such approaches despite training opportunities.
