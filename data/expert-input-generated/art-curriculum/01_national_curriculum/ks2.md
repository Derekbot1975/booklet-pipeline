# Art & Design at Key Stage 2

## Overview of Key Stage 2 Art & Design

Key Stage 2 (KS2) encompasses Years 3-6 (ages 7-11) and represents a critical developmental phase in children's artistic education. The National Curriculum for Art and Design (2013) establishes that KS2 pupils should be taught to "develop their techniques, including their control and their use of materials, with creativity, experimentation and an increasing awareness of different kinds of art, craft and design."

At KS2, the statutory requirements build directly upon the foundations established at Key Stage 1, with expectations of greater technical proficiency, critical awareness, and the ability to articulate artistic intentions. As John Steers, General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has emphasised, this key stage is where pupils transition from intuitive mark-making to more conscious artistic decision-making whilst maintaining creative spontaneity.

## National Curriculum Requirements

### Statutory Content

The 2013 National Curriculum specifies that pupils should be taught to:

1. **Create sketchbooks** to record their observations and use them to review and revisit ideas
2. **Improve their mastery of art and design techniques**, including drawing, painting and sculpture with a range of materials (for example, pencil, charcoal, paint, clay)
3. Learn about **great artists, architects and designers** in history

Unlike Key Stage 1, KS2 introduces the explicit requirement for sketchbooks, marking a significant pedagogical shift toward sustained artistic enquiry and reflection. Rachel Mason, in her research on art education assessment, notes that sketchbooks serve as "visual diaries of thinking" that document both process and progression.

### Progression from Key Stage 1

The progression from KS1 to KS2 involves:

- Moving from exploring materials to developing mastery and control
- Transitioning from simple observation to sustained, purposeful recording
- Developing from basic awareness of artists to analytical engagement with their work
- Building from experimentation to refinement and evaluation

## The Role of Sketchbooks

### Pedagogical Significance

The introduction of sketchbooks at KS2 represents what Eileen Adams describes as "a space for visual literacy development." Sketchbooks are not merely portfolios but active tools for:

- **Visual research and investigation** – collecting images, textures, and ideas
- **Experimentation** – testing techniques, materials, and approaches without fear of failure
- **Reflection and annotation** – articulating thinking through words alongside images
- **Planning and development** – working through ideas toward finished outcomes
- **Personal response** – developing individual artistic voice and preferences

### Practical Implementation

Effective sketchbook practice at KS2 includes:

**Regular, sustained use**: Weekly dedicated time rather than sporadic engagement
**Teacher modelling**: Demonstrating how artists use sketchbooks for thinking and exploration
**Valuing process over product**: Encouraging risk-taking and accepting "mistakes" as learning
**Varied approaches**: Including observational drawing, colour mixing, material sampling, critical responses, and personal reflection
**Purposeful annotation**: Teaching pupils to articulate their intentions, evaluations, and next steps

Leslie Cunliffe's research on primary art education emphasises that sketchbooks should be "owned" by pupils, allowing for personalisation and individual pathways of enquiry whilst meeting curriculum objectives.

## Technical Skills Development

### Drawing

At KS2, drawing skills should develop significantly in:

**Observational accuracy**: Using viewfinders, focusing on proportion, scale and composition
**Line quality**: Varying line weight, exploring continuous line, contour drawing
**Tone and shading**: Introducing hatching, cross-hatching, blending with pencil, charcoal
**Perspective**: Basic one-point perspective, understanding foreground/middle ground/background
**Figure drawing**: Proportions of the human figure, gesture drawing, capturing movement

Progression should be evident across the key stage, with Year 6 pupils demonstrating considerably more control and sophistication than Year 3.

### Painting

Technical development in painting includes:

**Colour theory**: Understanding primary, secondary and tertiary colours; exploring complementary and harmonious colour schemes
**Colour mixing**: Achieving specific hues, tints, tones and shades; mixing from limited palettes
**Brush control**: Varying brush strokes, understanding different brush types and their effects
**Paint application techniques**: Wet-on-wet, wet-on-dry, layering, glazing, impasto effects
**Compositional skills**: Planning and executing painted compositions with consideration of balance, focal points and visual pathways

The curriculum specifically mentions painting alongside other techniques, and teachers should ensure progression across different painting media including watercolour, acrylic, and powder paint.

### Sculpture and 3D Work

Three-dimensional work at KS2 should include:

**Modelling with clay**: Pinch, coil, and slab techniques; understanding structural integrity; surface decoration and texture
**Construction**: Using cardboard, wire, found materials; exploring joints, balance and stability
**Carving**: Safe introduction to subtractive sculpture using soap, plaster or soft materials
**Mixed media assemblage**: Combining materials thoughtfully; understanding juxtaposition and meaning

John Steers argues that 3D work is frequently underdeveloped in primary settings due to perceived resource and expertise limitations, yet it offers essential opportunities for spatial awareness and problem-solving.

### Printmaking

Though not explicitly mentioned in the 2013 curriculum, printmaking remains a valuable technique for KS2:

**Relief printing**: Developing from simple polystyrene and card prints to more complex lino (with safety cutters)
**Monoprinting**: Exploring unique prints through various techniques
**Collagraph**: Building textured printing plates
**Multi-colour printing**: Registration and overprinting

Printmaking develops understanding of positive/negative space, pattern, repetition and editioning.

### Textiles

Textile work at KS2 can encompass:

**Construction techniques**: Hand-sewing, running stitch, backstitch; understanding structure
**Surface decoration**: Tie-dye, batik (appropriate methods), fabric printing, appliqué
**Weaving**: Simple frame looms, paper weaving developing to fabric
**Embellishment**: Beading, embroidery, mixed media approaches

Eileen Adams has highlighted how textile work connects to cultural understanding and identity, offering rich cross-curricular opportunities.

## Knowledge of Artists, Architects and Designers

### Breadth and Diversity

The requirement to study "great artists, architects and designers in history" must be interpreted broadly and inclusively. Jeff Adams, in his work on visual culture in education, emphasises that "greatness" should encompass diverse traditions, cultures, genders and periods, avoiding the exclusive focus on Western canonical artists that has historically dominated art education.

An effective KS2 programme should include:

**Diverse historical periods**: From ancient to contemporary
**Global perspectives**: Artists from various cultures and continents
**Gender representation**: Ensuring female artists are prominently featured
**Different disciplines**: Painters, sculptors, architects, designers, craftspeople, photographers, digital artists
**Local and regional artists**: Connecting to pupils' own communities and contexts

### Depth of Study

Rather than superficial surveys, KS2 teaching should involve:

**Sustained investigation**: Spending sufficient time with individual artists to understand their work deeply
**Contextual understanding**: Exploring why and how artists worked, their influences and intentions
**Practical connections**: Responding to artists' work through pupils' own making
**Critical engagement**: Developing vocabulary to analyse, interpret and evaluate artwork
**Comparative studies**: Understanding similarities, differences and influences between artists

Rachel Mason's research on art education assessment suggests that knowledge of artists should be assessed through pupils' ability to make informed connections between artists' work and their own practice, rather than mere factual recall.

### Suggested Artists for KS2

While not prescriptive, a well-balanced KS2 curriculum might include artists such as:

**Drawing and painting**: Leonardo da Vinci, Katsushika Hokusai, Frida Kahlo, Vincent van Gogh, Georgia O'Keeffe, Pablo Picasso, David Hockney, Lubaina Himid, Yayoi Kusama

**Sculpture and 3D**: Barbara Hepworth, Henry Moore, Andy Goldsworthy, Antony Gormley, Anish Kapoor, Magdalene Odundo, Sokari Douglas Camp

**Architecture**: Zaha Hadid, Antoni Gaudí, Frank Lloyd Wright, Norman Foster

**Craft and design**: William Morris, Clarice Cliff, Cath Kidston, contemporary ceramic artists

**Photography and contemporary practice**: Cindy Sherman, Martin Parr, Chris Ofili, Grayson Perry

## Cross-Curricular Connections

### Literacy

Art & Design offers substantial opportunities for literacy development:

**Descriptive language**: Developing rich vocabulary for colours, textures, techniques, emotions
**Technical terminology**: Learning and applying subject-specific vocabulary accurately
**Annotation and evaluation**: Written reflection in sketchbooks
**Artist research**: Reading about artists' lives and work; comprehension activities
**Creative writing**: Using artwork as stimulus for narrative, poetry and descriptive writing

### Mathematics

Connections include:

**Shape and space**: Exploring geometric forms in art and architecture
**Proportion and ratio**: Figure drawing, scaling up images
**Measurement**: Accurate construction and planning
**Symmetry and tessellation**: Pattern-making inspired by artists like M.C. Escher
**Perspective**: Mathematical principles underlying accurate representation of three-dimensional space

### History

Art & Design naturally complements history teaching:

**Historical periods**: Art as evidence of historical contexts and cultures
**Ancient civilisations**: Egyptian, Greek, Roman art and architecture
**Local history**: Historical buildings and artworks in the local area
**Significant individuals**: Artists as historical figures with impact and legacy

### Geography

Geographical connections include:

**Landscape art**: Representing different environments and weather conditions
**Cultural art forms**: Art from different countries and cultures
**Environmental art**: Land art and artists responding to place
**Architecture**: How buildings reflect climate, resources and culture

### Science

Science links encompass:

**Materials and their properties**: Understanding how materials behave in art-making
**Light and colour**: Scientific principles underlying colour theory and perception
**Forces and structures**: Engineering principles in sculpture and architecture
**Natural forms**: Observational drawing of plants, animals, natural phenomena

## Assessment at Key Stage 2

### Principles of Assessment

The absence of levels since 2014 has given schools greater autonomy in assessment. Effective assessment in KS2 Art & Design should:

**Focus on progress over time**: Using sketchbooks and portfolios to document development
**Value process and product**: Recognising experimentation and refinement alongside outcomes
**Use clear criteria**: Based on curriculum objectives rather than subjective judgement
**Involve pupils**: Self and peer assessment developing critical awareness
**Inform planning**: Assessment information directing teaching and intervention

Rachel Mason's seminal work on art education assessment emphasises that assessment should enhance rather than restrict creativity, functioning formatively to develop pupils' artistic understanding.

### Assessment Criteria

Schools commonly assess against the three strands of the National Curriculum:

**Creating and developing ideas** (sketchbooks, research, experimentation):
- Quality of observation and recording
- Range and development of ideas
- Use of visual research including artist references
- Refinement and improvement over time

**Mastery of techniques** (drawing, painting, sculpture, etc.):
- Technical control and accuracy
- Range of techniques and materials used
- Sophistication and complexity
- Independence in selecting appropriate techniques

**Knowledge of artists**:
- Breadth of artists studied
- Depth of understanding demonstrated
- Ability to make connections between artists and own work
- Critical vocabulary and analytical skills

### Moderation and Standards

Without national testing in Art & Design, schools must establish internal consistency:

**Portfolio moderation**: Staff agreeing standards across year groups
**Professional dialogue**: Discussing what progression looks like
**External moderation**: Engaging with cluster groups or local authority networks
**Exemplification materials**: Using NSEAD or other professional body resources

## Planning and Pedagogy

### Unit Planning

Effective KS2 Art & Design units typically:

**Have clear learning objectives**: Aligned with curriculum requirements and building progression
**Allow sufficient time**: Multi-week units enabling depth rather than superficial coverage
**Balance skills and knowledge**: Combining technical development with artist study
**Provide authentic contexts**: Meaningful purposes for art-making beyond exercises
**Enable personal response**: Allowing for individual interpretation and expression

John Steers advocates for "enquiry-based" learning where pupils investigate visual problems through making, responding to artists' approaches, and developing their own solutions.

### Pedagogical Approaches

Effective teaching strategies include:

**Teacher demonstration**: Modelling techniques, processes and artistic thinking
**Scaffolded independence**: Gradually releasing responsibility to pupils
**Exploratory tasks**: Open-ended challenges encouraging experimentation
**Focused practice**: Targeted skill development addressing specific techniques
**Collaborative work**: Group projects developing teamwork and shared creativity
**Individual pathways**: Allowing personal interpretation within common frameworks

Leslie Cunliffe's research emphasises the importance of teachers' own engagement with art-making, arguing that "confident creative teaching requires confident creative teachers."

### Resources and Environment

Quality provision requires:

**Adequate materials**: Good quality, well-maintained resources appropriate to tasks
**Organised storage**: Accessible, labelled resources encouraging independence
**Display**: Celebrating pupils' work and showcasing artists' work
**Dedicated space**: Ideally specialist space, or well-organised classroom provision
**Technology**: Digital cameras, tablets, visualisers, access to online galleries
**Sketchbooks**: Individual books retained throughout the key stage

Budget constraints mean strategic planning is essential, with investment in versatile, quality basics prioritised over expensive consumables.

### Time Allocation

While not statutorily prescribed, NSEAD recommends approximately 5% of curriculum time for Art & Design at KS2 – roughly one hour weekly. However, schools vary significantly:

**Discrete timetabling**: Weekly dedicated Art & Design lessons
**Block timetabling**: Intensive units across several consecutive weeks
**Integrated approaches**: Art & Design taught through topics and themes
**Hybrid models**: Combining dedicated skills teaching with integrated application

Eileen Adams warns against the over-integration of Art & Design, arguing that while cross-curricular connections are valuable, art has intrinsic value and disciplinary integrity that should not be subsumed entirely within topic work.

## Inclusion and Differentiation

### Meeting Diverse Needs

Art & Design offers particular opportunities for inclusive practice:

**Multiple entry points**: Visual tasks accessible to pupils with varied literacy levels
**Different intelligences**: Valuing spatial and creative abilities alongside linguistic and logical
**Cultural connections**: Engaging pupils through culturally relevant artists and approaches
**Practical engagement**: Kinaesthetic learning opportunities for physically active learners

### SEND Provision

Adaptations for pupils with special educational needs might include:

**Physical adaptations**: Adapted tools, grips, larger materials for pupils with motor difficulties
**Sensory considerations**: Appropriate materials for pupils with sensory processing differences
**Scaffolding**: Visual prompts, step-by-step guides, exemplars
**Extended time**: Allowing for slower working pace or processing
**Alternative recording**: Verbal annotation, photographic documentation

John Steers has championed art education's role in inclusive education, highlighting how successful art experiences can build confidence and self-esteem for pupils who struggle in other curriculum areas.

### Stretch and Challenge

More able pupils should be challenged through:

**Technical complexity**: Introduction to more demanding techniques and materials
**Conceptual depth**: More sophisticated interpretation and personal response
**Independent investigation**: Self-directed research and experimentation
**Critical analysis**: Advanced vocabulary and comparative studies
**Refinement**: Higher expectations of finish and resolution

## Professional Development for Teachers

### Subject Knowledge Development

Many primary teachers lack specialist art qualifications. Professional development should address:

**Technical confidence**: Hands-on workshops developing teachers' own skills
**Curriculum understanding**: Deep engagement with progression and expectations
**Artist knowledge**: Building repertoire of diverse artists and their work
**Pedagogical approaches**: Strategies specific to art teaching and learning
**Assessment literacy**: Understanding what progression looks like in art

### Resources for Teachers

Professional support is available through:

**AccessArt**: Online resource providing comprehensive schemes of work and CPD
**NSEAD**: Professional association offering publications, networks and guidance
**Regional museums and galleries**: Education programmes and teacher resources
**Artist-educator partnerships**: Bringing practising artists into schools
**Local authority advisers**: Where still available, specialist support and network meetings

Jeff Adams advocates for "reflective practice" where teachers engage critically with their own teaching, documenting and analysing their pedagogical approaches as they develop their art education practice.

## Current Challenges and Debates

### Curriculum Time Pressures

The accountability focus on English and mathematics has squeezed Arts provision in many primary schools. The Education Endowment Foundation's research shows that creative subjects have declined in curriculum time since 2010, particularly in schools serving disadvantaged communities.

### Assessment Without Levels

Schools continue to grapple with assessment approaches post-levels, with inconsistent practices nationally. The lack of external moderation or exemplification materials makes judging standards challenging.

### Teacher Confidence and Expertise

Research consistently identifies lack of specialist knowledge and confidence as barriers to quality art education. The Warwick Commission (2015) highlighted the "arts divide" where pupils in advantaged settings receive richer arts provision.
