# Art & Design at Key Stage 1

## Overview and National Curriculum Context

Art & Design at Key Stage 1 (ages 5-7, Years 1 and 2) forms the foundation of children's formal artistic education in England and Wales. The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2013) establishes the statutory requirements that all maintained schools must follow, emphasising the development of creativity, visual literacy, and technical skills from the earliest stages of primary education.

The Key Stage 1 Programme of Study states that pupils should be taught:
- to use a range of materials creatively to design and make products
- to use drawing, painting and sculpture to develop and share their ideas, experiences and imagination
- to develop a wide range of art and design techniques in using colour, pattern, texture, line, shape, form and space
- about the work of a range of artists, craft makers and designers, describing the differences and similarities between different practices and disciplines, and making links to their own work

## Developmental Characteristics of KS1 Learners

### Cognitive and Physical Development

Eileen Adams, in her extensive work on art education and the built environment, emphasises that Key Stage 1 pupils are at a crucial developmental stage where visual and kinaesthetic learning predominate. Children aged 5-7 are developing fine motor control, hand-eye coordination, and the ability to manipulate tools and materials with increasing precision.

John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently argued that this age group benefits from exploratory, process-based approaches rather than product-focused outcomes. Children at KS1 are naturally curious and experimental, characteristics that should be nurtured rather than constrained by overly prescriptive teaching.

### Visual Literacy Development

Rachel Mason's research on international perspectives in art education highlights that KS1 represents a critical period for developing visual literacy. Children move from symbolic representation (typical of Early Years Foundation Stage) towards greater observational accuracy and awareness of visual conventions. This transition requires careful scaffolding and appropriate challenge.

## Curriculum Content and Progression

### Materials and Processes

The National Curriculum requires exposure to diverse materials and processes. At KS1, this typically includes:

**Drawing Media:**
- Pencils (various grades), graphite sticks, charcoal
- Felt tips, markers, wax crayons, oil pastels
- Chalks and pastels (introduction towards Year 2)

**Painting:**
- Ready-mixed powder paints, liquid tempera
- Watercolours (introduced carefully)
- Variety of brushes (thick, thin, flat, round)
- Alternative tools (sponges, fingers, found objects)

**3D and Sculpture:**
- Clay (air-drying and firing clay where facilities permit)
- Playdough and salt dough
- Construction materials (card, boxes, tubes)
- Malleable materials (Modroc, paper pulp)

**Textiles and Collage:**
- Fabric pieces, threads, wool
- Papers of various textures and weights
- Natural materials (leaves, twigs, seeds)
- Adhesive techniques appropriate to age

Leslie Cunliffe's work on practical art teaching emphasises the importance of teaching proper tool use and material properties from the earliest stages, establishing good habits and safety awareness.

### Formal Elements

The National Curriculum explicitly references the formal elements as central to art education. At KS1, children should develop understanding of:

**Line:** Mark-making with various tools; thick/thin, straight/curved, continuous/broken lines; line to describe edges and contours

**Shape:** Recognition and creation of geometric and organic shapes; positive and negative shapes; overlapping

**Colour:** Primary and secondary colours; colour mixing; warm/cool colours; colour for mood and expression

**Pattern:** Repeating elements; natural and manufactured patterns; pattern in different cultures

**Texture:** Actual and visual texture; rubbings; surface qualities; descriptive vocabulary

**Form:** Three-dimensional awareness; exploration through sculpture and construction

**Space:** Foreground/background concepts; arranging elements within a picture plane; size relationships

## Teaching Approaches and Pedagogies

### The Investigate-Make-Evaluate Cycle

John Steers advocates for a cyclical approach to art teaching that mirrors professional artistic practice:

**Investigate:** Looking at artworks, natural objects, the environment; collecting visual information; discussing and analysing

**Make:** Experimenting with materials; developing ideas; creating artwork; refining techniques

**Evaluate:** Reflecting on outcomes; discussing what worked; suggesting improvements; making connections

This cycle should be age-appropriate at KS1, with emphasis on practical exploration and oral reflection rather than written evaluation.

### Sketchbooks at KS1

While not statutorily required at KS1, many schools introduce sketchbooks (or similar exploratory journals) from Year 1. These should be:
- Large enough for easy manipulation (A4 or larger)
- Pressure-free spaces for experimentation
- Used for collecting ideas, testing materials, and recording observations
- Not assessed in the same way as finished pieces

Eileen Adams has written extensively about the importance of visual journals in developing children's observational skills and personal visual language.

### Direct Teaching and Demonstration

Effective KS1 art teaching balances freedom with instruction. Jeff Adams, in his work on contemporary art education, argues that technique teaching is essential but should serve creative ends rather than becoming mechanistic. Teachers should:

- Demonstrate tool use and techniques clearly
- Model artistic thinking and decision-making aloud
- Teach specific skills (e.g., brush control, clay joining) in context
- Provide adequate time for practice and experimentation
- Intervene supportively during making processes

## Artists, Craftspeople and Designers

The National Curriculum requirement to study artists, craft makers and designers is crucial for broadening children's visual experience and understanding that art is a human activity with cultural contexts.

### Selecting Appropriate Artists

At KS1, choices should reflect:
- **Visual accessibility:** Clear, engaging imagery that children can discuss
- **Technical relevance:** Connection to materials and processes being taught
- **Cultural diversity:** Artists from various backgrounds, times and places
- **Local artists:** Craftspeople and designers in the school's community

### Recommended Artists for KS1

**Painters and Illustrators:**
- Wassily Kandinsky (colour and abstract shapes)
- Henri Matisse (colour, pattern, cut-outs)
- Georgia O'Keeffe (close observation, flowers)
- Eric Carle (collage, pattern)
- Lubaina Himid (colour, pattern, cultural narratives)

**Sculptors:**
- Andy Goldsworthy (natural materials, environmental art)
- Anthony Gormley (human form, scale)
- Barbara Hepworth (form, holes, texture)

**Textile Artists and Designers:**
- William Morris (pattern, nature)
- Orla Kiely (pattern, colour)
- Gee's Bend quilters (pattern, community craft)

**Architects and Designers:**
- Antonio Gaudí (pattern, texture, organic forms in architecture)
- Contemporary ceramic artists and potters
- Local craftspeople and makers

Rachel Mason's research emphasises the importance of presenting artists as real people engaged in creative problem-solving, not as remote historical figures.

## Practical Organisation and Classroom Management

### Time Allocation

The National Curriculum does not specify time allocations, but NSEAD guidance suggests a minimum of one hour per week for art and design at KS1. Many schools block time for extended projects or rotate art with other foundation subjects.

### Resource Management

Effective KS1 art teaching requires:
- Accessible storage at child height where safe
- Clear labelling with words and images
- Established routines for distribution and collection
- Adequate preparation time for mixing paints, cutting materials
- Age-appropriate tools (chunky brushes, easy-grip scissors for some children)

### Health and Safety

The CLEAPSS Art & Design primary guidelines provide authoritative safety advice. Key considerations for KS1:
- PVA glue rather than solvent-based adhesives
- Non-toxic, washable materials
- Appropriate supervision for tools (scissors, clay tools)
- Aprons or old shirts for protection
- Handwashing facilities and routines
- Awareness of allergies (particularly to wheat-based materials)

## Assessment at Key Stage 1

### Statutory Assessment Requirements

Art & Design is not part of statutory assessment at KS1 (no SATs for art). However, schools must report children's progress to parents and have internal assessment systems.

### Assessment Approaches

John Steers has consistently argued against over-formalised assessment in primary art, particularly at KS1. Appropriate assessment should:

**Focus on:**
- Progress in using materials and techniques
- Development of ideas and personal responses
- Growing vocabulary for discussing art
- Engagement and perseverance

**Methods include:**
- Photographic documentation of process and outcomes
- Observational notes during lessons
- Occasional focused questioning or conversation
- Annotation of selected work (by teacher or child)

**Avoid:**
- Numerical levels or grades at this stage
- Comparison between children's creative responses
- Over-emphasis on neatness or conformity
- Assessment criteria that undervalue experimentation

### Recording Achievement

Many schools use:
- Digital portfolios showing range of work
- "Floor books" documenting class projects
- Individual folders with examples from each topic
- Wall displays celebrating process and product

## Cross-Curricular Links and Wider Learning

Art & Design at KS1 naturally connects with other curriculum areas:

**English:** Vocabulary development; visual literacy; illustration; responding to stories and poetry through art

**Mathematics:** Shapes, patterns, symmetry; counting and sorting materials; spatial awareness

**Science:** Observational drawing; investigating materials; natural forms; seasonal changes

**History and Geography:** Historical artists and periods; art from different places; local environment studies

**Computing:** Digital photography; simple image manipulation; researching artists

Eileen Adams' work on "learning through landscapes" emphasises how art can connect children to their environment and develop wider observational and critical thinking skills.

## Inclusion and Differentiation

### SEND Provision

Art & Design offers particular opportunities for children with special educational needs:
- Kinaesthetic and visual learning strengths
- Alternative means of expression and communication
- Confidence-building through success in non-written tasks
- Sensory exploration for children with learning difficulties

Adaptations might include:
- Modified tools (easier-grip implements, adapted scissors)
- Alternative materials (different textures, larger scale)
- Additional adult support for particular techniques
- Extended time or simplified steps

### Cultural Responsiveness

Rachel Mason's international perspective reminds us that art education must be culturally responsive. At KS1, this means:
- Representing diverse artists and cultural traditions
- Valuing different aesthetic preferences and artistic conventions
- Connecting with children's home cultures and experiences
- Avoiding cultural stereotypes or tokenism

### Gender Considerations

Research shows that gender stereotypes about art can emerge early. Teachers should:
- Challenge assumptions about "boys' colours" or "girls' subjects"
- Present male and female artists equally
- Encourage all children in 3D and construction work
- Avoid gendered praise patterns

## Role of the Subject Leader

The Art & Design subject leader in a primary school has particular responsibilities for KS1:

**Curriculum Planning:**
- Ensuring coverage and progression across Years 1 and 2
- Selecting age-appropriate artists and resources
- Supporting non-specialist class teachers

**Resource Management:**
- Maintaining adequate materials and equipment
- Budget management and purchasing
- Health and safety compliance

**Professional Development:**
- Supporting colleagues' confidence in teaching art
- Sharing good practice and new approaches
- Keeping abreast of developments in art education

Leslie Cunliffe's practical guides for subject leaders emphasise the importance of making art teaching accessible and achievable for all primary practitioners, many of whom lack specialist training.

## Transition and Progression to Key Stage 2

Effective transition planning ensures that Year 3 teachers build on KS1 foundations:

**Documentation should show:**
- Range of materials and techniques experienced
- Artists and cultural traditions explored
- Developing technical skills and vocabulary
- Particular interests or strengths

**Year 2 should prepare for KS2 by:**
- Introducing more sustained projects
- Developing greater independence in material choices
- Encouraging more detailed observation
- Beginning to make connections between different artists' work

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Digital Technology

While the National Curriculum mentions computing connections, debate continues about the role of digital tools in KS1 art. Considerations include:
- Balance between physical material exploration and screen time
- Age-appropriate uses (photography, simple drawing apps)
- Technology as a tool rather than replacement for making
- Digital literacy as part of visual literacy

### Assessment and Accountability

Jeff Adams and other contemporary art educators critique the pressure for measurable outcomes in creative subjects. At KS1, this manifests in:
- Pressure to demonstrate "progress" in quantifiable ways
- Risk of over-scaffolding to achieve particular outcomes
- Tension between process and product
- Need to advocate for exploratory, experimental approaches

### Time and Curriculum Squeeze

With pressure on literacy and numeracy time, foundation subjects including art face challenges. Subject leaders must:
- Articulate art's contribution to whole child development
- Demonstrate cross-curricular benefits
- Make efficient use of allocated time
- Celebrate and display art prominently

## Conclusion

Art & Design at Key Stage 1 establishes foundations for lifelong creative and visual literacy. Effective practice balances skill development with creative freedom, exposes children to diverse artistic traditions, and nurtures curiosity and expression. The work of thinkers like Eileen Adams, John Steers, Rachel Mason, Leslie Cunliffe and Jeff Adams provides both philosophical grounding and practical guidance for teachers seeking to deliver rich, inclusive art education that serves all KS1 learners.
