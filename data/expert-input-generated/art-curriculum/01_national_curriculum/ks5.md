# Art & Design at Key Stage 5 (A Level)

## Overview and Statutory Framework

Key Stage 5 Art & Design (ages 16-18) operates within the post-compulsory education sector and is not governed by the National Curriculum. Instead, provision is regulated through examination board specifications approved by Ofqual. As John Steers (2009) notes in the *International Journal of Art & Design Education*, A Level Art & Design represents "a crucial transition point where students move from teacher-directed learning to independent artistic inquiry and personal voice development."

The standard A Level course is typically studied over two years, with AS Level qualifications available as standalone one-year courses, though many schools now offer only the full A Level following 2015 reforms that decoupled AS from A2.

## Examination Board Specifications

### AQA Art & Design

AQA offers the most granular specialisation structure with eight distinct titles:
- Art, Craft and Design
- Fine Art
- Graphic Communication
- Textile Design
- Three-Dimensional Design
- Photography
- Critical and Contextual Studies (essay-based)
- Endorsement in Art, Craft and Design (broader pathway)

The specification requires students to complete Component 1: Personal Investigation (60%, including a 1,000-3,000 word related study) and Component 2: Externally Set Assignment (40%, with 15 hours supervised time).

### Pearson Edexcel Art & Design

Edexcel structures provision around four pathways:
- Fine Art
- Graphic Communication
- Textile Design
- Three-Dimensional Design and Craft

Assessment comprises Coursework (60%) and Externally Set Assignment (40%, with 15 hours supervised time). Edexcel emphasises what Leslie Cunliffe describes as "sustained investigation" where students demonstrate continuity of thinking over extended periods.

### OCR Art & Design

OCR provides three distinct qualifications:
- Art and Design (broad-based)
- Fine Art
- Critical and Contextual Studies (predominantly written)

Their specification emphasises "personal enquiry" and requires students to submit a portfolio (60%) and an externally set task (40%). OCR's distinctive feature is greater flexibility in portfolio presentation formats.

### WJEC Eduqas Art & Design

WJEC offers:
- Art and Design
- Art, Craft and Design
- Photography (separate qualification)

Component 1 (Personal Creative Enquiry) constitutes 60% while Component 2 (Externally Set Assignment) represents 40%, with 15 hours supervised time. WJEC specifications particularly emphasise the integration of Welsh cultural contexts where appropriate.

## Assessment Objectives (AOs)

All specifications align to four common Assessment Objectives established by Ofqual:

**AO1: Develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and critical understanding** (25%)

Rachel Mason (2010) argues this objective represents "the intellectual core of advanced art education, requiring students to construct meaningful relationships between their practice and broader cultural discourse."

**AO2: Explore and select appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes, reviewing and refining ideas as work develops** (25%)

**AO3: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions, reflecting critically on work and progress** (25%)

John Steers emphasises that AO3 should not be reduced to "annotated sketchbooks" but rather represents "critical reflective practice evidenced through multiple forms including written analysis, visual journals, photography, and digital documentation."

**AO4: Present a personal and meaningful response that realises intentions and, where appropriate, makes connections between visual and other elements** (25%)

## The Personal Investigation

The Personal Investigation represents the most substantial component of A Level study, typically developing across the first 18 months of the course. Eileen Adams (2008) describes this as "the space where students construct their artistic identity through sustained inquiry into personally meaningful themes."

### Structural Requirements

Students must produce:
- A practical portfolio demonstrating all four AOs
- A related written study (1,000-3,000 words for most boards)
- Evidence of developmental work showing inquiry processes

### The Related Study

The written component represents a significant departure from GCSE provision. As Jeff Adams (2014) notes in *A Level Art History: A Companion*, this requirement "acknowledges that advanced visual literacy requires both making and critical writing skills in complementary relationship."

Effective related studies:
- Connect directly to practical work rather than existing as separate essays
- Demonstrate critical engagement with primary sources (exhibitions, artist studios, original artworks)
- Show analytical frameworks drawn from art historical, critical, or theoretical perspectives
- Integrate visual analysis with contextual understanding

### Practical Implications for Teachers

**Year 12 Autumn Term:**
- Establish independent working practices through mini-projects
- Introduce contextual study methodologies
- Develop visual analysis skills through gallery/museum visits
- Explore diverse media and processes aligned to chosen pathway

**Year 12 Spring/Summer Terms:**
- Define Personal Investigation theme through mind-mapping and contextual research
- Begin sustained practical exploration
- Initiate related study research
- Review and refine direction through interim assessment

**Year 13 Autumn Term:**
- Complete Personal Investigation practical work
- Finalise related study writing
- Submit Component 1 (typically December-January)
- Begin preparation for Externally Set Assignment

## The Externally Set Assignment (ESA)

The ESA provides a common assessment point across all specifications, issued February 1st each year with submission typically in early May.

### Structure and Timing

Students receive a question paper containing 8-10 themed starting points. Following a preparatory period (typically 8-10 weeks), students complete supervised time:
- **15 hours supervised time** (can be consecutive or distributed)
- Work produced must demonstrate all four AOs
- Preparatory work submitted alongside supervised outcome

### Pedagogical Approach

Leslie Cunliffe emphasises that the ESA "tests students' ability to work under temporal constraint whilst maintaining artistic integrity and personal voice." Effective preparation involves:

- Analysing all starting points before committing to one
- Rapid ideation and experimentation techniques
- Time management within the 15-hour constraint
- Rehearsal of final outcome approaches

John Steers warns against "formulaic preparation" where schools over-prescribe responses, arguing this "undermines the authentic assessment of independent artistic thinking."

## Curriculum Design Considerations

### Breadth vs. Depth

Key Stage 5 specifications require schools to make pedagogical decisions about specialism. Rachel Mason (2016) identifies tension between:
- **Specialist pathways** (Fine Art, Photography, Textiles) offering deeper technical and conceptual development
- **Broader titles** (Art, Craft and Design) maintaining material diversity

Research by Steers and Swift (2009) suggests specialist pathways correlate with stronger progression to higher education in art and design disciplines, though this reflects self-selection as much as curriculum design.

### Independent Learning Development

Eileen Adams emphasises that Key Stage 5 represents "the crucial bridge to higher education studio practice" requiring explicit teaching of:
- Self-directed project management
- Research methodologies (primary and secondary)
- Critical reflection practices
- Professional presentation standards

Effective provision includes structured independent study time within the timetable (typically 50% contact time, 50% independent work for A Level subjects).

### Contextual Studies Integration

All specifications require contextual understanding, but approaches vary:

**Embedded model:** Contextual study emerges from practical inquiry, with students researching artists/designers relevant to their developing work.

**Parallel model:** Discrete art history/theory teaching runs alongside practical work, with students making connections.

**Integrated model:** Each project combines practical and contextual objectives equally.

Jeff Adams (2005) argues the embedded model proves most effective for developing "authentic critical voices" rather than "borrowed art historical discourse."

## Resources and Studio Provision

### Technical Requirements

Effective Key Stage 5 provision requires:
- Dedicated studio space accessible beyond timetabled lessons
- Specialist equipment aligned to offered pathways (darkrooms for Photography, looms for Textiles, digital facilities for Graphic Communication)
- Health and safety protocols for independent working
- Adequate materials budgets (typically £50-100 per student annually)

### Digital Resources

Contemporary practice demands:
- Digital SLR cameras and lighting equipment
- Adobe Creative Suite or equivalent
- Large-format printing facilities
- Digital portfolios and archiving systems

The 2015 Ofqual reforms emphasised "digital literacy as integral to contemporary art and design practice" across all pathways, not solely in Graphic Communication or Photography.

## Assessment Practices and Moderation

### Internal Assessment

Specifications require teachers to mark student work against assessment objectives using detailed mark schemes. Leslie Cunliffe emphasises this represents "professional judgement informed by exemplar materials and standardisation processes."

Effective internal assessment involves:
- Formative feedback throughout development (avoiding over-direction)
- Interim grading points (typically end of Year 12, mock submission)
- Standardisation meetings within departments
- Reference to board-provided exemplar materials

### External Moderation

Examination boards moderate samples of student work (typically highest, middle, and lowest plus students near grade boundaries). John Steers notes that Art & Design moderation "maintains the subject's distinctive characteristic of holistic professional judgement" rather than atomised mark schemes.

Moderation visits or postal moderation verify centre marking accuracy, with adjustments applied if necessary.

## Progression Pathways

### Higher Education

A Level Art & Design provides entry to:
- Art and Design Foundation Diploma (Level 3/4 transitional qualification)
- Specialist BA(Hons) degrees (Fine Art, Graphic Design, Fashion, etc.)
- Architecture and spatial design courses
- Art History and Theory degrees

Rachel Mason (2010) identifies that "the reflective and investigative practices established at A Level directly prefigure the independent inquiry expected in undergraduate study."

### Apprenticeships and Employment

Alternative progressions include:
- Creative industries apprenticeships (Graphic Design, Digital Marketing, Fashion)
- Direct employment in craft-based practices
- Technical roles in galleries, museums, and cultural sector

### Portfolio Development

Students progressing to Foundation or degree courses require portfolio preparation beyond A Level submission. This typically involves:
- Diversifying technical range
- Demonstrating conceptual ambition
- Professional photography and presentation
- Understanding of course-specific requirements

## Current Issues and Debates

### Decoupling of AS from A Level

The 2015 reforms made AS standalone rather than contributing to A Level grades. Steers (2016) argued this change reduced flexibility and disadvantaged students from lower socioeconomic backgrounds who might benefit from AS as an exit point. However, some evidence suggests this increased coherence in two-year course design.

### Take-up Patterns and Equity

DfE statistics show Art & Design A Level entries declined approximately 15% between 2015-2020, mirroring wider creative subjects decline. Factors include:
- EBacc accountability pressures at Key Stage 4 affecting progression
- Perceived lower value of creative qualifications in post-2008 economic climate
- Resource intensity in school contexts favouring "cheaper" subjects

Eileen Adams (2017) argues this represents "cultural impoverishment" with disproportionate impact on students from disadvantaged backgrounds who lack alternative access to arts participation.

### Technological Change

Jeff Adams (2019) identifies "the challenge of maintaining critical making practices in an increasingly screen-mediated cultural landscape." Specifications have evolved to recognise digital practice whilst maintaining emphasis on material exploration and experimentation.

### Written Component Debates

The requirement for extended writing (1,000-3,000 words) generates ongoing debate. Proponents argue it develops critical literacy essential for higher education; critics suggest it disadvantages students with specific learning differences or English as an additional language. Rachel Mason advocates for "multimodal critical responses" incorporating visual, verbal, and digital communication forms.

## Effective Teaching Strategies

### Demonstration and Modelling

While Key Stage 5 emphasises independence, Cunliffe stresses that "advanced technical skills require explicit teaching through demonstration." Effective provision includes:
- Technical workshops (printmaking, life drawing, digital processes)
- Visiting artists and practitioners
- Gallery educator collaborations

### Differentiation

Supporting diverse learners involves:
- Scaffolded research frameworks for less confident students
- Extension challenges for most able (theoretical reading, complex techniques)
- Reasonable adjustments for SEND students (modified word counts, alternative recording methods)

### Formative Feedback

Steers emphasises "dialogic feedback" where teachers facilitate reflection rather than direct outcomes. Effective practices include:
- Critique sessions modelled on art school practice
- Written feedback focusing on conceptual development
- Tutorial systems supporting individual trajectories
