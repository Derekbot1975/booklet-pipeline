# Art & Design at Key Stage 3

## Overview and National Curriculum Requirements

Art & Design at Key Stage 3 (Years 7-9, ages 11-14) represents a critical transitional phase in young people's creative education. The National Curriculum for England (2014) establishes that students should build upon their primary education to develop their "technique, including their control and their use of materials, with creativity, experimentation and an increasing awareness of different kinds of art, craft and design."

The statutory programmes of study stipulate that pupils should be taught to:
- Develop their creativity and ideas, and increase proficiency in their execution
- Use different techniques, media and materials creatively and effectively
- Develop skills in drawing, painting, sculpture and other art, craft and design techniques
- Increase their proficiency in the handling of different materials
- Analyse and evaluate their own work, and that of others, to strengthen the visual impact or applications of their work
- Know about great artists, craft makers and designers, and understand the historical and cultural development of their art forms

John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), emphasises that Key Stage 3 is where students should develop "visual literacy and critical thinking skills that will serve them throughout their lives, whether they continue with art or not."

## Transition from Key Stage 2

### Addressing the Transition Gap

Eileen Adams, in her work on environmental and community-based art education, has consistently highlighted the importance of recognising students' prior learning from primary school. The transition from Key Stage 2 to Key Stage 3 often represents what Leslie Cunliffe describes as a "regression in creative confidence" if not carefully managed.

**Practical implications for teachers:**
- Conduct baseline assessments that genuinely value primary school achievements
- Maintain emphasis on playfulness and experimentation while introducing more sophisticated concepts
- Recognise that Year 7 students arrive with varying experiences depending on their primary schools
- Build upon KS2 learning about drawing, painting, sculpture, pattern, texture, line, shape, form and space
- Acknowledge students' existing knowledge of great artists, architects and designers

### Building Progressive Skills

Rachel Mason's research on international perspectives in art education demonstrates that effective KS3 curricula balance continuity with challenge. Teachers should plan schemes of work that:
- Extend technical skills systematically across the three years
- Introduce more complex conceptual frameworks progressively
- Deepen understanding of artistic contexts and movements
- Develop increasingly sophisticated critical vocabularies

## Curriculum Design and Content

### Core Disciplines and Media

Key Stage 3 should provide breadth across multiple disciplines. The curriculum must include:

**Drawing:** As the fundamental discipline, drawing should be taught as both an investigative tool and an end product. This includes observational drawing, experimental mark-making, and conceptual drawing approaches.

**Painting:** Progressing from basic colour mixing to understanding colour theory, application techniques, and the expressive potential of different painting media (acrylic, watercolour, gouache).

**Printmaking:** Introduction to relief printing, monoprinting, and potentially screen printing, developing understanding of repetition, edition and matrix.

**Sculpture and 3D:** Working with clay, construction materials, found objects, and potentially digital fabrication tools, exploring form, space, structure and surface.

**Digital Media:** Photography, digital drawing, image manipulation, and potentially animation or graphic design, reflecting contemporary practice.

**Textiles:** Fabric manipulation, surface pattern, constructed textiles, recognising this as a contemporary fine art medium, not merely craft.

### Critical and Contextual Studies

Jeff Adams, in his writings on digital visual culture and art education, argues that contextual understanding must be integrated throughout practical work, not isolated as separate "artist research" tasks. Effective approaches include:

- Studying diverse artists, movements and cultures relevant to practical projects
- Analysing work from Western and non-Western traditions equally
- Examining contemporary practice alongside historical precedents
- Developing critical frameworks for discussing visual culture broadly
- Connecting art to students' lived experiences and popular culture

**Historical and cultural breadth should encompass:**
- Traditional fine art movements (Renaissance, Impressionism, Cubism, etc.)
- Modernism and Postmodernism
- Contemporary installation, performance and conceptual art
- Design history (graphic design, product design, architecture)
- Global art traditions (African, Asian, Indigenous, Islamic art)
- Craft traditions and their contemporary revival
- Popular visual culture and its relationship to fine art

## Assessment Without Levels

### Post-2014 Assessment Approaches

Since the removal of National Curriculum levels in 2014, schools have developed varied approaches to KS3 assessment. John Steers has argued that this provides opportunity for more authentic, formative assessment focused on developing students as artists rather than ranking them numerically.

**Effective assessment practices include:**

**Formative Assessment:**
- Regular dialogue about work in progress
- Peer assessment structured around clear criteria
- Self-reflection embedded throughout projects
- Development of metacognitive awareness about creative processes

**Summative Assessment:**
- Assessment against the National Curriculum aims
- Subject-specific criteria related to investigation, experimentation, refinement and presentation
- Recognition of conceptual development alongside technical skill
- Portfolio assessment across multiple projects

Many schools have adopted models that assess:
- **Technical skills** (media handling, technique proficiency)
- **Creative thinking** (experimentation, risk-taking, originality)
- **Critical understanding** (analysis, contextual awareness, evaluation)
- **Development and refinement** (sustained investigation, improvement)

### Recording and Reporting Progress

Without levels, schools must articulate progress descriptively. Effective approaches describe what students can do and identify next steps, rather than assigning numbers or grades at KS3. Some schools align KS3 descriptors with GCSE assessment objectives (AOs) to ensure continuity, though this must be age-appropriate and not simply "GCSE-lite."

## Curriculum Time and Resourcing

### The Time Allocation Challenge

Research by NSEAD consistently shows that curriculum time for Art & Design at KS3 has decreased in many schools since 2010. Many secondary schools provide only one hour per week in Years 7 and 8, with some reducing or removing Art entirely in Year 9 to focus on "options."

Leslie Cunliffe and others have documented how this time poverty impacts:
- Depth of skill development
- Ability to complete sustained projects
- Students' perception of the subject's importance
- Preparedness for GCSE study

**Practical strategies for teachers working within constraints:**
- Design projects that can meaningfully progress in 50-60 minute lessons
- Develop starter activities that enable quick engagement
- Create efficient classroom systems for resource distribution and clearing away
- Use homework strategically for gallery visits, research and sketchbook development
- Collaborate across subjects where possible (cross-curricular projects)

### Resources and Facilities

Quality art education requires appropriate resources. Essential provision includes:
- Specialist art rooms with appropriate storage, water access and display space
- Basic media: drawing materials, paint, clay, printmaking equipment
- Digital facilities: cameras, tablets, computers with imaging software
- Reference materials: art books, online resources, image collections
- Access to exhibitions and cultural venues (physical or virtual)

## Planning Schemes of Work

### Project-Based Learning

KS3 Art & Design typically operates through extended projects lasting 6-12 weeks. Eileen Adams advocates for projects that connect to students' lives and communities, making learning meaningful beyond the classroom.

**Effective project structures include:**

**Introduction Phase (1-2 lessons):**
- Contextual introduction to theme, artists and concepts
- Skills-based workshops introducing key techniques
- Mind-mapping and initial idea generation

**Development Phase (4-6 lessons):**
- Primary research (observation, photography, drawing)
- Experimentation with multiple media and approaches
- Refinement of ideas through testing and evaluation
- Regular formative feedback and dialogue

**Realisation Phase (2-3 lessons):**
- Production of resolved outcomes
- Refinement and presentation
- Evaluation and reflection

**Review Phase (1 lesson):**
- Peer and self-assessment
- Class critique and discussion
- Connection to future learning

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Rachel Mason's work on inclusive art education emphasises that quality teaching addresses diverse needs through:

- **Multiple entry points:** Projects accessible at different levels of prior experience
- **Choice:** Opportunities for students to direct their learning within frameworks
- **Various outcomes:** Accepting different interpretations and resolutions
- **Scaffolding:** Providing structure that can be adjusted or removed
- **Extension:** Challenging more confident students through additional complexity

Students with SEND may require:
- Adapted materials (larger brushes, adapted scissors, alternative media)
- Additional support with fine motor skills
- Visual instructions alongside verbal explanations
- Assistive technology for digital work
- Modified project requirements that maintain creative agency

## Sketchbooks and Working Processes

### The Role of Sketchbooks

Sketchbooks are central to KS3 Art & Design practice, functioning as spaces for investigation, experimentation and reflection rather than neat presentation folders. Jeff Adams cautions against sketchbooks becoming "performative" documents created primarily for assessment rather than genuine exploration.

**Effective sketchbook practice involves:**
- Regular, frequent use (not just homework tasks)
- Genuine experimentation and risk-taking
- Visual and written analysis of artists' work
- Recording from observation (drawing, photography, collecting)
- Testing materials and techniques
- Reflection on process and progress
- Personal response and creative thinking

**Teachers should encourage:**
- Variety in mark-making, media and approaches
- Annotation that demonstrates thinking, not just description
- Use of the sketchbook as a genuine thinking tool
- Acceptance of "mistakes" and learning from them
- Individual approaches to layout and organisation

## Literacy and Oracy in Art & Design

### Developing Subject-Specific Language

Art & Design makes substantial contributions to literacy development through:
- Specialist vocabulary (formal elements, media names, technical terms)
- Descriptive and analytical writing
- Comparative analysis
- Evaluation and justification of decisions

**Key terminology students should develop includes:**
- **Formal elements:** line, tone, shape, form, pattern, texture, colour, space, composition
- **Media and techniques:** specific names for materials, tools and processes
- **Conceptual language:** abstract, representational, expressive, symbolic, etc.
- **Critical vocabulary:** influence, interpretation, response, movement, style

### Talk and Discussion

John Steers emphasises the importance of "visual literacy" developed through structured discussion. Effective KS3 teaching includes:
- Whole-class critiques of work in progress
- Paired peer assessment conversations
- Presentations of work and ideas
- Small-group problem-solving discussions
- Questioning and justification of creative decisions

## Cross-Curricular Links and STEAM

### Art & Design Across the Curriculum

Eileen Adams' work on cross-curricular learning demonstrates that Art & Design naturally connects to multiple subjects:

**English:** Visual literacy, narrative, symbolism, interpretation, creative writing responses
**History:** Understanding historical periods through visual culture, propaganda analysis
**Geography:** Environmental art, mapping, sense of place
**Science:** Observation, pattern in nature, anatomy, colour theory, materials science
**Mathematics:** Perspective, proportion, symmetry, tessellation, geometry
**Computing:** Digital imaging, animation, programming generative art
**Design & Technology:** Product design, architecture, human-centred design

### STEAM Education

The STEAM movement (Science, Technology, Engineering, Art, Mathematics) positions Art & Design as integral to innovation and problem-solving. At KS3, this might include:
- Design thinking projects
- Biomimicry explorations
- Data visualisation
- Architectural design challenges
- Sustainable design projects

## Preparing for GCSE

### Year 9 as Foundation

While KS3 should have intrinsic value, Year 9 inevitably prepares students for potential GCSE study. This preparation involves:

**Understanding GCSE structure:**
- Familiarity with coursework portfolios and externally set assignments
- Experience with sustained projects over multiple weeks
- Practice with presenting and annotating investigations

**Developing independence:**
- Self-directed research and investigation
- Taking creative risks and solving problems independently
- Managing time and meeting deadlines
- Making informed decisions about media and approaches

**Building technical confidence:**
- Proficiency in multiple media to inform GCSE choices
- Understanding personal strengths and preferences
- Experience with both 2D and 3D disciplines

### GCSE Specifications Overview

All major exam boards (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure GCSE Art & Design around similar principles:

- **Component 1:** Portfolio of practical work (60% typically)
- **Component 2:** Externally set assignment (40% typically)

**Assessment objectives across all boards:**
- **AO1:** Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual understanding
- **AO2:** Refine work through experimentation and analysis
- **AO3:** Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions
- **AO4:** Present personal and meaningful response demonstrating critical understanding

KS3 teaching should authentically develop these capacities without prematurely imposing GCSE formats.

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Digital Technology

Jeff Adams argues that digital media should be integrated throughout curricula, not isolated as a separate discipline. Contemporary KS3 practice might include:
- Smartphones as research and documentation tools
- Tablets for digital drawing and painting
- Photography as a primary investigative medium
- Digital presentation of work (websites, blogs, digital portfolios)
- Social media for gallery visits and artist engagement

However, teachers must balance digital integration with maintaining hands-on, material engagement that develops different kinds of understanding.

### Decolonising the Curriculum

Contemporary art education recognises the need to diversify whose work is studied. This involves:
- Actively seeking artists from diverse backgrounds
- Questioning Western-centric art historical narratives
- Examining power structures in art institutions
- Ensuring students from all backgrounds see themselves represented
- Avoiding cultural appropriation while learning from global traditions

### Sustainability and Materials

Environmental concerns increasingly influence art education:
- Responsible use and disposal of materials
- Exploring sustainable and recycled media
- Projects addressing environmental themes
- Understanding the environmental impact of artistic production

## The Role of Cultural Institutions

### Gallery and Museum Learning

Engagement with original artworks is vital for deep learning. Effective partnerships with cultural institutions include:
- Structured gallery visits with focused learning objectives
- Artist-led workshops in schools
- Digital access to collections (Google Arts & Culture, gallery websites)
- Use of handling collections and loan boxes
- Virtual tours and online learning resources

Eileen Adams' research demonstrates that meaningful gallery engagement requires preparation and follow-up, not one-off visits in isolation.

## Professional Development and Teacher Support

### Maintaining Subject Knowledge

Art & Design teachers benefit from:
- Engagement with their own creative practice
- Attending exhibitions and arts events
- Following contemporary artists and designers
- Participating in CPD through organisations like NSEAD
- Networking with other practitioners through social media and subject associations

### Key Professional Resources

- **NSEAD (National Society for Education in Art and Design):** Subject association providing advocacy, CPD, publications
- **Engage:** Gallery education organisation supporting teachers
- **AccessArt:** Online resource bank with progressive projects
- **Artists' professional organisations:** Providing insight into contemporary practice
- **University education departments:** Research informing practice

## Conclusion: The Value of KS3 Art & Design

John Steers argues that Art & Design education at KS3 develops capacities valuable far beyond the subject itself: visual literacy, creative problem-solving, critical thinking, and the confidence to experiment and take risks. Leslie Cunliffe emphasises that these years should kindle lifelong engagement with visual culture, whether students continue to GCSE or not.

Quality KS3 Art & Design education:
- Values creativity and originality
- Develops technical skills across multiple media
- Builds cultural understanding and critical awareness
- Encourages experimentation and learning from mistakes
- Connects to students' lives and contemporary visual culture
- Prepares students for further study while having intrinsic worth
- Contributes to whole-person education

In an increasingly visual world, the skills, knowledge and dispositions developed through Art & Design at Key Stage 3 are more relevant than ever. Teachers must advocate for adequate curriculum time, resources and recognition of the subject's unique contribution to young people's education.
