# Art & Design National Curriculum Overview

## Introduction to Art & Design in the National Curriculum

The Art & Design National Curriculum in England represents a fundamental entitlement for all pupils to develop creative, aesthetic and cultural understanding. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has argued, art and design education develops "visual literacy—the ability to both read and create visual meaning" which is essential in our increasingly image-saturated world.

The current National Curriculum framework was established in 2014, with art and design specified as a foundation subject at Key Stages 1, 2 and 3. It is notably lighter in prescription than previous iterations, giving schools greater autonomy whilst maintaining core statutory requirements.

## Statutory Requirements by Key Stage

### Key Stage 1 (Years 1-2)

The statutory programme of study requires pupils to:

- Use a range of materials creatively to design and make products
- Use drawing, painting and sculpture to develop and share their ideas, experiences and imagination
- Develop a wide range of art and design techniques in using colour, pattern, texture, line, shape, form and space
- Learn about the work of a range of artists, craft makers and designers, describing differences and similarities between different practices and disciplines, making links to their own work

**Practical Implications**: Teachers should establish foundational vocabulary around the formal elements whilst encouraging experimental, play-based approaches. Eileen Adams, in her work on environmental education through art, emphasised the importance of direct observation and first-hand experience at this stage, arguing that "children need opportunities to look closely, to touch, to explore through all their senses."

### Key Stage 2 (Years 3-6)

Pupils should be taught to:

- Create sketch books to record their observations and use them to review and revisit ideas
- Improve their mastery of art and design techniques, including drawing, painting and sculpture with a range of materials (for example, pencil, charcoal, paint, clay)
- Learn about great artists, architects and designers in history

**Practical Implications**: The introduction of sketchbooks as a statutory requirement at KS2 represents a significant progression. Rachel Mason's research into assessment in art education highlights the sketchbook as "a crucial site for formative assessment and pupil self-reflection." Teachers should establish sketchbooks as working documents rather than pristine portfolios, modelling risk-taking and experimentation.

The requirement to study artists "in history" has generated debate. Leslie Cunliffe has argued for broader interpretation, stating that "historical context should not be limited to European canonical artists but should encompass diverse cultural traditions and contemporary practitioners who help children understand art's relevance today."

### Key Stage 3 (Years 7-9)

Pupils should be taught to:

- Use a range of techniques to record their observations in sketchbooks, journals and other media as a basis for exploring their ideas
- Use a range of techniques and media, including painting
- Increase their proficiency in the handling of different materials
- Analyse and evaluate their own work, and that of others, in order to strengthen the visual impact or applications of their work
- Learn about the history of art, craft, design and architecture, including periods, styles and major movements from ancient times up to the present day

**Practical Implications**: KS3 represents significant deepening of skills and critical engagement. Jeff Adams, writing about digital technologies in art education, notes that KS3 is where "pupils can begin to understand art and design as a system of communication and cultural production, not merely personal expression."

The requirement for analysis and evaluation should develop critical literacy. John Steers advocates for the "critical studies" approach pioneered by Rod Taylor, integrating practical work with contextual understanding through making, investigating, and appraising.

## Attainment Targets

The 2014 curriculum specifies that by the end of each key stage, pupils are expected to know, apply and understand the matters, skills and processes specified in the relevant programme of study. Unlike earlier iterations, it does not include level descriptors, giving schools responsibility for determining progression models.

This shift reflects what Rachel Mason termed "assessment for learning" principles, where formative assessment drives pedagogical decisions rather than summative level reporting. However, this has created challenges for consistency across schools.

## Beyond Key Stage 3: GCSE and A-Level

While not statutory beyond KS3, most secondary schools offer art and design qualifications at KS4 and KS5.

### GCSE Art & Design (KS4, Years 10-11)

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) offer GCSE Art & Design specifications aligned to the Department for Education's subject content requirements published in 2016. These require:

- Two assessed components: Portfolio (60%) and Externally Set Assignment (40%)
- Assessment against four Assessment Objectives (AO1-AO4):
  - AO1: Develop ideas through contextual investigation (25%)
  - AO2: Refine work through experimentation (25%)
  - AO3: Record ideas and observations (25%)
  - AO4: Present a personal response (25%)

Students typically choose from endorsed titles including Fine Art, Graphic Communication, Textile Design, Three-Dimensional Design, and Photography.

**Board Variations**: Whilst assessment objectives are consistent, boards differ in their emphases. AQA's approach tends toward portfolio-based working with sustained projects. OCR offers more distinct pathways within Fine Art. Edexcel has reputation for clear assessment guidance. WJEC Eduqas is used predominantly in Wales but available in England.

### A-Level Art & Design (KS5, Years 12-13)

A-Level specifications follow similar structures to GCSE but require:

- Greater independence in project development
- More sophisticated critical and contextual understanding
- Higher technical proficiency
- Personal Investigation (60%) including 1,000-3,000 word written element
- Externally Set Assignment (40%)

Assessment Objectives remain consistent with GCSE but demand is significantly higher. The written element introduced in 2015 reforms has been controversial; John Steers questioned whether it "privileges academic writing skills over visual communication," though boards and teachers have generally found it develops research and analytical capabilities.

## Curriculum Design Considerations

### Breadth and Balance

Eileen Adams has long advocated for breadth across art, craft and design. Schools should ensure curriculum encompasses:

- **Art**: Drawing, painting, printmaking, sculpture
- **Craft**: Ceramics, textiles, jewellery, constructed objects
- **Design**: Graphics, product design, fashion, environmental/architectural awareness
- **Digital media**: Photography, digital imaging, moving image, interactive design

Leslie Cunliffe warns against "painting-dominated curricula" where drawing and painting overshadow three-dimensional and applied art forms, arguing this "privileges fine art traditions and marginalises design thinking and craft knowledge."

### Cultural Diversity

The curriculum's reference to "a range of artists" and "different periods, styles and major movements" requires thoughtful interpretation. Rachel Mason's research on multiculturalism in art education advocates for "authentic engagement with diverse cultural traditions rather than tokenistic inclusion."

Teachers should:

- Move beyond Eurocentric canons (Picasso, Monet, Van Gogh repertoire)
- Include artists from African, Asian, Indigenous, and non-Western traditions
- Address contemporary artists reflecting Britain's diversity
- Examine how power structures affect whose work is valued and exhibited
- Explore local and regional artistic traditions

### Progression Models

Without prescribed levels, schools must develop coherent progression frameworks. John Steers suggests progression should be evident in:

1. **Technical proficiency**: Increasing control and skill with materials and processes
2. **Visual language**: Growing sophistication in use of formal elements
3. **Idea generation**: From teacher-led to self-initiated projects
4. **Critical understanding**: From description to analysis, interpretation and evaluation
5. **Independence**: From scaffolded tasks to autonomous investigation

Jeff Adams emphasises that digital progression should parallel traditional media, with pupils developing from "tool users to creative practitioners who understand software as a creative medium, not merely a finish."

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Demonstrating and Modelling

Teachers should regularly demonstrate techniques and model creative processes. Rachel Mason's work on teacher expertise shows that "subject knowledge demonstrated through making has powerful impact on pupil confidence and technical development."

### Project-Based Learning

Sustained projects allowing investigation, development and refinement are central to art and design pedagogy. Projects should:

- Begin with first-hand observation or experience
- Include contextual research and artist study
- Allow experimentation with multiple techniques and media
- Build toward personal outcomes
- Incorporate reflection and evaluation

### Sketchbooks and Visual Journals

Since their statutory introduction at KS2, sketchbooks have become central to art and design pedagogy. Leslie Cunliffe describes them as "spaces for thinking, not just recording" where pupils should:

- Collect visual research and observations
- Experiment with materials and techniques
- Develop and annotate ideas
- Make connections between their work and contextual sources
- Reflect on process and development

### Critical and Contextual Studies

Integration of making and studying art history is pedagogical ideal. The "critical studies" model, as articulated by John Steers, suggests pupils should:

1. **Look and respond**: Observe artworks closely, developing visual literacy
2. **Question and investigate**: Research contexts, meanings, intentions
3. **Make connections**: Link to their own practice and other learning
4. **Create responses**: Make work informed by their investigations

This approach challenges outdated models where "theory" and "practice" are separated; instead, contextual understanding should inform and arise from practical work.

## Cross-Curricular Links and SMSC

Art and design contributes significantly to pupils' spiritual, moral, social and cultural (SMSC) development:

- **Spiritual**: Through creative expression, aesthetic experience, and wonder
- **Moral**: Examining themes of social justice, environmental concern, identity
- **Social**: Collaborative projects, understanding art's social functions
- **Cultural**: Engaging with diverse cultural traditions and contemporary cultural production

Eileen Adams' work on "learning through landscapes" demonstrates how art connects to geography, science, and environmental education. Jeff Adams explores connections to media studies and digital literacy.

## Assessment in Art & Design

### Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment should focus on:

- Dialogue and questioning during making
- Sketchbook reviews and discussions
- Peer assessment and critique sessions
- Target-setting based on individual development

Rachel Mason emphasises assessment should be "developmental rather than judgmental," supporting next steps rather than merely measuring achievement.

### Summative Assessment

Schools must determine own systems for reporting achievement. Many adopt approaches using:

- Working towards/achieving/exceeding expected standards
- Subject-specific descriptors based on programme of study objectives
- Portfolio reviews at transition points

At GCSE and A-Level, Assessment Objectives provide clear criteria, though John Steers notes that "holistic judgment of creative work always involves professional interpretation beyond mechanistic mark schemes."

### Assessment Without Levels

The removal of levels in 2014 has proven liberating for many art departments, allowing assessment focused on artistic development rather than reductive grading. However, Leslie Cunliffe notes this requires "confident subject leadership and robust internal moderation to ensure rigour and progression."

## Resources and Professional Support

### Subject Associations

- **NSEAD** (National Society for Education in Art and Design): Primary professional association, offering publications, conferences, and advocacy
- **engage** (National Association for Gallery Education): Supporting gallery-school partnerships

### Publications and Frameworks

- *International Journal of Art & Design Education* (iJADE): Academic research journal
- NSEAD's *AD Magazine*: Practical teaching resource
- *Engaging Places* (CABE/RIBA): Architecture education resource
- Tate, National Gallery, British Museum education resources

### Continuing Professional Development

John Steers advocates for subject-specific CPD, arguing that "generic teaching and learning approaches, whilst valuable, cannot substitute for deepening subject knowledge and pedagogical content knowledge specific to art and design."

Teachers should engage with:

- Gallery and museum teacher programmes
- Subject association conferences and networks
- Artist residencies and partnerships
- Material and technical skills workshops
- Critical and theoretical reading in art education

## Contemporary Challenges and Debates

### Curriculum Time and Status

Despite statutory requirement through KS3, art and design has faced:

- Reduced curriculum time, particularly at KS3
- Pressure from EBacc subjects which exclude arts
- Some schools making art optional in Year 9

John Steers has been vocal that such decisions undermine entitlement and "create cultural poverty, denying pupils access to ways of knowing and communicating fundamental to human experience."

### The Digital Divide

Jeff Adams argues that whilst digital technologies offer "extraordinary creative opportunities," there remain "significant equity issues around access to hardware, software, and teacher expertise." Schools must ensure digital media enriches rather than replaces material practices.

### Examination Reform Impacts

The 2015-2016 GCSE and A-Level reforms increased academic demand, particularly through written components. Whilst this has validated art and design's intellectual rigour, there are concerns about:

- Impact on practical time
- Advantage to verbally articulate students
- Whether written components truly assess visual understanding

Rachel Mason suggests the challenge is ensuring "writing serves visual thinking rather than supplanting it."

### Diversity and Decolonisation

Contemporary discourse increasingly addresses whose art is taught and valued. This involves:

- Examining canonical curricula that privilege Western, predominantly white male artists
- Including diverse voices and perspectives
- Addressing historical contexts of colonialism and cultural appropriation
- Ensuring teaching staff reflect diversity
- Creating inclusive environments where all pupils see themselves represented

## Conclusion

The Art & Design National Curriculum provides framework for developing visual literacy, creative capacity, and cultural understanding. Its relative openness since 2014 offers opportunities for innovative, responsive curriculum design whilst requiring strong subject leadership.

As Eileen Adams argues, art education's value extends beyond producing artists: "It develops ways of seeing, thinking and communicating essential to navigating our visual world." The challenge for teachers and curriculum leaders is realising this potential through coherent, ambitious, and inclusive curriculum provision that honours both the discipline's traditions and its contemporary possibilities.

The expertise of teachers, supported by robust subject associations and sustained professional development, remains crucial. As John Steers consistently emphasises, "Quality art and design education depends ultimately on teachers who are themselves engaged with art, who understand its power and possibilities, and who can ignite that engagement in young people."
