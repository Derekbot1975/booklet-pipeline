# Art & Design in EYFS

## Introduction

Art and Design in the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) forms a crucial foundation for children's creative, cognitive and physical development. Whilst not explicitly designated as a discrete curriculum area in the EYFS statutory framework, creative development through artistic exploration is embedded throughout the curriculum, particularly within the prime and specific areas of learning. The EYFS framework (revised 2021) positions creative expression as integral to child development from birth to five years, recognising that young children's engagement with visual, tactile and creative experiences builds essential skills for lifelong learning.

## EYFS Statutory Framework Context

### The EYFS Framework (2021) Structure

The current EYFS statutory framework, implemented in September 2021, organises learning into three prime areas and four specific areas. Art and Design activities primarily support:

**Prime Areas:**
- **Physical Development** – developing fine and gross motor skills through mark-making, manipulating materials, and using tools
- **Personal, Social and Emotional Development** – building confidence, expressing ideas, and developing characteristics of effective learning

**Specific Areas:**
- **Expressive Arts and Design** – the primary home for artistic development, focusing on creating with materials and being imaginative and expressive
- **Communication and Language** – discussing creative work, developing vocabulary related to art and design
- **Understanding the World** – exploring materials, their properties, and observing the natural and built environment

### Early Learning Goals (ELGs)

The Early Learning Goal for **Creating with Materials** states that children should:
"Safely use and explore a variety of materials, tools and techniques, experimenting with colour, design, texture, form and function. Share their creations, explaining the process they have used."

This ELG emphasises both the making process and the articulation of creative thinking, establishing foundations for later Art and Design education.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Key Thinkers and Educational Philosophy

**Eileen Adams** has extensively researched environmental education and design awareness in early childhood. Her work emphasises the importance of observational drawing and engaging children with their built and natural environments from the earliest stages. Adams argues that young children are naturally attuned to visual and spatial qualities and that EYFS provision should capitalise on this innate curiosity through structured yet exploratory encounters with design in everyday contexts.

**John Steers**, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently advocated for the distinctive contribution of art and design to early childhood education. Steers argues against the marginalisation of creative subjects, emphasising that artistic development in EYFS is not merely decorative or therapeutic but constitutes genuine knowledge construction and cognitive development.

**Rachel Mason's** research into art education internationally provides important context for understanding quality indicators in EYFS art provision. Mason emphasises the importance of cultural diversity in early years art experiences, arguing that children should encounter varied artistic traditions, techniques and aesthetic values from their earliest educational experiences.

### The Reggio Emilia Influence

The Reggio Emilia approach, developed in Northern Italy, has profoundly influenced EYFS art and design pedagogy in the UK. This philosophy views children as competent, capable learners and positions the environment as the "third teacher." Key principles adopted in UK EYFS settings include:

- **The Hundred Languages of Children** – recognising multiple modes of expression, with visual arts as a primary language
- **Documentation** – making learning visible through careful observation and display of creative processes
- **The atelier and atelierista** – dedicated creative spaces and specialised practitioners who support sustained artistic investigation
- **Long-term projects** – extended explorations allowing depth and revisiting of ideas

## Developmental Progression in EYFS Art and Design

### Birth to Three Years

During this period, artistic development is primarily sensory and exploratory:

**Key Characteristics:**
- Random mark-making and enjoying movement for its own sake
- Exploring materials through all senses (mouthing, touching, smelling)
- Beginning to notice visual effects created through action (e.g., paint mixing)
- Responding emotionally to colours, patterns and textures
- Enjoying repetitive actions with materials

**Appropriate Provision:**
- Large-scale mark-making opportunities (vertical and horizontal surfaces)
- Safe, non-toxic materials for sensory exploration (natural materials, treasure baskets)
- Cause-and-effect experiences with materials
- Rich visual environments with patterns, colours and natural objects
- Adult co-participation and commentary on children's explorations

### Three to Four Years

Children develop increasing intentionality and representational thinking:

**Key Characteristics:**
- Beginning to use marks intentionally to represent objects or ideas
- Developing preferences for particular colours, materials or techniques
- Enclosed shapes appear in mark-making (typically circular forms)
- Interest in process often exceeds interest in product
- Beginning to name creations (often retrospectively)
- Increasing fine motor control enabling more detailed work

**Appropriate Provision:**
- Continuous provision of varied mark-making tools and surfaces
- Introduction to basic art vocabulary (colour names, texture words)
- Access to joining techniques (glue, tape, staplers with supervision)
- Opportunities to observe and discuss artwork from diverse cultures
- Modelling of techniques without prescribing outcomes
- Documentation and display of creative processes

### Four to Five Years (Reception)

Children demonstrate increasing sophistication in visual representation and design thinking:

**Key Characteristics:**
- Intentional planning of creations ("I'm going to make...")
- Development of visual schemas (repeated symbolic forms)
- Increasing detail in representations (baseline appears, objects differentiated)
- Combining techniques and materials purposefully
- Sustained engagement with creative projects over time
- Articulation of creative choices and evaluation of outcomes

**Appropriate Provision:**
- Project-based approaches allowing sustained investigation
- Introduction to artist studies appropriate for young children
- Opportunities for design thinking (planning, making, evaluating)
- High-quality tools and materials accessible independently
- Sketchbooks or journals to document creative journeys
- Gallery visits or artist interactions where possible
- Provocations that encourage creative problem-solving

## The Physical Environment for EYFS Art and Design

### Continuous Provision

Research by Leslie Cunliffe emphasises the importance of continuous, accessible art provision rather than isolated "messy play" sessions. Effective EYFS environments include:

**Creative Areas:**
- Dedicated art tables or workshop areas with constant access
- Organisation systems children can navigate independently
- Display of current projects and works-in-progress
- Inspiration stations with visual references (nature objects, postcards, fabric samples)
- Mark-making opportunities across the environment (clipboards, whiteboards, chalk boards)

**Material Organisation:**
- Clear containers allowing children to see contents
- Logical grouping of materials by type or colour
- Accessible storage at child height with visual labels
- Rotation of materials to maintain interest and introduce new techniques
- High-quality tools properly maintained (sharp scissors, good brushes)

### Indoor and Outdoor Provision

The EYFS framework emphasises learning outdoors as essential, not supplementary. Art and design should extend throughout the learning environment:

**Outdoor Creative Opportunities:**
- Large-scale mark-making (water painting, chalks, natural materials)
- Environmental art using found objects
- Observational drawing from nature
- Exploration of natural pigments and materials
- Building and constructing with diverse materials
- Shadow and light investigations

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Adult Roles and Interactions

The practitioner's role in EYFS art and design is sophisticated, requiring a balance between facilitation and expertise. Effective practice includes:

**Observation and Assessment:**
- Using observations to understand children's interests and schemas
- Documenting creative processes through photographs and annotations
- Identifying developmental progression in mark-making and representation
- Recognising individual creative "voices" and dispositions

**Skilled Interaction:**
- Participating alongside children as co-constructor rather than instructor
- Introducing technical vocabulary in context
- Asking open-ended questions that extend thinking ("I notice you've...", "What if...?")
- Modelling techniques whilst respecting children's own approaches
- Avoiding templates or models that limit creative thinking
- Valuing process over predetermined outcomes

**Planning and Enhancement:**
- Providing provocations that invite creative investigation
- Responding to observed interests with relevant resources
- Planning for progression in skills and conceptual understanding
- Creating connections between creative experiences and other learning areas
- Ensuring cultural diversity in materials, techniques and artist references

### Teaching Drawing in EYFS

John Steers and Eileen Adams have both emphasised the particular importance of drawing development in early childhood. Drawing is not merely a recreational activity but a fundamental mode of thinking and learning.

**Developmental Drawing:**
- Understanding that scribbling is meaningful exploration, not random marks
- Recognising schemas (repeated patterns) as children's theories about the world
- Supporting the transition from kinesthetic to representational drawing
- Avoiding premature pressure for "realistic" representation
- Providing diverse drawing tools (charcoal, pencils, pens, brushes)

**Observational Drawing:**
- Introducing careful looking from real objects, not images
- Starting with interesting, touchable objects children can explore
- Developing "slow looking" practices appropriate for young learners
- Making observational drawing playful rather than formal
- Celebrating individual interpretations and unique marks

## Curriculum Planning and Progression

### Characteristics of Effective Learning

The EYFS framework identifies three characteristics of effective learning that are particularly relevant to art and design:

**Playing and Exploring:**
- Finding out and exploring materials and techniques
- Playing with what children know about art materials
- Being willing to "have a go" with new creative challenges

**Active Learning:**
- Being involved and concentrating on creative projects
- Keeping trying when creative challenges arise
- Enjoying achieving what children set out to do artistically

**Creating and Thinking Critically:**
- Having their own ideas about what to make and how
- Making links between creative experiences
- Choosing ways to do things and finding new ways (developing artistic strategies)

### Long-Term Planning

Effective EYFS settings develop long-term plans that ensure breadth and progression:

**Coverage Across the Year:**
- Systematic introduction of materials (paint, clay, textiles, collage, printmaking)
- Progressive skill development (mixing, joining, shaping, pattern-making)
- Encounters with diverse artistic traditions and cultural practices
- Design projects addressing authentic purposes (making gifts, designing spaces)
- Seasonal and environmental investigations through creative response

**Integration with Topics:**
- Avoiding superficial "theme-based" art that merely illustrates topics
- Instead, using art and design as a legitimate mode of investigation
- For example: exploring colour mixing when investigating rainbows, rather than simply making rainbow pictures
- Ensuring artistic learning objectives alongside topic objectives

## Assessment in EYFS Art and Design

### Approaches to Assessment

Assessment in EYFS art and design should be formative, observation-based and focused on the characteristics of effective learning rather than normative comparisons.

**Documentation:**
- Learning journals capturing creative processes through photography
- Annotations recording children's commentary on their work
- Collections of mark-making samples showing developmental progression
- Video recordings of collaborative creative projects

**What to Assess:**
- Engagement and persistence with creative challenges
- Development of fine and gross motor skills through art activities
- Growing vocabulary related to art, design and materials
- Ability to plan, make and evaluate creations
- Experimentation with techniques and materials
- Expression of ideas through visual forms
- Cultural awareness demonstrated through creative work

**Reporting to Parents:**
- Sharing creative processes, not just finished products
- Explaining developmental progressions in mark-making
- Suggesting ways parents can support creative development at home
- Celebrating individual creative voices and approaches

## Inclusion and Diversity

### Cultural Diversity in EYFS Art

Rachel Mason's work emphasises the importance of multicultural art education from the earliest stages. Effective EYFS provision includes:

**Diverse Artist References:**
- Introducing children to artists from varied cultural backgrounds
- Exploring artistic traditions beyond Western fine art (textiles, ceramics, body art)
- Inviting community members to share artistic practices
- Using children's literature featuring diverse artistic traditions

**Inclusive Materials and Techniques:**
- Providing skin-tone colours for self-representation (beyond "pink" or "peach")
- Including tools and materials from various cultural traditions
- Exploring pattern, colour and design from global contexts
- Ensuring images and displays reflect diverse bodies, families and cultural practices

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities

Art and design in EYFS provides particularly valuable experiences for children with SEND:

**Access Considerations:**
- Adaptive tools enabling participation (grips, angled surfaces, stabilising equipment)
- Multi-sensory approaches engaging varied learning strengths
- Non-verbal modes of expression and communication
- Opportunities for success and competence in alternative domains

**Specific Needs:**
- For motor difficulties: various mark-making scales and tools
- For visual impairment: emphasis on tactile and three-dimensional work
- For communication needs: art as a primary language for expression
- For developmental delay: differentiated expectations whilst maintaining access

## Transitions and Continuity

### EYFS to Key Stage 1

The transition from EYFS to the National Curriculum at Key Stage 1 requires careful management to maintain creative development:

**Continuity Challenges:**
- Moving from integrated EYFS curriculum to discrete National Curriculum subjects
- Shift from play-based to more formal learning structures
- Increased emphasis on literacy and numeracy potentially marginalising creative work
- Different physical environments and resource access

**Supporting Smooth Transition:**
- Year 1 teachers visiting EYFS settings to observe creative practices
- Sharing EYFS profiles highlighting creative achievements
- Maintaining play-based and exploratory approaches in Year 1 art provision
- Ensuring adequate curriculum time for Art and Design in Key Stage 1
- Building on EYFS creative projects rather than starting afresh
- Continuing to value process alongside product

## Resources and Professional Development

### Essential Resources for EYFS Art Provision

**Basic Materials:**
- High-quality paint (ready-mix and powder), various brushes and tools
- Drawing materials (pencils, charcoal, pastels, felt tips, crayons)
- Collage materials (papers, fabrics, natural objects, recycled items)
- Three-dimensional materials (clay, playdough, construction materials)
- Printmaking supplies (sponges, stamps, printing blocks, inks)
- Joining materials (various glues, tapes, staplers, hole punches)

**Professional Resources:**
- NSEAD (National Society for Education in Art and Design) publications and guidance
- Early Education (British Association for Early Childhood Education) materials
- "Birth to Five Matters" guidance document (non-statutory)
- Reggio-inspired documentation resources
- Artist websites and online galleries with educational resources

### Professional Development Priorities

Teachers and practitioners benefit from CPD addressing:

- Understanding developmental progression in children's mark-making
- Pedagogical approaches balancing freedom and skill teaching
- Effectively questioning and extending creative thinking
- Curating inspirational resources and provocations
- Documentation strategies that make learning visible
- Critiquing and moving beyond template-based activities
- Understanding art and design as cognitive, not just manual, development

## Contemporary Issues and Debates

### Baseline Assessment Pressures

The introduction of the Reception Baseline Assessment (RBA) has raised concerns about pressures on EYFS practice. Art and design educators, including Jeff Adams, have highlighted that:

- Overemphasis on measurable literacy and numeracy outcomes may squeeze creative curriculum time
- The intrinsic value of creative development may be undervalued in accountability frameworks
- Play-based, exploratory approaches may be compromised by perceived academic pressures

**Maintaining Quality:**
- Asserting the statutory status of Expressive Arts and Design in the EYFS framework
- Articulating the cognitive and developmental value of artistic experiences
- Documenting and communicating creative learning achievements to parents and leaders
- Resisting premature formalisation of EYFS practice

### Digital Technology in EYFS Art

The role of digital technology in early years creative practice remains contested:

**Arguments for Digital Integration:**
- Tablets and apps offering new creative possibilities
- Digital photography enabling documentation and process reflection
- Access to diverse artistic images and virtual gallery visits
- Developing technological competencies from early stages

**Cautions and Concerns:**
- Importance of direct, sensory, material experiences in early childhood
- Risk of technology displacing tactile and three-dimensional work
- Screen time considerations in early childhood
- Ensuring technology enhances rather than replaces physical creation

**Balanced Approach:**
- Using technology purposefully to support creative learning (e.g., documentation)
- Maintaining primacy of direct material experiences
- Introducing digital creation tools alongside traditional media
- Critical consideration of age-appropriateness and developmental needs

## Conclusion

Art and Design in EYFS constitutes far more than recreational activity or peripheral embellishment to "core" learning. As articulated by John Steers and supported by research across the field, early creative experiences build essential cognitive, physical, emotional and social capacities that underpin all subsequent learning. The EYFS framework, particularly the Creating with Materials Early Learning Goal, provides statutory recognition of this importance, requiring practitioners to offer rich, sustained and progressively challenging artistic experiences.
