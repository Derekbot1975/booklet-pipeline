# SEND in Art & Design

## Introduction

Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) provision in Art & Design represents a critical dimension of inclusive practice within UK education. The Equality Act 2010 mandates that schools make reasonable adjustments to ensure pupils with SEND can access the full curriculum, whilst the SEND Code of Practice (2015) emphasises that high-quality teaching, differentiated for individual pupils, is the first step in responding to pupils who have or may have SEND.

Art & Design occupies a unique position within the curriculum for SEND learners. As John Steers has argued, the visual and tactile nature of art-making offers alternative pathways to expression and learning that can be particularly valuable for pupils who struggle with text-based subjects. Rachel Mason's research into multi-sensory learning in art education demonstrates how the subject's inherent emphasis on visual, kinaesthetic, and tactile experiences creates natural opportunities for differentiation and personalised learning.

## The National Curriculum Context

### Statutory Requirements

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2014) applies to all pupils, including those with SEND, with teachers expected to "set suitable learning challenges, respond to pupils' diverse learning needs, and overcome potential barriers to learning." The curriculum's emphasis on experimentation, exploration, and personal response aligns particularly well with differentiated approaches required for SEND provision.

Key statutory requirements include:
- Producing creative work, exploring ideas and recording experiences
- Becoming proficient in drawing, painting, sculpture and other art, craft and design techniques
- Evaluating and analysing creative works using the language of art, craft and design
- Knowing about great artists, craft makers and designers

### Access Arrangements

The curriculum acknowledges that for some pupils, modifications and adaptations may be necessary. These might include:
- Extended time for practical work
- Alternative recording methods (audio, photographic, video)
- Modified equipment and materials
- Adapted presentation formats
- Scribe or reader support for written elements

## Categories of SEND and Art & Design Provision

### Communication and Interaction Needs

Pupils with Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC), speech and language difficulties, or selective mutism often find Art & Design particularly accessible. Leslie Cunliffe's work on visual communication highlights how art-making can provide non-verbal channels for expression and communication.

**Practical strategies:**
- Use visual timetables and task sequences with photographs or diagrams
- Provide clear, concrete instructions with visual demonstrations
- Create structured workspaces with defined boundaries
- Allow time for sensory regulation through material exploration
- Offer alternative response methods (drawing, gesture, photography)
- Use visual prompts for evaluation rather than requiring verbal critique

### Cognition and Learning Needs

Pupils with moderate learning difficulties (MLD), severe learning difficulties (SLD), or specific learning difficulties such as dyslexia benefit from Art & Design's emphasis on practical, hands-on learning. Eileen Adams's research on experiential learning through art demonstrates how making processes can bypass traditional barriers to cognition.

**Practical strategies:**
- Break complex tasks into smaller, manageable steps with visual guides
- Use concrete, manipulable materials before introducing abstract concepts
- Provide multi-sensory approaches (touching, seeing, doing simultaneously)
- Allow extended processing time for idea development
- Use colour-coding and visual organisers for planning and sequencing
- Reduce literacy demands through visual resources and oral instruction
- Employ success criteria with visual exemplars

### Social, Emotional, and Mental Health Difficulties

Art & Design can be therapeutic and confidence-building for pupils with SEMH needs. Jeff Adams's work on art education and social justice explores how creative practice can support emotional regulation and self-expression.

**Practical strategies:**
- Create a calm, predictable studio environment with clear routines
- Offer choice in materials, subject matter, and working methods
- Use art-making for emotional expression and processing
- Establish clear boundaries and consistent expectations
- Provide one-to-one or small group support where needed
- Build in movement breaks and alternative activities
- Celebrate effort and process rather than just outcome
- Use reflective journals combining images and minimal text

### Sensory and Physical Needs

Pupils with visual impairment, hearing impairment, or physical disabilities require specific adaptations. Rachel Mason's research on multi-sensory art education provides frameworks for ensuring access.

**For visual impairment:**
- Provide tactile and textural materials for exploration
- Use high-contrast materials and large-scale working
- Employ verbal description techniques for artworks
- Arrange good lighting and reduce glare
- Provide enlarged or adapted visual resources
- Use 3D and relief techniques

**For hearing impairment:**
- Position pupils to see demonstrations clearly
- Use visual instructions and written summaries
- Employ signing or communication aids as required
- Reduce background noise in the art room
- Face pupils when speaking and ensure clear line of sight

**For physical disabilities:**
- Adapt tools and equipment (grips, extended handles, electric alternatives)
- Adjust table heights and seating
- Provide alternative methods (mouth-operated, eye-gaze technology)
- Employ collaborative making where pupils direct assistants
- Use stabilising equipment (non-slip mats, clamps, easels)

## Key Stage Specific Considerations

### Key Stage 1 and 2

At primary level, Art & Design offers crucial opportunities for developing fine motor skills, visual literacy, and creative confidence. The less formal, exploratory nature of primary art suits many SEND learners.

**Approaches:**
- Sensory exploration of materials builds foundational knowledge
- Process-based learning reduces performance anxiety
- Cross-curricular links support concept development
- Regular, short sessions accommodate attention difficulties
- Play-based approaches suit developmental stages
- Portfolio assessment captures progress over time

### Key Stage 3

The transition to secondary Art & Design can be challenging for SEND pupils. Maintaining differentiation whilst introducing more formal techniques requires careful planning.

**Approaches:**
- Bridge units ensure continuity from primary practice
- Skills are introduced progressively with clear success criteria
- Personal interests inform project selection
- Drawing skills development uses varied media and approaches
- Cultural studies employ visual as well as written resources
- Assessment focuses on individual progress and effort

### Key Stage 4 (GCSE)

All major exam boards (AQA, Edexcel/Pearson, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) offer GCSE Art & Design with access arrangements available through the Joint Council for Qualifications (JCQ) regulations.

**Access arrangements may include:**
- 25% extra time (or more with supporting evidence)
- Scribe or word processor for written elements
- Practical assistant for physical disabilities
- Modified papers or enlarged materials
- Separate invigilation
- Rest breaks

**Practical implications:**
- Portfolio assessment (60%) allows flexible timeframes and personalised responses
- Component 1 (sustained project) can be adapted to individual strengths
- Component 2 (externally set assignment) may require careful preparation
- Annotation requirements can be met through varied formats (audio recording transcribed, photographic documentation, reduced word counts)
- Coursework nature reduces exam pressure compared to traditional subjects

**Specific board provisions:**
- AQA offers clear guidance on alternative evidence formats in their GCSE Art & Design specification
- OCR provides exemplar materials showing adapted responses
- Edexcel/Pearson publishes accessibility guidance annually
- WJEC Eduqas includes specific SEND support in teacher resources

### Key Stage 5 (A-Level and Level 3)

A-Level Art & Design (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) continues to offer access arrangements, though the increased independence and critical expectations require robust support.

**Considerations:**
- Personal investigation (A-Level) can play to individual strengths
- Extended project timeframes allow for SEND processing needs
- 1000-word essay requirement may need access arrangements
- Critical understanding can be demonstrated through varied formats with board approval
- BTEC alternatives offer different assessment structures that may suit some SEND learners
- UAL Level 3 Extended Diploma provides portfolio-based progression

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

UDL principles align naturally with Art & Design pedagogy:
- **Multiple means of representation:** Visual, tactile, auditory approaches
- **Multiple means of action and expression:** Varied materials, techniques, and outcomes
- **Multiple means of engagement:** Choice, personal relevance, authentic purposes

### Differentiation Strategies

John Steers advocates for differentiation by:
- **Task:** Adjusting complexity, scale, or technique requirements
- **Resource:** Providing adapted tools, materials, or equipment
- **Support:** Varying adult input, peer collaboration, or scaffolding
- **Outcome:** Accepting diverse responses to the same starting point
- **Assessment:** Measuring individual progress against personalised targets

### Scaffolding and Modelling

Effective SEND provision in Art & Design employs:
- Live demonstration with think-aloud protocols
- Step-by-step visual guides left accessible during work
- Exemplar materials showing progression of skills
- Part-completed examples to build confidence
- Peer modelling and collaborative problem-solving

## Assessment and Progress Monitoring

### Formative Assessment

Ongoing assessment for SEND learners should:
- Focus on individual progress rather than age-related expectations
- Capture small-step achievements through photographic records
- Use developmental frameworks (e.g., P-Scales for pupils working below National Curriculum)
- Employ visual self-assessment tools (thumbs, faces, colour-coding)
- Include regular feedback conversations using pupils' work as stimulus

### Summative Assessment

Eileen Adams emphasises the importance of portfolio assessment for SEND pupils, as it:
- Demonstrates progress over time
- Accommodates varied working speeds
- Values process alongside outcome
- Provides concrete evidence for EHC plans and reviews
- Supports transition conversations

### Recording Evidence

For pupils with significant SEND:
- Photographic documentation of making processes
- Video evidence of engagement and skill development
- Adult observations and annotations
- Pupil voice captured through preferred communication methods
- Sensory responses and explorations valued equally with refined outcomes

## Collaboration and Multi-Agency Working

### Within School

Effective SEND provision requires:
- Close liaison between Art & Design teachers and SENCO
- Sharing of successful strategies across departments
- TA training in Art & Design pedagogy and materials
- Regular review of individual provision maps and IEPs
- Inclusion in whole-school SEND CPD

### External Partners

- Occupational therapists for fine motor skill development strategies
- Speech and language therapists for communication-friendly approaches
- Educational psychologists for cognitive profile insights
- Art therapists (noting distinction between art education and art therapy)
- Community arts organisations offering specialist SEND provision

## Case Study Approaches

Leslie Cunliffe's documentation of inclusive practice demonstrates:
- Pupils with profound disabilities engaging with mark-making
- ASC learners developing sophisticated photographic practices
- Dyslexic students excelling in visual communication
- SEMH pupils using art for emotional regulation and confidence-building
- Physically disabled students creating significant bodies of work through adapted methods

## Statutory Requirements and Documentation

Teachers must ensure:
- EHC plans include specific Art & Design targets where relevant
- Individual Education Plans (IEPs) inform curriculum planning
- Access arrangements are in place for practical and written work
- Risk assessments consider SEND-specific needs (sensory, physical, behavioural)
- Parents/carers are involved in curriculum adaptations

## Conclusion

Art & Design represents a uniquely inclusive curriculum area where SEND pupils can achieve, excel, and develop crucial skills. As Jeff Adams argues, the subject's emphasis on personal response, diverse outcomes, and multi-modal engagement creates natural opportunities for differentiated, personalised learning. When teachers employ the strategies outlined—rooted in research by Steers, Mason, Cunliffe, and Eileen Adams—Art & Design becomes not just accessible but transformative for SEND learners, offering alternative pathways to success, self-expression, and progression.
