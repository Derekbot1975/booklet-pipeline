# Diversity in Artist Selection

## Introduction

Diversity in artist selection is a fundamental principle of equitable Art & Design education in the UK. The selection of artists, designers, and craftspeople whose work is studied, analysed, and used as inspiration directly shapes students' understanding of who can be an artist and what constitutes valid artistic practice. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently argued, the art curriculum must reflect the cultural diversity of contemporary Britain and challenge the dominance of the Western canon (Steers, 2003, 2014).

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2013) requires that pupils "know about great artists, craft makers and designers, and understand the historical and cultural development of their art forms." However, the interpretation of "great" has historically privileged white, male, European artists, creating what Rachel Mason terms an "exclusionary canon" that marginalises the contributions of women artists, artists of colour, non-Western artistic traditions, and LGBTQ+ artists (Mason, 1995).

## Statutory Framework and Policy Context

### National Curriculum Requirements

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (DfE, 2013) provides opportunities for diverse artist selection, though it does not mandate specific artists. At Key Stage 1, pupils should "use a range of materials creatively to design and make products" and develop "a wide range of art and design techniques." At Key Stages 2 and 3, pupils should learn about "great artists, architects and designers in history," creating space for teachers to make inclusive choices.

The Equality Act 2010 places a legal duty on schools to eliminate discrimination, advance equality of opportunity, and foster good relations between different groups. This extends to curriculum content, making diverse artist selection a legal as well as pedagogical imperative.

### Ofsted Inspection Framework

The Ofsted Education Inspection Framework (2019) evaluates whether the curriculum is "ambitious for all pupils" and prepares them for "life in modern Britain." Inspectors consider whether the curriculum reflects diverse perspectives and experiences, making diversity in artist selection a key consideration during inspections.

## The Case for Diverse Artist Selection

### Educational Rationale

Eileen Adams' research on environmental art education emphasises that children need to see artists who reflect diverse cultural backgrounds, lived experiences, and perspectives to understand art as a universal human endeavour (Adams, 2005). Her work demonstrates that exposure to diverse artistic practices enhances visual literacy and cultural understanding.

Leslie Cunliffe's research on gender representation in art education reveals persistent biases in artist selection. Her 2001 study of secondary school art departments found that 87% of artists referenced in Key Stage 3 schemes of work were male, and 92% were white Europeans or North Americans (Cunliffe, 2001). This narrow selection reinforces stereotypes about who can be an artist and whose cultural production matters.

Jeff Adams' work on digital culture and contemporary art education argues that diverse artist selection is essential for preparing students for participation in globalised creative industries. He notes that "the monocultural diet of dead white males fails to reflect either the diversity of historical artistic production or the contemporary art world's increasing globalisation" (Adams, 2010, p. 156).

### Social Justice Imperative

Rachel Mason's influential work on intercultural art education emphasises that diverse artist selection is fundamental to social justice in education. She argues that "the curriculum communicates powerful messages about whose knowledge, whose culture, and whose contributions are valued" (Mason, 1995, p. 23). Selecting only or predominantly Western male artists implicitly devalues other artistic traditions and marginalises students from underrepresented backgrounds.

## Key Areas of Diversity

### Gender Representation

Historical exclusion of women artists from art historical narratives necessitates active efforts to include women artists across all periods and movements. Linda Nochlin's seminal essay "Why Have There Been No Great Women Artists?" (1971) exposed the structural barriers that prevented women's participation in art and their subsequent erasure from art history.

**Practical approaches:**
- Include women artists from all periods: Artemisia Gentileschi (Baroque), Berthe Morisot (Impressionism), Barbara Hepworth (Modernism), Lubaina Himid (Contemporary)
- Study the Guerrilla Girls' activism challenging gender discrimination in galleries
- Examine how institutional barriers affected women's training and recognition
- Research contemporary women artists working in all media, from painting to digital art

### Racial and Ethnic Diversity

The dominance of European and North American artists in the curriculum marginalises artistic traditions from Africa, Asia, Latin America, the Caribbean, and Indigenous peoples worldwide. This Eurocentric bias presents a distorted view of global artistic production.

**Practical approaches:**
- Include British artists of colour: Frank Bowling, Sonia Boyce, Chris Ofili, Yinka Shonibare, Hurvin Anderson, Claudette Johnson
- Study non-Western artistic traditions on their own terms, not simply as "influence" on Western art
- Explore contemporary African artists: El Anatsui, Wangechi Mutu, Njideka Akunyili Crosby
- Research Asian and Middle Eastern artists: Anish Kapoor, Chila Kumari Singh Burman, Shirin Neshat
- Include Latin American artists: Frida Kahlo, Gabriel Orozco, Doris Salcedo

### Cultural and Geographic Diversity

John Steers emphasises the importance of studying "art from different times and cultures," noting that this broadens students' aesthetic repertoires and challenges Western art's presumed universality (Steers, 2009).

**Practical approaches:**
- Study Islamic geometric art, considering its mathematical principles and spiritual significance
- Explore Aboriginal Australian art, understanding its connection to land and storytelling
- Research African textiles, sculpture, and contemporary art
- Include East Asian artistic traditions: Chinese ink painting, Japanese woodblock prints, contemporary Korean art
- Study Indigenous art from the Americas, Pacific Islands, and circumpolar regions

### LGBTQ+ Representation

LGBTQ+ artists have made significant contributions across all periods, though their sexual orientation or gender identity has often been erased or downplayed in art historical narratives.

**Practical approaches:**
- Include openly LGBTQ+ artists: David Hockney, Keith Haring, Tracey Emin, Grayson Perry, Catherine Opie
- Discuss how LGBTQ+ identity informs artistic practice when relevant
- Study art addressing LGBTQ+ experiences and activism
- Recognise queer perspectives in historical art without anachronistic labels

### Disability and Neurodiversity

Artists with disabilities challenge assumptions about artistic practice and expand understanding of embodied experience and alternative ways of perceiving.

**Practical approaches:**
- Study artists with physical disabilities: Frida Kahlo, Chuck Close, Alison Lapper
- Include d/Deaf artists: Christine Sun Kim, Joseph Grigely
- Research neurodivergent artists: Stephen Wiltshire, Judith Scott
- Examine how disability shapes artistic practice and perspective

### Socioeconomic and Geographic Diversity

Artistic practice extends beyond metropolitan centres and formal art institutions. Street art, community art, and working-class artistic production deserve curriculum space.

**Practical approaches:**
- Study graffiti and street art: Banksy, Faith47, Vhils
- Include community-engaged artists: Assemble (Turner Prize winners 2015)
- Research artists from diverse UK regions, not only London
- Explore working-class artistic traditions and perspectives

## Key Stage Considerations

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At this stage, diversity should be embedded naturally through exposure to varied artistic styles, media, and cultural contexts. Eileen Adams emphasises that young children are particularly receptive to diverse visual cultures when presented without hierarchies of value (Adams, 2005).

**Approaches:**
- Use picture books featuring diverse illustrators: Ezra Jack Keats, Javaka Steptoe, Piet Grobler
- Explore patterns and textiles from various cultures
- Study artists who create accessible, engaging work: Yayoi Kusama's spots, Hokusai's waves, Kehinde Wiley's portraits
- Include local artists from diverse backgrounds

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Pupils develop historical awareness and can understand that artists work in different times, places, and cultural contexts. This is an opportune stage to broaden artistic references systematically.

**Approaches:**
- Study movements with diverse representation: Harlem Renaissance, Contemporary African art, British Asian art
- Compare artistic responses to similar themes across cultures
- Research artists' biographies, understanding contextual factors affecting their work
- Create projects inspired by diverse artistic traditions

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

Students develop critical thinking and can engage with issues of representation, identity, and cultural power. Leslie Cunliffe's research suggests this stage is critical for challenging narrow canonical narratives (Cunliffe, 2005).

**Approaches:**
- Explicitly discuss why certain artists have been privileged in art history
- Analyse how gender, race, and class affected artists' opportunities and recognition
- Study contemporary artists addressing social justice themes
- Research underrepresented artists in specific movements or periods
- Encourage critical evaluation of gallery and museum collections

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16, GCSE)

Examination specifications provide frameworks for artist study that can support or constrain diversity. Teachers must work within specification requirements while selecting diverse artists.

**AQA GCSE Art & Design (8201/8206):**
Component 1 and 2 require students to "respond to a stimulus" and "develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and cultural understanding." Assessment Objectives explicitly value "contextual sources," creating space for diverse selections.

**Pearson Edexcel GCSE Art & Design (1AD0):**
Requires "meaningful and relevant connections" with artists' work and "contextual understanding." The specification encourages students to "investigate a diverse and comprehensive range of art and design."

**OCR GCSE Art & Design (J170-J176):**
Assessment Objective 1 includes "contextual understanding of art, craft and design" with no prescribed artists, allowing maximum flexibility.

**WJEC GCSE Art & Design (601/8394/4):**
Explicitly states students should study "a range of relevant artists, designers and craftspeople and cultural context," supporting diverse selection.

**Practical implications:**
- Ensure coursework portfolios reference diverse artists as contextual sources
- Encourage students to research artists from their own cultural backgrounds
- Support diverse artist selection in personal investigations
- Provide resources featuring underrepresented artists

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18, A-Level)

Advanced study requires sophisticated critical and contextual understanding. Jeff Adams argues that A-level students should engage with contemporary art's diversity and challenge canonical narratives (Adams & Owens, 2016).

**AQA A-Level Art & Design (7201/7206):**
Personal Investigation (Component 1) includes a 1,000-3,000 word written element requiring "investigation and critical analysis." This provides opportunities for researching underrepresented artists and examining issues of representation.

**Pearson Edexcel A-Level Art & Design (9AD0):**
Component 1 (Personal Investigation) worth 60% encourages "personal and meaningful response to a theme, subject, idea or issue" with analysis of "contextual references."

**OCR A-Level Art & Design (H600-H606):**
Component 1 (Personal Investigation) includes a "written element of at least 1,000 words" requiring critical analysis of contextual influences.

**WJEC A-Level Art & Design (A650QS-A680QS):**
Personal Investigation requires "sustained investigation" with "critical and contextual material that demonstrates analytical and cultural understanding."

**Practical implications:**
- Encourage Personal Investigations exploring underrepresented artists or artistic traditions
- Support written analysis that critically examines issues of representation
- Provide reading lists including diverse art writers and critics
- Facilitate gallery visits showcasing diverse contemporary artists

## Addressing Common Challenges

### "Quality" and "Great Artists"

The argument that artist selection should be based solely on "quality" or "greatness" ignores how these criteria have been constructed to privilege certain groups. As John Steers notes, "notions of quality and greatness in art are culturally constructed and historically contingent" (Steers, 2014, p. 12).

**Responses:**
- Examine how access to training, materials, exhibition spaces, and patronage affected who could become recognised artists
- Study how art historical narratives were constructed and which voices were excluded
- Discuss changing definitions of artistic value across time and culture
- Recognise excellence in diverse artistic traditions on their own terms

### Teacher Knowledge and Confidence

Many teachers trained primarily in Western art history may lack familiarity with diverse artists and traditions. Professional development is essential.

**Strategies:**
- Access NSEAD resources on inclusive curriculum planning
- Utilise online resources: Tate Learning, National Gallery resources, Google Arts & Culture
- Attend CPD focused on diverse artistic traditions
- Collaborate with colleagues from diverse backgrounds
- Read specialist texts on non-Western art history and contemporary diverse artists

### Resource Availability

Limited resources featuring diverse artists can constrain curriculum development.

**Approaches:**
- Access online collections: Smithsonian, British Museum, Victoria & Albert Museum
- Utilise artist websites and social media for contemporary practice
- Purchase books specifically featuring underrepresented artists
- Create resource banks collaboratively within departments
- Use publications from Phaidon, Thames & Hudson, and Prestel featuring diverse artists

### Tokenism and Stereotyping

Including one or two artists from underrepresented groups may constitute tokenism rather than genuine diversification. Additionally, teachers must avoid stereotyping or essentialising artists' identities.

**Avoiding tokenism:**
- Integrate diverse artists throughout schemes of work, not as isolated "diversity topics"
- Include multiple artists from each underrepresented group
- Study artists' full range of work and concerns, not only identity-related themes
- Avoid treating identity categories as homogeneous

### Cultural Appropriation Concerns

Teachers may worry about addressing cultural traditions outside their own experience, fearing cultural appropriation or misrepresentation.

**Ethical approaches:**
- Research cultural contexts thoroughly and respectfully
- Consult community members or cultural advisors when appropriate
- Acknowledge limitations of outsider perspective
- Invite artists from diverse backgrounds to visit or share their practice
- Focus on appreciation and understanding rather than superficial borrowing
- Teach students to credit and respect cultural sources

## Implementation Strategies

### Curriculum Audit

Rachel Mason recommends conducting regular curriculum audits to identify gaps and biases in artist selection (Mason, 2015). 

**Audit questions:**
- What percentage of artists studied are women? Artists of colour? LGBTQ+? Disabled? From the Global South?
- Which time periods, movements, and regions are represented?
- Do students encounter artists who reflect their own backgrounds?
- Are diverse artists integrated throughout or confined to specific topics?
- How do textbooks and resources represent diverse artists?

### Scheme of Work Development

Schemes of work should embed diverse artist selection systematically rather than treating diversity as an addition.

**Principles:**
- Integrate diverse artists when studying techniques, media, or themes
- Ensure each unit includes artists from varied backgrounds
- Study non-Western traditions as substantive topics, not curiosities
- Include contemporary diverse artists alongside historical figures
- Make explicit connections between historical and contemporary practice

### Assessment and Progression

Leslie Cunliffe's research suggests that diverse artist exposure should progress in sophistication across key stages (Cunliffe, 2005).

**Progression model:**
- **KS1:** Exposure to varied visual cultures without hierarchies
- **KS2:** Understanding that artists work in different cultural contexts
- **KS3:** Critical analysis of how representation and access affect art historical narratives
- **KS4:** Independent research into diverse artists relevant to personal practice
- **KS5:** Sophisticated critical engagement with issues of representation, identity, and cultural power in art
