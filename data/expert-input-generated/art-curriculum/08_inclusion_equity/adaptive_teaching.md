# Adaptive Teaching in Art

## Introduction and Conceptual Framework

Adaptive teaching in art and design education represents a sophisticated pedagogical approach that responds to the diverse learning needs, experiences, and capabilities of all pupils whilst maintaining high expectations and challenging learning objectives. This approach moves beyond traditional 'differentiation by task' models towards a more fluid, responsive teaching methodology that adapts instruction to meet learners where they are whilst maintaining curriculum coherence and artistic integrity.

As articulated by the Education Endowment Foundation (2021) and reflected in the Department for Education's guidance on inclusive teaching, adaptive teaching involves responding to pupils' needs through careful curriculum design, formative assessment, and flexible pedagogical strategies rather than creating entirely separate learning pathways. In art and design, this approach is particularly significant given the subject's multimodal nature and capacity to accommodate diverse forms of expression and thinking.

John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has long advocated for inclusive art education that recognises the 'multiple intelligences' at play in creative work, arguing that art and design education naturally lends itself to personalised learning experiences without compromising rigour or standards (Steers, 2009).

## The UK National Curriculum Context

### Statutory Requirements and Adaptive Approaches

The National Curriculum for Art and Design (2013) establishes clear aims for pupils to produce creative work, become proficient in drawing, painting, sculpture and other art, craft and design techniques, evaluate and analyse creative works, and know about great artists, craft makers and designers. Crucially, the curriculum requires that "teachers should ensure that all pupils are taught according to their age and ability" whilst accessing age-appropriate content.

The curriculum's emphasis on developing competence in specific techniques, combined with opportunities for personal exploration and response, provides natural opportunities for adaptive teaching. At Key Stage 1, pupils should "use a range of materials creatively to design and make products" and "develop a wide range of art and design techniques in using colour, pattern, texture, line, shape, form and space" – outcomes achievable through multiple pathways suited to different learners.

At Key Stage 2, the requirement to "create sketch books to record their observations and use them to review and revisit ideas" offers a personalised learning tool that can be adapted to individual capabilities, from visual note-making to written annotation, from observational drawing to conceptual mapping.

For Key Stage 3, where pupils should "use a range of techniques to record their observations in sketchbooks, journals and other media as a basis for exploring their ideas," adaptive teaching becomes essential in accommodating the developmental diversity of 11-14 year olds whilst building towards GCSE requirements.

## Theoretical Foundations

### Key Thinkers in Adaptive Art Education

**Eileen Adams** has extensively researched environmental and community-based art education, demonstrating how real-world contexts can provide accessible entry points for diverse learners. Her work emphasises the importance of connecting art education to pupils' lived experiences, arguing that "art education should enable young people to engage with their environment, to understand it better and to have some influence over it" (Adams, 2005). This contextual approach naturally supports adaptive teaching by providing multiple starting points and authentic purposes for creative work.

**Rachel Mason's** research into international art education practices highlights the cultural dimensions of learning in art, demonstrating that pupils from different backgrounds may engage with visual culture and creative processes in culturally specific ways. Her work encourages teachers to recognise and value diverse cultural perspectives and aesthetic traditions when planning for inclusive practice (Mason, 2016).

**Leslie Cunliffe** has contributed significantly to understanding art education for pupils with special educational needs, particularly in relation to visual impairment and physical disabilities. His practical research demonstrates how sensory experiences, multi-sensory approaches, and alternative materials can provide access to art education for all learners whilst maintaining artistic integrity (Cunliffe, 2007).

**Jeff Adams** examines the relationship between art education and social justice, arguing that adaptive teaching must address issues of equity and cultural capital. His work suggests that "art education has the potential to challenge educational inequalities, but only if teachers consciously work to make their practice inclusive and culturally responsive" (Adams, 2003).

## Practical Strategies for Adaptive Teaching in Art

### Assessment-Informed Planning

Effective adaptive teaching in art begins with robust formative assessment that identifies pupils' current capabilities, prior learning, and specific needs. Unlike other subjects where assessment might focus primarily on knowledge recall, art education requires assessment across multiple domains:

- **Technical skills**: manipulation of materials, control of processes, understanding of techniques
- **Visual literacy**: ability to 'read' images, understand visual conventions, recognise artistic elements
- **Conceptual thinking**: generating ideas, making connections, developing personal responses
- **Critical understanding**: contextual knowledge, evaluative language, appreciation of artistic traditions

Teachers should use baseline assessments at the start of new units that examine these domains through practical tasks, visual analysis activities, and discussion. For example, at the beginning of a Key Stage 3 portraiture unit, pupils might complete a rapid self-portrait whilst discussing their understanding of facial proportions, their experience with different drawing media, and their awareness of portrait traditions. This information directly informs subsequent teaching decisions.

### Scaffolding and Support Structures

Adaptive teaching in art involves providing temporary scaffolds that enable pupils to work at challenging levels whilst gradually building independence. These might include:

**Technical scaffolds**: Viewfinders for composition, stencils for complex shapes, pre-mixed colour palettes, marked measurement tools, or template shapes. Crucially, these supports are presented not as 'easier' alternatives but as professional tools (architects use templates; designers use colour systems) that enable focus on higher-order creative thinking.

**Conceptual scaffolds**: Thought-prompting questions on laminated cards, visual thinking routines (see Harvard's Project Zero), idea generation frameworks, or structured sketchbook templates. For instance, the 'See-Think-Wonder' routine adapted for art provides accessible entry to critical analysis for all learners.

**Process scaffolds**: Step-by-step visual guides, exemplar work at different stages, process checklists, or time management tools. These support executive function skills whilst maintaining creative autonomy.

John Steers emphasises that such scaffolds must be "gradually removed as pupils develop confidence and competence," ensuring that temporary support doesn't become permanent limitation (Steers & Swift, 1999).

### Flexible Grouping Strategies

Adaptive teaching in art benefits from strategic, flexible grouping that varies according to learning objectives:

**Ability grouping** may be appropriate for specific technical skill development (e.g., groups learning different printmaking techniques based on prior experience) but should be temporary and task-specific rather than fixed.

**Mixed-ability grouping** supports peer learning, particularly for conceptual development and critical discussions. Pairing pupils with different strengths (e.g., strong technical skills with strong conceptual thinking) creates reciprocal learning opportunities.

**Interest-based grouping** acknowledges that motivation significantly affects learning in creative subjects. Allowing pupils to explore themes or media that engage them personally can increase commitment whilst addressing the same learning objectives.

**Random grouping** for collaborative projects ensures pupils work with diverse peers, reflecting professional creative practice and building social skills alongside artistic learning.

### Differentiated Resources and Materials

Adaptive teaching recognises that pupils may achieve the same learning outcomes through different materials and processes:

**Media choices**: Offering alternatives such as soft pastels versus pencils for tonal work; collage versus painting for colour exploration; clay versus found materials for 3D work. Each medium has different physical and cognitive demands, allowing pupils to access learning through their strengths.

**Scale variations**: Some pupils work more effectively at larger scales (developing gross motor control and bold expression), whilst others prefer detailed, small-scale work (developing fine motor control and careful observation). Both approaches can address objectives around mark-making, composition, or artistic expression.

**Tool adaptations**: Modified brushes, adaptive scissors, textured papers, or digital alternatives provide access for pupils with physical disabilities whilst often benefiting all learners. The NSEAD's 'Access to Art' resources (2018) provide extensive guidance on inclusive materials.

**Research resources**: Differentiated access to artist research through varied texts (simplified language, visual-heavy presentations, video content, gallery visits) ensures all pupils can develop contextual understanding at appropriate levels.

### Adaptive Questioning and Dialogue

Dialogic teaching in art – where teachers use talk to stimulate and extend pupils' thinking – is a powerful adaptive strategy. Rachel Mason notes that "the quality of teacher-pupil dialogue in art lessons significantly affects the depth of learning and the development of artistic thinking" (Mason, 2005).

Adaptive questioning strategies include:

**Tiered questioning**: Preparing questions at different cognitive levels (Bloom's taxonomy) that can be deployed responsively. For example, regarding a Van Gogh landscape: "What colours can you see?" (remembering/understanding); "How has Van Gogh created texture?" (applying/analysing); "Why might Van Gogh have chosen these expressive techniques?" (evaluating/creating).

**Wait time**: Allowing extended thinking time after questions, particularly beneficial for pupils with processing difficulties or English as an Additional Language (EAL). In art, where responses often require visual thinking, additional wait time is crucial.

**Talk partners**: Structured peer discussion before whole-class responses reduces anxiety and supports language development whilst allowing all pupils to formulate responses.

**Sentence stems**: Providing linguistic frameworks ("I notice that...", "This makes me think...", "The artist might have...") supports pupils with language difficulties or limited experience with critical discourse.

## Key Stage-Specific Adaptive Strategies

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

At this foundational stage, adaptive teaching focuses on building confidence, experimentation, and basic skills through highly sensory, play-based approaches:

**Physical accessibility**: Ensure tools and materials suit varied fine motor development through chunky brushes, adapted scissors, easel work for pupils with postural difficulties, or finger painting alternatives.

**Multi-sensory learning**: Connect visual learning to other senses – textured papers, scented paints, sound-inspired mark-making – supporting pupils with visual processing difficulties whilst enriching learning for all.

**Simplified vocabulary**: Introduce artistic language gradually, using concrete examples and visual reference points. Create classroom 'word walls' with images demonstrating concepts like 'texture' or 'pattern'.

**Flexible completion**: Allow pupils to determine when work is 'finished' according to their own criteria, building autonomy and preventing frustration for pupils who work at different paces.

Eileen Adams' emphasis on environmental and first-hand experience is particularly relevant here, as real-world stimuli provide accessible starting points for diverse learners: "Young children learn best through direct experience, and art education should capitalise on their natural curiosity about the world around them" (Adams, 2010).

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Building skills and knowledge systematically whilst accommodating increasing learning diversity requires:

**Explicit teaching**: Demonstrate techniques clearly with step-by-step modelling, particularly benefiting pupils with working memory difficulties or developmental coordination disorder. Use visualisers to show hand positions and tool manipulation.

**Sketchbook personalisation**: Allow varied approaches to sketchbook use – some pupils develop visual journals, others use structured templates, some combine text and image extensively. All approaches can demonstrate progression if guided effectively.

**Project choice**: Within units, offer limited choices that maintain curriculum coverage whilst engaging individual interests. For example, in a 'Ancient Egypt' topic, pupils might choose to create canopic jars, cartouches, or tomb paintings, all addressing objectives around decorative design and cultural understanding.

**Success criteria co-creation**: Involve pupils in defining quality indicators for creative work, developing their evaluative capabilities whilst ensuring criteria are understood and achievable. This metacognitive approach particularly supports pupils with autism spectrum conditions who benefit from clear expectations.

**Peer assessment structures**: Train pupils in specific, constructive feedback techniques using frameworks like 'Two stars and a wish', supporting both assessor and recipient in developing critical awareness.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

This transitional phase requires adaptive strategies that prepare pupils for potential GCSE study whilst supporting the wide developmental range characteristic of early adolescence:

**Conceptual complexity**: Introduce more sophisticated artistic concepts and historical knowledge through differentiated resources. Use gallery education resources, artist films, and varied texts to ensure access to challenging content.

**Independence scaffolding**: Gradually transfer responsibility for planning, decision-making, and evaluation to pupils, with adjustable support levels. Provide planning frameworks for those who need them whilst allowing others to develop their own systems.

**Cultural responsiveness**: Draw on diverse artistic traditions and contemporary practices, ensuring curriculum representation that values pupils' varied cultural backgrounds. Jeff Adams argues that "curriculum choices in art education are never neutral; they either reinforce or challenge existing power structures and cultural hierarchies" (Adams, 2012).

**Assessment literacy**: Explicitly teach pupils how their work will be assessed, preparing them for GCSE assessment objectives whilst building self-evaluation skills. Use exemplar work at different levels, involving pupils in levelling exercises.

**Studio practice habits**: Establish working practices that mirror professional art practice (experimentation, risk-taking, iteration, reflection), supporting self-directed learning that will be essential at KS4.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16) - GCSE

Adaptive teaching at GCSE level must balance examination requirements with inclusive practice:

#### AQA Art and Design

AQA's assessment objectives (AO1-4) covering development of ideas, refinement through experimentation, recording ideas/observations, and personal response provide multiple pathways to achievement. Adaptive strategies include:

**Component choice**: AQA offers diverse endorsements (Fine Art, Graphic Communication, Textile Design, Three-Dimensional Design, Photography) allowing pupils to work in areas of strength and interest.

**Portfolio development**: The 60% portfolio component allows extended time and flexible pacing. Pupils can demonstrate progress through varied quantities of work – some create extensive sketchbooks, others fewer but deeply developed pieces.

**Externally set assignment**: The 10-hour supervised time can be adapted through access arrangements (extra time, rest breaks, use of transcription for annotation) whilst the preparatory period allows differentiated research and planning support.

#### Edexcel Art and Design

Edexcel's structure similarly supports adaptation through:

**Component 1 flexibility**: Personal portfolio allows teacher-led project design that can incorporate pupils' interests and strengths whilst meeting assessment objectives.

**Continuous assessment**: The portfolio component's continuous assessment nature enables responsive teaching, allowing interventions when pupils struggle and extension when they excel.

#### OCR Art and Design

OCR's qualifications emphasise personal investigation, which naturally supports adaptive teaching:

**Theme interpretation**: Broad themes allow varied responses suited to different capabilities and interests. Pupils interpret themes through their unique perspectives and chosen media.

**Critical study integration**: The requirement for contextual understanding can be demonstrated through varied evidence – written annotations, recorded discussions, visual responses, or comparative presentations.

#### WJEC Art and Design

WJEC specifications offer:

**Regional focus**: Opportunities to explore Welsh culture and heritage provide culturally responsive curriculum content whilst maintaining inclusive practice through universal artistic principles.

**Practical emphasis**: High weighting of practical work (80%) benefits pupils who struggle with written communication whilst still requiring some critical understanding.

## Supporting Specific Learning Needs in Art

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND)

Leslie Cunliffe's research demonstrates that art education "offers unique opportunities for pupils with SEND to achieve success and develop confidence, provided teaching is appropriately adapted" (Cunliffe, 2007).

**Visual impairment**: Emphasise tactile and sculptural work; use high-contrast materials; provide verbal descriptions of visual information; arrange gallery visits with touch tours; use technology like screen readers for research.

**Hearing impairment**: Ensure visual demonstrations are clear and sequential; provide written instructions alongside verbal; use BSL if appropriate; reduce background noise in practical spaces; utilise visual timers.

**Physical disabilities**: Adapt tools (extended handles, easy-grip brushes, switch-adapted tools); adjust working heights; provide alternatives to physically demanding processes; use technology like digital drawing tablets; collaborate with occupational therapists.

**Autism spectrum conditions**: Provide clear structure and routines; prepare pupils for sensory experiences with materials; offer quiet work spaces; use visual timetables; be explicit about open-ended tasks; allow special interests to inform project work.

**ADHD**: Build in movement opportunities; use timers for task segments; provide fidget tools during demonstrations; allow flexible working positions; break projects into smaller achievable steps; celebrate experimentation.

**Dyslexia and literacy difficulties**: Reduce written annotation requirements; accept verbal explanations; use assistive technology for research; provide keyword lists with images; allow visual note-taking; don't assume weak literacy indicates weak artistic ability.

### English as an Additional Language (EAL)

Art education is particularly accessible to EAL pupils due to its visual nature, but adaptive teaching enhances access:

**Visual communication emphasis**: Allow ideas and understanding to be communicated primarily through visual means whilst gradually building subject-specific vocabulary.

**Collaborative learning**: Pair EAL pupils with language-confident peers for mutual benefit – visual ideas transcend language barriers.

**Dual-language resources**: Where possible, provide artist information or instructions in first languages, or use heavily illustrated materials.

**Vocabulary development**: Explicitly teach artistic terminology through concrete examples, gestures, and images. Create personal visual dictionaries.

**Cultural capital**: Value and incorporate diverse artistic traditions, ensuring EAL pupils see their cultural heritage represented and valued.

Rachel Mason's international research emphasises that "artistic understanding transcends linguistic barriers, and art education provides unique opportunities for pupils developing English proficiency to demonstrate sophisticated thinking and capability" (Mason, 2016).
