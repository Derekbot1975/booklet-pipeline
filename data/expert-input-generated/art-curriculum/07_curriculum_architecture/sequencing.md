# Sequencing Art Across Key Stages

## Introduction

Sequencing in art and design education refers to the deliberate ordering of content, skills, and conceptual understanding across the key stages to ensure progressive and cumulative learning. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has argued, effective curriculum sequencing in art must balance technical skill development with conceptual understanding and critical engagement (Steers, 2014). This knowledge file provides comprehensive guidance for UK schools on constructing coherent learning progressions from Early Years through to Key Stage 5.

## Theoretical Foundations of Curriculum Sequencing

### Core Principles

Rachel Mason's seminal work *Art Education and Multiculturalism* (1988) established that curriculum sequencing must account for both vertical progression (complexity over time) and horizontal coherence (connections across concurrent learning). Leslie Cunliffe's research into progression in art education emphasises that sequencing should not merely repeat content at different 'levels' but should genuinely deepen understanding through increasing sophistication of artistic problems and responses (Cunliffe, 2007).

Eileen Adams has consistently advocated for sequencing that reflects authentic artistic practice rather than arbitrary hierarchies. Her work on environmental art education demonstrates how spiral curriculum models—where themes recur with increasing complexity—can maintain engagement whilst building expertise (Adams, 2005).

### Developmental Considerations

Jeff Adams, in *The Place of the Visual in Art Education*, identifies three key developmental trajectories that must inform sequencing:

1. **Technical fluency**: progressive mastery of materials, tools, and processes
2. **Conceptual sophistication**: deepening understanding of artistic ideas, movements, and cultural contexts
3. **Critical autonomy**: increasing capacity for independent judgement and personal voice

## National Curriculum Framework and Statutory Requirements

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum for Art and Design (DfE, 2013) specifies that pupils should:
- Use a range of materials creatively to design and make products
- Develop techniques including drawing, painting, and sculpture with various materials
- Develop understanding of the work of artists, craftspeople, and designers

**Sequencing implications**: At KS1, sequencing should prioritise foundational experiences with materials and basic technical vocabulary. John Steers emphasises that this stage should establish positive dispositions towards art-making rather than premature technical refinement (Steers & Swift, 1999).

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

The curriculum requires pupils to:
- Create sketchbooks to record observations and use for review and revisit
- Improve mastery of techniques including drawing, painting, and sculpture with varied materials
- Learn about great artists, architects, and designers in history

**Sequencing implications**: This represents the critical juncture for establishing systematic skill progression. Rachel Mason's research suggests introducing formal elements (line, tone, colour, pattern, texture, shape, form, space) sequentially rather than simultaneously, allowing deep understanding of each before integration (Mason, 1995).

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The statutory programme requires:
- Use of a range of techniques and media, including painting
- Increased proficiency in handling different materials
- Analysis and evaluation of creative works using art-specific vocabulary
- Understanding of historical and cultural development of art forms

**Sequencing implications**: Leslie Cunliffe identifies KS3 as the 'pivotal stage' where subject-specific literacy and conceptual frameworks must be explicitly taught. Sequencing should move from teacher-directed technical exercises to increasingly open-ended briefs requiring independent decision-making (Cunliffe, 2007).

## Sequencing Across Examination Specifications

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16)

All major examination boards (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) structure GCSE Art and Design around Assessment Objectives that implicitly suggest sequencing:

**AO1**: Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual sources
**AO2**: Refine work through experimenting and selecting appropriate resources
**AO3**: Record ideas and insights relevant to intentions
**AO4**: Present a personal and meaningful response

Effective sequencing at KS4 builds systematic capacity in each AO before integration. John Steers argues that many schools err by expecting simultaneous development across all objectives from Year 10, resulting in superficial coverage (Steers, 2009).

#### Practical Sequencing Model for GCSE

**Term 1 (Year 10)**: Focused skill development and contextual studies (primarily AO2 and AO1)
**Terms 2-3**: Structured projects requiring investigation and refinement (integrating AO1-AO3)
**Year 11**: Increasingly independent projects culminating in the externally set assignment (all AOs with emphasis on AO4)

### Key Stage 5 (Ages 16-18)

A-level specifications (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, WJEC Eduqas) maintain the four Assessment Objectives but expect greater conceptual sophistication and independence. Sequencing at KS5 must prioritise:

1. **Year 12 Term 1**: Establishing advanced technical competencies and research methodologies
2. **Year 12 Terms 2-3**: Extended investigations with increasing conceptual ambition
3. **Year 13 Term 1**: Personal investigation completion demonstrating intellectual depth
4. **Year 13 Terms 2-3**: Externally set assignment requiring synthesis of prior learning

Jeff Adams emphasises that A-level sequencing must facilitate genuine artistic inquiry rather than formulaic project structures (Adams, 2018).

## Models of Progression

### The Spiral Curriculum

Building on Bruner's concept, adapted by Eileen Adams for art education, the spiral curriculum revisits core concepts—such as portraiture, abstraction, or materiality—at each key stage with increasing complexity. For example:

**KS1**: Self-portraits exploring mark-making and colour mixing
**KS2**: Portraiture investigating proportion and contextual artists (Kahlo, Rembrandt)
**KS3**: Portrait projects examining identity, representation, and power
**KS4**: Extended portrait investigations addressing contemporary debates (gender, race, digital identity)
**KS5**: Sophisticated explorations of the portrait genre across historical and cultural contexts

### The Building Blocks Model

Leslie Cunliffe's research proposes sequencing as 'building blocks' where foundational competencies must be secure before progressing. This model suggests:

**Foundation Layer (KS1-2)**: Sensory exploration, material confidence, basic vocabulary
**Development Layer (KS3)**: Formal elements mastery, technical proficiency, critical vocabulary
**Synthesis Layer (KS4-5)**: Independent application, conceptual sophistication, personal voice

### The Domains Model

Rachel Mason's 'domains of learning' in art education identifies three interconnected strands requiring simultaneous but differentiated sequencing:

1. **Making domain**: Progressive technical and creative capacity
2. **Investigating domain**: Developing research and analytical skills
3. **Contextualising domain**: Deepening cultural and historical understanding

Effective sequencing ensures none of these domains is neglected whilst recognising their mutual reinforcement.

## Practical Strategies for Curriculum Leaders

### Mapping Prerequisites

Curriculum leaders should audit schemes of work to identify:
- **Essential prerequisites**: Skills/knowledge genuinely required for subsequent learning
- **Desirable foundations**: Helpful but not essential prior learning
- **False prerequisites**: Artificially imposed barriers to progression

John Steers warns against creating unnecessary 'gatekeeping' where students are prevented from engaging with sophisticated concepts due to assumed technical deficits (Steers, 2014).

### Scaffolding Complexity

Eileen Adams's research into project-based learning suggests sequencing complexity through:
- **Technical challenge**: Introducing demanding materials/processes progressively
- **Conceptual demand**: Moving from concrete to abstract ideas
- **Openness of brief**: Gradually releasing responsibility to students
- **Critical expectations**: Increasing analytical sophistication required

### Interleaving and Retrieval

Recent cognitive science research, increasingly influential in UK curriculum thinking, suggests sequencing should incorporate:

**Interleaving**: Mixing topics rather than blocking them (e.g., alternating between 2D and 3D rather than discrete half-term blocks)
**Spaced retrieval**: Deliberate revisiting of previously learned content across terms and years
**Variation**: Experiencing concepts in multiple contexts to develop flexible understanding

### Medium-Term Planning Sequences

A typical KS3 year might sequence:

**Autumn 1**: Drawing fundamentals—line, tone, mark-making (building observational skills)
**Autumn 2**: Colour theory through painting (building chromatic understanding)
**Spring 1**: 3D form through sculpture/ceramics (building spatial thinking)
**Spring 2**: Printmaking (synthesising drawing and design skills)
**Summer 1**: Artist investigation (building contextual knowledge)
**Summer 2**: Personal response project (applying integrated skills)

Each unit explicitly builds on prior learning whilst introducing new content, with sketchbooks providing ongoing formative assessment evidence.

## Addressing Common Sequencing Challenges

### The 'Project Archipelago' Problem

Many art departments suffer from disconnected projects with insufficient linkage. Leslie Cunliffe terms this the 'project archipelago'—isolated islands of activity without clear progression (Cunliffe, 2007). Solutions include:

- Explicit identification of 'golden threads' (recurring themes/skills) across projects
- Deliberate skill progression documents shared with students
- Regular curriculum review examining cumulative learning rather than discrete outcomes

### The Foundation Trap

John Steers identifies the 'foundation trap' where KS3 becomes endless preparatory skill-building without meaningful creative challenges. He argues for 'authentic difficulty' at every stage—projects that genuinely challenge students' current capabilities whilst building future capacity (Steers, 2009).

### Differentiation Within Sequences

Rachel Mason's research emphasises that effective sequencing must accommodate diverse starting points and learning trajectories. This requires:

- **Core learning**: Essential knowledge and skills for all students
- **Extension challenges**: Opportunities for greater depth within same projects
- **Alternative pathways**: Different routes to similar endpoints (e.g., conceptual vs. technical strengths)

### Cultural Breadth

Eileen Adams has consistently argued that sequencing must ensure diverse cultural representation isn't relegated to 'special topics' but integrated throughout progression. This means:

- Non-Western art forms presented as equally valid traditions, not exotic alternatives
- Contemporary diverse practitioners featured at all key stages
- Critical examination of historical Eurocentrism in art education itself

## Assessment and Sequencing

### Formative Assessment Informing Sequence

Effective sequencing requires responsive adjustment based on formative evidence. Department leaders should establish:

- **Diagnostic baselines**: Understanding student starting points at each key stage transition
- **Progress checkpoints**: Regular review of whether sequenced learning is being secured
- **Adaptation protocols**: Clear processes for adjusting pace/content based on student need

### Summative Milestones

While art education rightly emphasises formative assessment, summative milestones inform sequencing decisions:

**End of KS1**: Confident experimentation with basic materials; awareness of different artists
**End of KS2**: Systematic sketchbook use; developing technical control; growing contextual knowledge
**End of KS3**: Secure formal elements understanding; independent project planning; critical analysis capacity
**End of KS4**: Portfolio demonstrating integrated skills; personal artistic voice; sustained investigation ability
**End of KS5**: Sophisticated conceptual work; advanced technical proficiency; scholarly contextual understanding

## Sequencing for Inclusive Excellence

### Special Educational Needs and Disabilities

Sequencing must account for diverse learning needs without reducing expectations. Strategies include:

- **Pre-teaching**: Introducing key concepts/vocabulary before main teaching sequence
- **Extended foundations**: More time on foundational skills without preventing access to sophisticated concepts
- **Alternative demonstrations**: Multiple ways to evidence understanding (verbal, visual, practical)

### More Able Students

Jeff Adams warns against 'acceleration' approaches that rush students through content. Instead, sequencing for depth might include:

- **Conceptual complexity**: Earlier introduction to sophisticated artistic ideas
- **Technical mastery**: Opportunities for exceptional skill development
- **Critical challenge**: Higher expectations for analytical and evaluative thinking

## Cross-Curricular Sequencing

### Literacy Development

Sequencing should deliberately build subject-specific literacy:

**KS1-2**: Basic art vocabulary (names of materials, processes, colours)
**KS3**: Formal elements terminology, artistic movements, technical language
**KS4**: Analytical vocabulary, comparative language, specialist terminology
**KS5**: Academic discourse, theoretical frameworks, sophisticated critical language

### Numeracy and Spatial Reasoning

Art curriculum sequencing can reinforce mathematical learning:

**KS1-2**: Shapes, patterns, symmetry, measurement
**KS3**: Proportion, perspective, scale, ratio
**KS4-5**: Complex geometric understanding, mathematical relationships in composition

## Long-Term Curriculum Vision

### Five-Year Progression Narratives

Curriculum leaders should articulate clear progression narratives. For example:

**Drawing progression**: From exploratory mark-making (KS1) through observational accuracy (KS2) and expressive drawing (KS3) to drawing as thinking and research tool (KS4-5)

**Contextual understanding progression**: From awareness of famous artists (KS1) through studying specific movements and contexts (KS2-3) to critical analysis of art's social role (KS4) and scholarly investigation (KS5)

### The 'Finished Artist' Vision

John Steers encourages departments to articulate their 'finished artist' vision—what should a student leaving your art education be able to do, know, and value? This vision should inform all sequencing decisions, ensuring cumulative progress towards defined endpoints rather than arbitrary coverage (Steers, 2014).

## Conclusion

Effective sequencing in art and design education requires sophisticated understanding of artistic development, pedagogical progression, and curriculum architecture. As Leslie Cunliffe emphasises, this is not a mechanical process of listing topics but a dynamic, responsive approach to building genuine capability and understanding over time (Cunliffe, 2007).

The principles outlined here—progressive skill development, deepening conceptual engagement, increasing critical autonomy, and cultural breadth—provide a framework for curriculum leaders to construct coherent learning journeys from Early Years through to post-16 study. Regular curriculum review, informed by both formative evidence and the expertise of art education researchers like Adams, Steers, Mason, Cunliffe, and Adams, ensures sequencing remains purposeful and effective in developing young people as confident, capable, and critically engaged artists and designers.
