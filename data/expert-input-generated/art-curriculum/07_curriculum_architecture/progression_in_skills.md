# Progression in Art Skills

## Introduction and Theoretical Framework

Progression in art skills represents one of the most complex and contested areas within UK Art & Design education. Unlike subjects with linear, hierarchical knowledge structures, art progression operates across multiple dimensions simultaneously: technical proficiency, conceptual understanding, creative autonomy, and critical reflection. As John Steers, former General Secretary of the National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD), has consistently argued, progression in art cannot be reduced to simple skill acquisition but must encompass the development of visual literacy, cultural understanding, and personal artistic voice.

The National Curriculum for Art & Design (2013) establishes that pupils should "produce creative work, exploring their ideas and recording their experiences" whilst becoming "proficient in drawing, painting, sculpture and other art, craft and design techniques." This dual emphasis on creative exploration and technical mastery creates inherent tensions in defining progression that teachers must navigate throughout Key Stages 1-4.

## Defining Progression in Art Education

### Multi-Dimensional Models

Eileen Adams' influential work on aesthetic learning emphasises that progression in art operates across interconnected domains: perceptual development, practical capabilities, conceptual understanding, and critical awareness. Her research with Goldsmiths, University of London demonstrated that children's artistic development is not merely about "getting better at drawing" but involves increasingly sophisticated ways of seeing, thinking, and making.

Rachel Mason's research, particularly in 'Art Education and Multiculturalism' (1988), challenged Western-centric models of artistic development, arguing that progression must acknowledge cultural diversity in aesthetic values and creative practices. This perspective remains crucial for contemporary UK classrooms serving diverse communities.

Leslie Cunliffe's work at the University of Roehampton has explored how progression manifests differently across various art forms, noting that skill development in three-dimensional work, printmaking, or digital media each follows distinct trajectories that resist simplistic developmental hierarchies.

## National Curriculum Framework and Key Stage Progression

### Key Stage 1 (Ages 5-7)

The National Curriculum expects pupils to:
- Use a range of materials creatively to design and make products
- Use drawing, painting and sculpture to develop and share their ideas
- Develop techniques of colour, pattern, texture, line, shape, form and space

**Progression indicators at KS1:**
- Moving from exploratory mark-making to controlled, intentional marks
- Developing colour recognition to deliberate colour mixing and application
- Progressing from manipulation of materials to purposeful construction
- Building observational skills from general impressions to noticing specific details

### Key Stage 2 (Ages 7-11)

Pupils should:
- Create sketch books to record observations and use them to review and revisit ideas
- Improve mastery of techniques including drawing, painting and sculpture with a range of materials
- Learn about great artists, architects and designers in history

**Progression indicators at KS2:**
- Development of sustained working: single session activities extending to projects over multiple weeks
- Technical refinement: moving from basic tool control to selecting appropriate techniques for intended outcomes
- Critical vocabulary: progressing from simple descriptive language to subject-specific terminology
- Sketchbook usage: evolving from random collection to purposeful investigation and development space
- Cultural understanding: moving from simple recognition to contextual awareness of artistic movements and practitioners

John Steers has noted that KS2 represents a critical juncture where many pupils' confidence can decline if teaching focuses narrowly on technical correctness rather than creative experimentation supported by developing skills.

### Key Stage 3 (Ages 11-14)

The National Curriculum requires pupils to:
- Use a range of techniques and media, including painting
- Increase proficiency in handling different materials
- Analyse and evaluate their own work and that of others
- Understand art history and the work of great artists

**Progression indicators at KS3:**
- Sophisticated material exploration: moving beyond trial-and-error to informed experimentation based on understanding of media properties
- Conceptual development: progressing from teacher-directed themes to personal interpretation and artistic intent
- Technical vocabulary: advancing from basic terminology to confident use of discipline-specific language
- Analytical capabilities: developing from simple preference statements to reasoned critique using appropriate frameworks
- Independent research: moving from teacher-provided resources to self-directed investigation of artists, movements and contexts

Jeff Adams, in his work on digital arts education, has highlighted how KS3 progression must now incorporate digital literacies alongside traditional skills, with pupils developing understanding of how digital tools complement and extend conventional practices rather than replacing them.

### Key Stage 4 (Ages 14-16): GCSE Specifications

At GCSE, exam boards define progression through Assessment Objectives (AOs) that explicitly articulate skill development expectations:

**AQA, Edexcel, OCR and WJEC all structure GCSE Art & Design around four Assessment Objectives:**

- **AO1**: Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual and other sources
- **AO2**: Refine work by exploring ideas, selecting and experimenting with appropriate media, materials, techniques and processes
- **AO3**: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions
- **AO4**: Present a personal and meaningful response demonstrating analytical and critical understanding

**Progression from KS3 to GCSE:**
- Moving from teacher-scaffolded projects to increasingly independent creative inquiry
- Developing from competent handling of materials to purposeful selection and sophisticated application
- Progressing from basic analysis to sustained critical engagement with own work and others'
- Building from simple documentation to strategic use of recording methods that inform and develop practice

## Skill-Specific Progression Pathways

### Drawing Development

Drawing represents perhaps the most extensively researched area of artistic progression. Progression manifests through:

**Observational accuracy:**
- KS1: Recognition of basic shapes and general proportions
- KS2: Attention to comparative measurement, developing understanding of proportion
- KS3: Sophisticated observation incorporating tone, texture, spatial relationships
- KS4: Selective emphasis, artistic interpretation balancing observation with expression

**Mark-making and media control:**
- KS1: Exploratory marks, pressure variation, tool experimentation
- KS2: Controlled linear work, cross-hatching, blending techniques
- KS3: Expressive mark-making informed by intention, media combinations
- KS4: Personal mark-making vocabulary, strategic media selection

**Spatial understanding:**
- KS1: Overlapping objects, basic ground lines
- KS2: Developing sense of foreground/background, simple perspective concepts
- KS3: Linear perspective, atmospheric perspective, foreshortening
- KS4: Sophisticated spatial manipulation, deliberate spatial ambiguity or exaggeration

### Colour Understanding

Leslie Cunliffe's research emphasises that colour progression extends beyond technical mixing to encompass cultural and emotional dimensions:

**Technical progression:**
- KS1: Primary colour recognition, basic mixing discoveries
- KS2: Secondary and tertiary colours, tint and shade creation, understanding of complementary relationships
- KS3: Systematic colour theory application, colour temperature, harmonious schemes
- KS4: Intentional colour symbolism, sophisticated palette development, colour as conceptual tool

### Three-Dimensional Work

Progression in sculptural and constructed work follows distinct pathways:

**Material understanding and construction:**
- KS1: Additive and subtractive processes with malleable materials, simple joining
- KS2: Planning three-dimensional outcomes, armature construction, multiple materials combination
- KS3: Understanding material properties informing technique selection, structural integrity consideration
- KS4: Sophisticated material transformation, installation considerations, conceptual approaches to form

**Spatial awareness:**
- KS1: Front-facing construction, limited consideration of multiple viewpoints
- KS2: Awareness of 'in-the-round' viewing, balance and stability
- KS3: Deliberate viewpoint planning, relationship between form and space
- KS4: Environmental relationships, scale considerations, viewer interaction

### Printmaking Progression

**Technical development:**
- KS1: Mono-printing, textured surface experiments, repeat patterns
- KS2: Relief printing with developed compositions, registration awareness
- KS3: Multi-layer printing, reduction prints, screen printing introduction
- KS4: Complex processes (collagraph, drypoint, photo-transfer), editioning understanding

### Digital Art and Design

Jeff Adams has extensively documented how digital progression must be understood not as separate from traditional skills but as extending creative possibilities:

**Technical capabilities:**
- KS2: Basic digital image creation and manipulation, understanding of layers
- KS3: Strategic tool selection, combining digital and traditional processes, understanding of resolution and format
- KS4: Sophisticated digital workflows, integration across media, critical awareness of digital aesthetics

## Cross-Cutting Progressions

### Sketchbook Development

The sketchbook represents a crucial site for tracking progression across multiple dimensions. Research by NSEAD has identified clear developmental patterns:

**KS1-2:** Sketchbooks as collections and exploratory spaces
**KS3:** Development of investigative approaches, annotation emerging
**KS4:** Strategic use for research, experimentation, reflection and development; annotation demonstrating critical thinking

John Steers has warned against overly prescriptive sketchbook requirements that prioritise presentation over genuine investigation, noting that authentic progression involves increasingly personal and purposeful use rather than compliance with templates.

### Critical and Contextual Understanding

Rachel Mason's research emphasises that cultural understanding must progress alongside making skills:

**KS1:** Simple recognition and description of artworks, basic personal response
**KS2:** Beginning to make connections between own work and others', simple contextual awareness
**KS3:** Analysis using appropriate terminology, understanding of cultural and historical contexts
**KS4:** Sophisticated critical frameworks, articulation of influence and inspiration, independent contextual research

### Creative Independence and Personal Voice

Progression toward artistic autonomy represents a fundamental trajectory:

**Early years and KS1:** High degree of creative freedom within clearly defined parameters
**KS2:** Increasing ability to make informed choices about materials and approaches
**KS3:** Developing personal preferences and interests, beginning to identify artistic concerns
**KS4:** Emergence of individual artistic identity, self-directed inquiry, personal artistic intentions

Eileen Adams has documented how this progression is often non-linear, with young children displaying remarkable creative independence that can diminish during middle years if teaching becomes overly prescriptive, before re-emerging in later secondary education.

## Assessment of Progression

### Formative Assessment Approaches

Effective assessment of progression in art requires methods that honour the subject's distinctive character:

**Observation and dialogue:** Teachers tracking development through classroom interaction, questioning that probes thinking and decision-making
**Portfolios:** Collections demonstrating development over time across multiple dimensions
**Self and peer assessment:** Pupils developing critical frameworks that support metacognitive awareness of own progression
**Sketchbook annotation:** Written and visual reflection evidencing developing understanding

### Summative Assessment Structures

**KS1-2:** Teacher assessment against National Curriculum attainment targets
**KS3:** Teacher assessment, often using school-specific or commercial progression frameworks
**KS4:** 
- GCSE: Controlled Assessment (60% typically) and Externally Set Assignment (40%)
- Assessed against four AOs with standardised mark schemes
- Grade boundaries determined nationally by exam boards

## Barriers to Progression and Interventions

### Common Progression Plateaus

Research identifies several common points where progression may stall:

**Upper KS2 (Years 5-6):** Increasing self-consciousness can lead to creative risk-aversion
**Intervention:** Emphasis on experimental approaches, exposure to diverse artistic styles challenging realism dominance, celebration of personal expression

**Early KS3:** Transition anxieties, repetition of KS2 content, or excessive technical focus
**Intervention:** Clear building on prior learning, maintaining creative challenge, technical skills taught in service of creative intentions

**KS4:** Over-emphasis on examination outcomes constraining experimentation
**Intervention:** Assessment frameworks that reward creative risk-taking, teaching focused on genuine artistic development rather than formulaic responses

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Leslie Cunliffe's research on special educational needs in art education demonstrates that progression pathways must be flexible:

- Recognising that pupils may progress at different rates across different skill areas
- Providing multiple entry points to activities
- Valuing diverse forms of creative expression and achievement
- Adapting materials and processes to ensure access whilst maintaining creative challenge

## Teacher Knowledge and Progression

Effective teaching of progression requires specialist subject knowledge. The National Society for Education in Art and Design (NSEAD) emphasises that teachers need:

**Material and process knowledge:** Understanding how different media behave and what they enable
**Artistic literacy:** Familiarity with historical and contemporary art, craft and design
**Pedagogical content knowledge:** Understanding how artistic capabilities develop and how to support that development
**Assessment literacy:** Ability to recognise and articulate progression across multiple dimensions

John Steers has consistently advocated for subject-specialist teaching in secondary schools and specialist support in primary settings, arguing that generalist teachers without art training may struggle to recognise and nurture authentic artistic development.

## Practical Implications for Curriculum Design

### Vertical Progression Planning

Schools should develop progression frameworks that:
- Map skill development across key stages with specific exemplification
- Identify key vocabulary progression showing increasing technical sophistication
- Specify material and process introduction and development
- Plan strategic revisiting of concepts at increasing levels of complexity

### Horizontal Coherence

Within each key stage, curriculum planning should ensure:
- Balance across different art forms and media
- Integration of making, critical and contextual studies
- Progressive development of themes and concepts
- Building complexity in technical demands and creative independence

### Long-Term Projects

Research supports extended projects that allow pupils to:
- Work through complete creative cycles from research through experimentation to realisation
- Develop sustained engagement and deeper material understanding
- Experience authentic revision and refinement processes
- Build stamina and commitment essential for artistic development

## Contemporary Considerations

### Digital Technologies

Jeff Adams argues that digital progression must be understood as developing critical digital literacy alongside technical capabilities, with pupils progressing from consumption to critical creation and understanding how digital tools shape creative possibilities and constraints.

### Socio-Cultural Contexts

Rachel Mason's work reminds us that progression models inevitably reflect cultural values. Contemporary practice should:
- Acknowledge diverse aesthetic traditions and progression pathways
- Challenge Eurocentric models of development
- Recognise collaborative as well as individual progression
- Value community-engaged and socially-responsive art practices

### Assessment Reform

Recent qualifications reforms have prompted renewed debate about progression into post-16 study:

**A Level specifications** (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, WJEC) maintain the four AO structure but expect:
- Greater independence in project conception and management
- More sophisticated critical and contextual understanding
- Higher levels of technical proficiency
- Emerging personal artistic identity and coherent body of work

## Conclusion

Progression in art skills represents a multi-dimensional developmental journey that resists simple linear description. As Eileen Adams, John Steers, Rachel Mason, Leslie Cunliffe and Jeff Adams have variously demonstrated, effective progression frameworks must balance technical development with creative growth, acknowledge cultural diversity in aesthetic values, and maintain space for individual pathways and personal voice.

Teachers and curriculum leaders must understand that authentic progression:
- Operates across multiple domains simultaneously
- Varies in pace and pathway between individuals
- Requires sustained engagement over time
- Depends on teaching that balances instruction with creative freedom
- Must be assessed through methods appropriate to the subject's distinctive character

The National Curriculum provides a framework, but the art and expertise of teaching lies in translating those broad expectations into meaningful learning experiences that genuinely develop pupils' artistic capabilities, understanding and confidence across their school careers.
